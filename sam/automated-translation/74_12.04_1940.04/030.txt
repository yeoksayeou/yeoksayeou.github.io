Grand Plans for University Establishment by Future University Presidents

The movement to build a university has been a constant topic of discussion for about 20 years since the movement for the establishment of a people's university (민립 대학). Entering recent years, as the cultural fortunes of the peninsula have auspiciously advanced, the momentum to finally establish either a comprehensive university or a single-department college has grown stronger. Now, we hear about the intentions from the heads of various specialized schools. For reference, looking at the current state of university education in Europe and America:

A university is the pivot of learning in its society. The progress of a country's culture can be gauged by the number and quality of its universities. Looking now at the state of university education in European and American countries:

United Kingdom... In the UK proper, there are 16 universities. Among them, the famous ones are, of course, "Oxford" and "Cambridge." The total number of university students is over 53,800, while the number of professors and lecturers reaches over 5,970, giving the impression that it exists more for <32> the research of the professors than for the students.

Italy... There are 23 state universities and 3 private universities. The student population is over 72,940. There are over 6,690 professors.

Germany... With 23 comprehensive universities, several were literally the academic centers of the world until a few years ago due to their excellent quality. Among them, Berlin University is the most renowned.

France... The number of universities is 17, among which the University of Paris <33> has the longest history. The student population is over 73,850.

United States... Possessing a total of 1,123 universities, it reveals itself as a wealthy nation. Furthermore, 80% of these are private universities. "Harvard," "Yale," and "Chicago" are prominent.

Switzerland... Although the population is merely 4,060,000, it boasts 7 universities. Among them, the University of Zurich <32> is the most famous.

Finland... This nation, currently at war with the Soviet Union, is world-leading in the spread of education, with almost no illiteracy, and has 2 universities. The country's population is only 3.24 million.

Netherlands... Despite being a small country with a population of 8,550,000, it has 4 state universities, 2 private universities, 1 engineering university, and 1 agricultural university, preserving the legacy of its former status as an advanced and prosperous nation of the modern era.

Posŏng College President Kim Sŏngsu's Plan for a Six-College Comprehensive University (Law, Commerce, Literature, Engineering, Medicine, Science) - When the Current Four Million Won Becomes Ten Million Won - Interview with President Kim Sŏngsu

Founding Era and Current Endowment

On a very warm early morning in early spring (肇春), I visited the President of Posŏng Specialized School (普成 專門 學校), Mr. Kim Sŏngsu (金性洙), at his residence in Kyedong-jŏng (桂洞町). President Kim, dressed in a white *paji chŏgori* and white *turumagi*, greeted the reporter with a welcoming face <33> and guided me into the room, saying,
"You called the school and my home several times, but I happened to be out each time, I'm sorry about that." He then offered me a seat.
"Oh, not at all. You must be busy, sir, yet I've come to trouble you, so I'm the one who should be sorry," I replied, taking a seat and first looking around the room. Three or four calligraphic works by WIch'ang (葦滄) were on the wall, along with paintings by Ch'ŏngjŏn (靑田) and antiques such as Koryŏ celadon (高麗磁器). I stated the purpose of my visit and began asking questions.

Reporter: "How many years has it been since your school's founding this year?"
President: "I believe it's the 33rd year. Its origin dates back to the 9th year of Kwangmu (光武), when the late Yi Yong'ik (李容翊), former Head of the Korean Imperial Household Treasury (內藏院卿), obtained approval from the Korean Ministry of Education (學部) to establish it."
Reporter: "In the beginning, what departments were there? And who was the first president?"
President: "There were the Law Department and the Economics Department, and Mr. Sin Haeyŏng (申海永) took office as president. The original founders were Mr. Yi Yong'ik (李容翊) and Mr. Son Pyŏnghŭi (孫秉熙)."
Reporter: "What is the current endowment (基本金) of your school?"
President: "There is land yielding 8,000 *sŏk* (석, traditional unit of volume) of grain annually, a couple of mountains attached to the school, and other land (其外 其地) totaling 70,000 *p'yŏng* (평, traditional unit of area). Even at 15 won per *p'yŏng*, that would be about 1,000,000 won. Also, the buildings, at current market prices, are worth around one million won. So, all together, the endowment (基本金) might be nearly 4,000,000 won."

Intention for University Elevation

Reporter: "Lately, it seems there's a vigorous movement among some private specialized schools towards elevating their status to universities. Do you, sir, have any intention of promoting your school to university status?"
President: "I hold the hope and ideal for establishing a university, but considering our difficult financial situation and the current state of affairs (時局), we haven't reached the point of formulating concrete plans. For now, my thinking is just to make the current specialized school more substantial than others, perfect the Law and Commerce departments without fault, and strive to make at least some modest contribution to society."
Reporter: "If you were to establish the university you envision, sir, what departments would you want to install?"
President: "Talking about setting up specific departments when no concrete plan exists seems like an exaggeration, and I feel hesitant (忌諱하고 싶습니다마는) <34> as if putting out words lacking credibility to society. But if I must say, my ideal is to establish a comprehensive university. As for departments, I would keep the existing Law and Commerce departments, third would be Literature, fourth Engineering, fifth Medicine, and sixth Science, thus having six departments. I also intend to establish a preparatory course (豫科)."
Reporter: "If you were to establish Medicine or Engineering, how much funding do you estimate would be required for each?"
President: "To make them complete, there's no limit. I think Medicine would require 3,000,000 won, and Engineering would demand 4,000,000 won. Anyway, given our difficult circumstances, I believe we could establish the comprehensive university mentioned above with a minimum of about 10,000,000 won. When I went to the US several years ago, I found out Harvard University's total endowment was 2 billion dollars ($). Compared to that, a 10,000,000 won comprehensive university seems like child's play (아이들 작란같지요). How can they even compare? It's just too immense. (Laughs)"

Construction of Gymnasium and Main Auditorium

Reporter: "As a central institution of society or for student research, do you have any intention of establishing something like a Natural Science Hall or a Physics and Chemistry Research Institute (理化學 硏究所)?" <35>
President: "Our school currently only has Law and Commerce departments, so we don't yet feel the need (感하지 않습니다) to establish a Natural Science Hall or a Physics and Chemistry Research Institute."
Reporter: "Do you perhaps have any underlying plans (腹案) to establish them in the future?"
President: "Maybe if we establish a comprehensive university in the future, but right now there are no underlying plans (腹案)."
Reporter: "These days, the government strongly emphasizes health and greatly promotes physical fitness improvement, even considering health in entrance exams. Wouldn't a gymnasium be necessary for your school?"
President: "Yes, the establishment of a gymnasium is a pending issue, and we were about to start construction, but due to the emergency situation (非常時) and material shortages, we haven't been able to commence yet. Simultaneously, we also have plans drawn up to construct a main auditorium."
Reporter: "How much funding do you estimate for building the gymnasium? And how much for the main auditorium?"
President: "We budgeted 200,000 won for the gymnasium and set a budget of 300,000 won for the main auditorium construction."

Library Expansion

Reporter: "Your school's library, in terms of its building and scale within Korea (朝鮮), is something we can be proud of and arguably the best. Do you have any plans for further expansion?"
President: "Yes, we intend to expand. Originally, the main building was constructed with funds taken from the endowment, but the library was built purely through donations. We take pride in it as the crystallization of many people's sweat and blood. The highest donation was 20,000 won, the lowest was 50 *jŏn* (전, currency unit), and it was built by a total of 1,000 donors. Truly, if our school has something to boast about, it's the library. It wasn't built with the money of one wealthy person but by pooling the loving sweat and blood of many, so we take pride in it. It was built to commemorate the 30th anniversary of the school's founding. Since the library building was constructed before the Incident (事變, likely referring to the Sino-Japanese War), it cost 220,000 won then, but now it would probably cost nearly 500,000 won. We have about 50,000 books and purchase 14,000 to 15,000 won worth of books annually. Among the current books, there was a donation of 2,000 volumes from a certain Mr. Mo (某氏), and about 3,000 volumes donated by various people from home and abroad. To fill the current building with books, we would need to purchase another 150,000 volumes, requiring 1,000,000 won."
Reporter: "Among your library's books, how many are old Korean books (朝鮮 古書籍)?" <36>
President: "There are about 12,000 to 13,000 volumes. By the way, our library possesses a treasure that others don't. It's the 'Taedong Chiji' (大東地志), a work by Kosanja Kim Chŏngho (古山子 金正浩) in 15 volumes. The author personally wore straw sandals, endured starvation, and suffered all sorts of hardships (風霜) while traveling every nook and cranny of Korea (朝鮮) to research and write this, a so-called geography of Korea. Unfortunately, because this book was never published, only this single copy written in the author's own hand exists, making it an extraordinary treasure (貴物). It's a unique (無二) book that cannot be found anywhere else in the world. It's even more detailed and complete than maps drawn using current scientific methods. However, the author was suspected by the government of the time and viewed with aversion (忌諱함을 받아) because such a figure could reveal the country's secrets to foreign nations, leading to his execution at the hands of the Taewŏn'gun (大院君). Since it was the era of the Seclusion Policy (鎖國主義), it was natural for him to be suspected."
Reporter: "When you traveled the world, sir, which university library was the largest and most impressive to you?"
President: "All of them were grand in scale, so I envied them all. Among the university libraries I saw, the largest and most splendid was the Yale University library in the US. When I visited, the construction of their new library had just finished. They said it cost 7,500,000 dollars to build, about the size of the Government-General of Korea (朝鮮 總督府) building. It was also built through the donation of a single person, and there's a very beautiful anecdote about it. A person named Harkness (?) came to Yale University and asked the president if there was anything they needed money for. The president expressed the opinion that they needed to build a new library. Harkness then asked how much it would cost. The president replied that they would know once it was built. So, Harkness signed his name on a check (小切手) but left the amount blank, telling the president to go to the bank and withdraw as much as needed. After the construction was completed, the president filled in 7,500,000 dollars on the check (小切手). The person who gave without specifying the amount is admirable, and the person who spent as much as needed is also admirable. Of course, it must have been due to mutual trust, but it's a phenomenon unseen in Korea (朝鮮)."
Reporter: "Do you have any plan for library management?"
President: "Yes, there is one. If someone donates 10,000 won or more, we don't use that money to buy books. Instead, we buy land or deposit it in a bank, preserving the principal without spending it. Then, if land was bought, we use the harvest from that land, or if it's cash, the interest <37> to buy books. We then name a collection after the donor, calling it the 'So-and-so Collection' (아무개 문고). For example, if Kim Sŏngsu donated 10,000 won to the library, the interest from that 10,000 won would be used to buy books annually, and those books would be labeled the 'Kim Sŏngsu Collection' (金性洙 문고). Therefore, as long as Posŏng Specialized School (普成 專門學校) exists, for hundreds or thousands of years, the principal remains intact, so the Kim Sŏngsu Collection will also grow indefinitely."

Current School Status

Reporter: "Who are the current directors (理事) of your school?"
President: "Mr. Kim Yongmu (金用茂), Mr. Kim Yŏngju (金泳柱), Mr. Cho Tongshik (趙東植), Mr. Han Kiak (韓基岳), and myself, making five in total."
Reporter: "How many professors, lecturers, and other staff members are there?"
President: "There are 17 professors and full-time lecturers, and also 17 part-time (囑託) lecturers. Among them, there are 2 Japanese (內地人) full-time lecturers and 5 Japanese part-time lecturers, plus 1 Western part-time lecturer. Including other staff, it's about 50 people."
Reporter: "What is the current student enrollment?"
President: "There are 253 students in Law and 271 in Commerce, totaling 524."

Foreign University of Interest

Reporter: "When you traveled the world, sir, was there any university that caught your eye, meaning a university you'd want to emulate, one you wished you could build like?"
President: "To our eyes, they all looked splendid in scale and facilities, so it's difficult to single one out. Also, each university has its strengths and weaknesses (一長一短). Among those I saw, in England, it was Cambridge University and Oxford University, and in the US, Harvard University and Princeton University were particularly excellent and appealing to me."

My Hope and Ideal

Reporter: "Finally, if you have any helpful words for reference, I would be grateful to hear them."
President: "I await people of action rather than people of words, and I strive to cultivate such individuals. It may sound presumptuous, but we Koreans (朝鮮 사람), starting with myself, lack practical implementation (實踐 躬行) and are people of words only. Our standards are high, but we are not faithful to our own work. Therefore, education is needed to nurture people of action rather than words, and for the emergence of such figures. This <38> is my current hope and ideal."
Reporter: "Thank you very much for sharing so many valuable insights during your busy time."
Saying this, I rose from my seat, and President Kim kindly saw me off to the door.

Yŏnhŭi Section: In Ten Years, After 35 Years Since Founding, A University with Five Million Won - Including a Natural Science Research Institute and a Department of Religion - Interview with President Wŏn Han'gyŏng

Ten-Year Plan for University Establishment

On a warm spring day, just as spring was about to spread its wings (나래를 펴려는), I visited Yŏnhŭi Specialized School (延禧 專門學校), nestled in the pine forest (松林) of Sinch'on (新村) west of the capital (城西). When I knocked (뚜다리니) on the president's office door, it was lunchtime, and the president was absent, so I had no choice but to wait in the reception room. After waiting for about 20 minutes, President Wŏn Han'gyŏng (元漢慶) appeared and said,
"It must have been quite troublesome for you to come on such a muddy day. Please come in this way."
He then guided me into the president's office.
I followed him into the office, stated the purpose of my visit (來意), expressed my respect for his efforts in nurturing Korean youth, and began asking my professional questions in succession.

Reporter: "According to recent newspaper reports, Ewha Women's Specialized School (梨花 女子 專門學校) is abolishing its existing specialized school system to establish a university, and rumor has it that Severance Medical Specialized School (세부란스 醫專) is also planning to establish a comprehensive university. Does your school have any intention of abandoning the current specialized system and renewing its structure as a university?"
President: "Why wouldn't we? I believe our school was probably the first among the private specialized schools <39> in Korea (朝鮮) to have such an intention, and it has already become known to the public."
Reporter: "At that time, how many years was the plan set for, and from which year were you planning to start?"
President: "The initial plan was formulated in the 5th year of Shōwa (昭和 5年, 1930). We set a 10-year plan back then, intending to complete it this very year. This year marks the 25th anniversary of our school's founding, so we had a plan to commemorate this period—short if you consider it short, long if you consider it long—by completing the university establishment. However, unexpectedly, the China Incident (支那事變) broke out midway, forcing us to halt the plan. The reasons were, firstly, the impossibility of raising donations within Korea (朝鮮) under the conditions of the Incident, and secondly, the reluctance (忌諱하기) of the United States, upon which we greatly depended, to send funds due to the worsening international relations."
Reporter: "In that case, are you giving up the university establishment plan?"
President: "No (안요). We have established a new 10-year plan. That is, we aim to see the university established by the 35th anniversary of our school's founding. However, if the current Incident (事變) drags on into a long war extending beyond 10 years, we may inevitably be unable to realize it. But there's no rule that the Incident <40> must continue forever, nor that international relations must always remain strained, so we believe better times will come."

Five Million Won University Establishment Fund and Current Foundation

Reporter: "If you were to establish a university, how much funding do you estimate?"
Dean: "Well. I don't know how much other schools are budgeting, but we feel a need for about 5,000,000 won."
Dean (學監) Dr. Yi Myomok (李卯黙), sitting beside him, kindly answers with his gentle face.
Reporter: "What is the current capital (資本金) of your school now?"
President: "It's 450,000 US dollars, so let me see... (he takes out his fountain pen and calculates) converting it to local currency, it would exceed 2,000,000 won. However, books and land are not included in that calculation. The books are worth about 100,000 won, and the land is 230,000 *p'yŏng*, which would fetch 150,000 won per *p'yŏng* at current prices." (*Note: 150,000 won/p'yŏng seems incredibly high, likely a typo in the original, perhaps meant 15 won/p'yŏng or 150 won total for the plot? Given the context of Posong's land value, 15 won/p'yŏng seems more plausible, yielding 3.45M won. I will translate as written but acknowledge the likely error.*)
Reporter: "If you elevate to university status, do you have any underlying plans (腹案) to add more departments?"
President: "Of course, we intend to add more (늘이려고 합니다)."
Reporter: "If you add more, what departments do you plan to add?"

Department of Architecture and Department of Religion

President: "Currently, we have Literature, Mathematics & Physics (數物科), and Commerce, totaling three departments. We are considering adding a Department of Architecture. There is a professor at our school with abundant knowledge of architecture, and he particularly advocates for it. (Laughs)"
Reporter: "In my opinion, places like Tokyo Imperial University (東京 帝大), Rikkyo University (立敎 大學), Kyoto Doshisha University (京都 同志社 大學), or Nishinomiya Kwansei Gakuin University (西宮 關西 學院 大學) have departments like Theology (神科), Religious Studies (宗敎 學科), or Divinity (神學科) for studying Christianity or Buddhism. Especially since Christian universities often have courses like Comparative Religion, Philosophy of Religion (宗敎 ² 學), Systematic Theology, or Biblical Theology. Given that your school in Korea (朝鮮) is a Christian institution, establishing such a department seems like your school's mission, both from the perspective of its founding purpose and various other points. What are your thoughts on this?" (*Note: 宗敎 ² 學 is likely a typo for 宗敎哲學 - Philosophy of Religion*)
President: "Yes, we have considered that for a long time and even planned to implement the establishment of a Theology Department (神科) last spring. However, it wasn't decided by the board of directors, and since our school is jointly managed by the Presbyterian (長老會) and Methodist (監理會) churches, <41> we must respect the opinions of each church, so we are currently holding off. Presently, there is a Presbyterian Theological Seminary (長老會 神學校) in P'yŏngyang (平壤) and a Methodist Theological Seminary (監理會 神學校) in Kyŏngsŏng (京城). If we were also to establish a theology department, the church side might misunderstand it as opposing, challenging, and competing with those two seminaries."
Reporter: "Since the purpose of the Presbyterian and Methodist seminaries is purely to train clergy, if your school establishes a religious studies department purely from an academic standpoint, not aiming to train clergy, wouldn't both churches likely not see it as a major issue?"
President: "Yes, in any case, even if our school doesn't get promoted to university status, we intend to establish the Theology Department (神科) sooner or later (今明間). For this purpose, we have decided to invite Professor Matsumoto (松本), one of the authorities in Japanese theological circles and a professor at the Aoyama Gakuin (靑山學院) theology department, to our school this spring."

Relationship with the Mission Headquarters

Reporter: "Since the topic came up, may I ask about the current trend in the relationship between your school and the missionary organizations, and what stance you intend to take in the future? Severance Medical Specialized School (世醫專) has already severed ties with the Southern Presbyterian Mission (南長老派) and plans to cut ties with the Northern Presbyterian Mission (北長老派) next March to become independent. Does your school have any intention of cutting all ties with the mission headquarters?"
President: "Yes, Severance Medical Specialized School (世醫專) seems to have done that, but we don't feel the need to do so. We have also severed ties with the Northern Presbyterian Mission (北長老派), but we maintain our connection with the Southern Presbyterian Mission (南長老派) and the Southern and Northern Methodist Missions (南北監理派), and we intend to continue this relationship in the future. The Northern Presbyterian Mission provided an annual subsidy of 7,000 won, which isn't a major issue."
Reporter: "How much funding comes annually from the US mission boards?"
President: "About 52,900 won."
Reporter: "Pardon the personal question, but what is the total annual income, and how are the expenditures managed?"
President: "The annual income, combining everything, is over 162,900 won, and all of that money gets spent."

Library Construction and Natural Science Research Institute Design

Reporter: "Do you have any plans for a major expansion of the library in the future?"
President: "We intend to expand. We too are quite dissatisfied with the current library and have already drawn up building plans for a new library."
Saying this, the president stands up from his seat, retrieves the blueprints <42> from the desk under the far wall, shows them, and explains kindly,
"This drawing right here is the library we plan to build next to this office where we are currently sitting."
Reporter: "If you expand the library, how much do you estimate the total cost to be?"
President: "We estimate the building cost at 300,000 to 400,000 won, and adding the cost of books, we think we'll need about 1,000,000 won."
Reporter: "Do you have any intention of establishing a Natural Science Hall or a Natural Science Research Institute?"
President: "Why wouldn't we? That is also under considerable consideration right now. Starting this spring, we are renaming the traditional Mathematics & Physics Department (數物科) to the Science Department (理科) and intend to focus more on the natural sciences."

The Current Situation and Continental Culture

Reporter: "With the construction of a new order in East Asia (東亞), the advance of Koreans (朝鮮人) onto the continent is quite significant. Responding to these times, does your school have any plans for studying Chinese (支那) literature?"
President: "Well, it's not just because of the current times (時勢) that we feel the need, but studying Chinese (支那) culture and literature, or history and customs, in the sense of studying foreign cultures, is a very good thing. Our school has decided to incorporate Chinese language (支那語) classes into each department starting this spring. Also, along with the anti-British movement (排英運動) this spring, some seem to be advocating the 'English Unnecessary Theory' (英語 不必要論), but I have a different view on that. I believe that the more delicate international relations become, the more necessary it is to know the language and other aspects of that country."
Reporter: "Yes, there is the issue of reducing English instruction, but that's likely due to incorporating Chinese language (支那語) teaching hours, necessitating adjustments in the teaching schedule. A certain Japanese (內地人) professor at Kyŏngsŏng Imperial University (京城 帝大) reportedly argued that English is actually more necessary considering the current political situation (政局). By the way, who are the current directors (理事) of your school?"

General School Status and Future Thoughts

President: "The directors of our school include twelve Westerners. From the Japanese (日本 內地人) side, there are Messrs. Oda Yasuma (小田安馬), Niwa Kiyojirō (丹羽淸次郞), Mitsui Eijō (三井榮長), and Yamanaka Taikichi (山中大吉)—four individuals. From the Korean (朝鮮人) side, there are Messrs. Yun Ch'iho (尹致昊), Yang Chusam (梁柱三), *Hŭiyŏm (?, name likely misspelled or obscure - 崔喜炎?), Chang Kyumyŏng (張奎明), Ch'oe Sunju (崔淳周), Yu Hyŏnggi (柳瀅基), and Sin Pongjo (辛鳳祚), totaling 23 members." <43> (*Note: 崔喜炎 is a possible reading for the partly illegible name*)
Reporter: "How many professors are there?"
President: "There are 44 full-time professors, 8 adjunct professors, 3 assistants, and including other staff, a total of 77 people. Among them, 9 are Western professors and 7 are Japanese (日本 內地人) professors."
Reporter: "What is the total number of students?"
President: "About 450."

Foreign University to Emulate

Reporter: "Sir, if you were to elevate your school to university status, which foreign university would you want to emulate, and which university do you find most appealing?"
President: "Well, each university has its strengths and weaknesses (一長一短). I can't definitively say I want it to be exactly like this or that university."
Reporter: "Still, there must be some university that aligns with your preferences?"
President: "If I were to single out any specific university, other universities might get very angry thinking I've slighted them. (Laughs) In my opinion, Princeton University, while not entirely perfect, appeals to me."
Reporter: "Dr. Yi (李), which university do you think is good?"
I turned my gaze to Dean (學監) Dr. Yi (李博士).
Dean: "Well, it differs from school to school. Among those I've seen, Harvard University appeals to me in terms of its academic atmosphere (校風) and other aspects."
Reporter: "I'm very sorry to have taken up so much of your time today."
Expressing my gratitude to the two gentlemen, I stood up from my seat. <44>

Ewha Women's Specialized School: Establishing a Comprehensive Women's University with a Ten Million Won Fund - Aiming to Newly Establish Departments of Physical Education, Medicine, and Social Work - Interview with President Kim Hwallan

New Departments This Spring

February 28th, noon. Getting off (버리고) the train at Sinch'on Station (新村驛), I set foot in the Ewha Academy (梨花 學園), the hall of learning for the young daughters of Korea (朝鮮). Although I had arranged to meet the president via phone beforehand, she seemed to be in a meeting, so I had no choice but to wait in the reception room for 30 minutes.
Presently, President Kim Hwallan (金活蘭) entered, saying,
"I'm very sorry to have kept you waiting. And thank you for coming on such a cold day."
She greeted me warmly (반겨 맞어 주신다).
"Oh, not at all. You must be very busy at the end of the semester; I'm the one who should feel sorry," I replied, sitting down again, stating the purpose of my visit, and beginning to pose questions.

Reporter: "I hear that starting from the new semester this spring, some academic systems will change in certain departments at your school. Which departments are they?"
President: "Previously, the Ewha Childcare School (梨花 保育 學校) was independent and not incorporated into the specialized division, but we have elevated it to a department. So, the Ewha Childcare School is gone, and instead, a Childcare Department (保育科) has been created within the specialized school."
Reporter: "Is the year system the same as before?"
President: "Yes (네), it's a 3-year system (3년제얘요)."
Reporter: "And are there any newly established departments?"

**Summary**

This 1940 article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine interviews the heads of five prominent private specialized schools in colonial Korea—Posŏng, Yŏnhŭi, Ewha, Severance Medical, and Taedong Engineering—about their ambitious plans to upgrade their institutions into universities. The leaders (Kim Sŏngsu, Wŏn Han'gyŏng, Kim Hwallan, O Kŭngsŏn, Yi Chongman) discuss their visions for comprehensive or single-department universities, desired faculties (including law, commerce, literature, engineering, medicine, science, architecture, religion, physical education, social work, agriculture, arts), required funding (ranging from 5 to 10 million won, immense sums for the time), current endowments, plans for new facilities like libraries, gyms, auditoriums, and research institutes, and relationships with foreign mission boards. They compare their aspirations with renowned universities in Europe and America, acknowledging the vast difference in scale while highlighting unique Korean needs and contexts, such as the importance of practical skills, social service, continental connections, and nurturing national talent.

For historians, this article offers valuable insight into the aspirations for higher education among Korean elites under Japanese colonial rule. It highlights the significant financial hurdles, the reliance on philanthropy and foreign aid (especially from US missions), and the specific educational philosophies and priorities of key figures shaping modern Korean education. The discussions reveal tensions between ideals and reality, the impact of the ongoing Sino-Japanese War ("the Incident") on plans and resources, and the differing strategies schools adopted regarding mission board relationships and curriculum development (e.g., inclusion of Chinese language, debates over English, focus on fields like nursing or engineering deemed suitable or necessary for Koreans). The detailed plans and budgets provide concrete data on the state and ambitions of private education during this critical period.

未來의 大學 總長의 大學 創設 雄圖
대학을 만들자는 운동은 민립 대학 창설 운동이 있은 뒤 약 20년간을 두고 늘 화제에 오르던 문제인데, 昨今年에 들어서 반도 문운이 좋게 진흥하면서부터, 혹은 종합 대학으로, 혹은 단과 대학으로 기어히 창설하여 보겟다는 기운이 농후하여저 간다. 이제 우리는 각 전문학교 수뇌자로부터 그 의도를 듣기도하는 바, 참고로 歐米 각국의 현재의 대학 교육을 보건대
대학은 그 사회의 학술의 중추부다. 그 나라의 문화의 진도는 대학의 수와 질을 보면 알 수 있을 것이다. 이제 歐米 각국의 대학 교육의 현상을 보면
英國…英 본국에는 대학이 16교다. 그 중에서 유명한 것은 물론 『옥쓰포-드』와 『켐부릿지』다. 대학생 총수는 53,800여명인데 교수 강사의 수는 5,970여명에 달해서 실로 학생을 위해서라느니 보다는＜32＞ 교수의 연구를 위해서 있는 듯한 느낌이 있다.
伊太利…관립 대학 23교 私大가 3교다. 학생 수는 72,940여명이다. 교수는 6,690여명이다.
獨逸…종합대학 23교로써 질의 우수한 점으로서 몇해 전까지는 문자 그대로 세계의 학부인 곳이 여러 곳이였다. 그 중에서도 伯林 大學은 가장 이름이 높다.
佛蘭西…대학 수 17교인데 그 중에서는 巴里＜33＞ 大學이 가장 역사가 오래다. 학생 수는 73,850여명이다.
米國…총수 1,123교에 달하는 대학을 가지고 있어 돈 많은 나라임을 알게 한다. 또 그 중의 8할은 私大다. 『하-바드』 『예일』 『쉬카고』 등이 저명하다.
瑞西…인구는 겨우 4,060,000밖에 안 되는데 대학은 7교나 가지고 있다. 그 중에서는 『츄리히』 대학이＜32＞ 가장 유명하다.
芬蘭…방금 蘇聯과 대전 중인 이 나라는 교육이 보급되기로는 세계 제일이어서 문맹이 거진 없는 상태인데 대학은 2교가 있다. 이 나라의 인구는 겨우 324만명이다.
和蘭…인구 8,550,000의 소국가면서도 官大 4교 私大 2교 工大 1교 農大 1교로써 일찌기 근세의 선진국이오 부강국이던 여풍을 남겨 가지고 있다.
