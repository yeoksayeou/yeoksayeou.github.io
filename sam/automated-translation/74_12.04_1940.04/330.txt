**女流文章集 (Collection of Writings by Women)**

**印度 旅行 (India Travel)**
By 朴仁德 (Pak In-dŏk)

Ms. Pak is a graduate of Ewha Haktang, studied in the United States for many years, and currently engages in rural work and writing.

As the ship neared the port of Bombay on the west coast of India, I exchanged my money for Indian currency. The unit of Indian currency is called the rupee, and at that time, 1 rupee was worth about 72 *chŏn* of our money. One rupee is divided into 16 annas, and the anna coin looks like our 10-*chŏn* white copper coin, but it is square.

On the morning of August 16th, when the ship arrived at the Bombay docks, it was pouring rain. It wasn't ordinary rain; it was as if someone was dumping water from above. After a few minutes, the rain stopped, and I disembarked. Laborers had red towels wrapped around their heads multiple times, with one end trailing behind. They wore something similar to a Chinese *durumagi* (overcoat) with short trousers and carried luggage barefoot, balancing even moderately heavy items on their heads. Their complexion was iron-black, but their skeletal structure was like that of Caucasians.

From the pier, I went to the train station, bought a ticket for Agra, and checked my luggage. The way people dressed was incredibly diverse. It is said that Indians dress and live differently according to their religion and caste. The main religions are Hinduism, followed by Islam, Buddhism, etc., and it is said that there are countless <256> castes within Hinduism.

The area of India is 1,802,657 square miles. To the north lies the world's highest mountain range, the Himalayas; to the south, the Indian Ocean; to the northwest, another mountain range separates it from Persia and Afghanistan; and to the east, it borders China and Thailand. Within this area, 320 million people live, speaking 1,400 languages. Gandhi and the world-renowned literary figure Tagore live among them.

While waiting for the train, I went to see the "Tower of Silence" of the Parsi believers in Bombay. In a densely wooded mountain area, there was a tower, and vultures flocked to the trees surrounding it. This tower is the burial place for Parsis. When a believer dies, the body is placed on top of this tower. While a Parsi priest prays to the God of Fire for the soul of the deceased in the temple beside the tower, the vultures sitting in the trees devour the entire body.

In the evening, leaving behind Bombay, a city of 800,000 people, I boarded the train for Agra. The trains in this country use a narrow-gauge railway. As we headed north, the mountains and fields were covered in a continuous green hue.
It naturally felt like our spring. India also has a rainy season, generally lasting for three months from mid-June, though the timing varies by region. At dusk, the Indian farmers returning home carrying their tools did not look very sturdy.

Traveling with me in the train compartment were a Muslim mother and daughter and one Hindu man. The Muslim mother and daughter wore gold, silver, and jeweled rings, earrings, and bracelets, and their clothes resembled those of Chinese women. The Hindu man seemed to be traveling with a servant; whenever the train stopped, the servant would come in from the third-class car to attend to him. Upper-class Indians travel with servants even when journeying, prepare their own food, and carry their own drinking vessels <257>, which look exactly like brass bowls (*not*tagu*).

Entering the school, the building was made of mud, and the roof was thatched with local trees and leaves. The floor was covered with some kind of mat, and boys and girls filled separate rooms, learning. The children all wore simple loincloths, their dark faces shining with eyes like morning stars.
The teacher wrote picture-like characters on the blackboard and taught them. When I asked what characters they were, he said "Sanskrit."

From there, I went to visit the homes where these children lived. Going quite a way outside the city of Agra, I found a village made of mud houses. They happened to be preparing dinner. The girls wore wide skirts and blouse-like tops, earrings in their ears, anklets on their ankles, and carried younger siblings perched on their hips. A mother was kneading flour dough, placing it on an iron griddle (*pŏnch'ŏl*), and cooking it like a pancake (*chembbyŏng*) over a fire made of dried cow dung mixed with mud. Once cooked, she placed it on a large tree leaf, tore off pieces with her hands, and ate it by dipping it into curry (a spicy dish boiled with vegetables).
Pigs and chickens roamed freely through the middle of the village.

The next morning, I went to see the Taj Mahal memorial hall, the pride of Agra. It was built hundreds of years ago by King Shah Jahan in memory of his most beloved queen after her death. He invited famous architects from Italy at that time, and it took 20,000 craftsmen and laborers 20 years to build. Upon arriving at the gate, even the gatehouse was made of marble. Inside the main gate, a pool stretched from the gate to the front of the memorial hall, surrounded by tropical trees. The memorial hall itself is in the Byzantine style, exquisitely built with marble. The roof is dome-shaped, and there are four tall minarets surrounding it. Inside the hall, <258> in the center, are two stone sarcophagi, surrounded by a finely carved marble screen. Seen from a distance, the carved white marble looks as fine as gauze (*mangsa*).
When you make a sound here, the echo is much more beautiful and grand than the original voice. Around the area with the sarcophagi, there are several rooms. All feature inlaid work (*chŏn'gam segong*). Inlaid work involves breaking stones into pieces like domino tiles (*kolp'ae-tchak*) to decorate walls, ceilings, floors, or outer walls. The inlaid work in this hall was done with precious stones. If a crimson rose mallow was carved on the wall, it was made by breaking gems of that flower's color. Because it is a building made of such precious stones, seeing this hall in the morning sun, evening glow, or moonlight makes one's spirit truly ecstatic from its brilliant light. Behind this building flows the Jumna River.

After spending a night in Agra, the next day I headed to Lucknow to visit the women's university, the first established in the East. It's not far from Agra, only a few hours' journey.
This time on the train, I traveled with an upper-class Hindu woman. She wore a light pink sari (*ssare*). Sari is the name for women's clothing; it's about five yards of cloth, wide like Western muslin (*sŏyangmok*). Half is pleated and worn as a skirt, and the other half covers the shoulders and head over a blouse (*chŏksam*). It looks poetic but seems impractical. As ornamentation, she notably wore a single diamond stud in one side of her nose.

The university students here all wore saris. There were many dormitory residents, and the dining hall and kitchen were separate for Hindu students. Muslim and Christian students ate food without restriction, so there was no issue. That evening, I spoke with the students for an hour. They said that people from a country with a climate as good as Korea's would surely be several times more efficient in their work than them.

The next day, after giving a lecture to the middle school girls, the principal introduced two students, saying <259> that these children were studying thanks to tuition fees sent by students from Ewha in Kyŏngsŏng. With the utmost politeness, they just smiled brightly without saying a word, as if asking me to convey their gratitude.

After spending two nights in Lucknow, I headed towards Calcutta on the east coast of India. The journey was extremely hot, but the Indian countryside, occasionally visible through the palm trees, was beautiful.

Getting off at Calcutta station, I visited a school established for the unfortunate women of India. The school building was an ordinary wooden Western-style house, and inside, 300 women lived and studied for long periods. Many of them were said to be child widows. At that time, a lady in her 70s was the principal of this school. She was its founder and principal. She had come to India from America with her husband as a missionary, raised six children (boys and girls), and worked. One summer, she sent five of her own children and one adopted Indian daughter to the Darjeeling summer resort, only to lose them all at once in a landslide at the mountain villa. Since then, she resolved to love and help the unfortunate women of India and established this current school.

After spending one night, I went out to see the city of Calcutta. The population here is slightly larger than Bombay, and the city is more modern. Entering a Buddhist temple, the paintings on the walls, the offerings for Buddha, and the way prayers were conducted were very similar to ours. Returning to the school for lunch, after the meal, they served various kinds of tropical fruits, which I ate deliciously. Then I hired a horse-drawn carriage, went to the pier, and boarded the ship bound for Myŏnjŏn (likely referring to Rangoon or another port in Burma/Southeast Asia). <260>

**「聖誕節」날 저녁 ("Christmas Eve" Evening)**
By 朴恩惠 (Pak Ŭn-hye)

Ms. Pak is a graduate of Kyŏngsŏng Chŏngsin Girls' School, studied in the United States for many years, and is currently a professor at Ewha Women's College. She is the wife of Mr. Chang Tŏk-su.

One day, returning from somewhere, I found a calling card that read, "To Mr. Chang! I am inviting a few friends for dinner on the evening of the 24th. Would you be able to come?" The person who left the card was the husband of my friend. Since it was Christmas Eve, it would be proper etiquette to invite the wife (?) if inviting the husband. He suggested we go together that evening, and I myself felt an impulse to go and drag the hostess out to create a "wives' party," but not wanting to be an uninvited guest, I just sent a calling card via my husband to the lady of the house. It said, "I am quite indignant. Must the husband and wife always live separately? Am I supposed to just guard an empty room on Christmas Eve? I was going to follow you but refrained for your sake, so please send me some table scraps (*sang pusŏrŏk*)."

My husband, returning late at night, called out from outside the door as if he had brought back something special, so I ran out. It wasn't a joke. He had sent a few table scraps in their child's chamber pot (*pyŏndo*). And then <261> I was invited for lunch the next day. "Of all days, Christmas Day! And again, invited alone? Since getting married, we've never spent Christmas Day apart. Are you planning to treat the wives with the leftovers after entertaining the husbands?" I didn't go that day. However, many days later, after my anger had subsided, I visited one day. Alone, of course! That time, I was treated to a feast, not scraps, had a pleasant time, and came back. Even now, he still receives invitations and goes out alone. Haven't all the wives (?) in this land ended up with the same anger! No, sorrow as I have? My friends! When you invite each other for a pleasant time, I wish you would include the wives as well--.

One evening, Mr. and Mrs. A came over. They always enter cheerfully, chatting away. After the initial greetings and exchange of Seoul news, the hosts and guests gathered and started a team game. "This New Year, our women's team will win," Mrs. A boasted repeatedly. However, we two kind and earnest women were pitifully defeated without mercy.

It must have been the day after that. We two women received an unexpected invitation. It was supposedly a consolation invitation, feeling sorry for defeating the weaker women. As a privilege of being the weaker ones-- no! Thanks to losing the game, we got fed and looked at photos--. Of course, it was much more enjoyable than sitting defeated the previous evening. Is the phrase "Blessed are the losers" meant for times like these? <262>

**靑春의 饗宴-寸景 斷想 (A Feast of Youth - Fleeting Thoughts on Short Scenes)**
By 崔義順 (Ch'oe Ŭi-sun)

Ms. Ch'oe graduated from Sungmyŏng Girls' High School and Nara Women's Normal School, worked as a women's reporter for the Tonga Ilbo for several years, then married Mr. Sŏl Ŭi-sik and currently lives as a demure wife in T'ongŭi-dong.

One time--
The clear water revealed white sand beneath, and my body floated lightly upon it, swimming.
How a weight of over thirteen *kwan* (approx. 49kg) could float in such shallow water was baffling, and it was also a mystery how I, who originally didn't know how to swim, managed to drift about so buoyantly.

Another time--
Crossing mountains and fields, I became terribly thirsty and searched for water. Suddenly, a well with a neatly covered lid appeared before me. Hurriedly, I opened the lid and scooped some into the cup I held. It wasn't water, but creamy white milk.
Taking a sip, it was warm and soothingly moistened my throat.
Readily, I scooped another cup and gave it to the friend standing beside me. <263>

Yet another time--
On both sides of a narrow alley, stacks of logs were piled so high that the sky was barely visible. I walked along the gaps, heading towards the main street.
Fearing that the logs on either side might collapse, I stepped very carefully. Just as I feared, suddenly a pile of logs from one corner came tumbling down, and I let out a terrified scream.
But luckily, I wasn't hit by the logs; instead, I nimbly jumped onto a logging truck that happened to be passing by.

These were all stories from my dreams during this past New Year.
I have a habit of hurrying to interpret my dreams just as I'm about to wake up.
I always wished inwardly, like a child, even before falling asleep, that I would have good dreams rather than ominous ones.
Although I don't know how to interpret dreams, I also have a habit of trying to analyze them with simple interpretations based on my own judgment – whether they felt pleasant or unpleasant.
As these nights of dreaming and days of waking and thinking repeat, it seems that only good dreams are being woven within me. The confusing and stifling dreams seem to drift far, far away, while the clear and refreshing dreams remain vividly.
It seems an incidental effort has arisen in my mind: to weave and unfold good dreams while sleeping, and then, while awake, to think of good things and try to see and hear cheerful things.
I want to have only good dreams. And I want to cherish those good dreams more dearly. <264>

It could truly be called an ice sheet, like a pane of glass laid out.
It had thawed during the day, but in the evening, it froze solid, making it seem like I was clearly seeing again those glass panes that are said to be scarce these days.
Filled with the feeling of skating on the icy street, I walked carefully and reached the twilight hour.
"Spare a penny, please! Spare a penny, please--"
A insistently whining child's voice struck my ear sharply.
That pleading sound subsequently spread breathlessly down the street.
"What! Get away!"
At the sound of a stern, loud voice, I must have glanced back quickly.
A large hand had already struck the young beggar's head.
"Slap- Crack-"
At this sound, I almost lost my balance but managed to regain it.
The sturdy man who had struck the child had already crossed to the safety zone and boarded a tram, while the struck young beggar remained standing there, crying,
"Aww!"
My heart lamented that it was not simple enough to dismiss it merely with the thought that the one who hit was an adult, and the one hit was a child. <265>

After Oro (beloved dog) left, Hyang (beloved cat) became a member of our household.
Whenever I thought that even the faint purring breath of young Hyang could suppress the commotion of the mice, I could manage a light sense of relief and a smile.
Just last month, a young mouse, which had been darting in and out because the New Year's preparation rice cake crumbs had scattered, was found lying stiffly dead on our table.
When I discovered the mouse's corpse, I was embarrassed to realize I was overcome by sentimental feelings.
My heart fell into a state of contrary reaction, wanting to apologize to the mouse that had succumbed to the "Neko-irazu" (cat-not-needed mouse poison) I had set out.
To dispel this kind of melancholy and delusion, I brought home young Hyang starting this New Year.
Before any such-- sacrifice occurs, caught in Hyang's teeth, I hope-- just by hearing Hyang's young, weak purr-- the mouse family will quickly move far away to another territory. 2. 14. From T'ongŭi-dong <266>

**理想的 夫婦-特히 夫婦 生活을 主로 (Ideal Couple - Especially Focusing on Married Life)**
By 尹聖相 (Yun Sŏng-sang)

Ms. Yun finished Sungmyŏng Girls' High School, graduated from Tokyo Women's Normal School, served as a reporter for the Chosŏn Ilbo, and now manages a household in Sŏngbuk-dong.

Once, while passing through an alley of official residences for the Monopoly Bureau in Sŏdaemun-jŏng, I encountered an ideal couple. The husband was blind, the wife lame. On an early spring morning, with the sunlight dazzling, they were walking along a street still sparse with pedestrians, heading towards the main road where the trams ran. I came upon them from behind. The wife guided her blind husband, and in return, the husband supported his wife whose steps were unsteady. They had probably spent the night leaning against the wall of some grand house deep in an alley in Sajik-dong and were now setting out for their morning preparations. The husband carried a straw mat (*kamani*) on his back, and the wife held a rice basket (*pap paguni*) at her side. Yet, their expressions and actions somehow seemed wholesome. In that beggar couple, I saw a certain solemn aspect of life. Isn't the true meaning of being a couple found in mutual trust, reliance, help, and inseparability? Even now, often recalling their image that comes to my mind, I frequently seek the right path of the way of husband and wife.

**內助 (Supporting from Within) <267>**

History proves that countless great scientists, artists, revolutionaries, and inventors received the support of wise wives from within (*naejo*). There is no need to cite specific examples here. It's not necessarily that the wife of a scientist shared his interest in science, or the wife of an artist was skilled in art, that constitutes the merit of *naejo*. Merely providing sufficient understanding, encouragement, joy, and hope for the fruition of his work can be fully recognized as the merit of *naejo*.

**尊敬 (Respect)**
There is a saying, *Ku i kyŏng ji* (久而敬之 - Respect grows with time/familiarity). I believe this maxim doesn't only apply to friendships. The deeper the intimacy, the easier it is for us to neglect this virtue of respect. This is especially true in married life.

**虛僞 (Falsehood)**
It seems falsehood is an unfortunate trait humans are born with. We must strive to aspire to truth and reject falsehood. However, one cannot help but wonder how much bleaker our lives would be without this magic of falsehood.

**分福 (One's Allotted Fortune/Luck in Marriage)**
I once acted as a matchmaker for the first time in my life. Even then, I didn't do it alone but served as an assistant to my husband's arrangement. We did it because, looking from either the groom's or the bride's side, it seemed suitable. After both parties met and got acquainted, their intentions and plans seemed to align, and word came that they would soon hold an engagement ceremony. However, the bride's mother somehow discovered a new fact: the groom had a child, not listed in the family register, born to some *kisaeng*. This was, of course, a fact unknown to us as well, so it was natural for the bride's side to be distressed. The groom's response was that it was a momentary tactic driven by the desire to make the marriage <268> succeed, and now he was too ashamed to face them. Interpreted charitably, there was much room for understanding. However, from the bride's perspective, while the complication of what seemed a simple marriage plan might be somewhat tolerable, the primary concern sticking in her mind was whether he might repeat such misconduct after marriage. Being in the position of matchmakers, it was perhaps natural for us to receive the unreasonable question from the bride's mother asking if we could guarantee the groom's future. Rather than finding it difficult, we could only manage a wry smile (*koso*).
Is there anything in the world that isn't a gamble? But few things are as much of a gamble as marriage. If one could guarantee a person's future, it would likely relate to financial matters or similar issues. What good is guaranteeing the future of a marriage? We just laughed together, saying it was simply the bride's "allotted fortune" (*punbok*).

**P孃과 音樂-卒業을 압둔 P孃에게 (To Miss P and Music - To Miss P, About to Graduate)**
By 朴景嬉 (Pak Kyŏng-hŭi)

Ms. Pak is a graduate of Tokyo Music School and currently teaches music at Kyŏngsŏng Hyangsang Women's Domestic Science School.

Miss P! Having completed four years of student life in a foreign land, you must be so happy that in just two months, you will receive your honorable graduation <269>. I know that Miss P is now filled more with the hope of debuting in the music world she has longed for these four years than with the sorrow of leaving the familiar school grounds. The impressions and emotions gained upon taking the first step into society after graduation are always significant for everyone. Especially in the field of music, the direction of the initial start often governs the course of one's entire life. Therefore, it is necessary now to deepen your understanding of the current reality and prepare an attitude that will not be swayed by the wonders and disillusionments to come. Just as the basic vocal techniques learned in music school are always necessary, the pure determination you form now, facing graduation, will become a beautiful memory to recall when you enter society and a weapon to overcome the waves of society.
I hope you will accurately grasp the reality and make appropriate resolutions. In Chosŏn, music has not yet become widespread. Even if "Arirang" spread throughout the country some fifteen years ago, this has absolutely no relation to the music we discuss and signifies no promotion of music whatsoever. Rather, the invasion of low-quality popular songs (*yuhaengga*) is harmful. Commercialized art, "ragtime jazz," these are too far removed from what we should concern ourselves with. They are snacks for the audience, not nutritious staple food.
Even if the monthly concerts here at the Puminkwan (Civic Hall) are sold out, this is not proof that music has become widespread. If you move beyond superficial observation and analyze the audience in detail, this point will be understood. Using the audience as a barometer (*ch'ŏngugye*) to study music is extremely dangerous. Gaining temporary popularity through facial expressions and gestures is cheap pandering (*an'gahan yŏnghapchuŭi*) and artistic suicide. The life of a soloist is brilliant, but often they disappear from view without notice and vanish from public memory; I believe this is partly the reason. Do not pay much attention to the temporary mood of the audience or the sensationalism-seeking journalism (*chŏnarisŭm*). You must aim for the establishment of musical art that aligns with the breath of the times and the constructive will of society <270>. Of course, I know that various hardships will follow this path, but your heart will be adorned with the dignity of a monarch.
History proves that musicians unheard by our ears, nameless individuals who receive no social reward, later become artists representing their century. Chopin suffered from nervous breakdowns due to material hardship; Schubert, at his deathbed, had only 25 *wŏn*, causing difficulties even for funeral expenses; Berlioz often lamented lacking bread for the morrow.
The great sages of the past, the pioneers of their eras, always walked the path of art amidst hardship.
Music, once a privilege of the aristocracy in Europe, has now advanced into the streets among the general populace, enjoying popularity unseen in previous centuries. The unimaginable development of machinery makes this even easier. Indeed, Beethoven's Ninth Symphony, heard through loudspeakers on Chongno Street, is advancing into the streets and villages, often accompanied by the begging cries of street children. However, the musician's resolve remains unchanged throughout the ages.
Chosŏn is still relatively virgin territory on the international music stage. There is no history to follow yet, nor tradition to destroy. Everything is in a beautiful, hope-filled dawn period. When listening to refined performances becomes felt as necessary as the human desire for existence in all layers of society, whether rural or urban, then the social foundation of musical art can be considered solid.
To bring about such a foundation, music specialists, music lovers (*hoak*ka*), and the general public must all cooperate to perfect the incomplete reality; this is also a duty.
Every year, not a few newcomers cross the Korea Strait (*Hyŏnhaet'an*) to this land, but many lose their spirit due to the lack of response from reality <271> and instantly abandon their ideals and aspirations. Conversely, some among them, gaining excessive confidence from brief stage experience, adopt a self-righteous attitude, isolate themselves from the public, and even extinguish their own potential for progress. The dawning music scene of Chosŏn does not demand one or two heroes. It only needs soldiers who cooperate unanimously to pioneer the way.
Finally, as someone who knows Miss P's musical talent better than anyone, my only request is that you continue your research-oriented attitude, treating society as a practical training ground, with the same sincere attitude you had while studying at school.

**슬픈 先驅者 (A Sad Pioneer)**
By 金福鎭 (Kim Pok-chin)

Ms. Kim is a graduate of Kyŏngsŏng Childcare School (likely a misunderstanding or old name for a kindergarten/teacher training place), early devoted her passion to dramatic arts, appeared numerous times on stage and screen, earning praise as a famed actress (*myŏngu*). She continues to strive in the field of dramatic arts.

Now that I have some free time, memories of filming *Susŏnhwa* (Daffodil) come back piece by piece.
Among these fragmented recollections are the resentful face of the protagonist, a woman dressed in white mourning clothes like a fairy, unable to resolve her unjust situation and seeking eternal resolution by throwing her pure heart and body into the lake;
The rushing about of the maid who tries her best for her mistress; <272>
The retreating figure of the school teacher leaving behind the tiresome teaching life in the gossipy village;
Various scenes from the movie like these, or the laughter and chatter from those times, certainly come to mind, but what remains most unforgettable is the figure of Mr. Kim Yu-yŏng, the director of *Susŏnhwa*.
Mr. Kim tragically passed away, but the way he directed is still so vivid before my eyes.
I played a small role as an old woman in *Susŏnhwa*.
My participation in *Susŏnhwa* was permitted out of sympathy for Mr. Kim, who was struggling due to a lack of actors.
So, while working together, his enthusiasm for *Susŏnhwa*, which sprang forth like a fountain even as he was carried around in a handcart (*sur[y]e*) due to his illness, instilled even a sense of awe in those who watched.
It must have been around December 10th. Mr. Kim, his swollen body wrapped in cotton clothes, sat in the handcart being pushed down the slope near an old chestnut tree. My gaze lingered absently on Mr. Kim's back as he rolled away without end. The handcart wheels pushed through the clear morning light as if going to gather happiness, passing along the mountain path, by the water's edge, around the water, and finally reaching the location.
Even while unable to move his body freely and traveling seated in a handcart, his passion for the success of *Susŏnhwa* was stronger than his desire for his own life.
During the time for filming preparation, we gathered around a bonfire to forget the cold.
His appearance seemed so pitiful that I asked,
"What if your illness worsens because you don't take care of yourself? Shouldn't you rest a bit?" <273>
To this, he replied,
"Rest? If the illness worsens, I'll die working."
"But still..."
I only muttered these words under my breath; before someone exerting such desperate effort, driven by passion—or rather, desperation—I had nothing more to say.
Although his body was carried on a handcart, his spirit burned hot as the sun and shone more beautifully than a diamond.
So, who could advise him to rest? Who could tell him to warm himself by the fire because it was cold, or wish him sleep because he was tired?
We could only hope that *Susŏnhwa*, being filmed amidst such desperate efforts, would become a good movie as the director wished, and that his illness would improve as the film rolled on, foot by foot.
However, the merciless illness worsened day by day, rendering useless the burning passion for the film world and the desperate efforts.
Futilely and tragically, abandoning the pleading cries of his family and the desires of his comrades to save him, he departed from this world.
The image of the director holding the megaphone (*mekyap'on*) with a trembling hand is still vivid, but when I stood before his coffin, as if in a dream, my chest tightened, and words failed me. <274>
Thinking quietly now, the deceased is pitiable, of course, but his family seems even more pitiable.
Furthermore, as someone who would have contributed much more to the film industry in the future, his loss is even more regrettable.
It feels as though his spirit might be striving to create beautiful films in paradise.
That image from that time, when my gaze lingered endlessly on the handcart, comes floating back.
Even now, passing the mountain path, by the water's edge, that handcart keeps rolling on.

**노래를 이저버려 (Forget the Song / On Forgetting Things)**
By 趙敬姬 (Cho Kyŏng-hŭi)

Ms. Cho graduated from the English Department of Ewha Women's College last year, a talented student, currently working as a women's reporter in the Arts and Culture Department of the Chosŏn Ilbo.

Last March, when the patches of snow that had lain white on the mountains and ditches had all melted, on a day when the warm spring breeze seemed about to arrive, I left school and went to P'yŏngsan Hot Springs with my grandmother. I acted as grandmother's guide, her protector, tasked with leading her by the hand and getting her onto the train. After successfully completing my duty and getting off at Kyŏngsŏng Station, I immediately went to find Michael (*Mikael*).
"How did you manage not to lose your grandmother?" <275>
That's what Michael said then, pinching my cheek. We both laughed it off, but his words settled in my mind. Whenever I forgot something, they would readily pop into my head. Besides myself, Michael knows best that I have forgetfulness (*kŏnmangchŭng*), a tendency to forget things easily. I won't even bother listing all the incidents of losing things due to forgetfulness here.

One day, returning from a fun game of *yut* (*yunnori*), I lost my handbag under the bright electric lights. This was theft (*tonan*), slightly different in nature from forgetfulness. It felt like the lonely, dizzying feeling after seeing a friend off at the station and turning back. Shaking my head slowly, I stepped outside the gate, and sleet mixed with misty rain felt cold on my cheeks. The streetlights on the dark street were dim. Wasn't my armpit, which had felt reassuringly heavy, suddenly too empty? We had been friends for quite a long time. My younger sibling Yobae(?) had gifted it to me when they went on a school trip to Tokyo. It was cheap and low quality, old, and visually unattractive. But I had grown fond of it, worn and cracked and peeled as it was; just gazing at it quietly felt like it was murmuring stories. And when I clicked it open, various belongings would appear... my treasures, cherished like my own eyes, they held so many whispered secrets. Wasn't it like a map on the wall, a small piece of luggage containing my entire self? Wasn't it my bedroom that no one else had ever looked into? My handbag seems transparent like glass, its contents visible piece by piece. Precious, cherished things; it's heartbreakingly regrettable. Inside the handbag, a telegram slip allows me to hear Michael's interrupting voice.
"Whatever day it may be, do not lose your prayer. Do not lose his name from your prayer."
The lipstick, with its ticklish fragrance inside my nose, its whispered voice, "Many, many of your kisses <276> must be mixed in here," it says. "Must be!"
My keys, the compact (*k'omp'aekt'ŭ*) Insŏn gave me, the dog charm Chŏngsun gave me, the wallet Meri gave me, what should I do with all this? I quite adore small cosmetics. More than their practical value, I like the charm they possess.
Others hearing this news might think it trivial, but for me, it contained markers of memories, pieces of my heart.
"How did you manage not to lose your grandmother?"
Michael would surely scold me if he heard this news. One of my friends, upon hearing this, laughed and said she couldn't risk dating for fear of losing things too...
Now, there's only one path left. To move all the precious things from the handbag into my head, to treasure (*pijang*) them there.
The thought of them being mistreated in the hands of a stranger is heartbreaking.
If losing the handbag that grew under my armpit is this heartbreaking, what would it be like to lose something bigger, like affection (*aejŏng*)? Would the feeling be similar? It's unthinkable.
Theft is something I must guard against myself. But a person with forgetfulness, who can sometimes forget themselves, can also enjoy space cheerfully and be peaceful like children. People should always know how to maintain empty space (*yŏbaek*) in their minds. <277>

**밤차 (Night Train)**
By 李恩徽 (Yi Ŭn-hwi)

Ms. Yi graduated from the English Department of Ewha Women's College and currently teaches at Chŏngsin Girls' School.

Suddenly, my right leg slips *jjik-* on the frozen pavement (*p'odo*), startling me from a light doze. My raised knee drops with a thud, my whole body loses balance, swaying back and forth. *Thud*, the train stops. Then the door opens, and a man carrying a net bag (*mangt'ae*), a woman balancing a basin (*hamjibak*) on her head, a man in a yellowish corduroy suit, another in a shabby fedora (*chungjŏlmo*) enter, looking around. Their eyes seek only empty seats. All their energy is focused on finding a place to sit. Then, when they spot someone occupying a two-person seat alone, lying down, a look of profound relief spreads across their faces.
"Where are you going?"
An old woman, looking a bit over forty, asks the plump woman in the opposite seat, her narrow eyes crinkling into a smile. Her speech is fast-paced.
"Going all the way to Seoul."
A strong, accented dialect replies in a loud, unguarded voice. Slowly.
"My goodness, you're going far. I'm going to Ch'ŏrwŏn. My daughter just gave birth (*mom p'urŏssiyo*). <278>"
"Oh, what did she have (*musigi nasso*)?"
"It's a boy. Already the second one."
"Oh, you must be thrilled (*chilkkŏpkesso*). My son is studying (*kongbu hŏnŭn'gi*) in Seoul, and he says buying meals is so hard. So I thought, why don't I try cooking for him, so I'm taking this."
A simple intimacy already flows abundantly in their conversation. Such warmth is hard to find amidst splendor and luxury. Whether others notice or not, the two women pour out delightful boasts about their sons, daughters, and grandchildren amidst the mix of tobacco smoke, breath, nasal exhalations, and whiskey smells.
Before long, the Hamgyŏngbuk-to woman takes out a dark, delicious-looking lump of *yŏt* (Korean taffy) from her bundle (*pottari*), holds one end firmly, stretches it, twists off a piece, and offers it to the Hamgyŏngnam-do woman. Their bundles must be full of things like *yŏt* and salted fish (*chaban*) for their sons, soy sauce, chili paste (*koch'ojang*). The bundle destined for the grandchild likely contains heaps of dried seaweed stems (*miyŏkkkokchi*) and baby diapers (*kijŏgwi*). This grandmother must be eagerly anticipating making a colorful striped jacket (*saektongjŏgori*) for her newborn grandchild soon. With an hour left, they don't seem to run out of topics. The accented voices remain loud.
Bundles, bundles! At country train stations, these colorful bundles present a unique spectacle (*ilch'ae*). A few dried pollock (*myŏngt'ae*), rice cake crumbs, clothes—within these seemingly insignificant, unimaginable assorted items, aren't various stories 담겨 (*tamgyŏ*) contained, crafted by the devoted hands of women?
Thinking intently of her husband gone far away to work, trimming the lamp wick night after night, <279> solely weaving her lonely, anxious longing into stitch after stitch, folding and storing the *turumagi* (overcoat), trousers (*paji*), socks (*pŏsŏn*). The young wife (*siaksi*) waited and waited for news from her husband, and finally, this day arrives, bringing tears of joy. The young wife, seeking her husband who went off to earn money, packs that bundle with trembling hands and tonight, stays awake anxiously on this night train. Or perhaps she cannot sleep due to overwhelming joy. Thus, how joyful and enriched does a fragment of their livelihood become because of this one bundle? A woman's entire skill must have been poured into making just this one bundle. The husband's heart, receiving this bundle from his wife's head, must also be soaked in affection and homesickness (*hyangsu*).
Though much more cumbersome to carry than a hybrid-like trunk (*ch'urŏngk'ŭ*), the shabby, shapeless bundle feels somehow endearingly warm and pitiable. Indeed, it wouldn't suit a country grandmother to carry an expensive trunk, and how many people would find it charming if a chic Parisienne (*p'arijyannu*) carried a large, colorful bundle (*pojchim*) herself?

On the platform (*p'ŭllaet'om*), unfamiliar with bustle (*pŏnhwa*), though lacking glamorous send-offs and greetings (*songyŏngkkaek*), heart-wrenching partings happen constantly every day. The affection expressed by wiping tears with the corner of a coarse muslin (*mumyŏng*) skirt corner, thicker and slower than a silk handkerchief, and the sorrow of farewell (*chakpyŏl*) are even more poignant.
This colossal traveler (the train), repeating silent farewells with heavy breaths, and the fleeting encounters and partings inside and outside the train repeat dozens of times daily. The colossal traveler seems long immune (*myŏnyŏk*) to homesickness (*homsik*), but the small travelers within cannot forget their longing for home. <280>
"Express trains like the 'Akatsuki' or 'Tsubame' are no fun to ride. Even if it's slow like an ox, you have to take the local train (*wanhaeng*) for enjoyment. Watching the scenery of people getting on and off at country stations isn't boring."
My friend who enjoyed taking the local train, saying this, seemed to have bad taste (*akch'wimi*). But that's truly a wonderful sentiment. This is a great "poem of scenery" (*p'ungmulsi*) we can feel. However, nowadays, it's common sense for modern people that taking the express train, which dashes nonstop, is better. Pale hearts are all preoccupied with following culture.

2:00 AM
Sambanghyŏp. On the Kyŏngwŏn Line, passing through here is most pleasant. The mountain stream tumbling, gushing, foaming white, meandering down; mountains where various alpine plants tangle together, giving a deep forest (*ch'ongnim*) feel. In summer, this place would be a delight, but the charm of the winter, especially the night scenery, is regrettably lost. This place too, trying not to freeze on a cold night, sleeps quietly, quietly. Only the *thud-thud* sound of the traveler's footsteps, along with snoring, matches the breath of this gorge (*hyŏp*). The winter night train, where not a single star can be seen, feels saddening, as if only the people riding it are steadily growing older. While traveling hundreds of *ri* through the darkness, space and time change considerably. Listening to the chattering topics nearby—stories of gold mine brokers (*ppurok'ŏ*), seafood vendors, young students, miscellaneous talk (*chapt'ang yaegi*) of unidentified young men—sitting quietly alone in this atmosphere, dark shadows soon rummage through my mind and flee.
Three o'clock, four o'clock. All sorts of ugly sleeping postures (*ch'ut'ae*) unfold. I am one of them. Unable to stay awake through this long night <281>, I try closing my eyes.
On nights when sleep doesn't come, if I pick out a novel I like and lie down to read, the *ppeek ppeek* cry of the last train sounds. Listening to that sound, I think of various pleasant dreams. Sometimes I thought about catching that train and secretly going far, far away, to a place where I wouldn't meet anyone. Dreams were indeed beautiful. If dreams are realistic, there's no leeway. And they shouldn't be reality either. It's best if dreams have no conclusion and no possibility of becoming real. Here, a dream is not an ideal.
Closing my eyes and staying still, just like dreaming, if I keep running away like this, it feels like I'll suffocate soon in the atmosphere of this third-class car. That's why dreaming of blue clouds (*ch'ŏngun* - high aspirations) is for talented youths (*pangnyŏnŭi chain*); the old, with long experience, have long forgotten dreams.
As sweet dreams (fantasies) gradually fade and the bitterness of reality visits more often, I feel my mind growing within the world of reality, not just in the short time from evening to morning, but over long days. <282>

**Summary**

This collection features essays by eight educated Korean women in 1940, offering diverse perspectives on travel, society, personal life, and the arts. Pak In-dŏk recounts her journey through India, observing customs, landmarks like the Taj Mahal, and social conditions. Pak Ŭn-hye critiques gender segregation in social invitations and reflects on marital dynamics. Ch'oe Ŭi-sun shares introspective dream analyses and vignettes of street life and home. Yun Sŏng-sang contemplates the nature of an ideal couple, spousal support, and the risks of marriage. Pak Kyŏng-hŭi advises a graduating music student on navigating the nascent Korean music scene. Kim Pok-chin provides a poignant remembrance of the film director Kim Yu-yŏng. Cho Kyŏng-hŭi muses on forgetfulness and loss after her handbag is stolen. Yi Ŭn-hwi captures the atmosphere and characters encountered on a night train journey. These writings provide valuable insights into the thoughts, experiences, and concerns of Korean women during the colonial era, touching upon interactions with foreign cultures, evolving gender roles, artistic pursuits, and observations of everyday life and social strata. Historians might find the detailed account of travel in India, the commentary on social customs (like matchmaking and Christmas practices), the state of the arts (music, film), and the candid reflections on personal feelings and societal observations particularly interesting for understanding the period's social history and the perspectives of educated women.

기사제목	女流文章集
기사형태	문예기타

女流文章集
印度 旅行
朴仁德

氏는 梨花學堂 출신으로 다년간 米國 유학 지금은 농촌 사업과 저술로 지내신다.

배가 인도 서해안 뽑베이항에 거의 도착하게 되어 돈을 인도 화페로, 박꼬었다. 인도 화폐 단위는 루피라 하고 그 때 1루피는 우리의 72전 가량이었다. 1루피는 16애나스로 되는데 애나스 모양이 우리의 백통전 십전 짜리 같으나 네모가 졌다.
8월 16일 아침에 뽐뻬이 부두에 배가 도착하니 비가 막 쏟아진다. 비가 보통 비가 아니요 누가 우에서 물을 막 퍼붓는 것 같다. 몇 분 후에 비가 끗치어 상륙하였다. 로동자들이 붉은 수건을 머리에 여러번 휘휘 감고 한 끝은 뒤로 느러트리고 중국 사람의 두루매기 비슷한 것에 짧은 바지를 입고 맨발로 짐을 날르는데 웬만한 것은 머리에 인다. 얼굴 빛은 철빛 흑색이요 골격은 백인과 같다.
부두에서 기차 정거장으로 가서 아그라행 차표를 사고 짐을 역에 매끼어 보니 사람들의 옷 입은 모양이 천태만상이다. 인도 사람들은 종교와 계급을 따라 의복 모양이나 생활하는 것이 다 달르다고 한다. 종교는 인도교를 선두로 회회교, 불교 등이요 인도교에 계급이 수＜256＞ 없이 많다고 한다.
인도의 면적은 1,802,657方哩요 북으로 세계에 제일 높은 이말리야 산맥이 있고 남으로는 인도양이요 서북으로 또 산맥이 있어서 파사와 알프가니스탄을 막었고 동으로 면적이 있어 중국과 泰國을 접하였다. 이 안에서 3억2천만 명이 140만言을 쓰며 살고 깐디와 세계 문호 타고어가 그 중에 하나이 되여 살고 있다.
기차를 기다리는 동안 뽐베이에 있는 파-ㄹ시 敎人의 「寂塔」을 구경하러 갔었다. 수목이 울창한 등산 속에 탑이 있고 탑으로 둘러있는 나무에는 솔개 떼가 날어든다. 이 탑은 팔-시 교인의 매장지다. 교인이 죽으면 곳 이 탑 우에 갖다 놓는다. 팔-시교의 중이 탑 옆에 시는 교당에서 그 죽은 이의 령을 위하여 火의 神께 기도하는 동안 나무에 앉었든 솔개 떼가 있체를 다 뜨더 먹는다.
저녁 때 80만 명이나 사는 뽐베이를 뒤에 두고 아그라행 기차에 올랐다. 이 나라의 기차는 협궤도식이다. 북을 향하여 가는데 산과 들에 연연한 줄이 덮였다.
천연 우리의 봄 같은 기분이 생긴다. 인도국에도 雨期가 있어서 대개 6월 중순부터 석 달 동안인데 지방을 따라 때가 일고 느진 데가 있다. 황혼에 인도국 농부들이 장기를 들고 집으로 돌아가는 모양이 그리 튼튼해 뵈이지 않는다.
차깐에 동행하는 이들은 회회교인 모녀와 인도 남자 한 사람이다. 회회교인 모녀는 금, 은, 보석 반지와 귀의고리 팔찌를 거렀고 옷은 중국 여자의 옷 비슷한 것을 입었다. 인도교 남자는 하인과 동행하는지 차가 정거할 때마다 삼등차 칸에서 하인이 들어 와서 시중을 든다. 상류 계급의 인도인들은 여행할 때에도 하인을 데리고 다니며 음식을 따로 지어먹고 물＜257＞ 떠먹는 그릇도 가지고 다니는데 그릇 모양이 천연 놋타구 같다.
학교에 들어가니 집은 진흙으로 이 지방에서 나는 나무와 입사귀로 집웅을 이엿고 방바닥에는 무슨 자리 같은 것을 깔고 그 우에 남녀 아이들이 한 방씩 차서 배혼다. 아이들은 일제히 나리다지 하나씩 입었고, 검은 얼굴에 눈이 샛별가치 반득인다.
선생이 칠판에다가 그림같은 글자를 써놓고 가르치기에 무슨 자냐고 한즉 「산스크릿트」라 한다.
이 길로 이 아이들 사는 가정 방문을 나갔다. 아그라 시외로 한참 나가니 진흙집으로 일우워진 동리가 있다. 맛침 저녁 준비를 하는데 여아들은 폭 넓은 치마에 적삼 같은 것을 입고 귀에는 귀의고리, 발목에는 발목 거리를 걸고 어린 동생을 옆구리에 올려놓고 있다. 어머니는 막 밀가루를 반죽하여 번철 우에 놓고 소똥을 진흙 석겨 말린 것을 피어놓고 젬병처럼 붙친다. 익은 후에는 넓은 나무 입사귀에 놓고 손으로 똑똑 떼서 커리(매운 것과 채초와 끄린 것)을 찍어 먹는다.
도야지, 닭들이 동리 한가온대로 막 돌아다니고 있다.
다음날 아침에는 아그라 자랑인 타지마할 기념 전당을 보러갔다. 그것은 수 백년 전에 솨좌한 왕이 그의 가장 사랑하던 왕비가 죽으매 그를 기념하기 위하여 그 시대에 유명한 건축사들을 이태리에서 초빙하여다가 20년 동안 2만 명의 工夫와 役夫를 시켜 지은 것이라 한다. 문 앞에 당도하니 문깐 집도 대리석으로 지었고 대문 안에 들어서니 대문 앞에서 기념 전당 앞까지 못이 있고 돌아가며 열대 지방 나무를 심었다. 기념 전당은 빠이산틴式인데 대리석으로 묘하게 지였다. 집웅은 둥글고 高貴塔이 돌아가며 네 개가 있다. 전당 안에 들어서니＜258＞ 한가온대 석궤 둘이 있고 석궤를 돌아 곱게 색인 대리석 병풍이 있다. 멀리서 서대리석 색인 것을 보면 천연 망사가치 곱다.
여기서서 소리를 하면 반향 되는 것이 본 목소리보다 훨신 곱고 훌륭하다. 석궤 있는 데를 중심으로 돌아가며 방이 열어개 있다. 모두 剪嵌 細工이다. 剪嵌 細工은 돌을 골패짝처럼 깨트려서 방벽이나 천정이나 바닥이나 박갓 벽을 만드는 것인데 이 전당의 剪嵌 細工은 보석으로 하였다. 벽에다가 다홍 월게꽃을 색였으면 그 꽃빗 같은 보석으로 깨트러 만든 것이다. 이렇게 귀한 돌로 지은 집인 고로 아침 햇빗 저녁 노을 밤 달빛에 이 전당을 보면 실로 그 찬란한 빛에 정신이 황홀하여진다. 이 집 뒤로는 쩜나강이 흐른다.
아그라에서 하로 밤을 지내고 다음 날은 동양에서는 제일 먼저 창설된 여자 대학을 찾어 럭나로 향하였다. 아그라에서 몇 시간 걸리지 않는다.
이번에는 기차에서 인도교 상류 계급의 여자와 동행을 하였다. 그는 엷은 분홍 설레를 입었다. 싸레는 여자 의복에 대한 명칭인데 서양목가치 넓은 천에 다섯 야드쯤 긴 것을 가지고 반은 치마로 주름 잡어 가며 입고 반은 적삼 입은 위로 어깨와 머리를 덮는다. 보기에는 시적이나 실용적은 못 될뜻하다. 그 여자가 장식으로는 특히 금강석 한 개를 코 한 옆에 끼었다.
이 곳 대학생들은 다 싸레를 입었다. 기숙생이 많은데 식당과 주방이 인도교 학생들을 위하여 따로이 있다. 회회교 학생들과 기독교 학생들은 음식을 가리지 않고 먹기 까닭게 아무 문제가 없다. 이 날 저녁 학생들과 한 시간 담화를 하는데 조선 가치 좋온 기후를 가진 이들은 일에 자기들보다 능률을 몇 배 더 내리라고 한다.

나는 어느 날 재미있는 윳노리에 갔다가 환한 전등 불 아래서 핸드백을 잊어버렸다. 건망증과는 성질이 좀 다른 도난을 마젔다. 정거장에서 친구를 전송하고 도라서서 오는 서운하고 어찔하는 마음이다. 머리를 쩔래쩔래 흔들면서 문 밖으로 발을 옴기니 안개비 섞인 싸락눈이 볼에 차겁다. 컴컴한 거리에 가로등마저 침침하다. 뿌릇하고 묵직했던 내 겨드랑이 너무 허전하지 않으냐. 친구로 지내오는 지도 퍽 오래 되었다. 내 동생 요배가 東京으로 수학 여행 갔을 때 누나에게 선사한 것이다. 값도 헐고 질도 나즌, 보기에 아무런 매력이 없는 낡은 것이다. 그러나 낡어 금이 나고 베껴진, 그것에 정이 들었고 가만이 바라만 봐도 이야기하는 듯 은은했었다. 그리고 달칵 열면 각가지 살림사리가 보이드니.... 내 눈동자 같이 소중이 여기던 보물들, 고것들이 소근대는 비밀한 소리가 잔뜩 드렀었다. 벽에 붙은 지도 같이, 내 왼 몸둥아리를 꿍처 넣은 적은 짐이 아니었든가. 아무도 다른 사람은 여러 보지 못하였든 내 침실이 아니였든가! 내 핸드백이 투명한 유리 핸드백처럼 속이 낫낫이 드려다 보인다. 알뜰 살뜰한 것, 안타까웁도록 아깝다. 핸드빽 속에 전보 용지가 중단하는 미카열에 목소리를 들을 수 있다.
『어떠한 날이고 너의 기도를 잃지 마라, 너의 기도에 그의 일홈이 잃지 마라.』
코 속이 간지러운 향내를 갖인, 입술 연지, 그가 속사기는 음성, 「많고 많은 너의 입마침이＜276＞ 이 속에 섞여 있으리라」라고. 「있으리라!」하며.
내 그릇에 열쇠들, 銀善이가 준 콤팩트, 貞順이가 준 개, 梅里가 준 지갑, 이것을 다 어찌할까. 나는 적은 화장품을 퍽 사랑한다. 실용적 가치보다는 고것들이 갖인 맵시가 마음 든다.
나 아닌 사람이 이 소식을 드르면 심상들 하겠지, 그러나 내게는 추억의 표적이 드렀고 내 마음 조각이 드러서 그렇다.
『할머니는 어떻게 안 잊어버렸냐』
하던 미카열이 이 소식을 드르면 꾸지람하시겠지. 내 친구 한 사람은, 이 소식을 듣고 나도 잊어 버릴까바 연애 못 하겠다 하며 웃어 버린 일도 있지만...
지금에 이르러서는 다만 하나의 길 밖에는 없다. 핸드백 속에 깜직한 것들은 다 내 머리 속으로 옴겨, 秘藏하는 것이다.
낯서른 사람 손에서 구박을 받을 생각을 하면 애절타.
내 겨드랑 밑에서 자란 핸드빽을 잊고 이리도 애절프니 이 보다 큰 것, 애정이라던지를 잊게 된다면 어떠할까? 비슷한 마음일까? 생각할 수도 없는 일이다.
도난을 내 스스로 경계할 일이다. 그러나 때때로 나를 잊어버릴 수 있는 건망증을 갖인 사람, 그는 공간을 유쾌히 향락할 수도 있고 어린애들 같이 평화로울 수도 있다. 사람은 늘 마음의 여백을 갖을 줄 알어야 한다.＜277＞

벼란간 오른편 다리가 얼어붙은 鋪道 우에서 찍- 미끄러지는 바람에 어렴풋한 잠에서 소스라치며 세웠든 무릎팍이 툭 미끄러저 떠러지면서 온 몸이 균형을 잃고 앞뒤로 도리질 친다. 덜커덕 차가 서고 이어 문이 열리며 망태를 진 사나이, 함지박을 뒤집어 인 부인네, 누런 고루뎅양복, 허름한 중절모자들이 두리번두리번 거리며 들어온다. 그들의 눈에는 오직 뷘 자리다. 앉을 자리를 찾기에 온 정력이 든다. 그러다가 2인분 자리를 혼자 도맡어 가지고 누어 가는 사람이 많을 때 그들의 얼굴에는 저옥이 안심의 빛이 떠돈다.

『앙이 무시기 낳소(뭘 낳소?)』
『아들이 앵이요. 발세 둘채라우』
『앙이 여사 질겁겠소. 난 우리 아들아가 서울서 굉뷔(工夫)허능기 밥 사먹기가 그리 힘이 든댕요. 그렁이 어디 내 해 먹어 볼깐 해서 가지앵이유』
그들의 얘기 속에는 벌서 소박한 친밀이 흠뻑 떠돌고 있다. 화려하고 사치한 속에는 이런 정다움이 있기 어렵다. 남이야 알어주건 몰라주건 두 아낙네는 서로서로 자기들의 아들, 딸, 외손자에 대해 담배 연기, 입김, 콧김, 위스키 냄새들이 혼합된 속에 왼통 즐거운 자랑거리 화재를 쏟는다.
어느 듯 咸北아낙네는 보따리 속에서 껌언 맛있어 뵈이는 엿뭉치를 꺼내 한 옆을 꾹 잡고 잡어 늘궈 가지고는 배배 비틀어 떼여 내여 咸南아낙네에게 권한다. 그들의 보따리 속에는 아들을 위한 엿뭉치며 자반이며, 간장, 고초장, 이런 것들이 수북이 들었으리라. 외손자에게 가는 보따리 그 속에는 미역꼭지, 어린애의 기저귀라도 듬뿍 드렀을 듯하다. 이 외조모는 갓난 외손자의 색동저고리 어서 꾸미기를 은근이 바라고 있을 게다. 한 시간 남아 그들은 화재의 궁핍을 느끼지 않는가 부다. 여전히 악센트의 소리가 높다.
보따리, 보따리, 시골정거장에는 이 울긋불긋한 보따리가 한 異彩를 띤다. 말러 빠진 명태 몇 개, 떡 부스럭이, 옷가지들 이렇게 변변치 않은 이루 생각조차 할 수 없는 왼갓 물건들이 여인의 정성된 손끝에서 가진 각색의 說話를 담고 있는 것이 아닐까.
멀리 일터로 간 남편을 골몰히 생각하며 밤마다밤마다 등잔의 심지를 도두고 오로지＜279＞ 외롭고 안타까이 그리운 정을 한 뜸 두 뜸 떠가는 혼슬 속에 매디매디 담으며 꾸매여 포개 둔 두루메기, 바지, 버선, 시악시는 지아비한테서 소식 오기만 기다리고 기다리다 이윽고 눈물을 흘리도록 기뻐할 이날이 와, 시악시는 돈 버러간 남편을 찾어 저 보따리를 沈着을 잃은 손으로 꾸려 가지고 오늘밤은 몹시도 초조히 이 밤차 속에서 드새운다. 아니 너무나 기쁜 남어지 잠을 이룰 수 없음인지도 모른다. 이렇게 해서 그들의 살림사리의 한 조각이 이 보따리 하나로 말미암어 얼마나 즐겁고, 윤택이 나는 것일까, 오직 이 보따리 하나를 맨들기 위해서 여인의 왼 솜씨가 기우러 젔을 것이다. 이 보따리를 안해의 머리에서 받어드는 남편의 마음도 애정과 鄕愁에 흠뻑 젖으리라.
혼혈아 같은 추렁크 보담은 헙수룩하고 맵시가 없는 보따리가 가지고 다니기는 무척 꺼려하면서도 어딘지 사틋하게 정답고 측은해진다. 하긴 시골 할머니들이 값비싼 추렁크를 드는 것이 격에 맞지 않고 파리쟌누 같이 말쑥한 여인이 울긋불긋한 큰 보찜을 손소 지닌다면 그것을 귀엽게 보아줄 사람이 몇이나 될까.

繁華를 모르는 풀렛홈에는 화려한 送迎客이 없어도 날마다 날마다 애끝는 이별이 자지 않고 오직 비단 손수건 보다 정의 표정이 느리고 두터운 미명 치마꼬리로 빡빡 부벼대는 눈물의 애정과 작별의 서러움이 더욱 애처롭다.

『아까쯔끼니, 쓰바메니하는 급행은 탈 멋이 없어, 황소가치 느려도 완행을 타야만 재미있어, 시골 停車場에서 내리고 오르는 사람들의 풍경이 보기 심심치 안해』
하며 완행을 타기 즐기는 동무는 아무래도 악취미였다. 그러나 그것은 정말 멋있는 마음이다. 이런 것은 우리가 느낄 수 있는 하나의 커다란 風物詩다. 허나 요즘은 정차 없이 한 다름에 내닫는 급행을 타야 좋다는 것은 현대인의 상식이다. 창백한 마음들이 모두 문화를 따르기에 여념이 없다.

오전 2시
三防峽, 京元線에선 이 곳을 지날 때가 가장 유쾌하다. 돌돌 굴르고 샘이 솟아오르며 힌 거픔을 담뿍 내뿜고 구비구비 흘러내리는 이 山峽의 물줄기, 各樣各色의 고산식물이 한데 엉켜 깊은 叢林의 느낌이 있는 산, 여름이면 이 곳이 즐거움이렸만 겨을 더욱이 밤 풍경의 운치는 야속스러히 모두 일었다. 이 곳도 치운 밤 얼지 않으려 고요히 고요히 잠드러 오직 덜커덩 덜커덩 나그네의 발자욱소리만이 코 고는 소리와 아울러 이 峽의 호흡을 마처준다. 별 하나 볼 수 없는 겨을의 밤열차는 타고 있는 사람만이 자꾸자꾸 늙어 가는 듯하여 서글프다. 몇 백리를 어둠 속을 달리는 동안 공간과 시간이 무척 달러지고 옆에서 지꺼리는 화제, 금광 뿌로커들의 얘기, 해산물장사의 얘기, 어린 학생들의 얘기, 正體없는 청년들의 잡탕얘기, 요런 분위기 속에서 가만이 혼자 도사리고 앉었으면 어느 듯 시커먼 그림자들이 내 머리 속을 삿삿치 뒤지고 다라난다.
세 시, 네 시, 온갓 잠의 醜態가 벌려진다. 그 중에 나도 하나 이 긴 밤을 그냥 다 새울＜281＞ 수가 없어 눈을 감어본다.
잠이 오지 않는 밤은 맘에 드는 소설이라고 골라 읽고 누었을 라면 삑삑 막차의 우짖는 소리 그 소리를 드르며 가지가지의 재미있을 꿈을 생각해 본다. 어떤 때는 저 차를 잡어 타고 멀리멀리 저 멀리 아무도 만나지 않을 곳에 혼자 살작 가 버릴가도 생각해 보았다. 꿈은 역시 아름다운 것이었다. 꿈이 현실적이여서는 여유가 없다. 또 현실이여서도 안된다. 꿈은 결말이 없고 현실이 될 가능성이 없어야 좋다. 여기에 꿈은 이상이 아니다.
눈을 감고 가만이 있으니 이대로 이대로 꿈꾼 것과 마찬가지로 자꾸자꾸 다라난다면 얼마 안가 질식해 버리고 말 것 같은 이 三等車 내의 분위기. 그러기에 靑雲의 꿈을 꾸는 것은 芳年의 才人들, 오랜 경험을 갖인 늙은이는 꿈을 잊은 지 오래다.
점점 달가운 꿈(공상)이 희미해지고 현실의 쓰라림이 찾어 옴이 잦을 때 저녁이 아침 되는 그런 짧은 시간이 아니라 긴 날 동안을 두고 점점 현실의 세계에서 마음이 자라감을 느낀다.＜282＞
