Fishing Boom, Mining Boom

10 Million Wŏn Hamgyŏng North Sardine Boom
Who are the Millionaires Worth Hundreds of Thousands?

Since 80% of the total Korean sardine (鰮魚, *iwasi*, *chŏngŏri*) harvest is produced in the Hamgyŏng North region near the Maritime Province (沿海洲), it is truly a great source of wealth that holds sway over the lifeline of Korean fisheries. Thus, following the tens of millions generated by the Rajin (羅津) land boom, the biggest economic boom is now concentrated in this sardine fishery, and within the last 3-4 years, several sardine tycoons <17> have emerged.
The boats used to catch these sardines are called *kinchakuami-sen* (巾着船, purse seiners), and this is a licensed fishery. There are about 110 purse seiner operators licensed by the Government-General of Korea, about half of whom are Koreans.
According to recent newspaper reports, sardines start being caught in mid-September and the fishing continues until the end of November. Nowadays, a successful boat catches 2,000 *taru* (樽, barrels/casks) daily, while even less successful boats catch 700-800 *taru*, with an average of 1,200-1,300 *taru* being caught per day. The total quantity caught up to this date is already over 2.2 million *taru*. While the price per *taru* was over 3 wŏn last year, this year it is about 2 wŏn, meaning the value has already reached 4.4-4.5 million wŏn. Since the fishing is still ongoing, the total catch for this year is unknown, but in any case, as autumn turns to winter, the sardine boom in the Hamgyŏng North region shows an astonishingly rare vitality. Wŏn Yun(-su) Buys Up Land
Outside Chahamun Gate

For some time now, the mining tycoon Mr. Wŏn Yun-su (元胤洙) has been secretly buying up a large amount of land in the area near Segŏmjŏng (洗劒亭) outside Chahamun Gate (紫霞門) in the outskirts of Seoul, which has become a topic of conversation. Why is Mr. Wŏn buying land outside Chahamun Gate, which is said to be usable only for orchards or stone quarries? There is indeed a tear-jerking reason behind this. Caring for his aged mother, he would sometimes buy pears or melons, carry them himself on an A-frame carrier (지게) over the distant mountain path, sweating, go to Namdaemun Market in downtown Seoul to sell them, earning sometimes a few tens of *chŏn*, sometimes a few *wŏn*, barely making a living as a poor man. During that time, he must have experienced contempt from the local people more than once or twice. Later, Mr. Wŏn hit it big with the Paengnyŏn gold mine in Hwanghae Province (黃海道의 백년 금광) and instantly became a millionaire. Then, he started buying up the land around the place outside Chahamun Gate where he used to live, as if to say "Look at me now!" to the local people from whom he had endured contempt and scorn for 10 years, seeking to avenge the past humiliation (雪恥). Rumor has it that he is doing all this out of his deep filial piety for his mother, considering her feelings in her old age. <20>

**Summary**

This 1938 article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine provides a glimpse into various aspects of Korean society under Japanese rule. It begins by surveying the monthly salaries of prominent Korean business leaders, newspaper presidents, and school principals, revealing a significant income gap between business elites and those in media or education. The article then covers news snippets including paper rationing affecting newspapers, personnel changes in major companies and organizations, economic booms in sardine fishing and gold mining (listing successful entrepreneurs and notable figures involved), rising gold prices, celebrity news concerning film stars' low pay compared to Japanese counterparts, a popular singer's career earnings, and gossip about female writers. It also touches upon personal stories like a mining tycoon buying land to overcome past humiliations and another building a private air-raid shelter.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for its documentation of the Korean elite's economic activities and relative compensation levels within the colonial structure. It highlights the burgeoning (and sometimes volatile) capitalist enterprises like mining and fishing, heavily influenced by Japanese economic interests and wartime conditions (e.g., resource conservation, gold accumulation). The details on specific individuals, companies (Daedong, Hwasin, Chosŏn Ilbo, Tonga Ilbo), cultural figures (Mun Ye-bong, Ch'ae Kyu-yŏp, Chang Hyŏk-chu), and social trends (private wealth, celebrity culture, women writers' lives) offer valuable social history insights into urban Korean life during a period of increasing militarization and state control. The mention of former independence/social activists turning to business (mining) is also noteworthy.

李善熙 氏의 東洋美의 긴치마

氏는 신문사에서 나오시면 곳장 검정 치마를 길게 내려 입는 것이 버릇같이 되여있다. 그것이 또 氏에겐 어울닌다. 어느 날 저녁에도 낮에 지글지글 끓튼 종로 네거리에 바람이 시원해지자 그 예의 검정 치마를 길게 내려 입은 氏는 아들을 앞세우고 거리에 나섰다.
그런데 검정 치마를 입으시고 아들을 앞세우고 거리에 나선데 까지는 시비할 일이 아니겠지만 아들을 앞세우고 나선 氏의 표정은 혼자거나 혹은 동무들과 함께 거리를 거를 때와는 분명히 달느다는 것이다. 혹 그 귀여운 아드님의 것는 양에 입이 귀밑으로 도라가고 길가고 오는 사람을 다 못 보는 한이 있다 치드래도 그것쯤으론 누가 氏를 붓잡고 시비를 걸리 없겠지만 모자까지 버서서 예를 표하는 신사의 인사까지도 모르고 지나첫다니 氏도 역시 張德祚 氏와 같은 「나는 확실히 변태적 모성애야」하는 말을 연발하실지 모르지.
