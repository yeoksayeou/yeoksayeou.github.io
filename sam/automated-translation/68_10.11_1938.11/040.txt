"Border Site" Round-table Talk (Korean Army Command Officers and Government-General Officials Gather), The Changkufeng Storm and Subsequent Situation

Just as world opinion seemed to attest that a Japan-Soviet war would occur, a clash between our forces eventually took place at Changkufeng (張鼓峰), resulting in over 5,000 casualties. Now, what is the true nature of the Soviet forces sending illegal flames of war towards our nation, which is on the path of air defense and holy war? This is a precious historical record from the site, where military and Government-General officials gathered <30> locally.

Korean Army Command            Komatsu Mitsuo (小松三雄)     Lieutenant Colonel
Same                       Tsuchiya Sakae (土屋榮)       Lieutenant Colonel

Korean Army Information Dept.   Akutagawa Hiroshi (芥川浩)       Major
Same                       Tominaga Kametarō (富永龜太郞)   Captain
Korean Gendarmerie              Nagata Katsuyuki (永田勝之)     Assistant Captain (?)

Government-General of Korea Security Section Chief    Shimomura Susumu (下村進)氏
Foreign Ministry Secretary            Yoshimitsu Sanjirō (吉滿三次郞)氏 <31>

This Battle and the Border Issue

Reporter = Thank you for coming like this amidst your busy duties in the field. This incident was a serious issue in many ways. First, could you please tell us about the course of the battle?

OOO Commander Tanaka (Major) = I just arrived suddenly from the site today, but I originally took up my post the year before last. Soon after my arrival, the Yanggwanp'yŏng (洋舘坪) incident occurred. As you know, unless the border line is clearly determined, border disputes will never cease, so firstly, unless the border line was indicated urgently, I was in a difficult position to carry out my duties. Regarding the Yanggwanp'yŏng incident, according to the instructions received from the center at that time, the determination of the border line would be decided through diplomatic negotiations, so it couldn't be decided immediately. To avoid various border dispute incidents in carrying out duties, the front-line troops were told not to cross the Soviet border line of the Hunch'un Boundary Agreement (琿春 界約). Up to that point, we were told to passively observe Russia's actions. Regarding actions beyond the border line claimed by the Soviet side, we were told to act after obtaining approval from the center. However, the Soviet Union constantly dispatched patrols near Changkufeng and acted, and although we were all indignant, we were told by our superiors to tolerate it to avoid border disputes, so we left it as it was. But this time, the Soviet Union again built positions on the summit of Changkufeng, surrounded them with barbed wire, increased troop strength, brought in artillery, and clearly showed an attitude of permanent occupation. So, we thought this illegal action - this insolent attitude of the Soviet Union trying to take an important line by force as if looking down on Japan's weakness today while our country is engaged in the Japan-China Incident behind the scenes - was improper.
Regarding this matter, I have spoken to foreign newspaper reporters before; the fact that the Soviet side does not respond to diplomatic consultations and quietly brings out troops without warning to build positions is truly unacceptable. Furthermore, their attitude of not responding to Japan's peace proposals, only making unilateral arguments, and moreover, trying to pressure Japan with troops unexpectedly, especially their non-samurai (非 武士道) attitude of attacking while knowing Japan is currently in the midst of the Japan-China Incident battle, <32> this is something we cannot tolerate. Therefore, if Japan were not currently in the midst of the incident, it would have surely adopted a much more peaceful attitude, but regarding this Soviet attitude of exploiting Japan's weakness and attacking, Japan's martial way (武道) absolutely does not permit it, I explained to the foreign newspaper reporters, and the reporters seemed to understand our feelings well, saying they understood why the Japanese army was putting so much effort into the issue of a single hill.

The Course of Events Leading Up to the Counterattack

Major Akutagawa (Korean Army Information Unit) = It was July 29th, the first clash... The first border crossing before that was July 11th, right? From then until the 29th when the skirmish broke out - Major Tanaka, please briefly describe the situation on the Soviet side at the site.
Major Tanaka (OOO Commander) = The initial trigger was the report on July 11th that a few Soviet soldiers suddenly appeared at Changkufeng and started construction work in the vicinity. So, as I mentioned earlier, we were ordered not to touch anything up to the Hunch'un Boundary Agreement line. Therefore, if the Soviets entered from that line, it would naturally be a border violation, and although we thought we could strike them down without issue, we had already received cautions from superiors, so we initially just reported it.
Reporter = On July 16th, Gendarmerie Corporal Matsushima's...
Major Tanaka = Gendarmerie Corporal Matsushima (松島 헌병 伍長) went to the site on the 16th with the mission to monitor Soviet actions and scout the actual situation, aiming to confirm the state of the Soviet positions. However, south of Changkufeng, on the east side of Nojeong (盧頂?), he was shot by Soviet soldiers occupying the position and died in action. The corporal's body was taken by Soviet soldiers into their territory immediately after. Even before this, they had an attitude of shooting if residents went to the area, which led to the Yanggwanp'yŏng incident the year before last.
Captain Tominaga (Korean Gendarmerie) = I saw it for the first time when I went to the site, but there's a good road leading to Changkufeng, an excellent road from Sach'o-bong (沙草峰) to Changkufeng suitable even for freely moving artillery. It looks like an expression of aggressive, planned action.
Reporter = After the Soviet troops occupied Changkufeng on the 11th, during the period until the counterattack on the 29th, did our side continuously <33> maintain a policy of recapture?
Major Tanaka = Not quite.
Lieutenant Colonel Komatsu (Korean Army Command) = Anyway, initially, there was an intention to leave the matter unquestioned. However, if left alone, it would mean acknowledging the Hunch'un Boundary Agreement, and moreover, there was a need to issue a stern protest against this attitude of exploiting our weakness. If they still didn't listen, there was an intention that it would be acceptable to strike a blow and recapture it. However, it seems the policy was generally not to cause trouble over such matters, aiming to urge the Soviet side to reconsider from a broader perspective. On July 24th, an order came to withdraw the front line. It was decided to leave a part of the front line behind and wait for the outcome of diplomatic negotiations. This was done thinking of adhering to Japan's fair and just policy of seeking a peaceful resolution, withdrawing the main force while leaving a part behind. However, on the 29th, some Soviet troops advanced again and occupied a position. Even assuming the Hunch'un Boundary Agreement line was on the ridge crest, this point was clearly within Manchurian (滿洲) territory on our side, so the Soviet attitude was truly outrageous. At this point, tolerance and endurance reached their limit, so we counterattacked. That was July 29th.

Soviet Troops Continuously Gather at Changkufeng

Lieutenant Colonel Komatsu (Korean Army Command) = Our side, which counterattacked, had no tanks, of course, and very few troops, about one platoon. So we repelled them, but thinking that staying in that position would cause trouble, we withdrew from there. That was the night of the 30th. Gradually, the roar of vehicles and tanks could be heard from Sach'o-bong, and it sounded like troop strength was increasing. Furthermore, there were signs that some tanks were moving towards Changkufeng. Around that time, scouts reported that the Soviets had launched an offensive.
Major Tanaka = It was the night of the 31st.
Major Akutagawa = It continued from the night of the 30th until the morning of the 31st. The enemy's shelling, despite being at night, landed precisely on our positions. It hit very well. This couldn't have hit so well unless it was planned beforehand. So, this <34> frontal unit of ours also went on the offensive in response, and the two armies clashed right in front of the Soviet positions, capturing that line by dawn. When we occupied the Soviet positions at dawn on the 31st, most of the nearby Soviet troops had retreated to the line of the Hwadagi River (후아다기 川?).
However, on August 1st, the Soviet troops that had temporarily retreated began to move south again. From August 1st to 2nd, roughly one and a half rifle divisions, initially 70 tanks on the first day, then an increase of 70 tanks the next day, totaling about 180-200 tanks by the 4th. They started their attack from the 2nd. That is, on the 2nd, they began attacking the Changkufeng position with a portion of their forces. By around the 4th, their strength was roughly 180 to 200 tanks, two rifle divisions, and it seemed there was one cavalry division. Also, from the afternoon of the 2nd, enemy aircraft began operations, with generally 2, sometimes 3, occasionally 4 flights of planes circling the Changkufeng vicinity, the Hong'ŭi (洪儀) vicinity, and also coming around Kyŏnghŭng (慶興) from the Yŏnch'u (煙秋) direction. [Text garbled - possibly referring back to Matsushima] ...until the night of the 2nd ****** South of Changkufeng****** they attacked. So **** following the attack, they conducted some night raids.

Fierce Battle on the Front Line

Major Akutagawa (Korean Army) = For this reason, after Lieutenant Colonel Komatsu explained the course of the incident, I would like the two officers who were on the front line (Major Tanaka, Captain Tominaga) to share their impressions of the battle between our forces and theirs.
Lieutenant Colonel Komatsu (OO Commander?) = After the night raid on the night of the 2nd, aircraft started flying actively from the morning of the 3rd. As the enemy seemed likely to attack persistently, the Odaka (尾高) unit also increased its forces, and continuous battles took place there from the 4th, 5th, 6th, to the 7th. Until around the 4th, the Soviet attack wasn't that fierce, but from the 6th, both air and ground forces displayed extraordinary power, striving to recapture Changkufeng from the north and south. Thus, they repeated attacks day and night without pause. However, despite doing so, they were ultimately unable to reach the positions, leading to the ceasefire agreement on the 11th. At that time, the Soviet forces had advanced to a line about 70 meters before our positions <35> when the ceasefire occurred. That's the general course of events.

Marshal Blücher Appears on the Front Line

Major Tanaka = It became clear later through investigation, but until the 6th, there was one rifle division and one mechanized brigade. It seems reinforcements arrived on the 6th, as the attack on the 6th was particularly fierce. It appears Blücher (뿌류헬 / 부르헬) (Reporter's Note - Far Eastern Army Commander) came and commanded at that time. It was the morning of the 6th when the front-line units received the attack order from Blücher. The attack order at that time was:
"Far Eastern Military District Commander's Order - Both divisions shall break through the border line and conduct an enveloping attack from both flanks."
According to the accounts of the commanders of our front-line units OO and OO at the time, looking from the battlefield, a special headquarters seemed to be set up in the Hyangsan-dong (香山洞) area.
Looking closely, a imposing commander was seen spreading out a map and directing, and they thought that might have been Blücher. If the order was from the Far Eastern Military District Commander, it was Blücher's order, and indeed, a full-scale attack began on the 6th. However, before the ceasefire agreement was reached, their forces were depleted, and their offensive capability vanished. The enemy facing Changkufeng just before the ceasefire agreement was the third wave of reinforcement divisions.
In summary, by the 6th, four divisions had attacked, and another division was brought in, which joined the battle on the 6th. However, since both divisions had exhausted their strength by the 9th, they brought in yet another new division to attack the Changkufeng front.
Captain Tominaga = The border commander came out around the 4th. We heard it from our commander's intelligence.
Major Tanaka = That attack commander, surrounded by his staff, looked imposing, like he was overseeing a large-scale exercise with a map spread out.

How Was the Red Army's Combat Performance?

Major Akutagawa = On the 6th, there was intense activity with aircraft, bombing, shelling, and tanks appeared. I was also at the site and felt it was terrifying, but Captain Tominaga, who was inside the trench <36> on the front line, how was the Soviet combat performance?
Captain Tominaga = Generally, the enemy's attack tactics were not the slightest bit different from what we had often heard from superiors or studied in books.
First, the airplanes rush in. After the air raid, tanks come, front-line infantry advances, and concentrated artillery fire arrives. The air raids were particularly notable. Initially, one or two planes came for reconnaissance, then fighter planes followed, and then bombers came. This air attack, perhaps because they feared our ground fire, I don't know, was at an extremely high altitude. At least 1,500 meters or more. While the psychological effect was considerable, excelling in the so-called intimidation effect, the actual effect was extremely minimal. Our unit's losses due to air bombing were truly only a few men. Although they dropped so many bombs, perhaps due to the sturdy positions we had built, anyway, the Soviet air bombing performance was about this level. Then there was the tank attack. Enemy tanks also rushed towards my Sach'o-bong front, but they were often destroyed before reaching the position's barbed wire. Seeing the tanks in front being destroyed, the tanks following behind got scared and ran away. There were two or three that bravely charged, but those too were all destroyed by our close-quarters attacks. Of course, they might not have been well-trained, but on the other hand, the terrain was also unsuitable for tank use. The vicinity was marshy grassland, making it difficult for tanks to advance offensively, and they were destroyed while lingering there. Not a single one entered our line of positions. Two or three crossed the barbed wire, but they were all captured. We gained considerable confidence in anti-tank combat.
One thing to note here is that the tanks in this instance were in a place where movement was not free. If we had encountered the enemy in a fine, flat area where their movement was unrestricted, the tanks, displaying their high-speed performance, would likely have achieved considerable effect. In short, the reasons for achieving such results in anti-tank combat were this, and another thing was that the tanks lacked the will to fight to the end. There were instances where crews opened the hatches and fled midway.
This actually happened. <37> Then there's the infantry attack, which also showed aspects different from our conception. When we receive an attack order, every single soldier charges the enemy position; we have an attack spirit where we must charge.
While the Changkufeng front was fierce daily, on the Sach'o-bong front, I felt the infantry attack wasn't that significant.
Next is the shelling, which came very heavily. However, despite using so many shells, they didn't achieve significant effects. Overall, the coordination between aircraft, artillery, tanks, and front-line infantry skirmishers was insufficient, which is why we were spared a difficult fight.
Reporter = What do you think about the point that the Soviet Red Army's training was not good?
Captain Tominaga = I think the soldiers' training wasn't that bad, but there seemed to be something very lacking in terms of command.
Lieutenant Colonel Tsuchiya (Korean Army Command) = While this incident has, of course, provided us with much valuable data regarding combat against the Soviets, and as a result, not only the officers and soldiers who experienced it on the battlefield, but also all of us involved in the incident from beginning to end have firmly gained confidence in victory.
However, on the other hand, we keenly felt that there are aspects of the Soviet material power and technical training, which we tended to underestimate easily in the past, that should not be slighted. To give one example, although the Soviet tanks, boasting such elite status, were toyed with like sea turtles being dragged out before the brave Japanese soldiers, we must not forget the special conditions added, such as the terrain near Changkufeng being narrow, uneven, and the soil being soft sand (粕砂?), limiting tank speed to 4-5 km/h and restricting the parallel advance of numerous tanks.
As for the Soviet artillery, although its accuracy was very poor initially, by the end of the battle, it came to display effectiveness that certainly couldn't be slighted, and our losses due to this became predominant. The coordination between ground troops, air units, and attack units was generally clumsy, but the Soviet battle for Hill 52 on August 7th saw all three perfectly aligned, causing our defending troops extreme hardship. Therefore, it is extremely dangerous to evaluate their total strength based on instances of clumsy Soviet combat actions in past battles. <38>
Their machine gun firing technique was also extremely skilled; if a Japanese soldier poked his head even slightly out of the trench, he was immediately sniped, resulting in very many fatalities from head wounds. Although the assault troops lacked the power to charge with bayonets, their close combat using hand grenades was skillful, and our losses from this were by no means small. As for the construction of the communication network, both their technology and materials were far superior to the Japanese army's. If our soldiers were carelessly exposed near the trenches, within 2-3 minutes of being spotted by an observation post, they would receive concentrated artillery fire due to such swift communication.

Blücher's Fighting Spirit and the Red Army

Reporter = Did Blücher have an aggressive fighting spirit from the beginning?
Major Tanaka = He must have had it, which is why he kept attacking with such forces.
Reporter = Was it largely Stalin's policy in the Soviet center to avoid escalating the dispute as much as possible?
Lieutenant Colonel Komatsu = Formally, it seems so.
Major Akutagawa = On the 6th, I clearly saw the air raids and bombing at Chŭngsan (甑山) and Hong'ŭi. My feeling at that time was truly hazy, unable to see ahead clearly. What was the real feeling on the front line?
Captain Tominaga = At first, I could see, but later, I couldn't see at all. Because of the dust and smoke, completely...
Major Akutagawa = Were you in the trench at that time?
Captain Tominaga = It was so hazy, I couldn't see anything. We were in the trenches; the commanders must have had a hard time then too. The closest one fell 30 meters from my position, three rounds fell 60-70 meters away, my position was on the ridge, but two rounds fell below the ridge, not reaching my position.
Major Akutagawa = The enemy's assault was most intense towards Changkufeng Hill 52. It seems the enemy unit that came to this area had good quality or there were some special conditions.
Major Tanaka = The enemy on the Hill 52 front was the unit responsible for this incident, whereas the enemy on the Sach'o-bong <39> front was not. Therefore, the latter lacked the conviction that they absolutely had to take Sach'o-bong, while the enemy on the Hill 52 front, being responsible, likely had a considerable will to take it at all costs. Anyway, the Soviet Army seems to be held accountable for matters they are responsible for, but seem indifferent about matters outside their responsibility.

Did the Soviet Union Plan a Full-Scale Japan-Soviet War?

Mr. Takai (Military Non-Regular Staff) = They reported to the center from the field that they had repelled the Japanese army, which is why they tried to capture Changkufeng at all costs.
Captain Tominaga = They made a false report, it seems.
Mr. Takai = It was even reported by TASS (타스(通信)).
Mr. Yoshimitsu (Government-General of Korea Foreign Affairs Secretary) = So, the thinking in the Soviet central leadership seemed to be that they had to capture Changkufeng at least for the sake of face. If they took that, they thought it would be fine to enter ceasefire negotiations.
Captain Tominaga = From what I saw, they planted a red flag on the slope of Changkufeng, and at the same time, they must have telegraphed that they had recaptured Changkufeng. From our side, it looked like they had taken a corner of the mountain, but from the Soviet side, it must have looked like they had taken the summit. So, from the Soviet perspective, since the red flag was up, they probably thought they would proceed with ceasefire negotiations without giving the Japanese army a chance to counterattack.
Mr. Takai = They say they advanced several meters after the ceasefire agreement?
Major Tanaka (OOO Commander) = They talked as if they had taken Changkufeng, but that was a lie. They were deployed 70 meters away, so they came forward making various excuses, and facing each other across the mountain, 5 meters apart, they were digging trenches. Litvinov (리프비노후) (Reporter's Note - Foreign Minister) must have believed they had captured Changkufeng. <40> That's why when entering diplomatic negotiations with Ambassador Shigemitsu (重光 大使), Litvinov suggested settling based on the lines at midnight on the 11th.
Reporter = Lieutenant Colonel Tsuchiya - what do you think about the Soviet intentions?
Lieutenant Colonel Tsuchiya (Korean Army Command) = That the Soviet Union was not planning a full-scale Japan-Soviet war is already clearly proven by the outcome, and we thought so from the beginning. This can be judged from the internal situation of the Soviet Union. The situation at the outbreak of the incident, their use of forces, especially concentrating a large force exceeding a quarter of the Maritime Province Army Group and half of their prized mechanized corps in such an insignificant local area, and limiting aircraft actions to the local area, clearly indicate they were not planning a full-scale war. The second reason is that in the Soviet Army, independent action by front-line troops is absolutely forbidden. Even someone like Blücher could absolutely not provoke hostilities against Stalin's will or without any communication with him. Furthermore, for the Soviet Army, which possesses a national character of quickly falling into passivity once struck, despite pouncing on others' weaknesses, it is extremely difficult for them to maintain the kind of defiant spirit we might imagine.

How to Thoroughly Instill Understanding of the Soviets

Reporter = Is there a possibility that a situation like this time will arise again near Changkufeng? What do you think?
Lieutenant Colonel Tsuchiya = That is a very difficult question, and I cannot give a definitive answer, but I believe a large-scale dispute like this time will not occur for a while. The reasons are: first, this incident was not based on a planned Soviet initiative. Consequently, the Soviet Union will not likely seek opportunities to repeat such an attempt in the future. Third, it is absolutely unthinkable that Soviet soldiers on the ground would take retaliatory actions on their own.
Regarding the first reason, if the Soviet Union intended to check Japan by encroaching near the northern border of Korea (朝鮮), they would have prepared more thoroughly and tried to prolong the dispute. When news of Soviet troops advancing near Changkufeng <41> first arrived and a portion of the Japanese army concentrated near the border, the Soviet troops consisted merely of a small border guard force. Afterwards, they increased their forces daily from Vladivostok (海蔘威) and the Slavyanka (스라우양카?) area, concentrating about one division's strength near Hyangsan-dong only after 10 days. Also, the Soviet forces defeated in the battle of July 30th, fearing that the Japanese army would advance towards the Yŏnch'u area in one stroke, hastily withdrew forces from the Hyangsan-dong area and the Changnyŏngja (長嶺子) area east of Hunch'un to the area west and southwest of Yŏnch'u, adopting a defensive posture centered on Yŏnch'u.
However, on the 31st, perceiving that the Japanese army had no offensive intentions, the Soviet forces advanced troops towards the border area again on August 1st (misprint for September 1st?) and subsequently moved large numbers of troops south to the Hyangsan-dong area. If the Japanese army had launched an offensive towards the "Hanshi" (한시 - ?) area at this point, the numerous Soviet troops would have been like rats in a bag. Sensing this danger, the Soviet forces, from around August 5th or 6th, concentrated some troops near the high ground south of "Hanshi," facing south and west, and occupied positions. Thus, the Soviet actions were consistently passive, with nothing recognizable as planned operational conduct. Furthermore, their agreeing to ceasefire negotiations after only a dozen days of fighting and subsequently adhering faithfully to the agreed line, etc., shows not even a hint of intent to check Japan.
Major Akutagawa = Regarding the trends among civilians or in the region?
Captain Nagata (Korean Gendarmerie) = According to the materials gathered by us, the future of the incident was expected to end as a local border dispute. It wouldn't escalate. The observation was that it wouldn't become a full-scale Japan-Soviet war. The reaction in the homeland (内地 - Japan proper) was felt a bit more acutely.
Therefore, while Keijō (京城 - Seoul) had its neon signs lit, they were turned off outdoors in the homeland. There were many Koreans in Osaka (大阪), and many inquiries came from Osaka to where we are. Asking about the air defense situation, while Keijō's neon signs were brightly lit in the sky, people in the homeland seemed to think the matter was bigger.
Major Akutagawa = Mr. Shimomura, some seemed to question the fact that the Japanese side proposed the ceasefire agreement. What are your thoughts?
Mr. Shimomura (Government-General Security Section Chief) = There are some. However, generally, regarding the Korean public sentiment towards this incident, for the first 2-3 days after the incident began on the 29th, there were no reports whatsoever about public sentiment trends. Although the restriction on newspaper publications <42> might have been a reason, there were no reports on public sentiment trends. We thought it strange, but it didn't seem like the police were just standing idly by; that is, apart from the immediate area, it didn't seem to be treated as much of an issue. Since such a major incident had started, it seemed likely the general public would know, but the Korean public sentiment was calm to an extent that we found surprising. We felt very reassured. However, as you know, the other side was bombing indiscriminately, so questions began to arise, not only locally but also among the general public, as to why aircraft weren't being dispatched from our side. Consequently, some wondered if, despite boasting about the "Sea Eagle" (海의 鷲) or "Land's *" (陸의 *) in China (支那), perhaps they couldn't challenge the Soviet Union. To put it more extremely, weren't there no planes here because they had all gone to China? If they really had the strength to take on the Soviet Union, proposing a ceasefire agreement was wrong. Some viewed it this way. The future handling of those who see it this way ultimately depends on Japan's policy. To explain this well, I think it's necessary to thoroughly instill and enlighten them that we can crush the Soviet Union with a single blow.
Captain Nagata (Korean Gendarmerie) = Why did we exercise forbearance? It's necessary to clarify that. No, it's necessary to elucidate and make it widely known.
Major Akutagawa = Being at the site, I also thought such misunderstandings weren't entirely unreasonable. The enemy, valuing face, persisted in attacking Changkufeng relentlessly. Thus, the attacks on the 6th and 7th were the fiercest, and even afterwards, they were intense around Changkufeng and Hill 52. However, certainly from around the 9th or 10th, aircraft activity slowed down, and shelling also decreased. This was because fresh, elite Imperial forces arrived in the area, leading them to give up, thinking, "It's over now." Also, as mentioned earlier, the units that attacked previously retreated, and new divisions were taking their place. Simultaneously, it became known that Japan had made a grave decision. That's why the enemy bowed their heads. If we had hastily proposed a ceasefire through diplomatic negotiations at that moment, such doubts might have arisen, but that wasn't the case. The diplomatic negotiation initially was to restore the situation to before the 12th (of July). It's necessary to properly recognize that our country consistently maintained its policy of non-escalation and love for peace. <43>

Why Did the Imperial Army Exercise Forbearance and Self-Restraint?

Major Tanaka = I gave news to foreign newspaper reporters, and the things they wanted to hear were, "For what reason is Japan fighting in this local area at this critical time while engaged in the Japan-China Incident? We want to know the true intention," and secondly,
"Why does Japan insist on a non-escalation policy?" Thirdly, they were probing whether Japan had weakened due to the China Incident and lacked strength. In conclusion, regardless of the various causes, they wanted to know what the future course of the incident would be. Would it end as a local issue, or would it develop into a Japan-Soviet war? They wanted to decide the stance of the various powers based on this. These were their questions. So, first, I explained why Japan had to fight, the circumstances that necessitated provoking that issue, and then they wanted to hear why Japan, despite the Soviet Union's illegal bombing, didn't dispatch fighter planes and maintained such an attitude throughout.
Regarding this, I explained that Japan, in essence, fights risking its life for matters concerning its own survival, no matter the difficulties. However, it is by no means a belligerent nation. This is proven by the Sino-Japanese War, Russo-Japanese War, Manchurian Incident, this Japan-China Incident, and the Changkufeng Incident; Japan acts according to this policy in all cases. Therefore, this Changkufeng issue is not critical enough to constrain Japan's mission...
However, this issue cannot be ignored. If the Soviet Union shows peaceful sincerity, Japan is prepared and resolved to cease fire at any time. It's certainly not that the Japanese army cannot fight a war against the Soviet Union.
Now, if Japan were to engage in a full-scale war with the Soviet Union,
there is a risk of it becoming a world war. Japan has no intention of turning the Changkufeng Incident
into a world war. Explaining this way, they all understood well.
It took half a day to tell this story. Next, they asked frankly, "We understand that well, but isn't it that Japan cannot fight <44> because it's tied up in the China Incident? Isn't that why you're giving such reasons?"
To this, I replied,
"Absolutely not. That is, when Japan made the grave decision regarding the North China (北支) Incident around this time last year, the possibility that the Soviet Union would take advantage of this timing to attack Japan was something even any layman would consider. Furthermore, considering the past Soviet attitude and the Soviet-China relations at the time, we thought they might seize this opportunity to attack Japan.
Since today's Soviet actions were anticipated a year ago, it's unlikely Japan would have made such a grave decision without sufficient preparation and resolve.
The Kwantung Army in Manchukuo (滿洲國) sent a part of its forces to North China at the time of the incident, but since January this year, every single soldier has been withdrawn and is waiting here. They are prepared for such Soviet actions. Also, in the homeland, young reservists are prepared and waiting, ready to counterattack Soviet actions at any time. Next, regarding the current fuss about staple fiber or economic issues in Japan, it's a mistake to think Japan is in economic difficulty because of the long-term war. It's impossible to weaken after just about a year of war.
This is preparation for when the Soviet Union steps in. Already, through domestic efforts over the past year, all factories are on a wartime footing. They can exert industrial capacity at a steep angle. Shells, artillery – production will increase much more from now on. Therefore, viewing Japan as lacking the capability to fight the Soviet Union from that perspective is a misconception. Japan simply does not enjoy war," I explained this way.

Possibility of a Full-Scale Japan-Soviet War

Major Tanaka = Next, regarding the future course, I said the future means war. A full-scale Japan-Soviet war will occur. Why? Because currently, Japan is exercising forbearance and self-restraint, but if the Soviet Union, thinking Japan cannot fight because it's engaged with China, continues to challenge in the Changkufeng area, <45> then it's no longer just a Changkufeng issue. Even if Japan endures, if they do not reflect, the possibility of a Japan-Soviet war beyond the Changkufeng issue exists. The problem depends on whether Japan can endure this situation. Therefore, if the Soviet Union reflects and withdraws its guns, the war will end here; otherwise, a full-scale Japan-Soviet war will surely break out. When I explained this, they seemed to understand well.
Mr. Shimomura (Government-General Security Section Chief) = This explanation needs to be thoroughly conveyed to the Korean people.
Major Tanaka = Finally, as a conclusion, I thought a misunderstanding might arise: why did the Changkufeng incident happen? Many casualties resulted from it. Weren't the casualties ultimately meaningless? In this battle, the upright discipline of the Japanese army was revealed to the world, and its outstanding capability was made clear to the world. This is a great achievement of the Odaka unit. It's an achievement worthy of special mention and extensive writing. I think it's necessary to clearly inform the people about this.
Captain Nagata = I think it's deeply necessary to inform the people within Korea (鮮內).
Major Tanaka = At that time, I told the newspaper reporters,
"I just met the commander of the front-line unit, and the commander was crying."
They looked surprised and asked,
"Why was the commander crying?"
I replied,
"It's problematic if that's not understood. The commander said (We endure orders from above, even if hundreds of planes drop bombs, even if tens or hundreds of cannons line up and pour down shells like rain, even if tens of tanks attack. But there's something we absolutely cannot endure. Why can't we cross the border line? Because we are prohibited from crossing this border line, we can only respond with gunfire. If this were permitted, we could enter a battle of thorough annihilation. With one battle, we could make it impossible for the enemy to attack again. Aren't they attacking repeatedly? The line we claim is beyond Changji (長池?). The Soviets, without warning us, have come out onto the line we claim. That's why the battle occurred, so why is it wrong for us to advance to that line over there? This is unbearable.) while shedding tears. <46> But it's an order, so it can't be helped.
I returned, understanding the commander's distress."
Then they said,
"That's truly good news value,"
and sent telegrams. After that, I asked the foreign newspaper reporters,
"Did you talk with the Japanese press corps?"
They replied,
"No, we didn't talk because they don't know even if we ask. But they said their articles are almost half banned, and they're unemployed. We foreign reporters get news from you, but Japanese newspapers can't get news. This is regrettable towards the newsmen, we sympathize. Why aren't Japanese newspaper reporters given news? Why is it banned? We don't understand the reason." To this, I explained as follows: "The reason is, if the situation as it is were conveyed to the people, the people wouldn't remain idle. If that happened, even if the government adopts a non-escalation policy and the front-line troops defend the border, it would inevitably lead to a Japan-Soviet war. Therefore, the Japanese government is restricting the local situation to some extent to avoid further agitating the people's nerves. That's the reason." Hearing this, they said it was again good news and were about to send telegrams immediately, so I said,
"You shouldn't send the telegram."
When they asked why not, I said,
"You've seen the actual situation, so you should understand well. However, people in your home countries, reading just one telegram, might think that, but the front-line troops are in distress because of the airplanes. Therefore, it's problematic to inform the people about that, so the articles are restricted. You are being deceived by someone in Japan. If it turns out like that, you too will be in trouble. It's better not to publish articles that cause such pointless misunderstandings." They insisted they would publish anyway, so I told them to write carefully to avoid misunderstandings, and they all seemed to write well. Thus, finally, I explained that I wanted them to properly recognize the thoroughly consistent attitude of the Japanese army from beginning to end. The news about the unit commander crying seemed to have moved them. <47>

Where is the Objective of the War?

Lieutenant Colonel Komatsu = Generally, the meaning of why we don't fight is, anyway, we are fighting in China. Adding another country and fighting two countries is a loss overall. There's no other way to put it. However, regarding the point that the Soviet Union came out here, it's necessary to consider the Russian nature. If left unchecked, they will likely come out somewhere else again. If that too is left unchecked, won't they eventually force Japan into a situation where it has no choice but to fight a full-scale war? Fighting China and the Soviets on two fronts is a loss. Therefore, utilizing their nature, it's necessary to nip it in the bud (双葉). So, it's good if we can make them give up trying to come out again for half a year or a year. (Omission) Our use of force at Changkufeng was just this. To put it formally, it's just "non-escalation" (不擴大). Once this objective is achieved, there's no need to incur further sacrifices.
This time, it's the issue of airplanes. If airplanes are used, both sides will end up bombing each other's airbases. Then, it will eventually escalate. (Omission)
It's called purely defensive defense (專守 防禦), but the objective is to repel the enemy just by defending without engaging in offensive actions, thereby making the enemy abandon their attack. There are various kinds of war. I view this incident as one Japan-Soviet war. The Odaka unit, through 14 days of defensive battle, forced the Soviet Union to submit, thus fully achieving the war objective. The Soviet Union gave up, realizing they couldn't defeat the Japanese army no matter what. On the other hand, at an important time, it exerted a favorable influence on the China Incident, putting it on track.
Major Akutagawa = The achievement of the OO unit's forbearance and self-restraint was great. I also went with Interpreter Okade (岡出 通譯官) and newspaper reporters from Kyūshū on the afternoon of the 3rd, and I was surprised to hear stories from the homeland. Someone from Kokura City (小倉市) said that while Korea (朝鮮) is so brightly lit with illuminations, the homeland received a huge shock and is enforcing lighting controls. But the Korean compatriots trusted the Imperial Army and were calm.
Now, as much time has passed, let's hear the final thoughts from Captain Tominaga, who was at the center of the battle.
Captain Tominaga = Many sacrifices were made this time, and thinking about it brings tears to my eyes. My true feeling is that I sincerely hope you ensure that the noble actions of the victims are not made meaningless in practice. That is all.
Reporter = Thank you for speaking with us for so long. <48>

**Summary**

This 1938 round-table discussion features Japanese military officers and colonial officials rationalizing Japan's actions during the Changkufeng Incident (July-August 1938), a border clash with the Soviet Union. They emphasize Soviet aggression, Japan's "peaceful" intentions constrained by the ongoing war in China, and the alleged superiority and discipline of Japanese soldiers despite material disadvantages and tactical restraints like not crossing the border or deploying aircraft offensively. The participants justify the policy of non-escalation as a strategic necessity to avoid a two-front war, while simultaneously asserting Japan's readiness and capability for a full-scale conflict if provoked further, aiming to counter perceptions of weakness. For historians studying Korean colonial history, the article is revealing in its complete omission of any Korean perspective despite occurring on Korea's border and featuring officials from the Government-General of Korea; the discussion treats Korea purely as a strategic location and its people as passive observers whose morale needs managing through Japanese propaganda about military strength and Soviet untrustworthiness. The candid discussion about managing public perception (both domestic Japanese and Korean) and spinning the narrative for foreign correspondents offers insight into Japanese wartime information control and psychological operations within the colony.

「國境 現地」座談會(朝鮮軍司令部 士官과 總督府 官吏 모여), 張鼔峰 風雲과 其後 情勢
日蘇 一戰이 잇스리란 세계 여론을 證左하는드시 끗끗내 長鼔峰에서 彼我 양군의 충돌이 잇서 5,000여의 사상자를 내엿다. 이제 방공 성전 途上의 我國에 향하야 불법한 戰火를 보내는 蘇軍의 정체는 엇더한가. 현지에서 군부와 총독부 官史가<30> 모여 한 귀중한 이 역사적 현지 기록.

今番 戰鬪와 國境 問題

기자 = 분망하신 진중에 이렇게 와 주시니 감사함니다. 이번 사변은 여러 가지 의미로 중대 문제이였는데 爲先 그 전투 경과부터 말슴하여 주섯스면 좋겟서요.

田中OO長 (少佐) = 나는 오늘 돌연 현지로부터 도착하였는데, 원래 내가 着任한 것은 一昨年이었어요. 着任後 곳 洋舘坪 사건이 이러났었는데 아시는 바와 같이 국경선을 분명히 결정하여 노치 안으면 국경 분쟁이 늘 不絶할 터임으로 爲先 한 시급히 국경선을 示하여 주지 안으면 임무 수행상 곤란한 처지에 있었서요. 洋舘坪 사건 때에 그 때에 중앙에서 시달된 것에 의하면 국경선의 결정은 외교교섭에 의하여 결정될 것임으로 지금 즉시 결정할 수는 없는 것이다. 임무 수행상 종종의 국경 분쟁 사건을 회피하기 위하여 제일선 군대는 琿春 界約의 소련국경선을 넘으면 안된다. 거기까지는 露西亞의 행동에 대하여 수수하게 보아 두라고 하였어요. 소련측이 말하는 국경선 너머에 대하여는 중앙의 인가를 어든 뒤에 행동하라고 하였어요. 그런데 소련은 張鼓峰 부근에 항상 순찰대를 파견하여 행동하고 있는데 우리는 모다 분개하였지만 상사로부터 국경 분쟁을 피하기 위하여 묵인하랴고 하였음으로 그대로 두었지요. 그런데 이번에는 소련이 張鼓峰 정상에 다시 진지를 구축하고 철조망을 에워싸고 병력을 증가하며 대포도 가저와서 명확히 영구 점거의 태도를 示함에 至하였어요. 그래서 우리는 이 불법 행동에 대해서 - 我國이 日支 사변을 배후에 끼고 있는 금일 마치 소련이 일본의 弱味을 였보고 무력으로서 중요선을 제것을 만들랴는 이 무례한 태도를 적당치 못하다고 생각했지요.
이 일에 대해서는 내가 일즉 외국 신문 기자들에게 말도 하였거니와 소련측이 외교 협의에 응치않고, 가만히 무경고로 병력을 내여 진지를 구축하는 것은 실로 불가하다. 더구나 日本의 평화 제의에 대하여는 거기 應치않고 일방적 의론만 하며 더구나 불시에 병력을 가지고 일본을 누르려 하는 태도, 더구나 일본이 현재 日支 사변의 전투 와중에 있음을 알고 없는 틈을 타서 덤벼드는 非 武士道의 태도,<32> 이것을 우리들은 묵인할 수가 없어요. 그러기에 일본이 만일 현재 사변의 와중에 없었다면 훨신 더 평화적인 태도에 나왔을 터이나, 일본의 약점을 엿보고 달려드는 이 소련의 태도에 대하야 일본의 武道가 도저히 허락치 안는다고 외국 신문 기자에게 설명하였는데 기자단들도 우리 마음을 잘 아러 주어서 일본군이 일개 고지의 문제에 이렇게 힘을 드리는 이유를 알었다고 하여요.

當時 反擊까지의 經過

芥川 少佐 (조선 군 보도반) = 7월 29일이었는데 최초의 충돌이.. 그 전의 최초의 越境이 7월 11일이었지요. 그 뒤부터 퉁탕 - 하고 한바탕 해 대인 29일까지니 소련측 현지의 모양을 간단하게 田中 少佐 말슴하여 주서요.
田中 少佐 (OO長) = 7월 11일에 소련의 약간명의 병정이 돌연히 張鼔峰에 나타나 부근 일대에 공사를 개시한다는 報告 온 것이 최초의 발단이었어요. 그래서 우리로서는 았가 말한 모양으로 琿春 界約線까지는 손을 대이지 말나고 명령을 밧고 있음으로 그 선으로부터 소련이 드러오면 당연히 越境이 되기에 때려부숴도 상관없을 줄 알면서도 이미 상사로부터 주의가 있었기에 그대로 爲先 보고만 하였지요.
기자 = 7월 16일에 松島 헌병 伍長의
田中 少佐 = 소련의 행동을 감시하며 실상을 정찰할 임무를 띠고서 松島 헌병 伍長은 소련의 진지 상황을 실제로 확인하고저 16일 현지에 나갓지요. 그런데 張鼓峰의 남방, 盧頂의 동측에서 그 진지를 점령한 소련병에게 저격되여 伍長은 전사하였지요. 伍長의 사체는 그 직후 소련병에 의하여 자국 내에 가저가 버렸어요. 이전에도 주민이 현지로 가면 사격한다는 태도로서 1작년의 洋舘坪 사건이 이러났지요.
富永 大尉 (조선 헌병대) = 현지에 가서 비로소 처음 보았는데 張鼓峰에 至하는 데는 좋은 도로가 뇌여있고 대포도 자유스럽게 단닐만한 훌륭한 도로가 沙草峰으로부터 張鼓峰까지 통하여 있어요. 침략적 계획적의 행동의 표현이라고 보아저요.
기자 = 11일에 소련병이 張鼓峰 점거 후 29일까지의 반격하는 동안은 이쪽에서는 쭉 - 계속하여<33> 奪回할 방침으로 한 것임니까?
田中 少佐 = 그러치는 안었지요.
小松 中佐 (조선 군사령부) = 었쨋든 그 당시 최초에는 불문에 附한다는 의향도 있었지요. 그러나 이것을 그대로 방임하면 琿春 界約을 인정하는 것이 되며 더구나 우리 약점을 엿보고서의 이 태도에 엄중한 항의를 할 필요가 있었어요. 그러고도 듯지 안는다면 일격을 가하여 奪回하여도 좋겠다는 의도가 있었지요. 그런데 종종 大局上으로 소련측의 반성을 促하기로 하여 헛되히 이런 일로서 문제를 이르키지 안을 방침인 모양이었어요. 7월 24일에는 제일선 인양을 명하여 왔어요. 제일선의 일부를 殘置하고 외교 교섭 추이을 기다리기로 하였지요. 이것은 일본이 평화적으로 해결코저 하는 공명 정대한 방침을 직힐 생각으로 일부를 殘置하고 주력을 인양한 것이지요. 그런데 29일에는 다시 蘇兵의 일부가 전진하여 진지를 점하였어요. 이 지점은 가령 琿春 界約의 선을 능선의 정상에 있다고 가정할지라도 분명히 이쪽의 滿洲領임으로 소련의 태도는 참으로 괘심한 일이었지요. 이에 이르자 승인 持久에도 한도가 있어 그래서 반격했지요. 이것이 7월 29일이었지요.

張鼔峰으로 蘇軍 續續 集結

小松 中佐 (조선 군사령부) = 반격한 我方은 전차는 물론 없고 병력도 매우 적어서 일개 소대쯔음이었지요. 그래서 격퇴하였지만 그 위치에 머무르면 悶着이 생기리라 하여서 이쪽으로부터 피하었지요. 그런즉 30일 밤이었서요. 점점 沙草峰에 자동차, 전차의 폭음이 들니여오고 병력도 증가하여 오는 드시 들니였어요. 더구나 전차의 일부가 張鼓峰으로 이동하는 形跡이 있었어요. 그럴 지음에 斥候가 소련이 공세로 나온 것을 보고하여 왔어요.

이것은 소련이 나슬 때의 준비다. 벌서 과거 1년 간에 국내의 노력에 의하여 모든 공장은 전시 체제가 되여있다. 急角度로 공업 능력을 발휘할 수 있다. 砲彈이든지 대포든지 금후는 훨신 증가한다. 그러므로 그러한 점에서 일본이 소련과 싸울 실력이 없다고 보는 것은 誤見이다. 다만 일본이 즐기여 전쟁을 하지 않을 뿐이라고, 이러케 설명하여 주었어요.
