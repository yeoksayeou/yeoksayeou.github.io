Magazine Title	삼천리 제10권 제11호
Publication Date	1938년 11월 01일
Article Title	傳說 (Legend)
Author	李無影
Article Type	소설 (Novel/Fiction)

傳說
						李無影
(Continued from previous issue) A woman's wedding invitation is like a registration document for the transfer of some property. The statement "From today, I become so-and-so's wife" simultaneously declares, "From today, I sever all private connections I have maintained with you all until now."

Indeed, in relationships between men and women, this wedding invitation is an indispensable formality. And in terms of utility, it is truly effective. Moreover, when a relationship between a man and a woman has become most awkward, this single square piece of paper smoothly resolves matters that could not be settled even with dozens of pages of writing. "I am marrying Mr. So-and-so" – with this one phrase, there is no need for excuses, and even in situations where explanations with delicate words would be extremely difficult, the wedding invitation allows one to pass through smoothly.

The case between Chun-gu and Yongja now was also like that. By sending out one wedding invitation, Yongja had completely shed even the obligation to report on matters before or after it. Using that as a turning point, she cut off all contact. Considering the amount of back-and-forth they had,<250> and how close their emotions had become, for her to simply ignore him like that was not proper from any perspective, yet it was considered proper. Because they thought it proper, Yongja did not suffer, and Chun-gu also acknowledged its propriety. Although there were various things Yongja should have related, Chun-gu neither expected them nor felt the slightest unnaturalness in things turning out that way.

"Yongja is getting married..."

At that time, Chun-gu was staying at the mountain villa of a certain painter in S, a place near Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul) yet so remote that buying a single postcard required walking ten *ri* (about 4 km). Mail also arrived every other day, delivered by a postman who picked his days.

"Please, save me and go to Seoul quickly."

Receiving the mail handed over by the delivery man like this, he found one issue of the literary monthly magazine "P'yŏngnon" (Critique), one children's magazine, an invitation to R's poetry collection publication party, and a white square envelope.

This square envelope was Yongja's wedding invitation.

Chun-gu thought of Ham. But Ham had also closed his bookstore the previous winter and returned to H city, so he wasn't there. He felt utterly desolate. He had known beforehand that Yongja would marry someone other than him, and he himself had given up on her long ago, so he wasn't that surprised, but the fact that there was no friend around him now with whom he could talk about Yongja's marriage stirred up his mind.

"Now I've completely lost to Yongja!"

He muttered. Hearing Yongja's words relayed by Ham, he had thought to himself, "Just you wait—" and had determined to marry a woman who was in no way inferior to Yongja, to show her. He had planned to boastfully send his wedding invitation to Yongja first as soon as it was ready.

But Chun-gu had now lost. He knew well that the man Yongja was to marry was the second son of the Yun family, one of the leading wealthy families in H city. He had guessed this would happen last winter when Yongja had abruptly stood up and left, so ah, what's the use,<251> he regretted not having hurriedly produced an invitation stating he was marrying so-and-so at such-and-such place on a certain date and month to present before Yongja. Now, with no chance left to even compete with Yongja, his mind was utterly stirred up.
—It was to this H city, where Yongja lived, that Chun-gu had now come.

Although Chun-gu had visited H city four or five years ago, there was little trace of its former appearance. Since he had arrived during the day and left on the night train back then, he had no distinct memories, but getting off the train, he received the impression of a scholar who had worn a topknot suddenly appearing dressed smartly in a Western suit. There were many streets that hadn't existed when he came before, and barracks-style Western houses and multi-story buildings lined the streets. The signboards of the commercial district (商街) were much more adorned, and like a place celebrating a victory, there were many flags. Pallyongsan Mountain, which had stood scrawny like a lean horse fed on bean-curd refuse, now had cultural residences in blue, red, green, and vermillion penetrating halfway up its slope. It was already less a mountain and more a hill within a city. It seemed that as modern culture develops, mountains become shallower, as the lower half of Pallyongsan had been cut off and consumed by the H city urban area.

In the midst of a modern city where old things were disappearing one by one, Chun-gu, a cultural figure of the day, felt a stillness far removed from—no, diametrically opposed to—that splendor as he walked. This prosperity, this culture, these dazzling facilities—all the brilliance seemed cheap, like artificial flowers (假花) attached to an old tree. Though small and humble, the tile-roofed houses being demolished day by day seemed somehow to possess more tradition (傳統) than the large, ornate Western buildings. Why did the small fabric store, representing only a fraction in quantity and price, seem more established and its goods more like real merchandise than the Western goods shops selling neckties and hats...?

Chun-gu pondered these baseless thoughts while sightseeing the streets under the guidance of the X Ilbo (X Daily News) branch manager, who was hosting the event. Lecture posters and flyers caught his eye on every street. A large signboard was even erected at a busy intersection. Chun-gu's face flushed at the exaggeration. "Great critic and writer! Missing this opportunity would be a regret for a thousand autumns"—it was that kind of talk. Looking closely, he saw that the poster even had a large photograph of himself printed on it, obtained from somewhere.<252>

However, the result was the opposite of the publicity. The hall, which could hold perhaps five or six hundred people, remained largely empty. Even by the scheduled time, the second floor had only a countable number of people.

Thirty minutes past the scheduled time, the audience numbered only around three hundred. Needless to say, the organizers were embarrassed, but the speakers too felt mortified, like brides slapped on the cheek after applying powder and rouge.

As if completely forgetting they lived in H city with a population close to one hundred thousand, the audience burst into applause, urging the event to start on time. The host began, and P was dragged out according to the schedule. P passionately argued for an hour under the theme "Let's Have Our Own Literature."

P switched places with Chun-gu. The audience had grown considerably since the beginning, but the hall was still half empty. He suddenly began to look down on the audience. His talk was more of a chat than a lecture. His topic was "What is Literature?" but his knowledge was not so much gained from books as it was an anecdote based on his actual experience in creative writing. As his mind was not systematic, it naturally lacked coherence.

However, the reason his lecture that night turned out flat was not merely the small audience size. When he discovered that someone he had hoped wouldn't come, yet felt should have come, had not appeared, he even felt a kind of insult.

For some reason, even Ham was not visible.

Until he finished his lecture and stepped down from the podium, Yongja had not appeared.

The lecture ended at half past ten. Trying to avoid the customary welcoming party that usually followed such events, Chun-gu made various excuses, but he couldn't escape the fact that he was an "invited speaker." He was dragged along with the group to a restaurant.

He drank alcohol like never before.

He found it laughable that he, who hadn't flinched even when he first heard from Ham what Yongja had said, felt so agitated today when it had already become a legend of the past. Thinking it laughable, he still drank, and even after getting up from the table, he dragged P along, emptying his pockets to spend the night hopping between late-night establishments. H city seemed to spin dizzily, as if drunk on the late-night scene.<253>

The early spring dawn in the northern country was colder than Yongja's eyes. Embracing a damp utility pole on the cold northern dawn street, Chun-gu cried. He sobbed loudly like a child. Crying, he was dragged back to the inn by P, and crying like a child, he fell asleep.

He didn't know when, but feeling extremely thirsty, Chun-gu woke up. He tilted the water vessel by his pillow, but there wasn't a drop of water. Lying down again, he felt a stiff piece of manuscript paper. He picked it up casually; it was a telegram.

A telegram couldn't possibly have anything to do with him. So he threw it aside and fell asleep again, only to wake up a second time due to thirst and discover that the telegram was addressed to him.

The message requested him to leave Hamhŭng tomorrow morning and come to K Mountain Villa by B Lake for an urgent discussion.

The sender was Kim Tan-wŏn. Although he had never met Tan-wŏn in person, they had exchanged letters several times. Tan-wŏn was a poet from a working-class background. He had invited Chun-gu to visit in every letter, and Chun-gu had also wanted to meet him, but a year had passed without the opportunity. Just the other day, he had received a letter saying Tan-wŏn would also come out to H city if Chun-gu came there. At first, Chun-gu wondered if there was any news, but then he had forgotten about it all, and now the telegram arrived. It seemed Tan-wŏn had sent the telegram because he couldn't come due to unavoidable circumstances, insisting Chun-gu come instead.

Somehow, leaving H city just like this felt empty. However, since a telegram had been sent, he couldn't just ignore it. He woke K, the editor of "Munhaksa" (Literature Company) who was still sleeping soundly, pestered him for travel expenses, and tried to tempt P as well.

"If we just go, we can surely pester R for some money on the way back."

"Well, I want to go too, but..."

P seemed immediately interested, but unlike Chun-gu, he had a job, so he decided to go straight back to Kyŏngsŏng. Chun-gu left H city on the ten o'clock train alone. He held a certain expectation because he would have to pass through H city again on his return. He decided he would look for Ham on the way back and hear some news about Yongja, so he sent a postcard to Ham from the station.

The motor railcar bound for B Lake was empty. He struck up a conversation with a middle-aged gentleman around fifty in the next seat who seemed enjoyable to talk with, listening to stories about the hydroelectric construction project until the afternoon. Even just hearing about it was impressive, but he was surprised by such a large-scale construction.<254> He changed several times from the gasoline car to a mountain-climbing car called an "incline."

Although sightseeing groups reportedly swarmed B Lake like locusts during the peak season, what Chun-gu saw of B Lake was merely the awesome power of human effort. The water, collected by damming a mountain valley, lacked any fresh vitality. He couldn't understand the minds of people who praised such dead water (死水).

However, he felt deeply moved contemplating that the efforts of H city, which understood art, were mixed in, albeit as a minuscule fraction, with this massive project.

A small steamboat glided over the dead water. Along the banks, where not a single tree or blade of proper grass grew, only occasional mountain swallows following the water crossed horizontally. The water was dead water, the mountains were dead mountains, even the animals lacked vitality, but only his young blood throbbed. He felt an impulse to throw himself into this project that conquered such nature. He firmly resolved to ask R to let him work here for a few months and waited for the boat to reach the mountain villa visible in the distance.

As the boat approached the embankment, human figures became visible on the far bank. People, numbering even more than the boat passengers, waited for this boat to dock. There were three women in Japanese-style work clothes, two men in shirtsleeves, and then some woman and a young girl, and a young man in a grey suit.

Finally, the boat docked.

Although Chun-gu didn't recognize the face, he went up to the young man in the grey suit, gave his name, and grasped his hand firmly as if meeting a friend from ten years ago.

"I'm Kim Chun-gu."

"Pardon?..."

The young man looked puzzled, so Chun-gu also stepped back awkwardly and asked.

"Aren't you Mr. R?"

"No, sir. You must have mistaken me."<255>

"Where is K Mountain Villa?"

"It's that building there. Those women are from the villa, so why don't you ask them who you're looking for?"

Chun-gu went over to them and asked for R.

"R?... There's no one here by that name. Where did you come from?"

"He's with the Changjin Hydroelectric Company, and we were supposed to meet here..."

"Well, there's no such person staying here. We only opened for the season five days ago, you see."

Hearing this, he learned that there were no boats leaving or arriving from here. If the person was from the hydroelectric company, the construction site was down below, so they would likely come after six o'clock. He decided to wait inside anyway and took a six-mat room facing the lake. He left his business card, asking them to inform him if someone named R came looking, and first went down to the bathhouse. Perhaps because of the high altitude, the cold made him long for cotton-padded clothes.

Even so, soaking in the hot bath for about ten minutes completely relieved the cold.

Half past six came and went, but there was still no sign. He asked about the construction site and was told it was about five *ri* (2 km) away. Feeling hungry, he decided to eat dinner while waiting. Just as he received his dinner table and took a couple of spoonfuls, there was a light knock-knock. Chun-gu, still holding his rice bowl, rushed to open the sliding door, and unexpectedly, it wasn't R, but Yongja.

"Surprised?"

It was definitely Yongja. A willow-green jeogori (upper garment) and a long, straight skirt falling smoothly made her look even more composed. Below her hair, fluffy like corn silk in early autumn, her eyes, looking just as dreamy as they did three years ago, shone icily cold.

"But what is all this?"

Chun-gu newly realized that such a reality could exist.

"No, let's talk about that slowly later. First, please clear away the dinner table."<256>

"And you, Yongja?"

"I just finished eating and came out."

Instead of rice, Chun-gu chewed on old memories from three years ago.

"Mr. R sent the telegram, didn't he?"

Yongja spoke after waiting for the maid to bring side dishes and take away the dinner table.

"How did you know that? Mr. R somehow knew which inn I was staying at and sent a telegram."

"I'm the one who sent that telegram. I knew you were coming to H, so I figured if I sent it to the XX Ilbo branch office, it would reach you."

"Ah, so it came to the branch office— I didn't even look closely at the address. But Mr. R!"

"Please forgive me. It wasn't possible to send it in my name, and since I knew Mr. R was in this area, although I knew it wasn't right, I borrowed his name."

"..."

Chun-gu wondered what reason compelled her to drag him out here like this.

"At first, I decided not to meet you, Professor. So, since my older sister happens to be married and living in M village nearby, I deliberately took refuge here. But then, it somehow felt cowardly..."

"Cowardly..."

"And I also wanted to meet you at least once and hear what you had to say."

"What I had to say..."

Chun-gu sat there passively, as if taking dictation, smoking a cigarette. He felt this wasn't something the sharp Yongja he knew would do.

"In any case, this is not like you, Yongja. I didn't know you would resort to such melodramatic methods."

"I thought so too. But..."

"Whatever the reason, if you called me like this, you should have at least come out to my boat. I mistook a stranger for Mr. R and even shook his hand..."<257>

"Hohoho, I saw that from the second-floor stairs. Seeing it made me feel quite anxious. Just then, my brother-in-law had come all the way to M village and dropped by saying he wanted to see me before leaving..."

Yongja looked as if she might be sucking her fingers.

"No matter how I think about it, I can't understand. Why the Yongja I knew would imitate a popular novel like this... When I came to H, I anticipated meeting you. But the scene I imagined was..."

"You thought I'd pretend not to see you, or else bring my husband, introduce him, and show off, is that what you mean?"

"I thought at least you, Yongja, would do that. The Yongja who could say things that countless other women couldn't manage to say, making all sorts of excuses, without the slightest effort... I didn't think you would use such a method to bring me to a place like this. Because that's cowardly."

"Call it cowardly if you like!"

Yongja, perhaps at a loss for a reply, suddenly stood up. Chun-gu was so dismayed he reflexively grabbed her skirt hem.

However, unexpectedly, Yongja smiled softly.

"Are you afraid I'll run away? Not at all. Come, let's get up. The moon is out tonight, so let's stroll over to the embankment."

"Won't it be cold?"

Chun-gu asked, but stood up nonetheless.

Outside, it wasn't just cool, it was cold. Chun-gu resentfully ran back up, put on his suit jacket, and came down.

The moon of the seventeenth day was round.<258>

Leaving the embankment to their side, they traced a narrow mountain path that crossed the mountainside like a child's sash. Though it was supposed to be the third lunar month (spring), there was no scent of grass, only a cold wind. Somewhere, the sound of water falling strongly into a basin echoed.

"Shouldn't there be at least one fishing fire out there in the middle?"

Yongja seemed to feel the same thing Chun-gu had felt earlier from this dead nature.

But Chun-gu was different now. From this dead nature incapable of knowing the pulse of life, he no longer felt the same disillusionment, futility, or powerlessness as before. Though the water couldn't raise a single ripple, and the waterside lacked even a single tree, he felt some pulsation of life in this dead nature. It was ecstasy and transience. In the water, lifeless like boiled water, he felt waves, and on the barren mountain lacking even a single pine sapling, he felt as if he heard birdsong. It seemed as though schools of carp were thrashing their tails and frolicking in the lake, pressing against it.

It was a symbol of life, the throbbing of life force. At first, Chun-gu thought the full moon had infused this dead nature with the power of life. But only when the moon was hidden in the clouds for a while did he realize that the source of that life force was Yongja.

"Is the power of woman really this great?... Perhaps women possess a power unforeseen even by science, the greatest authority of this age. Didn't it instantly... revive dead nature to this extent...?"

Looking down at the unchanging moon, Chun-gu gently stole a glance at Yongja. Her face, immobile like a stone figure just unearthed, was still just as cold.

"Yongja, please tell me your story since then."

"My story since then?"

Yongja, still not turning her head, said,

"My story since then... looking back from now, it belongs to the past, doesn't it?... Is there any need to talk about things belonging to the past?"

"In that case, please tell me about the present."<259>

"About the present?... The present story is just this: a married woman, secretly without her husband's knowledge, has lured a man who is not her husband to a lakeside in the mountains and is sitting like this. That's all."

"You use the word 'lure'?"

"Yes, I did,"

Yongja said. Only then did she turn her head towards Chun-gu. Silence fell. The silence continued for about three or four minutes. Without breaking the silence, Yongja turned her face back towards the lake surface (湖面). Silence began again.

The silence of living beings is not beauty (美). When living beings maintain a long silence, it signifies death (죽엄). In this dead water and dead mountain, just when the pulse seemed to stop again due to the silence of Yongja, who alone had shown the pulsation of life (it had indeed been a long time until then), Yongja suddenly shot up. She threw her arms up towards the night sky and trembled violently. Then she cried out.

"Ah—"

Chun-gu also shot up. In a terrifyingly swift instant, the thought (Yongja is going to commit suicide by throwing herself in) flashed like lightning.

And unfortunately, his intuition was correct. Yongja turned to him and spoke. It was a terribly short utterance.

"Professor! Don't you want to jump down there! Into that surging water basin!"

This time, much more slowly, Yongja continued speaking.

"And don't you want to overturn the tranquil lake along with that tumbling water!"

Even as she spoke these words, Yongja herself probably didn't know what she was saying. She had become flushed, though she didn't know by what force. Not by any force seeping in from the outside. From within—from her heart where young blood boiled, some force—an uncontrollable passion had swept through her entire body like a storm. It was no longer the pulsation of life force. It was the overflowing of young blood spreading gushingly throughout her body.—No, it was the desperate frenzy of passion rebelling against intellect (知性)—precisely what morality (道德) calls a fall (타락).—Passion demands unruliness (無軌道). Right now, Yongja's arms were wrapped tightly around Chun-gu's neck.

However, the author believes. That you, dear readers, will not merely measure Yongja's actions now with the ruler of morality and attach a shameful name to her.

"Yongja, please stop! Stop!"

Chun-gu lifted Yongja's upper body from his lap. Right now, there were no thoughts in Chun-gu's mind. It was just hazy, just dim. The only thought he clearly grasped was that Yongja was definitely not happy.

"Yongja, calm down! You must not forget the circumstances you are in right now."

However, these were not words meant to admonish Yongja. Chun-gu was speaking to himself. But unexpectedly, Yongja lifted her head sharply.

"Is it intellect again? Since you, Professor Kim, are also a writer of this era, you probably can't despise intellect! But I'm afraid that contemporary writers, harping on intellect so much, might become scientists!"

"Then, Yongja, you don't think today's actions are excessive at all?"

"I am not a scientist. I am a human being. To love is good. It is beauty. What is excessive about doing good and seeking beauty?"

"This is truly the defeat of intellect!"

Chun-gu cried out to himself. He was clearly witnessing with his own eyes that even that brilliance, that reason, was powerless before passion.

Ham Yongja's brilliance had abandoned me. Her reason had insulted me! But, but, her passion, her pure heart, loves me this much, does it not? Yes. I must save Yongja! From the pit of brilliance—from the trap of reason, I must save<261> Yongja!

"Yongja! Only now do I understand. How much you love me—and you surely know well that I love you! Knowing this, and knowing how unhappy you are now, I cannot simply stand by."

Chun-gu took Yongja's hand. And he pressed his lips to her cheek, hot as a ball of fire.

"Professor, just once more! Again! This side this time!"

Yongja pulled Chun-gu's neck towards her and they shared a long kiss. Then, slowly releasing Chun-gu's hand, she moved back a little as if her passion was cooling down, and sat.

"Now, please continue what you were saying earlier."

"I, with Yongja..."

"No, wait a moment!"

Yongja, startled, covered Chun-gu's mouth as if to stop him.

"I'll say the next words. 'I will marry Yongja.'—That's what you were going to say, right? Right?"

"Yes."

For some reason, Yongja shook her head as if shuddering.

"No! That won't do, Professor!"

The conversation broke off abruptly. More than anything, Chun-gu needed to confirm whether he was dreaming right now.

"Then, Yongja, do you also love your current husband?"

"I love morality—the art of saving face."

"..."

"I will live my whole life with morality, and die together with morality..."

Chun-gu stared intently at Yongja.<262>

"No, absolutely not! I cannot just watch your unhappiness like this!"

"No. That absolutely cannot happen."

Yongja lowered her voice, speaking as if reasoning with a younger sibling.

"That absolutely cannot happen. Having been born in this age, we must uphold the morality of this age. We shouldn't defy recklessness, and we must serve even a husband we cannot love for our entire lives. Please leave tonight's events at B Lake as a legend. Let it become a legend passed down from person to person for hundreds, thousands of years, like the old story of Kyŏnu and Chiknyŏ... If the king of the celestial kingdom back then had been a bit more magnanimous, the legend of Chiknyŏ might not have arisen. But that was the morality of that time..."

Chun-gu only looked down at the moon reflected in the water.

"Once upon a time, a long, long time ago, in the year nineteen hundred and thirty-something, up on a mountain over four thousand *ch'ŏk* (feet) high in northern Korea (북조선), they say there was a large lake that generated hydroelectric power. By that lakeside, there was a married woman named Yongja—Professor, listen. Thousands of years after we are dead, this legend will be passed down along with this R [B] Lake. By then, this lake might be gone, and ancient trees thousands of years old might flourish in the very spot where we sit. Who knows. This whole area might become a bustling city street, and this vicinity might become a park, and right here where we are sitting now, if there's a bench (뻰취), perhaps some loving couple will sit side by side talking about us, who knows..."

"Yongja!"

"Now, Chun-gu. Don't think any other thoughts, and give me one more kiss—Then you go to your room, I to mine. You go your way, and I'll go mine. Now, a kiss—a kiss..."

It was a long, long kiss.

From the moment that long, long kiss ended, the life force that had clung to this high mountain nature began to wither once again. The water that had leaped and throbbed became listless like boiled and cooled water, and the bare mountain ridge began to lose its vitality, like the back of a hippopotamus (河馬) whose breath had ceased.

That this short story truly became a "legend" talked about among people happened just three days after Chun-gu returned to Seoul. The rumor that Chun-gu had gone to some temple intending to leave the material for this "legend" to posterity also circulated quite recently. (End)<263>

**Summary**

The story follows Kim Chun-gu, a writer, who travels to H city (likely Hamhŭng) for a lecture engagement, a place where his former lover, Yongja, now lives married to a wealthy man. Despite Chun-gu's initial feelings of rivalry and disappointment over losing her, he finds himself unexpectedly summoned by Yongja (using a deceptive telegram) to a remote mountain villa by B Lake (likely Pujŏn Lake), the site of a major hydroelectric project. During an intense moonlit encounter by the "dead" lake, Yongja reveals her unhappiness but ultimately rejects Chun-gu's impassioned proposal to save her, choosing to uphold societal morality. She frames their encounter as a future "legend," a poignant, tragic story for posterity, sealing their separation with a final kiss before they part ways.

From a historical perspective, the story offers insights into the complexities of modern relationships and the clash between personal desires (passion) and societal expectations (morality, intellect) among educated Koreans in the late 1930s. It vividly portrays the changing landscape of a provincial city like H under modernization (Western architecture, infrastructure projects like the massive hydroelectric dam at B Lake/Pujŏn Lake), contrasting this "progress" with a sense of underlying 'deadness' or alienation felt by the protagonist. The mention of specific locations like Hamhŭng, Pujŏn Lake (implied), the Changjin project, and the general atmosphere of a northern Korean city provide geographical and temporal context within the colonial period. The psychological state of the intellectual protagonist, grappling with personal failure, societal change, and the overwhelming power of suppressed emotion, is also revealing.

잡지명	삼천리 제10권 제11호
발행일	1938년 11월 01일
기사제목	傳說
필자	李無影
기사형태	소설

傳說
						李無影
(承前) 여자의 결혼 청첩장이란 어떤 물건의 이전 등기와도 같은 것이다. 오늘부터 나는 아무개의 안해가 된다-하는 말은 동시에 오늘부터는 지금까지 당신네들과 맺어 온 모든 사적 교분을 청산한다-이런 선언도 되는 것이다.
실로 남녀관게에 있어서는 이 결혼 청첩이란 없지 못할 형식이다. 그러고 이용가치로 보아서도 실로 효과적이다. 더욱이 남녀관게가 가장 거복상스럽게 된 때 수십장을 써도 다 하지 못할 말을 이 네모난 조이 한장이 무난하게 해결 지어준다. 나는 아무개씨와 결혼은 한다- 이 한마듸로서 변명도 할 필요도 없어지고 지금까지의 뗄리게트한 말로서 설명을 하러 든다면 더 없이 궁할 경우라도 그저 무난히 넘길 수 있는 것이 결혼 청첩장이다.
지금 준구와 용자와의 경우도 또한 그랬다. 용자는 결혼 청첩을 한 장 띄고는 그 전의 일이거나 그 후의 일을 보고 할 의무까지도 완전히 벗어버리었든 것이다. 그는 그것을 게기로 일체 소식을 끊었다. 원래 생각한다면 그만큼 설왕설래가 있던 사이요<250> 그만큼 감정과 감정이 접근해 있었드니 만큼 그대로 신지무니 한다는 것은 어디로 보아 온당한 일이 아니면서도 그것은 온당했다. 온당하다고 생각했기 때문에 용자도 고통을 받지 않었고 준구도 그 온당을 시인했든 것이다. 용자로부터서는 들러야 할 여러 가지 이야기가 있것만 준구는 그것을 기대하지도 않었고 또 그렇게 된다는 데 조고만한 부자연도 느끼지 않었다.
「용자가 결혼은 한다...」
그때 준구는 S라는 경성 근교기는 하면서도 엽서 한장을 사는 데도 십리 길이나 걸어야 하는 어떤 화가의 산장에 가서 있었다. 우편도 하로 걸렇금 기ㅅ수를 골라서 왔다.
「제발 절 살리느라고 어서 서울로 좀 가십시요.」
이렇게 배달부가 내 주는 편지를 받아들고 보니 한 장은 「평론」이라는 문예월간 잡지와 소년잡지 한 권, R의 시집출판 기념회 청첩장과 힌 사각봉투다.
이 사각봉투가 용자의 결혼 청첩장이었다.
준구는 함을 생각했다. 그러나 「함」도 지난 겨울에 책사를 말아 치우고 H로 돌아가고 없었다. 그는 더없이 쓸쓸했다. 용자가 자기 아닌 다른 사람과 결혼을 하리라는 것은 미리 알고 있었고 이쪽에서도 단념한 지 오란터라 그렇게 놀라지는 않었지마는 자기와 더부러 용자의 결혼을 같이 이야기할 말 벗이 지금 자기 주위에는 없다는 것이 그의 머리 속을 휘 저어노았다.
「인저는 용자한테 깨끗이 지고 말았구나!」
그는 중얼거렸다. 자기 딴에는 함이 전하던 용자의 말을 듣고 「어디 보아라-」 하고 어느 모르나 용자한테 애이지 안는 이렇다는 여성과 결혼을 해 보이리라 한 것이다. 그는 결혼 청첩이 되기만 하면 맨 먼저 용자한테다 여봐란 듯기 태기를 치랴고 별렀든 것이다.
그러나 준구는 이제 젔다. 용자의 남편될 사람이 H시에도 굴지의 재산가인 「윤」의 둘째 아들이라는 것을 잘 알고 있는 터다. 이렇게 될 줄은 지난 겨울 용자가 툭툭 털고 일어설 때 벌서 짐작했든 터고 보니 아무하구나<251> 서들어서 어느 달 몇인날 이러 이러한 장소에서 아무개와 결혼을 한다는 청첩장을 용자앞에 썩 내 놓지 못한 것이 한도 되었다. 인저 다시는 용자와 겨러 볼 기회좆아 없다는데서 그의 머리 속은 여지없이 휘저어 젔든 것이다.
-이 용자가 있는 H에 준구는 지금 온 것이었다.

사, 오년 전 준구는 H에 와 본 적이 있지만 H에는 그때의 면모란 별로 찾어 볼 길도 없었다. 그때라 낮에 나렸다가 그대로 밤차로 떠났던터라 이렇다고 기억에 남어 있을 턱도 없었지만, 상투를 틀었든 한 학자가 금시에 양복을 빠트리고 나선 것 같은 인상을 그는 차에서 나리면서 받았다. 길도 전에 왔을 때는 없던 길이 많었고 바락식 양옥이며 칭집이 거리에 즐비하다. 상가(商街)의 간판도 훨신 치장을 했고 마치 전승축하나 하는 곳처럼 기폭도 많었다. 비게 먹은 말처럼 꺼치러니 섰던 반룡산에도 어느듯 청, 홍, 녹 적의 문화주택이 산 중복에까지 들어 째었다. 그것은 벌서 산이라기보다 어떤 도시의 한 언덕이었다. 현대는 문화가 발달될 수록에 산도 얕어지는가 싶도록 반룡산의 하반은 H시가지가 잘러 먹고 없었다.
옛것이 하나 하나 없어지는 현대도시 한 복판에서 오늘날의 문화인인 준구는 그 찬란과는 인연이 먼- 아니 정반대되는 정막을 느끼면서 걸었다. 이 번영 이 문화 이 눈부신 시설-모든 찬란은 마치 고목에다 가화(假花)를 매단 듯이 값싸 보인다. 비록 적고 비록 추할망정 날르 날로 헐려가고 있는 기와집이 크고 화려한 서양건축 보다는 어덴지 모르게 전통(傳統)이 있어 보인다. 넥타이니 모자니 하는 양품점 보다는 양으로 치나 가격으로 치나 그 몇십분지 몇 밖에 안 될 조고만 포목상이 더 자리잡혀 보이고 상품다워 보이는 것은 무슨 까닭일가...
준구는 이런 턱없는 생각을 하며 주최측인 X일보 지국장의 안내로 거리를 구경했다. 거리에 마다 강연회 포스타-며 삐라가 눈에 뜨인다. 잡다한 네 거리에는 커다라니 간판도 내 세웠다. 준구는 얼골이 확근댈만콘 과장되어 있었다. 대평론가요 내 작가다. 이러한 기회를 놓진다면 천추의 한이 되리라고-이 식이었다. 어디서 구했는지 자서이 보니 포스다에는 준구의 사진까지가 커다랗게 인쇄되어 있다.<252>
그러나 선전과 결과는 정반대였다. 오륙백명이나 들어갈가 싶은 홀이것만 그대로 텅 비었다. 정각이 되도록 이칭은 헤어도 헤일만한 인수밖에 안되었다.
정각을 삼십분이나 지낫것만 청중이란 삼백명 내외였다. 주최자는 더 말 할 겟도 없것지만 그들도 마치 분 발르고 연지 찍고 갔다가 뺨맞은 신부처님 무안했다.
인구가 십만에 가까운 H시에 자기네가 살고 있다는 것도 까마득이 잊은 듯이 청충들은 시간 여행을 하라고 박수가 터진다. 사회가 시작되고 순서대로 P가 끌려나갔다. P는 「우리의 문학을 갖자」는 논지로 도도 한시간을 떠들었다.
P는 준구와 밖우었다. 처음 보다도 청중도 훨신 늘었으나 역시 홀 안은 반이나 비었다. 그는 갑작이 청중을 나추보기 시작했다. 그는 강연이라기 보다 좌담을 했다. 「문학이라 무었이냐」는 것이 그의 연제였으나 그의 지식이란 책에서 얻었다느니 보다도 실제 창작생활에서 얻은 한 경험담에 불과했다. 머리가 조직적이 못되는 그의 일이라 물론 두서도 없었다.
그러나 그날 밤의 그의 강연이 김빠진 이아기가 된데는 청중이 적었다는 단순한 그 이유 뿐만은 아니었다. 오지말기를 바랐으면서도 응당 왔어야 할 사람이 오지 않었다는 것을 발견할 때 그는 일종의 모욕까지를 느끼었다.
웬일인지 함까지도 보이지 않었다.
그가 강연을 맟우고 연단에서 나올 때까지도 용자는 보이지 않었든 것이다.
강연이 끝난 것은 열시 반이다. 이런 경우에 으레껏 있는 환영회를 미리 피할랴고 준구는 여러 가지 구실을 만들어 보았으나 그는 자기가 「초대받은 연사」라는 사실을 벗어나지는 못했다. 그는 일행과 함께 요리점으로 끌려갔다.

어쩐지 이대로 H를 떠나온 것은 허전했다. 그렇지마는 전보까지 첬는데 신지무니 할 수도 없어서 아직도 잠고 있는 「문학사」 주간인 K를 깨워 차비를 졸나 떼어 P도 유혹을 해 보았다.
