**Body Text Translation**
Secret Tales of the Human World: Mr. Yun Ch'iho and the Strange Story of Sŏgwangsa Temple

About 30 years ago, specifically in the spring of the 7th year of Kwangmu (1903, Year of the Water Rabbit), Mr. Yun Ungnyŏl, who was the Minister of Military Affairs at the time, arrived at Sŏgwangsa Temple (釋王寺) with his son Yun Ch'iho (尹致昊) (then the governor of Tŏgwŏn), other family members, and about ten escort soldiers. He set up lodging in the Sugundang hall of the main temple. The next day, he gathered the mountain monks and spoke as follows: "I cannot know the exact year, but presumably over 100 years ago, there must have been a monk named Haep'a Yŏsun (海波與涥) in this temple. Do his disciples (眷屬) continue to this day? Or even if there are no disciples, is there anyone who knows his deeds?" The assembled monks looked at each other and all replied that they did not know. "Then check old documents where his name might be recorded, or even inscribed wooden plaques (板懸). I will linger here for several days, so please search thoroughly and let me know." However, despite searching everywhere, they could only report that they could not find him. At this, the Minister (大監) also felt frustrated and gloomy, saying he would try hunting goat-antelopes, deer, or mountain goats. He took his retinue up the mountain. After two or three days of effort, they herded dozens of deer near Hyangjŏgam Hermitage (香積庵). As dusk fell, they came down to sleep. The next morning, he again took his retinue and went up towards the Hyangjŏk valley. Reaching the entrance of Naewŏnam Hermitage (內院庵), he told his attendants to rest for a moment. Just then, looking up at the stupa field (浮屠場), he saw what looked like some kind of grass (編簾草 - woven blind grass?) set up to dry on three stupas arranged in a row. The Minister took his cane and pushed aside the grass. On the front of the first stupa, the inscription "Haep'a-dang Yŏsun" (海波堂 與涥) clearly appeared. The Minister, his face brightening, called out, "Hey - Ch'iho! Ch'iho! You - bring the children here and bow to this stupa!" Yun Ch'iho, obeying his father's command, immediately performed the bow. The Minister spoke again, saying, "You - look at this," and took out something carefully wrapped (十襲封之) from his pouch (挾囊). Indeed, it was a note on which "Haep'a Yŏsun" was written. "Years ago, when I was in Chŏlla Province, I wrote this down and haven't spoken of it in detail until now, but I could never forget it, even in my dreams. Now - let's go down to the main temple."<24> Regarding this statement, the soldiers asked, "If you go up now, you'll surely catch all the deer and goat-antelopes. Are you going to stop?" The Minister replied, "Stop it - I started hunting because I was so frustrated at not achieving my purpose for coming here, but isn't hunting itself a misguided act (妄計) in the sacred realm of Buddha? I cannot explain everything here, so let's all go down." He then gathered the monks again at Sugundang hall and, addressing the elder monk of the mountain, Great Master Sŏrha (雪河 大師) (then 85 years old), said, "I thought Sŏrha would have considerable knowledge, but seeing you today, you are quite different from what I usually imagined. To put it simply, you seem only capable of handling the comings and goings of various guests, big and small, but utterly ignorant (蒙昧) of the temple's actual affairs. Why? Didn't I ask you the other day to find out about the monk Haep'a's deeds, and didn't you just say you couldn't find anything despite various checks? Yet today, I found it without difficulty on a stupa at the entrance of Naewŏn temple. Seeing that, how can it be that you children who have grown up here (長於斯 老於斯) for 60 or 70 years don't know him?" Sŏrha replied, "That is correct. Your words of reprimand are deserved. However, Your Excellency, for what reason did you wish to know about the monk named Haep'a?" he asked in return. "Hmm - I will tell you the story, so listen carefully. Years ago, wasn't I falsely accused by others and exiled (定配) to Wando (莞島) in Honam? Living there for three years, how distressed I must have been! Then one day, my house slave (床奴) came in saying that a fortune-teller using *myŏngdu* (鳴痘 - spirit mediumship, lit. 'crying pockmarks') had come to the neighboring house and was making predictions with one hundred percent accuracy, causing a stir among all the villagers. Although I had always rejected such things as superstition, I was so desperate that I thought I'd try asking, so didn't I go too? When I asked, 'Who is the fortune-teller here?' what they call the *myŏngdu* or sometimes *t'aeju* (胎主 - fetus spirit), only a voice was heard from the air, saying, 'I am here.' I said, 'I have something to ask. Where am I from, and for what reason have I come here?' It replied, 'Yes - Your Lordship is from the Capital (京城) and came here in exile.' 'Then when might I be released?' 'Since his crime is not particularly severe, the release order (解配 文字) will likely arrive in about half a month.' I thought, 'If that happens, how happy I would be! Is it definitely certain?' It replied, 'I never tell lies.' 'Then I have one more thing to ask. I have a son, but being here like this, I don't know where my child has gone. Please find that out for me.' It said, 'Yes - I will go find out and come back,' and made a *hwek* sound as if leaving. After a while, it returned, saying, 'Your Lordship's son is studying in the capital of America (美國).<25> He has become engaged to a woman from the Qing country (淸國) who came there to study. Next autumn, they will go to Shanghai (上海) for the wedding ceremony, and not long after, father and son will meet.' I asked, 'Since that is a future matter, I'll know when it happens. Can you tell what I was in my previous life?' It said, 'Yes - I will go check somewhere,' and seemed to go and return, then said, 'Your Lordship was a servant (종노릇) in your previous life.' So I asked again, 'A servant? Where?' It replied, 'Yes - at Sŏgwangsa Temple in Anbyŏn (安邊), Hamgyŏng Province.' 'Then would you know the name of the monk at that time?' 'Yes - his dharma name (法號) was Haep'a (海波) and his monk name (僧名) was Yŏsun (與涥)!' 'Then did I serve well as a servant?' 'Actually, Your Lordship's brother was also a monk. Your Lordship practiced diligently, so in your first rebirth, you were born in the great country (大國 - China?) and became a first-rank minister whose renowned name echoed throughout the land. In your second rebirth, you were entrusted to Chosŏn, but because all five fortunes (五福) are complete, you will soon be addressed as Minister (大監). However, the one who was Your Lordship's elder brother served very poorly as a monk. Claiming to renovate the Dharma Hall (法殿) or regild the Buddha statue (佛體改金), he consumed much money from believers and only filled his own pockets (私腹). For this, he suffered greatly in hell, and although he received human rebirth (人道 受生), he is now running a liquor business in a place called Saesulmak in T'ongch'ŏn County (通川郡), Kangwŏn Province (江原道). However, his house is wretchedly poor (집이 찌저지게 가난해서), and he is near death. Furthermore, both his hands are stumps (조막손), and his name is Yi Kyŏngun (李景云),' it said. So I nodded at everything and returned home, thinking I would test it, and didn't I write down each condition? Then, within two weeks, the release order came. The following autumn, a telegram arrived from Shanghai announcing my son's wedding ceremony. Not long after that, father and son were reunited. And again, not long after, I rose to the position of Minister (大臣位). How could there be even a hair's breadth of error (一毫 差錯) in that *myŏngdu*'s words? Four things had already come true, so I decided to test the remaining two. Whenever a monk from Sŏgwangsa came to my gate, I would call him in and ask, but none knew anything. I also secretly requested inquiries from the governors of Kwanbuk (關北 - northern Hamgyŏng), but the result was ultimately the same. So I decided internally, 'I will go personally and find out,' and after reporting to the throne (奏禀) my intention to spend a month recuperating at a scenic spot, I came here via Wŏnsan (元山)," he explained. Hearing this long story, the assembly was filled with wonder and praise (希有 贊歎). The Minister continued, "But there is still one thing remaining. Hey, Chuksan! (He called Kim Chuksan (金竹山), a man who had served as the governor of Chuksan under the Minister and was accompanying him like a confidential secretary). Chuksan answered, "Yes." "Call Yunho (允旿) for me." (Yunho is the current owner of Kimt'aek Inn, who was then a novice monk (沙彌) under 20 temporarily attending the Minister). When Yunho came in, "Oh - you've come! I need to send someone on an errand to Kangwŏn Province now, so find me a reliable person (信用이 상당한 사람).<26>" Yunho replied, "Yes - there is one," and recommended a man named Yu Taebang (劉大方). The Minister asked, "What is your name?" "Yes - it is Yu Taebang." "Hmm - is there such a name as Taebang?" "Yes - in Kyŏngsang Province custom, if a boy doesn't marry until late, he is called Ch'onggak Taebang (총각 大方 - Bachelor Taebang). I also came to this temple intending to just sell some ginger, but ended up staying, so Taebang became my name." The Minister then said, "Well, I need to send you on an errand (신바람 - older form of 심부름) to Kangwŏn Province. Go find Saesulmak in T'ongch'ŏn. There should be a man named Yi Kyŏngun running a liquor and food business (酒食 營業); bring him here immediately. Both his hands are stumps (조막손), so he should be easy to find." Taebang replied, "Yes," and left, returning after four days to report that he had brought him. Shortly after (未幾), the double-stump-handed Mr. Yi entered. Kim Chuksan escorted him and told him to bow to the Minister. The Minister said, "Never mind the bow, just sit down." Then the Minister spoke, "The reason I called for you today is none other than this: currently, you are Mr. Yi and I am Mr. Yun, but in a past life, you were my elder brother. Knowing you have fallen into difficult circumstances, how could I just pretend not to know? I will give you 10 bolts (疋) of white cotton cloth (白木) and 100 *nyang* (兩) of cash (錢). Use the money, perhaps on the day you receive it, to make arrangements for your household's basic living (兩主 糊口), and use the white cloth to make some clothes! Everything is due to the grace of Buddha (佛陀), so repent your past deeds and from now on, chant the Buddha's name frequently (念佛을 만히 하야) to extinguish your karmic hindrances (罪障)! However, it would be difficult even for an able-bodied person to carry what amounts to two loads (짐). Can you manage it? Therefore, I will arrange a money transfer (換) for you through the T'ongch'ŏn county magistrate (通川 郡守); go to the county office and collect it!" He handed over a payment voucher (於音標). Mr. Yi, asking, "Are we siblings from a past life?" said he was better than his parents in this life, shed tears of gratitude, and returned home. Afterwards, the Minister invited the monks again and said, "This is the temple (寺院) where I cultivated blessings in my past life. I express my meager sincerity (微誠) with 200 *nyang* of *yŏpjŏn* coins (엽전). Please use this (此) to supplement the funds for Buddha's incense and candles (香燭 資)!" Then he departed gracefully for the capital (上京).<27>

**Summary**

This article recounts a story about Yun Ungnyŏl, a high-ranking official in the late Chosŏn/Korean Empire era, and his son Yun Ch'iho. While exiled, Yun Ungnyŏl consults a spirit medium (*myŏngdu*) who accurately predicts his release, his son's activities abroad, and his own future success. Crucially, the medium reveals Yun's past life as a servant at Sŏgwangsa Temple and his former brother's reincarnation as a poor, disabled liquor seller named Yi Kyŏngun. Years later, Yun visits Sŏgwangsa, confirms the story by finding the stupa of the monk Haep'a (his former master/brother), locates the reincarnated Yi Kyŏngun, provides him with financial aid, and donates to the temple, attributing his fortune to his past life's deeds there.

For historians, this article offers a glimpse into the personal beliefs of elite figures like Yun Ungnyŏl during a period of significant social and political transition. His reliance on folk divination (*myŏngdu*) and strong belief in reincarnation, even using it to explain his current status and justify acts of charity towards his "past-life brother," contrasts with the modernizing trends often emphasized for this era. The story also implicitly touches upon social mobility (or lack thereof) across lifetimes and the persistence of traditional Buddhist and folk beliefs among the ruling class. The mention of Yun Ch'iho adds another layer, given his later complex historical role.

人世密話 尹致昊 氏와 釋王寺 奇譚
距今 30년 전 즉 光武 7년 癸卯 春에 당시 군부 대신이든 尹雄烈 氏가 그 아들 尹致昊(其時 德源 監理)와 기타 가족 及 호위병정 10여명을 다리고 釋王寺 寺에 도착하였읍니다. 큰 절 壽君堂으로 舍處를 정하였는데 익일에 산중 승려를 회집하여 놓고 아래와 같이 말하였읍니다. 年條는 확실히 알 수 없으나 추측으로도 아마 100년 이상이겠지요. 海波與涥이란 중이 절에 있었을 텐데 그 중의 眷屬이 오늘까지 계승하여 오는가. 혹은 眷屬이 없드래도 그 행장은 아느니가 있느냐고 하였다. 대중은 서로 도라보며 전부 모른다고만 대답이였읍니다. 그러면 그의 명의를 기재한 고문서라든가 또는 板懸같은데 까지 참고하여 보라. 내가 幾日間은 留連할테니 아못조록 搜探하여보고 此를 알게 하라 하였으나 어데든지 조사하여 보와도 찾어 낼 수 없다고 하였을 뿐입니다. 그제는 大監 역시 답답하고 울적하여서 노루 사슴의 산양이나 붙어보겠다 하고 수행원을 다리고 산상으로 올나 2,3일 전력으로 수십 마리의 사슴을 香積庵 부근으로 모라넣고 日暮함으로 나려와 자고는 익일 잃은 아츰에 역시 수행을 다리고 香積 골작운이로 올라가게 되였읍니다. 內院庵 입구에 이르러 侍輩들를 향하야 잠시 歇脚다가 마츰 浮屠場을 치여다보니 나열한 3위 浮屠에 무신 발풀(編簾草)을 세워 말리는 모양이라 대감은 단장을 가지고 그 풀을 헤처보니 제 1위에 섯는 浮屠 정면으로 海波堂 與涥이라 색인 것이 뚜렸하게 날아난다. 대감은 반색을 하며 얘 - 致昊야, 致昊야 너 - 아히들 다리고 이리 와서 이 浮屠에다 절을 하여라 하는 명령을 하였다고. 尹致昊 氏는 父命이라 즉시 奉行하였읍니다. 대감은 다시 말하되 너 - 이것 보와라 하면서 挾囊으로 십심封之하여 둔 것을 끄내여 보이난대 과연 海波與涥이라 기재한 문구이다. 내가 年前 전라도에 있을 적에 이것을 적어두고 아즉까지 상세한 말을 하지 않었으나 몽매에 있지 못하든 것이다. 자 - 큰 절로 나려가자.<24> 이 말에 대하야 - 지금 올라가시면 노루 사슴을 죄다 붓잡을 터이온데 그만두시렵니까 라고 병정들이 였줍니다. 대감은 말하되 그만두어 - 내가 當地에 온 목적을 達치 못함에 하도 울적하여서 산양을 시작하였으나 佛陀영경에 田獵이란 것부터 妄計가 아니냐?
