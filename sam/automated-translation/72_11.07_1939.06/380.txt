Article Title: 文壇使節 歸還報告, 皇軍慰問次 北支에 단여와서 (Report on the Return of the Literary Delegation, Having Visited North China to Console the Imperial Army)
Author: 朴英熙 金東仁 林學洙
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Roundtable Discussion)

Report on the Return of the Literary Delegation, Having Visited North China to Console the Imperial Army
						Pak Yŏnghŭi, Kim Tongin, Im Haksu
June 9th, 1:00 PM
At Kyŏngsŏng’s Yoda Grill

The three members of the literary delegation, dispatched jointly by the Korean literary world and publishing circles to console the Imperial Army officers and soldiers scattered across the North China front who have been fighting for two years bearing the fate of the fatherland <4>, departed Kyŏngsŏng Station on April 15th and completed their long journey’s mission, returning to Kyŏngsŏng on May 15th. Our company has invited the three gentlemen to hear their report from the ground and held this roundtable discussion.

I. North China Itinerary Record

Reporter: Thank you for traveling through areas like the Liaodong Peninsula, Shanhai Pass, and the Great Wall, which we only hear about in words, visiting North China, and consoling the loyal, brave, and fierce Imperial Army officers and soldiers. First, could you tell us about your travel itinerary?

Kim Tongin: Thanks to the generous monetary contributions from various individuals in the Korean literary and publishing circles for this great undertaking, and the various arrangements and guidance provided by the Army Command and the Government-General's Police Affairs Bureau, the three of us were able to safely complete our mission of consoling the Imperial Army and return. I wish to express my gratitude once again here for the kindness shown during that time.

Pak Yŏnghŭi: The travel itinerary was generally as instructed by the Army Command <5> and the Government-General when we left Seoul. We departed Kyŏngsŏng Station on April 18th and arrived at Beijing station exactly 37 hours later by train. The itinerary from Beijing was:
Beijing
Shijiazhuang
Yuci
Fenlin (?)
Yuncheng
Anyi
Taiyuan
Thus, we also visited Tongzhou, the seat of the infamous East Hebei Government due to the past incident, and then toured Lukouch'iao, the place where the current China Incident originated. Of course, the places listed here are all locations where we stayed for one or two nights. We would travel all day by car, arrive at the unit headquarters in the evening, meet the unit commander there, convey our message of consolation, see the local battlefield, return to our lodgings to sleep, and then leave the next day, taking a car again to the next destination, and so on... that's how it was.

Reporter: Did you not see the Yellow River?

Im Haksu: To see the Yellow River, we would have had to transfer from the Dongbao (?) line to the Jinghan line and travel quite a bit further, but we couldn't go that far.

Reporter: And the Great Wall?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: On the way there, we passed *Hai*ji*ji [unclear text] where the Great Wall *** but we intended to take time to see it on the way back, but unfortunately, the schedule was too tight, and we couldn't.

Reporter: Did you see Tianjin, said to rival Shanghai with its complex foreign concessions?

Kim Tongin: That too, due to the tight schedule, we couldn't sightsee the city and just passed through, looking out briefly from the car.

Pak Yŏnghŭi: Since we were making the trip anyway, our initial thought was to visit Fengtian and Hsinking, and if possible, even make a quick 'viewing flowers on horseback' style visit to Shanghai and Nanjing, but it didn't work out that way. There were truly many regrets about the itinerary.

Tales of the Imperial Army's Fierce Fighting and Deep Emotion

Reporter: What kinds of consolation goods did you bring for the Imperial Army?

Kim Tongin: We brought 1,000 packs of tobacco, 1,000 pieces each of caramel milk candy and chocolate. We also gave them fruits and sweets we acquired there. <6>

Reporter: Did you meet with high-ranking commanders at the unit commander level?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: Yes. We met high-ranking officers of general and field officer rank everywhere we went, starting with His Excellency the commander of the OO unit. Also, perhaps because prior notice had been given, several field-grade officers from the military side came out to meet us at each station to provide guidance, so we received very courteous and kind treatment.

Reporter: Due to military confidentiality, you can't say which general you met where and when, but could you perhaps share a representative story, like the circumstances surrounding meeting a certain Excellency at a certain location?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: We met His Excellency OO, the commander of the OO unit, at location OO. The three of us arrived there that evening, and guided by a staff officer, we met General OO at the headquarters. We conveyed the purpose of our visit, mentioning that currently in Korea, even children and common folk understand the noble meaning of this Holy War, and that everyone is praying for the continued good fortune in war of the many valiant soldiers dispatched to the front. After listening to the entire account, the general, with a very solemn expression, said—
"Thank you. Thank you for visiting our camp representing the collective will of the intellectuals of the Peninsula behind the lines. Please do your utmost to convey to the masses that this incident is a Holy War for the construction of a New East Asia. Since you gentlemen are particularly active in the field of writing, I especially want to ask this of you, and I hope you will observe the war situation here thoroughly, return, and serve the nation greatly. Furthermore, please convey our deep gratitude for the passionate send-off given by the people of the Peninsula when our many officers and soldiers passed through Korea on their way to the front." That's what he said.

Reporter: Did you not meet Army Commander Sugiyama in Beijing?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: We did not meet him. However, among the various unit commanders we met, one who left an unforgettable impression was Lieutenant Colonel OO whom we met at OO; he was a Waseda University graduate. He was a truly fine person. We spent two nights drinking heavily with him, and the Lieutenant Colonel was overjoyed, saying he had finally met "real Koreans." When we were leaving, he deliberately came out to the station, and even outside of his duties, among those we met, we truly encountered many fine individuals.

Kim Tongin: Even the officers and soldiers we met briefly while traveling were truly kind when they heard about our mission. They would even bring us fruit and ask about Korea ** <7> ** stories, generally seeming innocent and passionate. The soldiers at the front!

The Hardships of Officers and Soldiers and Chinese Female Prisoners

Reporter: Since you went to the front line, did you hear cannon fire or bomb explosions? Or witness hand-to-hand combat between Japanese and Chinese troops?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: Generally, in North China now, large-scale trench warfare seems rare, and mopping-up operations against remnant soldiers are common. Therefore, because these are encounter battles, they are not easily witnessed. We just heard the incessant sounds of gunfire and artillery from a distance several times.

Reporter: It would have been a story if you had come back with an honorable wound, haha— Did you experience trench life? Even for just 2 or 3 days?

Kim Tongin: We did not.

Reporter: Did you see Chinese regular troops? What are they like, in terms of equipment or combat capability?

Kim Tongin: We didn't see them.

Pak Yŏnghŭi: Although we didn't see regular troops, we saw 17 Chinese prisoners of war, among whom were two women. I tried communicating with them through writing (筆談). These women were not part of a women's corps (娘子軍) that directly participated in combat, but were captured while working as nurses in a Chinese army field hospital. Both had bobbed hair; one was 19 and the other 17, young women.
The nineteen-year-old had finished elementary school and possessed some education and awareness. After coming over to our army's side, she even married a Chinese man from her own country and is now active as a member of the women's pacification team. The other, slightly younger girl, when talking with me, still showed a child-like heart thinking of her old hometown and parents, saying things like, "Last night in my dream, I saw my mother," "I saw my older brother coming in smiling," and "The peach tree by the well must be in full bloom now." However, day by day, her sorrowful heart seemed to lessen, and eventually, she became cheerful, laughing and talking.

Reporter: Did you see any "plainclothes units" (便衣隊)?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: We didn't see them. Since it's a war zone, they likely appear and disappear, but we didn't see them. Besides this, the stories generally pertain to military secrets, so please don't ask further.

Im Haksu: Let me tell you about one impactful thing from the front line.
When we arrived at OO station, the train <8> made an unscheduled stop due to the situation at the front, and we waited for about 6 hours. By the time we finally boarded the train again and departed, it was dusky twilight. Outside the window was truly a vast plain stretching for ten thousand li. There were no houses, just an endlessly connected, desolate field, and the night was growing darker. We didn't know where enemy soldiers might be hiding. In this quiet wilderness, looking out the train window by chance, I saw a single sentry standing tall on a nearby mountaintop with a bayonet fixed to his rifle tip. He was looking towards our train window. In this vast foreign land, in this desolate field, a lone figure, without a shred of fear, standing guard to protect this position. My heart suddenly grew warm.

Seeing Beijing City

Reporter: Now let's hear about Beijing. How many Koreans are there in Beijing after the incident?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: They say there are over ten thousand. In Shijiazhuang too, they say there are 3,000-4,000 Koreans. According to the Ōsaka Mainichi (?) regarding the number of Japanese mainlanders in North China, it was roughly like this:
Beijing 35,000
Shijiazhuang 9,600
Qingdao 27,000
Jinan 10,000
Xuzhou 2,000
Kaifeng 1,400
Xinxiang 2,000
Tianjin 400 (?) [likely a typo in original, Tianjin had many more Japanese]

Reporter: How are Beijing's cultural institutions?

Pak Yŏnghűi: Since our mission this time was a special one, we didn't pay much attention to matters other than consoling the Imperial Army, nor did we intend to. Therefore, we don't know much about the situation in areas like culture and industry. We didn't even visit a single university or library. However, we did stop by a bookstore (冊肆) on the street, and it seemed several specialized magazines covering politics, literature, and social affairs are published monthly, and there were even specialized monthly magazines for film, theater, and music.

Reporter: We hear that many cultural figures are in Beijing, such as Mr. Chu Yosŏp, who is said to be a professor at some university, Mr. Kim Hwan and Mr. Sin Illyong, former writers from the old "Creation" (創造) era, and Mr. Han P'oryong, formerly the political editor of the Chosŏn Ilbo. Did you meet them? How are they all doing? <9>

Kim Tongin: Mr. Sin Illyong runs a printing press employing about 80 workers; Pak Yŏnghŭi happened to meet him on a train. Kim Hwan runs a traditional medicine shop in Beijing. We didn't meet the others.

Reporter: Are there any cultural institutions run by Koreans?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: I don't know for sure. It seemed like all the newspaper branch offices were there. Important magazines published in Seoul also seemed to be available—

Reporter: Did you visit the Korean People's Association (朝鮮人民會)? Is it very active?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: We heard it has become very active since the incident.

Reporter: Is Beijing a highly modernized city?

Kim Tongin: As the center of Central Plains culture for centuries, not to mention the 260 years of the Qing Dynasty, it goes without saying that its civilization and institutions are immense, bustling, and splendid. The city itself is laid out on a plain, and it's incredibly vast. The streets are all densely covered with trees like acacia and willow—the term 'sea of trees' (樹海) is truly fitting.
And the scale is immense! The old palace sites, or the Summer Palace (萬壽山), their grand structure and majestic atmosphere are astonishing coming from Seoul.

Pak Yŏnghŭi: The grandeur of the ancient capital has already been introduced through the words and writings of many who have visited Beijing, so we'll keep it brief. Through the courtesy of the military, we rode in a military vehicle to see the Summer Palace where Empress Dowager Cixi (西太后) used to stroll, and the Forbidden City (紫禁城), and especially the vast pond called Beihai (北海). The overwhelming impression was simply that it is an incredibly massive place.
One surprising thing was the Beijing students. Although not all were in Western clothes, they wore neat, short-sleeved Chinese outfits and, like American female students, mostly commuted by bicycle, which looked fresh and cheerful. And they all had bobbed hair. However, for some reason, apart from these 'new women,' one rarely saw women on the streets. The buildings on the streets were generally not very tall; high-rise buildings were scarce. You couldn't see anything like the Marunouchi Building in front of Tōkyō Station. Perhaps there are many in the foreign legation quarter—

What are the Prices Like in North China?

Reporter: Are prices higher than in Korea?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: Leather goods were cheap, but food items seemed expensive. Things like shoes, bags, and cameras were cheap. <10>

Reporter: What about inn and meal costs?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: In Beijing, we stayed at a first-class inn called the "Yamato Hoteru" (大和ホテル), which cost around 4 or 5 yen. Other Japanese-run inns in more remote areas were generally about 3 or 4 yen per night, but in a place like Yuncheng, even a modest inn charged 5 yen.

Kim Tongin: Beijing prices also seemed higher for Japanese mainlanders and Koreans, but cheaper for transactions among the Chinese themselves.
We visited the Dong'an Market, and it was truly spectacular. And the low cost of rickshaws was surprising. For short distances, it's 5 or 10 sen, and even for longer distances, 20 sen would get you anywhere.

Reporter: What do Koreans do for a living there?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: Most are illicit traders/profiteers (不正業者), dealing in things like opium smuggling or the restaurant business. While the environmental circumstances they find themselves in are likely a major cause, this results in the low social standing (低) of Koreans there. The local Chinese residents don't readily trust them—
Although there are a few individuals who reportedly made tens or hundreds of thousands of won before and after the incident, their number is small, and the vast majority seem to make a living through illicit means. A surprising thing everywhere we went was the large number of Korean women, a 'women's corps' (娘子軍) of sorts, scattered throughout the front-line areas. On the train, you'd see them sitting on one side with just a small trunk, drifting like water weeds, heading to this region today and that region tomorrow.

Reporter: Which provincial origins are most common among the Koreans scattered in North China?

Kim Tongin: North and South P'yŏngan Provinces. Just like people from Kyŏngsang and Chŏlla provinces are numerous in Tōkyō and Ōsaka—it's likely related to geography.

Reporter: Are there no Korean pacification team members or police officers?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: I heard there are some active as pacification team members. Speaking of police, when we got off at Beijing station, there was a police officer in uniform, but with bobbed hair! So I asked a military police officer (憲兵) what that was, and he said it was a female police officer. They mainly seemed to search female passengers getting on and off at the station.

Reporter: Do they still conduct body searches?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: Everyone goes through it. When you get off at the station, that's usually the first thing that happens. Right away, they separate people, saying "Japanese over here (日本人は こちらへ)," "Chinese over there (支那人は あちらへ)," and search their bodies. Koreans go to the "Japanese over here" side, where the search isn't too intense, but Chinese people must all <11> attach a "good citizen certificate" (良民證) to their clothes, and those without the certificate are subjected to a stricter search.

Reporter: Finishing your month-long journey, what feelings did you have on the train coming back?

Kim Tongin: Even though we only toured a part of China, North China—and within that, mainly several cities centered around Beijing, in a cursory 'viewing flowers on horseback' manner—what satisfactory observations could we have made with our limited prior knowledge? But generally, the feeling I got is that China is an enormous entity.
Out on the streets, you can find ultramodern women who wouldn't be out of place in the most cutting-edge cities like Paris or New York, while there are also old-fashioned women still secluded indoors, straight out of the Four Books and Five Classics. Buildings too—on one side, there might be a 'building' (뻴딩), while on the other, the old systems adhered to for 3,000-4,000 years remain. Civilization is the same. There's a dullness that lets Western powers encroach for decades, yet on the other side, various movements arise—it's just like a vast ocean. Civilizations and peoples from all sorts of countries enter and mingle within it; that's what China seemed to be.

Pak Yŏnghŭi: At a certain place on the front, there was a graveyard for soldiers fallen in battle, so we went to pay our respects. It was nighttime, inside a large, empty temple-like building, where the spirits, represented by wooden memorial tablets (木碑) inscribed with names, were enshrined. Seeing them, my head bowed once more in respect for the loyalty and bravery of those who were martyred (殉) in defense of the fatherland. And what I felt on the ground was admiration for the soldiers' truly strong sense of responsibility, never neglecting their duties in the slightest. Besides that, there are countless inspiring tales of loyalty and valor heard on the battlefield, but let's save those for another occasion.

Im Haksu: As I mentioned earlier, seeing that lone sentry standing guard over the battlefield in the vast wilderness as the curtain of night was falling made me reflect on whether our service as subjects on the home front (銃後臣民) is still insufficient. Also, witnessing scenes of our army's soldiers genuinely protecting and caring for Chinese civilians was truly moving.

Reporter: How will the three of you convey the powerful emotions from the war zone to the subjects on the home front?

Pak Yŏnghŭi: Well... I'm thinking of writing a book. A travelogue format might be a bit stiff, so I'm considering creating characters and writing it in a novel format. I'm currently organizing the materials. I'm also re-reading "Wheat and Soldiers" (麥과 兵隊).

Kim Tongin: I plan to write a novel. <12>

Im Haksu: I am currently writing with the intention of publishing a collection of poetry.

Reporter: Then, while we await your respective works, let's conclude here for now. You must be tired from your long journey, thank you for speaking with us for so long. -End- <13>

**Summary**

This 1939 roundtable discussion features three prominent Korean writers—Pak Yŏnghŭi, Kim Tongin, and Im Haksu—reporting on their recent month-long 'consolation mission' to North China, sponsored by Korean literary circles and Japanese authorities, to visit Japanese soldiers fighting in the Sino-Japanese War. They describe their itinerary, meetings with Japanese military commanders (expressing admiration), distribution of comfort items, and impressions of the war front, Beijing (noting its scale, Korean population issues), and the perceived dedication of Imperial soldiers, contrasting it with the 'backwardness' or complexity of China. They express gratitude for Japanese support and plan to produce pro-war literary works (novels, poetry) based on their experiences, reinforcing the "Holy War" narrative.

Historically, this article is revealing as it documents the active participation of leading Korean intellectuals in Japanese imperial propaganda during the colonial period. It showcases the mechanism of using cultural figures for war mobilization ('consolation missions') and provides a contemporary, albeit heavily biased, glimpse into occupied North China, the lives of Japanese soldiers, and the situation of the Korean diaspora, highlighting the complex and often coerced collaboration that occurred under colonial rule. The writers' uncritical adoption of Japanese war rhetoric ("Holy War," "New East Asia") and their plans to create literature supporting it are particularly noteworthy for historians studying colonial ideology and cultural politics.
