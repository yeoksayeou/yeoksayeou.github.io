An Actress's Stage Diary
                        Theatre Research Troupe, Kim Pok-chin

In Place of My Body

"Tomyŏn, you really worked hard today, didn't you? Hurry, lie down and rest comfortably. Stretch out your arms and legs and dream of finding your mother."

Sister,
Do you know who you're talking to! For three days starting today, I am Tomyŏn (道念), a boy of only 14 years.
In other words, as an actor on stage who embodies the character (Tomyŏn) from the play into reality, for these three days, I am the boy named Tomyŏn.
So now, having returned from the Pumingwan (府民舘) and lying down, I'm thinking about the hard work and sweat on stage.

Sister!
I think this is the first time I've gone on stage with such an anxious heart for a play. Of course, any time a character from a play replaces my body to express their personality and all their circumstances, <200>
I feel extremely anxious until the play ends, and it's extremely difficult to achieve success as a result. But with this Tomyŏn, I wonder to what extent I can substitute for him; a greater anxiety than before burns within me.

Sister,
At times like this, I feel the desire for my body to be more suitable even more keenly.

Sister,
I really had a hard time today. Trying to substitute for a 14-year-old boy at my age, how could I not break out in a sweat?
On top of that, due to insufficient rehearsal days, the curtain rose before I could completely transfer Tomyŏn into my mind.

Sister,
Thinking about my appearance makes me laugh anyway, and it's killing me when girlish traits emerge when I should be boyish. During the play's progress,
"What is that used for? That!" I said, and my words just stopped.

Sister,
It's truly awful at times like that. Wouldn't beads of sweat run down my forehead?
I barely remembered, so the audience didn't notice, but Tomyŏn had a tough time.

"Tomyŏn, how did you become an abandoned child at a temple, longing so much for the secular world and wanting to see your mother, <201> becoming such a pitiful boy? And why do you make me, who substitutes for you, sweat so much?"

Sister,
After barely managing to say the forgotten lines, I find myself murmuring these words. If you had seen my state this time, Sister, you would have found it terribly funny, wouldn't you?

Sister,
However, alongside such hardships, there is the joy for an actor of running freely before thousands of audience members, being able to express joy and sorrow at will, impressing them, and giving them pleasure.
When I feel this joy,
"Why do you do plays that only make you tired day and night?"
I really want to put you, Sister, who used to say that, right up on the stage.
Sometimes as an old woman, sometimes as a boy like this—it's something unimaginable outside of the stage.

Sister,
Lastly, I think once more of what "Henry Irving (헨리, 아-빙)" said: "The actor's task is first to express what the writer conceived in their mind, giving it form, character, color, and life, thereby inducing the audience, as much as possible, to feel as if they are watching reality." And Tomyŏn! I will try harder to express this Tomyŏn as the Tomyŏn conceived in the writer's mind.           Night of April 3rd, Respectfully, Tomyŏn       <202>

Record of The Pirate and Carmen
Central Stage, Yi Paek-hŭi

November __
Before the fatigue from the performance tour in the Western Chosŏn (西鮮) region had even faded, a plan for another performance tour in Southern Chosŏn (南鮮) was announced by the planning department. I'm surely not the only one who misses the Pumingwan stage. It's not that we lack effort or passion, but reality is too cold towards our art. After expressing agreement at the general meeting regarding the Western Chosŏn tour, I returned home, thinking about the agony of the day I would have to deceive my sick father again and leave for the South. At night, I entered a certain teahouse and cast all distracting thoughts into the serene music.

November __
Practicing *The Pirate* and *Carmen*. Many comrades are submerged in gloom due to the lack of preparation funds. Mr. N, angered by the listless rehearsal, passionately spoke about the significance of regional performances and reprimanded the artistic conscience of several comrades. That's right. Art possesses a colossal educational tower. Its audience is always the masses. Wherever these masses are, one must devote all passion.

November __
Departure day. We leave by the night train, but my heart has been tense since dawn, and I wake up early. <203> I thought it miraculous (神房한 일) that I, who always got scolded for sleeping late, woke up so early. That's right. This must also be a lesson learned from collective life. I now feel anew the common sense that one must regard one's duties seriously. Avoiding the eyes of my elderly father lying in the next room, I pack clothes and cosmetics into a trunk (튜령크), hide it in a corner of the kitchen (부억), and ask my only sympathizer, my older sister, to hand it over the plank wall (板張) at night. Ah, the agony of a traitor! The solace in my life is only the determination to love art and dedicate my life to art. Peeking at my father through a crack in the door, I leave the house. Father's groans echo in my ears, making me halt my steps. Resenting the environment that tries to cool my passion, I enter the practice room. As it's partial rehearsal for the newly assigned role, I mend torn clothes as ordered by the Costume Department. But the work didn't go well. I couldn't even nurse my father, and moreover, I can't judge whether this path, which I swore several times to quit, is bad or good for me. My heart's passion is too hot to compromise, and my parents are too pitiful to rebel against. However, harmonizing these two things lies only in my current passion—this, I think, is in adhering to my path to the very end. It's departure time. I order dinner with several comrades and eat, then go home. Still unable to bid farewell to Father, I knock on the plank wall (板張), call my sister, take the trunk (튜령크), and leave the house. Sister, don't curse me! If you curse me, my heart aches too much. People think actresses are just like delinquent girls (불량소녀), but we are part of a group striving to create our own world, even while receiving this constraint. Lacking time, I emptied my poor pockets to take a car to the station. The person in charge of leading the group (率引者), Mr. S, became furious and reprimanded me. Comrade, reprimand me to your heart's content. But I will continue to smile before you comrades until the end. The departing train—ah! I too must be this <204> strong. I must not wander in darkness but break through the darkness. Midnight must have passed; the inside of the train is quiet. Only the tired breathing of the comrades, struggling for their duties amidst poverty, can be heard. Their dreams might already be running on the stage that will open in the provinces tomorrow.
Tomorrow, the stage, our lifeblood, awaits.
What will be written in this diary starting tomorrow! On the train bound for Mokp'o (木浦). Writing this diary in the sleepless train car. <205>

**Summary**

These two diary entries from 1939 provide intimate glimpses into the lives of Korean actresses during the colonial period. Kim Pok-chin reflects on the artistic challenge and anxiety of embodying a 14-year-old boy character, Tomyŏn, stressing the mental and physical effort required but also the unique joy derived from stage performance. Yi Paek-hŭi describes the emotional turmoil of balancing her passion for theatre with familial obligations (especially deceiving her sick father) and societal prejudice against actresses, while preparing for a financially strained regional tour with her troupe, emphasizing the commitment to bringing art to the masses despite hardships. From a historical perspective, the articles vividly illustrate the constraints faced by female artists (familial disapproval, societal judgment), the economic precariousness of theatre troupes, the demanding nature of performance schedules including regional tours (mentioning Sŏsŏn and Namsŏn), and the underlying dedication to art as a form of expression and connection with the public ("masses") within the context of colonial Korea. The mention of Pumingwan (a major venue) and specific plays grounds the experiences.

나라는 肉體를 代身하여
「道念아 오늘 참 애썼지? 어서 자리에 누워서 편이 좀 쉬여라. 팔다리를 *-뺏고 엄마 찾을 꿈이나 꾸어라」
언니
이게 누구더러 하는 소린지 아세요! 난 오늘부터 3일간은 道念이란 14세밖게 안된 소년입니다.
즉 말하자면 희곡중에 인물(道念)을 현실에다 形象化시히는 무대에 연기자로서 이 3일간은 내가 道念이란 소년이란 말입니다.
그래 지금 府民舘으로부터 도라와서 자리에 누먼서 무대에서 진땀 빼든 일을 생각하고 하는 소립니다.
언니!
난 이번 연극처럼 불안스런 맘으로 무대에 오르긴 첨인가 봐요. 그야 어느 때든지 희곡 중 인물이 나란 육체를 대신해서 자기의 성격과 모든 사정을 표현식히느니 만큼 연극이＜200＞
끝나는 날까지 몹시 불안을 품게되고 그 결과가 성공을 보긴 극히 어려운 일입니다 만은 이번 道念이만은 어느 정도까저 대신할 수 있을른지 전보다 더 큰 불안이 내 속을 태워 줌니다.
언니,
내 몸이 좀 더 具備했으면 하는 욕망이 이럴 땐 더 느껴짐니다.
언니,
오늘은 정말 혼낫담니다. 글세 내 나이에 14세 소년을 대신하려니 진땀이 안나겠어요.
게다가 연습 日字가 부족해서 道念이를 완전이 내 맘에다 옴겨 노치도 못한 채 막이 오르게 됏군요.
언니,
글세 갓득이나 내 꼴을 생각하면 우숨이 나오고 소년다워야 할 때 소녀 틔가 나와서 죽겠는데 연극 진행 중에
「엇다 쓰는 게 머예요. 저!」하곤 말문이 꼭 맥켰군요.
언니,
그럴 땐 참 죽겠군요. 이마에 구슬 같은 땀이 쭉 흐르지 안켔서요.
겨우 생각이 나서 관객은 몰랐음니다만 道念이 혼났죠.
「道念아 너는 엇쩌다 절깐에 버림받은 몸이 되여서 그다지도 속세를 그리워하고 어머니를＜201＞ 보고싶어 하는 가엽슨 소년이 됏드란 말이냐. 그리고 너를 대신하는 나까지 땀을 빼게 하느냔 말이다.」
언니,
이저버렸든 말끝을 생각해서 겨우 하고나니 이런 말이 중얼거려지는군요. 언니가 이번 내 꼴을 보섯드면 끔직이 우스실 걸 그랬죠.
언니,
그러나 이러한 苦心이 있는 반면엔 數千 관객 앞에서 자유로 날뛰고 깃뿜과 슬픔을 맘대로 표현할 수 있고 그들을 감명도 주며 쾌감도 주는 그런 기뿜이 연기자에겐 있담니다.
이런 기뿜이 있을 땐
「밤났 고단만 하게 연극은 왜 하니?」
하든 언니를 무대에다 꼭 올려 놔 듸리고 싶어저요.
어떤 땐 할머니로 어떤 땐 이런 소년으로 무대가 아니고는 꿈도 꾸기 어려운 일입니다.
언니,
끝으로 「배우의 할 일이란 첫재 작가가 머리 속에 생각한 것을 표현하야 여기에 形象과 성질과 색채와 생명을 부여하야 연극을 보는 사람으로 하여곰 될 수 있는 대로 현실을 보는 것과 갓다는 관념을 가지게 하도록 誘致하는 데 있다」고 「헨리, 아-빙」이 말한 것을 다시 한번 생각하고 道念! 이 道念을 작가가 머릿속에 생각한 道念으로 표현해 보기에 더욱 노력해 보렵니다.           4월 3일 밤  道念 올님       ＜202＞

海賊과 카르멘記
中央舞臺 李白姬

11월  일
西鮮地方 공연의 旅勞가 풀니기도 전에 또 南鮮 공연을 하라는 案이 기획부에서 발표되였다. 府民舘 무대를 그리워하는 것은 오직 나 하나 뿐이 아닐 것이다. 노력이 업고 정열이 없는 것은 아니나 현실은 우리의 예술의 대하야 너무나 차다. 西鮮 공연의 대한 전체회의의 찬성을 표하고 도라오며 병석에 게신 아버님을 또 속히고 南으로 떠나야 할 그날의 괴로움을 생각했다. 밤엔 어는 다방에 드러가 모-든 잡념을 고요한 음악 속에 던지다.
11월  일
