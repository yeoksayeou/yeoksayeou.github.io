**Body Text Translation:**
Impression of Prince De

From morning, the day was cloudy and the wind unusually cold, making me dread going outside. I stayed hunched up in my room all day, not seeing the sunlight, until the sun set. When it reached 5 or 6 PM, the thought of the rickshaw that came daily felt dreadful.

At the unfailing appointment time, I went to Myŏngwŏlgwan, curious about who the guest would be today.

Unexpectedly, it was said to be a welcoming banquet for Prince De (德王) of Monggo (蒙古). Eager to see this Prince De, I whispered with two or three companions (동모) as we waited. Unexpectedly, Prince De had his hair braided down like plaited hemp, tied firmly with a blue ribbon, wore a sword at his waist, and dangled jade treasures, looking like someone from ancient times, which made him seem even more solemn.
I truly hadn't thought that in this day and age, Prince De would still be wearing his hair braided.

There, to introduce Chosŏn entertainment, we danced and sang songs. Prince De, with a dignified posture, sat with his arms crossed, silently <222> watching intently for a while. Then, with a pleased expression, he remarked that the sounds and customs of Korean language were similar to those of China (支那) and Monggo.
It felt like listening to some old history, and I felt an even greater sense of closeness.

When I raised my hand to request a handshake, he also raised his hand and shook it warmly. I then took a sheet of drawing paper (敷紙), dipped a brush in ink, and requested his signature (싸인). He did not refuse and wrote the character for blessing/fortune (福字), adding kindly, "This means 'may you have blessings'," even providing the interpretation, for which I didn't know how grateful and honored I felt.
Feeling deeply honored, I took it back, hung it on the wall of my lonely room, and pictured Prince De's braided hair hanging heavily and his dignified posture, his refined impression.
Ah – he was indeed a great man.

Drunkard

On a night when snow fell heavily, at two AM. Three companions (동무) were walking together down a desolate main street, talking animatedly amongst themselves. On the way, from an alley, a young man, heavily (갑북) drunk, unable to keep his balance, came staggering (빗척빗척) out as if stumbling (떼나쓸듯이), bumped right into a utility pole, and fell onto the snow. He just lay there face down, like a large sick beast (즘성).

My two companions remarked, based on something like experience or secret knowledge, that men who drink too much (好酒) don't know how to love women. That statement is not without reason. The drunkards we associate with every night are indeed too ignorant (無智) and pitiful. However, on the snow where he fell, bright red oranges spilled out from his pocket.
Ah – what a good-hearted man he must be. Even when completely (억망) drunk, if he puts even just one in his pocket and goes back home, how much affection and harmony must there be in that household? Such a sincere, affectionate, and good-hearted man might not be common. <223>

**Summary**

The author, Kim Suk, identified as a "literary kisaeng," recounts two distinct experiences. First, she describes attending a banquet at Myŏngwŏlgwan for Prince De of Mongolia, noting his traditional appearance (braided hair, traditional attire) which surprised her, his dignified manner, and his comment on the similarity between Korean, Chinese, and Mongolian customs after watching Korean entertainment. She felt honored to receive his autograph, a character for "Blessing." The second anecdote contrasts this formal encounter with a scene on a snowy night where she and her companions observe a heavily intoxicated young man fall, spilling oranges from his pocket, which leads her to reflect on his presumed hidden kindness and faithfulness to his home, contrasting him with the usual drunkards she encounters. From a historical perspective, the article offers a valuable glimpse into the life and observations of a kisaeng during the colonial period, her interaction with a significant political figure (Prince De, involved in Japanese-Mongolian affairs), the setting of high-class entertainment venues like Myŏngwŏlgwan, and her social commentary reflecting on human nature observed both in elite circles and on the street.

德王의 印象
아츰부터 날이 흐리고 유난이 바람이 차서 밖았을 나가기 두려워하고 종일토록 내 방 아래 꿀혀서 해빛을 못 보고 해가 저물었다. 오후 5, 6시가 되면 매일같이 오는 인력거가 끔직하니 생각된다.
어김이 없는 약속 시간에 나는 明月館에를 가서 오늘은 또 손님이 누구인가 하고 궁금하였다.
의외 蒙古 德王 환영 연회라 하여 어듸 그 德王을 좀 보고 싶은 생각에 2, 3 동모와 수근대며 기다렸다. 뜻밖게 德王은 삼딴 같은 머리를 따 내리우고 청띄를 질근 띄고, 환두 차고 옥보물을 주렁주렁 달고 그 무슨 오랜 옛사람을 보는 것 같어야 더욱 엄숙해 보인다.
설마 지금 세상에 德王이 아직도 머리를 땃코 있으리라고는 생각지 않었다.
거기서 朝鮮 여흥을 소개하기 위하여 춤도 추고 노래도 불넜다. 德王은 점잔은 태도에 팔장을 딱 끼고 앉어 무언으로＜222＞ 한참 심각히 보드니 조선말 소리와 풍속은 支那 蒙古에 비슷하다고 반가운 낯을 띄우고 말하였다.
나는 무슨 옛날 역사나 듯는 것 같어여 한층 더 친절미가 생겼다.
내가 손을 들어 악수를 청한즉 같이 손을 들어 반갑게 악수를 하였다. 나는 敷紙 한 장과 붓에 먹을 찍어 들고 싸인을 또 청하였으나 그는 거절치 않고 福字 한 자를 써서 주며 이것은 복이 있으란 말이다 하고 다정히
통역까지를 하여주는 것이 얼마나 고마웁고 황송한지 모르겠다.
나는 황송한 마음에 바다가지고 와서 쓸쓸한 내 방 벽에다 걸어놓고 그 충충 따 내린 德王의 머리와 그 점잔은 태도에 단아한 인상을 그려본다.
아- 그는 과연 위인이더라.

醉客
눈이 펄펄 날이는 밤 두시. 쓸쓸한 큰 거리를 세 동무가 작반하여 세 입을 모아 자미있게 떠들며 돌아오는 도중 어떤 골목에서 물이 갑북 취한 청년이 몸을 간우지 못하고 빗척빗척 떼나쓸듯이 나오다가 電柱에 딱 부딧고 눈 우에 너머저 맞치 병드른 큰 즘성같이 업흐러진 체 다만 업드렸다.

두 동모는 남자가 너머 好酒하면 여자를 사랑할 줄 모른다고 경험담 비슷 비결을 말하였다. 그 말도 무리는 아니다. 우리가 每夜 친하는 취객은 넘우도 無智하니까 가없으니까. 그러나 그가 너머진 눈 우에는 포켙트에서 샛빩안 밀감이 주루룰 헤여젔다.
아- 얼마나 선량한 남성이냐. 억망 취하야도 다만 한 개라도 주머니에 너어 가지고 집에 도라가면 그 가정은 얼마나 정이 있는 화목할 것인가. 고만한 진실과 애정 있는 선량한 남성이 그리 흔한 일이 안이겠다.＜223＞
