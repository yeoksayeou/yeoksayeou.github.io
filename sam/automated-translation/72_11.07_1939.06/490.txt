**Summary**

This 1939 Korean scenario depicts the struggles of a poor couple, Hyŏnbo and Suni, living in the mountains and making charcoal for a living under Japanese colonial rule. Hyŏnbo faces accusations of illegal logging, likely instigated by the corrupt Forest Manager Kim Sang, who desires Suni. While Hyŏnbo is imprisoned, Suni endures hardship, fending off Kim Sang's advances and the temptations offered by Ch'ilsŏng, a seemingly prosperous miner. Suni almost leaves with Ch'ilsŏng but ultimately chooses to return, reaffirming her faith in the village shrine (Sŏnghwangdang) and her bond with Hyŏnbo upon his release. The story concludes with the couple praying together at the shrine for a better future, specifically for a son.

From a historical perspective, the scenario vividly portrays the economic pressures and social anxieties of rural Koreans during the late 1930s Japanese colonial period. The mention of rising prices, the "Emergency Period" (非常時, referring to wartime mobilization), oppressive forestry regulations enforced by figures like Kim Sang, and the threat of arbitrary imprisonment highlight the harsh realities faced by ordinary people. The contrast between the traditional, arduous life of charcoal burning (Hyŏnbo) and the potentially more lucrative but unstable life of mining (Ch'ilsŏng) reflects broader economic shifts. The central role of the Sŏnghwangdang underscores the persistence of traditional folk beliefs as a source of solace and hope amidst colonial modernity and hardship. Historians might find the depiction of colonial power dynamics (Kim Sang vs. villagers), economic vulnerability, gender relations under stress, and the blend of traditional faith with modern anxieties particularly interesting.
