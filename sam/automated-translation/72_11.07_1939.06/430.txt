「朝鮮文壇使節」特輯, 北支戰線에 皇軍慰問 떠남에 際하야
						金東仁 외

Heading Towards the North China Front
Kim Tongin

Since last autumn, there has been an opinion within the Chosŏn literary world that someone should be dispatched to the China front. The idea was to dispatch someone so that the Chosŏn literary circle could console the officers and soldiers at the front, while also attempting to foster war literature in Chosŏn. Holding such an opinion, I sometimes expressed it verbally.
Once spoken, I found that surprisingly many people shared the same sentiment. No, almost everyone held the same opinion. They just hadn't expressed it verbally.
Why didn't they express it? The answer is simple. First, money. Travel expenses were uncertain. It was a time when all prices were soaring, *******[unclear text]**, printing costs more than doubled, leading to a publishing depression, and the pockets of literary figures were quite empty.
Second, even if one had such an opinion, they couldn't speak recklessly because they didn't know what opinions others held.
For these reasons, although we mutually considered observing the front lines meaningful, we hadn't yet put it into practice.
However, publishers, who have an inseparable relationship with the literary world, also shared the same opinion, and the issue rapidly took concrete shape. Publishers decided to provide the travel expenses, and the literary world would provide the people. Thus, through the cooperation of the literary world and publishers, a front-line observation group was formed, and I consider it an honor that even an incompetent person like myself became one of those dispatched. <232>
It is embarrassing that we, who have not received military training and are completely ignorant of military matters, know nothing about the military aspects of this Holy War. We only wish to help, even slightly, in carrying out this great undertaking, not limited to military action, using the education we have received.
So, what is this great undertaking? It's awkward to explain it anew now, so I'll refrain, but in short: the Yamato race, the Chosŏn race, the Manchu race, and the Chinese race—peoples with yellowish-brown skin and not particularly tall stature—should achieve Great Unity. While it would be most ideal for the entire world to become one bloc and enjoy peace, since that remains merely an ideal theory with no visible possibility of realization, we should at least secure peace among related peoples. Let's stop being puppets manipulated by unrelated peoples. Consequently, any individuals or groups holding ideas contrary to the above are, so to speak, our common enemy, even if they belong to our related peoples. Being enemies, they must naturally be annihilated—this must be the true intention and purpose of this Holy War. Therefore, this Holy War is more a political operation than a military action. Moving troops is a necessary action to carry out this political operation.
Unfortunately, this great movement of the yellow races is not thoroughly known to the Chosŏn populace. Even newspapers distributed to every nook and cranny of the provinces only report brief details like "somewhere was occupied" or "the enemy forces were annihilated." Barely, urban intellectuals can imagine the situation through magazines coming from the Tokyo direction. Those who do not know the national language (most people, excluding urban dwellers) and those living where magazines published in the Mainland (內地) are not sold are entirely unaware of the true significance of this Holy War and the hardships of the soldiers carrying it out.
The grave mission and duty to report this to the Chosŏn populace and awaken them from their ignorance (蒙) rests upon the shoulders of us Chosŏn literary figures. Thinking about it, I lack the confidence to bear such a great mission. I accepted it because it was such a joyful and glorious duty, but as the departure date approaches, I feel the gravity of the mission accelerating.
Amidst the difficulty, the single joy is being able to go to the battlefield soon and see firsthand the hardships of the officers and soldiers, whom I have only known through writing (文面).
I am incompetent. And a blind man with open eyes. What I will see and report upon my return will likely not convey even one-hundredth of the events unfolding in China.
However, I can boast that even my report, conveying less than one ten-thousandth of the current situation, will increase the understanding (which is extremely meager) that the people in the Chosŏn provinces have of this Holy War by a hundredfold—that is how ignorant the people in the Chosŏn provinces have been regarding this Holy War. The military authorities and the Government-General (總府府) authorities strongly supported this endeavor because they expected this effect. <233>
Even if the results fall short of expectations, please believe me when I say it will be due to our incompetence, never a lack of sincerity.

War and the Duty of the Writer
Pak Yŏnghŭi

Since the outbreak of the Sino-Japanese Incident (日支事變), the interest of the Chosŏn populace in the current situation has been immense. As it became a long-term war and long-term construction, our interest became extraordinary, and we came to understand the fundamental spirit of the Japanese Empire in this incident, striving towards establishing eternal peace in the East.
For that reason, not only the general populace but also former nationalists and communists all declared their conversion (전향), united, and keenly felt their duty as Imperial Subjects (皇民).
From then on, Chosŏn people resolved to firmly protect the home front (銃後), and on the other hand, expressed gratitude to the Imperial Army (皇軍) by sending comfort goods (慰向品), visiting the front lines, or sending letters of encouragement.
However, unfortunately, the literary world had not yet undertaken any related activities. This was because the Chosŏn literary world lacked an organization capable of organic coordination.
In the Mainland (內地), writers were dispatched to the front lines, creating literary works for the populace to read, and works of war literature frequently emerged. The Chosŏn literary world and writers envied this and wished to do the same but were unable to execute it.
Therefore, initiated by prominent figures in the literary world, the consolation of the Imperial Army and observation of the front lines were undertaken. We cannot forget the deep understanding and enthusiastic support of the military and police authorities. The kindness shown to us by the authorities is truly moving beyond words.
The Chosŏn literary world has become very vibrant, and writers are tense with talk about the Imperial Army consolation event. Everyone is pleased.
Many people were pessimistic, saying that war literature could not exist in the Chosŏn literary world. That's understandable. We have never seen war, nor can we see it.
However, the dispatch of Chosŏn writers to the front lines is not limited to this instance, and I believe it will continue depending on the results of this trip. In that sense, one could say that war literature can exist in the Chosŏn literary world. However, it is questionable whether I, lacking talent (菲才), having been selected for this task, can fulfill <234> this heavy responsibility. I only have conviction—it is certain that excellent works will emerge from the talented pens (才筆) of my elder colleagues, Kim Tongin and Im Haksu.
Our consolation of the Imperial Army this time contains two significant meanings. One is to express gratitude to our Imperial Army, who are dedicating their loyalty to the nation on the front lines. I believe our most important duty is not just to end with consolation but to convey the scenes of the battlefield through writing to the Chosŏn populace. However, many works of this nature have already been produced by Mainland writers. Starting with Mr. Hino's (火野氏) "Wheat and Soldiers" (麥と兵隊), countless frontline reportage literature pieces have been written.
Judging by these examples, the form of the frontline reportage literature we will write may not differ in scope, but I think the method of structuring the content will be somewhat different.
Chosŏn people do not know war. Even if they hear stories of war, because they have never experienced it, they lack a profound, real sense of war.
I think the reason why even air defense drills seem less tense is because people cannot imagine the reality of war. Since the Incident, the Chosŏn populace on the home front has shown patriotic devotion (赤誠), but I believe we must further enhance that sense of national duty and adopt the mindset of being soldiers of the Imperial Nation (皇國).
Firmly guarding the home front is difficult without knowing the reality of war. That is, if people understand well how things change during war and what becomes necessary, they will also understand how to manage life on the home front. Although the government guides people's lives, good results won't occur unless each individual acts voluntarily.
Taking this opportunity of observing the war zone, I intend first to educate myself in this reality and then convey that reality to the populace.
Next, I intend to report on the construction aspect. This war is not aimed at war itself but is for the eternal peace of the East, a battle to annihilate the anti-Japanese (抗日) and anti-Japanese sentiment (排日) factions disturbing that peace. Therefore, wherever our Imperial Army goes, there will be cheerful construction. This construction work must be pioneered with Japan and Korea as one body (內鮮一體). On this occasion, I intend to gather materials related to the continental advancement of Chosŏn people and provide them as reference for those in that field.
There will be many things to observe and research. However, it is regrettable that there is no time to discuss them here. Rather than stating my aspirations before departure, the report upon return is more necessary, so I will postpone other matters. <235>

Seeking the Banks of the Great Yellow River
Im Haksu

Having held a post in the provinces for two years, I somehow became close to the mountains. This was because, for washing away the fatigue of midday and occasional melancholy, there was nothing for me other than the mountains.
But then, sometime later, I grew tired of the mountains. What awaited me then was the train station. Late at night, I always walked along the monotonous Ponjŏng Street (本町通) in Kaesŏng to the station.
Two lines of rails stretching endlessly long, the wheels of incoming trains sounding a hoarse whistle, the flashing lights illuminating even the waiting room, the queue of passengers—among them, an old woman covered in wrinkles showed an envelope with her son's address, talking about going to find her son in Mutanchiang (牡丹江).—The bell rang, and with a loud whistle, the train departed north.
My inherent wanderlust began here. Isn't life like the ceaselessly moving hands of a clock, like flowing water that never stays in one place for even a moment, constantly flowing and moving without rest? My words cannot be dismissed as mere foolishness. Let's go. Even for a week, let's travel and live freely. Thus, over the past year, I wandered alone west to the Yellow Sea (黃海), east to the Mainland (內地), south to the Korea Strait (朝鮮海峽), and north to Harbin (哈爾賓) in North Manchuria (北滿). However, this summer, I firmly decided to go to Peiping (北京) in this manner.
Just then, I was selected as a delegate for the Imperial Army consolation mission this time. A one-month journey! Of course, it doesn't suit my personality, but at the moment of departure, I feel a strange tension. The reason I am going this time is not to tour the ancient sites in the remote regions of China (支那), which I have long yearned for, climbing Yueyang Tower (岳陽樓) or floating a boat at the Red Cliffs (赤壁). It is to console the Imperial Army, which has fought battle after battle for two years, enduring repeated hardships and suffering (艱難辛苦), and to report the progress of East-West construction to the people on the home front. Moreover, thinking that I am representing the Peninsula's literary world makes it difficult to have confidence in my mediocre talent.
Friends! A one-month continental journey! It is certainly not something to simply admire as romantic while sitting in a warm room (온돌방).
However, my companions, Messrs. Kim and Pak, are like elder brothers to me in terms of both experience and age. I feel considerably reassured because of these two elder brothers. Yet, even if I cannot achieve the second objective with my own strength, the first objective seems possible. This is because I feel I can understand the state of mind of a brave soldier (壯士) fighting under a foreign sky, uncertain whether his life will end today or tomorrow, far from his parents and siblings <236> back home. Saying this is based on an experience not even one ten-thousandth as significant, but *because I myself have felt such sentiments these past few days. That is, I suddenly moved my job to Seoul, and with the consolation trip a week away, tasks like moving, completing procedures for the previous job, and handling the children's school transfers are still unfinished.
Returning home late at night, I gaze at the high pile of books and belongings, let out a long sigh (長太息) in bewilderment, and lament my lonely situation for the first time. How much more so for those left at home when I leave Kyŏngsŏng the day after tomorrow? My wife and two children are frail, and while I, leaving them, feel anxious, those remaining at home must be most uneasy. However, the mission is critical. Now is not the time to worry about this and that. It's a situation of shared sympathy among comrades (同志相憐): how are the brave soldiers at the front managing? For those heroes whose return to tread the soil of their homeland—stretching from summer through winter and into the next spring—is hard to predict, how nostalgically must the sound of rain falling softly, or the temple bell tolling in the deep mountains at sunset, echo in their ears? Most must be worried about their circumstances, livelihood, and various other things.
At the very least, I intend to fulfill my mission, first express gratitude to the patriots who have offered their lives, and repay their contributions.

Farewell Address for the Literary Delegation
The Significance of the Literary Delegation
Yi Kwangsu

This time, by the general consensus (總意) of the Chosŏn literary world, a delegation to console the Imperial Army regarding the China Incident (支那事變) was dispatched. Messrs. Kim Tongin, Pak Yŏnghŭi, and Im Haksu departed Kyŏngsŏng on the 15th of this month for North China (北支). Regarding this event, the newspapers in Kyŏngsŏng adopted an extremely indifferent attitude. The handling of news articles almost seemed intended to ignore it, which is truly difficult to comprehend. This likely stems from the newspaper authorities' failure to recognize the significance of this event, <237> or else it can only be interpreted as a deliberate contempt for the expression of the collective will of Chosŏn writers, arising from the Chosŏn people's habit of self-deprecation (自賤性); this is truly deeply regrettable. Why is this so? Because no newspaper authority would dare publicly sneer at this event. However, we do not wish to interpret this maliciously.
We intend to interpret it charitably as their lack of understanding. Therefore, I intend to explain the significance of dispatching this literary delegation.
It goes without saying that the primary purpose of this event is for the writers and publishers of Chosŏn to express their gratitude towards the Imperial Army. It is also common sense that the secondary purpose is to directly inform and make the Chosŏn populace feel a part of the China Incident through the eyes, hearts, and hands of Chosŏn writers. Furthermore, how effective this will be for the Chosŏn populace is beyond question.
However, besides these, there is truly another important aspect. It is this:—
Until the end of the European Great War, the Chosŏn literary world was monochromatic, characterized by so-called nationalist thoughts and sentiments. Then, following the post-war surge of communist ideology, a faction of leftist writers and leftist literature emerged in Chosŏn. Thus, until the China Incident, these two currents flowed in opposition. However, writers or literature attempting to create or critique literary works from the standpoint and perspective that the Chosŏn nation is part of the Japanese nation—that is, nationalist (국민주의) writers or literature—were entirely absent in Chosŏn. But with the China Incident as a catalyst, a great transformation occurred: the Imperialization (皇民化) of the Chosŏn nation. This literary delegation is, in fact, a manifestation of this transformation from the standpoint of the literary world.
Looking at the list of initiators or attendees of the preparatory meeting, one can see how figures previously divided into left and right-wing camps—and who were rather anti-state in spirit—gathered in one hall (一堂) to express a common national sentiment. This signifies that future Chosŏn literature* will transcend the former so-called left and right-wing positions and advance towards a unified national standpoint, marking a new epoch (신기원) in the history of Chosŏn literature. Why did the newspapers treat this important event as if it were extremely bothersome, seemingly lamenting the unavoidable necessity of covering it? One cannot help but want to know their motive.

New Order Construction and Literature
Yu Chino

To those departing for China (渡支) this time to console the brave soldiers <238> fighting on the continental front for the construction of the new East Asian order, I wish you good health and hope you safely fulfill this crucial mission. War is indeed the most profound human activity that strikes the deepest chords (琴線) of humanity. I concurrently expect that your consolation visit to the front lines this time will surely manifest as great literary achievements.

A Historic Valiant Undertaking
Kim Kijin

Crossing the Great Wall (萬里長城), even further beyond the Yellow River (黃河), I take up this brush wishing health and a safe journey to the three delegates embarking on this long expedition of tens of thousands of *li*.

Between China and us, cultural exchange has continued uninterrupted historically since ancient times. Wasn't this especially true during the Tang (唐) dynasty era, or even in modern times during the Qianlong Emperor's (乾隆帝) era? Setting aside spiritual culture, weren't there so many similarities in architecture, fine arts**, clothing, and housing systems?
However, cultural exchange was almost severed midway, and just as sprouts were beginning to emerge for renewed connections, thanks to the appearance of insightful individuals like Professor Zhou Zuoren (周作人) of Peking University (北京大學), we have now encountered this Incident.

I will not speak longer. On this journey, Messrs. Delegates, first**, convey our heartfelt consolation to the soldiers on behalf of us, the people of the Peninsula on the home front. Then, please do not just return after that; by all means, research and understand the culture of Rising Asia (興亞) there, gather a rich harvest, and return to the Peninsula's literary world.
Outside the window, myriad flowers (百花) are scattering. Perhaps along the banks of the Great Yellow River where you gentlemen are heading, the willows (柳絮) and flowers of the ancient continental capital, seemingly suffocating, will scatter profusely, evoking feelings of travel weariness (旅愁). By all means, I hope you have a safe journey.

Sending Off the Delegates Crossing to China
Chŏng Insŏp

At this time when the world situation is critical and human civilization is about to be rebuilt, the responsibility of the three delegates, selected and dispatched not only in the sense of national total mobilization but also out of a fervent desire for Literary Service to the Nation (文壇報國), is felt to be great and important.
Please, observe various places in good health, convey vividly the scenes of the Imperial Army's fierce struggles, and simultaneously <239> encourage and console the officers and soldiers at the front who are striving against all difficulties (萬難) in a foreign land (異域), working fully towards achieving the final objective of the Holy War (聖戰).
Furthermore, I hope you will thoroughly discern the hopes and demands of the new Chinese officials and people being organized into the new order of East Asia, and that your pens, along with the beauty of the natural landscape, will bring us much good news.

Human Bullet Wedge (肉彈的 楔機)
Kim Munjip

As this was something utterly unimaginable in the history of the literary world until recently, this event involved considerable strain and sacrifice. But it finally succeeded. It is the gain resulting from inverting dialectics twice. Regarding this matter, people might still misunderstand me. However, in 10 years, there will surely come a day when my spirit will be reasonably evaluated.
By the time this issue of this magazine makes a splash in bookstores, our delegates Tongin, Yŏnghŭi, and Haksu might have returned from the war zone. But the farewell rally for them has not even been held yet. So, nothing definitive can be said. There is nothing more to say than "Have a safe trip."
But come to think of it, it's not that there's nothing to say. That is, the term "consolation mission" (위문사) is still quite unsatisfactory to me. It somehow feels like "acting like a stranger" (他人行儀, taningyōgi). For instance, if a major earthquake occurs somewhere in America, and the tragic news is conveyed to the world, if Britain or Japan sends a consolation mission, that term feels quite natural. But for the Chosŏn literary world to send a consolation mission to the Imperial Army front line feels somewhat awkward to me.
In fact, it's not just awkward; unless a situation arises where several members from our literary world are drafted, shoulder rifles, and go off to die, and among the remaining writers, several join the army (종군) under the same ideology, I cannot truly be satisfied.
We hope for the happiness of our historical future, and if this delegation serves as some effective catalyst to shorten, even by a fraction, the unpredictable path towards bringing about the aforementioned situation in our literary world, we should be content with that.
Only from this standpoint do I newly realize that this event is no* trivial matter. In other words, the three delegates must be nothing less than the three human bullets (육탄 3용사) for the national unification of the Chosŏn race.

The Safety of the Three Heroes
Yi Sŏnhŭi

First and foremost, I offer my blessings for our literary world's troop of three heroes who have departed for North China.
Traveling for five days, several days, without seeing a single mountain, on land where red dust (赤塵) covers the sky* and obscures the sun and moon (日月), the magnificent spectacle of that battle <240> defies our imagination.
"When I see the young children of the Chinese villagers, I think of my own children back home."
I believe this was a line from a letter by an Imperial Army hero.
Those shedding blood for the fatherland cannot forget their families left behind in the homeland.
Now, through this greatest event in our literary world, this consolation visit, if even a small measure of joy can be brought to our soldiers, there could be no greater success.
Please, you three—heroes, return safely. And console them with all the talent you possess.

The Great Mission of Cultural Exchange
Kim Tonghwan

I am writing this on my way back from seeing the three gentlemen off at Namdaemun Station. My heart is filled only with the wish for their safe journey.

Perhaps by tomorrow, you will alight at Beijing's Chaoyangmen Station (朝陽門驛頭). The culture of Beijing (北京), being the capital during the golden age of the Qing (淸朝) dynasty, boasts palace and fortress layouts (布置), orderly streets (街衢), and splendid cultural institutions (文物制度) that will surely fill you, gentlemen, born and raised in the small Peninsula region, with wonder at every turn. I have often heard that world cultural figures highly value the Qing dynasty culture centered on this Beijing cradle (胎盤), and although it may lag somewhat behind modern civilization, the classical culture of those people, possessing the tradition of the Great Yellow River (大黃河) civilization from 4,000-5,000 years ago, is known to be astonishing enough to captivate our mind's eye (心眼).
By all means, absorb plentifully the parts that can contribute to Peninsula culture from within this cradle of past East Asian civilization (東洋文明) and return shouldering a full load.

I hear that most of Beijing's cultural figures have disappeared to the other side of the front line. It is said that the intellectuals who taught at Peking University (北平大學) or Tsinghua University (淸華大學) have scattered far away to Xi'an (西安), Sichuan (四川), or Hong Kong (香港). Although Lu Xun's (魯迅) younger brother, Zhou Zuoren (周作人), is said to remain, he too is reportedly secluded in his study (書齋), engrossed in translating Greek Mythology (希臘神活) and the Japanese Kojiki (日本古事紀). Thus, it may be difficult to find friends with whom to discuss the construction of Great East Asian (大東亞) culture. However, discerning individuals *who grasp the new era surely await your visit with pleasure. Please sit together with them, engage in sufficient discussion and coordination, and bring back the results. Just as Silla (新羅) and Paekche (百濟) sent cultural envoys to Tang (唐) China in the past to fulfill the great mission of cultural exchange, we entrust this mission to you three gentlemen.

Now, visiting the front lines and trenches, your hearts will surely have many opportunities to feel thrilling emotions. Please capture all that raw material well and produce masterpieces surpassing "Wheat and Soldiers" (麥と兵隊), making a great contribution** to the populace on the home front. <241>

**Summary**

This collection of short articles from the June 1939 issue of *Samch'ŏlli* magazine announces and celebrates the dispatch of three prominent Korean writers—Kim Tongin, Pak Yŏnghŭi, and Im Haksu—to North China to "console" the Japanese Imperial Army. The writers express their sense of honor and duty in undertaking this mission, framing it as crucial for demonstrating Korean loyalty as Imperial Subjects (皇民), understanding the "Holy War" (聖戰) for a "New Order in East Asia," and conveying the realities of the front lines and Japanese construction efforts to the Korean populace, who are portrayed as largely ignorant of the war's significance. Several other prominent literary figures (Yi Kwangsu, Yu Chino, Kim Kijin, Chŏng Insŏp, Kim Munjip, Yi Sŏnhŭi, Kim Tonghwan) contribute brief pieces praising the mission's importance, seeing it as a historic step towards national unity under the Empire, fostering war literature, promoting cultural exchange, and marking a turn away from previous nationalist or leftist literary stances towards a unified, pro-Imperial "national literature." The articles repeatedly emphasize gratitude towards the Imperial Army, the need for Koreans to understand the war's true meaning (i.e., the establishment of Japanese hegemony and the "Great Unity" of Asian peoples against Western influence and opposing Asians), and the role of literature in solidifying Korea's place within the Japanese Empire (內鮮一體).

From a historical perspective, this special feature is highly revealing of the intense pressures and ideological conformity demanded of Korean intellectuals during the late 1930s wartime mobilization period. It starkly illustrates the co-option of major literary figures into the Japanese imperial project, showcasing their public pronouncements of loyalty and participation in propaganda efforts. Historians would find the explicit justifications for collaboration, the framing of the war effort, the discussion of creating "war literature," the self-censorship or internalized ideology regarding past political leanings (nationalism/communism), and the rhetoric surrounding "Imperialization" (皇民化) and "Japan and Korea as One Body" (內鮮一體) extremely valuable for understanding the complexities of colonial rule, collaboration, and cultural politics in late colonial Korea. The criticism directed at Korean newspapers for allegedly downplaying the event also hints at internal tensions or differing levels of enthusiasm for such overt displays of pro-Japanese activity within Korean society.

기사제목	「朝鮮文壇使節」特輯, 北支戰線에 皇軍慰問 떠남에 際하야
필자	金東仁 외
기사형태	문예기타

「朝鮮文壇使節」特輯, 北支戰線에 皇軍慰問 떠남에 際하야
						金東仁 외
北支戰線을 향하야
金東仁

조선문단에서 누구든 支那戰線에 파견하고싶다. 파견해서 朝壇으로서 전선의 장병을 위문하는 한편 朝鮮에도 전쟁문학을 이르켜보자 하는 의견이 昨秋부터 있었다. 이러한 의견을 가지고 있는 만큼 때로는 말로도 발표해 보았다.
말이 발표되자 나와 동감의 의견을 가진 자가 의외로 많다. 아니 거진 전부가 같은 의견을 가지고 있었다. 다만 말로 발표하지 않었을 뿐이지.
웨 발표하지 않었는가. 그 대답은 간단하다. 제1은 돈이다. 여비가 위테롭다. 때는 바로 모든 물가가 등귀해서 *******字**代가 배 이상임으로 출판 공황시대가 되여 文士의 포켙은 아주 가난할 때다.
제2는 그런 의견을 가젔드래도 타인이 어떤 의견을 가젔는지 몰나서 함부로 말할 수 없섰음에서였다.
이런 까닭에 서로 전선관찰을 有意義하게 생각하면서도 아직껏 실행하지 못했다.
그랬는데 문단과 불가분 관계를 가진 출판업자들 사이에도 같은 의견이 있어서 급속도로 문제는 구체화되여 출판업자들은 여비를 내고 문단에선 사람을 내여 문단과 출판업자와의 협력 아래서 전선 관찰부대를 보게 되여서 무능한 나도 被파견인의 한 사람으로 된 것을 光榮으로 생각한다.＜232＞
군사교육을 못 받고 군사문제에 전연 무지한 우리들은 붓그러운 일이나 이번 聖戰에 군사방면에 관해선 아모것도 모른다. 다만 우리들이 받은 교양으로 군사행동에 끈치지 말고 위대한 사업을 수행하기 위해서 조곰이라도 도으려는 마음이다.
그러면 그 위대한 사업이란 무었인가. 지금 새삼스럽게 설명하긴 쑥스러운 일임으로 그만 두지만 요컨대 황갈색 피부를 가지고 키가 그리 크지 못한 인종인 大和民族 朝鮮民族 滿洲民族 及 支那民族은 대동단결을 하자. 전세계가 한 뭉치가 되여 평화를 즐김은 가장 이상적일 바이겠으나 그것이 한 개의 이상론에 끈치고 실현의 가능성이 보이지 않는 바엔 적어도 同係의 민족만으로래도 평화를 보장하자. 異係 민족의 조종밧는 인형노릇은 그만 두자. 따라서 이상과 같은 의견에 반대하는 사상을 가진 자 혹은 단체가 있으면 그것은 말하자면 우리와 同係 민족에 속할지라도 우리의 공동의 적이다. 적인 이상 당연 잔멸하지 않으면 안 된다.-이것이 이번 聖戰의 本意요 참다운 목적일 것이다. 그러므로 이번 聖戰은 군사행동이라기보다 오히려 정치공작이다. 정치공작을 수행하는 필요행동으로서 軍兵을 움즉이는 것이다.
이런 위대한 황색민족의 대운동이 불행히도 朝鮮民衆에게 철저하게 알여 있지 않다. 지방 방방곡곡에까지 배포되는 신문에도 「어딜 점령했다」 「적군을 全滅했다」 쯤으로 자세한 것은 보고하지 않는다. 근근 도회지식인이 東京쪽에서 오는 잡지로 그 모습을 상상할 정도다. 국어를 모르는 자(도회인을 제외하고 대부분)와 內地발행 잡지판매소가 업는 데 사는 사람은 이번 聖戰의 참 의의와 聖戰을 수행하기 위해서의 병사들의 노고를 전연 모른다.
이것을 朝鮮民衆에게 보고하고 그 蒙을 깨닫게 할 중대한 사명과 의무가 우리들 朝鮮文士의 억개에 지워저 있다. 생각하면 이러케 큰 임무를 질만한 자신이 없는 나다. 너무 즐겁고 光榮스런 임무이길래 사퇴하지 않고 바덧으나 출발일자가 닥처오매 가속도로 임무의 중대함을 느낀다.
괴로운 중에서도 단 하나의 기뿜은 오라지 않어 전장에 가서 지금까지 文面으로만 알었든 장병의 노고를 직접 눈으로 볼 수 있는 것이다.
나는 무능자요. 또 눈 뜬 장님이다. 내가 이제 가서 보아서 보고할 글은 支那에서 전개될 사건의 백분지일도 전지 못하리라.
하나 현황의 만분지일도 못되는 나의 보고문으로도 朝鮮의 지방인이 이번 聖戰에 대해서 가젔든 관념(그것은 극히 빈약하다)을 백배 더하게 하리라는 장담은 할 수 있다-그만콤 朝鮮의 지방인은 이번 聖戰에 관하여 무지했다. 군 당국에서나 總府府 당국에서 이번 이 일을 극력 후원해 준 것은 이 효과를 기대했기 때문이다.＜233＞
결과가 기대보다 적다 치드래도 그것은 우리들의 무능한 탓이지 결코 성의가 부족해서가 아닐 것이다-라는 말슴만은 믿어 달나는 것이다.

戰爭과 文學者의 任務

日支事變이 발발해서 朝鮮民衆이 시국에 대한 관심은 매우 컸다. 그것이 장기전이 되고 장기건설이 될 적엔 우리들의 관심은 비상하게 되고 日本帝國의 이번 사변에 있어서 東洋 영원의 평화확립에로 邁進하는 근본정신을 이해하게 된 것이다.
