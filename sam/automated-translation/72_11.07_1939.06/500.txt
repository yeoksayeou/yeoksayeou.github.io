Article Type: 사고·편집후기 (Notice/Editor's Postscript)

We are now sending out the early summer issue amidst fresh greenery. We are pleased that it includes several important articles:
1. A record of a meeting between Government-General officials (總督府 當路者) and prominent private citizens (民間 有志).
2. Reports from those returned from the battlefield and the Diet (議會).
3. A gathering where female writers asked Yi Kwangsu (李光洙) about literature, religion, and love.

Furthermore, we believe the contribution from Mr. Kim Kyŏngjae (金璟載) regarding Mr. Pak Sŏgyun (朴錫胤), who is going from Xinjing (新京) to become the Consul* to Poland (波蘭), and the piece by Mr. Hong Yangmyŏng (洪陽明) recounting his impressions upon meeting Mr. Wang Zhaoming (汪兆銘) in Shanghai (上海) are quite noteworthy and fine articles.
Regarding the literary section, we had especially planned to feature as a special publication the complete famous translation (名譯) by Mr. Nishimura Shintarō (西村眞太郞) of Hino Ashihei's* (火野*平) "Wheat and Soldiers" (麥と兵隊). However, due to circumstances at the very end, it became unavoidable, and we were unable to publish it. We share deep regret with our readers.

However, in this literary section, we are pleased to have put together a special feature on the "Dispatch of the Imperial Army Comfort Mission" (皇軍慰問使節 派遣), a grand event (盛事) in the Chosŏn literary world (朝鮮文壇). Probably around next month's issue, the heroic poems and writings (壯烈한 詩文) of the three gentlemen, Kim Tongin (金東仁), Pak Yŏnghŭi (朴英熙), and Im Haksu (林學洙), who rushed to the battlefield, will be published. We expect works that strike the pulse (血脈) of the populace on the home front (銃後民衆) will be produced.
We believe the "Japanese Literature Special Feature" (邦文特輯) section is becoming more substantial month by month (다달히). This month too, we believe we have included* (入) several exclusive materials (門外不出). We are striving to make it even more substantial going forward.

The four major books planned for publication by our company's publishing department:
*   *Wartime Great Speeches Collection* (戰時大演說集)
*   *Pak Hŭngsik's Theory of Enterprise* (朴興植 事業論)
*   *My Peninsula Mountains and Rivers (Travelogue Collection)* (나의 半島山河(紀行集))
*   *Ch'unwŏn's Theory of Love* (春園作-戀愛論)
Some are currently printing, and others are still being edited, but all will be presented to the world (世에 公하겟나이다) in the early summer season.
Now, flowers, flowers are everywhere. It is said that in Seoul's (서울) Ch'anggyŏngwŏn (昌慶苑), visitors for the night cherry blossoms (夜櫻) surpassed 300,000 over 10 days. However, is it alright to just go flower viewing like this? At this tense time, when every moment counts in planning and preparing for the future (압날), isn't this something we must reflect upon? (End) <300>

**Summary**

This editor's postscript from the June 1939 issue of *Samch'ŏlli* magazine highlights key articles, including reports from the war front, a meeting between colonial officials and Korean figures, and an interview with Yi Kwangsu. It notes contributions about Pak Sŏgyun and Wang Zhaoming. The editor expresses regret over the cancelled publication of Hino Ashihei's "Wheat and Soldiers" but takes pride in a special feature on the dispatch of Korean writers (Kim Tongin, Pak Yŏnghŭi, Im Haksu) to comfort Japanese troops, anticipating their "heroic" writings. Upcoming books, including works by Pak Hŭngsik and Yi Kwangsu (Ch'unwŏn), are announced. The postscript concludes with a moralizing note questioning the appropriateness of leisurely flower viewing during tense times requiring focus on the future.

From a historical perspective, the article is revealing about the cultural and political atmosphere in late 1930s colonial Korea. It clearly shows the magazine promoting collaborationist activities, such as the "Imperial Army Comfort Mission" involving prominent Korean writers, and publishing works by figures like Yi Kwangsu and Pak Hŭngsik, known for their pro-Japanese stances. The cancellation of Hino Ashihei's popular war story "Wheat and Soldiers" might hint at censorship or changing propaganda needs. The final paragraph reflects the state-promoted discourse of national mobilization and austerity ("tense time," "reflect upon" leisure), urging readers to prioritize the war effort over personal enjoyment, even amidst signs of popular leisure like mass flower viewing. Historians would find the explicit promotion of collaboration, the mention of specific pro-Japanese figures and events, and the juxtaposition of cultural activities with wartime mobilization rhetoric particularly interesting.
