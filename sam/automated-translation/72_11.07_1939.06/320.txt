映畵批評界의 危機, 無情評의 讀後感
						朴基采

Four or five days ago, I went to Changgyŏngwŏn Garden to hear the news of spring with K-kun, whom I hadn't seen in a long time.
While circling the garden once, K-kun and I sat down on the lawn to rest our tired legs. K-kun lit a cigarette and immediately brought up the subject.
"Pak-kun, do you read every single critique of your works?"
"Why? Yes, I read each one at least once."
"So, what do you think of the critiques of *Mujŏng* (無情) this time?"
"Just as the critics felt no inspiration from *Mujŏng*, I felt no interest in their critiques and simply laughed them off."
"Should you do that? When a critique points out flaws in a work, shouldn't you reflect on them and make corrections to improve?"
"Well, if it were a fair point, I wouldn't just make corrections. I should visit them and express my gratitude. However, the film criticism prevalent in Korea (朝鮮) is nothing more than a literal imitation (이미떼-슌) of the ideological and political aspects <190> of film criticism from advanced countries. When criticizing Korean films (朝鮮映畵), I really don't know where they focus their critique (着眼). When talking about Korean films, shouldn't they at least know the process through which Korean films are produced before writing reviews or criticizing?"
"ChoYŏng (朝映) has decent facilities set up, so you can't say *Mujŏng* was bad because the facilities were bad, right?"
"Listen, I'm not making excuses just because my work failed. The filming for *Mujŏng* started on May 19th last year, before the ChoYŏng Ŭijŏngbu studio was completed. It came out on the market on March 15th this year, taking about 10 months. However, the actual shooting days amounted to 23 days to complete the silent version of *Mujŏng*. Furthermore, half of the entire film consisted of indoor scenes. Did we build sets (셋트) inside the studio and film? No, we didn't. We rented currently inhabited private houses (人家). The original plan was to shoot those scenes over 4-5 days, but suddenly the production manager demanded we finish them in 24 hours, day and night (밤낫). If this order had come before filming started, we might have considered other methods, but hearing this midway through shooting, all I could think was that we just had to finish filming these scenes somehow. That was a moment when I felt despair as a Korean filmmaker."
"So, you couldn't film in the newly built Ŭijŏngbu studio then."
"What studio? It wasn't even a studio yet. For indoor scenes, we rented a private house, squeezed lights (라이트) into one and a half rooms, borrowed the main auditorium of the Chosŏn Ilbo Sa (朝鮮日報社) for the wedding scene, using 17 kilowatts of electricity, and borrowed the social affairs department of the Chosŏn Ilbo Sa again for the newspaper office scene. Critics said the actors' movements (ウゴキ) were static, but even if an actor needed to stand, we couldn't move the camera far back because their head would be cut off by the frame (原面). So, we had them sit, which naturally resulted in static movement."
"What was the reason for dragging out *Mujŏng* for a year before releasing it?"
"Well, there was a significant reason others wouldn't know. It should naturally have been released around August or September last year. However, the executives at ChoYŏng stubbornly insisted that, for the reputation of the joint-stock company, sound recording (錄音) absolutely could not be done elsewhere. We waited for ChoYŏng's recording studio to be completed. Soundproofing was partially installed, and recording began in the Ŭijŏngbu ChoYŏng Recording Studio in late January. The preview screening as a sound film (有音映畵) took place in late February. If you want to make an issue of it, fine. It was ChoYŏng's inaugural commemorative work. A film released by ChoYŏng, with its complete facilities, after taking a whole year. I remember seeing a piece in a student section of some newspaper where a Kyŏngsŏng University student (城大學生), after watching *Mujŏng*, wrote that they were even more disappointed because it was the inaugural commemorative work <191> produced by ChoYŏng, supposedly equipped with the most capital and facilities, pouring all its energy into it. But this must be disappointment only with *Mujŏng*. It might not be disappointment with ChoYŏng itself, but perhaps the university student's disappointment was justified as an objective (客觀) member of the audience who didn't know the motives and conditions under which *Mujŏng* was produced. Perhaps this disappointment stems from a love for Korean cinema, but the audience for Korean films must always keep in mind that it is 'a Korean film made in Korea'. Why? Because Korean cinema grew out in a deformed shape, without a proper developmental process (過程). Have we ever had even one silent Korean film that we could call a masterpiece? We just started making talkies (토-키) because others were making them, that's all."
"Well, that must be the agony of Korean film producers. But can the audience be expected to understand and watch every Korean film with that perspective? It's natural for the audience to pay the same price and want to see good movies. However, as you said, there might be curiosity (好奇心) about Korean films simply because they are made by Koreans."
"The other day (日前), a friend from literary circles (文壇) saw Julien Duvivier's (쭈리안데뷔붸) *Pépé le Moko* (望鄕), Naruse Mikio's (成瀨喜己男) *Hataraku Ikka* (はだらく一家, The Whole Family Works), and *Mujŏng*. He said that while *Hataraku Ikka* was extremely childish, it had some better points than *Mujŏng*. Hearing that, I felt truly pathetic. I couldn't say anything to the fact that he compared *Mujŏng* to a world-class masterpiece like *Pépé le Moko* and a top Japanese domestic film like *Hataraku Ikka*."
"Still, wouldn't the fact that the audience's standards have risen be helpful for the qualitative improvement of Korean cinema?"
"Of course, there's no rule saying Korean cinema cannot produce films reaching world-class standards, but at least one condition needs to be met. The April issue of *Munjang* (文章) also discussed the Korean film world, but idealistic empty theories (空論) like the short essay (小論) by Mr. Chu Yŏng-sŏp (朱永涉), who doesn't know the reality (實際) of the Korean film world, are better understood by those actually producing films."
"Overall, what do you think of the *Mujŏng* you made?"
"Without a doubt, it's a failure. If only I had the courage, I would have burned it immediately before releasing it to the public to clear my conscience..."
"Then shouldn't you accept whatever criticism comes your way?"
"Who's arguing otherwise! It's just that the duty of art criticism should not be limited to criticizing art but should also involve reforming art. Consequently, the duty of film criticism should not be limited to criticizing films but should involve reforming films. But looking at the critiques of *Mujŏng*, that's not what they do. They merely fill the blank spaces of the paper using the labor of printing workers, while making huge errors (誤謬) in judgment (鑑賞眼). Film criticism should point out the producer's ignorant flaws (不覺한 欠陷) in the <192> work, and at the same time, grasp what the creator intended (意圖한) with that work and discuss the quality of its expression (表現). But that's not it. Looking at the *Mujŏng* review presented as the 'March Film Review' in a certain newspaper (某報), it confuses the novel and the film, calling it a human-interest novel from Korea's early New Literature movement and noting its fluent writing, thus offering a film-novel type of criticism. This doesn't stem from film criticism; it seems like a calculated attempt to slander (重傷) an individual, which ironically ended up exposing utter ignorance about film."
"You seem quite dissatisfied with the March Film Review."
"I am by no means defending (辨) my work, which I myself admit is a failure. Is this even the form of film criticism? Is it the content? Shouldn't the filmmaker's technical skill (技能問題) be the central focus? Generally, what kind of critique did this reviewer offer? Firstly, they pointed out the flaw of unrealistic expression (非現實的 表現) methods in *Mujŏng* in the scenes with swinging, seesawing, and coin-tossing, concluding that this shows a lack of recognition (認識) that a film director is a film author. The reviewer probably knows that swinging is a common pastime for men and women, young and old, on holidays in Korea. If the 'first scene' (퍼스트씬) depicts a holiday, the reviewer would likely agree that swinging fits the season. Then why are men playing coin-tossing nearby? This is the sole amusement for village shepherd boys on holidays. It's something likely to happen in any village. The issue seems to be why girls are playing on a seesaw nearby, which is out of season—this seems to be the focus of the reviewer's 'unrealistic etcetera (云云)'. However, the reviewer should have watched that scene (씬) more carefully. Three or four girls, aged 10 to 12 or 13, are playing together. This isn't a planned activity by adult women (婦女) but the curiosity of young girls. Beautiful childlike innocence (童心) not only lacks a sense of season, but one must understand that childlike innocence possesses the qualification to engage in play, not just seesawing but even more elaborate games next to swinging, if it's for their own satisfaction.
If we were to point out non-filmic and unrealistic descriptive methods by film authors in recent Korean cinema, we could point to examples in many films, but let's specifically look at the past works (過作品) of this 'realist' critic. On a traditional restaurant stage (料亭舞臺), a kisaeng dances for patrons. During this dance, there's an 'overlap' (오버렢) showing the kisaeng dancing similarly in the suburbs, then another overlap back to her dancing on the restaurant stage. What did this creator intend? Was it merely to show the beautiful kisaeng dancing, shifting from the stage to the suburbs and back? Judging by the mood of the scenes, the stage is at night, and the suburbs are during the day (낫). When the dance on stage ends, the patrons clap approvingly (조타고). Did the souls of these patrons and the kisaeng travel to the suburbs? Even if their souls went out, shouldn't the patrons also be shown in a cut? Would the creator consider this a supra-realist descriptive method as a filmic technique? If they deny it, isn't this ignorance stemming from the lack <193> of recognition, as the critic (評者) themselves stated, that 'this creator is a film director, meaning a film author'?
Furthermore, the critic discussed film techniques, specifically mentioning 'suspense' (써스펜스) and pointing out the introduction of the newspaper reporter Sin U-sŏn. After Wŏlhyang strikes Mukwa, she says it's Sin U-sŏn. Sin U-sŏn looks at Mukwa and says, 'If you were going to give it to me anyway, why didn't you write a love note?' Then Wŏlhyang writes something again. The scene cuts (컷트) and transitions to the next scene where Kim Hyŏn-su is drinking, claps his hands to call a boy, waiting for Wŏlhyang. When he asks who the guests in that room are, the boy replies they are guests from the newspaper company, and someone nearby points out that among the 'Seven Senses' (七感?), it's Sin U-sŏn. Through this conversation (會話), doesn't the impression of Sin U-sŏn deepen objectively, revealing that he had previously asked for this Wŏlhyang at the restaurant? The critic, who didn't know the newspaper reporter Sin U-sŏn during the Mukwa-striking scene but found out in the next scene, has clearly experienced a form of suspense, waiting anxiously without knowing who he was first, then finding out clearly in the next scene. As the critic suggests, there might be cases where one should introduce 'This is newspaper reporter Sin U-sŏn' upfront, but in description, emphasizing the surface, meaning naturalness, is crucial, making this a very important issue in film technique. And another point, the critic pointed out film techniques in scene transitions (畫面連結), grandly stating (大言), 'If today's Korean film directors don't know this basic film knowledge, whose shame will this be?' Truly, does this critic imagine the film techniques of the old silent movie era? Or are they trying to extend the film techniques of the silent film era as they are? The critic should at least know the basics of talkie techniques, yet they admit to being a 'blank slate' (白紙), so whose shame would this be?
The critic pointed out that the transition from Yi Hyŏng-sik and Sin U-sŏn meeting and parting to Sin U-sŏn and Wŏlhyang walking by the lake ignored the passage of time. Regarding this, I'd like to explain it a bit more concretely as a matter of common sense for the general audience's film appreciation. The filmmaker doesn't show the action of Yi Hyŏng-sik and Sin U-sŏn parting. Instead, where Kim Sŏn-hyŏng is waiting alone for Yi Hyŏng-sik, using an 'effect' (에픡트), dialogue like 'Well, see you again,' 'Alright, take care,' is heard. Some time after those words end, Yi Hyŏng-sik runs into the frame where Sŏn-hyŏng is alone, saying, 'You waited long, didn't you?' 'No. Who was that person?' They talk and walk away. From this walking 'cut,' it transitions to the lake. Following the movement, their walking figures appear by the lake, starting with their reflections in the water. Before understanding this scene transition method (畫面連結法), one must first understand that in talkie film transitions, connecting from an object to a space is considered a filmmaker's <194> kind gesture towards the audience in talkies. Otherwise, transitioning from that object to the next 'cut' with just one line of 'dialogue' (다이야로), whether they travel 10 li or 1,000 li, is not unnatural at all regarding the passage of time. A similar example of this kind of scene transition as in *Mujŏng* can be seen in a recently released (封切) Japanese domestic film.
In the Shōchiku Ōfuna (松竹大船) film *Kodomo no Shiki* (子供の四季, Children's Seasons), Part 2, the 'first scene' shows the grandfather putting Sanpei (三平) and Zenta (善太) on a horse, followed by Sanpei's mother and grandmother in a cart. The camera follows these walking figures. In the very next 'cut,' it moves within an empty space inside. Horse sounds are heard. When the camera stops (傷止 - likely 靜止, stopped), the grandfather is lecturing (訓戒) Zenta, Sanpei, his mother, and family members. This is a talkie technique and is absolutely not unnatural. According to the critic's logic, how did the horse and cart walking on the village road arrive at this house so quickly! In terms of time, it wasn't even a minute, was it? Shouldn't a critic possess this level of film common sense?
My pointing out the critic's errors now is by no means a complaint (苦情) about the harsh critique (酷評) directed at my work, but merely a request for the critic to exercise self-restraint (自重) in their attitude."
"Generally, what is the significance (意義) of film criticism?"
"The significance of film criticism can be described in two ways. Preview reviews (試寫評) have a significance similar to, for example, 'new publication introduction reviews' in the literary world. For a proper understanding of film criticism, as mentioned earlier, its duty should not be limited to criticizing the film but holds social significance in reforming that film. Regarding art criticism, as Mr. Chŏng Pi-sŏk (鄭飛石) wrote in his letter to critics in the April issue of *Chogwang* (朝光) concerning literary criticism, in film criticism, the critic must first analyze (解剖하여야) the creator's intention behind the work."
During this time, the bell rang. Of course, it was the signal to leave the garden (退苑). K-kun and I stood up, brushing off our suits.

An Open Letter to Director Pak Regarding “Mujŏng”

1. Director Pak Ki-ch'ae (朴基采), I offer this letter to you. I (小生) am not a critic (評家), just someone who loves movies and especially hopes for the development of Korean Cinema (朝鮮映畵). In presenting this letter, please forgive the rudeness of a complete outsider (外漢) whom you have never met (一面識), <195> and instead cherish me as one of your fans.

Recently, I saw *Mujŏng* (『無情』), which you adapted and directed. After watching this film, produced with the utmost conscience, I wanted to record fragments of the impressions I received, however clumsy (稚拙), and convey my gratitude along with some childlike requests to you, who will surely continue to produce good films in the future. I am an outsider with no theoretical study of film, nor the qualification to ever critique this new art (I am also burdened by being an outsider for life), so I dare not use words like 'critique' or 'impressions,' opting for the more fitting phrase 'fragments of impressions' (『인상의 片片』).

'Pale, rambling stories like children playing house'—this was my impression of Korean Cinema until recently. Therefore, after watching *Mujŏng* this time, I saw infinite hope for the future of Korean Cinema, and also tasted the joy of liberation from the recent so-called 'moving picture' melancholy. I am simply impressed that you have delivered such a fine work (快作) displaying this level of scientific quality (과학성 - perhaps a poor choice of words) from within a 'non-scientific' (『비과학적』) production system (製作機構), as you mentioned. I also pay my respects (敬意) to your conscientious directing.
The depiction of the kisaeng in the latter half and the beauty (美麗) of the camera work were truly splendid (훌융하였음니다). The handling of the single white rubber shoe (힌고무신) swept away in the clear water, the lighthearted treatment of the *avec* (?) scene (아벡구신 - possibly 'avec couple' scene), the Uolgnyn (?) of Wŏlhyang and Mukwa (was it effective or not is a separate issue) etc.—I was surprised (놀났음니다) that Korean Cinema had grown so much from mere child's play or awkward staged performance filming. Hurray for Director Pak Ki-ch'ae!

However, you must not forget that the audience's greed is like that of a beggar. Hereafter, I (生等 - likely 小生, 'I') intend to express my dissatisfaction and make bold, childish demands. I trust that you, being a person of high caliber, will indulge me.
As the main title (메인·타이틀) ended (the audience most curiously awaits what comes after this title ends, what it will start with. And during this wait, the psychology is anxious (焦) and apprehensive (恐). Therefore, the impact of the first * part on the entire work is likely profound (深大)). The scene with the girl with flowing hair (머리 꼬랑이) swinging, the scene of bending over (꿉으리고) on the seesaw, the *changgi* board, coin-tossing, etc.—seeing these scenes sluggishly (어슬넝 어슬넝) come and go for several minutes (數分間) was dizzying. Why? Because the unpleasant feeling (不快한 感) of 'Ah, I'm watching a Korean film again' arose, and the preconception 'It's failed again' <196> filled me. If this scene were an introductory panel for a tourism film, it might have a different meaning, but we couldn't accept (甘受) it as the opening thread for an ambitious work claiming to be a literary film (文藝映畵). What impression did I receive other than feeling *? This is my actual feeling (實感). Mainland Japanese (內地人) critics often complain about the unnecessary 'tediousness' (침長 - ?) in Korean films; I wonder if they are referring to scenes like this.

Under what intention did you create this opening? Were you trying to historically verify the Korean era of 20 years ago? Were you trying to introduce Korean customs (朝鮮風俗)? (Assuming this film is exported to mainland Japan and receives the lowest * rating for commercial value (興行價値淪의 極*)) It's a matter of great curiosity.
Overall, the unclear theme of this film seems to be a major flaw (欠陷). As you stated in *Yŏsŏng* (女性*), you emphasized Yŏng-ch'ae as representing the traditional spiritual beauty (精神美) of Korean women (the fidelity (志操) of a virtuous woman (烈女)). However, despite this statement and theme, the impression after watching the film was truly faint (미미하였음니다). In other words (즉 말하자면), I received no emotion whatsoever, couldn't get swept into its atmosphere, and ended up just watching the kisaeng for over an hour until the end. This might just be my subjective view (主觀), but I've considered the reasons as follows:
1. Could it be that among our young people (청년들), the so-called cultured individuals (문화인), there is simply no interest in the traditional spiritual beauty you find interesting? Whether this is a good trend or a bad one, it's a fact that interest has been lost. I wonder what the impressions of the Ewha Womans College liberal arts students (梨專文科生) who saw this film were, and I'd like to know their ethical leanings. If so, was choosing the novel *Mujŏng* itself a mistake from the start? I've offered excessively absurd (荒唐無稽) ravings (妄辭). When the piece of paper window (창호지) inscribed 'A virtuous woman does not remarry' (烈女는 不更二夫) was torn and thrown onto the water, wasn't it only the 'new woman' who felt an unpleasant sensation (不快한 感) like muddy water for a while, but also the men watching who felt the same?
2. Regarding the method of expression (表現手法), I praised (찬양하였음니다) your many achievements at the outset (劈頭). However, that was relative and referred to the latter half. In the first half, after the swing scene ended, I broke out in a cold sweat wondering if friends playing *yut* (윷가리?) and checking family registers (호적부) would appear next. It seems the thorough focus on the theme was diminished (減*) by that unnecessary opening scene I mentioned earlier. Instead of that useless scene, wouldn't describing and explaining the childhood relationship between Yŏng-ch'ae and Hyŏng-sik and their families, even at a fast tempo (急 템포), have been more effective in clarifying the theme? It's regrettable that their lives and interactions were treated too lightly (等閑히) and <197> omitted, and even with effort, everything failed to converge on the theme, I think (생각함니다). Why do Hyŏng-sik-kun and Sŏn-hyŏng-yang, who should have significant involvement in the plot development, give the impression of mere onlookers who just dropped by?

I have presented two reasons above. Please read the nonsensical words of an outsider with a smile.
I'll add a little more nagging.
In the scene where Yŏng-ch'ae and her maternal grandmother bite into whole apples (통사과), even a friend who would applaud a Western action film (西部 活劇) was heard muttering embarrassedly, 'What is that?' I felt the same (동감이었음니다).
The elder (長者?) hitting the kisaeng (妓生) during the boat excursion (船遊).
In Western action films, they shoot with a *pachinko* (?) and kill; in Japanese period dramas (日本時代劇), they stab chests with swords, but the opponent (相對) was always male. I (小生) am not a feminist, but it seemed a bit inappropriate (안된 것 같앳슴니다).
Discussing the actors (배우 云云) would be an unreasonable demand (無理한 要求) at this point, but couldn't you at least fix the child actors' (子役) lines (臺詞?), delivered as if reading from a book?
If the fact that all the characters moving in the first half wear white clothes (白衣) was unavoidable (不可避한) to highlight the special characteristics (特長) of Korean color (朝鮮色), then so be it, but the camera must have faced considerable difficulty (곤란받엇을 겝니다). Despite this, the viewer (見る人 亦) also reaches the point of blurred vision (亂視).
Whether to kill Yŏng-ch'ae or let her live at the end (終末) seems to have been considerably debated (論議) with the original author (原作者), but this is an important issue. It seems it's not a problem for the likes of me (小生等) to contemplate. However, ending with tearing the paper window (창호지)—didn't that harm the spiritual beauty (精神味) you strongly advocated (主張하신)? If you wanted to insist on that fidelity (志操), wouldn't it have been better to just kill her off, since she had already started down that path? In that case, couldn't you expect the effect (效果) of leading the audience towards appreciation or antipathy (逆?)? Wouldn't a groundless (根據 없는) compromise (妥協) leave behind the lament of being neither a monk nor a layman (俗漢)?

The above is the foolish and complacent whining of an outsider. Please forgive my many presumptuous words (妄言多辭).
Once again, I express my respect (敬意) to Director Pak Ki-ch'ae, who showed us a film truly worthy of the name in terms of technique, and wishing you a great future and good health, I conclude my writing (擱筆하나이다).
March 23rd
Pak Yŏng-hwan (朴暎煥)

For your esteemed perusal (玉机下) <198>

**Summary**

The article presents a dialogue between director Pak Ki-ch'ae and an acquaintance, K-kun, followed by an open letter to Pak from a fan, Pak Yŏng-hwan. Pak Ki-ch'ae defends his film *Mujŏng* against criticism, detailing the severe technical and logistical challenges faced during production (lack of proper studio, rushed schedules, primitive equipment). He argues that Korean critics often apply inappropriate foreign standards without understanding the realities of colonial Korean filmmaking and misinterpret directorial intent and technique. The open letter from Pak Yŏng-hwan praises *Mujŏng* as a step forward for Korean cinema compared to previous works but also offers detailed criticism regarding the film's slow opening, unclear theme, weak character development (especially for the main couple), and specific scenes deemed awkward or inappropriate, while questioning the ending's compromise.

From a historical perspective, the article is revealing about the nascent state and struggles of the Korean film industry under Japanese rule in the late 1930s. It highlights the technical gap between Korea, Japan, and the West, the director's frustration with inadequate resources and uninformed criticism, and the complex negotiation between artistic aspiration, commercial viability, audience expectation, and colonial reality. The discussion about representing "Koreanness" (customs, white clothes) and traditional values (female chastity) versus modern sensibilities provides insight into cultural debates of the time.

映畵批評界의 危機, 無情評의 讀後感
						朴基采
4, 5일 前 오래간만에 K君과 같이 봄소식을 드르러 창경원을 차저 갔었다.
원내를 한 바퀴 빙 도는 동안에 뻐근한 다리를 쉬기 위하야 K君과 나는 잔듸밭에 주저 안저버렸다. K君은 담배를 피여 물면서 곳자댓자로 말을 끄집어낸다.
「朴君 자넨 자네 작품에 대한 비평을 일일이 읽나?」
「웨? 읽기야 한 번식은 읽지」
「그래. 이번 『無情』에 대한 비평을 어떻게 생각하나」
「평필을 든 분들이 무정에서 아무 감흥을 느끼지 못한 것과 맛찬가지로 나도 비평에 대한 흥미를 느끼지 못하고 다만 一笑에 부처버렸지」
「그래서야 되나. 평가가 작품에 대한 결함을 지적할 땐 그를 반성을 하고 矯正을 해나가야 향상이 있지」
「그야 정당한 지적을 하면야 교정만 하겠나. 차저가서 감사하단 禮라도 드려야지. 그러나 朝鮮서 통용되는 영화비평이란 선진국의 영화비평에 대한 思想性과 政治性까지라도＜190＞ 문자 그대로를 『이미떼-슌』한데 불과하니까 말이지. 朝鮮映畵를 비평을 할 때 대체 비평의 着眼을 어데다 두는지 모르겠단 말야. 朝鮮映畵를 말을 하게될 땐, 朝鮮映畵가 어떤 과정을 발버서 제작되는 줄이나 알고서 평을 쓰든지 욕을 하든지 해야 할 것 아니야?」
「朝映에서는 그만한 설비를 해놓고 설비가 나뻐서 「無情」이 나뻤단 말은 못하겠지?」
「내가 작품을 실패하였다고서 결코 변명을 하는 말이 아니니 들어보게. 「무정」 촬영 개시가 朝映議政府 촬영소가 되기 전 작년 5월 19일이였는데 금년 3월 15일에 시장에 나왔으니 약 10개월의 시일을 要하였으나 撮影日數는 23일간에 「無情」이란 無聲版의 화면이 완성된 셈인데 더구나 全篇의 절반이 방안 장면이였는데 촬영소 내에서 「셋트」를 만드러서 촬영을 햇느냐 하면 그도 아니고 현재 사는 人家를 빌어서 원칙은 4, 5일간 그 장면의 촬영을 예정하여 노앗든 것을 밤낫 24시간에 끝을 마처 노아야 한다는 進行係의 돌연한 주문이니 그도 촬영 개시 전에 이런 주문이 있었다면 다른 방법이라도 강구해보지지마는 촬영 중도에 이런 말을 하니 할 수 없이 이 장면은 아무케나 촬영만을 완료식혀야겠다는 것이 머리에 가득할 뿐 이였으니이까 조선영화 제작자로서 절망을 느끼든 순간이란 말이야」
「그럼 신축한 議政府 촬영소에서는 촬영을 못햇군 그래」
「촬영소가 다-먼가. 촬영소가 되기 전이니까 방안은 人家를 빌어 한간 半에다가 「라이트」를 디려노코 하구 결혼식장은 朝鮮日報社 大講堂을 빌어서 電力 17키로를 어더 하고 新聞社는 역 朝鮮日報社 社會部를 빌어서 하구 햇으니 비평에는 배우 동작이 「ウゴキ」가 없다고 햇지마는 가령 배우가 서야 할 때도 스면 카매라를 멀니 놀 수가 없어 배우의 고개가 原面 외에 떠러저 나갈테니 그리도 못하구 안저서만 하게되니 「ウゴキ」도 없게되지」
「無情을 1년 동안이나 끄집어 시장에 내논 이유는 어대 있나?」
「그야 남이 모를 중대한 원인이 있었지. 당연히 작년 8,9월경에 시장에 나와야만 할 것이였으나 朝映 간부들의 고집이 주식회사의 체면상 절대로 다른 곳에서는 녹음할 수 없다 하야 朝映의 녹음실이 완성되기를 기다려 일부 방음장치를 하면서 議政府 朝映錄音室에서 녹음„ 시작한 것이 지난 1월 하순이였고 有音映畵로서 試寫를 본 것이 2월 하순이였으니 이를 문제를 삼을라면야 말이야 조치 朝映創立紀念作이요. 완전한 설비를 가진 朝映이 1년이란 시일을 要하야 세상에 내놋는 영화라구 某新聞學生*에 城大學生이 無情을 보고서 가장 자본과 설비가 구비된 朝映이 그 전력을 기우리여 제작한＜191＞ 창립기념작이란데 더 실망을 느겻다고 쓴 것을 본 기*이 있지마는 이는 단지 無情에 대한 실망이겠지. 朝映에 대한 실망이 아닐 것이로되 無情을 제작하든 동기와 조건을 모르는 객관* 관중의 한 사람으로서 城大學生의 실망이 정당하였을지 모르지. 이도 朝鮮映畵를 사랑함으로서 실망을 하였을는지 모르지만 朝鮮映畵 관중은 「朝鮮에서 만드러 내는 朝鮮映畵」라는 것을 항상 염두에다 두어야 할 것이야. 웨 그러냐 하면 朝鮮映畵는 발전의 과정이 없이 기형적 형태에서 성장하여 나왓단 말야. 朝鮮映畵로서 無聲映畵에 있어서도 우리가 걸작이라고 할만한 영화가 어대 하나나 있었나. 남이 토-키를 만드니 그대로 우리도 토-키를 만들게 되였을 뿐이지」
「그야 朝鮮映畵 제작자의 고민이겠지. 영화 관중이야 朝鮮映畵라구 일일이 이해를 하고 볼 수 있나? 같은 요금을 내고 좋은 영화를 보는 것이 관중이야 당연하지. 그럿치만 朝鮮映畵라면 자네 말한 것과 같이 朝鮮사람이 만드러 낸 영화란데 호기심이야 있겠지」
「日前 文壇 앀‡던 친구가 「쭈리안데뷔붸」의 望鄕과 成瀨喜己男의 「はだらく一家」와 「無情」을 보고와서 「はだらく一家」도 유치하기 짝이 없지마는 無情보다는 좋은 데가 있드라는 말을 들을 땐 참 한심이 나드군. 세계적의 명작이란 「望鄕」과 日本 內地 우수작이란 「はだらく一家」와 「無情」을 비교해 밧다는 덴 무어라구 말할 수가 없었단 말야」
「그러고 관중의 수준이 노팟다는 것은 朝鮮映畵의 질적 향상에 좋은 도음이 되지 않을가?」
「그야 朝鮮映畵도 세계적 수준에 달할 영화를 못 맨든는다는 법이야 없겠지만 무었 한 가지 조건이 구비해야 말이지. 文章 4월호에도 朝鮮映畵界 이약이가 있지만 朝鮮映畵界의 實際를 모르는 朱永涉씨의 小論과 같은 理想的 空論이야 누구보다도 실제적으로 영화를 제작하고 있는 그 사람들이 더 잘 알고 있지.」
「대관절 자네가 만든 無情을 자낸 어떻게 생각하나?」
