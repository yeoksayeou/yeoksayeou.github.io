Report on Returning from the Battlefield and the Diet
Returning from the Battle Lines in Shanxi Province.............Kim Sŏk-wŏn, Major
Returning from Tokyo after the Suffrage Movement.......Chŏn Pu-il
Returning from Hong Kong and the Philippines.....................Yi Ryang, Lieutenant Colonel

Three-Year Record of the North China Campaign
Army Major Kim Sŏk-wŏn
(Major Kim returned in mid-March after participating in the war. This manuscript is a record of the main points of the battle* written down by a reporter from this company after receiving them.)
Since Major Kim went to war, there were hundreds of battles, including subjugations, advances, and annihilation campaigns. Among these, picking out the most horrifying and hard-fought battles, starting with the attack on Tuanhochen (團河村), also known as Hành Cung (行宮), the Battle of Nanyuan, the attack on Ling-shih (靈石), the capture of Hungwŏn Heights (洪原高地) and Tongwŏn Heights (東原高地), the engagement with Yan Xishan's (閻錫山) direct forces in Chi-hsien (吉縣) and Hsiang-ning (鄕寧), the Battle of Hsin*hsingho (新*興河), and the capture of Houma Town (候馬鎭), which was the most perilous situation in the North China War, falling into enemy encirclement for a time, followed by major battles in sixteen locations including Ch’ü-wo (曲沃), Hengling Pass (橫嶺關), Wen-hsi (聞喜), Yü-men-k’ou (禹門口), * County (*縣), and Ning-hsiang (甯鄕).
We will introduce the valiant battles fought relentlessly back and forth.
Furthermore, for the battles at Tuanhochen, Hungwŏn Heights, and Tongwŏn Heights <64>, he received a certificate of commendation from the Regimental Commander and a letter of appreciation from the Army Commander, allowing us to guess how fierce and intense the battles were. On July 27th of the year before last, our unit commander, Major Kim Sŏk-wŏn, marched ahead of the main regimental force from Tianjin (天津) towards the area south of Beijing (北京). Receiving orders to disembark at Huangtsun Station (黃村驛), pass through Tuanhochen, and advance to Sanhe Zhuang (三合莊), about 12 kilometers south of Beijing, he marched while maintaining strict vigilance, gathering intelligence from Chinese people along the way about the enemy lines. During the march, they unexpectedly engaged the enemy at Tuanhochen. This occurred the day before they anticipated it would be the first battle for the dispatched unit in North China. The sorghum (高粱) stood densely packed, over two *gil* tall, and the heat rose above 120 degrees Fahrenheit, making it difficult even to sit still, let alone march. The nearly 30-40 *ri* distance was almost entirely through sorghum fields over two *gil* tall, so the moment the sound of gunfire erupted, it felt like they were already surrounded by the enemy.
Then, as they gathered intelligence again while holding their breath, the following information came in.
This Tuanhochen, about 2 kilometers south of the destination Sanhe Zhuang, was already fortified with strong positions. Normally garrisoned by 2* reinforced battalions, about 5,000 reinforcements from the Chihli Army's 19th Route Army (直隸軍 19路軍) arrived from Baoding (保定) on the 26th. This place was formerly a temporary royal palace (假宮), surrounded by a large wall about 4 meters high, with multiple layers of defensive fortifications (複廓防禦). Outside the wall, ditches were dug, and on the outer perimeter, there was a 40-meter high ground with several deep defensive positions (縱深陣地). Recognizing the nature of the terrain and the enemy's strong fortifications, they understood this point was crucial for the advance of the Emperor's sacred army (聖軍) in conquering North China. To annihilate the enemy, they deployed the battle line about 800 meters from the enemy front at 2 PM on the 27th and began the attack.
However, the enemy had deployed dozens of mortar shelters (掩蓋), heavy* machine guns, and automatic rifles in their foremost positions. About 100 meters directly in front of their lines, they had laid out numerous obstacles, and built "pillboxes" (토치카) at crucial points to block our army's advance. Furthermore, realizing the danger if our side set fire to the sorghum fields, they cut down the sorghum to create a fire control zone (火制地帶) and established points of concentrated fire (火力急*點), employing fire* tactics to ** ** ** ** *** ** *** <65> new positions and
laying telephone lines, intensifying their surveillance of our side. Moreover, it was the peak of summer heat, a scorching 130 degrees Fahrenheit. Except for the fire control zone where the sorghum had been cleared in front of the positions, the fields were filled with sorghum over two *gil* tall, obstructing the view, and there was no breeze. Our officers and soldiers suffered from thirst and hunger, collapsing, and the scene was described as indescribably tragic. However, our Commander Kim endured everything and issued the order to attack and advance like autumn frost. The assault began with the 9th Company, machine* *squads, and battalion artillery providing relentless suppressive fire, like an iron wall. As our side initiated the attack, the enemy lines responded with even more intense fire from various weapons,
causing some casualties among our officers and soldiers. The battle became truly desperate for a time, and the unit led by Commander Kim continued to fight hard.
At this point, Commander Kim urged his officers and soldiers to once again deeply engrave the words "Loyalty and Patriotism" (盡忠報國) in their hearts. Burning with the spirit of self-sacrifice for the public good (滅私奉公), the soldiers raised their spirits further and finally managed to charge. However, the enemy, under the command of the Battle Oversight Team (督戰隊), showed no signs of easily yielding their stubbornly held first line of defense. This was the moment of highest tension for both sides. Then, Captain Yano*** (矢野 大尉=前**少將 令息), asking why they hesitated when they could charge*, drew his long sword and ordered the front-line soldiers to attack. At this, Second Lieutenant Yano (矢野 少尉) also bravely rushed to the forefront, stormed the enemy position, jumped into the trench*, cut down dozens of enemy soldiers, and let out a great cry.
Commander Kim himself climbed onto the trench parapet, and with the charge of the rear transport*, finally captured a corner of the Hành Cung high ground. In this battle, the officers and men under our commander not only engaged in naked hand-to-hand combat but also endured various hardships, including concentrated fire and deploying troops in the opposite direction. Meanwhile, the enemy retreated but counterattacked several times with night raids,
which were consistently repelled. Just then, the order for the Nanyuan (南苑) offensive arrived, and Major Kim's unit, without rest, marched forward, trampling over the abandoned enemy corpses and fallen military horses. At this time, an ordinary person would have been utterly exhausted, but this unit, having undergone all sorts of training and being strongly united in spirit, continued its advance even after such a difficult battle.<66>
Marching for 15 *ri*, thirst became severe, and some soldiers collapsed. During the Nanyuan attack, friendly aircraft finally began operations, and traces of the brave battle still remained. Thus, after four days of sleepless and restless marching since the 27th, they plunged into the enemy's crossfire (十字砲), striking their heartland like a thunderbolt. The enemy fought back with their last desperate efforts, utilizing the full capacity of all their weapons. However, just as Major Kim was about to take off his boots to rest his weary legs for even one night, his right ankle suddenly felt sharp pain, making it impossible to sit or stand. His subordinates repeatedly urged him to retreat for treatment, but he refused. Riding a horse and relying on others to help drag his leg, he continued marching for five days until a certain cadre forcibly hospitalized him. Our Commander Kim, finally yielding, reluctantly knocked on the hospital door, clutching his sword and swallowing his tears.
While hospitalized, Major Kim prayed day and night for a swift recovery. Consequently, in mid-November of the year before last, he was discharged and bravely returned to command on the front line. In February of last year, he carried out a subjugation operation near P’ing-ting (平定). On February 24th, he participated in the battle west of Ling-shih (靈石), achieving brilliant results with courage multiplied a hundredfold. He also fought fiercely for four days at Hungwŏn Heights (洪原高地) and Tongwŏn Heights (東原高地), steadily gaining military merit.
After the Hojin (河津) and Chiksan (稷山) battles, near Chi-hsien (吉縣) and Hsiang-ning (鄕寧), they fought against Yan Xishan's (閻錫山) direct forces. After ten days of engagement, they destroyed the enemy in both locations, but they were reportedly surprised by the enemy's military strategy and various preparations
at that time. Hsinfêng (新豐), the second largest emerging city in Shanxi Province, even had electricity and textile factories. In this area and towards Hsing-ho (興河), they fought fiercely for ten days, braving the extreme cold. The battle at Houma Town (候馬鎭) was reportedly no less intense than those at Tuanhochen, Hungwŏn Heights, and Tongwŏn Heights. Furthermore, due to a lack of provisions, Commander Kim Sŏk-wŏn's unit resorted to eating cat and dog meat while fighting, painting a vivid picture of the situation. For a month, enemy shells, 500 or 1,000 a day, flew relentlessly,
making it impossible to stay in one place for even 30 minutes, day or night. The story that during the Houma battle, they could only eat one meal a day due to lack of food is heartbreaking, and the fact that both sides advanced and retreated due to ammunition shortages suggests that there was no harder battle throughout the entire North China front.<67>
Enduring such difficulties with a spirit of sleepless dedication, they advanced and advanced again, reaching the Battle of Ch’ü-wo (曲沃). Next, they achieved brilliant results in the Hengling Pass (橫嶺關) and Wen-hsi (聞喜) battles. They participated again in the Chi-hsien and Hsiang-ning battles from Yü-men-k’ou (禹門口), fighting in snow deeper than one's height. Although the enemy force was only 300, making the subjugation
much easier than the first battle, the geographical disadvantage made it a tough fight.
In Ning-hsiang (甯鄕), the retreat route of the enemy forces trapped within the encirclement of friendly forces (友軍) lay directly in front of Commander Kim's position. The story of how, from morning till evening, they rained fire from the high ground on both sides, annihilating the enemy pouring out through the valley, makes listeners hold their breath. Now, I will conclude this article by introducing the story of the capture of Hungwŏn Heights and Tongwŏn Heights, among the tales of our commander's fierce struggles and military achievements.
On February 24th of last year, during the attack on the enemy positions near Ling-shih (靈石), Commander Kim's unit, acting as the Right Flank Corps (右翼隊),
advanced towards the high ground north of Fengtsun (馮村) at dawn after reaching the vicinity of Hsia-liu-chia (下柳家) during the night. Near Fengtsun, they unexpectedly encountered the enemy's 54th Division (第54師) in a meeting engagement. Witnessing the enemy's main force of about 5,000 executing an enveloping maneuver (包圍迂廻) from Hungwŏn Heights, Commander Kim Sŏk-wŏn received orders to attack them. Leading his subordinates, he swiftly and boldly deployed his troops on the treacherous high mountain peaks with crumbling rocks and sheer cliffs, launching a flank attack on the enemy, driving them back before they could fully engage. Thus, they cornered the confused and routed enemy main force into mountain ravines with cliffs tens of feet high, subjecting them to concentrated bombardment from all sides, crippling the 54th Division's main strength so it could not recover. Furthermore,
pursuing the fleeing enemy remnants, they advanced to Tongwŏn Heights by noon on the 25th. Suddenly, the enemy's 85th Division (第85師), about 3,000 strong, attacked from the northwest. Commander Kim's unit, mustering fresh courage, launched a bold counterattack and overwhelmed the enemy. Although the enemy gradually reinforced their troops, surrounded our position, and fiercely attacked with mountain artillery (山砲) and mortars, launching over twenty counterattacks day and night, our troops fought fiercely and finally achieved victory at 4 AM on the 26th. -(End)- <68>

Suffrage and the Tokyo Diet
Tokyo Return Committee Member Chŏn Pu-il
General Affairs Secretary, Kokumin Kyōkai
(This spring, Mr. Chŏn Pu-il went to Tokyo representing the Kokumin Kyōkai (國民協會) to campaign for Korean suffrage during the Imperial Diet session. Upon his return, he and another executive of the Kokumin Kyōkai responded as follows:)
Q: What were the achievements of your activities in Tokyo this year?
A: We brought a petition requesting the granting of suffrage and fought hard for about three weeks while the Diet was in session. Ultimately, it was adopted unanimously by the subcommittee.
Q: Isn't that something that happens every year? For over ten years, hasn't it been the case, year after year, that the Petition Committee adopts it, Diet members give speeches in favor, and then the government representative cites "premature timing" as a reason to postpone it until the next year? I'm not asking about such formalized, habitual results. Given that since the outbreak of the China Incident (支那事變), the loyalty of the Korean populace (朝鮮民衆) to the nation has been expressed significantly in both material and spiritual ways, the general public expected that the central government's attitude towards the Korean suffrage issue would surely be different from previous years, and anticipated some results this year. Did it still end with just the adoption of the petition this year as well?
A: Although the government representative did respond that "Since the outbreak of the China Incident, Korean compatriots (朝鮮同胞) have demonstrated patriotic devotion (愛國의 赤*을 披*), but the level of education in Korea (朝鮮) is only one-third that of the mainland (內地) in terms of dissemination (elementary education), and the national language (國語) is only spoken by about 11 out of 100 people..." giving this as a reason for further study, on the other hand, observing the atmosphere in the Diet*, until the year before last, there was a tendency among Diet members to completely disregard the Korean suffrage issue. However, influenced by the Incident, their perception of Korea has significantly changed*, and they treated our movement with respect. Therefore, this time*, I endeavored to explain the situation in Korea to about one hundred and several tens of members from both the House of Peers and the House of Representatives <69>. The unanimous adoption of the petition in the subcommittee can be considered a significant achievement.
Q: How do you conduct your campaign when you go to Tokyo? Whose coaching do you receive?
A: Generally, we first seek out members of the House of Peers and House of Representatives who have understanding and sympathy for Korean issues (朝鮮問題). We meet with individuals like Pak Ch’un-gŭm (朴春琴), Maruyama Tsurukichi (丸山鶴吉), Mizuno Rentarō (水野鍊太郞), and Baron Sakatani (坂谷男爵) to discuss campaign methods. Then, we visit the members of the Petition Committee, either at their private residences or the Diet Building (議事堂), provide them with the draft petition and reference materials we brought, and seek their understanding.
Q: Does the petition submitted by the Kokumin Kyōkai include attached signatures of public support?
A: The year before last, we submitted it with signatures of support from 30,000 prominent figures from all over Korea (全 朝鮮). However, we cannot do that every year, so this year we submitted it with signatures of support from one mainland Japanese (內地人) and one Korean (朝鮮人) member from each Provincial Council (道議會), totaling 26 people.
Q: Does your association advocate for the immediate application of the current universal suffrage law, as implemented in the House of Representatives elections, to Korea? Or do you propose a special modified approach, such as limiting suffrage based on tax payment qualifications or implementing it first in urban areas (都會地)?
A: In principle, we hope for the same universal suffrage law (普選法) currently implemented on the mainland (內地 本州). However, given the reality that the level of culture and institutions in Korea has not yet reached parity, we are currently advocating for its implementation first regionally in important cities such as Kyŏngsŏng (京城), P’yŏngyang (平壤), and Pusan (釜山). We believe this would be best as a first step.
Q: If it were implemented in urban areas, what would be the ratio of Japanese (內鮮人) voters and elected officials?
A: As you know, the influence of mainland Japanese (內地人) is predominant in urban areas, so we believe there wouldn't be an imbalance.
Q: Approximately how many people have been mobilized in support of the suffrage movement so far? Around 100,000?
A: More than that. Until 5 or 6 years ago, there were times when 50,000 to 60,000 people were mobilized annually. Also, 30,000 the year before last... it's probably around 200,000 people.
Q: And how much funding has been spent? If you calculate the total amount spent on the suffrage movement since its inception?
Q: The entire establishment and activities of our Kokumin Kyōkai (including the current publication of the weekly newspaper Minjung Sinmun (民衆新聞)) were for the suffrage movement <70>, so adding it all up, the expenses since its founding probably amount to 300,000 to 400,000 won.
Q: About how many years has it been since the start of the Diet campaign?
A: It's been nearly 20 years. It's already been 18 years since Chairman Min Wŏn-sik (閔元植) was sacrificed in Tokyo. During that time, since Taishō 9 (1920), we have submitted proposals to the government 17 times and petitions to the House of Representatives 9 times.

From Hong Kong and the Philippines
Manchukuo Army Lieutenant Colonel Yi Ryang
(A few days after returning from Hong Kong, I met Mr. Yi on the terrace of the Chōsen Hotel in Seoul one late spring day and had this conversation. Lieutenant Colonel Yi took out a notebook from his breast pocket, and tucked inside was a letter of introduction from a general at the Imperial Headquarters (參謀本部) in Tokyo. While his missions wandering through Central and South China were beyond our knowing, on this day, we decided to avoid political discussions and only listen to his travel experiences.)

Koreans in Hong Kong
Reporter: "I hear it hasn't been long since you came from Hong Kong (香港). I'd like to hear about the situation in South China (南支). Are there any Koreans in Hong Kong?"
Lt. Col. Yi: "There are a few people. I only met two or three, but I hear there are probably 10 or 20."
Reporter: "Are they doing well with substantial businesses*? Were there any successful individuals?"
Lt. Col. Yi: "Most seem to have gone to South China intending to advance into the Nanyang Archipelago (南洋群島) or the Philippines (比律賓), but ended up lingering around Guangdong (廣東) or Hong Kong, eventually settling down. They seem to be making a living as patent medicine sellers (賣藥商) or brokers (居間). There didn't appear to be anyone with significant economic power. Even in Shanghai (上海) or Nanjing (南京), as peace and order have been restored since the China Incident (支那事變), the Koreans there have taken over management of decent <71> factories, and some have entered large banks or companies, holding high-level positions, gradually establishing their social and economic status. But in South China, their influence is very weak."
Reporter: "Was the Korean presence (着足) in Guangdong also that insignificant?"
Lt. Col. Yi: "I don't know about before the incident. I heard there were some associated with the Party Headquarters (黨部) or the Municipal Government (市政府系), but I have no news after the Imperial Army's (皇軍) occupation. There isn't even a single influential commercial presence established by a Korean in the city, which felt desolate."
Reporter: "What about the future? Is there potential for development in the Hong Kong or Guangdong area of South China, or perhaps on Hainan Island (海南島)?"
Lt. Col. Yi: "It's still wartime, so it's hard to say anything about industry. Of course, since it's Imperial Army occupied territory, once political stability and public order are established, we too can advance into that region. However, generally speaking, commerce and industry are highly developed in the South China region, comparable to the Osaka (大阪) or Kobe (神戸) areas. Therefore, without considerable agility, skill (手腕), and capital backing, it won't be easy. Also, Southerners are different from Northerners; they are very clever. Especially since they have a long tradition of trading with places like the Malay Peninsula (馬來半島) or Australia (濠太利), unless one possesses exceptional business talent (商才), it will be difficult to compete (結抗) with them."
Reporter: "Are the people currently there also mostly ginseng peddlers or patent medicine sellers?"
Lt. Col. Yi: "Probably mostly that type."
Reporter: "Are there no wealthy individuals who have saved tens or hundreds of thousands of won?"
Lt. Col. Yi: "I haven't heard of any."
Reporter: "What were your thoughts observing the British influence in Hong Kong?"
Lt. Col. Yi: "Hong Kong feels less like a British territory (英領) and more like the British homeland (英本國) itself, perhaps London (倫敦) or Manchester (만체스타-). The city streets are entirely made of ** and marble, and the corners are adorned like parks with flowers and trees (花草와 樹木), truly elegant and luxurious beyond description. The British people there all seemed to be big bourgeoisie (뿌로조아).
At night, I stood on the shore and gazed at the cityscape for a long time. The multi-storied stone buildings forming a 'city that never sleeps' (不夜城)!
A sigh escaped me involuntarily. Those fellows (년석들은)
came with their flag and pounds (*드 - 磅) and exploited China (支那) as a semi-colony to live so well. If we Asians could unite and avoid being exploited by Britain and America (英米) <72>, couldn't we also develop our resources (富源) with our own hands and achieve such a luxurious lifestyle?
Reporter: Is the entire population of Hong Kong composed of such high-class citizens?
Lt. Col. Yi: Not at all. If you go to the 'back streets' (裏町), there are slums where lowly Chinese people (支那 賤民) live. It's truly a wretched existence amidst stench and disease (惡疫), like a place where cattle or livestock (牛馬나 가축) live. Truly, tears of indignation (悲憤) well up. The luxury of the British and Americans versus the miserable lives of the native Chinese.

Koreans in the Philippines
Reporter: When did you visit India (印度)?
Lt. Col. Yi: India was already several years ago, before the China Incident (支那事變).
Reporter: Let's not ask about India here. What about the Philippines (比律賓)?
Lt. Col. Yi: I just visited a few months ago. I heard there are several dozen Koreans in the capital, Manila (마닐나). Someone supposedly from Ŭiju (義州) in P’yŏngan Province (平安道) is said to have established a foothold (地盤) and possesses several tens of thousands of won. I didn't meet any Koreans in the Philippines either; I just heard rumors (風聞).
Reporter: "If there are several dozen, what do they all do for a living?"
Lt. Col. Yi: "I don't know the details, but I heard many are itinerant merchants (行商) traveling around the Nanyang region. They carry miscellaneous goods (雜貨) on their backs, seeking out rural villages rarely visited by people, making a living like the 'peddlers' (박물장사) here. How well could they possibly be living?"
Reporter: "How many Americans live in the Philippines?"
Lt. Col. Yi: "About 10,000. Besides them, there are 7,000 garrison troops. So the total number of Americans (米國人) there is said to be 17,000."
Reporter: "Conversely, the number of Filipinos (比律賓人) who immigrated to America?"
Lt. Col. Yi: "Statistics say about 75,000 people."
Reporter: "The number of Japanese nationals (邦人) in the Philippines (比島)?"
Lt. Col. Yi: "Around 21,000, mostly engaged in large-scale farm management and fishing. The base for Japanese nationals is not so much Manila but rather Davao (다바-), located towards the Dutch East Indies (蘭領 印度), which has a first-class naval port (軍港). They are mainly active there, using it as a base for fishing."
Reporter: "Where are you heading now, leaving Seoul?"
Lt. Col. Yi: "After finishing lunch with Mr. Song OO (宋OO) today, I will head straight to North China (北支)." <73>

**Summary**

This 1939 article from *Samch’ŏlli* magazine presents three distinct reports from Koreans involved in different spheres of the Japanese Empire. Major Kim Sŏk-wŏn provides a detailed, pro-Japanese military account of his intense combat experiences in North China, emphasizing bravery and hardship. Chŏn Pu-il discusses the Kokumin Kyōkai's persistent but largely unsuccessful efforts to gain suffrage for Koreans in the Japanese Diet, noting a slight shift in Japanese attitudes post-China Incident but continued governmental reluctance. Lieutenant Colonel Yi Ryang offers observations on the relatively marginal economic status of Koreans in Hong Kong and the Philippines, contrasting it with British colonial luxury and Japanese economic presence, and reflecting on pan-Asian possibilities. The article is interesting for Korean colonial history as it juxtaposes the experiences of a Korean officer fighting for Japan, the political maneuvering for rights within the empire, and observations on Korean diaspora life and colonial dynamics in other parts of Asia, revealing complex attitudes towards Japanese expansion, colonial hierarchy, and Korean identity/aspirations during wartime. Historians might find value in the firsthand account of battles by a Korean officer in the Japanese army, the detailed description of the suffrage movement's tactics and frustrations, and the colonial perspectives on Southeast Asia.

지나인들을 모하가지고 적진의 정보를 모흐며 행군하다가 도중 단가촌에서 적군과 뜻하지 안었든 교전을 하게 되얏다. 이것이 북지 출정부대의 처음 싸움이 될 줄을 예상하기 전날의 일인데 高梁은 두 길식 백백하게 들어찻고 더위는 120도를 오르나리여서 행군은 고사 고 거저 안저있서도 가삼이 탁 멕히는 때인데 3, 40리를 거이 전부 두 길이 넘는 고량밧 이여서 탄환 소리가 나는 순간에 벌서 적진의 포위 속에 드러선 듯 십헛*고 한다.

기자 「比律賓에는 米國사람이 얼마나 사는가요」
李 中佐 「약 1만명 있지요. 이밖게 駐屯하는 군대가 7,000명. 이리하여 米國人이 와 있는 總數가 17,000명이라 해요」
기자 「그 반대로 米國에 이주한 比律賓人 數는?」
李 中佐 「약 75,000人이라고 통계에 났어요」
기자 「比島에 있는 邦人의 數는?」
李 中佐 「약 21,000명 정도인데 대개가 큰 농장경영과 어업 등에 종사하고 있지요. 그리고 邦人의 근거지는 마닐나보다도 蘭領 印度의 방향에 있는 「다바-」라는 곳으로 第一流의 軍港 있는 곳인데 주로 여기를 어업 근거지로 하고 활약하고 있어요.」
기자 「이제 당신은 어데로 가요. 서울을 떠나서?」
李 中佐 「오늘 점심을 宋OO씨와 함께 마치고 이 길로 北支로 가겠소이다」＜73＞
