**Body Text Translation:**
Singer Wang Su-bok, Who Wants to Go to Italy

The third day of the third lunar month is approaching, and on the weeping willows drooping over the Ryŏn'gwang Pavilion (練光亭) by the Taedong River (대동강), light green leaves are beginning to appear. Perhaps because of this, the water of the Taedong River seems clearer and redder on a certain day...

P'yŏngyang's (평양) Sinch'ang-ni (新昌里) is the neighborhood where the Kisaeng School (妓生學校) is located. Feeling the footsteps of evening strollers (夕陽 遊客) naturally lighten amid the riverside willow branches fluttering listlessly, I turned into an elegant alley of Sinch'ang-ni and stood before a beautifully painted gate. This is the home of Miss Wang Su-bok (王壽福), who once reigned as the queen of the record world, making the ladies and gentlemen of the capital weep with her tender songs, and who is now in Tokyo, wholeheartedly devoting herself to vocal studies. I was able to meet her in her room, as she had returned after a year for her mother's memorial service.

Inside the room stood a life-sized full-length mirror, and on the bookshelf were several original Italian books and novels like "Gone with the Wind" (風と共に去りぬ), displaying the high level of culture of this hostess.

Reading and the Change in Her Thoughts

With eyes full of wonder, I looked at her plump-looking face and decided to probe this young lady's mind.
"Have you read 'Gone with the Wind'?"
"Yes. I already read the first volume in Tokyo, and I brought the second volume back this time and am reading it in my spare time."
"It was greatly welcomed in American reading society, but it's truly surprising and delightful that it has even reached your bookshelf."
"Why? Are we not supposed to read it? Hohoho. The subject matter, drawn from the American Civil War, is quite captivating. The style is fresh and the descriptions <118> are good too."
"What else have you read?"
"'Madame Curie' (규-리 夫人傳)."
I actually hadn't yet read this famous work, which reportedly sold 500,000 copies in the UK and US, was translated into 17 languages, and is already on its 20th or 30th printing in Tokyo. But Miss Wang had apparently already read it through. How embarrassing.
"Was it such a good work?"
To my question, she shook her head gently and said,
"It's not a novel. Rather than a novel, it's the biography of a great woman. But it moves one's heart more than a novel. It deeply touches the reader. The author isn't a famous novelist either, but Madame Curie's daughter, a woman named 'Ève'. It's just written truthfully and gracefully."
"What is the content?"
"You know radium, which brought about a great revolution in today's scientific civilization? It depicts Madame Curie's tearful efforts leading up to the discovery of radium. Madame Curie was born into a poor family, apparently. So she worked as a governess in the countryside, then came to Paris (巴里) as a poor student, married a genius physicist, and after all sorts of hardships, discovered the element radium. Then her husband died, and when the war broke out, she went around as a military nurse. It's the record of that noble woman who perfected her *innate talent* (天分) amidst this effort and hardship, and there was truly much to be gained from it."
"And any other *books* (*書)?"
"Gide's (지이드) 'Strait is the Gate' (좁은 門). And then *older* (*날) <119> things. I read a bit of Dostoevsky (터스터 엡스키-). Like 'Crime and Punishment' (罪와 罰). But though I read them, what do I really understand? Hohoho!"
"What about Korean works?"
"Last year when going to Tokyo, I borrowed Teacher Ch'unwŏn's (春園) 'Beyond Love and Lust' (愛慾의 彼岸) from Sŏnu Il-sŏn (鮮于一扇) and recently read it, and nowadays I feel like reading 'Love' (사랑)."

Longing for Italy

"Have your singing skills improved much?"
"Art, especially music, requires innate talent, but what talent do I have? However, I've learned a lot, including vocal techniques (發聲法), for two years."
"Are you formally attending a music school?"
"I attended one at first, but now I'm receiving private lessons from a famous female teacher. You probably know her too, sir. Merittora (메리트라)? Ch'ae Sŏn-yŏp (蔡善葉) also studied under her. She's a Japanese woman (內地人) who married the famous Italian poet Merirato (메리라토), lived in Italy for a long time, returned after her husband's death, and teaches at the Ueno Music School (上野音樂學校). They say her teaching method is better than Fujiwara Yoshie's (*原義江) or Miura Tamaki's (三浦環), and she's highly regarded throughout the country. And she's a middle-aged lady full of warmth. This time too, she said she's traveling to the Kansai region (關西地方), so I should take my time relaxing at home until the 20th of this month (April) and then return."
"After you've learned everything you can from her? Will you come back to Korea?"
"Originally, when I broke ties with Polydor (포리돌-) and rushed off to Tokyo, it was because I had resolved to study for about 10 years. But now, barely three years have passed, so how could I come back already? I'll study a bit more."
"To Italy (伊太利)?"
"I'm always dreaming and longing for it. Teacher Merittora also tells me I must go once. But who will pay the tuition? Staying in Italy costs 500-600 won per month due to the exchange rate (爲替關係). It's not that living expenses are so high, but one has to take private lessons from music professors there, and it costs 20 won per hour. I'd need to take about three hours a week, so how much would the lesson fees alone be? The desire to go burns like fire, but the funds are nowhere in sight." <120>
"According to a recent article in the Osaka Asahi (大阪朝日記事), just as Miss Ch'oe Sŭng-hŭi (崔承喜) brings glory to Korean art worldwide through dance, you too wish to greatly glorify Korean art through music, especially through Korea's unique folk songs."
"Yes. I think so. If only I had talent like Miss Ch'oe Sŭng-hŭi, I would want to do that. Even if I can't, I intend to put forth all my effort to become like that. Aren't Korean folk songs wonderful? Truly, in the past, I sang a tune or two just mimicking others without knowing anything, but now, as I gradually learn what music is and what vocal technique (發聲法) is, looking back, I realize that Korean folk songs are truly great works of art with a superior local color (로켈 칼나). I have only just discovered that now. However, just leaving them as they are in their traditional form is useless. I believe they will become a unique music that can reach a global level if they are rearranged in a Western music style and sung with Western music vocal techniques. Therefore, to revive folk songs, there must be outstanding arrangers (編曲者) and singers (歌人) who sing with the new vocal techniques."
"I heard that last fall at the Military Hall (軍人會舘) in Tokyo, you broke your long silence and sang, receiving rave reviews. Did you also sing Korean folk songs then?"
"Yes. As I just mentioned, I tried singing Korean folk songs following Western vocal techniques as a first experiment, and fortunately, it was well-received. The songs I sang were several Italian songs and also the very old-style 'Arirang' (아리랑)."
"If you were to arrange and sing them in a Western style, besides 'Arirang,' have you specifically thought about *other* (*은) folk songs?"
"That melody 'Eolneolleo sangsa dwi' (얼널너상사 뒤) from the Nongbuga (農夫歌), a type of farmers' music sung in the Samnam (三南) region, or the refrain (後念) of Yangsan-do (梁山道) – don't they all contain a gentleness and Korean charm hard to find in the folk songs of any other country?"
Saying this, Miss Wang softly releases her beautiful voice and lets me hear several melodies. Indeed, they are surprisingly beautiful. It seems even the modern populace, who hadn't paid attention to old songs, would give these絶大한好評 (great acclaim).
"I know that reviving folk songs is the first step to reviving the traditional <121> music of the people. Teacher Metorari (메토라리) always says that too. To become a world-class vocalist with the songs originating from one's own homeland. No matter how well one sings in Italian, how could one possibly match an Italian? Moreover, she says that without something from one's own homeland, truly vital music cannot emerge. I believe all her words are correct."
"Those are wonderful words. Please succeed by all means. Revive Korean folk songs globally and then return."
"Thank you. In Tokyo, I don't socialize much, and although I receive many requests to perform at concerts, I avoid them all and intend to strive single-mindedly along this path until I feel confident in myself."
As she spoke, her passionate eyes shone with the wisdom, intelligence, and excitement found only among artists. Until ten o'clock at night, so late, I listened to the young lady's story with a respectful mind before leaving. I am deeply satisfied that in the future, the Peninsular music scene will gain a lofty new figure. <122>

**Summary**

This 1939 interview profiles Wang Su-bok, a famous Korean singer originally trained at a kisaeng school, who is pursuing advanced Western vocal training in Tokyo. She discusses her studies under a Japanese teacher with Italian experience, her intellectual interests (reading Western literature like *Gone with the Wind* and *Madame Curie*), and her strong desire to study music in Italy, though hindered by financial constraints. Wang expresses a passionate commitment to reviving traditional Korean folk songs (like Arirang and Nongbuga) by arranging them in a Western style and singing them with Western vocal techniques, aiming to elevate Korean music to a global stage, much like dancer Ch'oe Sŭng-hŭi was doing for Korean dance. For historians, the article is revealing about the cultural aspirations and challenges faced by Korean artists during the Japanese colonial period. It shows the intersection of traditional Korean arts (kisaeng background, folk music) with modern Western influences (literature, classical music training, global ambitions) and underlying nationalist sentiment expressed through cultural preservation and promotion, even while studying under Japanese tutelage and navigating the realities of the colonial system. Wang's cosmopolitan reading habits and specific plans for modernizing folk music offer insight into the intellectual and artistic currents among educated Koreans at the time.

伊太利 가려는 王壽福 歌姬
삼월 삼진날도 갓가워 대동강까 練光亭에 느러진 수양버들에도 연두색 푸른 닢이 달니기 시작한다. 그리보니 그런가 대동강 물빛도 더 맑고 더 붉어지는 어느 날...

평양 新昌里라면 바로 妓生學校 있는 동리라. 시름 없이 나붓기는 강가 버들가지에 夕陽 遊客의 발자곡이 저절노 가비워짐을 늣기면서 나는 그 신창리의 아담한 골목을 도라 곱게 단청 칠한 문깐에 섰다. 여기가 한때는 레코-드게에 그 연연한 노래로서 장안 士女를 울려 王者의 位에 군림하엿고 지금은 동경에 드러가 歌謠修業에 一心精進하고 있는 미쓰 王壽福씨의 집이다. 어머님 소상으로 일년만에 나온 氏를 나는 그 방에서 마지할 수 있었다.
방 안에는 等身大의 體鏡이 노였고 書架 우엔 伊太利의 原書 몇 권과 「風と共に去りぬ」의 소설 등 멫 책이 있어 이 여주인공의 敎養의 놉흠를 보이고 있섯다.

讀書와 그의 思想의 變化
나는 驚異의 눈으로 그의 裕福스럽게 생긴 얼골을 처다 보며 이 색시의 머리를 打診키로 했다.
「이 風と共に去りぬ를 보서요.」
「네. 첫 권은 이미 東京서 보았고 그 하권을 이번 가지고 나와서 틈틈이 보고 잇서요.」
「米國 讀書社會에서는 크게 환영 받었다 하나 이것이 이 宅이 書架에까지 실닐 줄은 참으로 놀납고도 깁분 일인데요.」
「웨요. 우리는 보아서 안 되나요. 호호호 그 아메리까의 南北戰爭에 취재한 것이 퍽으나 마음을 끄러요. 문체도 참신하고 묘사도＜118＞ 조쿠요.」
「또 무얼 읽었어요?」
「규-리 夫人傳.」
英米에서 50만 부가 팔였고 17개 국어로 번역되였고 東京서도 벌서 20版인가 30版째인가 나온다는 이 유명한 작품을 실은 나는 아직 보지 못하였다. 그런데 미쓰 王은 벌서 通讀하였다 한다. 붓그러운 일이다.
「그러케 조흔 작품이든가요.」
하는 물음에 그는 고개를 살네살네 흔들면서
