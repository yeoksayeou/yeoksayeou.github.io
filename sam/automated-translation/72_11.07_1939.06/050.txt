**Body Text Translation:**

To Master Yi Kwang-su - A Gathering of Female Writers Asking About Literature, Love, and Religion

We hosted the literary giant Ch'unwŏn [Yi Kwang-su's pen name] and three renowned female writers of the time to discuss literature, love, and religion, seeking to understand the full picture of Ch'unwŏn. We only fear there might be inaccuracies in our company reporter's record...
Mo Yun-suk
Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi
Yi Sŏn-hŭi
Our Company's Kim Tong-hwan <46>

**Master's View on Women**

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Looking at "Mujŏng" (Heartlessness), you depicted the sentiments of kisaeng (female entertainers) like Yŏngch'ae and Kye Wŏl-hyang beautifully. Have you, Master, occasionally visited kisaeng houses yourself?

Yi Kwang-su - I have read with fondness the writings of famous kisaeng like Hwang Chin-i.

Mo Yun-suk - The way you depicted it so vividly suggests you might have gone. Isn't there such a scene in "Mujŏng"? In P'yŏngyang, a young child kisaeng lights a cigarette for Hyŏngsik, and her delicate fingertip gets burned red by the match head in the evening glow – such an impressive description doesn't seem like it could arise realistically from the author's imagination alone.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Also, the conversation Kye Wŏl-hyang has with Yŏngch'ae, the scent of the kŏmun'go, kayagŭm, and cosmetics (脂粉) that subtly stirs one's heart, passing softly and quietly like a gentle breeze in the May sky, yet passionately touching – that's the kisaeng house atmosphere (妓房 情調) we mean.

Ch'unwŏn - When I wrote "Mujŏng," I think it was during a summer vacation from Waseda University. On my way to Osan, I stopped by P'yŏngyang. A few friends dragged me out for an evening at a restaurant (料亭). It was an old-style restaurant right in front of Yŏngwang Pavilion (鍊光亭), with weeping willows hanging down. There, I listened to the kayagŭm and the "Susimga" (Song of Melancholy) and played around half-drunk.

Mo Yun-suk - So the kisaeng from that time entered "Mujŏng".

Ch'unwŏn - That experience might have had some influence. Later, I was dragged by another friend to visit a kisaeng room in Ch'aikwan-ni (釵貫里). There, I saw a truly innocent young girl, untouched by even a speck of dust. She was beautiful and lovely, and her impression remains unforgettable. If something unconsciously appeared in "Mujŏng," it might be that girl. <47>

Kim Tong-hwan - So you didn't engage in "romance" but merely appreciated her like looking at the moon!

Ch'unwŏn - It wasn't love, no.

Kim Tong-hwan - Who is your "Beatrice," Master? Besides Madam Hŏ Yŏng-suk, of course. Maybe one or two others...

Ch'unwŏn - None yet.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Even if not such an "eternal, beloved, mystical woman," perhaps a woman who deeply impressed you, who crossed your mind?

Ch'unwŏn - There are such people, yes.

Mo Yun-suk - Who might that be?

Ch'unwŏn - I don't know if you've read it, but it's the young girl mentioned in "Insaeng ŭi Hyanggi" (The Scent of Life), the one I played with, gathering pebbles during my boyhood. It was already about 30 years ago, in my hometown Osan in P'yŏngan Province. We played together gathering pebbles on the night of the first full moon of the year, and I can never forget her. She was only 14 or 15, but perhaps because I saw her face under the full moonlight that night, she looked so pure, beautiful, and noble. We didn't exchange a single word of love, yet her face remains unforgettable, as if it were today.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Have you met her since then?

Ch'unwŏn - Just once.

Mo Yun-suk - When?

Ch'unwŏn - A few years ago, I went to my hometown. There she was, drawing water with a bucket (드레박) from a well by the stream, filling her water jar. She was already married, had sons and daughters, perhaps even saw her daughter-in-law. I looked closely for a while, and it was indeed her. She also looked at me for a moment, then lowered her head again and continued drawing water. I don't know if she recognized me or not – the shadow of youth had long departed from her face, but still, the image of her from the old days when we played together gathering pebbles remained.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Please don't speak of such "faint, ephemeral lovers" (淡い はかない 戀人). Who else is there?

Ch'unwŏn - The one I met in Siberia, who appears in "Yujŏng" (Affection). <48>

Mo Yun-suk - Is it alright to take that as it is in the novel?

Ch'unwŏn - It was exactly like that. In that case, I tried to be faithful to the facts without mixing in imagination.

Kim Tong-hwan - People say Kim Il-lyŏn, who appears in "Ŏrin Pŏsege" (To a Young Friend), was "OO Sŏk," a student in Tokyo at the time. Was she also one of your "Beatrices"?

Ch'unwŏn - No. That's just a rumor (風說).

Kim Tong-hwan - So, regarding the women you've loved, Ms. Hŏ Yŏng-suk was the "first one" and also the "last one"?

Ch'unwŏn - Hahaha—That's right.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - In that case, let's choose women you haven't loved. (Everyone laughs loudly) Among those you've seen so far, who was the most beautiful?

Ch'unwŏn - In my 40 years, I've seen truly beautiful women exactly twice.

Kim Tong-hwan - Excluding Madam Hŏ, of course. (Everyone laughs)

Mo Yun-suk - Where?

Ch'unwŏn - In Siberia, right near Irkutsk (이루쿡쿠). **[censored text]** ***** ***** I was in Siberia ***, and it was early summer, May, when the birch trees (白樺) were just getting their sap. Unable to bear the nostalgia, I went for a stroll in the local park after dinner. On a bench over there sat a Russian (露西亞) girl, about 18 or 19 years old, truly beautiful. It was as if the moon had risen on earth. The area around her seemed luminous, as if enveloped by the mystical halo (圓光) behind a Buddha. Her skin was white, her eyes burned with passion. As you might know, twilight in Siberia lasts a very long time. A long period of dusk (薄明), neither dark nor bright, continues. Perhaps she seemed even more radiant because of that twilight, but she was truly a beauty. I've seen many Western women said to be beautiful in Shanghai or Siberia, but among those I've seen, she was the most, the most beautiful.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - And the other one?

Ch'unwŏn - And there's another time I saw one. It was when I was in Tokyo. ***. I went in to take the provincial line train (省線), and apparently, some *** was arriving, so on the platform ***** ****** **** **** *<49> there was a Japanese woman from the mainland (內地). She didn't seem like a maiden, perhaps a married woman. She was strikingly tall, her nose sharply defined as if carved, her posture and demeanor truly beautiful, hard to see twice. I gazed at her, mesmerized for a while. They say there's a sacred painting (聖畵) titled 'Holy Virgin' (聖處女); she looked exactly like that. She seemed to be a lady (閣氏) from some noble house. Like this, once in Siberia and once in Tokyo, I saw two Venus-like beauties.

Kim Tong-hwan - How do you view women, Master?

Ch'unwŏn - Whitman's (휫트멘) naturalistic poetry (自然主義的 詩歌) is astonishing. He boldly praises women. Isn't there a song praising the mystical body of woman under the blue sky, in the clear air? Probably every man feels something like Whitman.
Praising healthy love—and thus hoping to have healthy sons and daughters. Isn't this a grand aspect of humanity? Only because we must uphold social morals do we restrain desire (愛慾) and possess a sense of shame (羞恥) more than necessary...

Mo Yun-suk - Please tell us more about your view of women.

Ch'unwŏn - Longing for the opposite sex is human instinct. It's natural for a young woman to long for a young man. Even a sister follows her older brother, who is of the opposite sex, more than her younger sister; a father-in-law cherishes his daughter-in-law; a mother-in-law cherishes her son-in-law more—this isn't just about blood relations (血緣關係). The instinct to long for the opposite sex manifests in human psychology and behavior without one even realizing it.

Mo Yun-suk - Rousseau's (룻소) biography says he already longed for the opposite sex at thirteen or fourteen. Is that really true?

Ch'unwŏn - It can be. Most boys have sisters, so they understand the longing for the opposite sex through them. But a boy without sisters might feel a "faint longing" (淡い戀しさ) for an older woman, even if it's not romantic love. You see quite a bit of this in Maupassant's (모-팟상) works.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - In Browning's (부라우닝) poetry, didn't a 70-year-old poet long for the lips (입설) of a 17- or 18-year-old girl, saying "Young again," "Love again," <50> wishing to be young once more, to love once more? Have you experienced such feelings, Master?

Ch'unwŏn - Of course—not just me, but probably every man in this world does. Only, not in a physical sense.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Judging by what you depicted in "Kaech'ŏkcha" (The Pioneer) or "Kŭ Yŏja ŭi Ilsaeng" (That Woman's Life), you portrayed Platonic love (푸라트 러부), detached from the body, as a model of "pure love." Do you still hold a view of love that emphasizes only the spiritual aspect?

Ch'unwŏn - Love is pure, isn't it? It's the contact between souls ignited by fire, transcending money, status, and all interests, so isn't it somewhat sacred (神聖) and pure white (純白無垢)? I wish all "love" on earth could be so clean, but since "love" is something humans do, how can it be separated from the human body? Therefore, one seeks the union of spirit and flesh (靈肉의 合一).

Mo Yun-suk - What is the "love" you are thinking of now, Master? Do you consider the kind of love shown by Sŏksunok in the novel "Sarang" (Love) – such unnatural and self-sacrificing love – as the pinnacle of romance?

**Ch'unwŏn's View on Religion**

Ch'unwŏn - My thinking has changed. Compared to the time I wrote "Mujŏng" or "Kaech'ŏkcha," it has become very different by the time I wrote the current "Sarang."

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - How so?

Ch'unwŏn - Now, if love doesn't possess eternity—

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Eternity, you mean?

Ch'unwŏn - A young girl's body eventually becomes someone's wife, then an old woman, aging and decaying like rotten wood, inevitably facing a day of transient decline. If the object of love is the body, then when that body can no longer be the object of desire (愛慾), that love will surely collapse. Feeling disillusioned (幻*), love becomes a desolate, empty field in life, adding emptiness upon emptiness (空虛). <51>

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - So, "love" is?

Mo Yun-suk - You seem to be leaning towards the religious aspect.

Ch'unwŏn - When thinking of eternity, we cannot help but see everything religiously. Including "love," of course.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - If you see even love religiously?

Ch'unwŏn - Compared to the universe spanning hundreds of millions of years—what Buddhists (佛家) call eons (億劫)—isn't this 60 or 70 years of human life too short and frail? People are born, die, die, and are born again—eternally repeating reincarnation (輪廻). Even if one reincarnation cycle is 60 years, if you remove childhood and old age, life is merely twenty or thirty years. How much happiness can a person feel in this time? That's why one cannot help but forget the fleeting life (須臾의 인생) and think of eternal love.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - How can we believe in the next life (來生) when we don't know the previous life (前生)? We common folk (凡俗) only long for and cherish this present life (今生).

Ch'unwŏn - Ah, you don't understand. Because there was a previous life, the present life exists; because the present life exists, the next life exists. These three lives (三生) are our lives, eternally and ceaselessly revolving.

Mo Yun-suk - Is your theory of karma (因果說), which you emphasized so much in the recent "Sarang," also based on this?

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Karma (因果) is another name for fate (宿命) or destiny (運命). I dislike living under such a suffocating view of life. I understand that if you commit a sin, punishment follows, and if you do good deeds, good results (善果) come, and if you do bad deeds, the fruits of evil are served. But widening that scope significantly, to say that suffering in this life is because of many sins in a past life, receiving karmic retribution (業報)—wouldn't one want to curse (呪咀) that immutable fatalism (宿命說)?

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Didn't you once vehemently deny fate, the four pillars (四柱), and destiny (宿命), saying that "what ruins Koreans is blaming fate (八字타령)"?

Ch'unwŏn - The karma spoken of in Buddhism is different from the fatalism we commonly think of.

Mo Yun-suk - I understood the difference from Dr. An Pin's words repeated more than ten times <52> in "Sarang." But after closing the book, I still don't get it.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Is love sacrifice? Arishima Takeo (有島武郞) said, "Love takes without reservation" (愛は惜みなく奪ふ). So, according to your thinking, Master, is it not about gaining everything but rather about giving everything?

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Does it require a sacrifice like Sŏksunok's, offering her body to a man she doesn't even love, while spiritually always yearning for another?

Ch'unwŏn - "Love" is sacrifice. It's emptying oneself (虛) to fill (盈) another. Love is offering pure affection without expecting any reward (報酬).

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - How so?

Ch'unwŏn - Just as a mother cherishes her child (아히), cherishing her offspring (제자직)—is there even a speck of impure conditions like money or fame sought in such maternal love (母性愛)? Such maternal love, love that only sacrifices itself—this kind of love can be called noble, pure, and possessing eternity.

Mo Yun-suk - Can such love exist? Can love like maternal love exist between young people of the opposite sex?

Ch'unwŏn - Why would it be difficult?

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - It is difficult.

Ch'unwŏn - Physically?

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Yes.

Ch'unwŏn - I don't deny the physical aspect either.

Mo Yun-suk - In what case?

Ch'unwŏn - Only, in order not to deny the physical, one must reach a certain state of mind (境地).

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - State of mind?

Ch'unwŏn - A high monk (高僧) said, "Love a woman, but only after you have experienced the austere philosophy and psychology (枯淡한 哲理와 심리) of embracing her body as if embracing withered wood (枯木)." Just like that...

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Embracing like withered wood?

Ch'unwŏn - That's right. Like clouds resting on a rock and then <53> flying away freely with the wind without any attachment—if it's love based on bright, carefree affection.

Mo Yun-suk - It seems men and women of the world have a long way to go to reach such a high monk's state of mind.
Rather, let's critique Sŏksunok. Was it right for this precious and good woman to sacrifice her life for such a parasitic (寄生蟲的) man—a drunkard, full of lies, a fantasist (狂想家), contributing nothing to human society? Of course, if that husband were a great scientist or artist whose existence brought happiness to millions in the world, then living one's life devoted to one man with a pure heart like Nightingale's (나이딩겔) might be right.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - I don't understand that point either. I understand Sŏksunok's pure spirit (純情), but isn't that purity a worthless sacrifice and devotion (갑 없는 희생이고 순정이 아니리까), unrelated to humanity?

Ch'unwŏn - Is a flower blooming unseen in a mountain valley not a flower? Isn't it perhaps purer and cleaner? Isn't it fine as long as it aligns with the spirit of loving humanity? If even one pitiful sentient being (不相한 중생) is saved, even if it's a weedy existence (잡초적 존재), isn't that effort, that devotion, all valuable (갑 가는 일)?

Kim Tong-hwan - Master, you previously admired Christ (基督), but now you have converted (歸依) to Buddhism (佛敎).

Ch'unwŏn - Where there is truth, can there be a difference between Buddha and Christ?

Mo Yun-suk - Do you still highly regard the spirit of Christ?

Ch'unwŏn - Of course. As one who saved humanity—only, I am someone who was excommunicated (破門) from the Christian gate long ago. Even now, I might differ somewhat from the Christ that contemporary Koreans believe in, but in my own way, I sincerely bow my head to his personality and his "religion of Love," and try to practice even a ten-thousandth of it myself.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Master, you earlier described the Buddha's love as being like maternal love, boundlessly wide and deep, and its spirit lies in sacrificing oneself and forgiving a hundred times. What is the relationship between that statement and the law of karma (因果律)? <54> If it's a "love" that is boundlessly wide and forgives a thousand or hundred times, isn't it okay not to bring the karmic retribution (業報) from a past life into the present life to torment our actual lives?

Mo Yun-suk - That's right. Isn't it right to forgive all the karma (業) from the past life in that past life, and let the present life be lived joyfully and happily, so that everyone can universally benefit (遍普) from the Buddha's love?

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Whether it's right or wrong, we wish for that. If love doesn't go as we wish, if we can't enjoy glory as we wish, and if all this happens because the evils (惡) of a past life have accumulated and we must receive this suffering (苦生) as karmic retribution, wouldn't people suffocate (窒息) under that law of karma?

Ch'unwŏn - You all consider karmic retribution based on the present world, hence your view. What if you thought more broadly, that if you do good deeds in this life, you will receive blessings (祝福) in the next life? Wouldn't this present life then become a life of light, a life of hope, a life of training consistently through goodness (善)?

Mo Yun-suk - How can we expect to live a whole life without karmic retribution, unless we are saints like Johanna (요한나)?

Ch'unwŏn - You think of karmic retribution too severely. Can a mother watch her young child playing (작난) with a knife and just leave them be? Even if the child cries, complains, and throws a tantrum, she temporarily takes the knife away, right? Then gives it back later.
It's the same. When Buddha looks upon us tiny insects (버러지), these human lives, there must be more than one or two incredibly dangerous antics we perform. So, just as a mother takes away the knife her child is playing with, sometimes our playthings are temporarily taken away. Then people cry, complain that it hurts (압흐고) and is painful (쓰리다), thrashing and weeping. That is karmic retribution (業報). That psychology of crying, hurting, and complaining is the psychology of receiving karmic retribution. But isn't that a suffering that will resolve once wisdom (섬) dawns?

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - I feel like I understand, yet I don't quite grasp your words, Master. <55>

**Discussing Literature**

Kim Tong-hwan - For the "point of not featuring a single female character" and the "point of digging into the very bottom of human evil (惡)," "Mumyŏng" (Ignorance/Avidyā) was a groundbreaking (劃時機的) work.

Ch'unwŏn - Mr. Kim Tong-in (金東仁), Mr. Yi T'ae-jun (李泰俊), and others reviewed it recently in "Samch'ŏlli" and elsewhere, which I read with deep interest. Everyone criticized that the character of "I" was depicted weakly (稀薄하게), but I did that intentionally, fearing that if the character "I" became too distinct, the existence of other characters might fade (열불가바서).

Kim Tong-hwan - What were your impressions after reading the reviews of "Sarang"?

Ch'unwŏn - Many people wrote quite a bit, but from the author's perspective, the one who clearly grasped (붓잡어) the intention behind writing this novel was P'albong Kim Ki-jin (八峯 金基鎭). I believe P'albong grasped and understood best what I intended to portray in this piece.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - Is there a medical basis for the terms like 'amorogen' (아모르겐) and 'anphinodoxin' (앤피노독신) that appear in it?

Ch'unwŏn - People have criticized and commented on that. But for me, there is a basis. Because chronic illness (身病) hasn't left my body for a long time, I've become familiar with medicine (醫藥) over several decades and researched it myself. When I was hospitalized at the Medical College Hospital (醫專病院), I showed that piece to the doctors and professors there and asked for critique, and they all said it was correct. It sounds difficult because it's written that way, but if translated into ordinary language, don't women have secretion functions (汾泌作用)? What is called body odor (體臭). This body odor can be emitted as sweat from the whole body, or—it's a bit awkward to explain—it's emitted outside the body due to certain physiological relationships. If all that body odor comes from a pure mind, it has a kind of fragrance; if it comes from an impure mind, it emits a foul odor (臭氣). That is precisely amorogen and anphinodoxin.
Furthermore, that secretion function is seen more intensely not in mature women (盛年 婦人), but in young women around 20 years old, <56> in their developmental stage (發育期). Because younger women constantly have this secretion function.

Mo Yun-suk - Will you be writing many more novels in the future?

Ch'unwŏn - Originally, when I came out of prison, I vowed never to write novels again, but due to livelihood issues, I wrote "Sarang" and "Mumyŏng" this time.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - What are you currently writing (집필중)?

Ch'unwŏn - Well... I have requests (부탁마튼 것) for three full-length novels, and I'm thinking of trying to finish them all by next spring.

Yi Sŏn-hŭi - Among the works you've read recently, which ones moved you?

Mo Yun-suk - From the Korean literary scene (朝鮮文壇)?

Ch'unwŏn - I haven't read much, so I don't really know.

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi - What about works by female writers?

Ch'unwŏn - All three of you gathered here are female writers. How can I critique the works of the three of you right in front of you? Hahaha (Everyone laughs)

Mo Yun-suk - Aren't you going on a trip somewhere?

Ch'unwŏn - I'd like to visit Kŭmgang Mountain (金剛山) once more, but my health must permit it. However, my house in Segŏmjŏng (洗劒亭) is situated in a truly scenic spot. If I open the front and back windows, the clear breeze (淸風), bright moon (明月), and fantastic rocks and cliffs (奇岩絶壁) always visit. For a patient like me who needs clean air and good scenery, my current home is perfect. Therefore, I plan to recuperate (정양) my body for the time being and write. (End) <57>

**Summary**

This 1939 roundtable discussion features prominent female writers Mo Yun-suk, Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi, and Yi Sŏn-hŭi interviewing the renowned author Yi Kwang-su (Ch'unwŏn) about his views on women, love, religion, and literature. Yi discusses the inspirations behind characters in his famous novel "Mujŏng," recounts seeing exceptionally beautiful women in Siberia and Tokyo, and shares memories of a childhood crush. He explains his evolving philosophy of love, moving from youthful passion towards a more spiritual, eternal love concept influenced by Buddhism, emphasizing sacrifice akin to maternal love but not entirely denying the physical aspect if approached with detachment. He discusses his shift from Christianity (from which he was excommunicated) to Buddhism, explaining his understanding of karma (因果) not as fatalism but as a consequence of actions across lifetimes, a process guided by benevolent cosmic forces. He also comments briefly on his recent novels "Sarang" and "Mumyŏng," the critical reception, and his future writing plans while recuperating at his scenic home.

From a historical perspective, the article offers insight into the intellectual climate of late 1930s colonial Korea, showcasing a frank discussion (albeit potentially edited) among leading literary figures. Yi Kwang-su's evolving religious views, particularly his embrace of Buddhism and nuanced interpretation of karma, might reflect broader cultural shifts or personal responses to the turbulent era and his own difficult experiences (including imprisonment). The cautiousness in naming certain individuals ("OO Sŏk") and the censored sections hint at the underlying political sensitivities and surveillance of the time. The discussion of Yi's works, both old and new, provides context for his literary trajectory and contemporary reception.

崔貞熙 -  「無情」을 보면 英釆요 桂月香이요 하고 妓女情緖를 곱게 그리섰는데 기생집에 선생님도 각금 발길을 돌리신 적이 잇슴니까.
李光洙 -  「黃眞伊」 같은 名妓의 문장은 일즉 愛讀한 적이 있섯지만요.
毛允淑 -  너무 逼眞하게 그리신 걸 보면 가신 적이 있는 듯 해요. 「無情」에 그러한 대목이 있지 않슴니까. 平壤서 어린 童妓가 亨植에게 담배불 부처 드리는데 석양갑이에 색기 손끄락이 빨가게 데여젔더란 그런 인상적 묘사 같은 것은 작가의 상상력만 가지고는 그러케 실감이 이러날 것 갔지 않어요.
李善熙 -  또 桂月香이 英釆더러 하는 대화라든지 거문고 가야금과 脂粉의 내음새가 풍기는 무에라 말할 수 없시 사름의 마음을 간열푸게 흔들고 지나가는, 마치 5월 하늘의 실바람과 같이 부드럽게 고요하게 그러면서도 정열적으로 스치고 지나가는 그런 妓房 情調가 말슴이야요.
春園 -  「無情」 쓸 때에는 그게 어느 해 방학이든가요. 早稻田大學 다니다가 여름방학이 되어서 五山 가는 길에 平壤에 들넛더니 몃몃 친구가 하로저녁 料亭으로 끄을기에 놀녀간 적이 잇섰서요. 바로 鍊光亭 압 수양버들 느러진 었든 古風的 요정이였는데 게서 가야금도 듯고 愁心歌도 듯고 半醉하여 놀었지요.
毛允淑 -  그때의 妓女가 「無情」으로 드러 가섰군요.
春園 -  그때의 영향이 좀 있섰슬 걸요. 그 뒤 또 한번 친구에게 끄을려 釵貫里엇든 기녀방에 구경하러 간 일이 있섯는데 그때 정말 티끌 한 점 뭇지 안은 순진한 소녀 하나를 보았서요. 아름답고 귀엽고 그 소녀의 인상이 이처지지 않어섯는데 「無情」에 작자도 모르게 무엔가 나타난 점이 잇다면 아마 그 소녀일 걸요.＜47＞
金東煥 -  「연애」는 안 하시고 그저 달 보듯 鑑賞하섰군요!
春園 -  사랑이야 아니지요.

毛允淑 -  누구심니까.

崔貞熙 -  거기에 나오는 아모로겐이니 앤피노독신이니 하는 의학적 근거는 있는 것임니까.
春園 -  거기 대하여 여러분이 비난도 하시고 논평도 하섯는데 나는 그것이 근거가 있어요. 오랜동안 身病이 몸에서 떠나지 않음으로 醫藥과 수십년 친해오는 사이에 내가 연구해내인 것인데 나는 그 一篇을 醫專病院에 입원해있을 때에 그곳 의사와 교수들에게 보이면서 비평을 청했더니 다들 올타고 그래요. 그것을 어렵게 써 노흐니 그러치 보통 하는 말로 옴기면 여성에게 汾泌作用이 있지 안슴니까. 소위 體臭라는 것이지요. 이 체취는 全身에서 땀으로 化하여 發하기도 하고 또 조곰 설명하기 거북함니다. 만은 생리적 엇든 관계로 體外에 發散되지요. 그 모든 體臭가 깨끗한 마음에서 나는 것이면 一種 향기가 있고 不純한 마음에서 나오면 臭氣가 나지요. 그것이 곳 아모르겐 앤피노독신지요.
더군다나 그 분비작용이란 盛年 婦人에게서보다 20 좌우의＜56＞ 發育期에 있는 젊은 여성에게서 더욱 심히 보게 되지요. 젊은 여성일사록 늘 不絶히 분비작용을 하니까요.
