A Meeting Where Civilian Volunteers Inquire with Military and Government-General Authorities Regarding Conscription, Compulsory Education, and General Mobilization Issues
Regarding the current three major issues of "Conscription, Compulsory Education, General Mobilization," military, government, and civilian volunteers gathered at the Chōsen Hotel one late spring day for a frank question-and-answer session. This text is a transcript from that evening. (Responsibility for the text lies with the reporter)

Compulsory Education Issue
Chu Chong-ŭi (Posŏng Middle School Teacher)= I would like to ask the Director of the Bureau of Education the following few points.
First, regarding the elementary school education issue - Now that the Government-General's education policy of "one school per myeon (township)" has been completed, it is thought to be time to implement compulsory education. Around when, that is, within how many years from now, will compulsory education be implemented?
Second, regarding the middle school education issue - Although it's a recurring issue every year, the difficulty of entering middle school is truly a major problem. Looking at the entrance rate for Keijō (Seoul) middle schools this year, it reached 10 to 1 (meaning about 1,000 applicants for 100 available spots), leading to miserable situations like parents' disappointment and students committing suicide or running away from home.
As a remedy for this, the urgent task is to increase the number of middle schools. If this is impossible due to finances or other related matters, please allow the implementation of a two-shift system for night middle schools. That is, we hope you will establish night middle schools within the existing middle schools to accommodate middle school students who couldn't enroll. It is said that these night middle schools are already implemented in Ōsaka and other cities. I would like to hear the Bureau of Education's thoughts on this.

Shiobara, Director of the Bureau of Education = I will answer the first question. Regarding elementary education, the "one school per myeon" policy was completed in Shōwa 11 (1936). As a result, what percentage of school-aged children could be accommodated? 27 out of 100 school-aged children were enrolled.
However, as of the end of Shōwa 12 (1937), how many school-aged children applied for admission? It reached 60 out of 100. At the very least, this 60 percent are all planned to be accommodated between Shōwa 11 (1936) and Shōwa 20 (1945) by expanding schools and increasing classrooms; both schools and classrooms are planned to be doubled.
After that, this plan progressed rapidly, and as things stand now, it is expected to be completed by Shōwa 17 (1942), not needing to go until Shōwa 20 (1945). That is, the 10-year plan has been shortened to 6 years.
Because of this new plan to accommodate 60 percent, it was decided to spend 15 million Won (円) in education expenses. The reason elementary schools cannot be increased rapidly is that although there is money like this, there are no teachers. Teachers produced in Chōsen (Korea) alone are insufficient, so 800 are brought from the Mainland (Naichi, Japan) each year. It is a situation where more than 800 cannot be brought from the Mainland; generally <32> because the current military supply boom is good, talented people are flocking to that field, making teacher recruitment extremely difficult.
Returning to the earlier issue of compulsory education, after accommodating 60 percent by Shōwa 17 (1942), a plan for the remaining 40 percent will be established. According to rough estimates, around Shōwa 25 (1950), it is believed that most will be accommodated, and then, with just one chapter of law, compulsory education can be implemented. I will answer the second question. The issue of difficulty entering secondary education is known to be most painful, and there is a plan to add 700 classrooms over the next 10 years. If 70 classrooms are added per year, accommodating 50 students per classroom, that means 3,500 students per year, and over 10 years, 700 classrooms should accommodate 35,000 students.
We are proceeding with this policy, but even so, there will be population growth and an increase in elementary school graduates in the future. However, it is generally believed that about one-third of the applicants will be accommodated.
Regarding higher education beyond middle school, we hope you will be satisfied with the current level.

Yi Sŏng-hwan (Taedong Industrial Specialty School Director) - I have listened carefully to your words. In short, compulsory education in Chōsen will not be implemented until Shōwa 25 (1950), meaning we must wait another 10 years or so, and the expansion of middle schools is also slow, with 700 classrooms to be added over the next 10 years, barely reaching the point of accommodating one-third of the applicants. The reason for this delay lies solely in the two problems of finance and teachers.
Then, we have a proposal. If you allow the issuance of education bonds worth about 10 million Won to secure funds for education, then civilian volunteers will rise up, travel around the 13 provinces, collect funds for those bonds, and offer the 10 million Won.
Regarding the teacher shortage, if the current treatment of teachers is improved, that is, if their social treatment is generous and their remuneration is increased, plenty of individuals willing to become teachers will surely emerge. Please realize these two things.

Shiobara, Director of the Bureau of Education = It is not that the Government-General lacks money. Currently, the money spent on education across all of Chōsen from the national treasury and local funds amounts to about 100 million Won (円) per year. There are no financial worries. Money can be gathered as much as needed if we set our minds to it. However, there is no solution to the teacher shortage. Just as no matter how many airplanes are built, pilots cannot be produced suddenly, schools can be built, but obtaining teachers is a major problem. <33>
That being said, we cannot entrust the precious education of the second generation of citizens to unqualified teachers.
The shortage of middle school teachers is even more severe. While teachers for Japanese language and English are plentiful, teachers for mathematics, physics, and chemistry are truly extremely difficult to find.

Son Hong-wŏn = Is there no plan to establish a Higher Normal School (高等師範) in Chōsen for training middle school teachers?

Shiobara, Director of the Bureau of Education = There is no plan for a Higher Normal School. We are considering thinking of Keijō Imperial University as something similar to a Higher Normal School.

Yi Chŏng-sŏp (Keijō Broadcasting Station) = Are there restrictions on establishing private middle schools? Also, there is a rumor that if one wants to establish a middle school, it is restricted to public middle schools and vocational schools. How is it?

Shiobara, Director of the Bureau of Education = There are no restrictions placed on establishing private middle schools. If the financial foundation is solid and the applicant's character is acceptable, permission is granted. However, what is the point of only having middle school graduates? We need citizens who engage in commerce, industry, and agriculture, hence we feel the need for vocational schools.

Ch'oe Tong (Severance Medical College Principal) = Are there no plans for a music school and an art school? What kind of plans exist for those special education fields?

Shiobara, Director of the Bureau of Education - I want to establish both. Moreover, Chōsen people have special talents in those fields. We want to foster these talents, and also, music teachers for secondary schools have traditionally been brought from the Mainland, but in the future, they cannot always be brought, so in the sense of self-sufficiency, we want to establish a music school.
In this field, there are plenty of teachers, but again, there is no money. We will strive for this in the future.

Sŏ Ch'un (Former Chōsen Ilbo Chief Editor) - A fundamental point to consider regarding the current entrance difficulty problem is that if the tendency of banks and companies that hire people to overemphasize school qualifications is corrected, people could become socially useful members without necessarily going through school doors, which would alleviate the entrance difficulty. Also, if public and private middle schools conduct entrance exams on the same date throughout Chōsen, the tragic ratio like 10 to 1 could be eliminated.

Shiobara, Director of the Bureau of Education - When thinking about the entrance difficulty, there are generally three ailments. First is the thought that if children are not put into school, they become useless for anything; second is the thought focused solely on getting employed by banks or companies; and third is that becoming a salaried worker is the sole goal after graduating school. Can the nation sustain itself with only salaried workers? From this perspective, the thinking of society as a whole seems to be lagging by about a century <34>.

Conscription System Implementation Issue
Kim Tong-hwan (Samch'ŏlli Company Publisher) - Regarding the conscription system issue, I would like to ask several points to the military authorities and also to Director Shiobara, who is the head of the volunteer soldier training center.
First question - Please widely open the doors of the current volunteer soldier system. That is, do not limit the intake to 400 per year, but increase it significantly. At the very least, looking at this year's example, out of 12,000 applicants from all over Chōsen, the number of qualified individuals in terms of physique, ideology, etc., is said to be 7,000. Please immediately and fully accept and train these 7,000 qualified individuals.
Second question - Please expand the assignment (配屬) to barracks nationwide. Currently, young men who graduate from the training center are assigned only to the two divisions within Chōsen, in Nanam (羅南) and Yongsan (龍山). In the sense of fostering camaraderie between Mainland and Chōsen youth within the barracks, and from the perspective that Chōsen volunteer soldiers are members of the defense of the Japanese Empire, please assign them to all 16 divisions nationwide, including Tōkyō, Ōsaka, and other divisions in Honshū, as well as Hokkaidō, Taiwan, etc.
Third question - The reason for implementing the conscription system in Chōsen as soon as possible lies in the allied relationship. Manchukuo (滿洲國), an allied nation and part of the East Asian Co-Prosperity Sphere, already has an army of about 100,000 Manchurian soldiers just 6-7 years after its founding, spending 1.8 million Won (円) annually in regular expenses. It is regrettable that after 30 years of annexation, the conscription system has not yet been implemented in Chōsen. When this is said, it is always argued that compulsory education and Naissen Ittai (內鮮一體 - Japan-Korea Unity) integration must precede it. However, during the Great European War (WWI), it is said that only 2 percent of the 1 million American troops who went to Europe had elementary education, meaning education is not necessarily an indispensable condition for national defense. Also, even if a conscription ordinance is implemented, not all adults need to be inducted from the first year. Just as in the Mainland, where unfit individuals classified as Grade B (乙種), Grade C (丙種), etc., based on physical and other examinations are temporarily excluded, couldn't those deemed unsuitable in physique or ideology during examination be classified as B or C, while selecting outstanding individuals as Grade A (甲種) for induction?

Shiobara, Volunteer Soldier Training Center Director = The second and third questions are entirely handled by the military authorities; I only carry out decided matters, so it is outside my authority and I cannot answer. Regarding the first question, in the future, if **a certain need arises**, I think the number of volunteers accepted will also increase. <35> The performance of the current 400 enlisted soldiers is very good. Since it's only been 4 months since enlistment, we are observing them until then. However, the physique of Peninsular youths was not good, which was due to poor living standards; up to 90% had parasites, and also, about 30% initially needed medicine—it's good they got better; [otherwise] they wouldn't have recovered well. All volunteer soldiers had parasites; Dr. Kinoshita (木下), a medical doctor, said parasites are more fearsome than tuberculosis. There were also those with venereal diseases. In terms of physical strength, although their appearance was robust, their lower back (腰) strength was weak, but it is gradually improving with training after enlistment.

Staff Officer Kita (Chōsen Army Command Headquarters) = I will answer the first question.
Regarding the number of volunteer soldiers, "the state does not want to take more than necessary," so while the number is small now, I think it will gradually increase in the future. Moreover, initially when this system was implemented, because our country was in the midst of war, there was some misunderstanding that Peninsular people were needed as soldiers due to the need for human resources, but now, the military strength within Japan's Mainland is sufficient. The progress of volunteer soldiers after enlistment, in terms of training and daily life, is sufficiently good, so there are no concerns at present.
Regarding the second question, the request to assign them to military units nationwide - if only 400 are distributed among more than 10 divisions, the number assigned to each becomes too small, so it is thought better not to do so yet.
Regarding the third question, the conscription issue cannot be discussed simply. The military cannot experiment and then reverse course if it turns out badly; if we lose a war, nothing can be done about it. Generally, it must become known that Chōsen people will not lose when fighting against the Soviet or German armies. This is primarily a matter related to strong patriotism and education. Therefore, the issue of implementing a conscription system in Chōsen is considered a very serious matter.

Major Chŏng (Chōsen Army Command Headquarters) = Having only 10,000 or 20,000 volunteer soldiers is unsatisfactory. Unless there are at least around 200,000, the conscription issue...

Kim Tong-hwan = After enlistment, aren't there some minor ** [conflicts/friction] between Chōsen youth and Mainland youth due to differences in habits, environment, and language they grew up with (definitely not ideological)? We worry if such things might exist.

Director Shiobara = Such issues are addressed during the training center period through education and practical life <36> to bring them to the same level as Mainland youth before enlistment, so because of that*, relations between the two groups are extremely smooth. (* **닭에 likely means 까닭에 - because of)

Yi Sŏng-hwan = I hope efforts will be made to properly guarantee the status of volunteer soldiers after they complete their military service and are discharged (出營).

Director Shiobara = I agree completely. As authorities, we are considering it.

General Mobilization Alliance Issue
Sin T'ae-ak (Lawyer) = I would like to ask about roughly six points sequentially.
First question - Due to the urgency of national spiritual general mobilization under wartime conditions, a large-scale, extensive Chōsen Alliance (朝鮮聯盟) was organized throughout Chōsen. Since then, apart from two or three news items about General Minami (用島 大將 - referring to Minami Jirō, the Governor-General) assuming the presidency, it has been very quiet. Please tell us about the state of the Alliance's activities since then.
Second question - Regarding the organization of the Chōsen Alliance, the structure so far seems top-down. Is there any intention to reorganize it while considering popular will (民意)? That is, how about restructuring it into a system suitable for popular will to create an organization of the people?
Third question - The current headquarters organization of the Chōsen Alliance seems too large and diffuse (閑漫). How about dividing it into specialized sections and bringing in experts from various fields to work in each? For instance, establishing specialized sections for industry, literary arts, film, theater, etc., and letting them operate? How about that?
Fourth question - The key (鍵) to the Alliance's rise or fall ultimately lies in the personnel issue. How about appointing more civilian figures?
Fifth question - Currently, the target (對照) of the general mobilization movement seems mainly directed at Chōsen people. Rather than that, among the Mainland people (內地人*) residing in Chōsen, there are people who act contrary to the spirit of Naissen Ittai (內鮮一體 - Japan-Korea Unity) even more than Chōsen people. I hope you will place emphasis there. Even looking at the legal profession, let alone the situation in other social circles, there is sometimes a tendency among Mainland people to dislike (실혀하는 - older spelling of 싫어하는) the Naissen Ittai movement. Therefore, I hope the Alliance's focus will also be somewhat directed towards Mainland people.
Sixth question - (In **** and Tōkyō, is there a relationship between the Mainland *** and ***? Is it a mutually organic*** organizational body?)

Director Shiobara (National ** Spiritual General Mobilization Chōsen Alliance ***) - The questions ** reached six *****, but because they are *** ordered questions = (The following 4 lines are illegible) <37> I will answer sequentially from the last one.
Sixth question: Organizationally, there is no connection between the General Alliance (總聯盟) in the Mainland and the Chōsen Alliance. However, the common point is the desire to communicate spiritually and cooperate with each other. The organizational methods of the Mainland Alliance and the Chōsen Alliance are also different. In the Mainland, 91 organizations came together to form the Alliance. In Chōsen, Patriotic Neighborhood Associations (愛國班) were established in small rural villages, and the alliance was established in Myeon, Eup, and thus Budo (Cities/Provinces), encompassing both individuals and organizations. However, although the organization is widespread like this, it has a somewhat thin aspect.
Fifth question: I completely agree. There are more than one or two things that the Mainland side needs to become aware of. The Governor-General (總督) also speaks very strictly on this point.
Fourth question: Since it is a joint government-civilian (官民合作) organization, having many good people active is a good thing. We also hope for the activities of civilian figures as much as possible. In the Provincial Alliances (道聯盟), if there are suitable individuals, we let civilian figures take the leadership roles. For example, Mr. Hyŏn Chun-ho (玄俊鎬) also served as the Chairman of the Chŏnnam (South Chŏlla) Alliance.
Third question: I also agree with dividing the alliance into various sections. It's ideal. I hope for this too.
First and Second questions: The alliance's activities are as you all see them. However, the entire Alliance moving and carrying out large-scale activities across the board is something that happens only a few times a year. But the Patriotic Neighborhood Associations (愛國班) are probably moving in some way every day. Generally, large cities have lagged the most. Among them, Seoul (서울) has fallen behind the most. Therefore, in the future, we plan to focus our efforts on urban areas.
Also, I believe this movement will achieve its full form if we continue for another 7 years.

Yi Chong-man (Taedong Mining Association President) - What I ask of the Chōsen Alliance is to urge them, whatever the matter, large or small, not just to pay lip service (空念佛), but to actually carry things out. Not just conceptually or abstractly. For example, things like adhering to schedules (時間 勵行) should be strictly practiced starting from the top, from the high officials of the Government-General, and it would be good if all other various aspects of lifestyle improvement were the same.

Director Shiobara - I agree.

Yi Sŏng-hwan - Generally, since the Incident (사변 - likely referring to the Second Sino-Japanese War start in 1937), various currents flowing through Chōsen society have been cleared up and unified. Today, in the spirit of "strike while the iron is hot," I believe there is a need, especially in the early stages of the Alliance, to thoroughly <38> instill its purpose so that the populace themselves recognize it as their own organization and move forward. To achieve this, it ultimately comes down to the personnel issue. Looking at the situation in various regions currently, in cities (府), counties (郡), townships (面), everywhere, there is usually at least one influential person capable of leading the local populace. I mean someone outside of public officials. I think we should seek out such individuals who were involved in past nationalist movements or social movements and have now changed their stance (전향한), and enable them to be active.
Secondly, how about mobilizing the core youth stratum more?

Director Shiobara - I agree completely with your opinion. We want to proceed in that manner. Currently, the number of people included in the Alliance structure is about 20 million. Except for several hundred thousand slash-and-burn farmers (화전민) who haven't joined, people of all ages and genders are included. Among them, there are about 2 million school students, which is the best-organized group, and besides them, 4 million are members of Patriotic Organizations (愛國團體).
Therefore, excluding these 4 million, the remaining 16 million are not yet organized, meaning they are people outside the system (化外의 民). I believe the area for future action lies here. Anyway, we have discussed important and urgent issues like compulsory education, conscription, and the National Spiritual Mobilization Alliance. I ask for your further cooperation.

**Summary**

This 1939 roundtable discussion held at the Chōsen Hotel in Keijō (Seoul) brought together Japanese military officials, Government-General bureaucrats (notably Education Bureau Director Shiobara), and prominent Korean civilian figures (educators, businessmen, intellectuals) to discuss three pressing issues: compulsory education, conscription for Koreans, and the National Spiritual General Mobilization movement. Korean participants pressed for faster implementation of compulsory education, expansion of secondary schooling (including night schools), easing of entry restrictions, and issuance of education bonds. Regarding conscription, they urged expanding the existing volunteer system significantly, assigning Korean soldiers throughout the Japanese Empire, and implementing full conscription soon, countering official arguments about needing prior education and "Japan-Korea Unity." On mobilization, Koreans critiqued the top-down, seemingly inactive nature of the Chōsen Alliance, advocating for more grassroots involvement, inclusion of capable civilian figures (including former activists), and addressing the lack of commitment to "Naissen Ittai" among Japanese residents in Korea. Japanese officials generally acknowledged the issues but cited constraints like teacher shortages (not funding) for education delays, the need for caution and proven loyalty/capability for conscription, and gradual progress for the mobilization movement, while agreeing on the need for Japanese residents to better embody unity ideals.

This article is historically significant as it reveals direct dialogue between Korean elites and Japanese colonial authorities during the intensified wartime mobilization period. It showcases Korean aspirations for equal treatment and integration within the empire (demanding education and military service rights comparable to Japanese) while simultaneously expressing frustrations with the slow pace and limitations imposed by the colonial government. The officials' responses highlight the colonial logic emphasizing gradualism, control, suspicion (regarding Korean loyalty for conscription), and the instrumentalization of education and mobilization for imperial purposes. The discussion on the mobilization alliance and the call to involve former activists and target Japanese residents' attitudes provide insights into the complexities and contradictions of the "Naissen Ittai" policy on the ground. Historians can find valuable material here on Korean elite agency, colonial education policy, military service debates, wartime mobilization strategies, and the lived realities of Japanese rule in the late 1930s.

義務敎育 問題
朱鍾宜(普成中學校 敎諭)=學務局長에게 左記 數三點을 문의하고저 함니다.
제1. 小學校育문제에 대하야-總督府의 교육정책이 이미 一面一校가 완성된 금일에 있어서는 이제는 의무교육을 실시할 때이라고 생각되는 바 언제쯤 즉 今後 何年 이내로 의무교육을 실시하게 되겠읍니까.
제2. 중학교 교육문제에 대하야-매년 번복되는 일이나 중학교의 입학난은 실로 큰 문제올시다. 京城 각 중학교의 금년 入學率을 볼지라도 10대1(즉 백명 수용에 약 천명의 지망)에 달하야 이 때문에 학부형의 落望 생도의 自殺 出家 등 비참한 현상이 이러나고 있음니다.
此의 구제책으로는 중학교의 增設이 急務이며 財政 기타관계로 그가 불가능할 시는 2부제의 실시를 하야 야간중학을 許하여 주서요. 즉 현재의 중학교에 다시 야간중학을 設施하여 미취학 중학생을 수용하여 주기를 바람니다. 此 야간중학은 이미 大阪 기타 도시에서도 실시한다 함니다. 여기 대한 學務局의 의향을 듯고저 함니다.
塩原 學務局長=제1문에 답함니다. 小學敎育에 있어 一面一校는 昭和11년도에 완성하였어요. 그 결과로 얼마의 수용률이 생겼는가 하면 학령아동의 100분지 27이 입학되어 있어요.
그런데 昭和12년도 말 현재로 학령아동 중 입학 지망자수가 얼마인가 하면 100분지 60에 달하는데 적어도 이 60파-센트는 昭和11년으로부터 昭和20년도에 이르기까지 모도다 다 수용하게 되도록 학교를 증설하고 학급을 늘니여 학교, 학급 모도다 倍加計劃을 진행하고 있읍니다.
그 뒤 이 계획은 급속도로 진전되어 現狀으로서 보면 昭和20년까지 가지 않고 17년도까지에 완성될 예정인데 즉 10개년 계획을 6개년으로 단축하였어요.
이러케 60파-센트를 수용하기 위한 이 새로운 계획때문에 교육비로 1,500만원의 교육비를 쓰기로 되었어요. 소학교를 급작히 늘니지 못하는 것은 이러케 돈은 있으나 敎員이 없어요. 朝鮮서 나오는 敎員만 가지고는 부족함으로 內地로써 매년 800명式 가저와요. 800명 이상은 內地에서도 가저올 수 없는 형편인데 대체로＜32＞ 지금 軍需景氣가 조와서 그 방면으로 인재들이 쏠니기 때문에 교원 보충이 대단히 곤란함니다.
다시 앗가의 의무교육문제에 도라가는데 이러케 昭和17년도까지 60파-센트를 수용하고 난 뒤 다시 남저지 40파-센트의 계획을 세워 대략 예상에는 昭和25년경이 되면 아마 대개 수용할 수 있어 그제는 법률 한 章이면 의무교육을 실시할 수 있게될 줄 암니다. 제2問에 답함니다. 중등교육의 입학난 문제는 가장 고통으로 아는 바로써 今後 10년간에 700학급을 증설할 작정이외다. 1년에 70학급式 1학급에 50명式 수용한다면 매년 3,500명 10년간에 700학급이면 35,000명을 수용할 수 있을 줄 아러요.
이러한 방침으로 하여나가는 터인데 그래도 今後 인구의 증가와 소학교 졸업생의 증가가 있겠으나 대체 희망자의 3분지 1은 수용하게 될 줄 암니다.
그러고 중학교 이상의 고등교육에 대하여는 현재 정도에서 만족하여 주기를 바람니다.
李晟煥(大同工業專門學校 理事)-말슴은 잘 드렀읍니다. 요컨대 朝鮮에 있서 의무교육이 昭和25년도에나, 즉 今後 10여년을 기다리지 않으면 실시하게 되지 못하고 또 중학교의 증설도 遲遲하야 700학급을 今後 10년간 증설하여서야 겨우 희망자의 3분1을 수용함에 이르리라 하는 말슴인데 이러케 느저지는 이유가 어데에 있는가 하면 오로지 재정과 교원의 두 가지 문제에 있는 줄 압니다.
그러면 우리에게는 草案이 있어요. 교육상의 재정을 얻기 위하여 교육공채 1천만원정도를 발행하도록 하여 주시면 그런다면 민간의 有志는 蹶起하여 13道 방방곡곡을 도라다니며 그 공채을 모집하여 1천만원을 만들어 밧치리다.
교원 부족에 대하여는 현재 교원의 대우를 개선하여 놋는다면 즉 사회적 대우를 후하게 또 報酬를 증가하여 준다면 얼마든지 교원될 인물이 출현할 줄 암니다. 이 두 가지를 실현하여 주서요.
塩原 學務局長=總督府에 돈이 없는 것이 아님니다. 현재 국고와 지방비로써 全鮮에서 교육비로 쓰고 있는 돈이 1년에 약 1억원이나 됨니다. 재정은 염려없어요. 돈은 모으자면 얼마든지 모으지요. 그러나 교원 부족에는 방법이 없어요. 가령 비행기를 아모리 많이 많들어도 조종자만은 얼는 급작시리 만들어질 수 없드시 학교는 만들 수 있어도 교원을 얻기 어려운 것이 큰 문제입니다.＜33＞
그런다고 자격 없는 교원들을 붓잡어다가 소중한 제2의 국민교육을 맛낄 수는 없어요.
중학교 교원의 부족은 더욱 심해요. 국어 영어의 교사는 얼마든지 있으나 수학, 물리, 화학의, 교사는 참으로 얻기가 極難해요.
孫弘遠=중학교원의 양성으로 朝鮮에 高等師範 설치의 계획이 없음니까.
