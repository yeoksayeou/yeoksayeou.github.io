**Body Text Translation:**
An Evening for the Film "Mujŏng"
						Ch'unwŏn (Original Author), Pak Kich'ae (Director)
Writers' Side: Kim Tongin, Yi Hŏn'gu, Yi Muyŏng, Paek Ch'ŏl, Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi
Actors' Side: Mun Yebong, Han Ŭnjin
Directors' Side: An Sŏkchu, Pak Kich'ae, Yi Kyuhwan, Kim Chŏnghyŏk <124>
Date: April 18th
Place: Arangju Cafe near Mugyo Bridge, Seoul

Kim Tonghwan (Samch'ŏlli) - Tonight, the film "Mujŏng" is being screened right here in the city at the Hwanggŭmjwa Theater and is drawing popularity from the gentlemen and ladies of the entire capital. At this time, we are holding this 'Evening for Mujŏng' to create an unforgettable, beautiful memory concerning this artistically esteemed work.
First, I must mention that although Ch'unwŏn was definitely supposed to be here, he developed a high fever this afternoon and unfortunately had to be absent. It's regrettable, but instead, we have received a letter he sent to Mr. Pak Kich'ae, who directed this work, which I will now share.

Tales of Hardship from the Lead Actors
Let's leave it at that...
First, Ms. Han Ŭnjin, who played the lead role, please tell us about your hardships. How many times did you read "Mujŏng"?
Han Ŭnjin - I read it once about 4 or 5 years ago when I was in school, and then I read it thoroughly once more when I was cast in the role of Pak Yŏngch'ae.
Reporter - When you were performing, where did you think the spirit of Yŏngch'ae lay, and what did you strive to express?
Han - Well... perhaps it was in her "father's" words, "A virtuous woman does not serve two husbands" (烈女는 不更二夫)? That purity is extremely precious to a woman, and to protect that purity, she shouldn't change husbands—wasn't that the message? I saw it that way and tried hard to elevate that spirit through speech and action.
Reporter - Ms. Mun Yebong, how did you see it?
Mun Yebong - I suppose the entirety of "Mujŏng" sought to depict the pain and transience of that era, the dawning period just about to break, but thinking as Yŏngch'ae myself, I also believe the spirit of "A virtuous woman does not serve two husbands," found in the Lesser Learning (小學), must serve as the pillar.
When the creed she had known so deeply in her bones could no longer be upheld due to various unavoidable circumstances, and because of this, in the last scene, she tearfully sends the white paper with those words her father wrote floating down the river—I see that as the greatest climax.
Reporter - How many times have you seen "Mujŏng"?
Mun - I saw it long ago when I was in a theater troupe, but I'd like to read the original work again.
Reporter - How do you view Ms. Han Ŭnjin's acting? <125> If you, Ms. Mun Yebong, had taken the role of Pak Yŏngch'ae, were there any parts where you thought, 'I would have expressed it this way,' or 'If it were me, I would have done that part differently'?
Mun - How can I say? However, I think Ms. Han Ŭnjin did well. Having been on stage many times, she seems quite skilled and good. Hohoho.
Reporter - While acting, was there ever a time you truly felt excited, thinking, "I am Pak Yŏngch'ae"?
Han - While filming that work, I tried very hard to capture the feeling of "Pak Yŏngch'ae."
Reporter - Were there times you cried out of genuine emotion, not just acting?
Han - Yes. When searching for the body of Kye Wŏlhyang unni along the Taedong River bank at dawn, during the location shooting for that scene, tears flowed naturally. And another time, when filming the scene watching Hyŏngsik and Sŏn[hyŏng]'s wedding ceremony from the church doorway, I truly cried again.
Reporter - The role of Yŏngch'ae involves many scenes as a "kisaeng" (妓生). Have you ever visited the kisaeng houses in Seoul's Taean-dong (茶坊골) or P'yŏngyang's Ch'aegwan-ri (釵貫里) for observation?
Han - No. I just followed Director Pak Kich'ae's instructions regarding costume and posture. What do I know?

Critiques from Writers and Film Critics
Reporter - Why do you suppose Ch'unwŏn titled the novel "Mujŏng" (lit. Heartless/Unfeeling)? In which parts of *Mujŏng* do you see that meaning manifested?
Kim Tongin - The emotions felt by a forerunner (先覺者) inevitably embrace feelings of sorrow and solitude. So perhaps it symbolizes the group of forerunners of that era.
Pak Kich'ae - I see it that way too. When an era makes a great turn from darkness (無明) to light (光明), many sacrifices are crushed by those gears, so it seems like a word symbolizing that scene (情景).
Reporter - How so, for example?
Pak - When the conflict between old and new moral views regarding female chastity arises, tragedy occurs there.
Reporter - And?
Pak - Through Hyŏngsik, the agony arising from the clash between idealism and realism felt by the intellectual youth of that era, etc.
Reporter - I understand well. Since we have film critics like Mr. Yi Hŏn'gu and writers like Mr. Kim Tongin, Mr. Paek Ch'ŏl, Mr. Yi Muyŏng, and Ms. Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi here, let's now get to the main point and critique this film adaptation of "Mujŏng" <126> without reservation. Please give us your critique.
Kim Tongin - It wasn't Ch'unwŏn's *Mujŏng*, but Mr. Pak Kich'ae's *Mujŏng*. (All laugh)
Yi Hŏn'gu - At the preview screening yesterday evening, after watching the whole thing, Ch'unwŏn himself said that this was a *Mujŏng* created by Mr. Pak Kich'ae.
Kim Tongin - I also rushed over to see it just this afternoon, and if I were to list a few flaws that struck me immediately: first, this filmed *Mujŏng* has lost the consistency of the story. Without having read the original work, one cannot grasp the plot's thread just from the facts presented in this film.
Reporter - And second?
Kim Tongin - Second, it's unclear what the director tried hard to express. That is, the theme is not distinct.
Reporter - And?
Kim Tongin - One more thing, and I don't know if this is the actors' fault or the director's, but the performances of the cast lack suppleness; they are all stiff like wooden posts (長承).
Yi Muyŏng - Though there might be differences in the strength of wording, I also share the same thoughts as Mr. Kim Tongin's critique. There were even parts where the artistic beauty of the original work seemed marred rather than brought to life.
Reporter - Please point things out starting from the overall structure.
Yi Muyŏng - I believe there are three particularly good parts in the work "Mujŏng."
Reporter - Which ones?
Yi Muyŏng - First, the scene where the students of P'aesŏng School in P'yŏngyang sing as they cross over Pubyŏk Pavilion (浮碧樓) and Ŭlmil Terrace (乙密臺) in the evening sun, against which Kye Wŏlhyang tightly hugs Yŏngch'ae and laments to herself, "Ah, there's a poet among them. There's a poet among them," grieving and yearning alone.
Second, the part where Kye Wŏlhyang wakes at dawn, places the ring from her own finger onto Yŏngch'ae's finger, runs out to the riverbank, and commits suicide.
Third, amidst the flood at Samnangjin, where all these young people in their triangular and quadrangular relationships forget their feelings of "love and hate" (愛憎), cherish each other, forgive each other, hold a concert, and sing together. Of course, there might be a few other similar parts, but I think these are three scenes that should not be *omitted* if adapting it to film. However, Mr. Pak Kich'ae, for whatever reason, cut out the most important first and last scenes. That's why, as Mr. Kim Tongin mentioned earlier, the film's theme—the director's intention <127> of what he tried to express—ended up being indistinct.
Reporter - Ms. Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi, how did you see it?
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - I generally agree. I don't know where Mr. Ch'unwŏn placed the emphasis (力點) when writing this novel, but from my perspective, the scenes Mr. Yi Muyŏng mentioned are all the high points ("ヤマ" - yama). I too expected that scene where the P'aesŏng School students disappear over Pubyŏk Pavilion singing:
The dawn light rises.
All things in the universe dance.
Am I the only one?
Singing a sad song.
—I expected a splendid canvas to unfold, like the scene in that famous German film "Heidelberg" where the university student and Käthi were playing. It was disappointing that not even a shadow of it remained. Also, the melon patch at Pyŏnguk's house in Hwangju, which could be called the pinnacle of pastoral description (田園 描寫), was completely omitted, and the waiting room at Samnangjin Station was omitted—too much poetry, romance, and nature were left out.
Reporter - Please don't just mention flaws; tell us about the good points that were brought to life even better than the original work. There must be plenty of good points. First, the very first scene shows a young girl on a swing; that is a masterpiece painting (名畵). It captures the local color of Korea 100 percent. The sight of that white skirt flashing above the weeping willow is truly impressive.
Paek Ch'ŏl - That scene was the cleanest and best in the whole film, but it also dragged on unnecessarily and artlessly long.
Kim Tongin - And in the original work, Hyŏngsik and Yŏngch'ae's childhood is described in just two or three lines, but the film spends nearly two-thirds of its length depicting this childhood flatly. Wouldn't it have been better to condense the childhood period concisely?
Reporter - Mr. Yi Hŏn'gu, from the standpoint of a film critic, please give us individual evaluations. On structure, character performances, musical effects, background, costumes, everything.
Yi Hŏn'gu - In a word, the film "Mujŏng" can only be called a "bland work" (淡淡한 作品).
Reporter - Why is that?
Yi Hŏn'gu - This work, which should be tangled with tears, sighs, and resentment (情怨), is overly calm, lacks shadow, and seems to just slide flatly from one scene to the next. The director was too deferential ("遠慮して" - enryo shite) to the original work and also compressed emotions extremely in his own style (自己流), failing to make the audience feel impact (迫力).
Reporter - Which actor's performance was the most <128> successful?
Yi Hŏn'gu - Perhaps it was Mr. Yi Paeksu, who played Sin Usŏn. Ms. Han Ŭnjin was also excessively pure and clear (淸楚), Mr. Ch'oe Namyong, who took the role of Hyŏngsik, and Mr. Kim Ilhae as Kim Hyŏnsu were all so-so. Perhaps the most lively was Ms. Hyŏn Sunyŏng, who played Kye Wŏlhyang. The scene heading towards the Taedong River at dawn, combined with the musical effect, was the most vivid (生動). Ms. Kim Sinjae as Sŏnhyŏng was also elegant and simple (雅淡).
Kim Tongin - If you ask me, Ms. Han Ŭnjin's expression was too stiff and lacked variation. Also, Mr. Ch'oe Namyong was too cheerful to portray the anguished intellectual type of Yi Hyŏngsik, and he was excessively extravagant for a school teacher.
Paek Ch'ŏl - In the original work, Sin Usŏn is unrestrained (浩蕩) and unconventional (파탈), making one think of Yang Chu (揚朱), but Mr. Yi Paeksu's appearance (風采) and actions were insufficient to express that aspect.
Also, the initial swing (鞦韆) scene was good, but the coin-tossing game (돈치기) players were all jumbled together, and it was too long, which had the regrettable effect (遺憾) of diminishing (半減) the rising emotion (感興).
Yi Hŏn'gu - And what I particularly noticed was that the music was good. Generally speaking, although we've pointed out several flaws, it was a clean, neat, and tasteful literary work. I think the director's effort (勞) was considerable.

The Director's Intentions
Reporter - Your ears must be hurting, but you've listened patiently until the end. (All laugh) Now, Mr. Pak Kich'ae, the director, please say a few words.
Mr. Pak, did you carry out the direction while maintaining close contact (連絡) with Ch'unwŏn?
Pak Kich'ae - My initial thought was to do so, but in the end, I produced it entirely according to my own ideas without any contact.
Reporter - What point caused you the most hardship (苦心)?
Pak Kich'ae - "Mujŏng" is one of the finest works in our literary world (文壇), so while trying not to damage its artistic beauty (藝術美), on the other hand, this film is the inaugural work (創立紀念作品) of the Chosŏn Film Company (朝鮮映畵株式會社). This means it shouldn't cause financial loss to the company in terms of public screening (興行上). To achieve that, popular appeal (通俗味) had to be added (加味). Having to create it carrying this handicap, I tried my best, in my own way, to harmonize artistry and popularity.
Reporter - Traces of that were visible.
Pak Kich'ae - And next, during location shooting <129>, I felt many constraints (不自由). We shot on location in P'yŏngyang city 3 or 4 times, but as you know, P'yŏngyang has many restrictions (制限) on filming (撮影). Because of that, we couldn't rotate the camera (캐메라) from high ground (高臺), so the area around Pubyŏk Pavilion couldn't become a lively (生動) picture. Access to Nŭngna Island (陵羅島) was difficult, so we couldn't handle those wonderful weeping willows freely. That's why we had no choice but to repeatedly film that flat water flow (水流).
Also, we needed to use extras (엑스튜라). For example, when filming the initial scene with the swing, seesaw (널), and coin-tossing (돈치기), we used many extras, but they were all rural farmers and children who wouldn't make the expressions as directed.
It couldn't be helped, as these folks, who had never seen a moving picture (活動寫眞) in their lives, were grabbed off the street, paid 30 or 50 jeon at a time, seated, and sent away. Hahaha. (All laugh)
Reporter - Anything else besides that?
Pak Kich'ae - The cost of film (필님) became extremely expensive, so we couldn't use film freely. Because we tried to shoot without NGs (No Good takes), we couldn't freely select and gather material during editing.
Reporter - Were there any parts cut (캇트) by censorship (檢閱)?
Pak Kich'ae - There were none. Because we were extremely careful.
Reporter - Please offer some rebuttal (反駁) to the critiques from everyone earlier.
(All laugh)
Pak - They are all correct statements. I listened intently and gained much. However, if I may state a few of my opinions, because we had to make it within a limited film length (尺數), there were several places I reluctantly cut (割愛) even though I knew they were scenes that truly needed to be included.
As Ms. Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi pointed out earlier, the pastoral scenery (田園 風景) of Pyŏnguk's house in Hwangju, the flood relief scene (水害救濟 光景) in Samnangjin, and the scenes of P'aesŏng School in P'yŏngyang, etc.
Initially, I planned to make this *Mujŏng* in two parts, upper and lower volumes. That's why I initially used up a lot of footage depicting the childhood period.
Then, circumstances changed midway, and suddenly trying to prepare it as a single volume created strain (無理), so I compressed sections here and there throughout, ending up cutting out many scenes.
Also, I deliberately refrained (삼갓서요) from heightening scenes like the one at P'aesŏng School. I trust (밋어요) that you wise people will understand <130> why.
Similarly, I deliberately avoided tragic scenes of houses being swept away and people and animals dying during the flood. So, I too thought it turned out to be a somewhat bland work, lacking much dramatic fluctuation (波瀾屈曲). And regarding the actors' performances, because the filming schedule (撮影 時日) was short (짤너서) and rushed, we couldn't practice sufficiently, so I know (아러요) there were parts that seemed stiff (生硬). Anyway, I intend to put my full effort into my second work.
Pak Kich'ae - Mr. An Sŏgyŏng, who just finished making "Sim Ch'ŏng" (沈淸), please say something.
Sŏgyŏng - It's a bit awkward. As someone who also earns a living from Choyŏng (朝映 - Chosŏn Film Co.), talking about my own company's work... However, while there were undoubtedly flaws, there were many parts where I admired (歎服) the director's conception (着想) and technique (手法).
The humorous scene of the stone Buddha outside the village as young Yŏngch'ae flees her hometown, or when Sin Usŏn and Pak Yŏngch'ae are about to fall in love (想愛), the wedding procession passing far in the background (背後)—there are many places I viewed favorably besides these. Mr. Pak certainly put in a lot of effort (苦心).
And as Mr. Pak also expressed earlier, it wasn't feasible to make a simple literary work, nor could one make a popular work thinking only of box office success; this is where the difficulty lies. Didn't even Na Un'gyu fail to achieve great success with "Kaecheokcha" (開拓者)?
Reporter - Yes, I understand. Mr. Yi Kyuhwan of "Nagŭne" (The Wanderer), please say something.
Yi Kyuhwan - My impressions (感想) are similar to Mr. An's. The difficulty of coordinating the weather (日氣) during location shooting, the trouble with extras ("엑스추라"), all of it is the same. Moreover, the difficulty of character portrayal (性格 描寫), especially in a literary work, is something we both feel. However, regarding the filming of literary works mentioned earlier, I'd like to say one thing: in Tokyo (東京), Ishizaka Yōjirō's (石坂洋次郞) "Wakai Hito" (若イ人 - Young People) or Tasaka Tomotaka's (田坂具隆) "Robō no Ishi" (路傍ノ石 - Wayside Pebble) and "Tsuzurikata Kyōshitsu" (*方敎室, likely 綴方教室 - Spelling Classroom) were all successful, but I believe one must consider the educational level (敎養) of the audience there.
And thinking about whether putting out such works would achieve box office success or not, there are many doubtful points.
An Sŏkchu - Perhaps works like "Taki no Shiraito" (瀧の白系 - The Water Magician) or "Ryōjin no Teisō" (良人の貞操 - A Husband's Chastity) are easier to handle than purely literary pieces (純 文藝物).
Reporter - Since Mr. Kim Chŏnghyŏk from the Choyŏng Company has come (오었으니), what's the verdict (評判) on "Mujŏng"? (Continued on page 63) <131>
Kim Chŏnghyŏk - I just came straight from the theater, and the morning show was sold out (滿員), the second afternoon show was also sold out, and for this third showing, they put up the "Sold Out" sign (札止) and hundreds of audience members had to turn back. It was a huge success (大盛況). As "Mujŏng"'s reputation (聲譽) has been great for over 20 years, it seems everyone wants to see it at least once.
Paek Ch'ŏl - What about the audience demographic (層)?
Kim Chŏnghyŏk - It seemed to be mostly intellectuals (인테*), teachers (敎員*), and students. It looked like many female students were mixed in (석인 듯 했어요) as well.
Samch'ŏlli - Thank you (感*했슴니다) for speaking (람슴하여 주서) for so long. (End) <63>

**Summary**

This article is a roundtable discussion held in April 1939 about the newly released film adaptation of Yi Kwangsu's (Ch'unwŏn) famous novel *Mujŏng* (1917). The discussion features the film's director Pak Kich'ae, actors Han Ŭnjin and Mun Yebong, prominent writers like Kim Tongin and Yi Muyŏng, film critic Yi Hŏn'gu, and other directors. While acknowledging the film's popular success, the participants offer sharp critiques, noting its deviation from the original novel, lack of thematic clarity, stiff acting, and omission of key scenes (like the P'aesŏng School students' song and the Samnangjin flood sequence). Director Pak Kich'ae defends his choices, citing budget constraints (expensive film stock), limitations on shooting locations (especially in P'yŏngyang), difficulties with untrained extras, the need to balance artistic integrity with commercial viability for the Chosŏn Film Company's first major release, and pressures possibly related to censorship (implied in avoiding potentially sensitive scenes like student gatherings or disaster depictions). The discussion touches upon the actors' interpretations of their roles, particularly focusing on the theme of female chastity ("A virtuous woman does not serve two husbands").

From a historical perspective, the article is revealing about the challenges and constraints of filmmaking in colonial Korea. The discussion highlights the tension between adapting canonical Korean literature and the commercial demands of the nascent film industry under Japanese rule. The director's comments on budget limitations, location restrictions (especially in P'yŏngyang), and the careful handling of potentially sensitive themes (student activities, disaster) underscore the economic and political pressures faced by Korean filmmakers. The mention of the Chosŏn Film Company's founding and the reference to audience demographics (intellectuals, teachers, students) provide insights into the cultural landscape and film reception of the late 1930s. The explicit mention of Japanese films and directors also shows the cultural dialogue and influence occurring at the time.

金東煥(本社)-오늘 저녁 바로 시내 黃金座에서 映畵 「無情」이 상영되어 滿都 士女의 인기를 끄을고 있슴니다. 이 때에 우리들은 『無情의 밤』을 여러서 영원히 藝術上 尊貴한 이 작품에 대하야 이칠 수 없는 아름다운 기억을 삼고저 함니다.
몬저 말슴할 것은 春園께서 이 자리에 꼭 나오시기로 되였든데 았가 오후부터 身熱이 심해서 그만 결석하섰슴니다. 유감임니다만은 그 대신 이 작품을 감독한 朴基采씨에게 보내는 書翰一札이 入手되섰기에 이 아래 공개함니다.
