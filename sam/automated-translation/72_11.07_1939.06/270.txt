女學校 寄宿舍監 日記 (Girls' School Dormitory Supervisor's Diary)
Now it is early summer. Just this past April, welcoming the new students chirping like skylarks, how are the appearances of the dormitories, especially among the girls' schools in Seoul city bustling with new colors and fragrances (香*)? Now, we decide to delve into the diaries of the dormitory supervisors (舍監선생님) from various schools.

梨專篇 (Ewha Women's College Section)
Patrolling Chin, Sŏn, and Migwan Dormitories
Yi Chŏng'ae (李貞愛), Dormitory Supervisor, Ewha Women's College

February Xth
Though it's still dark outside, footsteps sound frequently below (*下). <153> It seems the Music Department (音樂科) girls are already up. Those kids get up as soon as it's five o'clock. Even on nights with winter blizzards, they wake up that early to study. It takes quite a while to get down to the music building from the dormitory, but they go down readily, seemingly unafraid of the pitch-black night. It seems they have no thoughts other than studying. They are twice as busy as the Literature (文科) and Home Economics (家專科生) students. Sometimes they even practice singing in their rooms. Because they make "oong-oong" sounds while holding their sheet music (악보), other department students often get annoyed. Although I tell them to be quiet in the rooms, observing their desire not to rest even for a moment, there are times I let it be somewhat. The graduating class girls say their fingertips have developed calluses.

March Xth
Time flies. It seems like only yesterday when last year's graduates left, crying and packing their bags, but a whole year has already passed, and the time for another sad farewell has arrived.
Parting is sad whenever and wherever it occurs, but this task of sending them off every year is truly unbearable. The feelings of those leaving and those sending them off are likely not much different, but the heart of the person remaining is indescribable. That must be why the proverb says, "You don't know the spot of someone who came in, but you know the spot of someone who went out." This year, especially, we have to send out 70 students, so the house will feel empty. Thinking about that already makes a corner of my heart feel hollow and lonely. On top of that, the younger students are already preparing gifts for their graduating seniors (언니들). Outings have sharply increased lately compared to other times, and when they return, they each carry a bundle (보퉁이), having bought something. Upon returning,
Some ask, "What did you buy?" while others retort, "You tell me first."
One child says, "I bought something really cool."
Another says, "Mine is absolutely secret." Anyway, it's quite a fuss. They seem to find joy and comfort in giving gifts to their friends like this. Truly an interesting time! It feels like a happy period. I feel envious, wondering if I ever had such times.

March Xth
After lights out (消燈), making a full round of the dormitory (舍內) is a daily task, and tonight was no exception. It seemed all the children had gone to bed and were sleeping quietly. I toured the "Chin" (眞) and "Sŏn" (善) dormitories and quietly walked up to the second floor of "Migwan" (美舘), <154> careful not to wake their sweet slumber. Then I discovered a light still on in one room. At first, I wondered what they could be doing with the light on after lights-out time, and worriedly thought perhaps someone had suddenly fallen ill (病者), so I quickly opened the door. Inside, a Literature Department student (文科生) was sitting facing her desk, writing something, while the other children were all asleep. I scolded her, saying it was disturbing others' sleep and also wrong to break the dormitory rules by not turning off the light,
"What are you doing not sleeping? What were you doing during the day that you have to study now?" The child gently pushed what she was writing (*) onto the desk and said,
"Please forgive me, Teacher. It's very quiet now, and the moon is so bright, I felt I could write something, so I got up after lying down." Then she silently turned off the light and went to bed. I gently closed the door (사르지 닷고) and turned to leave. Indeed, the moon was truly bright. Stepping out into the yard, a moon as large as a big pumpkin hung among the pine branches, so bright that the mountain behind the dormitory, the wide field in front of the school, the village rooftops, and the river flowing far in the distance were clearer than daylight, the pine trees looked even bluer, and everything was vividly visible. "It really is a night that makes one feel like writing something," I murmured, repeating the Literature student's earlier words as I headed towards my room. It seems a train bound for Fengtian (奉天行) is just leaving Sinch'on Station (新村驛); the moonlight itself seems to tremble with the powerful (**찬) steam whistle (汽笛).

March Xth
Today, starting at 3 PM, there was a reception for the graduating students at the school's (*관) small auditorium, so the entire graduating class (卒業班全名) attended. They searched out and wore the most beautiful clothes they owned.
There was a child in a navy skirt and yellow jeogori, one dressed head-to-toe in pink; anyway, they looked much prettier (엡버들) than when wearing their school uniforms. Since school rules allowed them to dress up as much as they liked (낏것 채려도) just for today, some children wore new clothes and even makeup, and there was also a child in a yellow-trimmed jeogori, a red silk skirt (갑사치마), and flower-patterned shoes (꽃신), dazzling both classmates and teachers. I thought they only knew how to study and nothing else, but it seems they knew quite a bit about how to dress prettily (엡부게 채리는 법식). <155>

京保篇 (Kyŏngsŏng Childcare School Section)
Peacefully Sleeping Young Butterflies
Yi Sŏnhŭi (李善徽), Dormitory Supervisor, Kyŏngsŏng Childcare School

As I was asked to disclose the dormitory life, I cannot refuse, so I am disclosing it. However, I cannot help but feel embarrassed. Why, you ask? Because revealing the secrets of this Queendom, where only lovely young ladies gather, unconditionally before everyone – where could there be greater embarrassment than this? However, since there seem to be many who wish to know too much (넘어도 알고자 하시는 분도 많이 게심으로), I close my eyes tightly and write this.
Our dormitory (寄宿) is located a little distance from the school in Songhyŏn-chō (松峴町), in a clean, cozy, yet stately traditional Korean-style house.
Inside this house live 61 energetic and lovely young ladies, and including maids and servants, there are 70 people in total. You can imagine how large the house is.
At 6 AM, I wake them from their peaceful sleep by ringing a bell. The children, waking from sweet dreams, plan their day's activities. From various rooms, faces still sleepy emerge; some friends greet each other saying "Slept well?" (잘 잣서), some students say "Ohayo" (ォハョ) or "good morning" (good r oing), while some cute students just smile sweetly.
By 6:30 AM, after finishing cleaning their rooms and all preparations, it is study time. In a good mood from breathing the fresh air, they sit facing their desks for self-study. However, while most are studying, there are also younger students who emerge belatedly, loitering (어정어정) with their washbasins (세수 대야).
At 7:30 AM, the dining hall bell rings, and everyone gathers there.
Pink jeogoris, navy jeogoris, various colored jeogoris, with full skirts or long skirts – this dining hall, where they arrive dressed like noble ladies (though many students are frugal), feels like a gathering place for aristocrats. However, when they put on their school uniforms and set off with their book bags tucked under their arms, they transform into neat and demure schoolgirls, which is truly marvelous and astonishing. What's laid out (버리저) on the dining table is either bean sprout soup <156> or seaweed soup, noodle soup – one type, plus kimchi; this is the meal provided. However, not a single student complains about the food; while exchanging interesting stories (滋味있는 이약이), the meal ends. Seeing this, one can see that they have thoroughly recognized the current situation (시국), and possess sufficient qualifications to manage a household (살님할 수 있는 자격) adequately even if they go to any rural village.
As soon as the meal ends, some students head to school, while others wait for letters from their parents back home, some wait for documents, some eagerly peruse newspapers, and there are also cute students who cling to the dorm mother's skirt tails, calling her "Mother." When the students leave for school, I tell them, "Study well." This single phrase seems to give them great strength. Having left home, they regard the dorm mother's words like those of their own mother and father.
After finishing their lessons (學課) at school all day, they return to the dormitory, dragging their mentally (精神上으로나) and physically (肉身上으로나) exhausted bodies. From this time until dinner is free time. There are those who play the reed organ, do laundry, iron, or go into the kitchen (부억) to help the maids – many students showing potential to be good housewives in the future. On the other hand (其 반면에), it is also true that there are many avid readers diligently absorbed in books.
Waiting only for their three meals a day, when the dinner bell (食鍾) rings at 6 PM, they flock (몰여드러) to the dining hall and eat the same kind of meal as breakfast with more relish than if they were drinking milk or coffee (伽排). After dinner, until 6:30 PM is free time. This is truly the students' happiest and most enjoyable (滋味있는) time of the day. As if by prior agreement, they line up side-by-side (나란이) on the wooden floor like a flock of swallows (제비떼), singing songs and playing games (遊戱) learned at school. As if fairies are singing and dancing, during this time, they seem to transcend human suffering (苦) and enter a different world (別世界). While some are immersed in this feeling of pleasure (享樂感), in another corner, others are busy earnestly playing the harmonica (『하모니카』). The sound of their playing comes across like the trumpets (나발) of the heavenly host (天軍), quite fittingly.
Amidst this, the study bell rings. Quickly, as if nothing happened, they all return to their rooms, sit facing their desks, study, do handicrafts (手工), some students whisper stories (속싹속싹 이약이), some diligently write letters, and there are students studying early childhood education, future dedicated childcare providers (保育資格인). <173>
When studying finishes, they prepare for bed for about 30 minutes. However, if snacks arrive from home (親家), they use this time to gather in a large room and share even just one apple each equally (고루고루 노나 먹슴니다).
10 PM is bedtime. The students all go to their beds. But please don't think (아지 마집시요) they are all sleepyheads. There are times when they lie in the dark room, just blinking (깜박깜박하며), trying to realize their ideals, and on nights when the moonlight shines through the small window (들창), they seem to lie awake, unable to sleep, thinking of all sorts (온갓) of things. Once my footsteps fade away, the sound of soft whispering (쏘근쏘근 속사거리는 소리) has occurred more than once or twice. Among them are many pranksters who sometimes concoct amusing nonsense (년센쓰), making it impossible not to burst out laughing (못 박일 만큼한 일이 종종 있음니다). Once, one of the pranksters disguised herself (變飾하고) as a handsome man and entered the room of a peacefully sleeping friend, giving her a startling fright (가슴을 선듯하게 하여 한바탕 놀내인 일이 있었담니다).
However, they don't just play pranks like this night after night (밤났). One can also see protagonists with beautiful, fairy-like hearts who nurse their sick friends (病床에 있는 동모) through the night (밤을 새위가며).

淑明篇 (Sookmyung Section)
Forbidden Outings and Punishment
Im Yŏnggil (林英吉), Dormitory Supervisor, Sookmyung Higher Girls' School

It has been a while since I received the request from the editor (編輯氏) of Samch'ŏlli (三千里) to write down and send my diary as a dorm mother (舍監 선생), but somehow I feel I still lack the qualifications of a dorm mother, so picking up the pen is awkward.
Primarily, a dorm mother should firstly be of a respectable age, and secondly, have a personality that is strict yet motherly and affectionate. But looking at my age, I am not much older than the girls, my personality is not strict, nor am I affectionate. Given this, I am embarrassed (붓그러읍다) to be called a dorm mother.
Still, it's already been three years that I've continued this way (셉이다). Living day by day without any particular plan, just managing somehow, nothing has particularly improved <174> or worsened compared to the beginning. The only thing that has increased is nagging (잔소리). At first, I couldn't even nag properly; when the girls talked back, I would get upset and make a big fuss (뒤법석 야단법석) while nagging. Nowadays, I don't do that, and I've learned to nag somewhat systematically. So, perhaps the experience gained from three years of dorm life is this increase in nagging. It's pathetic and embarrassing. I experienced it during my student days too – there was nothing I disliked more than the dorm mother's nagging.
Even wanting to go out once required answering who you were going to see, being told to come back early (일즉 드러와야 한다), or listening to all sorts of lectures if you wanted permission (허락을 바들여면) to eat something. Going out briefly into the schoolyard or dormitory garden at night prompted a huge fuss (야야단단이고). If you happened to visit a friend's room, you'd hear "Why aren't you studying in your own rooms and flocking together (몰여 다니냐는)?" If your face and hands got chapped (터저서) in winter and you applied a little cream (그림), you'd hear "What's the point of dolling yourself up?" You'd be told to walk quietly (거름을 조용조용 거르라는 둥) – anyway, the very sound of the dorm mother's voice was enough to give you a headache. But now that I'm in this position, I find I must repeat that nagging; it's a pitiful situation.

Today too, I punished a child for not returning before the curfew after an outing. After giving the punishment, I returned to my room and thought for a long time about my past days. I sometimes conduct inspections to see if any girls have cosmetics (화장품) or if any are flocking together (몰켜 다니는) due to same-sex affection (동성연애래도 해서 한테). After conducting these inspections, something I always feel is that there seems to be no animal more easily governed by its environment than humans.

It is fully spring. The pussy willows (버들강아지) on the desk grow plumper (쩌가고) daily, and an unknown energy shimmers (아릉긴다) by the window sill. The fourth-year students, facing their imminent graduation, are distracted (散亂), and even their usual loud chatter and playfulness have died down lately.
The girls going on to higher education are fine, but those going into domestic life or society, and those who haven't decided anything yet, look pitifully depressed. Seeing them makes me regret (후회나군 한다) all the nagging I've done so far. But why does that feeling not last long, and I end up showing my old habits again (제 버릇을 내놋는 일이 웬일인가)?
Still, today passed without a single word of nagging. The night duty student did very well, earning praise from the kitchen and dining hall, and the girls who went out (외출했든 아이들) all came back early (죄다 일즉 들어와서), making me feel cheerful. Perhaps the sermon by Vice-Principal Nomura (野村 선생님 설교) during chapel this morning (on Sundays, only dormitory students gather for worship led by Vice-Principal Nomura 野村副校長) deeply resonated with them. <189>

**Summary**

This article presents excerpts from the diaries of three dormitory supervisors (舍監) at prominent girls' schools in Seoul (Ewha, Kyŏngsŏng Childcare, Sookmyung) in 1939. The supervisors describe the daily routines, joys, and challenges of managing the dormitories: the rigorous schedules, the students' dedication (especially music students at Ewha), the simple wartime meals accepted without complaint (Kyŏngbo), the bittersweet farewells to graduating seniors, the students' moments of leisure and pranks, and the supervisors' own reflections on their roles, particularly the necessity and burden of enforcing rules and nagging. The entries offer glimpses into the disciplined yet vibrant lives of young Korean women pursuing education under Japanese colonial rule, capturing moments of artistic inspiration, youthful camaraderie, anxiety about the future, and the complex relationship between students and authority figures. For historians, the article is interesting for its portrayal of elite women's education, the normalization of Japanese figures in school administration (Vice-Principal Nomura), the subtle reference to wartime austerity ("시국"), the mention of travel links to Manchuria (Fengtian), and the social anxieties reflected in inspections for cosmetics and "same-sex love" (동성연애). It also showcases Korean language usage from the late 1930s.

舍監 선생의 일기를 적어 보내라는 三千里 編輯氏로부터 부탁을 바든 지는 오래나 어쩐지 나는 아직도 舍監의 자격을 가추지 못한 듯해서 붓 들기가 거북하다.
원체 舍監이라면 첫재 나이가 듬직해야 할 것이고 둘재 성격이 엄격하면서도 어머니와 같이 자애로워야 할 것인데 나는 아직 나이로 보드래도 아이들보다 별로 더 만치 못하고 또 성격이 엄하지도 못하고 그러타고 자애롭지도 못하다. 이렇게 되고보니 나는 舍監이라고 불니우기에 붓그러읍다.
그래도 벌서 3년을 게속해온 셉이다. 날마다 아모 계획 없이 그럭저럭 꾸려가는 살님이라 처음이나 지금이나 別달니 낳어진 것도＜174＞ 없고 못해진 것도 없다. 오직 한 가지 늘었다면 잔소리뿐이겠다. 처음엔 잔소리나마 바로 못해서, 아이들이 말을 일키면 속이 상하여 뒤법석 야단법석을 치며 잔소리를 했는데 요새는 그렇지 않고 좀 체계있게 잔소리할 줄 알게 되었으니 3년간 사감생활에서 어든 경력이 이 잔소리 늘은 것이라고나 할까. 한심하고 붓그런 일이다. 나도 학생시대에 지내 본 일이지만 사감 선생의 잔소리처럼 싫은 것은 없었다.
외출을 한 번 하재도 누구한태 가느냐, 일즉 드러와야 한다 둥 뭐 좀 먹고 싶어서 허락을 바들여면 온갓 설교를 듯는 둥 밤에 학교 마당에나 기숙사 뜰에 잠간만 나가도 야야단단이고 혹 동무 방에 가는 일이 있으면 웨 제 방에서 공부들 안 하고 몰여 다니냐는 둥 겨울에 얼골과 손이 터저서 그림을 조곰식 발녀도 모양을 내군 뭘 하겠느냐는 둥 거름을 조용조용 거르라는 둥,-었쨋든 사감 선생의 말소리라면 골치가 지긋지긋할 정도였다. 그러나 지금 내가 당하고 보매 그 잔소리를 되푸리하지 않으면 안 되니 딱한 노릇이다.

오늘도 한 아이가 외출을 나갔다가 시간 전에 돌오지 않어서 벌을 주었다. 벌을 주고 방에 도라 와서 내 지내간 날을 오-래 생각해 보았다. 화장품 가진 아이가 있나 또는 동성연애래도 해서 한테 몰켜 다니는 아이가 있나하고 각금 조사를 해보는데 이렇게 조사를 하구 나선 언제나 느끼는 바이지만 사람처럼 환경의 지배를 잘 밧는 동물은 없을 것 같다.

完然히 봄이다. 책상 우에 버들강아지가 날마다 살이 쩌가고 창 기슭에 아지 못할 기운이 아릉긴다. 4학년생들은 얼마 남지 않은 졸업 날을 앞에 두고 마음이 散亂해서 그렇게 잘 떠들고 잘 놀고 하든 것도 요새는 풀이 죽어 있다.
상급학교에 갈 아이는 그렇지도 안치만 가정이나 사회에 나갈 아이들과 아직 아모 데도 定치 못하고 있는 아이들은 보기 딱하리 만치 우울해 있다. 그런 것을 볼 때엔 내가 지금까지 그들에게 잔소리한 것이 후회나군 한다. 그러나 그 마음이 오래 가지 않어서 또 제 버릇을 내놋는 일이 웬일인가.
