**Body Text Translation:**
Ewha Woman's College Professor [Runs] a Coffee Shop, A Couple Both Graduates of American Universities
Seeking the House of Labor (1)

Amidst this street where the cherry blossoms, forsythia, and azaleas stir people's hearts, there is another curious story: an intellectual couple has set up a coffee shop right in the heart of Seoul, on Chongno, and are making the tea, cleaning, and collecting the money themselves.

-They say the wife even taught at Ewha Woman's College.
-The husband is said to be an American graduate.
-The wife is an American graduate too, isn't she?
-But why on earth would people like that run a tea house?
-Perhaps they're doing it as a hobby.

These are the words coming from the mouths of people fond of curiosity about others' affairs. In any case, it seems these people find **something peculiar** in the fact that someone who studied in America and was even a full-time professor at Chosŏn's top school is **running a tea house**. Truthfully, the reporter was not without curiosity either. So, one afternoon at five o'clock, on a day when flowers seemed to fill the streets, I sought out the Chongno Coffee Shop run by the intellectual couple who were the talk of the town.

The coffee shop was located right where the **Olympia** used to be. As I stepped inside the door**, it was unchanged from the old days (following 2 lines illegible) <115> At the register sat the wife, Mrs. Pak Maria, while her husband, Mr. Yi Ki-bung, was cleaning the hall.

"So it's true that they do the cleaning, make the tea, and take the money themselves," the reporter quickly realized. Addressing Mrs. Pak Maria seated at the register, I conveyed the purpose of my visit, and then sat opposite her at a corner table she guided me to.

Sitting face to face, the reporter abruptly asked,
"Who makes the tea? Does Mr. Yi make it? Or else, does Mrs. Pak?" To this, Mrs. Pak replied that neither her husband nor she makes the tea.

"But the rumor is that you both make the tea yourselves?"
"We haven't tried making tea yet. I don't know how to make it, and my husband probably doesn't know either." This she said, pointing towards Mr. Yi, who, after his wife had stood up from the register, was now busy answering the phone and taking money, unable to only do the cleaning.

"When did you start this business?"
"We opened our doors on Lunar New Year's Day of this year."

"How does it compare in enjoyment to being a professor?"
"Well... It hasn't been long since we started, so... We're just sort of feeling our way around, bewildered. Besides, I only come in the afternoons to help out briefly; it's mostly my husband doing it all alone."

"Some people seem curious as to why you chose a tea house, of all things. Really, why a tea house?"
"There's no special reason. It's just out of a desire to make a living. But it seems there's still very little understanding of tea houses in Chosŏn. Many people find it strange and ask me directly how a professor could run a tea house. What distinction can there be in business? Since my husband also did business in America, he wanted to stick to business here too. In the meantime, he tried gold mining, other ventures, and even ran a general store in Chae-dong, but they all failed, and now he has started this."

"What will you do if you make money?"
"Make money? Ho ho ho... We're burdened with others' debts, when will we ever make money?"

"I mean, after you've paid off all the debts and are making money."
"What's the use of someone with empty pockets sitting around making rough calculations? If I had the money, I'd like to try a good business, but who knows if my mind might change again once the money is in hand.<116> My husband says that if his plan goes well, he wants to start a coffee shop chain."

"A chain store, you say?"
"He means not just having this one, but making several tea houses."

"Could you please explain that plan?"
"I don't know the details well. I'll ask my husband to tell you."

Mrs. Maria called over her busy husband, Mr. Yi, to the table where we sat.
"This gentleman is asking you to talk about the chain store plan."

As Mr. Yi sat at the table, his wife introduced her husband to the reporter and said this.
"It all depends on having the money. We haven't laid out a definite plan yet. But I think doing a chain would be quite good. If we run several, the intention is to make everything used in the coffee shops – raw materials, ingredients, dishes, everything – in our own factory. That way, we won't have to pay intermediary profits. Frankly, I don't want to do such small work. I want to try a larger business. Before starting this, I had intended to try opening a large restaurant. But then this opportunity came up, so I took it."

"How much did you take it over for?"
"For 7,000 won."

"What's the approximate total daily income?"
"On good days, it sometimes exceeds 50 won."

"About how much is the profit?"
"I don't know yet. Right now, money is just constantly going out."

"Well, it hasn't even been two months yet."
This was Mrs. Pak Maria, adding to her husband's words. While we were having this conversation, the setting sunbeams shone more brightly into the hall, making the coffee shop as clear as a fishbowl (魚황). In the clear room, the chairs, tables, the photographs attached to the walls, and the few plants all harmonized well, so even though there were many customers, it didn't feel bustling.

"What kind of customers usually come?"
"Befitting the location, many customers come for brief business discussions. We try to decorate it as tastefully as possible, and hang the pictures on the walls carefully. We haven't put in a gramophone or a radio. That's why students don't come at all."
Listening and looking around, it truly seemed that most of the customers were either company employees or business people (Continued on page 122). <117>

"What time do you open in the morning and close at night?"
"We open the doors at eight in the morning. Because there are people who must have their morning coffee. We go home at twelve at night. We don't have a cook (쿡), but there are six young staff members, and to make it convenient for them, we let them rest once a month. And they rotate shifts daily. The kids who come in early in the morning leave at eight at night, and those who come in later stay until twelve. Although it's about making a living, we try, as much as possible, to be more beneficial to the kids working with us than others might be."

During this conversation, customers came, the phone rang, and the couple had to get up I don't know how many times. Feeling awkward about talking for too long, the reporter rose from the seat at this point. <122>

**Summary**

This 1939 article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine interviews Yi Ki-bung and Pak Maria, a highly educated couple (both studied in America, Pak was a professor at Ewha Woman's College) who recently opened a coffee shop (*tabang*) on Chongno, Seoul. Despite societal surprise at intellectuals engaging in such commerce, they explain it's a necessity after previous business failures. They run the shop themselves, aiming for a tasteful, quiet atmosphere catering to businesspeople, not students. Yi Ki-bung expresses ambitions for a larger business, potentially a chain of coffee shops with integrated production to cut costs. The article details their daily operations, including staff management, and highlights their hands-on approach.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting as it reveals contemporary attitudes towards social class and occupation in colonial Korea, particularly the surprise or even disapproval directed at highly educated individuals ("int'eri") engaging in manual labor and retail. It highlights the economic difficulties faced even by the elite, forcing them into commerce despite past prestigious roles. Furthermore, Yi Ki-bung's ambition to create a "chain store" with vertical integration reflects nascent modern capitalist ideas penetrating the colonial economy. Historians might find the detailed description of the *tabang*'s operation, clientele, and the couple's rationale valuable for understanding urban life, social mobility (or lack thereof), and entrepreneurial ventures under Japanese rule.
