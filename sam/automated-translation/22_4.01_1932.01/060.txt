My Alma Mater and Respected Teachers
Army Military Academy

Former Army First Lieutenant O Sanggŭn

I entered the Army Military Academy that existed in the Old Korean Era right in the 10th year of Kwangmu, when the military academy was founded. I entered as a first-batch student, and my age at that time was 18. The school's location was in front of Hŭnghwa Gate (興化門) (the current site of Kyŏngsŏng Middle School and adjacent official residences), and the training ground was also right there, encompassing the current Kyŏngsŏng Middle School sports field and the surrounding wide plains, all of which belonged to the academy at that time.
As for the instructors, the Principal was Yi Hakkyun, the Commandant was Yi Pyŏngmu, and the instructors included Yi Hŭidu, Cho Sŏnggŭn (now a Lieutenant General), Sin Ugyun, Kwŏn Hakkyun (deceased), Yi Taewi (deceased), Wang Yusik, and others. To briefly mention their backgrounds, Principal Yi Hakkyun had studied at a school called Yugyŏng Kongwŏn before the Kabo Reforms, had excelled in military science through self-study, and also had deep knowledge of English. Commandant Yi Pyŏngmu was a graduate of the Japanese Military Academy, and the other instructors were also all graduates of the Japanese Military Academy.
Besides them, instructors for military arts included Chŏn Ugi, Yi Ch'anggŭn, Yi Subong, and others, and there were also assistant professors.
Among the fellow students who entered as the first batch and studied together, many have passed away now, and many of the survivors are scattered far away overseas, but I wish to refrain from describing them.
Regarding the school expenses at that time, everything was covered by the government. The curriculum mainly referred to Japanese, German, and British materials. There was a specialized translator at the school who translated foreign military science, considered traditional military science, printed it, and used it as teaching material. Besides that, lectures were sometimes written down as notes.
As for military drill, a unique Korean-style drill method was created and used.
Also, recalling the time after my graduation, immediately after graduating, I served as an apprentice in the 1st Royal Guard unit, then was assigned as a platoon leader in the 2nd Royal Guard unit. After that, I transferred to the military police<12>, and later served as an instructor at the Yŏnsŏng School (練成學校). I resigned from my position as instructor in the spring of the Pyŏngo year (丙午年); my military rank at that time was First Lieutenant (正尉).
After submitting my resignation, I was appointed as a teacher at a school in P'yŏngan Province, then returned to the capital and served as a teacher at a school called Sangdong Youth Academy (尙洞청년학원). After living abroad for about 3-4 years, I returned to Korea and took a position at Kyŏngsin School (儆新學校). After that, I moved around, working as a teacher at Kaesŏng Anglo-Korean Academy (開城 漢英學院), Chŏngju Osan School (定州 五山學校), and Chŏnju Sinhŭng School (全州 新興學校). After finishing my teaching job in Chŏnju, it was the Kimi year (己未年) [1919]. After experiencing a period of restricted life, I attempted to publish a private newspaper with comrades, but things somehow did not go as planned. Shortly after, the Tonga Ilbo (東亞日報) was born. Later, as the currents of new thought began to flow in Chosŏn, I was involved in organizing the Labor Mutual Aid Society and the Youth Federation. Since then, I have been engaged in the business world.

I would like to describe briefly the situation around the opening of the Army Military Academy at that time. As mentioned earlier, the first principal of the Army Military Academy, Mr. Yi Hakkyun (who died as a guest in Shanghai), was proficient in English. As a military man, he frequently visited the palace and was in a position to communicate with Minister Min Ch'ungjŏnggong (閔忠正公) and plan affairs. These two gentlemen often discussed their opinions on the reform and national defense of Korea. As a result, feeling the need for regular military training, they agreed on the opinion to first establish the 52nd Regiment (3 battalions, 1 regiment), and decided to hasten the training of officers. This was one aspect of the motivation behind the founding of the Army Military Academy.
Thus, the Board of Marshals (元師府) was established within the palace, and four chiefs were appointed for the Military Affairs Department, Inspectorate Department, Accounting Department, and Records Department.
Recruitment for students began, and out of over 3,000 applicants, 200 were selected. This was in the fourth lunar month of the Musul year (戊戌年). The entrance examination took place on the 25th and 26th of the same fourth lunar month. There were two types of exams: the military department exam and the imperial presence exam.
The military department exam included:
1.  Height must be 5 ch'ŏk (feet) or more.
2.  Vision test.
3.  Physical examination.<13>
4.  Reading test.
5.  Composition.
6.  Lifting an iron bar.
These were the requirements.
On the 7th day of the same fifth lunar month, the imperial presence exam was held. Emperor Kwangmu (光武) personally attended at Yungmu Pavilion (隆武亭) inside Yŏngsŏng Gate (永成門). The Minister of Military Affairs and the four chiefs were present. After composing and reciting a composition, height was measured.
The training period for the first batch of officer cadets lasted a total of 28 months, and the graduation ceremony was held at Tŏksu Palace (德宮).
Emperor Kwangmu wore the full dress uniform of the Grand Marshal, and Emperor Sunjong (純宗帝) wore the full dress uniform of the Vice Marshal. The four chiefs, the principal, and instructors were present. Then, Emperor Kwangmu personally read the imperial edict (칙어) (omitted - original text), personally conferred the graduation certificates, bestowed a feast (賜찬), and patted the graduates on the back, saying (omitted - original text).
Recalling the Army Military Academy of that time now is merely an exciting and memorable event of the past. (End)

P'yŏngyang Taesŏng School and An Ch'angho

Kim Hyŏngsik, Chosŏn Ilbo

After the Independence Club (獨立協會), Chosŏn's only political party, disappeared, around 1906-1907, thoughtful people throughout Chosŏn cried out everywhere for "cultivation of strength" (실력수양). The land of Chosŏn saw the emergence of young patriots, and private schools sprouted up (簇生) in every nook and cranny. Among these, the Sŏ Chosŏn (Western Chosŏn) region, in particular, had an educational fervor that burned like fire, more so than other regions. While there might be historical reasons specific to that region, the environment at the time meant that there were notably more leaders there than elsewhere.

To Cultivate Leaders of the Masses
Among these leaders, figures like Tosan An Ch'angho (島山 安昌浩) were truly great personalities who gave an indelible impression to the people of Sŏ Chosŏn. His life's devotion was a crystallization of blood and sincerity, expressed as "Love the nation." When he delivered a speech, everyone was moved, shed tears, and became excited. Women threw off their rings and ornaments they wore to support his cause.<14>
This man of blood and sincerity, An Ch'angho, established a secondary educational institution first in Sŏram-ri, P'yŏngyang, the center of Sŏ Chosŏn, to accommodate the graduates of the private schools sprouting up everywhere and, at the same time, to cultivate leaders of the masses—patriotic individuals who would execute his ideals and plans. He named it Taesŏng School (大成學校). Taesŏng School was truly the crystallization of Tosan An Ch'angho's sincerity and blood, and a microcosm of the popular thought at the time.

Flood of Admission Applicants
In 1907, when the news of Taesŏng School's establishment spread, the voices supporting and endorsing it shook all of Chosŏn. Admission applicants poured in like a tide, and suddenly 500-600 young men gathered.
(At that time, as a result of the entrance examination for the many students, almost all were placed in the preparatory class, with only 50 in the first grade. Yun Ch'iho (尹致昊) was nominated as the principal, while An Tosan acted as the acting principal himself. Additionally, a normal course and an agricultural course were attached.)
Although they were middle school students, the students of Taesŏng School at this time were young patriots aged 20 or 30. When they opened their mouths, it was all lamentation about the world's worries (憂X慨世); their actions showed they prided themselves as leaders of the masses.

Training with Military-Style Drills
The school's curriculum at that time, though called middle school, was much more advanced than today's middle schools. The 4th-grade curriculum was equivalent to about the 3rd year of some professional schools. Mathematics was particularly advanced, and the school facilities were exceptionally well-equipped for a middle school, perhaps unparalleled. Not only that, but as mentioned before, this school aimed to inspire the "Patriotic Spirit" (愛X精神). Every morning, a solemn assembly was held, where after loudly singing the XX song, there was a moral lecture (훈화) on "Patriotic Love," which all students obeyed as sacred words. Physical education time was most valued. The physical education teacher at the time was Chŏng Inmok (鄭仁穆) (now deceased), originally a military officer with noble aspirations, a man of iron and blood, who drilled the students entirely in military style.
Even in deep snow and biting cold, he made them exercise in the open fields; under the scorching sun that could melt iron, he gave lectures on tactics. Occasionally, he held emergency night assemblies, fostering courage in treacherous mountains and valleys. On the frozen river under the moonlight, they sang,
"Magnificent are our students! In military-style marching!"
and marched solemnly to cultivate vitality. Consequently, the students' spirit was lively, and discipline was strict, making them an object of general praise and admiration. In the 3rd year of Yunghŭi (隆熙) [1909], when the then Emperor (Sunjong), along with Viceroy Itō (伊藤統監), toured the west, the authorities ordered those welcoming them to cross the Japanese and Korean flags. However, the school (3 lines omitted - original text) maintained its formation without disorder and proceeded with the planned march, possessing such a dignified presence that even a thousand soldiers and ten thousand horses would not dare challenge its spirit.

Military Band and Football Competition Too
Also, among the students, an alumni association (동문회) was organized to allow complete self-governance. Within the alumni association, there were departments for debate, music, sports, inspection, and social activities. Each department was fully equipped. The football competition between Kyŏngsŏng and P'yŏngyang originated from this, and baseball games against Westerners were also a first (矯矢) for P'yŏngyang. Furthermore, the establishment of a military band within the school was a first at this time, and its instruments were also fully equipped. On the days the debate club met, everyone delivered impassioned and indignant speeches. The inspection department belonged to the physical education teacher and strictly enforced discipline.

Then, Then
Thus, the school's fortunes prospered day by day, but the footsteps of detectives and military police in the schoolyard became increasingly frequent. When An Tosan went into exile (망명) in Shanghai,<15> the more radical students scattered to North and South Manchuria and other foreign lands. Shortly after, when the Japan-Korea Annexation occurred, all students tried to X the curriculum. However, Chang Ŭngjin (張膺震), who succeeded An Tosan as principal at the time, placated the students with the principle of "focusing on building strength" (실력위주). Furthermore, due to repeated warnings from the authorities, when the curriculum chart hanging in the classroom had "Japanese Language" (日語) changed to "National Language" (國語), the students (rest omitted - original text)

Outward, Outward
Subsequently, the famous 105-Person Incident (the plot to assassinate Governor-General Terauchi) occurred. Centered around the P'yŏngyang T'aegŭk Sŏgwan (平壤太極書舘), which was a supporting organization for the school, individuals like An T'aeguk (安泰國) (passed away in Shanghai years ago), whose spirit was like steel, were imprisoned, and many students were also implicated (被X). This truly created an era of terror throughout the Sŏ Chosŏn region. Among the students, those with strong beliefs who attracted the attention of the authorities all went into exile. (At that time, prominent student patriots included Ok Kwanbin (玉觀彬) and O Taeyŏng (吳大泳), who were considered top figures). Consequently, the activities of the alumni association could not flourish as before, and the school's spirit began to shrink.

19 First Batch Graduates
However, in the spring of 1912, the first batch of 19 graduates emerged. This was indeed the flower-like fruition of the Mass Leader/Patriot=Lover Training Center, which had focused the aspirations and expectations of not just Sŏ Chosŏn but all of Chosŏn. However, among the students of Taesŏng School at that time, the so-called patriots with radical ideas were not particularly numerous among these graduates. Nevertheless, invitations came from all over Chosŏn welcoming these graduates and recruiting them as teachers. From the day after graduation, the majority of these individuals became teachers. One year after producing this first batch of graduates, the surrounding environment changed drastically, and the school was inevitably closed.

The Graduates' Subsequent Paths
Thinking back today, times have changed, stars have shifted; it's already an old story from 20 years ago. Moran Hill (牧丹峰) and the Taedong River (大同江), which cultivated the students' vitality, still remain as they were, but on the site of Taesŏng School—the crystallization of An Tosan's blood and sincerity, where 700-800 hot-blooded youths delivered speeches lamenting the world's worries (憂X慨世 XXXX) and energetically moved about with a spirit that could shake mountains and rivers—weeds grow profusely, evoking feelings in passersby. Now, only the Ch'ŏndogyo P'yŏngyang parish church stands there. How can one not feel the impermanence of human affairs! Moreover, where are the 19 young men who, at that time, garnered the aspirations of countless people and graduated with glory, and what kind of lives are they leading now!?
These 19 young men, who once bustled with hot blood, have now transformed into middle-aged men over 40. The world has already forgotten them, and some among them have become guests of the netherworld, harboring unresolved regrets. Investigating what they are doing now reveals:

The Teachers of That Time
Furthermore, besides these 19 graduates, the thousand or so young men who set foot in this school, even briefly, are now scattered both overseas and domestically, yet the spirit of An Tosan's ideology remains connected and consistent. After the Kimi Manse Movement [1919], the graduates in P'yŏngyang initiated an alumni reunion in P'yŏngyang, and hundreds gathered from all over Chosŏn. Holding their old school flag under the bright daylight, they sang their old songs together on the Taedong River,<16> stirring the people of P'yŏngyang. But that too was already 10 years ago. That so many could gather can be seen as an extension of An Tosan's "Sincerity" (誠).

Hansŏng Normal School and Respected Teachers

Kim Ch'angje, Teacher at Ewha Girls' High School

The Government Hansŏng Normal School (官立 漢城師範學校), established in the Ŭlmi year (乙未年) [1895], was the highest educational institution in the Old Korean Era. It was not only the sole normal education institution but also produced many talented individuals.
I knocked on the gates of Hansŏng Normal School when I was not yet 19 years old, five years after its establishment. Its location was the site of the current Kyodong Public Elementary School. At that time, the residence of Minister Cho (趙政丞邸) was temporarily used as the school building.
The academic system was a single track, the duration of study was 4 years, and the academic year was divided into two terms—Summer and Winter.
As for admission qualifications, the standard was quite high. Unlike today, it was an era when Chinese characters (Hanmun) were undoubtedly predominant, so one needed proficiency in the Seven Books (七書) and the academic ability to compose competently in pure Hanmun to be admitted.
At the time of the entrance examination, there were about 120 applicants, but only 18 passed. One reason for this was the limited capacity due to classroom and dormitory space, but in a sense, it could be said that the kind of examination hell we see today already existed back then.<17>
The entrance exam was held in the main hall of the Ministry of Education (學部). Paper, brushes, and ink were distributed. The chief examiner was the Minister of Education, the vice-examiner was the Director of Academic Affairs, and the general examiners were the various instructors. The examinees ranged from the youngest at 18 or 19 to those in their 40s or 50s, some already past middle age. They sat wearing large gat (갓, traditional hats), some even stroking their beards. Comparing it to the present, it must have seemed like a peculiar spectacle.
Recalling my own experience, the first day was a reading test, where they had us read from anywhere within the Seven Books. I was asked to read the preface to the Great Learning (大學) at that time.
About half failed the first day's reading test. On the second day, we wrote a composition in pure Hanmun, and again, more than half failed. On the third day, the results were announced.
Fortunately, I passed as the 8th among the 18 successful candidates. Considering the significant age differences and the intense lobbying (私請) among examinees at that time, passing with a relatively good score despite overcoming all these difficulties remains a pleasant memory to this day.
Thus, among the 18 admitted students, there were only one or two younger than me; everyone else was older, ranging from their 20s to 30s, 40s, and even over 40.
To briefly describe the school scene: there were classrooms and dormitories. In the classrooms, there were desks and chairs, and a blackboard on the teacher's platform. Both teachers and students sat wearing gat, teaching and learning. All school expenses were covered by the government (官費).
The curriculum was not as comprehensive as today's, but Hanmun included the Seven Books, with emphasis on the Analects (論語) and Mencius (孟子). There were three composition assignments per week (in pure Hanmun and mixed script). Mathematics ranged from arithmetic to algebra and geometry. Physics, chemistry, geography, and history were also taught, but Hanmun was the subject with the most class hours.
Although I later primarily taught subjects from all these areas, I had more interest in subjects belonging to the sciences rather than the humanities.
The principal was initially Mr. Kim Kakhyŏn (金珏鉉), who was then the Director of Academic Affairs, serving temporarily. Later, Mr. Yi Chongt'ae (李鍾泰), who was head of the Ministry of Education's compilation section, was formally appointed and took office. Among the instructors from the founding period, Mr. Chŏng Un'gyŏng (鄭雲慶), a renowned Hanmun scholar residing in Chin'ch'ŏn, taught Hanmun and pure Hanmun composition. Later, he was replaced by Pak Ŭnsik (朴殷植), a Hanmun scholar from P'yŏngan Province famous as the chief editor of the Hwangsong Sinmun (皇城新聞). Mr. Kim Hyŏngsik (金亨湜) [taught something unspecified], Mr. Pak Chŏngdong (朴晶東) taught physics, and Mr. Hulbert (헐벗), an American with deep knowledge of Korean and author of several books in Korean script, taught geography and mathematics.

Regarding the students' trends of thought at that time, I'll avoid details, but they were generally divided into two factions. One faction consisted of those over 30, driven by the desire for official positions (科宦慾), hoping to secure a government post after graduation. Some who came from the countryside aimed to study at government expense and achieve success. Some treated the school as a kind of dormitory, frequenting the homes of ministers or teachers, hoping for a good opportunity to drop out. It was common for students, after being enrolled for 6 months or a year, to transfer to the Telegraph School (電報學校) (similar to the current postal service training center, where one could become a telegraph official (電報主事) after just 3 months) or the Military Academy, so supplementary entrance exams were often held.<18>
On the other hand, the younger students were engrossed in absorbing new knowledge. Learning even one character of new science felt "wonderful," and learning two characters spurred intense investigation and inquiry. At that time, there were fewer than 10 people in the entire country who understood algebra or geometry. We young students lamented the ignorance of science and researched its dissemination.
Consequently, the older students held opposing views towards new theories. For instance, if told the Earth is round, they'd insist it's flat. If told water is composed of hydrogen and oxygen, they'd call it a lie. Some even adopted a mocking attitude. The younger students, however, enthusiastically welcomed new learning, pursuing study after study. Even some instructors regarded new learning as heresy and cast disdainful glances. Once, instructor Kim Hyŏngsik called me to discuss new theories. I explained what I knew clearly, providing examples as if giving a lecture. Mr. Kim listened as if it made sense and even acknowledged its correctness. Nevertheless, compared to today, the phrase "feeling the generation gap" doesn't even begin to cover the difference back then.
Also, looking at the ideological trends of the general student body, the faction described earlier as somewhat corrupt was engrossed in the desire for official positions, the ambition for high posts, and the pursuit of hollow fame. But the other faction of students, observing the turbulent situation at the end of the Korean Empire, harbored patriotic thoughts (X國思想) and eagerly read the newspapers of the time, Hwangsong Sinmun and Cheguk Sinmun (帝國新聞). I was an avid reader of the Hwangsong Sinmun back then.
A few people would go to Hŭnghwa School (興和學校) (a school founded by Minister Min Ch'ungjŏnggong, on the site of the current Kyŏngsŏng Childcare School) at night to learn English and surveying. Once a week, on Saturdays, they held debate and speech meetings, pounding on desks, arguing loudly, condemning political corruption, and advocating for political change. Sometimes during these debates, discussions became too radical, and several people were imprisoned for allegedly making seditious remarks about political reform.
In any case, my debut on the public speaking platform began during these Hŭnghwa School debate meetings.
Regarding teacher-student relationships, there were no special gatherings or informal talks. The way of showing respect was still the traditional Confucian style (서당식), treating the teacher as a master (사부).

Among my classmates from that time, some have passed away, and others scattered into government service midway through their studies. The only person I occasionally meet today is Mr. Kim Kyohŭi (金敎熙) from the sales department of the Tonga Ilbo.

Graduation Ceremony—I graduated from Hansŏng Normal School as part of the 6th graduating class. The ceremony was attended by the Minister of Education, Vice Minister of Education, Director of Academic Affairs, other ministry officials, and instructors. The ceremony included the awarding of diplomas by the Minister of Education, and afterwards, we received a tray of refreshments (교자 한 틀).
I graduated second in my class, and there's an anecdote related to this. Telling this story now is rather pointless, but at the time, being young, I was quite indignant, so I'll write it down as an amusing tale. It's none other than this: the initial 18 students dwindled mid-course, leaving only 7 at graduation. I had consistently ranked first throughout my studies but ended up second only at graduation. The subject was geography, and my score was about 10 points lower than the top student's. No matter how I thought about it, I should have received full marks in geography as my answers were accurate, but it was incomprehensible. Feeling puzzled and wronged, during the busy graduation period, an assistant instructor entrusted me and a few other students with organizing documents. While sorting the papers, I happened to see my answer sheet. Looking at it again, it was indeed accurate, and in terms of points, I had actually scored 9 points higher than the top student. Since I didn't have any particular ill feelings towards the assistant instructor, who had always treated me kindly, I felt even more disappointed and questioned him. The teacher then realized he had made a mistake during inspection due to being busy. However, he said it was difficult to correct it now and change the rankings. But fueled by youthful righteousness, how could I remain silent?<19> I protested to the principal.
However, the principal threatened, saying that correcting this now would necessitate dismissing the assistant instructor due to responsibility, and I would have no choice but to graduate the following year.
Thus, although I was extremely resentful, I couldn't cause the teacher to be dismissed over such a matter, nor did I want the hassle of staying another year, so I kept quiet and let it go.
Later, I found out that after this incident arose, the student who ranked first had lobbied extensively (四面 청도), and I had also offended a ministry official on some occasion. So, things were smoothed over one way or another. Thinking about it now, it's just an amusing story.

At that time, there were about 10 government elementary schools in Seoul and about 10 public elementary schools in each province, totaling only about 20+ schools in all of Chosŏn. After I graduated, all the schools were fully staffed with teachers, so I was unemployed for a while.
Could this also be called an employment crisis even back then? I graduated in January but was appointed in April. Positions opened up in two places in Hamgyŏngbuk-do: Sŏngjin (城津) and Kyŏngsŏng (鏡城). Transportation was extremely inconvenient back then. It took about 10 days to reach Wŏnsan by horse, then take a steamship (輪船) from Wŏnsan to Tokchin (獨津), and finally reach Kyŏngsŏng quickly. Traveling on foot took over 20 days. I hesitated to take the post because it was so far, but I decided to go out of curiosity, treating it like going abroad—even going to America now takes only about 20 days. Mr. Kim, who graduated first, went to Sŏngjin, and I went to Kyŏngsŏng to teach at the public elementary school. It truly felt like being in a foreign land.
Later, the Russo-Japanese War broke out, and Kyŏngsŏng was occupied by Russian troops. I even lived in the same house with Russians for a time. Transportation was cut off; I couldn't return to Seoul because sea routes were closed, and traveling by land was difficult. I continued teaching for about 3 years on a meager salary. When the war ended with Japan's victory, the political situation changed dramatically overnight, and a new treaty was concluded. Afterwards, the school became a public common school, and I was reappointed as principal, serving for about a year. While serving as principal, I followed the new trend and cut my hair (단발). At that time, besides members of the Ilchinhoe (일진회), nobody had short hair, so many people pointed fingers and expressed concern.
After about a year as principal, I was invited to teach at the private Hamil School (咸一學校) and Posŏng School (普成學校) (secondary level schools) in Kyŏngsŏng. I taught there for about two years in total. Looking at the ages of the students then, the oldest was 57, with others aged 50, 40, 30, and the youngest being 20.
The students I taught were generally older than me, the teacher.
Now I teach subjects belonging to the humanities, but back then, I taught many subjects belonging to the sciences.
Subsequently, I served as dean of studies at the YMCA Middle School, dean of studies and teacher at Hamhŭng Yŏngsaeng School (咸興 永生學校), taught at Kaesŏng Songdo High Common School (開城 松都高普) and Holston Girls' School (好壽敦女學校), then came to Ewha Girls' High School (梨花女高), temporarily taught at Ewha Women's College (梨花女專) and Ewha Normal School (梨花師範). Currently, due to time constraints, I am only teaching at Ewha Girls' High Common School (梨花女子高等普通學校).
Throughout my career as an educator to this day, I have continually honed new knowledge by attending workshops held at Tokyo Imperial University and lectures by other authoritative scholars during my spare time.

Finally, although there are many "episodes" from my life in education and my experiences in society up to today, due to time and space constraints, I will defer them to a later date. (End)<20>

Government Foreign Language School and Principal Yi Sangjae

Taught 6 languages; the Minister of Education gave an encouraging speech at the graduation ceremony.

Yun T'aehŏn, Lecturer at Chungdong School

My beloved alma mater and respected teachers! It is a great pleasure to recall the alma mater and teachers who left such strong memories for the people of this land decades ago and then disappeared.
My alma mater is the Foreign Language School (外國語學校). This Foreign Language School was established under the grand spirit of absorbing the cultures of various advanced Western nations and widely promoting Korean culture to the world, aiming for great national opening and progress in our newly awakening society back then. The very first established was the Government English School (官立 英語學校). Thinking back, it was before the Kabo Reforms (甲午). Through the hands of pioneers in education, Yugyŏng Kongwŏn (育英公院) was established in Hanyang. This Yugyŏng Kongwŏn transformed into the Government English School.
This Government English School merged with various other language schools (어학교) to establish the Foreign Language School.
The Foreign Language School was divided into 6 schools:
Chinese Language (漢語) (i.e., Chinese)
English Language (英語)
Russian Language (俄語) (i.e., Russian)
French Language (法語) (i.e., French)
German Language (德語) (i.e., German)
Japanese Language (日語)
These were the schools.
Among the six schools, I studied at the English School. So, I was a first-batch student.
At that time, the Foreign Language School was located in Susong-dong (壽松洞) (in Chŏn-dong (磚洞)). The main gate of Chungdong School (中東學校), where I currently teach, was exactly the main gate of that school. The school building was where the current Normal School Affiliated Elementary School is located. Then, the following year, it moved to the back alley of Kongjo (工曹) (site of the old Sayŏgwŏn (司譯院)).
The principal of this school at that time seems to be Mr. Yi Sangjae (李商在), who passed away years ago. As for the renowned instructors back then, perhaps it's the fleeting nature of time, but Mr. Hong Kimu (洪起模), who has already passed into the underworld, was there. Among those still living today are Mr. Kwŏn Yusŏp (權柔燮) and Mr. Kim P'irhŭi (金弼熙). Also, quite remarkably,<21> there was a foreign teacher at that time; she was a British lady called Madam Hutchison (매담 허치신, 許致信 여사).
That was during the Yunghŭi (隆熙) and Kwangmu (光武) eras. At this time, the fervor for foreign worship was tremendous in Korean society, and the number of young students applying to the Foreign Language School increased daily. Nowadays, even after graduating from school, finding a job is difficult, but back then, it was very easy to get positions in the Foreign Ministry (外部), various foreign consulates, or as school teachers. Job placements were often decided even while students were still enrolled.

80 First Batch Students
There were 80 students in the first batch of our school. The course duration was 5 years, but most students probably only reached the 3rd grade before leaving to take up government posts as Customs Assistant Officials (稅關帮判).
I completed the entire prescribed 5-year course and became an instructor at my alma mater, taking up the teaching baton again.
There were many classmates at that time, and with the passage of time and changing circumstances, I cannot recall all of them now, but I believe most have passed away.
Among the survivors I remember is Mr. Yun Kiik (尹基益). After finishing the Foreign Language School, he packed his bags again and went to London, England, where he specialized in mining engineering (鑛山學) for several years. After returning, he is now working day and night as an engineer at a gold mine in Suan (遂安), P'yŏnganbuk-do. Also, Mr. Sang Ho (尙灝) went to Japan, studied naval architecture (造船學) at the Faculty of Engineering, Tokyo Imperial University, and after returning, served as the Director of the Commerce and Industry Bureau for several years, then held positions as county magistrate (군수) and provincial councilor (참여관). I hear he is now a member of the Central Council (중추원 참의). I recall that the other surviving members have all been considerably active in society.

Entrance and Graduation Ceremonies
The entrance ceremonies back then were not as grand as today's, but they were significantly more meaningful. The Minister of Education (學部大臣) attended every entrance ceremony and gave an encouraging address (훈시) to the young students.
The students of that time were all lively and energetic. During my student life, I performed military-style drills (兵式 체조) before the Emperor twice. After each drill performance, the Emperor was greatly pleased and bestowed a feast (賜饗).
There were no graduation ceremonies for the first 4-5 years after the school opened, but afterwards, they were held every spring. The Minister of Education and the Director of Academic Affairs (學務局長) would come to the school and give addresses.
I taught as an instructor at my alma mater for 11 years, then resigned around the time of the Kyŏngsul year (庚戌年) [1910]. Since then, I have been moving around as a lecturer at various private schools up to the present day.
The site of the old Foreign Language School still remains in Chŏn-dong—and even now, when I sit alone on a deep night with silently falling snow and think back 20 years, the faces of my respected teachers and classmates from that time seem to appear, smiling. Ah, was it all just a fragment of a dream that flowed away with history! (End)

The Chinese Language School within the Foreign Language School

Yu Chŏngnyŏl

The school I attended was the Chinese Language School (漢語學校) within the Foreign Language School. The school was established perhaps in the Chŏngmyo year (丁卯年), and I entered in the Musul year (戊戌年).<22> There were only third-year students above me, so I probably graduated in the third batch.
At that time, the Chinese Language School was located at the site of the former Chŏn'ŭigam (典醫監跡) (Chŏn'ŭigam was a kind of medical training institution), which is the current location of the Tonghaeru (東海樓) Chinese restaurant in Kyŏnji-dong (堅志洞).
When I entered, the principal was Mr. Chŏng Kyo (鄭喬), and the instructors were Messrs. Yi On (李溫), Pang Taeyŏng (方大榮), Kim Namsik (金南植), and others. The Chinese instructor was a teacher named Mr. Hu Wénwěi (胡文煒).
The academic term was 5 years. Among my classmates from that time who are still engaged in education today is Mr. Cho Tongsik (趙東植), the current principal of Tongdŏk Girls' High Common School (同德女子高等普通學校), who was in the same cohort. There is also Mr. O Kyusin (吳圭信), who is currently a Chinese language instructor at Yonhŭi College (延禧專門學校). Kim Pyŏngnyŏl (金炳烈), who experienced many hardships abroad and passed away in Sinŭiju prison, was also a classmate back then.
At the time of admission, there were about 80 students, but many scattered into government service midway, or dispersed domestically and overseas, so my memory is hazy now.
The graduation ceremony was quite grand. The guests were not figures from social circles like today, but ministers and consuls from various countries attended. Primarily, instructors from the schools within the Foreign Language School network, principals from other schools, and an imperial attendant (궁내 부시종) came as an imperial envoy (칙사) to give an address (훈화). The Minister of Education also attended and gave a meaningful address. Current students and graduates filled the venue, making it truly solemn and grand. After graduation, because my classmates had studied Chinese, and due to the rapidly changing political situation at the time, many crossed the Yalu River, dragging their lonely shadows of wandering (漂浪), and continued lives of misfortune. As talent was scarce back then, many scattered into government positions.
During our time as students, the turmoil at the end of the Korean Empire was intense. The hopes and aspirations of young students were great, and the path ahead of us was wide yet burdensome, truly filling us with spirit and emotion. Whether facing the teaching masters, interacting with fellow students studying together, or when I myself took up the teaching pointer and stood on the podium, everything was imbued with the same feeling.
After finishing at my alma mater, the Chinese Language School, I served as an honorary instructor (名譽敎官) (an honorary instructor was someone who, after finishing school, taught without pay if there were no instructor positions available, and would be formally appointed when a position opened) for about a year, teaching voluntarily. Later, I was formally appointed as an instructor and continued this teaching life for about 9 years and 6 months. I poured all my energy into nurturing talent and educating young students, considering it my life's duty. Afterwards, as the general situation changed and the environment shifted too drastically from what I had envisioned, I too became one of the overseas wanderers (海外漂浪客), heading north, north across the river, enduring a rough vagrant life in foreign lands for over 10 years.
—After returning to Korea, I took up teaching again. To this day, I teach Korean and Hanmun at Chungdong School, and at night, I conduct Chinese language classes within the Chosŏn Education Association (朝鮮敎育協會).
My old alma mater! It truly belongs to the distant past. (End)<23>

**Summary**

This collection of memoirs from the January 1, 1932 issue of *Samch'ŏlli* magazine features alumni reflecting on their experiences at prominent educational institutions during the late Korean Empire and early Japanese colonial period: the Army Military Academy, P'yŏngyang Taesŏng School, Hansŏng Normal School, and the Government Foreign Language School (specifically the English and Chinese departments). The authors (O Sanggŭn, Kim Hyŏngsik, Kim Ch'angje, Yun T'aehŏn, Yu Chŏngnyŏl) recount details about founding dates, locations, curriculum (often blending traditional Korean/Chinese studies with Western/Japanese models), instructors (including foreign teachers and notable Korean figures like An Ch'angho and Yi Sangjae), student life (ranging from patriotic fervor and military drills to careerism and political activism), and the eventual fates of these schools and their graduates amidst the turbulent political changes, including Japanese annexation and suppression.

These recollections offer valuable historical insights into the transition of Korean education from traditional systems to modern institutions under intense political pressure. Historians might find particular interest in the firsthand accounts of nationalist sentiment cultivation (especially at Taesŏng School under An Ch'angho), the emphasis on military training even in civilian schools, the challenges of integrating 'new learning' (science, foreign languages) with traditional scholarship, the direct impact of Japanese colonial policies on schools (e.g., curriculum changes, surveillance, the 105-Person Incident), and the subsequent diverse paths of graduates, many of whom became educators, officials, activists, or exiles, reflecting the complex social landscape of colonial Korea.

나의 母校와 恩師
陸軍武官學校

前陸軍 正尉 吳祥根

내가 舊韓國時代에 잇섯든 저 陸軍武官學校에 입학하기는 바로 그 무관학교가 설립되든 光武 10년인 바 제1회의 학생으로 드러갓는데 그때의 내 나희 18세이엿다. 학교의 위치로 말하면 興化門 압(現 京城中學校와 엽 관사 자리)이엿스며 교련장도 바로 그 곳 지금의 경성중학 운동장과 그 부근에 잇든 널분 벌판이 전부 당시 것이엿다.
교관으로 말하면 교장 李學均, 교두 李秉武, 교관 李熙斗,趙性根(현 중장),申羽均,權學均(死), 李大偉(死), 王瑜植 등 諸氏이엿는데 氏등의 이력을 잠간 말하자면 교장 李學均 씨는 甲午 이전에 育英公院이란 학교에서 수업하엿스며 늘 독학으로 兵學에 長한 이엿고 또 영어에 조예가 깁헛스며 교두 李秉武 씨는 日本士官學校 출신이엿고 기타 교관들도 전부 日本士官學校 출신이엿섯다.
그 외 병술과 교관으로는 全佑基, 李昌根, 李守鳳 등 諸氏가 잇섯스며 기 외에 조교수 등도 잇섯다.
당시 제1회로 입학하여 동문수업하든 학우 중에는 지금에 사망한 분도 만코 생존한 분으로도 멀니 해외 등지에 흐터진 분도 만흐나 나는 그들에 대한 기술을 피코저 한다.
그 때의 학비로 말하면 전부가 관비이엿섯스며 교과는 주로 日本, 獨逸, 英國의 것을 참조하야 썻스니 학교에 전문 번역관이 잇서서 외국병학을 번역하고 재래병학을 참작하야 그것을 인쇄로 하야 교재로 썻썻는데 그 박게 강의를 필기로 하여 쓴 일도 잇다.
교련으로 말하면 한국식의 독특한 교련법이 생기여 이것을 썻다.
또한 내가 졸업한 후의 일을 회상하건대 졸업 직후에 친위 1대에 견습으로 잇다가 친위 2대 소대장으로 보직되엿고 그 후에 헌병대로 전직되엿섯고<12> 그 후 練成學校 교관으로도 잇섯다가, 丙午年 봄에 교관의 職을 사퇴하엿스니 당시의 군직은 正尉이엿다.
사표를 드린 후 平安道 모 학교의 교사로 부임하엿다가 다시 상경하야 尙洞청년학원이란 학교에 교사로도 잇섯고, 그 후 해외생활을 한 3,4년 한 후에 귀국하야 儆新學校에 職을 두엇섯스며 그 후에는 開城 漢英學院, 定州 五山學校, 全州 新興學校 등에서 전전 교원생활을 하엿다. 全州서 교원생활을 마친 후가 바로 己未年이엿난대 잠시 부자유한 생활을 치른 후, 동지와 함께 민간신문 발간을 꾀하여 보앗스나 일이 엇전지 여의치 못하게 되든 중 뒤밋처 東亞日報가 탄생하엿다. 그 후 朝鮮에 신사상의 조류가 흐르기 시작하자 노동공제회와 청년연합회를 조직하는데 관계를 가젓든 일도 잇섯스며, 기후에는 실업계에 종사를 하고 잇섯다.

당시 육군무관학교 개교 전후의 상황을 약간 기술코저 하면 제1회 육군무관학교 교장 李學均 씨(上海에서 객사하엿다)가 앗가 말한 바와 갓치 영어에 통하엿스며 신분은 군인으로서 대궐 출입을 자조 하엿고, 항상 閔忠正公과 정을 통하고 事를 謀하는 처지이엿난대, 이 두 분이 韓國의 유신과 국방에 관한 의견을 종종 의논하든 결과, 정규적 군인양성의 필요를 늣기고, 위선 52연대(3대대 1연대)를 두자는 의견에 일치되여, 사관양성을 급히 하자 하엿스니, 이것이 곳 육군무관학교가 창립케 된 동기의 일편이엿다.

平壤 大成學校와 安昌浩

朝鮮日報 金瀅植

朝鮮에 유일한 정당 獨立協會가 업서진 뒤 1906,7년 경 朝鮮의 뜻잇는 이들은 도처에서 「실력수양」 등을 절규하야 朝鮮천지에는 청년유지자가 배출하고 방방곡곡에 사립학교가 簇生하얏다. 그 중에도 西朝鮮지방은 다른 지방보다도 교육열이 열화와 갓치 熾盛하얏섯는데 그 원인은 그 지방이 역사적으로 가진 이유도 잇겟지만은 당시의 환경으로 그 지방에는 선도자가 특히 다른 지방보다도 만헛든 까닭이다.

民衆의 指導者를 養成코저
그 선도자 중에도 島山 安昌浩씨와 갓튼 이는 실로 불멸의 감동을 西鮮민중에게 준 위대한 인격자로 그의 運身은 『민족을 愛한다』 혈과 성의 결정이엇다. 그가 한 번 辯說을 토하게 되면 만인은 모다 그에 감동되어 눈물을 흘니며 흥분하얏섯고 부녀는 끼엇든 指環과 찻든 패물을 던지어 氏의 사업을 원조하얏섯다.<14>
이 혈과 성의 人 安昌浩 씨는 방방곡곡에 簇生한 사립학교의 졸업생을 수용하는 동시에 자기의 이상과 계획을 실행할 민중의 지도자―유지인사를 양성키 위하야 西鮮의 중심지 平壤薛岩里에 干先 중등교육기관을 설립하고 그 일홈을 大成學校라 하얏다. 大成學校는 실로 島山 安昌浩 씨의 성과 혈의 결정이엇스며 당시 민중 사상의 축도이엇다.

殺到하는 入學志願者
1907년 大成學校 설립의 報가 세상에 전파되자 이를 성원 지지하는 소래는 全朝鮮을 흔들엇스며 입학지원자는 조수와 갓치 밀니어 불시에 5,6백명의 청년이 모히엇다.
(당시에 만흔 학생들은 입학시험을 행한 결과가 거의 전부가 예비반으로 편입되고 1학년은 50명에 불과하얏스며 교장으로는 尹致昊씨를 추대하얏고 安島山은 스사로 代辨 교장이 되엇스며 따로이 사범과와 농과를 부설하얏섯다)
중학교 학생이라 하지만은 이 당시 大成學校의 생도들은 20세, 30세의 청년유지들로 입을 널니면 모다 憂X慨世의 탄식이엇고 행동은 모다 민중의 지도자를 자부하얏섯다.

兵式軆操로 訓練식혀
그 당시 학교의 과정은 중등학교라 하지만은 지금의 중등학교보다는 훨신 고등이어서 4학년 과정은 엇던 전문학교의 3학년 정도와 대등하얏스며 또 수학은 월등하게 고등하얏고 학교의 설비도 중등학교로서는 유례가 업스리만콤 완비하얏섯다. 그 뿐안이라 이 학교는 前記와 갓치 『愛X精神』을 고취하는 것을 목적으로한 학교이엇슴으로 매일 아츰 엄숙한 조회를 하되 XX歌를 고창한 후 『愛X』에 관하 훈화가 잇서 학생은 모다 이를 성화로 服應하얏다. 그리고 체조시간을 제일 존중하되 당시 체조교사로는 원래 군대의 사관으로 뜻놉든 鐵血의 人 鄭仁穆씨(지금은 고인)이엇든 바 전혀 군대식으로 학생을 교련하얏다.

漢城師範學校와 恩師

梨花女子高普敎諭 金昶濟

乙未年에 설립된 官立 漢城師範學校는 舊韓國時代에 잇서서 최고학부엿스며 아울너 유일한 사범교육기관이엿슬 뿐 안니라 만흔 준재를 배출하엿든 것이다.
내가 한성사범학교의 교문을 두다린 것은 19세가 채 못되는 때엿고 한성사범학교가 설치된 지 5개년을 경과한 때이엇스니 위치로 말하면 現 校洞 공립보통학교 터이엿스며 그 당시에는 趙政丞邸를 임시 校舍로 썻섯든 것이다.
학제는 단급제이며 수업 년한은 4개년이엿고, 학기는 2학기―夏期, 冬期로 난호엿다.
입학자격으로 말하면 퍽 수준이 놉핫스니, 당시난 지금과 달너서 한문이 말할 것도 업시 전성하엿든 시대이라, 7書의 소양이 잇고, 純한문으로 능히 작문을 지을만한 학력이 잇서야 입학이 되엿든 것이엿다.
입학시험 때의 응모자는 120명나 되엿는데, 그 중에서 합격된 자가 겨우 18인에 불과하엿스니 이것은 교실과 또는 기숙사 관계로 수용범위가 좁앗든 것도 한가지 이유이겟스나 어느 의미로 보아서는 지금과 갓흔 시험지옥이 벌서부터 잇섯든 것이라고도 할 수 잇다.<17>
입학시험은 學部의 큰 대청에서 보앗는대 紙筆墨을 죽 난호아 주엇고 上試로는 학부대신, 副試로는 학무국장 그리고 일반 試官으로 각 교관이 열석하엿스며, 수험생은 최소년자 18,9세 이상으로 4,50세의 장년 또는 장년기를 지난 사람들노서 큰 갓을 쓰고 혹자는 수염까지 쓰다듬고 안젓스니, 지금과 비교하야 상상컨댄 일종 奇觀이라 할 것이다.
내가 치른 기억을 들추건대, 제1일은 독서시험이니 7書 가운대 어느 중에서나 읽혀보앗다. 나는 그때 大學 서문을 읽히웟다.
그리하야 제1일 독서시험에 한 절반이 떨니고, 제2일에는 純한문으로 작문을 지엇스니 이 결과 또한 절반 이상이나 떨니웟고, 제3일에는 합격자 발표가 잇섯다.
다행히 18인 합격자 중 제8번으로 합격이 되엇스니 연령의 차도 심하엿고 수험자 중에 私請이 격심하든 그때에 이 모든 난관을 헛치고서 비교적 양호한 성적으로 합격이 된 것을 생각함에 지금까지 유쾌하기 짝이 업다.
그리하야 입학생 18인 중 내 아래로는 한 두어 사람 잇섯고 모도 연장자, 20으로부터 30, 40, 40여세 되는 분들이엿다.
학교광경을 약술하면 교실과 기숙사가 잇섯고, 교실에는 책상과 걸상이며 교단에는 칠판이 잇고 선생이나 제자이나 갓을 쓰고 안저서 가르치고, 배호고 하엿스며, 학비로는 일체 官費로 되엿다.
학과는 지금과 갓치 구비치는 못하엿스나 한문은 7書, 7書 중에도 論語와 孟子를 치중하엿고, 일주일에 작문 3회(純한문 及 국한문 등) 수학으로는 산술로부터 대수, 기하와 물리, 화학, 지리, 역사 등도 잇섯스나, 그 중 제일 만흔 학과가 한문이엿다.
나는 이 모든 학과 중에 후일 이것을 주로 교수하엿스나 비교적 문과에 속하는 것보다 이과에 속하는 학과에 취미를 만히 가젓다.
교장은 당시 학무국장으로 잇든 金珏鉉 씨가 대리로 잇다가 후에 학부편집과장으로 잇든 李鍾泰씨가 정식으로 교장으로 임명되여 부임하엿고 교관으로는 창립시부터 鎭川 게신 有數한 漢學者 鄭雲慶 씨가 한문 及 純한문작문을 담임하고 후에 鄭씨 代로 皇城新聞의 주필노 유명하든 平安道 출신인 漢學者로 朴殷植이가 부임하엿스며, 金亨湜씨, 물리교관으로는 朴晶東씨가 담임하엿고 米國人으로 조선어에 조예가 깁고 조선문으로 여러 가지 저서를 낸 헐벗씨가 지리와 수학을 교수하엿다.

당시 학생들의 사조로 말하면 상세난 피하나 대개 2파로 난호엿스니 그 일파는 30이상된 사람, 科宦慾의 충동을 밧어 졸업 후 감투나 한아 엇더 쓸 욕심이 잇는 사람, 시골서 온 이는 관비로 공부를 하고 출세를 할녀는 사람, 학교는 일종 기숙사로 알고 大臣 집에나 출입하고 선생의 사택이나 자조 차저서 혹 조흔 기회가 잇스면 퇴학을 하고 마는 자, 학적을 둔 지 6개월 過 則 1개년을 단이다가, 電報學校(電報學校라 함은 현 체신관 양성소와 흡사하고 3개월만 단이면 電報主事라는 것을 엇는다)로 넘어가거나 무관학교로 넘어가는 학생이 만허서 각금 보결시험을 보게 된다.<18>
그리고 또 한편 졂은 학도들은 신지식 흡수에 열중하엿스니 새로운 과학에 1字를 배워도 『원더풀』하게 생각하고 2字를 배워도 그 고증과 탐구에 열중하엿스며, 당시 대수나 기하를 해득한 사람은 전국을 턴대야 10인에 미만인 형편이니 우리들 年少學徒는 과학의 무지를 통탄하고 그 보급을 연구하엿다.
그리하야 학생 중에도 나히 만흔 축은 新학설에 대하야 반대의견을 갓고, 가령 지구는 둥글다 하면 지구는 평평하고, 물에 성분이 수소와 산소로되엿다 하면 거짓말이라는 둥 심한 자는 조소적 태도를 가지엿스며 졂은 학도들은 신학문을 비상히 환영하야 연구에 연구를 거듭하는데, 심지어 교관들도 新학문을 이단시하야 암연히 멸시에 눈을 던젓다. 엇던 때 교관 金亨湜 씨가 나를 불너서 新學設에 대한 토론을 한 일이 잇섯는대 나는 강의하듯이 아는대로는 똑똑 예증을 들어 말하엿드니 金선생도 그럴든이 듯고 올킨 올타는 시인까지 밧엇스나 엇젯든지 지금에 비하면 격세의 감이란 말도 분수가 업든 때이다.
또한 일반 학생들의 사상적 사조로 보아 前記 부패하다고 할 만한 일파는 科宦慾에 열중하고 감투 쓸 야욕과 뜬 명예욕에 지배되엿스나 또 일파라=할 학생들은 韓末 풍운에 鑑하야 X國思想을 품고 당시의 신문 皇城, 帝國 양 신문들을 애독하엿스니 나는 그때 皇城新聞 애독자이엿다.
그리고 몃 사람은 밤이면 興和學校(閔忠正公이 설립한 학교로 現 京城保育學校 터)에 가서 영어와 측량학을 배우며 일주일에 한 번식 토요일이면 토론회 겸 연설회를 여러 책상을 치며 노후하고 高言壯談과 정치의 부패를 매도하고 정치변혁을 고취하야 엇던 때 토론회 중에서는 넘어 과격하여 이른바 정치개혁의 불온한 말을 하엿다는 이유로 몃 사람이 징역을 밧은 일도 잇섯다.
엇젯든지 내가 후일 연단에 나아가기 시작한 初무대가 이 興和學校 토론회 時부터이다.
사제간의 정의로 말하면 별노히 회합이라든가 또는 간담의 형식은 업섯고 禮하는 법은 의연히 서당식으로 선생을 사부로써 섬기는 예의이엿다.

그 때 동창으로는 지금 더러 사망하엿고 또 修學 중도에 官界로 헛터지고, 오늘까지 혹 만나는 사람으로는 東亞日報社 판매부에 게신 金敎熙 씨를 기억할 뿐이다.

졸업식―나는 한성사범학교에서 제6회에 해당한 졸업생으로 학부대신, 학부협판, 학무국장 기타 학부관계자들과 교관들 열석 하에 졸업을 밧엇스니 식은 학부대신의 졸업장 수여가 잇섯고 나종에 교자 한 틀 먹을 것을 밧엇섯다.
나는 2호로 졸업을 하엿스니 여기에 한 揷話가 잇다. 이것을 지금에 이약이 한들 한낫 무용한 일이나 당시 나희 졂은 나는 퍽 분개하엿스매 한낫 우수운 이약이로 써 보기로 한다. 이것은 다름이 안이라, 입학시 18인이든 학생이 중도에 헛터지고 졸업시에는 다만 7인 밧게 업섯는대 나는 평시에 늘 수석으로 나려왓다가 졸업시만 2위로 되엿다. 과목은 지리로 수석한 사람과 한 10점이 틀엿스니 나는 아무리 생각하여도 지리에 만점을 밧어야 올토록 정확히 해답을 하엿난대 알 수 업는 일이다. 그리하야 의아하고 억울한 생각만 가지고 잇든 중 졸업시 황망한 때이라 서류정리를 부교관이 나와 또한 몃 학생에게 의뢰하야 가서 서류를 정리타가 우연히 내 답안서를 보앗다. 그리하야 다시 보니 역시 정확하고 점수로 따지자면 오히려 수석한 사람보다 실수 9점이 내가 더하니 나는 특히 부교관과 그닥지 감정의 상위가 업고 늘 나를 사랑하든 선생이라 일층 섭섭히 생각하고, 질문을 하엿다. 그런즉 선생도 그 時에 검사를 해보고 잘못된 것을 알엇스니 선생이 밧분 중에 잘못 본 것이다. 그러나 지금 와서 이것을 정정키 곤란하니 席位를 다시 밧구기 어렵다 한다. 그러나 졂은 의협심에, 엇지 가만히<19> 잇슬 것이냐. 교장에게 항의를 하엿다.
그러나 교장의 말이 지금 이것을 정정하자면 책임상 부교관을 면직식히고, 군은 명년에나 다시 졸업을 할 수 밧게 업다고 위협 비슷하게 한다.
그리하야 분하기는 한 업섯스나 선생을 그만 일노 면직케 할 수 업고, 또 나도 한 해를 묵게 되니 귀찬어서 그만 입을 담을고 말엇다.
그러나 후에 알고 보니 이 일이 나자 수석을 한 사람이 四面 청도 하엿고 또는 내가 엇던 기회에 학부관리를 거슬닌 일도 잇섯든 터임으로 이럭저럭 넘기고 말엇든 것이니 지금 생각하면 다만 웃스운 일일 뿐이다.

당시 서울에 관립 소학교가 10여개소이엿고 지방 각 도에 공립 소학교가 10여개소 합 20여개소 밧게 全鮮에 업섯스니 내가 졸업한 후에는 학교마다 선생이 다 차서 얼마간 놀게 되엿다.
이것은 또한 그 때만 하여도 취직난이라 할넌지? 졸업은 1월에 하엿는대 4월에 임명이 되엿다. 咸北에 城津과 鏡城 두 군데에 자리가 낫스니 그 때만 하여도 교통이 심히 불편하야 元山까지 말을 타고 元山서 輪船으로 獨津까지 그리고 鏡城까지 빨니 가야 한 10여일이 걸녓고 도보로는 20여일이나 걸녓다. 넘어 멀어서 부임키를 주저하엿스나 외국 가는 셈 치고―지금 米國을 가재도 약 20여일 밧게 안 걸니는데―호기심으로 가기로 하엿스니 首位 졸업한 金모씨는 城津으로 갓고 나는 鏡城으로 가서 공립 소학교에서 교편을 드니 참으로 異域에 온 감이 낫다.
그 후 日露戰爭이 개장되여 鏡城은 露軍이 점령하엿다. 그리하야 露人과 갓치 한집에서 잇서 본 일도 잇스니 교통이 두절되여 상경을 할녀야 海路에 배가 끈허젓고 육로로 오기 곤란하여 박봉을 밧고 한 3년간 교원생활을 하엿다. 전쟁이 끗나 日本의 승리로 돌아감애 시국은 급전직하로 변환되여 신조약이 체결되엿다. 그 뒤 공립 보통학교로 되여 다시 교장으로 임명되엿스니 약 1년 동안 교장으로 잇섯다. 교장으로 잇슬 때 新思潮을 딸어 단발을 하엿는대 그 때는 일진회 사람 외에는 단발한 사람이 업는 때라 여러 사람이 손꼬락질도 하엿고 근심도 하여주엇다.
교장으로 약 1년 잇다가 鏡城 사립 咸一學校와 普成學校(중등 정도 학교)에 초빙을 밧어 전후 2년 간이나 교원생활을 하엿스니 당시의 학생들의 년령을 보면 최고 57세, 50세, 40, 30, 최연소자가 20세이엿다.
가릇키는 선생인 나보다 배우는 학생들 나희가 대개 더 만헛섯다.
지금은 내가 문과에 속하는 것을 가릇키나, 그 때는 이과에 속하는 과목을 만히 가릇켯다.
그 후 기독청년회 중학과 학감, 咸興 永生學校의 학감 겸 교사로 開城 松都高普에 好壽敦女學校에로서 梨花女高에 와서 一時난 梨花女專, 梨花師範 등에 교편을 들엇고 지금은 시간관계로 梨花女子高等普通學校에만 교사로 잇다.
오늘까지 나려오는 교육자 생활 중에서도 틈틈이 東京帝大에서 열니는 강습회나 기타 권위 잇는 학자의 강연회 등―에서 새로운 지식을 늘 연마하엿다.

제1회 入學生 80명
우리 학교의 제1회 입학생은 80명이엇슴니다. 수업 년한은 5개년이엿스나 결국 3학년까지 진급하엿슬가 말가 하여서 대개는 정부의 稅關帮判이란 벼슬을 하여 나가버리엇슴니다.
나는 5개년의 소정 학과를 모다 마치고 모교의 교관으로 잇게 되어 다시 교편을 잡엇든 것임니다.
그 때 우리의 동창생은 만허서 物換星移한 지금에 모다 기억하여 내일 수는 업스나 아마 대개는 작고한 줄로 암니다.
생존한 이 중으로 기억에 남은 이는 尹基益씨가 外國語學校를 마춘 뒤 다시 笈을 負하고 英國 倫敦에 赴하야 그 곳에서 여러 해동안 鑛山學을 전공하고 도라온 뒤 지금은 平北 遂安 잇는 금광에서 기사로 日夜 애쓰고 게시며 또 尙灝 씨는 日本에 드러가 東京帝大 공과대학에서 造船學을 연구하고 도라온 뒤 정부의 상공국장으로 여러해 잇다가 군수와 참여관까지 역임하더니 지금은 중추원 참의로 잇다 함니다. 其外 생존하여 잇는 분으로는 모다 사회적으로 상당한 활약들을 한 줄로 기억함니다.

入學式과 卒業式
그때 입학식으로 말하면 오늘날과 가치 그러케 성대하지는 못하엿지만은 그래도 의의 잇섯든 점에는 수배 월등하엿든 것임니다. 입학식마다 학부대신이 행차하여 청년학도를 격려하는 훈시를 주엇든 것임니다.
그 당시의 학생은 모다 활발하고 기운이 잇섯슴니다. 내가 학생 생활하든 가운데도 두 번이나 어전에 나가서 兵式 체조를 하엿지요. 체조 끗마다 우에서는 크게 깁버하시어 賜饗이 잇섯슴니다.

내가 수학한 학교난 外國語學校 중 한어학교이엿는데 그 학교의 설립이 丁卯年인가 엿스며 내가 입학하기는 戊戌年이엿고<22> 상급으로 3학년생 밧게 업섯스니 아마 졸업한 것이 제3회일 것이다.
당시 한어학교난 典醫監跡(典醫監이라는 것은 일종 의사양성기관이다) 現 堅志洞 중국요리점 東海樓 터이엿다.

입학 당시에는 한 80여명이나 되엿섯스나 중도에 官界로 헛터지고 海內海外로 헛터지여 지금 기억이 흐미하다.
졸업식으로 말하면 퍽 성대하엿스니 내빈이라고는 지금과 가치 사회방면 인사들이 안이고 각국의 공사, 영사들이 來參하얏스며 주로 外國語學校 권내에 속하는 각 학교의 교관 등과 기타 각 학교의 교장들과 궁내 부시종이 칙사로 와서 훈화를 하고 학부대신이 참석하야 의미 깁흔 훈화를 하엿스며 재학생 及 졸업생이 가득히 모엿스니 실노 엄숙하엿고 성대하엿든 것이나 졸업 후에 동창생들은 배호기를 중국어를 배혼 까닭에 당시의 급격한 시국도 시국이여서 대개가 압록강을 건너 漂浪의 외로운 거림자를 껄고 불우의 생활을 계속한 분이 만코 당시에 인재가 귀하든 때이라 官界에 흐터짐이 만헛다.
당시 재학시대난 韓末의 풍운이 심한 때이라 청년학도의 희망과 포부는 커섯고 우리들의 압길은 넓고도 짐이 무거워서 실노 의기와 감격이 커섯스니 교수하는 선생을 대할 때나, 동문수학하는 동창들과의 교분이나, 또한 내가 교편을 들고 강단에 올은 때가 모도 한 기분으로 차섯다.
