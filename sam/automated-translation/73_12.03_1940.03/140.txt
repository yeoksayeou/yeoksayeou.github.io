Chungang Buddhist College Professor Kwŏn Sangno

Yes, I have decided not to engage in "creating a family name and changing my given name" (創氏改名). Looking at the clauses of the promulgated ordinance, it only states that the *ssi* (氏) system will be implemented from now on; it does not necessarily require the creation of a new *ssi*. While creating a *ssi* according to one's own intention is fine, the deadline for creating it is six months from January 1st, Showa 15 (1940). Therefore, for those who do not report the creation of a *ssi* within that period, their existing surname (*sŏng*, 성) will legally become their *ssi*. Therefore, I do not intend to report the creation of a *ssi*, but rather let the natural legal effect occur, continuing as Kwŏn Sangno with my surname and given name (성명) until the end of June, Showa 15, and becoming Kwŏn Sangno with my *ssi* and name (씨명) from July 1st.

Salvation Army Lieutenant Colonel Hwang Chongnyul

**Summary**

This 1940 article from the magazine *Samch'ŏlli* lists numerous prominent Korean social figures, primarily officials within the Japanese colonial administration (including provincial councilors, judges, police inspectors, county magistrates, and Central Council advisors), regarding their stance on the mandatory Sōshi-kaimei policy (changing Korean names to Japanese-style names). For most individuals listed, their detailed response (presumably in Japanese, justifying compliance) is noted as omitted. However, the article includes the Korean statements of three individuals: Professor Kwŏn Sangno explains his decision *not* to create a new Japanese-style family name (*ssi*), relying on the legal provision allowing his existing Korean surname (*sŏng*) to automatically become his *ssi* after the deadline. Dr. O Kŭngsŏn states he will keep his surname as 'O' (吳) since it also exists in Japan. Police Inspector Yi Myŏngch'im indicates he is still consulting with his clan. This article is historically significant as it documents the varied responses among the Korean elite to a deeply resented assimilation policy. While implying widespread compliance (given the omitted Japanese texts), it also captures instances of passive resistance (Kwŏn Sangno, O Kŭngsŏn) and the perceived gravity of the decision within traditional family structures (Yi Myŏngch'im), offering valuable insight for historians studying collaboration, resistance, and social dynamics under Japanese rule.
