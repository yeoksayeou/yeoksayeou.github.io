**Summary**

This 1940 article from 'Samch'ŏlli' magazine presents survey responses from numerous prominent Korean figures ("Changan Gentry") living in Seoul (referred to as Changan). The survey covers personal details like age, hometown, education, marriage type (arranged vs. love), wedding details, family mottos, and future wishes (wills/epitaphs). Critically, it also asks about contributions to the Japanese war effort (the "current incident," i.e., the Second Sino-Japanese War) through patriotic bonds, defense donations, and sending volunteer soldiers, as well as life insurance details. The responses offer a glimpse into the lives, values, and varying degrees of cooperation or reticence among the Korean elite under late colonial rule. For historians, this survey is highly valuable as it captures direct, albeit potentially curated, statements from key individuals on sensitive topics like marriage customs, family values, and, most significantly, their participation in or attitudes towards Japan's wartime mobilization policies. The varied responses—ranging from specific financial contributions and endorsements of military service (like Hyŏn Yŏng-sŏp's starkly pro-Japanese stance) to vague answers, omissions, or claims of poverty—reflect the complex pressures and positions within colonial Korean society during the war period.
