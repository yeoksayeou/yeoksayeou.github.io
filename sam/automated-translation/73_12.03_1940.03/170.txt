Article Title: 總督府記者 座談會 (Government-General Reporters' Roundtable Discussion)

Government-General Reporters' Roundtable Discussion
Attendees: Tōa Nippō...Yang Chae-ha; Chōsen Nippō...Hong Chong-in; Mainichi Shinpō...Kim In-i; Chōsen Shimbun...Ch'oe Chu-ha
December 15th at Keijō Kinchōdai Hall

**Scene of the Royal Family Visiting the Government-General**

Reporter: Did you see the scene when His Majesty King Yi made an official visit to the Governor-General at the Government-General building?
Yang (Tōa): The scene when members of the royal family visit is just as reported in the newspapers. More memorable than that, perhaps it was last summer, when Their Majesties the King and Queen Yi went to pay respects at Hwasan Rŭng in Suwŏn. I had the opportunity to accompany them as a special correspondent for the Tōa Nippō and saw their honored faces up close. Forgive my presumption, but he was truly benevolent and possessed dignity, and Her Majesty the Queen always smiled, like a spring breeze blowing. By the way, it was said to be King Yi's first visit to Hwasan Rŭng in Suwŏn in 30 years, although Emperor Sunjong had made a royal procession there before... Perhaps because of that, from Seoul to Suwŏn town, and along the thirty-li route from Suwŏn town to the Royal Tomb, tens of thousands of people gathered from various towns in Kyŏnggi Province, including Inch'ŏn, Yŏju, and Yongin, bowing along both sides of the road to see the royal couple. Seeing such a multitude dressed in white was probably the first time since the Insan (royal funeral procession). Along the route they traveled, people sprinkled yellow earth and even sprinkled water over it to prevent dust from rising, cleaning it so not a speck of dust arose. The sight of the populace, neatly dressed in fresh clothes, respectfully bowing with hands raised in welcome and farewell, truly made me bow my head after more than ten years as a newspaper reporter.

Reporter: I heard Their Majesties are very happily married.<209>
Yang (Tōa): They often smiled at each other, and when climbing places like the Royal Mausoleum, when it seemed difficult for the Queen, wearing high-heeled shoes, to ascend the high stone steps, he would raise his hand each time to signal for her to stay put. And whenever he went up first himself, he would perform *hoeseok* (目禮 - bow with the eyes), and also when coming down after finishing the bows, he would raise his arm to greet the Queen... They are very meticulous about etiquette.

Hong (Chōsen): It was like that when they went to Suwŏn, but also some years ago when Their Majesties visited Pulguk Temple in Kyŏngju. I saw them then; King Yi had the Queen stand beside a flowering tree next to the Tabo Pagoda and personally took photos with a camera. He would ask her to smile more or lean her body more this way, and each time the Queen would smile and readily comply. The two seemed truly affectionate, whether riding in a car or walking...

Kim (Mainichi): Both Their Majesties enjoy cameras and are quite skilled with them. I heard that in Tōkyō, they sometimes go incognito to places like Mitsukoshi and participate in camera competitions with other royalty and nobility. Some years ago, after returning from a tour of Europe, they held a garden party at the Secret Garden of Ch'angdŏk Palace in Seoul. Amidst the gathered dignitaries of the capital, I saw His Majesty holding a camera. Director Chōda of the Yi Royal Household Office once mentioned that there are many photos His Majesty personally took while touring European Great War battlefields like Verdun during his Western travels.

Ch'oe (Chōsen): On a different note, recently when His Majesty returned to his Tōkyō residence after going to the front, a photo appeared in Tōkyō newspapers showing the Queen, leading the nine-year-old Crown Prince, respectfully bowing to welcome her husband His Majesty right at the entrance hall. It was truly heartwarming.

Reporter: I also had the chance to see His Majesty King Yi up close. Once, I saw him in military attire on the platform at Namdaemun Station, and another time, on the day of Emperor Sunjong's Insan, I saw him emerging from Ch'angdŏk Palace in mourning clothes, riding in a carriage. He seemed both benevolent and dignified. What was the procession like when they went to pay respects at the tomb in Suwŏn earlier?
Yang (Tōa): There was a dark blue banner with yellow and blue flags, and the Queen rode in a four-bearer palanquin.<210>

**Stories of Foreign Envoys**

Reporter: What happens when foreign envoys make official visits to Governor-General Minami at the Government-General?
Hong (Chōsen): The British and American Consuls General don't really make official visits. They might visit the Governor-General at the Government-General building for New Year's greetings or to pay respects on holidays like the Emperor's Birthday. More notably, some years ago, perhaps around the time the Wanpaoshan Incident occurred and public sentiment was uneasy – just before the Manchurian Incident – the Republic of China's Ambassador to Tōkyō, Chiang Tso-pin (蔣作賓), arrived in Seoul via Beijing and Fengtian. When he visited the Government-General, our group of reporters surrounded him for an interview. He was a tall man, over 6 *ch'ŏk* (feet), with a large build. He maintained a calm composure ("悠然と構へゐ"). When we asked, "Isn't the fault in the Wanpaoshan Incident on your Chinese side?", he remained silent, saying, "I will speak about that incident when I get to Tōkyō." So we asked, "Are you aware that last night unfortunate incidents occurred in P'yŏngyang and elsewhere in Korea, resulting in many Chinese casualties?" He merely replied that he had received telegraphic reports while on the Kyŏngŭi Line train, again remaining silent. Finally, when asked if, given the situation, he wouldn't return directly to Nanjing without taking up his post, he said, "No, I will definitely proceed to Tōkyō," showing no sign of alarm.

Kim (Mainichi): Diplomats shouldn't get flustered. They always have to show courage. Hahaha (Everyone laughs). I met Chiang Tso-pin too; he had a good physique and considerable nerve.

Yang (Tōa): Recently, the newly appointed Soviet ambassador stayed in Seoul for 2-3 days en route to Tōkyō. When he came out after meeting Governor-General Minami at the Government-General, we reporters surrounded him for an interview. Perhaps all diplomats are like this, but regarding the Nomonhan Incident, the Japan-Soviet fishery treaty issue, and various other questions, he completely evaded them. The interpreter was "Ivanov" (이와노푸) from the Keijō Consulate General, but his interpreting skills were only at about a 3rd or 4th-grade elementary school level, leading to completely tangential answers (東問西答)... Questions that were difficult to answer were all met with evasion... He gave the impression of being a "tough customer" (喰へない男).

Hong (Chōsen): I saw him that day too. When it came to points favorable to the Soviet side, their propaganda points, he spoke energetically and straightforwardly. But when faced with our questions, he acted as if he couldn't understand, repeatedly asking "Eh? Eh?", and the answers he did give were completely nonsensical... His nerve was considerable. Maybe all diplomats are like that.

Reporter: What about the Manchukuo Consul General, Mr. Fan Hanchieh (范生漢)?
Ch'oe (Chōsen): Having been in Korea for a long time, he seems well-versed in Korean affairs.

Kim (Mainichi): At the beginning of the China Incident, if unfavorable<211> rumors circulated about our country, he would inquire with the Nanjing government and then diligently call the newspaper companies, saying, "I received a telegram back stating no such thing happened, so please rest assured."

**Critique of Speeches by the Governor-General, Vice Governor-General, and Bureau Directors**

Reporter: Please give your assessment of the speeches by the recent Governor-Generals, Mr. Minami and Mr. Ugaki.
Kim (Mainichi): I don't know how well Itō Hirobumi, Terauchi, or Hasegawa spoke, but the current Governor-General gives good speeches. I've heard him several times; he speaks without a manuscript. When the broad-chested general speaks back and forth in a loud voice, the audience is simply captivated. Originally, the secretary prepares a draft, but he looks at the outline, remembers the key points, and speaks without looking at the script. Of course, at meetings like the Provincial Governors' Conference, where every word and phrase must be carefully considered, it's not really a speech but instruction or pronouncement (演述), so it's different—even without the title (カタカキ - *katagaki*, title/position) of Governor-General, he would be known as a good speaker. Whether at a General Mobilization League meeting or a gathering of many businessmen.

Ch'oe (Chōsen): Sometime ago, Army Commander Nakamura's speech was well-organized and systematic.

Hong (Chōsen): Mr. Ugaki's speeches are scholarly, while Mr. Minami's are perhaps more like those of a politician.

Reporter: How about the Vice Governor-General?
Kim (Mainichi): Neither the current Vice Governor-General Ōno nor the former Vice Governor-General Kodama is particularly outstanding; they just fulfill their duties.

Ch'oe (Chōsen): Former Vice Governor-General Shimooka was said to be a standout among past Vice Governors-General and also gave good speeches.

Reporter: Among the current bureau directors, who stands out?
Kim (Mainichi): Education Bureau Director Shiobara and Police Affairs Bureau Director Mitsuhashi probably stand out.

Hong (Chōsen): Probably so. When Mr. Shiobara addressed the Tōa Youth Association rally at Keijō Stadium this summer, with about a thousand representatives from youth groups in Tōkyō, Ōsaka, Manchukuo, North China, Central China, and the 13 provinces within Korea before him, speaking through a microphone outdoors, he had a fine appearance and spoke well, albeit briefly.

Kim (Mainichi): I also heard Police Affairs Bureau Director Mitsuhashi speak before a large crowd once. As someone who was active on the central stage in Tōkyō, he seemed quite composed ("腹がすわる" - *hara ga suwaru*, composed/steady). There are no other notable speakers among the other bureau directors. The individuals capable of standing on the<212> balcony (屋臺 - *yatai*) on the second floor front of the Government-General building at Kwanghwamun Intersection before a crowd of tens of thousands and delivering a policy speech with dignity are likely Governor-General Minami, Education Director Shiobara, and Police Director Mitsuhashi.

Reporter: Are newspaper reporters permitted to observe the Provincial Governors' Conference?
Hong (Chōsen): No, they are not.

Reporter: What about the Police Affairs Directors' Conference?
Kim (Mainichi): Of course not. However, while the Police Affairs Directors' Conference held in the Government-General's second conference room is important, the meeting held on the very last day at the Police Affairs Bureau Director's official residence is truly secret, where many aspects of Korean governance are decided.

Reporter: How about the Central Council (Chūsuin) meetings?
Yang (Tōa): I've never been, but they are probably closed to the public.

Reporter: At meetings like the Spiritual Mobilization executive meetings or gatherings where private citizens participate, who speaks well?
Kim (Mainichi): At some meeting or other, I saw Pak Chung-yang speaking confidently, even with the Governor-General and Vice Governor-General present. There were rumors that even the Director of Home Affairs and the Director of Police Affairs struggled with him when he was a Provincial Governor, and he spoke thoroughly. His speech was orderly, clearly distinguishing principles, and the entire assembly listened attentively.

**Relationship between Private Individuals and the Government-General**

Reporter: After Marquis Pak Yŏng-hyo passed away, there was talk of Marquis Yun Tŏk-yŏng becoming the Vice Chairman of the Central Council. How did Mr. Min Pyŏng-sŏk end up getting the position?
Kim (Mainichi): Mr. Min Pyŏng-sŏk had been in the Central Council for a long time, served as a Minister four or five times in the old days, and was the Director of the Yi Royal Household Office for a long time. Considering his career, and his stature as a person, it was probably his turn (順席).

Reporter: Seeing Marquis Yun Tŏk-yŏng appointed as the Head Scholar (大提學) of the Kyŏnghagwŏn suggests Mr. Yun also had prospects, didn't it?
Kim (Mainichi): The Governor-General is a person of sentiment who greatly respects the lineage of the nobility, so it seems he intends to bring Marquis Yun onto a larger stage in the future. The position of Head Scholar was held by leading scholars of the generation like Kim Yun-sik and Chŏng Man-jo. Bringing Mr. Yun there is likely part of the Governor-General's highest policy regarding the unification of the Confucian scholars (儒林).

Yang (Tōa): Regarding Marquis Yun Tŏk-yŏng, once when I had business in the Honam region and was boarding a train at Namdaemun, the Marquis happened to be heading to a Confucian scholars' conference in either Ch'ungch'ŏng Province or Kyŏngsang Province. Many Government-General officials, including Mr. Yu Uk-kyŏm, were at the station to see him off. By chance, in the second-class carriage, I met Mr. Wada Ichirō, who was returning from Manchukuo at the time, and saw the Marquis conversing and interacting with him. He seemed quite skilled. His Japanese was decent too... Wada was formerly the Director of the Railway Bureau and held prominent positions.

Hong (Chōsen): Among the nobility, Marquis Yi Tal-yong, who seems to have received a French-style or Western baptism, appears quite capable. When His Majesty King Yi visits, Mr. Yi often acts as his guide.

Reporter: Who is likely to become a member of the House of Peers (貴族院)?<213>
Kim (Mainichi): Well, it's hard to say. It will probably be among Yun Tŏk-yŏng, Min Pyŏng-sŏk, and Yi Tal-ho (李達鎬 - *likely a typo for Yi Tal-yong* 李達鎔).

Reporter: Do Ch'oe Rin and Han Sang-ryong have no prospects?
Ch'oe (Chōsen): Who knows about that.

Reporter: Recently, Kim Kye-su and Pak Hŭng-sik are included in various Government-General committees, but Han Sang-ryong seems to be left out. Why is that? For a while, the names Pak Yŏng-ch'ŏl, Han Sang-ryong, Kim Kye-su, and Pak Hŭng-sik were routinely included in any committee, whether related to agriculture or historical sites.
Kim (Mainichi): From what I hear, it seems the top leadership of the Government-General views Kim Kye-su and Pak Hŭng-sik as capable figures and is trying to elevate them further. Mr. Han's name might be included when drafting proposals at lower levels, but the final decision is made higher up. Perhaps his popular reputation is somewhat less than before.

Reporter: What about Min Kyu-sik and Ha Chun-sŏk?
Ch'oe (Chōsen): There's talk that Ha is a bit impatient ("あせる" - *aseru*, to be impatient/hasty), and Min presents himself purely as a banker.

Yang (Tōa): The person of character viewed by the Government-General as weighty and upright was Mr. Pak Yŏng-ch'ŏl. Possessing assets worth millions of won, having graduated from the Military Academy, and having knowledge and virtue, it was natural that he received such respect.

Hong (Chōsen): It's said that the late Mr. Pak Yŏng-ch'ŏl was the favorite companion for bureau director-level officials to drink with. He made no requests after socializing and was incorruptible.

Reporter: What kind of influence ("押し" - *oshi*, push/influence) does Diet Member Pak Ch'un-gŭm have with the Government-General?
Kim (Mainichi): Given his position and background, he certainly cannot be disregarded.

Reporter: What about financial world figures like Ch'oe Ch'ang-hak, Kim Ki-dŏk, Pak Ki-hyo, and Pang Ŭi-sŏk?
Kim (Mainichi): Since they are purely men of finance, they would be respected in that field, but generally...

Reporter: Which private individuals visit the Government-General most frequently?
Kim (Mainichi): It's likely the Manchukuo Consul General Kim Kye-su and Hwashin's Pak Hŭng-sik. Both need frequent contact with various bureaus and sections for their duties...

Reporter: How did Kim Kye-su become the Manchukuo Consul General after Pak Yŏng-ch'ŏl, given there are many former provincial governor-level individuals?
Kim (Mainichi): Mr. Kim got the position amidst many recommendations, both self-promoted and external. I know the details from that time well, but although certain figures from the Central Council, former governors, and nobles all hoped for it and were busy maneuvering, it went to Mr. Kim. Mr. Kim certainly did not campaign for it himself. The Government-General had Watanabe Toyohiko, the former Director of the Education Bureau and currently at the Chōsen Shokusan Ginkō (殖銀),<214> negotiate, but initially, he firmly refused (姑舍不受). While there are many unscrupulous individuals seeking official posts, Mr. Kim was upright. It was good that Mr. Kim eventually accepted. This time, with the spin-off from Keijō Bōseki, he's also establishing a ten million won spinning company in Manchukuo.

Hong (Chōsen): The funds coming from the Manchukuo government for the Consulate General's expenses are not much, so former Consul Pak also used his own money, and it seems the current Mr. Kim also has to spend several thousand won annually.

Reporter: Many prominent officials holding important positions in Manchukuo frequently visit the Government-General. What are their reputations?
Kim (Mainichi): Director of Home Affairs Ōtake returned from the Manchukuo Provincial Governors' conference recently and reported that Kantō Province Governor Yi Pŏm-ik "positively shines" (斷然光る - *danzen hikaru*). He said that among the many provincial governors, vice ministers, and various ministers gathered to discuss national policy, Yi was the best. He praised him highly. Mr. Yi had a good reputation since his time as South Ch'ungch'ŏng Governor for being knowledgeable about affairs and having great capacity.

Reporter: What about Pak Sŏk-yun?
Yang (Tōa): He has now left Poland and is said to be in Germany or Russia. According to hearsay, when he first went to Poland, he was slated to become Minister. Because the procedures for Manchukuo's recognition were delayed, he was initially sent temporarily as Consul General. If times had been better, he might have been called the Minister to Poland.

Reporter: What about Ch'oe Nam-sŏn, Chin Hak-mun, and Yun Sang-p'il?
Kim (Mainichi): Ch'oe Yuk-tang (pen name of Ch'oe Nam-sŏn) is a scholar, and both Mr. Chin and Mr. Yun are well-regarded ("受け" - *uke*, reception/reputation), whether considering their Manchuria-related affairs work or otherwise.

**Anecdotes from Accompanying the Governor-General**

Reporter: Do you have any impressions from traveling north and south accompanying the Governor-General?
Yang (Tōa): Back in Governor-General Ugaki's time, I accompanied him as a newspaper special correspondent when he went on an inspection tour of the Western provinces (西鮮). One day, after observing various things according to the schedule, only the rural village visit remained. I thought they might skip it, but they deliberately had someone carry lanterns ahead and visited house after house as planned until 8 PM. As he was implementing policies with great emphasis on rural development, he was extremely enthusiastic and energetic regarding rural issues.

Reporter: What about Governor-General Minami?
Kim (Mainichi): What I feel on every trip accompanying him is Mr. Minami's respect for the elderly (敬老). When visiting rural villages, if he meets an old person, he asks their age and says, "Huh! Two years younger than me, so you're like a younger brother," then pats their shoulder or expresses very friendly affection. The Governor-General personally exemplifies and promotes the custom of respecting elders, which is most beautiful in Eastern morality.

Hong (Chōsen): Generally speaking, major national policies like "Southern Sheep, Northern Cotton" (南羊北綿 - *Nanyō Hokumen*), bringing in Imaidan, an expert in electric power, as Vice Governor-General, persuading Noguchi to build the Changjin River hydroelectric plant,<215> and establishing large-scale gold production policies – it seems Mr. Ugaki laid the foundation for many of these significant undertakings. By Governor-General Minami's era, times had changed, entering a wartime footing and indeed an era of war, so various policies were established in Korea as a supply base (兵站基地). Furthermore, regarding spiritual matters, much was done, including "Unity of Japan and Korea" (內鮮一體 - *Naissen Ittai*) and the creation of clan names (氏族創定 - *shizoku sōtei*, likely referring to *Sōshi-kaimei*). In any case, both were Governors-General who accomplished much for Korea in recent times.

**Promising Officials with Potential for Provincial Governorship**

Reporter: Just as Yi Chin-ho was appointed Director of the Education Bureau during Governor-General Saitō's time, if one bureau director position were to be given to a Korean official now, who would be considered? It would inevitably be one of the current governors.
Kim (Mainichi): Whether such a day will come or not is in the minds of the Governor-General and Vice Governor-General, so we cannot speculate (特度). But assuming it could happen, someone like Hwanghae Provincial Governor Kang P'il-sŏng might have a chance. I hear that no matter how large the budget proposal Governor Kang brings, once he comes up and negotiates with the Government-General bureau directors and section chiefs, it generally passes, so his reputation within the Hwanghae provincial office is said to be considerable.

Reporter: Also, among the current young-turk (少壯派) Government-General officials (事務官 - *jimukan*), who is likely to become a provincial governor in the future?
Yang (Tōa): There are currently seven such individuals within the Government-General. They are all young and talented, so if things go smoothly (雨順風和 - *ujun p'unghwa*, lit. timely rain and gentle wind), they are all likely candidates for governorship.

Hong (Chōsen): Among them, the one likely to be appointed first would be someone like Yi Wŏn-bo, Chief of the Social Education Section.

Kim (Mainichi): At the Provincial Councilor (道 參與官 - *dō san'yokan*) level, there's Kyŏnggi Province's Yi Sŏng-gŭn. He's still only 40, passed the Higher Civil Service Exam (高文 - *kōbun*), and has already served in various local posts... His promotion to governor (*yŏnggam*) is probably near now... The officials within the Government-General are all capable administrators (能吏 - *nōri*), so they will all succeed, but among them, someone like Kye Kwang-sun in the Mining Section is only in his early 30s, came via the Ministry of Colonial Affairs (拓務省), and is full of talent (才氣潑潑).

**Past Government-General Reporters**

Reporter: Do newspaper presidents like Pang Ŭng-mo of Chōsen Nippō, Paek Kwan-su of Tōa Nippō, Ch'oe Rin of Mainichi Shinpō, and Kim Kap-sun of Chōsen Shimbun still occasionally visit the Government-General these days?
Kim (Mainichi): Matters related to editorial affairs are generally handled by the newspaper's editorial director visiting and negotiating, so unless there's a special reason, do the presidents really make visits?<216>

Reporter: Perhaps once a month on average?
Kim (Mainichi): It might be about that often.

Reporter: Who were some of the well-known predecessors among the Government-General reporters?
Kim (Mainichi): On the Japanese side, there seem to have been quite a few. Adachi Kenzō, who later became Home Minister, also covered the Government-General in his youth when he was in Seoul working for the *Hansŏng Sinbo*. Nakano Seigō of the current Kokumin Dōmei also visited the Government-General daily while serving as the Keijō bureau chief for the *Ōsaka Mainichi Shimbun*. Makiyama Kōzō of the Minseitō also covered it during his time at the *Chōsen Shimbun* company. There were many others besides them.

Reporter: Tōa Nippō?
Yang (Tōa): In the early days around 1920-1921 (Taishō 9-10), Yi Sang-hyŏp and Chang Tŏk-su covered it. Later came Han Wi-gŏn, Ch'oe Wŏn-sun, Kim Tong-jin<pers>, <pers>Kuk Ki-yŏl, and others. I seem to recall Kim Chun-yŏn also covered it for a while, but I'm not certain.

Reporter: Chōsen Nippō?
Hong (Chōsen): Well, I don't know the old days well, but probably Dr. Yi Kwan-yong, Kim Tong-wŏn, Kim Ŭl-han, Pang Han-min, Hong Yang-myŏng, and others.

Reporter: Mainichi Shinpō?
Kim (Mainichi): I also don't know for sure, but probably Yi Sang-hyŏp, Min U-bo, followed by Yi Sang-ch'ŏl, Yu Kwang-nyŏl, Nam Sang-il, and others. Looking at the figures, it seems each company has changed quite a bit over time.

Reporter: Which Government-General officials have consistently interacted with the press community?
Kim (Mainichi): The two who have consistently dealt with newspaper companies for around 20 years are likely Interpreter Nishimura and Interpreter Tanaka. Both speak Korean well and are thoroughly familiar with Korean society and affairs.

Yang (Tōa): Both speak Korean well, but Mr. Nishimura is the scholarly type, while Mr. Tanaka is the conversational type (focused on interpreting). Looking at his recent translation of Hino Heisuke's (火野軍曺 - *actually Hino Ashihei*) "Wheat and Soldiers" (麥と兵丁 - *Mugi to Heitai*), he clearly put much effort into it; he knew many Korean vocabulary words that even we don't know.

Hong (Chōsen) *Typo, likely Hong (Chōsen)*: The translation of "Wheat and Soldiers" had a feel of using too many terms common in upper-class society. We learned many words we didn't know; he seems to have done hidden research into etymology.

Kim (Mainichi): I felt a desire for it to have been translated a bit more smoothly and easily.

Reporter: Any other officials?
Ch'oe (Chōsen): Nakamura Kentarō, who used to be in the censorship section, was also a long-serving official who spoke Korean well.

Reporter: How does the position of Chief of the Library Section (圖書課長) compare to other section chiefs?
Kim (Mainichi): Now is the era of the large Library Section, so it's a top-tier section chief post. In the past too, those who served as Library Section Chief all moved on to provincial governor-level promotions. The first chief, Tanaka Takeo (concurrently Security Section Chief), went on to become Police Affairs Bureau Director and is now Vice Minister of Colonial Affairs. The next, Shimizu, is now Governor of Wakayama Prefecture. The one after that, Tatsumi, also got promoted to governor of some prefecture in Japan proper. The recent one, Yagyū, too (continued on page 225)<217> is now Chief of the Local Affairs Section and will surely be transferred out as a governor any day now... Otherwise, would they appoint just anyone to a position that deals with several major newspapers influencing public opinion across the land?

Reporter: Thank you for speaking with us for such a long time. It would be nice to hold such informal talks once or twice a month. (End 10 PM)<225>

**Summary**

This 1940 roundtable discussion features four Korean journalists assigned to cover the Japanese Government-General of Korea. They share anecdotes and opinions about the Korean royal family (King Yeongchin and Queen Yi Bangja), visits by foreign diplomats (Chinese, Soviet), the speaking styles and policies of Governors-General Minami and Ugaki, assessments of other high-ranking Japanese officials (Vice Governors-General, Bureau Directors), potential Korean candidates for high office (governors, bureau directors), the relationships between prominent Korean elites (nobles, businessmen like Pak Hŭng-sik, Kim Kye-su) and the colonial authorities, and the history of Government-General reporters. The discussion provides insights into the workings of the colonial government, the personalities involved, the social dynamics among Korean elites under Japanese rule, and the observations of contemporary journalists navigating this environment. For historians, this article offers a valuable glimpse into the perspectives of Korean journalists operating within the colonial system, their assessments of both Japanese officials and Korean figures cooperating with or navigating the colonial structure, and details about specific events and political currents (like *Naissen Ittai* and *Sōshi-kaimei* mentioned indirectly) in late 1930s Korea. It also reveals the careful language used when discussing sensitive topics like the royal family and colonial policies.

王族께서 總督府 訪問 光景

記者 - 李王 전하께서 總督府에 총독을 공식으로 御訪問 오시든 때 광경을 보섰어요?
梁東亞 - 王公族이 御訪問 오실 때 광경이란 그저 신문에 보도되는 것과 같지요, 그 보다도 작년 여름이든가요, 李王, 同妃 兩전하께서 水原 華山陵으로 展謁하시러 가실 적에 나는 東亞日報의 특파 기자로 수행하게 되어 지척에서 존안을 뵈온 적이 있었는데 황송하온 말슴이나 참으로 인자하시고 위엄이 게시고 또 妃殿下께서도 늘 미소하시며 춘풍이 도시더군요. 그런데 水原 華山陵에 李王 전하께서 참배하신 것은 30년 만에 처음이시래 예전 純宗께서 行次하신 적이 있었으나... 그런 까닭인지 서울서 水原邑까지, 또 水原邑에서 御陵까지의 삼십리 沿線에는 尊駕를 뵈옵겠다고 仁川, 驪州, 龍仁 할 것 없이 京畿道 각읍으로 여러 만명 백성들이 몰려와서 두 길까에 읍하고 있었는데 이렇게 白衣群象을 만히 보기는 因山 때 이후 처음일 걸요. 가시는 길가마다 백성들이 황토 뿌리고 몬지가 날가바 그 우에 물까지 뿌리여 티끌 하나 안일게 청소하고, 하야 말숙하게 새 옷 가라 입은 백성들이 공손히 두 손을 들어 읍하여 奉迎奉送하는 모양은 신문 기자 십 수년에 진정으로 머리 숙여지더군요.
기자 - 두분 전하께서 퍽으나 금슬이<209> 좋시다더군요.
梁東亞 - 각금 서로 미소하시며, 또 殿陵 같은 데를 오르실 때에는 그 높은 石段 우를 굽 높은 구두 신은 妃殿下께서 오르기 곤란하실 것 같해서, 그 때마다 그대로 제자리에서 게시란 뜻으로 손을 들어 만류하시고 그리고 손수 먼저 올나 가실 적마다 會釋(目禮)를 하시며, 또 절을 다하시고 내려 오실 적에도 妃殿下께 팔을 들어 인사하시고... 퍽도 예절이 밝으시여요.
洪朝日 - 水原 가실 적에도 그리섰지만 年前 兩 전하께서 慶州 佛國寺로 행차하신 적이 있는데 그 때 뵈오니 多寶塔 옆 꽃나무 옆에 妃殿下를 서시게 하고 李王 전하끠서 손소 「카메라」를 들고 사진 박으시는데 얼굴 표정을 좀 더 우스라고도 하여 보시고 몸을 이 쪽으로 좀 더 기우시라고도 하시고 그럴 적마다 妃殿下께서는 미소하시면서 잘 순종하시고, 두 분께서 참으로 誼가 좋으시더군요, 자동차를 타실 때나 보행하실 때나 모다...
金每新 - 「카메라」는 두분 전하께서 다 좋와하시고 그 기술도 상당하시대요. 東京에서는 각금 미복으로서 「三越」같은 데 가서 카메라 경기회에도 다른 왕귀족 분과 함께 참가하신다더군요. 年前에도 歐羅巴를 일주하시고 도라오서서 서울 昌德宮 秘苑에서 원유회를 하신 적이 있는데 長安 貴紳이 운집하신 속에서 전하께서 「카메라」를 드시는 것을 뵈었지요, 洋行하섰을 적에도 「벨단」같은 歐洲大戰의 戰跡을 巡遊하실 적에 친히 박으신 사진이 많다고 李王職 長官 條田 박사가 언젠가 이야기하더군요.
崔朝新 - 딴말이나 일전 전하께서 출정하섰다가 東京 御邸에 도라오실 적에 어저 현관 앞까지 나오서 妃전하께서 아홉 살 나는 세자 전하를 다리시고 공순히 절하며 부군 전하를 맞으시는 사진이 東京 각 신문에 났는데 참으로 좋으시더군요.
기자 - 나도 왕전하를 지척에서 뵈온 적이 있었지요. 한번은 南大門 停車場 푸렛트 홈에서 軍裝하신 것을, 한번은 純宗 因山日, 昌德宮으로부터 마차을 타시고 상복하시고 나오시는 것을 뵈였어요, 인자하시고도 위엄이 게시더군요. 아까 水原 展陵 가실 적 행차는 어떠섰어요.
梁東亞 - 남색 대*에 누른 기, 푸른 기 드섰고 비전하께서는 四人驕를 타섰더군요.<210>

外國 使臣 이야기

기자 - 외국 사신들이 總督府로 南總督을 공식 방문올 적에 어떻게 해요.
洪朝日 - 英米國 총영사들은 별로 공식 방문이 없지요. 그저 신년 축하라거나 그 밖에 天長節같은 날 가례를 드리러 總督府로 총독을 찾는 일이 있지만은 그 보다도 年前에 그때가 아마 萬寶山사건이 일어나서 인심이 뒤숭숭 하든 때엇찌요. 滿洲事變 직전이니까, 東京으로 부임하는 中華民國 蔣作賓 대사가 北京, 奉天 거처 서울에 왔는데 總督府을 방문한 것을 우리들 기자단이 둘러싸고 회견한 적이 있는데 체격은 6尺이 넘는 큰 키에 큰 몸집이더군요. 「悠然と構へゐ」하엿는데 우리가 「萬寶山 사건의 非가 당신네 中國 측에 있지 않으냐니까, 그 사건에 대하여는 東京 가서 말하겠노라」하며 함구하더군. 그래서 「昨夜 平壤을 위시해서 朝鮮 안에서 불상사가 있어 支那人이 다수가 사상하였는데 이것을 아느냐」하니까, 京義線 車中에서 전보로 보고받었노라 할 뿐으로 역시 함구하더구만. 그래서 나종에 이런 사태 있는 중이니 부임하지 않고 그양 도로 南京으로 가지 않겠느냐고 하니까, 아니다 기어히 東京으로 부임하겠노라 하며 말하는데 조금도 놀라는 기색이 없더군요.
金每新 - 외교관이 놀라서 쓰나. 언제든 뱃장을 내 밀어야 하지. 하하하 (일동 笑) 蔣作賓은 나도 만나 보았는데 체격 좋고 뱃심도 어지간 하더군.
梁東亞 - 요전에 新任하는 蘇聯 대사가 東京 가는 길에 서울서 2,3일 묵었는데 그때 總督府에 南總督을 만나보고 나오는 것을 우리들 總督府 기자단이 둘러싸고 회견하였는데 외교관이란 모다 그런지 노몬한 사건, 日蘇 어업조약 문제 등, 여러 가지를 물어봐도 전부 딴청 이거든요. 京城 총영사관에 있는 「이와노푸」가 통역인데, 이치가 겨우 소학교 3,4년 정도 밖에 몰으는 말로 통역하는데 당초에 東問西答이고... 대답하기 어려운 말은 모다 딴청이고... 「喰へない男」란 인상을 주더군요.
洪朝日 - 그날 나도 보았는데 蘇聯 측에 유리한 저이네의 선전거리 말은 활발히게 쭉-쭉- 터러 놓고 그러고 이쪽 질문에는 말귀 어두운 모양으로 「네? 네?」하고 작고 뭇기만 하고, 대답한다는 것이 당초에 얼토당토않은 말을 하고...... 그 뱃심이 상당하더군. 외교관이란 다 그런지 몰아도.
기자 - 滿洲國 총영사 范生漢씨는 어떤가요.
崔朝新 - 朝鮮에 오래 있든 사람으로 朝鮮 사정은 잘 아는 모양이더군요.
金每新 - 支那事變 初에 우리나라에 불리한<211> 테마가 돌면 제가 南京 정부에 조회하여 본 즉 그런 일이 없섯다고 回電 왓스니 안심하라고 일일히 신문사로 전화질 잘하고.

總督, 總監, 局長의 演說評

기자 - 최근의 총독인 南씨와 宇垣氏의 연설평을 하여 주구려.
金每新 - 伊藤博文이나 寺內, 長谷川이 얼마나 연설을 잘 하였는지 몰으지만 지금의 현 총독이 연설은 잘 하더군요. 여러 번을 드렀는데 草稿없이 내리하는데 앞가슴이 떡 버러진 장군이 높직한 소리로 說往說來하는 것을 들으면 청중이 그만 반하고 말지. 원래 비서관이 원고를 지어 받이는데 그 대체를 보고 대개 중요한 부분만 기억하고서 초고를 안보면서하여요. 물론 도지사 회의 같은 때는 一言一句를 신중히 하느라고, 그때는 연설인 것이 아니라 훈시요, 演述이니까 그렇지 않겠지만--총독이란 カタカキ(眉書)가 없더라도 연설 잘 하는 이로 통할 걸요. 총동원 연맹 席上이거나 실업가가 많이 뫃인 좌석에서의 연설이든가.
崔朝新 - 언젠가 中村 군사령관의 연설은 조직적이요 체계있는 연설을 하더군요.
洪朝日 - 宇垣씨 연설은 학자적이고 南씨 연설은 정치가식 연설이랄가요.
기자 - 총감은 어떼요.
金每新 - 大野 現 총감이나 兒玉 前 총감이나 다 특출한 배 없이 그저 직책을 다하는 터이지요.
崔朝新 - 예전 下岡 정무총감은 역대 총감 중의 걸물일뿐더러 연설도 잘 하였다고 하더군요.
기자 - 현재 국장급 중에는 누가 나슨가요?
金每新 -鹽原 학무국장과 三橋 경무국장이 나슬걸요.
洪朝日 - 그럴걸요. 鹽原씨가 금년 여름에 京城 운동장에서 東亞청년단대회를 할 적에 東京 大阪과 滿洲國과 北支, 中支의 각 청년단과 鮮內 13道 청년단 대표 약 천여 명을 앞에 세우고 「마이쿠로」를 통하야 야외 연설을 하는데 風釆도 좋고 말도 단시간이지만 잘 하더군요.
金每新 - 三橋 경무국장도 언젠가 많은 군중을 뫃아놓고 하는 것을 드렀는데 東京 중앙 무대에서 노든 인물이니 만치 「腹がすわる」 하엿더군 其外의 국장에는 연설가가 없어요. 光化門 네거리에 여러 萬名 군중을 모아놓고, 總督府 二층 정면의<212> 발고니(屋臺)에 올라서서 당당히 시정 연설할 만한 인물은 南總督, 鹽原 學務, 三橋 警務의 정도일 걸.
기자 - 도지사 회의는 신문 기자에게 방청을 許하는가요.
洪朝日 - 안 하지요.
기자 - 경무부장 회의도--
金每新 - 물론 안하지요, 그러나 경찰부장 회의야 總督府 제2회의실에서 하는 것도 중요하겠지만 맨 最終日 하로를 경무국장 官舍에서 하는 會가 있는데 여기야 정말 비밀로 朝鮮 정치의 많은 부면이 이 회석에서 결정되지요.
기자 - 中樞院회의는 어때요.
梁東亞 - 가본 적이 없지만 아마 비공개일걸요.
기자 - 精神총동원 이사회석이거나 또는 민간 인사가 끼우는 회의 석상에서 누가 말 잘 하는가요.
金每新 - 언젠가 무슨 회의든가에 보니 총독 총감도 다 열석한 석상인데 朴重陽이 당당합데다. 예전 도지사 당시에도 내무부장, 경찰부장이 쩔쩔 매였다는 소문이 있느니만치 철저하게 이야기 하더군요. 말에 질서 있고 사리를 잘 分辨하는 말이고, 滿座가 경청하더군요.

民間人士와 總督府 關係

기자 - 朴泳孝 후작이 도라간 뒤 中樞院 부의장은 尹德榮 侯說이 있었는데 어떠게 閔丙奭씨가 되었는가요.
金每新 - 閔丙奭씨야 오래 中樞院에 있었고 옛날 시절에 大臣 4,5차 지냈고 李王職 長官에도 오래 있었고, 관록으로 보아, 또 인물로 보아, 順席일걸요.
기자 - 經學院 大提學에 尹德榮 侯를 내세우는 것을 보아서는 尹씨도 가망 있었겠는데요.
金每新 - 총독은 귀족의 문벌을 몹시 존중하는 인정있는 분이기에 尹 후작을 앞으로 큰 무대에 등장시키려 하고 있는 듯해요. 大提學이라면 金允植, 鄭萬朝 등 一世의 석학들인데 氏를 거기 가저온 것은 儒林統一에 대한 총독의 최고 정책일걸요.
梁東亞 - 尹德榮 侯爵은 언젠가 湖南 방면에 내가 출장갈 일이 있어 남대문서 차를 타는데 정차장에, 尹 후작이 마침 忠淸道인가, 慶尙道인가 어느 유림 대회에 出席次로 가더군, 정차장에는 兪億兼씨 등, 總督府 관리도 많이 나왔더군, 마침 이등車에서 그때 滿洲國으로부터 돌아오는 和田一郞씨를 만나서 尹 후작이 대화와 교제하는 것을 보았는데 상당히 숙련하섰더군요. 국어도 웬만한 편이고... 和田이라면 예전 철도국장관으로 또 顯職에 있든 분이지요.
洪朝日 - 귀족층의 인물로는 佛蘭西式인지 洋風洗禮를 마진 李達鎔 후작이 상당하더군요, 李王 전하가 오시면 氏가 늘 안내역을 하더군요.
기자 - 貴族院 의원에는 누가 될 듯해요.<213>
金每新 - 글세요, 아마 尹德榮, 閔丙奭, 李達鎬 사이에서 될 걸요.
