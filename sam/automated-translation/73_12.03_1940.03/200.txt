Magazine Title: 삼천리 제12권 제3호
Publication Date: 1940년 03월 01일 (March 1, 1940)
Article Title: 戰爭 長期化 「家庭 生活」 主婦 座談會 (Prolonged War and "Home Life": A Housewives' Roundtable Discussion)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Roundtable Discussion)

Prolonged War and "Home Life": A Housewives' Roundtable Discussion
How is general home life as we face the fourth year of the China Incident? We invited various housewives to hear about the actual situation and their feelings.
December 18th, from 5 PM at the Peninsula Hotel, Social Room
Attendees
Female Critic… Pak Intŏk (朴仁德)
Tonga Ilbo Reporter… Hwang Sintŏk (黃信德)
Female Writer… Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi (崔貞熙)
Wife of former Tonga Ilbo Editor-in-Chief Sol Ŭisik… Ch'oe Ŭisun (崔義順)
Hyangsang Kiye School Teacher, Musician… Pak Kyŏnghŭi (朴景嬉)

Wartime Life Reform Discussion

I. Opinions on Collective Weddings and Nursery School Establishment

Reporter - Facing the fourth year of the China Incident, we believe it is time for a major reform of national life by revising all kinds of systems and customs to promote resource conservation and simplification of life<232>. Isn't the first thing to implement "collective weddings"? I hear that this system, where the ceremony is completed in a short time at a cost of 10 or 20 wŏn, is common in places like Shanghai and Nanking. How about we emulate that?
Hwang Sintŏk - In Kangso, P'yŏngan Province, the local church pastor gathered eight couples, that is 16 brides and grooms, in one place, had them dress in ceremonial attire with official hats and belts (Samo Kwandae 禮裝), and performed the wedding ceremony all at once. I heard it was already the fifth or sixth time. They say they do it around spring and autumn every year.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - Also, the pastor of Namsan Church in P'yŏngyang held the weddings of both his son and daughter on the same day, earning favorable reviews.
Reporter - Are "collective weddings" common in foreign countries too?
Pak Intŏk - I've heard that twins get married on the same day, but I haven't particularly heard about collective weddings.
Reporter - I see. So it's not as common in the West then. Looking at war news from the Western Front, there are cases where many departing soldiers and several village girls hold large-scale wedding ceremonies in places like churches or fields in front of military camps. How about we in Chosŏn society try to follow this system from now on?
Ch'oe Ŭisun - It would certainly be good in terms of collectivizing and simplifying national life. However, marriage is still a romance of youth and a very mysterious event. Stripping away all this dream and romanticism, such a mechanized ceremony doesn't seem to suit the tastes of modern men and women. Wouldn't that dry ceremony, that scene, feel dull?
Pak Kyŏnghŭi - Also, if the reason is that it costs less money, why don't the bride and groom just go out to a field by themselves and have a ceremony if they want to save money? In movies, loving Western couples drag a pastor out to a deserted field and do it very well themselves without spending a penny. As Ch'oe Ŭisun-ssi said, "collective weddings" sound rather stiff to imagine.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - Furthermore, young men and women of marriageable age in Chosŏn haven't yet had <233> training for such things. Among the dozens of brides standing in a row, when a groom sees that his wife-to-be is unattractive or inferior to others, wouldn't he try to get a divorce right there on the spot? Even if it doesn't go as far as divorce, if he becomes disillusioned, would their newlywed life be happy? It seems the current wedding style with only the two main characters is better in this regard.
Reporter - Let's put aside such personal sentimentalism for a moment - how can we actually start collective weddings?
Hwang Sintŏk - As mentioned earlier, in a rural area like Kangso in P'yŏngan Province, the area is small, so it might be easy to gather the brides and grooms of one village at one place and time. But in Seoul, it seems difficult to gather them.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - Thinking practically about actual methods, perhaps the three newspapers, Chosŏn, Tonga, and Maesin (Maeil Sinbo), could take the lead, and the newspaper presidents could officiate 10 or 20 couples at a time in the newspaper halls, maybe four times a year in spring, summer, autumn, and winter.
Ch'oe Ŭisun - Or perhaps each church could hold weddings for the children of its members once a month, with the church pastor officiating.
Hwang Sintŏk - Those are all possible, but perhaps the easiest method first would be for middle school or girls' high school principals to gather graduating students for weddings in school auditoriums. The parents would trust them, the marrying couples would feel secure, and being educators, they would handle the procedures well.
Pak Intŏk - If ceremonial expenses can be saved solely through collective weddings, the methods you all have suggested are good. However, the wedding style will depend on the tastes of the parties involved, so as long as costs are reduced, it doesn't have to be a collective wedding.
Reporter - Those are all good ideas. Let's somehow thoroughly break down this old custom that makes life unbearable due to the costs of coming-of-age, wedding, funeral, and ancestral rites (冠婚喪祭). And let's create "nursery schools (탁아소)". Are there any good proposals?
Hwang Sintŏk - They are truly necessary. Do you know how many women cannot become factory workers or professional women because there are no nursery schools? I recently visited a spinning factory outside Tongdaemun, and next to the machinery <234> there was a shelf (棚), and something wrapped in a cloth was moving inside it. I thought the female workers had left packages there, but they were young infants. They couldn't leave them at home as there was no one to watch them, and they couldn't not go to work. So they brought them to the factory and left them like bundles. When you go to factory districts, not only maternal love but also the lack of infant protection brings tears to your eyes. Let's strive to build many nursery schools in labor districts. Even in Seoul, if such facilities existed at busy places like Chongno intersection or in the east, west, south, and north, wouldn't female teachers, female reporters, and female clerks be fortunate? They could drop them off when commuting in the morning and pick them up briefly like retrieving a simple package when returning in the evening. And wouldn't it be great if the daily childcare fee (탁아賃) was around 10 or 20 chŏn?
Ch'oe Ŭisun - Of course! Not only working women, but housewives too, wouldn't you know how convenient it would be? Briefly dropping off the child for an hour or 30 minutes while cooking rice, or dropping them off for 2-3 hours when going out to sell rice – that could also be possible, right?
Pak Kyŏnghŭi - Currently, a modern housewife I know uses the nursery school like this: when going to the theatre, she gets off the tram at Tongdaemun, drops the child off briefly, and picks them up on her way back. Also, when going out to places like Hwasin department store to buy things, it's cumbersome to bring the child all the way to Chongno, so she drops them off briefly – like that. If it goes a bit further, even idle upper-class ladies (有閑 부인) might use them that way.
Hwang Sintŏk - As far as I know, the wife of Dr. Han Ŭnje makes good use of the Tongdaemun nursery school. It's worth entrusting a child there. Inside, they have equipped it well for children to play, with swings, rocking horses, and all sorts of toys. If you just buy food and leave the child, they can play happily absorbed in fun all day without crying and without any worries. And the daily fee is supposedly 10 chŏn.
Reporter - How many are there currently in Seoul?
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - There is a nursery school inside the Tongdaemun Women's Hospital<235>, and another one at Severance. I also heard there's one at the Salvation Army nursery school, and there's talk of putting one in the newly built T'aehwa Women's Hall (泰和女子會舘) in Kyŏnji-dong.
Reporter - How can we make it so that there are nursery schools on every corner?
Hwang Sintŏk - It would be nice if various large households in Seoul could open up their detached guest quarters (사랑채). Also, it would be great if wealthy widows could contribute money and effort as a charitable act, accumulating merit for their later years.
Pak Intŏk - For nursery schools to spring up on every corner as just discussed, besides private entrepreneurs stepping forward, the government must also take action, just like establishing parks in cities for children to play in. In rural areas, the county (군) or township (면) could arrange to utilize the local development association halls (진흥회당).

II. Regarding "Communal Cooking" and Apartment Living

Reporter - Abe Isoo, the leader of the Social Masses Party (社會 大衆), moved his entire family into an apartment. How about we eliminate these cumbersome homes we currently live in and move into these communal dwellings, apartments? Provided that new apartment construction increases.
Hwang Sintŏk - That's good, it's always been my wish. But to do that, we'd have to change our lifestyle first. We'd need to drastically reduce household goods, get rid of things like crock stands (장독대), and modify things like kimchi and radish kimchi (깔둑이). It should be very simple, with temporal discipline, receiving training in communal living - that's what would be needed. To live in communal housing, since many families live together, children shouldn't cry excessively, marital discord and fighting sounds shouldn't reach the neighbors, and economic income should be around the middle-class level to cook rice on a gas stove, have bread and soup for breakfast, and always be able to wear clean clothes. Therefore, I think it would be good to popularize the apartment living system after simplifying our lives somewhat more.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - In Tokyo, there are apartments for single women. It would be nice if there were such things in Seoul too.
Pak Kyŏngh嬉 - Gathering young women in one place might cause trouble. Like a "House Forbidden to Men" (금남의 家). (Everyone laughs)
Ch'oe Ŭisun - But if apartment construction increases, I look forward to seeing how the housing shortage might be alleviated. If the dilapidated houses built closely together like crab shells<236> were completely torn down and replaced with multi-story buildings well-designed for one family per unit, then 10 or 20 families could live together, which would be truly great. Having communal recreation areas, communal kitchens, communal laundry rooms, etc., to modernize life. Wouldn't that be nice?
Pak Intŏk - From the perspective of hygiene or convenience of living, living separately is best, but since land prices are high in the city, apartment living might be beneficial.
Reporter - How about "communal cooking"?
Pak Kyŏngh嬉 - Is there anywhere doing that? Since the Incident...
Reporter - In Seoul, in a certain neighborhood in Chin'gogae (Honmachi - 本町), they cook rice communally, but prepare side dishes in their own homes. They say it costs less money and the results are much better. In places like Tokyo, this has been happening for a long time.
Ch'oe Ŭisun - Our food is complex, how would that work?
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - Rice tastes best when served freshly cooked, but if it's cooked elsewhere and delivered, it will get cold, and the taste of the rice will be greatly diminished. Therefore, wouldn't it be difficult to implement quickly in Chosŏn households?
Hwang Sintŏk - I think so too. Also, cooking rice in a Chosŏn house indirectly serves the purpose of heating the ondol floor. Even if rice is cooked elsewhere, they still need to light a fire in their own house, so firewood costs would still be incurred. Wouldn't that be uneconomical again?
Pak Kyŏngh嬉 - Communal cooking would probably be convenient for those living a modern lifestyle in Western-style houses or those living in tatami-matted rooms.
Pak Intŏk - Probably so. It would likely still be inconvenient for us.

II. How is the Household Economy Regarding Rice, Firewood, Clothing, etc.?

Reporter - Tokyo women like Konjin Shiko (近神市子) and Hayashi Fumiko (林芙美子) confessed that managing household life under this incident is becoming increasingly difficult from a housewife's perspective. What are your actual experiences?
Ch'oe Ŭisun - Of course, it has become difficult. Especially these days, rice is scarce. Before, for one mal (approx. 18 liters) of rice, they gave one toe (approx. 1.8 liters) of miscellaneous grains like barley, but now, to buy one mal, you have to buy three toe of barley.<237>
Reporter - I heard that even Ch'unwŏn's (Yi Kwang-su's) household couldn't buy rice in time and had to get by on flour and glutinous rice for several days.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - Our house was like that too. Also, they give us Taiwanese rice (臺灣米), but the quality isn't good. However, they say rice will arrive in Seoul soon, so I'm waiting. It will probably become plentiful again.
Reporter - What about clothing fabric?
Hwang Sintŏk - Two years ago, a bolt of cotton cloth cost 10 wŏn 50 chŏn, but now it's supposedly 22 wŏn, for one mixed with staple fiber (스푸). Even looking at official prices, some things have doubled or tripled. However, I wait, believing that the day will come when clothing items become cheaper and more abundant.
Reporter - How about the price of women's Western-style suits? Since you always wear Western clothes.
Pak Kyŏngh嬉 - Yes, they've become expensive. And it seems there isn't good fabric like before. Therefore, I don't think about buying now; I just mend, wash, and tidy up old clothes to wear... that's how it is.
Hwang Sintŏk - Let's avoid hoarding (買溜メ - kaidame). Then prices wouldn't rise, and goods wouldn't become so scarce. Let's emphasize this to general housewives during this wartime.
Reporter - Let's briefly discuss this issue and move on.

III. What if a Ration Coupon System (切符制度) is Implemented?

Reporter - According to an article in the Osaka Mainichi Shinbun the other day, the topic of a "Coupon System" (切符制 - kippu sei) for clothing and food came up from ministers during a cabinet meeting. According to the argument of economist Takahashi Kamekichi (高橋龜吉), a member of the Central Goods Committee and a supporter of the current cabinet's policies, the "Coupon System" is the best method to ensure fair distribution and prevent uneven distribution of goods.
Rice, firewood, clothing, etc.
If a Coupon System is implemented, what will happen to Chosŏn households?
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - If a Coupon System is implemented, what will actually happen?
Reporter - Currently, there's a distribution system for gasoline ("까소링" - gasoring?). It will probably be like that. You would likely go to the county office or police station to receive a coupon stating you can buy, say, one sŏm (approx. 180 liters) of rice per month or one set of clothes per household member. Then you would present that coupon and money at a store to make the purchase.
Ch'oe Ŭisun - Wealthy households might find it somewhat inconvenient as they couldn't live as affluently, but the general public would probably be able to live with much more peace of mind.
Hwang Sintŏk - Goods would be distributed evenly.<238>
Pak Intŏk - Nowadays in Seoul, you need a ticket to buy rice, so in a way, a partial coupon system is already in place for buying rice. I have another appointment, so please excuse me as I leave first.

Critique of Various Social Issues

I. The Issue of Following the Husband's Surname

Reporter - The Civil Decree (民事令) has been revised, and now, no matter how proudly independent a woman was, she must inevitably take her husband's surname. What are your resolves regarding this?
Hwang Sintŏk - If they tell us to take the husband's surname, we'll take it. For a woman, whether she takes her father's surname or her husband's surname, it's not really a fundamental issue.
Reporter - So what happens then? Since your husband is Mr. Im, do you become Im Sintŏk? And Sol Ŭisun? And Kim Kyŏnghŭi? Is that how it works?
Hwang Sintŏk - That's right. Also, if a four-character name is needed, I plan to call myself Im Hwang, Sintŏk.
Reporter - Someone said they would put the character 新 (Sin - new) in front. Like Sin Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi, Sin Pak Kyŏnghŭi.
Ch'oe Ŭisun - The husband is heaven (남편은 하늘이니까), so prefixing his surname to one's name is also a good thing.
Pak Kyŏngh嬉 - Anyway, it will probably be confusing for a while. Especially for women active in society. If their surname changes, how will the world remember their husband's surname too? So it might feel awkward, like meeting a new person.
Hwang Sintŏk - Having used this name, familiar to the mouth and ears, thousands, tens of thousands of times over 40 years since birth (落地 이후), changing it overnight will require considerable effort and struggle for a while to re-establish recognition.
Reporter - Then, like annotations in the Thousand Character Classic (千字 뒤푸리), explanations will be needed. Like "Former Ms. Hwang Sintŏk is the current Ms. Im Sintŏk." But there might be cases more difficult than that. For example, if someone divorces and remarries, someone called Pak Oksun yesterday could become Kim Oksun today and Ch'oe Oksun tomorrow.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - But a bigger issue crossing the minds of us new women<239>, more than such cases of divorce, is that for a female writer, for instance, her name is her life, her "value mark" (價値票). It's hard to abandon this name, built over 20-30 years, overnight. Therefore, I want to create a separate name required for the civil register (民籍上), and continue using my old name socially, like a pen name (雅號) or nickname. Although in Chosŏn, the issue of taking the husband's surname arises because of the newly revised Civil Decree, even in the mainland (내지 - Japan), where it's already customary to take the husband's surname, female writers like Uno Chiyo (宇野千代) or Hayashi Fumiko (林芙美子) never took their husbands' surnames. Moreover, women like Uno Chiyo remarried two or three times; if she had to change to her husband's surname each time, she might have become Kimura Chiyo, then Watanabe Chiyo... but seeing that she always remains Uno Chiyo suggests that even if she has a formal name on the family register (戶籍上), society seems to tolerate the continued use of her established name. I hope this much tolerance will be shown towards Chosŏn women as well.

II. Systems Disadvantageous to Women

Reporter - What are some systems that are disadvantageous to women?
Hwang Sintŏk - When I start thinking about such issues, I get angry and so agitated that I don't want to think about them anymore. This world is just organized based on male standards, unilaterally obligating women (女性 片務的).
Reporter - For example, licensed prostitution (公娼)?
Hwang Sintŏk - That too is a sad and regrettable issue.
Reporter - The fact that even within a marriage, the wife has no property rights?
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - That too.
Reporter - The double standard regarding chastity (貞操)? That is, being lenient towards men but harsh in questioning and punishing women?
Pak Kyŏngh嬉 - There's that aspect too.
Reporter - Not granting women positions like lawyers?
Ch'oe Ŭisun - That door was opened years ago.
Reporter - Are there any Chosŏn women qualified as lawyers?
Ch'oe Ŭisun - Not yet. Those who passed the exam - however, in Tokyo, two or three Japanese women are practicing law. In the future, female lawyers will likely emerge in Chosŏn too.
Hwang Sintŏk - I know for sure there is one Chosŏn woman who finished law at Meiji University (明治大學), then finished women's college, and then entered Waseda University (早大) for research while preparing for the bar exam.<240> Since she is studying now, there is hope.
Pak Kyŏngh嬉 - I heard rumors that Ms. Ko Ponggyŏng (高鳳卿) also finished law at the University of Michigan in the United States (米國 미시칸 대학).
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - I heard she has a Ph.D. in philosophy.
Reporter - Do you look forward to the emergence of female lawyers?
Hwang Sintŏk - Looking at the case of the female doctor involved in the Gibus (typhus) incident (女醫 지부스 사건) that occurred in Tokyo, without a female lawyer, it would have been impossible to defend the position of a fellow woman so profoundly and tearfully.

III. Critique of the Female Doctor Gibus Bacillus Incident (女醫 지부스 菌 事件)

Reporter - Speaking of which, how do you view the Gibus incident? The attitude of that female doctor?
Ch'oe Ŭisun - The woman is pitiful. She seems to be a tearful victim who acted out the fate of womanhood herself.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - Sending the Gibus bacillus (지브스菌 - Typhus bacillus) to Manchuria is a crime, it's evil, so I don't defend the act itself, but I can deeply, deeply understand the psychology behind it. Is it only poison? I can even understand the psychology of a woman who goes as far as killing the man she loves.
Reporter - If a Chosŏn woman were put in that situation?
Pak Kyŏngh嬉 - The psychology would likely have been the same. Only, if she lacked medical knowledge, she might have set fire to the house of the husband who betrayed and left her, or mixed rat poison in his food, committing the kind of tragedy often reported in newspapers.
Hwang Sintŏk - I agree. That woman ended up behind bars because she chose the wrong means. Though her own body was sacrificed, her crime gave men of the world a chance for deep reflection, which was a significant gain. Her suffering seems not to have been without value.
Reporter - From the man's perspective, couldn't he argue that he just wanted to separate if living together became undesirable...? Isn't it possible that women betray partners more often than men?
Ch'oe Ŭisun - Women usually grit their teeth and endure it because of the consequences of not being able to support themselves (後難). But are men like that?
Hwang Sintŏk - In the Gibus case earlier, the man consciously used the woman for his own advancement, which is base. Morally, he is the guilty one. That point is hateful. Of course,<241> there's no rule saying cases of separating after living together happily don't happen in the world, but this wasn't like that. That's the point to condemn (打罵).
Pak Intŏk - Probably so.
Reporter - Regarding this incident, there were two main criticisms in Tokyo society. One, argued by people like Sugiyama Heisuke (杉山平助), was, "The woman's method of revenge was wrong. Why didn't she become a more outstanding person than the man, achieve success, and make him bow his head in conscience by saying 'Look at me!' instead of doing such a thing?" The other was, "Since it was a crime without intent to kill (殺意), let's treat the woman leniently." These were the two lines of thought.

IV. Whether Friendship Between Opposite Sexes Can Exist

Reporter - Recently in our society, there have been several instances where the level of friendship between opposite sexes changed, sublimated into romantic relationships, and caused domestic disputes. Generally, can friendship exist between opposite sexes?
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - By friendship, do you mean just exchanging greetings?
Reporter - Going a bit beyond casually greeting and parting.
Ch'oe Ŭisun - Then, is it like an older brother-younger sister relationship (옵바 누이)?
Reporter - Probably so. A relationship where there's warm affection, though not husband or lover, could be considered friendship between opposite sexes.
Hwang Sintŏk - When I was in Tokyo back in the day, in a student mood, I was genuinely close with male students, laughing, joking, and playing around just like with my female friends. Without any impure thoughts. So this thinking continued even after entering married life, associating without any impure aspects. But it seems men aren't like women in their thinking. Hahaha. (Everyone laughs)
Reporter - Generally, isn't it that there's a time when "friendship between sexes" can exist and a time when it cannot? That is, from age 17-18 to 25-26, the marriageable age, there might be affection, but could friendship really exist? That period is when friendship equals affection. And isn't it only after marriage that one can finally relax and have the mental余裕 (yoyū - composure/leeway) for friendship?
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - Between men and women, excluding the sexual meaning, friendship doesn't seem likely to form. How can one expect a friendship as pure as "friendship between same sexes," without a speck of dust, to exist between opposite sexes? I think it's difficult.
Hwang Sintŏk - Friendship is necessary as a path towards affection, so before marriage, "friendship between sexes" can<242> exist and it's fine if it does. However, once married, affection for the husband is everything, and one should be satisfied with that. I believe friendship with other members of the opposite sex is unnecessary.

Before Youth is All Gone

Reporter - Before youth is all gone, before that mournful New Year's Eve bell tolls again, with a tender heart cherishing this life, is there anything more you wish to do? Is there anything more you wish to say?
Hwang Sintŏk - I am now 40, a person standing in the afternoon at three o'clock, so my youth is all past. Are you saying I still have youth left? Hahaha.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - I want to permanently scrape the idea of getting old out of my head. I don't want to think about it. Also, somehow, I have confidence in a corner of my heart that I won't get old.
Pak Kyŏngh嬉 - They say artists have no age. Keeping the mood fresh always might prevent premature aging.
Ch'oe Ŭisun - But being hassled by household chores and having children makes the body age. Is there any way not to age?
Reporter - Just thinking of an old, white-haired woman is sad. So, before this youth is all gone, isn't there something you want to do? Ms. Pak, being a musician, you must have greater ambitions.
Pak Kyŏngh嬉 - I have ambitions. In these youthful days, I want to travel around singing in Shanghai, Nanking, and if possible, all over the world. Such thoughts arise like fire.
Ch'oe Ŭisun - I also want to roam freely through the 400-some provinces of China (支那). I want to see the mountains and rivers there, trace the footsteps of Paek Nakch'ŏn (Bai Juyi - 白樂天) and Yi T'aebaek (Li Bai - 李太白), and bow at the tomb of Wang So-gun (Wang Zhaojun - 王昭君). Aren't there just so many famous sights in China that I've heard about since childhood, like Soju (Suzhou - 蘇州) and Kōshū (Hangzhou - 杭州), or places where talented men and beautiful women (才子佳人) frolicked, like Hwanghangnu (Yellow Crane Tower - 黃鶴樓) and Chŏkpyŏk-kang (Red Cliff - 赤壁江)? I truly want to go.
Hwang Sintŏk - I too have many people I want to meet and places I want to see when the war ends and peace is restored. Peiping (Beijing - 北京), Shanghai, Wuch'ang (武昌), Kwangtung (Guangdong - 廣東), etc., I want to go to them all.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi - Yes. Moreover, I have a desire to go to the war zone<243> and see and hear about the lives of Chinese women and the situation of children. I want to handle that good material and write a good novel.
Reporter - Among the books and movies you've seen recently, what were the most impressive?
Ch'oe Ŭisun - I read "The Biography of Madame Curie" (큐-리 夫人傳) and it was good. For movies, I saw "Heartless" (無情), but it wasn't as good as the play. I saw the Kojong Hyŏphoe Theater Troupe's (高協 劇團) play "Sardine" (정어리), and the actors' performances were amazing. Pearl Buck's "The Good Earth" (大地) movie was good. And recently I'm reading "Okrumong" (玉樓夢) in classical Chinese, and also reading "Kokuminshi" (國民史 - National History) by Tokutomi Sohō (德富蘇峯).
Pak Kyŏngh嬉 - I also enjoyed "The Biography of Madame Curie." As for movies, I make sure to see all the musical films. Schubert's "Unfinished Symphony" (未完成 交響樂) was particularly good.
Hwang Sintŏk - Movies - especially newsreels, are living lessons, so I always try to go see them, but when do I get the chance? Do I even set foot in a movie theater once a month?
Reporter - Don't you read books about the current situation, like air defense (防空) or poison gas (毒瓦斯)?
Ch'oe Ŭisun - Those things are detailed in magazines and newspapers, so I pay attention and always read them.
(The other ladies say the same.)
Reporter - Thank you very much for speaking for such a long time. You must be busy with the year-end approaching; how can I repay this kindness?
(End) <244>

**Summary**

This 1940 roundtable discussion features five prominent Korean women (a critic, reporter, writer, editor's wife, and musician/teacher) discussing the impact of the protracted Sino-Japanese War on household life in Korea. Key topics include proposed wartime reforms like collective weddings and communal kitchens (met with skepticism regarding practicality and loss of romance/tradition), the urgent need for nursery schools to support working women, the potential benefits and challenges of apartment living, economic hardships reflected in rising prices and scarcity of goods like rice and fabric, the potential implementation of a ration coupon system, the controversial mandatory adoption of husbands' surnames under revised Japanese colonial law (raising issues of identity, especially for professional women), critiques of societal disadvantages for women (property rights, chastity double standards, the Gibus bacillus case as a symbol of female desperation against male betrayal), the possibility (or impossibility) of platonic friendship between sexes, and personal aspirations for travel and artistic pursuits after the war. The discussion reveals the complex reality of urban, educated women navigating wartime austerity, colonial policies, and evolving gender roles in 1940s Korea. Historians might find valuable insights into the blend of modernizing ideas (collective living, women's rights) and traditional constraints, the tangible economic pressures of war on civilians, the specific ways colonial legal changes impacted women's identities, and the differing perspectives among elite women on social reforms and personal fulfillment during a tumultuous period.

戰時, 生活 改造論

一, 集團 結婚과 托兒所 設置 意見

기자 - 支那事變 제4년을 맞는, 지금 우리들은 물자의 절약과 생활의 간이화를 도모하기 위하여 온갓 제도와 관습을 뜯어 고처서 국민 생활의 대개조를 할 때이라고<232> 봅니다. 먼저 단행하여야 할 것이 「집단 결혼」이 아니리까. 10원, 20원의 비용으로서 단시간에 예식을 치루는 그 제도가 上海, 南京 등지에는 많다 하는데 그를 본받기로 하면 어떠하리까.
黃信德 - 平安道 江西에서 그곳 교회당 목사가 여덜 쌍 즉 16명의 신랑 신부를 한 곳에 모여놓고 沙帽冠帶한 禮裝을 차리게 한 다음 한꺼번에 결혼식을 거행한 일이 있었어요. 벌서 다섯 번째라든가요, 여섯 번채라든가요. 해마다 춘추 양절쯤은 한다더군요.
崔貞熙 - 또 平壤 南山 교회 목사는 자기 아들과 딸의 두 가지 결혼식을 같은 하로에 거행하여서 호평을 사더군요.
기자 - 외국서도 「집단 결혼」이 많습니까.
朴仁德 - 쌍동이는 한 날에 결혼한다는 말은 들었으나 집단 결혼에 대해서는 별로 듣지 못한 걸이요.
기자 - 네 알겠어요. 그러면 서양서는만치 못한 게군요. 서부 전선의 전쟁 뉴-스를 보면 출정하는 병정 다수와 그 마을 색씨 여러시 교회당이나 군영 앞 벌판 가튼에서 대규모로 결혼식을 하는 것이 잇더군요, 이런 제도를 우리들 朝鮮 사회에서도 이제부터 본바더 하여보면 어떨까요.
崔義順 - 국민 생활을 집단화시키고 간이화시키는 점에서는 물론 좋겟지요. 그러나 결혼이란 것은 역시 청춘 시대의 한 로-맨스요 또 몹시 신비스러운 일인데, 이 꿈과 낭만성을 다 빼여버리고, 너무 기계화시키는 그런 예식이 현대 남녀의 구미에 맞을 것 갔지 않쿠만. 무미건조한 그 의식, 그 광경이 승겁지 않을가요.
朴景嬉 - 또 돈이 덜 든다는 이유로 본다면, 경제하고 싶으면 신랑 신부 저이들끼리 벌판에 나가 예식하면 어때요, 영화에서 보면 서양의 사랑하는 남녀들은 목사를 끄을고 무인 벌판에 나가 돈한푼 안 드리고 저이들끼리 뚝덕뚝덕 너무 잘 하더군요, 崔義順씨 말슴 모양으로 「집단 결혼」은 어떻게 상상하기에 퍽으나 딱딱한 듯하군요.
崔貞熙 - 또 더구나 朝鮮의 적령기의 젊은 남녀들은 아직 그런 데<233> 대한 鍜練이 없어서, 여러 10명 신부가 쭉- 서고 있는 속에서 자기 안해 될 여자가 얼굴이 못생겼거나 남보다 떠러진 것을 바라볼 적에 그 현장에서 이혼하려 들지 않을가요? 설사 이혼까지 않가도 환멸을 받어서 그 신혼 생활이 행복스러워질가요? 역시 등장 인물이란 단 둘뿐인 현재의 결혼식이 이런 점으로 보아 좋을 것 같해요.
기자 - 개인적인 그런 감상성은 잠깐 논외로 하기로 하고- 실제로 어떻게 하면 집단 결혼을 시작할 수 있겠어요.
黃信德 - 아까 말슴한 平安道 江西같은 시골이면 판안이 좁아서 한 동리의 신랑 신부를 하로 한시에 한 곳에 모여놓고 하기가 쉽겠지만 서울서는 모아내기 힘들 것 갓해요.
崔貞熙 - 상식으로 생각하여 실제 방법을 생각한다면 朝鮮, 東亞, 每新의 세 신문사가 주동이 되어 그 곳 신문사 홀에서 신문사장이 주례가 되어서 10조 20조式 1년에 춘하추동 네 번쯤 한다든지요.
崔義順 - 또는 각 교회마다 한달에 한번씩 교인의 자녀 결혼을 그 교회 목사가 주례되어 한다든지요.
黃信德 - 그는 다 할 수 있는 일이나 위선 제일 쉬울 방법으로는, 중학의 교나 고등여학교 교장들이 졸업생의 결혼을 학교 강당 같은 데서 모아 놓고 한다면 그 부모도 신임하고 장가드는 저들도 안심할 것이고, 절차도 교육자이니만치 잘 발버 할 것 갓군요.
朴仁德 - 집단 결혼으로만 관혼 경제가 된다면 방식에 대해서는 여러분이 임의 말슴하신 것이 좋읍니다. 그러나 결혼 방식에 있어서는 당면한 당사자의 취미에 따라 할 테니까 집단 결혼 아니라도 비용 절약만 되면 그 뿐이겠지요.
기자 - 다 좋은 안이군요. 어떻게 그렇게하여 冠婚喪祭 비용 때문에 못 살고마는 이 舊慣을 철저적으로 타파합시다, 그리고 「탁아소」를 만듭니다, 좋은 안이 없으서요.
