기사제목	文人의 半島八景紀行 第一篇, 아아·落花岩 (A Writer's Journey to the Peninsula's Eight Scenic Views, Part 1: Ah, Nakhwaam Rock)
필자	李光洙
기사형태	기행문 (Travelogue)

文人의 半島八景紀行 第一篇, 아아·落花岩
						李光洙
Braving the pouring rain, I left Iin (利仁) early in the morning. The roughly 50-ri distance between Iin and Puyŏ (扶餘) was mostly a narrow mountain path. The smooth new road running through the long, narrow mountain gap was like a clear stream. Moreover, acacia trees lined both sides of the road, creating an incomparably picturesque scene.

I walk alone, trudging through the mountains and slopes. On the green mountains left and right, there is only the sound of rain and insects. This is the road King Ŭija (義慈王), the ruler of ten thousand chariots, who hadn't even shaken off the revelry of playing at Chawŏndae and Taewangp'o (自溫台大王浦), took when fleeing to T'aech'ŏn (態川) with the Queen Mother and Crown Prince (太后太子) on the night Paekche's capital, Panwŏlsŏng (半月城), was reduced to ashes by the torch of the Silla-Tang allied forces (羅唐聯合軍) 1,250 years ago. Since that was in the seventh month, he probably heard insect sounds like these. Stopping my staff and standing tall, I looked around left and right; on the green mountains, same as of old, the sound of horse hooves seemed audible, and I couldn't suppress a surge of emotion (焮然). As I stood atop the ridge of a pass called Sinjigyŏng Pass (新地境고개), the sudden sound of several cuckoos (杜鵑) truly seemed to tug at the traveler's heartstrings. Turning this way and that, crossing this pass and that pass, I saw three or four thatched houses nestled under the shade of drooping willows, shrouded in the evening rain (暮雨). When I asked a beautiful woman selling makgeolli, she said this place was called "Wangjat'ŏ" (왕자터 - Prince's Site) and was 20 ri from Puyŏ.

Being thirsty and hungry, I drank a bowl of makgeolli with catfish (메어기) as an accompaniment. She said there's a river called Ch'ŏnggang (淸江) in front, where many catfish are caught.

About 10 ri from there, in Kajŭngni, Yongnaemyŏn, Puyŏ County (扶餘郡 欲內面 佳增里), lies a famous prehistoric burial site.
According to the appraisal by Dr. Kuroita (黑板博士), commissioned by the Government-General the year before last, it dates back no less than 4,000 years. Though the ancient tombs (古墳) give no answer when asked what kind of people lived how back then, seeing the graves of ancestors lost amidst chaos (亂裡) felt like finding my own, and I lingered for a while, unable to bear leaving (徘徊不忍去).
Since there are said to be remains <58> of inhabitants from 4,000 years ago in Nabongni, Puyŏ (扶餘 羅福里) as well, it seems culture flourished in this region from quite ancient times. Gradually, the view opens up, and the field of vision widens. Naturally, my steps quicken with the thought of what the capital of Paekche, which possessed such magnificent civilization, must have been like. At the same time, it feels as though military music (行軍吹打) might be heard just over this pass, and the lofty, brilliant palaces of Panwŏlsŏng might soar into the sky, and the peaceful strains of pipes and strings (管絃) might resound clearly (喇喨) over the Sabisu River (泗泚水). However, crossing that pass reveals only rough summer grass and farmers planting rice seedlings.
Foundation stones and worn steles (碑) lying silently by the roadside merely hasten the traveler's tears. Lingering for several moments (徘徊數瞬), overcome with emotion (感懷無量), I rounded the eastern corner of Pusosan Mountain (扶蘇山) and reached the so-called town of Puyŏ, where two or three thousand thatched houses (草屋) lay quietly nestled. "Is this Puyŏ?" is said to be the exclamation uttered by every first-time visitor.
Who would believe this was once the site of the Sabi (泗泚) capital? Though they say human affairs are untrustworthy, could it be this extreme?

The next morning, I set out to see the old sites of Paekche (百濟). Right within the former Military Police (憲兵隊) compound lay two stone tubs (石槽). These were used by Paekche nobles for bathing. One is just right for sitting with legs stretched out, and the other just right for lying down flat.
I imagined the Romans of their prime. Paekche people lived that splendidly.
Pusosan is more of a hillock (罔) than a mountain. I don't know what Rome's (羅馬) Seven Hills (七罔山) are like, but they were probably like this. The path is entirely covered with fragments of roof tiles (盖瓦). They scattered in the flames of that night. They once caught the scent of incense from the royal brazier (御爐), and heard the peaceful songs of the South Wind (南薰). Here was the site of the grand palace (大闕), there the quarters of the consorts and concubines (妃嬪); the Moon-Welcoming Pavilion (迎月台) and Moon-Sending Pavilion (送月台) were here; the Cuju field (蹴鞠場 - ancient football) was here; some hall for song and dance (歌舞) was here. When 150 years of glory vanished in a single night, when the entirety of Pusosan became a mass of flames, illuminating the seventh-month night sky and the Sabi River (泗자 水), the tragic and gruesome (悲壯悽慘) scene of that time seems visible when I close my eyes. The image of the Nine-Layered Palaces (九重宮闕), once drunk on dreams of glory, entirely filled with terror (驚惶), weeping and wailing, stumbling and falling, running this way and that, rolling about; the image of hundreds of consorts and concubines, beautiful as flowers (꼿), delicate as fine willows (細柳), parting the black smoke and heading towards Nakhwaam Rock (落花岩) under the slanted moon of the Songwŏltae; the victorious shouts of the Silla-Tang allied forces, charging like a storm towards Sutkogae (?) and the Sabi River, seem audible if I listen closely.
I plopped down on the green grass (靑草) and tried hard to imagine that time. Before my eyes is the Panwŏlsŏng of that time. The palaces of that time are there, and the people of that time are there. The colors of that time are visible, and the voices of that time are heard. I too become a person of that time, singing and dancing. Only the rough grass of the desolate Panwŏlsŏng site (半月城池) is visible. The shape of Pusosan is elegant, the Paengmagang River (白馬江) flowing gently this way and that is also lovely, and the distant chain of mountains of Ch'ŏngyang and Chŏngsan (靑陽定山), lined up like eyebrows, is also lovely. The landscape (江山) is a lovely landscape. But even that landscape gains its luster only when it finds its owner. The landscape of Puyŏ truly shines only when it has the cultured Paekche people (百濟人). Now that the Paekche people are gone, who will make that landscape shine? I saw the granary site beyond the Yŏngildae (迎日臺 - Sun-Welcoming Pavilion) east of Pusosan. Rice, wheat, and beans still remain, charred black into ash. From there, I turned back*, passed through fields lush with millet (禾黍油油) like the *text*, stopped for a while at the site of Songwŏltae, exited through the western gate of the palace fortress (宮城西門), spiraled down the thousand-fathom cliff (天仞絶壁), and reached the famous Koransa Temple (皐蘭寺), situated on a flat rock (盤石) at the foot of the cliff where the sound of the Paengmagang River water is heard.
On the ancient pine tree (老松) standing tall (几立) on the cliff before the gate, there is a magpie nest where magpies chirp, and though unseen below it, perhaps among the weeping willows, the song of orioles echoes up. Though historical materials (史料) regarding this temple's origin have been lost to smoke (煙滅), it was likely the guardian temple (守護寺) of the Paekche royal family, which revered Buddhism (佛法).
On the foundation stones carved with lotus flowers (蓮花) and the smoothly worn stone steps, there must be the footprints of nobles from that time. The palace ladies whose fragrant souls (芳魂) perished on Nakhwaam Rock probably prayed for final repose in this Dharma hall (法室).
From there, grabbing onto the green vines (碧蘿) and hugging the layered rocks (層岩), going several tens of paces, one finds Nakhwaam Rock, a rugged crag standing tall, piercing the dark sky. Though surely not the blood of the palace ladies from that time, water droplets drip from crevices in the rock, and below one's feet (足下), the swirling floodwaters roar (怒吼)<59>. Hoping to perhaps find a single flower (꼿), I scanned the surroundings, but there was only nameless grass from which a viper seemed likely to emerge at any moment.
Looking up at the rock and down at the water, I could not bear to stay long and hastily turned on my heel (踵).

*As the setting sun slants on the waters flowing down the Sabi River (泗자水),*
*Willow blossoms scatter where they call it Nakhwaam Rock (落花岩).*
*Ignorant children just play their flutes,*
*But it tears at the heart (창자) of the thoughtful traveler (나그내).*
*Nakhwaam Rock, Nakhwaam Rock, why are you silent?*

*On the old site of Puyŏ Castle (扶餘城), passed down for 700 years,*
*The green grass, greeting spring, is green as of old.*
*Where is the site of the shining Nine-Layered Palaces?*
*The whereabouts of the noble sovereign of ten thousand chariots (萬乘) are unknown.*
*Nakhwaam Rock, Nakhwaam Rock, why are you silent?*

*One night, sounds of weeping were heard from within the water's path,*
*Where did the flower-like palace ladies go?*
*Clutching the silk skirts given by their lord to their breasts,*
*Did they throw themselves into the deep waters of the Sabi River?*
*Nakhwaam Rock, Nakhwaam Rock, why are you silent?*

Resting my tired legs for a moment, I sought the P'yŏngje Pagoda (平濟塔) amidst the dense green bamboo (離離한 靑竹中). Although the name "Pagoda of Great Tang Pacifying Paekche" (大唐平百濟塔) is shameful, the culture of our ancestors who bequeathed such an eternal masterpiece to posterity is also something to be proud of. The pagoda, catching the slanted rays of the setting sun, seems ready to spread its wings and soar into the sky at any moment. How could such a design (構想) have arisen, and how could the skill (技術) be so adept? How noble must have been the spirit of the great artist who conceived such a harmonious, grand, and yet beautiful form (形象)? And how noble must have been the spirit of our ancestors of that time, who produced such great artists? Even if all historical records were to vanish in smoke (煙滅), as long as the P'yŏngje Pagoda stands majestically (几然) in the old capital of Paekche, the nobility and refinement of our people's (吾族) spirit will not be forgotten (닛치지 못할 것이다). Since a few drops of this ancestral blood surely flow in our veins today, I believe (밋는다) that if it meets new fertile soil (新沃士) and receives new sunlight (新日光), there will surely be a day when it blooms brilliantly (燦然히).
Who would readily believe (고지 들으랴) that the Koreans who produce the architecture and crafts of today's Korea (今日의 朝鮮) are the descendants of the Koreans who built the Paekche Pagoda? Today's Koreans have declined, become corrupt, ugly, powerless, and inactive (無能無爲).
Over the 700-800 years from the mid-Koryŏ period (高麗中葉) through the Yi Dynasty (李朝來), the valiant, wholesome, and noble spirit of the Three Kingdoms period (三國時代) completely vanished. The biased and narrow-minded Confucian ideology (偏僻挾隘한 儒敎思想) completely consumed (食盡) the vital energy of the Korean spirit. In the two thousand-odd years since the birth of Confucius's Confucianism (孔子의 儒敎), I have heard of those ruined by it, but I have not heard of those who prospered by it. Confucian thought may be material for cultivating the self and rectifying the mind (修身正心) in part, but it is definitely not the Way (道) to govern the state and bring peace to the world (治國平天下). Confucianism is truly a scorching sun (曝陽) that kills the vitality of the lively spirit and causes the sprouts (萌芽) of all civilization to wither and die (枯死).
The blame for making the Koreans of the Three Kingdoms period into the Koreans of today <60> lies solely in the tyranny (專橫) of Confucian ideology.
I want to erase Koryŏ (高麗) and the Yi Dynasty (李朝) from Korean history (朝鮮史). And I want to trace back to the Three Kingdoms (三國). Among them, the Korean history of the Yi Dynasty period is definitely not the Korean history of the Korean people, but the Korean history of certain slavish Koreans trying to abandon themselves and become sinicized (支那化). That is definitely not my history. I am a Korean of the Three Kingdoms period. I am a Koguryŏ person (高句麗人), a Silla person (新羅人), a Paekche person (百濟人). I do not know Koryŏ, and I do not know the Yi Dynasty.
In the same sense that the new civilization of the West (西洋) lies in the revival of ancient thought (古思想 復活), the new civilization of Korea will lie in the revival of the Three Kingdoms period. Ah! I cannot bear wanting to return to the old days of Sabi Castle (泗자城). Looking again and again at the P'yŏngje Pagoda, I yearn for the ancestors of former days (昔日).

The sun hanging over Pusan Mountain (浮山) is red as blood. Evening mist has settled on the willows lining the banks of the Sabi River (泗자水). What do the crows, crying as they circle back over Panwŏlsŏng's heights (半月城頭), lament? After the evening meal (夕飯), I rented a bicycle and sought out the Paekche royal tombs (百濟王陵) and Paekche-era burial grounds outside the East Gate of Panwŏlsŏng. Before the three royal tombs buried in the twilight green grass, I shed hot tears of reminiscence (懷古), and in the northern mound (北邙) of numerous stone coffins (累累한 石槨), I stirred feelings of impermanence (無常). Ah—longed-for capital of Sabi. Sabi capital reduced to ashes. Tragic Sabi capital. Desolate Sabi capital. Sabi capital that tears at the heart of a young poet a thousand years later.
I boarded a boat. Our boat left Kyuamjin (窺岩津). This is where the merchant ships (商船) and warships (兵艦) of old Paekche used to ply. It's where merchant ships from Tang (唐), Japan (日本), and Annam (安南 - Vietnam), fully loaded (滿載) with various goods (各色物貨), converged (輻湊). The strange rocks (奇岩) of Chawŏndae (自溫台) are renowned today as the place where King Ŭija dallied (逸遊), but in those days, they were probably famous as the Farewell Rock (離別岩). When the enterprising and lively Paekche people roamed far and wide, finding the world too small, heading east today and west tomorrow (今日 東 明日 西), there must have been many beautiful women who shed red tears (紅淚) upon this rock. I too left Kyuamjin with the feeling of a Paekche person setting off for Tang. Gazing once more at the emotionally evocative Pusosan, the single-leaf boat (一葉遍舟) drifts leisurely down the middle of the Sabi River amidst the sound of orchids and irises (芝蘭叢소리). Without a speck of wind (一點風) or a speck of cloud (一點雲), not a single wave stirs (一波が不動), only the fine willows (細柳) on both banks are shrouded in mist. Occasionally, a nameless fish leaps, shattering the reflected mountain shadow (倒映한 山影).
The water is lovely, the green mountains are lovely, and the white clouds floating in the blue sky are lovely. All are scenes from a painting (畵中), sentiments from a poem (詩中).
Coincidentally, the three people sharing the boat are all extraordinary. A blind, unmarried old man playing the Tongsŏ (洞蕭 - vertical flute) and an old man with white hair and a tattered hat playing the Haegŭm (奚琴 - two-stringed fiddle) are marvelous enough, but sharing the boat with a graceful (말낙한) beauty, lightly made-up and plainly dressed (淡粧素服), appearing just past her prime (年光이 2,8이 넘엇슬), is an even more wondrous fate (奇緣).
I requested a piece from the two musicians (樂人). They gladly consented and played several immortal tunes (仙曲) in harmony (和秦). The beauty with tear-stained eyes (淚痕) also moved her willow-like eyebrows (柳眉) and listened intently, then, perhaps unable to overcome the rising excitement (흥), beat time once (擊節1番) and sang a verse of "Changsengsul kŏjin mari" (長生術거진 말이 - The Art of Longevity is a Lie), and then at my further request:

*In the deep night of Panwŏlsŏng (半月城), what is that blaze of fire?*
*Do the 3,000 court ladies perish at Nakhwaam Rock?*
*Oh, green willows (楊柳) by the water's edge, so indifferent.*

*The landscape is lovely, but who are the people?*
*Magpies nest at Chawŏndae Taewangp'o,*
*Unable to bear the absence of King Ŭija now.*

*Only barley lies yellow where Sabi Castle's (泗자 城) palaces stood,*
*Where can one find the prosperity of that time?*
*Only the setting sun slants on the numerous tombs (累累한 무덤) outside the East Gate (東門).*

The beauty sang three verses consecutively, her voice trembling (소리를 떠러가며). The boatman who had been rowing had also stopped his oars before long, and the boat drifted along on its own with the current. After a while (이윽고), a gust of wind (一陣風) swept over the river; was it the beautiful spirits (넉시) of Nakhwaam, dozing in the water for a thousand years, awakened by this song? The boat also rounded a bend in the water, and a small peak (峯), tempting one to place it on a desk, came into view, with a thatched house pressed against the rock (岩上) as if stuck there. The boatman said we had reached Kanggyŏng (江景).<61>

**Summary**

Yi Kwang-su recounts his rainy journey to Puyŏ, the ancient capital of Paekche. He reflects deeply on the lost glory of Paekche, vividly imagining the kingdom's vibrant life and its tragic fall, especially the story of the court ladies at Nakhwaam Rock. He visits historical sites like royal tombs, the P'yŏngje Pagoda, and Koransa Temple, lamenting the stark contrast between past grandeur and present desolation. Yi strongly criticizes the influence of Confucianism during the Koryŏ and Yi dynasties, blaming it for the decline of the "noble" Korean spirit evident in the Three Kingdoms period, and expresses a fervent desire to revive that earlier spirit. The article concludes with a poignant boat trip down the Paengmagang River, filled with music and reflections on the landscape and history.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, the article is revealing in several ways. Yi Kwang-su's romanticization of the pre-Yi Dynasty past (specifically Paekche) and his harsh critique of Confucianism and the Yi Dynasty itself reflect a common nationalist sentiment of the era seeking sources of national pride and explanations for perceived contemporary weakness, often framed in contrast to a glorious, ancient past. His desire to "erase" Koryŏ and the Yi Dynasty and identify solely with the Three Kingdoms period demonstrates a radical reinterpretation of history aimed at spiritual revitalization, a theme significant under colonial rule. Historians might find Yi's specific framing of Paekche's "culture," his explicit blame placed on Confucianism for Korea's decline, and the underlying call for a national rebirth based on ancient models particularly interesting in the context of intellectual responses to colonialism and burgeoning nationalism.

文人의 半島八景紀行 第一篇, 아아·落花岩
						李光洙
퍼붓는 비를 무릅쓰고 早朝에 利仁을 떠낫다. 利仁서 扶餘 50餘里間은 대개 山陜길이엿다. 坦坦한 신작로가 狹長한 山陜間을 다라난 것이 마치 一條淸流와 갓햇다. 게다가 道路左右 엽흐로 어까시아가 쭉 느러서서 그 風致잇슴이 비할 데가 업섯다.
나는 산 속 빗탈 속으로 터벅터벅 혼자 거러간다. 좌우 靑山에는 비 소리와 버레 소리 뿐이로다. 1250년전 百濟서울 半月城이 羅唐聯合軍의 一炬에 灰燼이 되던 날 밤에 自溫台大王浦에서 놀든 흥도 깨지 못한 萬乘의 임군 義慈王께서 太后太子와 함께 態川으로 蒙塵하던 길이다. 그때가 7월이라니까 아마 이와 가치 버레소리를 드럿슬 것이다. 집행이를 멈추고 웃둑 서서 좌우를 도라보며 녜와 가튼 靑山에는 말굽소리가 들리는 듯 하야 焮然한 감회를 禁지 못하엿다. 新地境고개라는 고개 마르턱에 올나 설 적에 문득 들리는 杜鵑數聲은 참말 遊子의 애를 끈는 듯 하엿다. 이리 돌고 저리 돌고 이 고개 넘고 저 고개 넘어 느러진 버들 그늘에 서너 茅屋이 暮雨에 잠겨잇슴을 보앗다. 막걸리 파는 미인에게 무른즉 이 지명은 「왕자터」요 扶餘서 20里라 한다.
목도 渴하고 시장도 함으로 메어기 안주에 막걸리 한 잔을 마섯다. 문 압헤 淸江이란 강이 잇슴애 메어기가 만히 잡힌다 한다.
거기서 약 10리를 오면 扶餘郡 欲內面 佳增里에 유명한 有史以前의 묘지가 잇다.
前年 총독부의 屬托을 바든 黑板博士의 鑑定에 의하건대 少不下 4천년 전 것이라 한다. 그때 엇더한 사람들이 엇더케 사랏는지는 물어도 古墳에 대답이 업건만은 亂裡에 일허바렷든 先祖의 墳墓를 보는 듯하야 暫間 徘徊不忍去하엿다.
扶餘 羅福里라 하는데도 4천년 전 주민의＜58＞ 유적이 잇다한즉 이 지방에는 퍽 古代부터 문화가 열엿든 듯 하다. 차차 압히 툭 터지며 眼界가 널버 간다. 대체 그 굉장한 문명을 가젓든 百濟의 서울이 엇더한 것이던가 하는 생각에 자연 거름이 빨너진다. 동시에 이 고개 넘어서 行軍吹打가 들릴 듯 하고 하날에 날아오르는 半月城의 巍峨燦爛한 궁전과 泗泚水上에 管絃의 太平曲이 喇喨히 들릴 것 갓다. 그러나 그 고개를 너머서면 여전히 거치른 여름풀과 모 심는 농부뿐이로다.
길까에 말 업시 누운 지추돌과 날근 碑가 행인의 눈물을 재촉할 뿐이로다. 徘徊數瞬하야 감개무량하면서 扶蘇山 東便 모통이를 도라 草屋 2, 3천이 寂寂히 누어잇는 소위 扶餘邑內에 다달엇다. 「이것이 扶餘든가.」함은 初來者의 누구나 發하는 감탄이라 한다.
이것이 일즉 泗泚 서울터이라고야 누라서 미드리요. 인사를 미들 수 업다 하건만은 이러토록 심하랴.

이튼날 아츰 百濟의 舊蹟구경을 떠낫다. 바로 녯 憲兵隊 構內에 石槽 2개가 노혓다. 이것은 百濟의 귀인이 목욕하던 것이다. 1개는 다리 뻣고 안끼 조흔리만하고 1개는 반듯이 눕기 조흐리만 하다.
나는 한창적 羅馬人을 연상하엿다. 그러케 百濟人은 번적하게 살앗다.
扶蘇山은 山이라기 보다 罔이다. 羅馬의 七罔山이란 엇든 것이지 모르나 아마 이러할 것이다. 길에 盖瓦쪼각이 한벌 깔렷다. 그날 밤 火焰에 튄 것이다. 御爐의 香내 맛던 것이오. 南薰의 太平歌 듯든 것이다. 여기는 大闕자리요 저기는 妃嬪이 잇던데요 달 맛는 迎月台 달 보내는 送月台도 여기요 공 차든 蹴鞠場이 여기 歌舞하든 무슨 殿이 여기 150년의 榮華가 一夜에 살아질 때 扶蘇山 全體가 왼통 불길이 되여 7월의 밤하늘과 泗자 水를 비최일 때 그때의 悲壯悽慘한 광경이 눈을 감으면 보이는 듯 하다. 그때의 榮華의 꿈에 취하엿든 九重의 궁궐이 왼통 驚惶하야 울며불며 업더지며 잡바지며 이리 뛰고 저리 굴고 하든 樣 꼿가치 아름답고 細柳가치 軟弱한 수백의 妃嬪이 黑煙을 헤치고 送月臺의 빗긴 달에 落花岩으로 가든 樣 숫고개와 泗자 水로 폭풍가치 말 몰려오든 羅唐聯合軍의 乘勝한 고함소리가 귀를 기우리면 들리는 듯 하다.
나는 靑草우에 펄석 주저 안저서 힘껏 그때 일을 상상하려 하엿다. 내 눈 압헤는 그때의 半月城이 잇다. 그때의 궁전이 잇고 그때의 사람이 잇다. 그때의 색채가 보이고 그때의 음성이 들린다. 나도 그때의 사람이 되여서 노래하고 춤춘다. 황량한 半月城池의 거칠은 풀이 보일 뿐이다. 扶蘇山의 모양도 얌전하거니와 비스듬이 저리 이리 흘러 도라가는 白馬江도 좃코 멀리 눈섭가치 둘러선 靑陽定山의 連山도 좃타. 江山은 조흔 江山이다. 그러나 그 강산도 그 주인을 어더야 빗이 난다. 扶餘의 강산은 암만해도 文雅한 百濟人을 어더가지고야 비로소 빗이 난다. 지금은 百濟人이 업슴애 그 강산을 뉘라서 빗내일가. 扶蘇山 동쪽 迎日臺 넘에 잇는 창고터를 보앗다. 아직도 쌀과 밀과 콩이 까맛케 재가 되여 남아잇다. 거기서 다시 발을 돌* 文字와 가치 禾黍油油한 밧흘 지나서 送月台자리에 한참 발을 멈추고 宮城西門을 빠저도라가 螺線狀으로 天仞絶壁을 다 내려가서 白馬江 물소리 들리는 절벽 밋 盤石 우에 잇는 것이 유명한 皐蘭寺다.
門前絶壁에 几立한 老松에는 까치둥지가 잇서 까치가 지저귀고 또 그 밋헤 보히지는 아니하나 아마 수양버들 속에는 꾀꼬리 소리가 울려 올나온다. 이 절의 來歷은 可考할 史料가 煙滅하엿스나 아마도 佛法을 尊崇한 百濟王室의 守護寺일 것이다.
蓮花를 아르색인 지츄돌이며 빤빤히 달아진 섬돌에는 당시 귀인의 발자국이 잇슬 것이다. 落花岩上에서 芳魂이 슬어진 궁녀들도 아마 이 法室에 최후의 명복을 빌엇슬 것이다.
거기서 碧蘿를 더위 잠고 層岩을 안고 돌아 數十 步를 가면 까마케 하날을 폭 찌르고 웃둑 선 울퉁불퉁한 바위가 落花岩이다. 설마 그때 궁녀의 피는 아니지만 바위틈으로서 물방울이 뚝뚝 떠러지고 足下에서는 소용돌이치는 장마 물이 怒吼＜59＞한다. 幸여 꼿 한 송이나 어들가하고 사방을 살폇스나 오직 동시에 독사가 지어나올뜻한 일홈 모를 풀이 잇다.
나는 우흐로 바위를 보고 아래로 물을 보다가 참아 오래 머물지 못하야 급히 踵을 돌렷다.

困한 다리를 잠간 쉬여 離離한 靑竹中에 平濟塔을 차잣다. 大唐平百濟塔이란 일홈은 수치언만은 如此한 萬古의 대걸작을 후세에 끼친 우리 祖先의 문화는 또한 자랑할 만하다. 석양을 빗기바든 탑은 즉시 날개를 버리고 半空으로 소사올 듯 하다. 엇더케 저러한 構想이 생기고 엇더케 저러케 기술이 능한고. 저러케 조화잇고 장중하고 그러고도 미려한 형상은 案出하는 그 大예술가의 정신은 얼마나 숭고하엿던고. 또 그러한 大예술가를 出하는 당시의 우리 祖先의 정신은 얼마나 숭고하엿던고. 역사의 모든 기록이 다 煙滅하고 말드라도 平濟塔이 几然히 百濟의 舊都에 섯는 동안 吾族의 정신이 숭고하고 세련됨은 닛치지 못할 것이다. 지금에 血管中에도 이 祖先의 혈액의 數滴이 흐를지니 이것이 新沃士를 맛나고 新日光을 바드면 반다시 燦然히 꼿을 피울 날이 잇슬 줄을 밋는다.
今日의 朝鮮의 건축과 공예를 出하는 朝鮮人이 百濟塔을 작성한 朝鮮人의 자손이라한들 뉘가 고지 들으랴. 今日의 朝鮮人은 쇠퇴하엿고 타락하엿고 추악하고 無能無爲하게 되고 말엇다.
高麗中葉以降으로 李朝來에 至하는 7,8백년간에 三國時代의 勇壯하고 건전한 숭고하던 정신은 왼통 소멸되고 말앗다. 偏僻挾隘한 儒敎思想은 朝鮮人의 정신의 생기를 말끔 食盡하고 말앗다. 孔子의 儒敎가 生한지 2천여 년에 그것으로 망한 자가 잇슴을 드럿스나 흥한 자 잇슴을 듯지 못하엿다. 儒敎思想은 일부 修身正心의 자료는 되는지 모르되 결코 치국평천하의 道는 아니다. 유교는 진실로 潑剌한 정신의 활기를 죽이고 모든 문명의 萌芽를 枯死케하는 曝陽이다.
