Article Title: 畵家의 妻 (一幕) (The Painter's Wife (One Act))

Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

The Painter's Wife
						Written by Yun Paengnam
Time......Contemporary
Place......A studio
Characters
Yu Tongho......Painter
Kagyŏng......Yu's wife
Ku Yunhyŏp......Tongho's friend
Frame Shop Clerk
Old Woman

Scene......A meager studio. Though over two *kan* (間) wide, the room lacks any decoration worthy of the name. An easel, canvases, discarded painting fragments, paints, etc., lie about in disarray. A sense of decline permeates everything. However, standing out amidst this is a magnificent bed covered with a clean coverlet. This is a splendid bed decorated in a classic style, about which Tongho habitually boasts to others, saying, "I paint for the pleasure of this bed." Tongho is lying reclining on the bed, the Frame Shop Clerk is rummaging through canvases piled on a desk near the easel in the center of the room, and Kagyŏng is brewing tea at a table in a corner of the room.

CLERK: It’s six sheets in total, sir.

TONGHO: Is that all?

CLERK: Hahaha. ‘Is that all?’ you ask. You, the very gentleman who painted them, speak as if you were someone else, sir.

TONGHO: Well, since I just grab the brush thinking ‘this too is a painting,’ who knows how many sheets there ended up being? I just paint without thinking.

KAGYŎNG: (Looking this way) That's why I get so anxious. I tell him, why don't you just dash those things off any old way, what's the point of putting so much effort into them? But does he listen? And then later he complains about his head hurting or whatnot.

CLERK: That's enough. But madam, you really go too far with someone right in front of you. Calling them 'those things' or whatnot—money went into them, you know. Hahahaha.

TONGHO: Kagyŏng is right about that. Is everything art just because money went into it? If so, should we call the cheap imitation (뎀뿌라) watch chain hanging from your vest <124> a work of art because money went into it?

CLERK: Now you're looking down on me too much, sir. This is still supposedly 18-karat gold, you know.

KAGYŎNG: You wouldn't be able to boast like that at the pawn shop (典當局), would you? Hohoho.

CLERK: Oh, madam, really. Hehehehe. Whenever I come to this house, I end up losing a year of my life from the combined attack of the husband and wife.

TONGHO: Why live longer in this troublesome world anyway?

CLERK: It's true, isn't it? But really, we have a big problem.

TONGHO: What is it?

CLERK: Almost half the goods in the shop are gone, and with only about six sheets, I won't even know where they're tucked away. I came today expecting there to be at least twenty sheets, you see.

KAGYŎNG: Be that as it may, we can’t accept less than that price anymore. I mean, what profit is there at one won per sheet?

CLERK: Why bring that up again now? Is this the first time you've known the price? Do you know how much I get after buying them for one won and putting them in a frame? Barely five or six won, madam. Do you think I make much profit either?

TONGHO: No, I'm not saying you make a lot of money. Anyway, what I mean is, firstly, my health won't hold up if I pour my energy into that sort of thing.

CLERK: Well, yes. That is another matter, though.

KAGYŎNG: Whatever the case, the exhibition is approaching.

CLERK: Ye-es. So, are you exhibiting this time?

TONGHO: Let’s try submitting one. If I were to starve to death, people would probably scold me, saying, "What a pathetic person. He should have at least painted cheap signboards (펭키화) to make a living." But now that I'm painting cheap signboards because I can't survive, haven't I earned the nickname "Tongho has fallen"? Just out of spite, I'll put out a real work of art for once and... (He gets extremely agitated and his words catch in his throat.) (Kagyŏng prepares tea, brings it over, and places it before the CLERK.)

KAGYŎNG: Firstly, I'm so resentful and frustrated I could die. It seems it’s all my fault that this gentleman has fallen and doesn't get a penny from his family. They say it's because he married the likes of a cafe waitress (카페-의 웨이트레쓰) that his family doesn't even look his way, that it's my fault he has nothing to eat and has to paint cheap pictures (펭키화), and even that his illness is my fault.

TONGHO: What's the point of telling him that kind of story? As if it's pleasant to hear.

KAGYŎNG: It just came up in conversation.

CLERK: Can you make sense of what people in the world say?

KAGYŎNG: My ears are sharp when it comes to my enemies, you know.

TONGHO: (Jumps up abruptly and paces in front of the bed) Of course, I must paint it. I must put those fellows' noses out of joint.

CLERK: Anyway, congratulations. Well then (he gathers up the six cheap paintings (펭키화)), I'll take these with me.

KAGYŎNG: Then the advance payment is all settled?

CLERK: That's right.

TONGHO: Why are you leaving already?

CLERK: While I'm lingering here like this, do you know how many customers might have come looking for these? For me, these easily sold items are my bread and butter, rather than some magnificent painting that might sell once a year. This seems like the greatest art form. Hehehehehehe.

KAGYŎNG: Whether you go straight or crooked, as long as you get to Namdaemun Gate.

CLERK: Making money is my first priority.

TONGHO: Right, you're exactly right, Kagyŏng. Hahaha. We who struggle to paint unsold pictures are ultimately fellows who can't get to Namdaemun, neither straight nor crooked. Hahaha.

KAGYŎNG: Hohohoho.

CLERK: Goodbye. <125>

TONGHO: See you again.

KAGYŎNG: This briefcase.

CLERK: Oh, dear. Right.
(The clerk takes the black leather folding briefcase and exits.)

KAGYŎNG: He's an interesting person.

TONGHO: Witty, isn't he? He originally worked as an outreach agent for a newspaper advertising department. He's the nephew of the person who used to manage our household effects.

KAGYŎNG: Ah, so that's why he's so eloquent.

TONGHO: Ah-ah- It's starting to hurt today. (He clutches his head and bows it.)

KAGYŎNG: Then please rest quickly.

TONGHO: No (He coughs repeatedly, suppressing a rising cough). Even if this work becomes my final piece... (coughs again).

KAGYŎNG: (With a worried expression, supporting her husband's head) Don't say such things. You always say things like that whenever you get the chance.

TONGHO: No, it's a painter's wish to die holding his brush.

KAGYŎNG: Anyway, rest for a bit before you continue. Besides, didn't you promise to get another model and paint her?

TONGHO: Well, initially I used you as a model because I had no money. Now that the money situation is sorted out, I hate to ask you to strip naked, so I made that promise, but...

KAGYŎNG: Why the change of heart again?

TONGHO: It's not a change of heart, but how good can a model who comes for money be? It's only because this is Chosŏn, but she can't possibly match your physical beauty. And besides, wouldn't I have to discard what's almost finished and start again? Just pose for a few more days.

KAGYŎNG: Even if I agree to pose again, please rest today. I have an appointment to meet him this afternoon.

TONGHO: Him?

KAGYŎNG: Oh, I mean the school principal.

TONGHO: The person who arranged the money this time?

KAGYŎNG: Yes.

TONGHO: Why does he want to meet?

KAGYŎNG: I don't know. Since he asked me to come, I have to go, don't I?

TONGHO: I don't know whether to call it strange or peculiar, I just don't understand it at all.

KAGYŎNG: What?

TONGHO: A principal I don't even know readily puts up a hundred won. Just hearing a word from you... Kagyŏng.

KAGYŎNG: ...?...

TONGHO: You haven't deceived me, have you? I don't care about honor anymore. I hate painting too. All I need is one pure Kagyŏng. Don't blame me. Yesterday, after you left, while I was looking at your nude painting (라체화), an unspeakable anguish and turmoil arose in me... I realized I must possess Kagyŏng's body and soul completely.

KAGYŎNG: Don't get too agitated. It's bad for your illness. Thinking such thoughts is also because of your illness. Aren't my heart and body always devoted to you?

TONGHO: I know that too. It's me who thinks such thoughts even while knowing it.

KAGYŎNG: When your nerves get frayed, you think like that even more. Now, stop thinking such sentimental thoughts and take a nap. I'll be back quickly. Old Woman!

OLD WOMAN: (From outside) Yes-.

KAGYŎNG: Come here for a moment.

OLD WOMAN: Yes-. (Opens the door halfway and pokes only her head in).

KAGYŎNG: Look at this.
(She takes out a wallet from her waist sash and opens it. Only then does the old woman enter the room and take a couple of steps to stand before Kagyŏng).
Go to the store and buy some apples to give to the master. I'm <126> going out for a bit.

OLD WOMAN: Yes-. Apples, you mean the ‘Kokkaeng'i’ ones?

KAGYŎNG: Kokkaeng'i?

TONGHO: She means Kukkwang (국광 Ralls Janet), I suppose. Hahaha.

KAGYŎNG: Hohoho. You too, Old Woman... Yes, buy those Kokkaeng'i.

OLD WOMAN: Apples have all sorts of names... How am I supposed to know? (Exits).

KAGYŎNG: Well then, I'll be back quickly.

TONGHO: Be back before evening.

KAGYŎNG: Why would I stay out so late?
(Kagyŏng picks up her handbag and goes outside. Tongho blankly watches her retreating figure, then, as if suddenly remembering something, gathers his painting tools and sits down facing the canvas. A knocking sound...)

TONGHO: Who is it?

KU: (Opening the door and entering) So you are here.

TONGHO: Knocking so formally.

KU: Hey, man. Before, it was fine to just barge right in, but now that you have a madam, can I still do that?

TONGHO: Sit down.

KU: Mm. By the way, what’s going on? The madam isn't here, and the old woman has also vanished.

TONGHO: Watch your words, please.

KU: If I watch my words, will you buy me drinks?

TONGHO: Who knows if I will.

KU: Hey, forget it. Just don't trample on me. If you bow your head even a little, worldly fellows think you're easy pickings and look down on you.
(At this moment, the Old Woman enters with apples on a tray, places it on the table, and exits.)
Hey, look at this. Instead of drinks, apples have appeared. This is a miracle. But when did you order them?

TONGHO: My wife sent them. Peel one.

KU: (Picks one up, briefly rubs it with his handkerchief, then bites into it peel and all) Did the wife go out somewhere today too?

TONGHO: ...?... She went out. By the way, where did you meet her yesterday?

KU: Ah- no, it wasn't a meeting. You probably didn't see me, but I saw her getting into a taxi in front of the Chaedŭng Building (재등삘딩) inside Namdaemun Gate.

TONGHO: Alone?

KU: No, not alone. I passed by quickly, so I didn't get a good look.

TONGHO: (Hmm.)
(Ku crunches the apple and stands up to look at the painting on the canvas.)

KU: Is this what you're submitting this time?

TONGHO: ...

KU: I'm a layman, but when it comes to using color, nobody can match you. And the model is a magnificent model too.

TONGHO: Chaedŭng Building, you say? You mean the one by the Subakdari bridge?

KU: Yes. But you've only done the sketch (떼싸인 - dessin); when will you apply the color?
(Tongho doesn't answer, lowers his head, and clutches it with both hands.)

KU: What's wrong? Are you feeling unwell?
(Tongho, head still bowed, nods.)
Then lie down. Yes, lie down. I'll be going. Hey, Old Woman...

OLD WOMAN: Yes-.

KU: Well, I'll come again soon.
(He takes a few steps, meets the Old Woman about to open the door, stops her with his hand.)
If anyone comes, tell them he's not here. He's feeling unwell. (He disappears outside with her.)
(Tongho lifts the head he had bowed. His face is pale, and his eyes have a murderous glint. His bitten lips are bloodless. <127>
With trembling hands, he takes a knife from the desk drawer and slashes the canvas on the easel to ribbons. Then he stomps on the frame, breaking it. Then he picks up the easel and smashes the paint pots to pieces. A whirlwind of fury...
Startled by the crashing sounds, the Old Woman opens the door and stands frozen, her eyes wide with terror.)

TONGHO: Old Woman.

OLD WOMAN: Yes, yes.

TONGHO: Take all this, throw it into the furnace, and set it on fire.
(At this moment, Kagyŏng returns from outside and, hearing the strange noises, rushes into the room. Simultaneously, she stares in horror at the scene inside the room and stands stiffly like a totem pole.)

TONGHO: (Stares intently at Kagyŏng. A few seconds of dreadful silence.) You finally deceived me after all. Get out. Get out. Hey- you filthy woman!
(Tongho grabs whatever painting debris is within reach and throws it at Kagyŏng. Kagyŏng dodges it here and there.)

KAGYŎNG: (Her pale face shows pity for her husband. Softly) Honey, why are you so agitated, saying I deceived you? Huh?

TONGHO: Still trying to deceive me? I'm not the one to be fooled anymore.

KAGYŎNG: What on earth did I do? Calm yourself and tell me.
(The Old Woman begins gathering the scattered items and placing them on the table.)

TONGHO: You went to the Chaedŭng Building yesterday.

KAGYŎNG: I went.

TONGHO: Do I not know who is there? That fellow named Pilburok'a (삘부로카-) you were crazy about when you were in Monaco. Where did you go with that fellow? Where did you go, I ask! The hundred won was also obtained from that fellow. And in return for that, hey, you filthy woman!

KAGYŎNG: (Slightly relieved, but with a sad smile) I went to the Chaedŭng Building. But that fellow you suspect is currently serving time in Kŭmgye-dong (금게동) for extortion.

TONGHO: ... ? ...

KAGYŎNG: I went there to borrow the hundred won from the person who runs the barbershop in the basement of the Chaedŭng Building.

TONGHO: Where did the principal go? Then why did you lie, saying it was the principal?

KAGYŎNG: That person *is* the principal. The owner who runs the barbershop is also the principal of Ch'angdong Academy (창동학원).

TONGHO: ...

KAGYŎNG: Honey, think again. When I came to you, I didn't expect this kind of impoverished life, did I? To use harsh words, you bought me. We came to live together out of affection, so why do you suspect me like this?

TONGHO: Still, there's one more thing.

KAGYŎNG: You mean there's something left you don't trust? The reason I looked for him again today was because he advised me to help my husband, even if it meant working as a female instructor (교부) at the silk reeling company (제사회사) in Samp'ant'ong (삼판통) outside Namdaemun, commuting only in the afternoon. So yesterday I went and met only the president, and today I went and received the letter of appointment (사령서). (Takes the letter of appointment from her handbag and gives it to her husband) Take a look at this.
(Tongho takes the letter of appointment and glances at it briefly.
Suddenly, an indescribable expression of confusion appears on his face. And the next moment, with a groan of "Uhm-," he collapses backward.)

KAGYŎNG: (Rushing over and supporting his upper body) Honey... Hey, Old Woman!

OLD WOMAN: Yes- (rushes over). Wordlessly, the two lift Tongho and lay him on the bed. Violent coughing. Kagyŏng covers her husband's mouth with a handkerchief.

KAGYŎNG: Old Woman. Quickly go get a basin of cold water.
(The Old Woman hurries out.)
(From the street outside, the cry of a tofu seller is heard.)
—(Quietly, curtain)— <128>

**Summary**

This one-act play depicts the strained relationship between Yu Tongho, a proud but impoverished and ill painter, and his wife Kagyŏng, a former cafe waitress. Tongho struggles between artistic integrity and the need to paint cheap pictures ("p'engk'ihwa") for survival, blaming his "fall" partly on societal judgment of his marriage. His paranoia and jealousy, fueled by his illness and a friend's comment, peak when he suspects Kagyŏng of infidelity after she secretly borrows money and finds work to support them. He violently destroys his artwork, but Kagyŏng calmly reveals the truth, showing him her letter of appointment, causing Tongho to collapse. The play highlights the harsh realities of artists' lives, societal prejudice, marital mistrust exacerbated by poverty and illness, and a wife's quiet sacrifices.

From a historical perspective, the play offers insights into the economic struggles of artists in colonial Korea, forcing them into commercial work despised as "p'engk'ihwa." It reflects social hierarchies and prejudices, particularly towards women like Kagyŏng who came from a "cafe waitress" background. The mention of specific locations like the Chaedŭng Building, Namdaemun, Samp'ant'ong, and institutions like Ch'angdong Academy ground the story in 1930s Seoul (Keijō). The casual mention of someone imprisoned for extortion (공갈취재) and Kagyŏng seeking work in a silk factory (제사회사) hint at the socio-economic conditions and opportunities (or lack thereof) during the period.

						尹白南 作

具允爕......東浩의 친구
프레임 상점원
할멈
景......빈약한 화실. 넓이는 두어 間이 넘은 방이나 아모 장식다운 장식이 업는 방이다. 畵架 캄바쓰 그리다가 내던저 둔 그림조각 繪具 등이 난잡히 뇌여잇다. 모든 것에 추락한 빗이 가득하다. 그러나 그 중에 異彩를 띄인 것은 淨한 카바-가 씌여잇는 훌륭한 침대이다. 이 침대는 東浩가 日常 남더러 「나는 이 침대맛에 그림을 그리네」하고 자랑하는 고전식 장식으로 꾸민 훌륭한 침대이다. 東浩는 그 침대위에 비스듬이 누어 잇고 푸렘 상점원은 방 중앙에 노인 畵架 엽 책상 우에 싸히여 잇는 캄바쓰를 뒤적거리며 可敬이는 방 한 구석 테-불에서 차를 끄리고 잇다.
店員 『모두 여섯 장이올시다 그려.』
東浩 『그 박게 안되나.』
店員 『하하하. 그 박게 안되나 라니오. 그리신 양반께서 바루 남의 말 허시득히 허심니다 그려.』
東浩 『그것두 그림이라고 붓을 잡구보니까 몃 장이 됏는지 뉘 아나. 정신 업시 그리구만 잇게되니까.』
可敬 (이편을 돌아다보며) 그러기에 내가 아주 애가 씬다우. 그까진 것 아무러케나 그려 버리시지 그러케 정신은 드려서 무얼 허시느냐구 허면 드른체나 허신답딋가. 그러군 나중엔 머리가 압흐시니 어쩌니 허시지.
店員 『이만 헙니다. 그러치만 부인께서는 그저 사람을 압헤 노시구 너머 허십니다. 그까진 그림이니 무엇이니 돈이 드럿담니다. 하하하하.』
東浩 『그건 可敬이 말이 올아. 돈만 들면 다 예술인가. 그러면 자네 족끼에 매달린 뎀뿌라 시게ㅅ줄도 돈이 들엇스니 ＜124＞ 예술품이라구 그럴까.』
店員 『이건 너머 깔보심니다 그려. 이건 그래도 헌다헌 18금 이람니다.』
可敬 『典當局에 가면 큰소릴 못헐께 아뇨? 호호호.』
店員 『원 부인께서두. 허허허허 난 이 댁에 오면 내외분 련합공격에 한 살씩은 감수를 허구 감니다.』
東浩 『귀찬은 세상 더 살어 뭘헐라구.』
店員 『아닌게 아니라 그래요. 그런데 참 큰일 낫슴니다.』
東浩 『무에.』
店員 『가게에 잇는 물건은 거지 반 다 나가구 여섯 장쯤 가지고는 어느 구석에 백힌 줄도 모르게 되엿슴니다. 저는 오늘은 적어두 스무 장은 되엿스려니 허구 왓습죠.』
可敬 『그러나 저러나 인제는 그 갑 밧구는 못허겟습디다. 글세 한 장에 일원이면 무에 남을께 잇소.』
店員 『새삼스럽게 그 말슴은 왜 내심니까. 지금 처음 아신 갑시오니까. 일원내구 저이가 사가지고 가서 푸렘-에 끼어서 얼마를 밧는 줄 아심니까. 겨우 오원이나 륙원이람니다. 저인들 무에 남는 줄 아심니까.』
東浩 『아니 그야 자네가 돈을 만히 냉긴다는 말은 아니지. 하여튼 그런데다 정력을 드리다가는 첫재 내 건강이 보존을 못허겟다는 말이 아닌가.』
店員 『글세요. 그건 딴 문제 올시다 만은.』
可敬 『그건 어찌 됏던지 전람회가 갓가워 오니까요.』
店員 『녜에- 그럼 이번엔 내시게 됏슴니까.』
東浩 『어디 하나 내 보자지. 남들은 내가 만약에 굶어 죽엇다면 「못생긴 사람 가트니. 허다못해 펭키화라두 그리어 먹구 살어노코 볼일이지」이러케 핀잔을 줄 위인들이건마는 지금 내가 살 수가 업서서 펭키화를 그리어 먹는다구 「東浩는 타락햇서」가 내 별명이 되지 안엇나. 어디 분해서라두 내 한번 작품다운 작품을 내 노쿠(몹시 흥분해서 말이 탁 막힌다.)(可敬은 차를 만드러 가지고 와서 店員 압헤 노흐며.)
可敬 첫재 내가 분허구 절통해서 못살겟다우. 저 냥반이 타락을 허구 본 택에서 돈 한푼 안주게 된 것두 모두 나의 죄람니다 그려. 카페-의 웨이트레쓰 따위 허구결혼을 허기때문에 집에서 돌아다 본체두 허지 안케 된 게구 먹을께 업서서 펭키화를 그리게 된 것두 나 때문이구 심지어 저 냥반의 병환까지두 나 때문이라는 구려.』
