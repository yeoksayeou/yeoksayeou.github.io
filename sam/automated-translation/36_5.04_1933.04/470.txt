Publication Date: 1933년 04월 01일
Article Title: 능수버들 (Weeping Willow)
Author: 파인 (P'ain)
Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Screenplay)

Weeping Willow
						P'ain
Ch'unsam ― a twenty-year-old village boy
P'allyŏn ― an eighteen-year-old village maiden
Bandit ― his subordinates, many shepherd boys
Time ― Several hundred years ago, late spring

This is Ch'ŏnan Samgŏri (Cheonan Crossroads) in Ch'ungch'ŏng Province. Light green weeping willows, weary of their own charm, droop lazily, swaying gently in the southeast wind. Occasionally, they stand tall, as if, like people, the willow branches feel spring fatigue. Now, beneath this willow embankment, village boys and girls gather, forming a circle like a ring. Holding hands, they dance, spinning round and round.
Boy 1 (Carrying a drum, standing at the very front, strikes it impressively once—*Tung!*—then leaps like a deer and sings the lead verse in a fine voice).

Willows, willows, weeping willows
At Ch'ŏnan Samgŏri, weeping willows
When those willows turn green, life comes
When those willows wither, life goes
Eyara tteyara ehehe-yo
Tteyara eyara hehehe-ra

(The others join in, taking up the song, hopping one step together. The lead boy, getting more excited, beats the drum several times in quick succession).

Don't break those fresh green willows
Though weary of their charm, they shed tears
Until the willows of Samgŏri are green <129>
Let's not leave these rivers and mountains of ours
Eyara tteyara ehehe-yo
Tteyara eyara hehehe-ra

(The voices of the others, echoing like one, ring out ever more clearly. And as they spin, unknowingly spinning, the rustle of crimson skirts brushing the ground and the light *todadak todadak* sound of the boys' footsteps, falling in rhythm like bouncing pebbles, echo softly, quietly. As this happens, the faces of the group flush crimson. Just then, Ch'unsam comes from beyond the embankment, leading a yellow cow. He quickly ties the cow's rope to a riverside willow branch and comes running this way, shouting "Uwo uwo-". He carries an armful of azaleas, vividly blooming, likely picked from the mountain.)
Ch'unsam (Suddenly scattering the bundle of flowers over the girls' heads, takes the drum from the boy and puts it on) Hey kids, let me join in too. I'll sing a verse, you follow along. Alright... let's pass over a verse like passing over the lanterns on Buddha's Birthday in the fourth lunar month.

From the peak of Pugak Mountain
The sound of drums goes *dung dung dung*
At the Changan crossroads
Friends gather.
Chihwaja, wonderful! Chŏlsigu!
The friends we missed are gathering

(Along with the resonant melody, the drumbeat gets faster and fiercer, echoing powerfully through the mountains and rivers of Ch'ungch'ŏng Province. The scattered azaleas—red petals and green leaves—flutter down like snowflakes onto the foreheads, shoulders, and lustrous hair of the dancing girls. At that moment, Ch'unsam, as if struck by a thought, shyly approaches P'allyŏn, who had been quietly singing and following at the very back).
Ch'unsam ...... Did you leave your smile at home today? If you won't laugh, at least make an owl sound.
P'allyŏn ...... Who asked you to worry? Knowing only as much as a deer's tail, yet acting all high and mighty. Menfolk are like that...
Ch'unsam ...... Who are you relying on to talk so big? Just shut your little mouth. The cow will laugh.
P'allyŏn ...... If it laughs, it'll laugh at you, not me. Good grief!
(Just then, someone from behind shouts, "Save the marriage for later, just beat the drum! Even flowers wilt if handled too much," followed by a burst of laughter—*Wa-*. P'allyŏn's face turns red, and she spins away sharply. Ch'unsam, looking even more cheerful, grabs P'allyŏn's wrist and pulls her back to his previous spot.)
Ch'unsam ...... Hey, peers. Please pretend not to see, like a white seagull flying through white clouds. Alright, let's pass over another verse.

On Kwach'ŏn's Kwanak Mountain
Just because my cave isn't there
Did you think the fire was out?
Inside, a fire pillar ten thousand fathoms high
Chihwaja, wonderful! Chŏlsigu!
A fire ignites on Kwach'ŏn's Kwanak Mountain

(Just as this song is about to end, an arrow suddenly flies from somewhere—*p'urŭrŭk*—and strikes Ch'unsam squarely in the chest—*p'ok!*. Ch'unsam collapses, uttering a single cry—*At!*. From over there, bandits wielding spears charge forward shouting—*Wa-*. The boys and girls, startled by this sight, scatter in tears. In an instant, the playground becomes a scene of ghastly carnage. Only P'allyŏn stays, holding the fallen Ch'unsam, quickly pulling out the arrow and wiping the flowing blood with the hem of her skirt.)
P'allyŏn ...... Ch'unsam. Ch'unsam. Get a hold of yourself. Your senses.
Ch'unsam ...... (Twisting in pain) Which bastards were they? Ugh, how frustrating. <130>
P'allyŏn ...... Only a little blood came out. Quick, get on my back. Let's go to the riverside. Go drink some water. Ch'unsam, even if it hurts, open your eyes. Get a hold of yourself.
Ch'unsam ...... Unng. It doesn't hurt. It's frustrating. If I'd just held on a bit longer, these bastards... Who shot it anyway?
P'allyŏn ...... Hey, if you shout like that, more blood will spurt out. Look! Blood is coming from your mouth too. Quick, get on my back. Let's go to the riverside. Who would shoot? It must be those wretched bandits. They were always after you...
(As P'allyŏn tries to lift Ch'unsam, bandits suddenly surround them from behind, striking the ground forcefully with the tips of their spears and roaring.)
Bandit 1 ...... You wench, step aside. We haven't slept well for a long time, but now we've caught him. (He roughly pushes P'allyŏn aside, throwing her down, and with the others, drags Ch'unsam like a slaughtered dog to a nearby willow tree trunk and ties him up. Then, searching his body, they find one bow and three arrows at his waist.)
Bandit 1 ...... You're Ch'unsam, aren't you? We've been looking for you for a long time. They said you go around day and night singing wicked songs, inciting people from village to village, saying "Strike the bandits, let's strike the bandits." So, how much striking did you manage to do? Sing that rousing song once more. Let us hear it too.
Ch'unsam ...... (Glares and grinds his teeth).
Bandit 1 ...... Unng, you don't want to? Then forget it. By the way, where are all your comrades? Buried in which mountain?
Ch'unsam ...... ...
Bandit ...... How many are there?
Ch'unsam ...... ...
Bandit ...... Can't you speak? Tell us. Before you become dew on a spear tip.
(Saying this, he strikes Ch'unsam's head hard with the spear shaft.)
Ch'unsam ...... What good would it do you to know? Know this: if you are ten, we are a hundred; if you are a thousand, we number over ten thousand. If you have spears, we have arrows. The day one arrow flies from my bow, a thousand, ten thousand arrows will fly from the hands of all the Paeksŏng (百姓, common people) in this village! Do you know what happens to this village after your bandit gang sweeps through? Houses are burned, grain is plundered, women are raped... Because of you bastards, there isn't a single day this village is free from weeping. The only things that don't cry in this village are the willows at Samgŏri and the stones on the embankment. Everything that breathes cries out in agony. Everyone lives because they cannot die.
Bandit ...... Are you angry? If you're angry, then resist. Build up the strength not to be robbed and attack us. Live wearing hides like cow leather that don't cry out under our whips, or come having built the strength to keep what's yours, say what you want to say, and do what you want to do. It's strength against strength. Hahaha... Are you angry?
Ch'unsam ...... Alright. A thunderbolt will strike that mouth of yours. Heaven hears the resentment of the people.
Bandit ...... Letting this punk run his mouth like this. Aish, hurry up and tell us. Which mountain are you bastards hiding in? Mudŭng Mountain? Ch'ilbo Mountain? Hurry up and tell!
Ch'unsam ...... (Bites his lip) I don't know!
Bandit ...... You're the leader and you don't know? Let's see if you can endure without telling.
(The bandits violently break his arrows and begin beating him mercilessly with spear shafts and willow branches. P'allyŏn, seeing this, cries out loudly, rushes over, and shields Ch'unsam with her own body.)
P'allyŏn ...... You bastards, hit me! Egu, Ch'unsam, Ch'unsam. P'allyŏn is here. Help! They're beating someone to death here!
Ch'unsam ...... P'allyŏn, get away from here. You run away. Quick.
P'allyŏn ...... Let's go together. Let's go together.
Ch'unsam ...... Go first. Run away. Run... run...
(Ch'unsam, unable to withstand the beating, his voice growing faint like a mosquito's buzz, spits out a clot of blood and lets his head hang down. P'allyŏn, dangling from the bandits' arms, tries to snatch the rods and pounds on their chests. Just then, the Bandit Leader, who had been watching from afar, <131> steps forward and strikes Ch'unsam's head hard with his pipe stem.)
Leader ...... Aah, satisfying. That punk's spirit is broken now. Keep beating him to death. Make an example of him, show the villagers the fate of a struggling rebel. When he's dead, don't forget, display his head on the main road at Samgŏri as a warning (호수하렷다).
P'allyŏn ...... (Clinging to the leader's chest, bites his chest) You murderous bastard!
Leader ...... This wench. This wicked wench. What kind of girl is this?
P'allyŏn ...... (Spits—*T'oe!*) Do you think you'll die a clean death?
Leader ...... This wench, this wench! This damn wench! Tie her up too and beat her.
P'allyŏn ...... (Sticks out her tongue mockingly).
Leader ...... That girl's fiery. Goodness, she looks pretty but she's quite fierce. Quick, drag her away over there. These struggling wenches.
(The bandits rush forward and seize P'allyŏn to drag her away. P'allyŏn resists, clinging desperately to Ch'unsam's neck, crying.)
P'allyŏn ...... Ch'unsam, are you dead? Are you really dead? At least open your eyes. Look at me just once. I'm standing right here beside you.
Ch'unsam ...... (As if talking in his sleep) Go quickly. Run away.
P'allyŏn ...... Please, open your eyes. Egu, you're bleeding from your arm too. Cruel world. May fire lanterns fall from the sky. May fire lanterns fall on the heads of these bandit bastards.
(The bandits now pull P'allyŏn away with more force. They are separated, then cling together again, separated, then cling together again. But a frail woman's strength could not prevail in the end. Dragged away by the bandits, she is forced over there.)
P'allyŏn ...... (Stumbling with large steps, gritting her teeth) You bastards, why are you doing this? You devils. T'oe... T'oe! Ch'unsam, Ch'unsam, P'allyŏn is here. I'm by your side. I'll always be by your side. Ch'unsam, Ch'unsam, open your eyes.
Ch'unsam ...... (Only then realizing she's being dragged away) Ah! P'allyŏn, where are you going? Where are you going? P'allyŏn, P'allyŏn, where are you going, leaving me behind? P'allyŏn...
P'allyŏn ...... (Crying) Ch'unsam, Ch'unsam...
(P'allyŏn is dragged away completely. Beside Ch'unsam, a single bandit stands guard with a spear. Willow leaves, carried by the wind, fall one by one, covering Ch'unsam's face. Ch'unsam, like an unconscious person, faintly calls P'allyŏn's name, then falls silent, his strength apparently exhausted. From a distant mountain, a cuckoo cries a few times.)
...... Stage darkens for a moment ......

(P'allyŏn appears from beyond the embankment, staggering like someone who has lost her mind. With a piece of firewood she was carrying, she strikes the guard—*ttak!*—who was leaning against a zelkova tree trunk, dozing. The guard collapses on the spot without a word. P'allyŏn rushes to Ch'unsam's side, unties the ropes binding him, cradles him on her lap, and busily massages his cold limbs. Then she runs to the stream, scoops up cold water with both hands, and gives it to him to drink.)
Ch'unsam ...... (After a while, opens his eyes, sits up abruptly, realizes the ropes are undone and he is free, and quickly grabs P'allyŏn's wrist, pulling her).
Hey, let's go. Let's run away. Why suffer a dog's death? Quick, P'allyŏn—let's go.
P'allyŏn ...... (Stares intently at Ch'unsam's happy face, fighting back tears) No.
Ch'unsam ...... Let's go quickly. They'll come again. Those bastards. Look there. I hear dogs barking in the distance. I hear footsteps too. Let's go quickly before those bastards come.
P'allyŏn ...... I don't want to. You go alone.
Ch'unsam ...... Why? What are you saying?
P'allyŏn ...... I don't want to go. I am now a body that cannot go beside you.
Ch'unsam ...... Are you crazy? What are you saying?
P'allyŏn ...... I now have no more wishes in this world. I am a body that has no face to meet my parents, you, or the village children. I'm already... I'm already... (She points to her own body with her finger. <132> Her skirt is stained with blood and torn to shreds, her hair completely disheveled. Ch'unsam finally understands everything. He grinds his teeth.)
Ch'unsam ...... Alright. So you mean you—your body—was defiled by that bastard, the Bandit Leader, that enemy. (Clenches both fists and trembles violently) Eit, you dirty wench! If only you hadn't had feelings for him, couldn't you have resisted, no matter how he attacked! Eit, you dirty wench. And if you couldn't resist, why didn't you just die then? Was your life so precious? Era, I'm wrong to even speak to you. Go back quickly to the bandit's den, serve him well, and live happily. (In his anger, Ch'unsam kicks P'allyŏn's side and runs off without looking back. P'allyŏn watches Ch'unsam's retreating figure blankly, without shedding a single tear.)
Ch'unsam ...... (Clenching his fists as he runs, suddenly turns back and runs towards her) Hey P'allyŏn, was that your true heart? Did you do that willingly?
P'allyŏn ...... (Closes her eyes and remains silent).
Ch'unsam ...... Hey P'allyŏn, say something. A clever girl like you, couldn't you have fought him off? Couldn't you have escaped that situation? What on earth happened? (He grabs his own head wildly with both hands) Then did that monstrous bastard entice you, saying he'd spare Ch'unsam's life if you obeyed him? It probably seems like that. Even so, you wench, if you did that, you are an even more worthless girl. You misjudged me. No matter how pathetic Ch'unsam is, he wouldn't go so far as to save his own life by selling his woman's chastity. Saving my life by selling my woman's flesh? T'oe! Eit, disgusting. Tie me back to that tree. Tie me up, and you, hurry back to the bandit's den! Quick, get out of my sight. Rather than looking at you, I'd be more at peace dying tied to that tree, pecked by crows.
P'allyŏn ...... (Stares intently at Ch'unsam, then suddenly collapses, finally weeping. Ch'unsam, unable to contain the anger rising in his chest, clenches his fists and paces around her. Then he grabs P'allyŏn's wrist again.) Let's go! I spoke too harshly. If only I endure! No one will know, and it will be as if nothing happened. Hey, alright. I will avenge you. The day your vengeance is taken, won't that also be the day your body becomes clean again? I thought wrongly. In this wretched world where everything is twisted, broken, and ruined, how could I expect only your body to remain pure? Alright, fine. Fine. Everything is fine. Let's close our eyes to the past. Besides, isn't it the same kind of crippling, whether my body was pierced by an arrow just now, or your body was defiled by dirty hands? Let the crippled live together. Come on, let's go. Let's go quickly.
P'allyŏn ...... (Turns her head away).
Ch'unsam ...... I misspoke earlier. Let's go quickly. Only the task of avenging our enemy remains before us. Are you the only woman in the world whose body is defiled? Am I the only man whose chest receives an arrow? There are thousands, tens of thousands, we don't know how many. Even though we have become like this, shouldn't we avenge our enemy so that the people who come after us do not suffer such calamities? Let's go quickly.
P'allyŏn ...... I can't go. Even if you permit it, my heart does not permit it. Quick, go alone.
Ch'unsam ...... If you don't go, I'll drag you.
P'allyŏn ...... (Growing increasingly distressed) Even if dragged, I can't walk more than three steps. I... I... when I came out from there earlier... I already... I already...
(As she says this, she spits out a clot of blood. Ch'unsam, startled, holds P'allyŏn's face; its color is already pale like wax, and a foul smell comes from her mouth. The poison she took when leaving the bandit's den is now spreading throughout her body.)
Ch'unsam ...... (Startled and unsure what to do) Then you even took poison? You defiled your body to save me... and then took poison to preserve your honor before me? P'allyŏn, P'allyŏn, what is this act? This is too much. Ah-.
P'allyŏn ...... Don't be angry. Still, dying in your arms, I have no regrets. <133> Please don't forget me... And, as if I were alive, visit my parents morning and evening. This is my request. Ch'unsam, lend me your hand. Egu, a fire burns in my chest, feels like my bones are burning, press down hard on my chest with your hand...
Ch'unsam ...... Don't talk like that, quick, vomit the poison you took. You have a place to go with me, alive, don't you?
P'allyŏn ...... (Eyes barely open) Where?
Ch'unsam ...... To the mountains. To the deep mountains behind us.
P'allyŏn ...... There?
Ch'unsam ...... Aren't my comrades there? Hiding in the mountains away from the world's eyes, practicing swordsmanship and archery, aren't they? Hey, P'allyŏn. You and I must go there alive. We must avenge the bandit enemy together with our comrades.
P'allyŏn ...... (Weakly) To the mountains... the mountains behind... I want to go. But it can't be helped. You go to those mountains alive, and I will go to those mountains dead... Ai, it's painful. Feels like a fire is burning in my chest. Water... water... water...
(Ch'unsam lays the dying P'allyŏn on the grass and rushes frantically to the stream, scoops a mouthful of water, and brings it to her.)
Ch'unsam ...... P'allyŏn. P'allyŏn. Just as I recovered with the water you brought earlier, this time, recover with my water. P'allyŏn. P'allyŏn. (But P'allyŏn gives no response. Her eyes are already closed, her face pale. Ch'unsam embraces the dead body and sobs for a long time. Then he abruptly stands up, clenches both fists, and runs up towards the back mountain.)
Ch'unsam ...... Alright. P'allyŏn, just as you said, I alive, and you dead, let's meet in those mountains.
...... Stage darkens again ... after a short while ......

(The sound of many footsteps is heard from afar, and Ch'unsam appears from beyond the embankment, dragging the Bandit Leader tied with ropes. Behind him, several comrades armed with spears surround them, and the village boys and girls who were dancing and playing earlier also follow. Ch'unsam ties the bandit to the same willow tree where he himself was tied earlier. Then, the group breaks off willow branches and beats him indiscriminately. He struggles in agony. Finally, various travelers passing through Samgŏri also strike him once each before moving on.
Only then does Ch'unsam come to P'allyŏn's laid-out corpse, rubs his cheek against her face, and weeps. The boys and girls, also holding back tears, form a circle around P'allyŏn's corpse as before and sing, spinning round and round. While singing, they gently cover the corpse with the willow branches they used to beat the bandit leader. P'allyŏn and Ch'unsam, amidst their former companions singing mournfully, become gradually enveloped by the willow branches. The late spring breeze rustles through the willow embankment at Samgŏri once more.)

Willows, willows, weeping willows
At Ch'ŏnan Samgŏri, weeping willows
When those willows turn green, life comes
When those willows wither, life goes
Eheya tteheya hehehe-yo
Heaya tteaya hehehe-ra

Don't break those once-green willows
Though weary of their charm, they shed tears
Until the willows of Ch'ŏnan are green
Let's not leave these rivers and mountains of ours
Eheya teheya hehehe-yo
Heaya tteaya ehehe-ra

〓(Curtain)〓 <134>

**Summary**

Set in historical Ch'ŏnan Samgŏri, the play "Weeping Willow" depicts a tragic love story amidst oppression. Young villagers, led by Ch'unsam and P'allyŏn, enjoy a spring day until bandits attack, wounding Ch'unsam. The bandits capture Ch'unsam, accusing him of inciting rebellion. P'allyŏn tries to protect him but is captured by the bandit leader. Later, she frees Ch'unsam but reveals she was violated, having sacrificed her honor (and secretly taken poison) apparently to save him. Initially enraged, Ch'unsam urges her to join him and his comrades hiding in the mountains to fight back, but P'allyŏn dies from the poison. Ch'unsam rallies his comrades, captures the bandit leader, and enacts vengeance by having him beaten by the villagers at the same willow tree. The play ends with the villagers mourning P'allyŏn, covering her body with willow branches while singing a folk song.

From a colonial history perspective, the play, written in 1933, uses a historical setting likely as an allegory for contemporary Korean suffering under Japanese rule. The bandits represent the oppressive colonial power or its collaborators, brutally disrupting peaceful village life, exploiting resources (implied), violating women (P'allyŏn), and suppressing resistance (Ch'unsam). Ch'unsam's calls for resistance, his secret group training in the mountains, and the emphasis on defending "our rivers and mountains" reflect nationalist sentiment and the clandestine independence movement. P'allyŏn's tragic fate symbolizes the profound sacrifices and suffering, particularly of women, under colonial oppression, highlighting themes of lost purity, forced choices, and death as a final act of defiance or despair. The ultimate, albeit brutal, retribution against the bandit leader offers a cathartic, perhaps aspirational, vision of eventual justice for the oppressed. Historians might find the play interesting for its allegorical representation of colonial resistance, the portrayal of gendered suffering, the emphasis on collective action and retribution, and the use of folk elements (songs, setting) to ground nationalist themes.
