Writer's Diary (作家日記)
Four-part Prose Poem (四部作 散文)
Yi Kwang-su (李光洙)

March Oth
Hwang'a was discharged from the hospital today. Myŏn'a, who had been home alone, was delighted, hugging his older brother and pressing his cheek against his.
At night, after singing the two little ones to sleep, my body is tired, but sleep doesn't come. Sadness arises.
"After singing the ailing child to sleep," I thought of a sijo (traditional Korean verse form). The quiet, snow-falling night deepens.
"An early March dusk, still too early to call it spring."
I also had such thoughts.
"An early spring night where one can almost hear the sound of the ground, melted by the sun, freezing again."
I also had such thoughts.
"It seems one can even hear the sound of snow falling outside the window."
I also thought like this.
Suddenly, life seems tinged with sadness.
My own life seems pitiful too.
"Busy all day with big matters."
Such ambition also arises.
The quiet night and the quiet room pull me out of the busy mundane tasks and worldly worries of daily life, drawing me into a philosophical, religious state of mind, contemplating life itself and the universe itself for a moment. A state of mind I hadn't been able to find for a long time.
I composed a four-part prose poem titled "Daughter," "Sister," "Wife," "Mother."
As the little ones turned over, I helped them pee and arranged their pillows.

Maeryŏn (梅蓮)
Mo Ch'ung-suk (毛充淑)

March 13th
On a sunless day, ghostly clouds circled the waist of Namsan Mountain and filled the streets. Breakfast never sits well with me. Friend C in the inner room worries that I eat too little. It's kind of her.
When I arrived at the office, a letter came from my old friend Maeryŏn. The writing was sorrowful. I mindlessly pressed the letter to my cheek and cried for my pitiful friend. With what can I take revenge on the devilish <108> man who caused the downfall of the once so brave and vibrant Maeryŏn?
To Maeryŏn, caught in the net of love and unable to bloom, I once gave the advice, "Be strong. Don't trust love too much." But the unbreakable, affectionate iron wall embedded in Maeryŏn's heart was not shaken in the slightest by my weak advice.
Maeryŏn was a possessor of will with higher learning. She was not the type of person to fall into endless sorrow due to love for one person.
However, she is an unfortunate girl who possesses a fragile strength towards the opposite sex, remarkably different from her reputation among her own gender. That man, who swore to die following Maeryŏn around and wrestled with life, has now been tempted by another woman and stabbed a knife into Maeryŏn's heart. How heart-wrenchingly painful must it have been for the overly sentimental and pitiful Maeryŏn to drink poison and lie sick in bed? My dear friend, facing death with hot tears in a countryside hospital with no friends around. If I knew where that unprincipled man, like a dog or a pig, was, I feel like I would confront him with all my might and fight. My heart just aches, and unable to go, my head just pounded all day. All the men coming and going on Chongno seemed frightening. What was so great about that man who deceived a friend like Maeryŏn?
Returning in the evening, I wrote a letter to Maeryŏn. But she might die before this letter even reaches her. Kajusa, Ch'unhŭi, Tess – women who died tragic fates flash through my mind. Maeryŏn is by no means a weak woman. But why is the once so vigorous Maeryŏn trying to overcome this with death?
Is love painful? Is youth an empty sorrow? Why must you die just like that, without living a little longer to fully display all your talents?
Tearing up the letter I was writing, I decided to go see Maeryŏn tomorrow morning and lay down. In the dormitory where the electric lights had gone out, pale moonlight spread its silver arrows. I hate seeing the moon. I closed my eyes. Today, I couldn't do anything all day and was just sad.
Maeryŏn, don't die. Muttering the words to myself in my mouth, I drifted off to sleep.

Three Days (三日間)
Yi Hyo-sŏk (李孝石)

February _th (Saturday)
Snow fell white.
A day off other than Sunday is always particularly welcome. Reading the creative writing section of M Magazine, while that month's magazines piled up high on the desk, blurring before my eyes, I can't get my hands on any other work. The plan to read all of that month's magazines—at least the creative writing section—at the beginning of the month falls apart every month, and lately, the start of the month is spent wrestling with new magazines, and before I know it, the 30 days of the month have passed.
A work by a writer I'm seeing for the first time. Very excellent. A masterpiece (佳作) whose concise style presses forward, line by line. The extremely skillful technique suggests it might be an autobiographical novel. Otherwise, it couldn't resonate so powerfully. The first half is sticky and gloomy, the second half happy—. There's no difference in the weight of the style, but the first half is much better than the second. Contrary to the happy side of life easily flowing into 'easiness,' it seems the painful side of life more often becomes great art. Just like all of Dostoevsky's works, or rather, all the great art of all time.
In the afternoon, alone, I stoked the charcoal embers under the brazier. A request for a contribution to "Julia" (주리야) came by letter, and I simply accepted by letter, but after accepting, I feel anew the sensation of having taken on a great burden. How will I manage it for half a year? It's not a work to painstakingly stitch together with blood, nor do I think it's the time for that <109> yet, but still, as long as it's not just playful scribbling out of boredom, the feeling of burden is the same. Without even crafting the story for the entire piece or forming a concept, facing the manuscript paper, my mind is tangled like threads, and after several hours, I barely managed to write four pages.
In the evening, notification that Mr. Y cannot come. Inevitably, I visited Mr. M's house alone at night. A cozy home with just the couple. The wife is a beauty (佳人) with intelligent, bright eyes (明眸)—possessing a beauty of nerves rare to find in the countryside. The only people here with whom I can discuss literature are Mr. Y and this Mr. M's family. Taste and sentiment can surely unite beyond blood ties. Chatting is much more to my liking than the intended French study. As someone who has been as far as Mexico, he is knowledgeable about overseas affairs.

February _th (Sunday)
A postcard arrived from S requesting the manuscript. What I had planned to start running next month has now become urgent, and I hastily picked up the brush. Five pages by noon.
Reading Tanizaki's (谷崎) essay in "Taech'o" (大朝), I felt relieved by his penchant for slow writing (遲筆癖). For some reason, I feel reassured by his confession that he has to cut off all external relations and struggle all day holed up in his room to barely write 4 or 5 pages, the equivalent of one day's installment for a newspaper novel. It's not comparing a turtle to the moon, but if I hole myself up all day, I can write about 10 pages, so I think that's sufficient. I think slow writing (遲筆) and fast writing (速筆) are purely physiological, regardless of talent or disposition. There's no need for a writer to worry about speed.
At night, sleeping at school, five pages.
Total 10 pages today. The room is too hot and the air is dry, making my throat scratchy.

February _th (Monday)
If only I could go to Ranam and have a cup of strong, hot coffee—. Feeling chilly and shivery, I spent half the day lost in coffee daydreams.
Now it seems I'm almost addicted. It's not that I'm imitating the refined taste of Balzac, who supposedly had a lifelong partiality (偏嗜) for coffee, but somehow, without knowing when, a deep addiction has set in. But how difficult it is to find good coffee in the countryside. Even buying whole beans, grinding them, and brewing the supposedly genuine "Java" brought from a Ranam teahouse (喫茶店) doesn't produce the real taste. Ground and canned "Brazil" is even two levels lower in taste. How fortunate are my friends in Seoul who drink strong, dark brown, fragrant "Mocha."
At night, Miss O Il (?) came to learn English. I sent her away after two pages of lecture. Returning from the K Hall bathhouse, I continued writing 10 pages. In total, 22 pages written over several days. Due to the deadline, I decided this would be the first installment, sealed it, and added a letter to S. I plan to ask Wŏn to mail it first thing tomorrow morning. Lying in bed, it's well past one o'clock. Severe headache.

R Writer Couple (R作家 夫婦)
Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi (崔貞熙)

March Xth
I want to have quiet time.
First, I wish I could move—. With six households crammed into one yard, how could there ever be quiet time—? On top of that, seven or eight family members in one room. After dinner, putting the little one to sleep, I finally found a moment and sat at my desk, but the kids next door are kicking the wall, fighting with each other, crying, and making a racket.
Furthermore, since the room right next door became a Tenrikyō Branch (天理敎 分敎) or something a few days ago, the sound of wooden clappers, scripture (?) chanting, and hand clapping is incessant, making it impossible to find a quiet moment.
Other times might be different, but when they steal my most precious morning hours, I want to run over, yell at them, and kick over the god they've made.
What's even more detestable is that the scripture (?) chanting and prayers are all in Japanese. And the teacher (?) there also has a Japanese name, wears Japanese clothes, and acts Japanese.<110>
Do they think God won't give blessings if he knows they are Korean? If so, who would believe in such an unfair God?

March Xth
The child woke up two hours early because of the sound of the wooden clapper. "Ugh, I hate them," I shouted involuntarily.
I haven't been able to fulfill any of the magazine's manuscript requests, and the deadlines (시메끼리 날자) have passed, so what should I do? Moreover, it's essays and miscellaneous writings (雜文) that are annoying to write. I desperately want to live in another, quieter house. This household (살님), though threadbare, is robbing me of the literary talent I possess.

March Xth
I went out early because there was a lot of work at the office (社). I spent the day busier than expected.
By the time I returned home, the electric lights were already on. It was my habit to sleep right after dinner, but the house was unusually quiet, so I wanted to write.
For the first time in a long time, I felt the mood of spring in this yard. I thought about deceased friends, about the R Writer Couple (R作家 夫婦) whom I used to visit and talk about literature with, and with these thoughts and others, my mood somehow turned melancholic, and in the end, I wanted to cry.

Life · State of Mind (生活·心境)
Yi Chong-myŏng (李種鳴)

February 25th (Saturday)
In the morning, a letter arrived from my older brother (형님) in Andong County (安東縣). It said his life was a mess, and moreover, my sister-in-law's (형수) illness was critical (위중하다). He asked me to send a copy of the family register (호적등본) so he could file a residency report (居住屆) in case anything happened, allowing him to be notified by letter.
"With four of those rowdy kids, how could their mother not get sick?"
Father (아버지) said, choking back tears.
I barely ate breakfast and rushed out of the house. This atmosphere, pressing down on my chest, was unbearably depressing.
Does poverty really strip people of their spirit (의기) like that? I thought about my father holding back tears. Lately, whenever some misfortune (우환) occurred in the family or worries arose, Father would always choke back tears. However, I hadn't thought much of Father's sentimentality (쎈치멘탈), or perhaps weak character (懦弱한 성격), anyway, his sudden increase in tears lately.
"In my 60 years of life, I used to be known as somebody—."
Considering his constant refrain, Father is already a tragic figure created by the times (時代가 맨드른 비극). He is hit by the fierce, scorching new era, the lament of a straggler (낙오자) unable to endure it. We young people breathing in the new era have neither the time (경황) nor the need to look back at such things. Crying at the slightest provocation—crying—.
But even as I think this, I myself become afraid that tears might come out for some reason—ah!

February 27th (Sunday)
It's a chilly day.
In the morning, at home, I wrote a couple of pages of the continuation of "Hell of Love and Lust" (愛慾地獄), serialized in Yŏsŏng Chosŏn (女性朝鮮), then threw it down. In the afternoon, with members of the Film Art Research Society (映畵藝術硏究會), I watched a preview screening (試寫) of "Blonde Venus" (뿌론드 븨너쓰) at Tansŏngsa Theater (團成社). My expectations were perhaps too high, so I was slightly disappointed, but overall, I thought the technique (수법) and acting were good. The scene where the main character, Dietrich (데도리치), after her beloved child is taken by her husband, goes to a lodging house and throws an envelope (封套) containing $1,500 to a woman who says, "I don't even have 10 sen, so I'll kill myself tomorrow," asking, "If you have this, you won't kill yourself, right?" <111> mimicking her husband's words to her, like a monologue (獨白),
"If only I hadn't sold that, I could have made a fortune—" I thought the scene where she turns and leaves was very good.

February 27th (Monday)
After breakfast, I thought I'd settle down and try to write some overdue manuscripts today, but my wife ruined the mood again.
Her nagging complaint was that I hadn't handled the girl's school transfer procedures (전학수속). Last spring, I tried to enroll the girl in a public elementary school, but was rejected because she was just two months short of the school age. After sending her to kindergarten for another year, it felt pointless, so I had no choice but to send her to a private school. Now, this year, she is of school age, so she has been pestering (졸나대엿다) me to enroll her in the public elementary school. However, I had been avoiding this annoying task under the pretext (구실) that public or private, it's all the same. That's what caused the trouble (말성) again today.
She criticized me for being too indifferent to household matters. She enumerated my overly nonchalant (대범한) attitude—never supervising the child's homework, never knowing how to go out and take her side (역성) even when she comes home after fighting somewhere. And then she indignantly asked, "What's the point of sitting around writing stuff all night and day that doesn't even bring in manuscript fees?"
Without a word, I grabbed my hat and rushed out of the house. I couldn't feel offended by my wife's words because I knew my existence was too powerless (힘이 업는) domestically.
Not just my wife, but everyone in the family despises my writing. Whether I write well or poorly, or whether it's important work or minor, if they despise what I am trying my best to do, no one could feel good about it. But even so, what can be done? In the evening, I came home drunk (취하야).
—(The end) —

Looking for a Rented House (셰집 어드러)
Sŏnghae (星海)

March 12th (Sunday)
Office (社) is closed for public holiday. From morning, selected (棟選) 3 potential rental houses (貸家), but couldn't find a suitable one.
In the afternoon, went with C to Kyŏngsŏng "Ground" to watch a rugby match. Seeing the heat (열기) of healthy lads (健兒) on a day when the spring chill (春寒) still feels biting is quite exhilarating (狀快). The crowd (관중) was extremely small. Among them (中에도), a disabled middle school student leaning on crutches under both arms (兩腋) was present (介在). What must be his state of mind, watching the swift running (快走) of able legs (健脚) with his disabled body? A feeling that tugs at the heartstrings is unavoidable. Isn't his world entirely different (달치 안흔가)? Who could fail to sense, even with eyes closed, the tolerance (관용) and envy (羨望) flowing from the face of that boy watching soccer with one leg (隻脚)? Went to a temple (寺), gazed at the spring atmosphere shimmering on the tree branches, waited for night, and returned to the city (歸城).

13th (Monday)
Returning from the office (社), my elderly parent (老親) is worried again about the house situation. It seems Mother went to see the house in Suŭn-dong (授恩洞), which was almost decided but delayed because the current occupants had nowhere to go. She said she wouldn't take such a house even if given for free. The reason = apparently there's a ghost of someone who hanged themselves from a beam (梁木) in that house. A profligate son hanged himself from the rafters because his father wouldn't let him spend money, they say. And that no one who lived in that house ever prospered; all went bankrupt and moved out. Of course, it's likely a curse from someone whose peaceful dwelling is being disturbed, calling it an unlucky house (凶家), but even transcending superstition (미신), it's not pleasant to hear. It's understandable that Mother is reluctant (끄리다). I could only reply, "Let's give up (단념) on that house."

March 14th (Tuesday)
After leaving the office, looked for rentals (貸家) again. Nothing met my satisfaction. At night, I was unaware of the late hour, listening to Mr. C's cheerful (快濶) conversation in K-dong. <112>

Several Entries from a March Diary (三月 日記의 數節)
Yi Mu-yŏng (李無影)

March Xth
It's been over a month since I accepted a manuscript request from H君 at C Company (C社). I hate seeing his frowning face, so I pass by C Company without stopping in. It's distressing that I couldn't immediately respond to H君's request even while I myself am struggling with manuscript collection, but it's even more distressing that he misunderstands, thinking I'm not writing when I actually can't.
However, I'm not *not* writing; I *can't* write. It's not a matter of time, nor is it due to laziness (怠慢), but it's a fact that I can't write. "Yukidzumari (ユキヅマリ - Deadlock)?" But that's not it either.
"I absolutely must quit this damned profession!" It was a few months ago. Realizing how much life as a magazine reporter at B Company (B社) kills creative inspiration (感興), I quit B Company. Yet, here I am, starting this job again after only a few months.
H君, it's not that I'm not writing. I can't write. Please don't misunderstand.
At night—lotion (노-신?) two dabs, a bowl of cold water.
My sister (누이) came from the countryside. I was glad. But after hearing she's been in Seoul for over 20 days, my affection cooled. I can't understand the mind of my older brother who sent her only now.

March Xth
Met C君. He says there are too many themes that almost become novels but get trapped by ideology (이데오로기-). He also mentions maybe writing a romance novel. Is it the literary expression of ideology (이데오로기-의 文學化) or the ideologization of literature (문학의 이데오로기-化)?
When I told him to choose the latter (後者), C君 stared blankly at me. Why are you looking at me?
Met H君 on Chongno (鐘路). Got nagged again and made the fifth promise for tomorrow afternoon. Who knows about tomorrow afternoon? Creative writing—this is one thing that's difficult to comply with according to an editor's orders (命令). In a month of 30 days, there are only 4 or 5 days with creative inspiration (感興). Fortunately (僥倖히), if no other issues (like depression - 憂鬱病) arise during those 4 or 5 days, I can write readily.
The creative work of a magazine editor—even if they are a writer—ends up being merely "reporter's writing." "The desperate cry for writers' livelihood stability actually stems from this."
Creative writing is ultimately the description (描寫) of emotions. Only when the writer's mood and emotions fuse (融合) can the theme (테-마) intended by the writer be expressed.
There probably isn't an editor outside Korea (朝鮮以外) who would ask for creative work from someone who dodges streets because they're hounded for small change (잔돈푼) and sneaks into their own room like a stray cat (도둑고양이) because they're pressed for meal money (食代).
If only they knew, even vaguely, what kind of cultivation (교양) a writer needs...
If a writer endures this kind of life for more than a year and doesn't reach a "Yukidzumaru (ユキツマル - stalemate)," they are a writer worthy of respect. At night, read "Martin Eden (마르티네이밤?)." At two, stole and ate rice, then wrote a short piece for P Company (P社).
Two cigarettes. Two letters, one to Mr. S in Hongwŏn (洪原) and one to Mr. N in Wŏnsan (元山).

March Xth
In the morning, visited Mr. S for a 2-hour meeting (會談).
In the afternoon, spent the day at the printing house, and met H君 at six. Had tea bought by S君.
Organized (整理) the manuscript I was writing for a long article. S君's manuscript arrived. This was after hearing about S君's plagiarism (剽竊) of a work. Tore it up. Because I feel no hesitation in despising this type of person.
Drafted a plan (푸란) for issue 4 of "Munhak Times" (문학 타임스). Agreed with E君 to firmly boycott (뽀이코트) works, even by established writers (旣成作家), if they don't meet a certain standard. If we can't publish because there are no manuscripts, so be it. That's not my responsibility.
From one o'clock, read "Night (밤?)." When finished, it was around five o'clock. "Yukidzumaru no ka? (ユキツマルノカ? - Is it a deadlock?)" <113> Like dawn, stole and ate one of Kyŏngjae's pears (?).
March 15th

A Diary That Lost Its Place (갈피 일흔 日記)
Kim Cha-hye (金慈惠)

January 6th (Friday)
There was a round-table discussion (좌담회) starting at four. Many gentlemen (各士) attended to discuss sex education (성교육) and social etiquette (사교문제). It felt almost miraculous that we could openly discuss the guiding principles for this issue, considering that just a few years ago, even uttering the character "性" (sex) was viewed as sinful (罪惡視). Indeed, rather than just empty talk about theories, there is a desperate need for educators to guide children practically, as if loving their own sons and daughters. But how many hypocrites (위선자) among educators wear masks and act shamelessly (철면피)! If you lift the wrapping cloth they wear, scandalous things might be exposed (탈로 될지), yet outwardly (것흐로) they all pretend to be "educators with many merits (貢*이 만흔 교육자)." When will a world come where people live openly (적나라하게), shedding pretenses (것발님)?
The first condition for male-female interaction is the emergence of true leaders. How much need there is for parental hearts that offer genuine understanding and solutions for countless young students... How many students yearn for true guidance to set their course (行路) before they are drawn into delicate (떼리켓잇트한) problems?

Cosmopolitan Diary (코스모포리탄 日記)
Kim Ki-rim (金起林)

March XXth
My sister (누이) gave me rice gruel, worried about my throat.
The gruel is bland. I rather ate rice mixed with water and kimchi cubes.
After breakfast, I had my sister call D Company's (D社) R君 and C Company's (C社) R君, asking them to visit.
Lying sick in bed for 4 or 5 days without seeing a single friend is unbearably lonely.
C Company's R said he would come at night. D Company's B said he couldn't come because a guest arrived from the countryside. And told me to take good care of myself (조섭).
Humph. Whether R tells me to take care of myself or not, I will. I decided I would send R a letter breaking off relations (絶交狀). Thinking that, the friendship (친교) R and I cultivated over 4 years feels entirely wasted (앗갑다).
I probably must carry out the firm decision I made during my illness. Departure time (출발시간) is set for tomorrow morning. I took out the trunk (튜렝크) and basket (뻐스켓) and packed my things (짐을 꾸렷다). My throat hurts a bit.
Afternoon—Opening the small window (들창), snow flutters on the main street (한길), and across the street, the bare branches of the apricot trees cry mournfully (앙앙 운다). I get goosebumps. My sister tries to dissuade me.
But I wrapped my throat tightly with a scarf (목도리) and went out onto the main street. My legs stagger weakly (힘업시 허둥거린다).
Went to D Company (D社). They gave me 2 Won in manuscript fees (稿料) and 3 Won more for next month's fees. Because I'm short on travel expenses (路費).
The three gentlemen working there, Yi (李), Chu (朱), and Ch'oe (崔), stood up and wished me "Bon voyage" (봉보야쥬). Thank you. Passing the second floor, I thought R must be in here, and wanted to meet him. But as small revenge for his coldness, I would leave without meeting him. I passed the second floor and came down.
I saluted the guard (守衛氏) with the mustache of an army officer (陸軍將校) and came outside.
Feeling a bit unwell, I thought about turning back, but instead went to the familiar S Company (S社). There <114> I met Sŏ (徐), Pak (朴), and Kim (金). I couldn't meet Chongmyŏng (鍾鳴). Disappointing.
They all regretted my leaving Seoul very much. Kind people.
I decided I wouldn't tell K氏 alone that I was leaving. I cautioned Chŏnghŭi-ssi (貞熙氏).
K氏 came. He asked if the editing of the magazine I was overseeing at K Company (K社) was finished. I had no choice but to tell him I had left K Company. He was very surprised.
I shook hands with everyone and left S Company. K氏 followed me to the stairs (층층게), asking what happened. I thought I shouldn't, but ended up telling him everything. Even that I was leaving Seoul tomorrow morning—. I regretted bringing it up unnecessarily.
K氏 tells me not to go for just 5 or 6 days. But wasn't I the one who left him, the one nobody asked to come in the first place? Would it not be a shameless act for a man to just accept his offer to stay now, saying thank you?
"I probably have to go."
"But how can you leave with this snow falling?" K氏 laughed.
"Isn't that even more 'romantic'?" I laughed too.
"Where are you going, leaving this beautiful spring behind?" He patted my shoulder (억게) and urged me again and again (재삼) to stay (만류).
Stopped by K Company (K社) and then visited S氏's house.
"I just caused trouble (싯그럽게 굴고) for everyone, and nothing worked out— I'd rather go to the countryside too."
"Why?" S氏 was also surprised. He also told me to hang around for a few more days.
So what choice is there—. Came back home. My throat hurts more. In this state, it doesn't seem likely I'll leave tomorrow. Heard R came and went.

March XXth
The weather is good today. Persuaded by my sister, I decided to rest for a few more days and then leave. I quickly (불이나케) wrote a letter to Father (아버지). "My throat is fine, so please don't worry (안심하소서). I'll rest for a few days and then come home," because I heard my sister had written to Father when I was very sick before, telling him I was ill (알른다고). What need was there to trouble the old man's heart with such a minor illness? Back when I was studying, if money was even a few days late, I routinely sent telegrams saying "Seriously ill. Send money quickly." But I don't want to do that now. Years ago, when I went home and had tonsillitis like this time, Father didn't eat until I fully recovered. I remember Father, bustling about, bringing the doctor (의원을 다려온다), brewing medicine (약을 다린다), sweat beads forming on his furrowed brow (구름쌀 자핀 이마). I resented my sister's carelessness (경솔한 것).
There is nothing more precious in the world than true love.
Today, I played the role of a prisoner (죄수노릇) in my room. At lunch, my brother-in-law (매부) and a guest played go for stakes (내기바둑), and the guest bought Hottŏk (호떡).

March XXth
Woke with a start (스스라처) from a nap to repeated calls from outside. It was Kyŏngjae (璟載), Myŏnghwan (明煥), Yuyŏng (幽影), and Sunsŏk (順石). They said they were off to break the record for mountain excursions (山노리). Sure enough, under the flap of Sunsŏk-kun's overcoat, an X liquor bottle was hidden, and a bundle of newspapers was tucked under his arm (겨안겨저). Everyone urged me to go along. I worried my throat would get worse.
But the spring day was clear high above Inwangsan Mountain (仁王山), the companions were good—so, even bracing myself for possibly being sick in bed for a night (하로밤즘), could I resist (백일 수) not playing?
Wrapping my throat firmly, I followed everyone. Facing Chahamun Gate (紫霞門), we climbed, winding through the green pine forest (푸른 소나무 숩).
Went out to Segeomjeong Pavilion (洗劍亭). We were the only sightseers. Beneath the thick sheet of ice (어름장), the sound of water wandered.
Far off in the valley bottom (골작속), a patch of golden grass (금잔듸밧) spread out, tinged with fresh green (파릿파릿). It's the "Green Bed" (綠色寢臺) mentioned by the poet Sin Sŏk-chŏng (辛夕汀). It would have been an even better scene if two or three country girls (시골 시악씨), with herb baskets beside them (여페끼고) and their skirt ties (초마꼬리) looped behind them (뒤로 돌려간), were standing there.
In summer, this alley is supposedly packed with people. Then, this scenery <115> too will become endlessly chaotic. I couldn't help but thank everyone for leading me here today.
This village is steeped in a rustic scent. The rice cooked at Sunsŏk-kun's house also tasted better than Seoul rice.
Only when the sun was gently setting (너울너울) over the mountain did we leave for the city. I didn't feel tired.
"Bravo (뿌라보-)." Our joyful Sunday.

Ear to the Wall (벽에 귀를 대이고)
Yi Ŭn-sang (李殷相)

March 1st
Today too, I was busy until sunset. But counting up, I did nothing. There wasn't even a mustard seed's (게자씨) worth of work my 'life' could be proud of. Lying in bed at night, pressing my ear to the pillow (벼개), I asked, "What do I live for?" but heard no answer...

March 2nd
Continuing solitude (적막)!
Today too, there is nothing to write besides the word "solitude" (적막). Shall I add one more line, "Why is there nothing more to write than solitude?"

March 3rd
I don't know when it started, but when my heart feels heavy, I've developed a habit of opening a map (지도) and sitting silently for 10 or 20 minutes.
However, the map I need is neither a military (軍用) map nor an industrial (産業) map. One showing only land and water is sufficient. While thinking of wanting to go anywhere (어듸로든), anywhere, if my mind becomes free and refreshed, that is enough. Looking at distant lands and long waters...
Tonight too, I spread out the map for a long time...

Recent Diary (最近日記)
Pang In-gŭn (方仁根)

March 8th (Wednesday) Cloudy
After planning for a long time, decided to visit Na Hye-sŏk Yŏsa (羅蕙錫 女史) with Ch'un'gang (春江).
For a spring day, it was very chilly and the wind was biting. Yŏsa lives alone on the second floor of a Japanese house in Susong-dong (壽松洞), having left her husband and children.
We heard she happened to be out, and were about to turn back (돌아서랴고), but decided to go up (올러가서) and wait a bit, so we entered the unoccupied room. Opening the door, we were startled. A strange man glared at us. We hesitated (믐칫하엿다). But the next moment, we laughed again. It was because we mistook a large portrait (초상) of some man that Yŏsa was painting and had stood up.
The spacious tatami room (다다미방) was filled with paintings, but in the center (가온대) stood a desk, and beside it (엽헤), water bubbled furiously in a brazier (화로). A skirt hung on the wall. On the floor lay a half-finished knitted vest (짜겟), presumably for her children. Her maternal love was unusually profound. Yŏsa has recently become a devotee of Buddhism, reportedly at the recommendation of Ichiyō Joshi (一葉女史). In the tokonoma (床間), there was an artistic cast-iron Buddha statue (佛像). We lit incense (향불) for it. Somehow, Yŏsa's lonely atmosphere seemed to fill the room. And I felt the solitude of life.

March 14th (Tuesday) Cloudy
Today, my second child, Hŭisŏng (熙星), graduated from Ewha Kindergarten (梨花幼稚園), so I <116> went to the kindergarten.
The children came out and sang songs and danced what they had learned.
The principal gave a farewell address (고별사) to the graduates. She spoke tearfully about their parting (리별). But the children listened, smiling. They kept smiling and looking at the teacher. For them, there was no parting or sorrow, only joy and laughter. Watching that, I cried while smiling.

March 15th (Wednesday) Rain
Came home from the office (社), tidied the house a bit, and patched (발럿다) the torn (뚜러진) window paper.
The window paper needs to be newly applied once a month. Four-year-old Hŭiwŏn (熙園) is an expert window tearer. So this time, I applied paper (조희) and then pasted silk fabric (상사) over it (덧발럿다).
Now, even if they drum on the window like a changgu (traditional drum), there's no worry of it tearing. After dinner, played singing songs (唱歌) with the children, and after ten o'clock at night, once those noisy children were all asleep, I wrote one day's installment of the novel "Incense of the Devil City" (魔都의 향불), lay down in bed, and flipped through (뒤적거리엿다) a book. And for a while, I indulged in the fantasies (공상) I have every evening before sleeping. Spring rain pours down (쏘다진다).

Lonely Spring Night (孤獨의 春宵)
P'ain (파인)

March 9th
They say women's hearts flutter (썰넨다) in spring and men's in autumn, but somehow, whenever spring comes, I want to quietly cry under a ginkgo tree (은앵나무), twisting (비탈면서) its light green baby leaves. I want to quietly pluck (뜻고) a guitar (키-타). I want to quietly whistle (휘파람) and stay awake (새이고) all this night.
Truly, when March and April arrive, I feel as if something is silently flowing past my ears (귓가으로). I feel as if something is quietly passing by on cat's paws (고양이 거름), careful not to wake me. What could it be? Could it not be the "flow of life" I avoided thinking about even in dreams? Could it not be the "passing of youth"? Thinking this, suddenly (갑쪽히) I feel a solitude, as if something empty stands before me.
Lately, I've come to love the melody (멜로-듸) "Sake wa Namida ka Tameiki ka" (酒は淚か溜息か - Is Sake Tears or Sighs?). The lyrics are insignificant, but in that melody, I feel as if one stream of my own heart flows out. When I hear that melody, I see the lonely shadow (구름자) of a man, unlucky in his time, who has abandoned all hope, fame, and lovers, dusting off his empty hands and coming down a mountain at sunset (夕陽녁), quietly expressing his feelings with a low whistle. That shadow clearly seems like a self-portrait (자화상) of myself. Oh, sorrowful melody of "Sake wa Namida ka Tameiki ka," seeping silently into the very bottom of my heart like mist quietly settling on a white sandy shore (白沙불).
Could this be the periodic solitude that comes once every ten years? Ten years ago, I visited a French priest (佛蘭西神父) in Tokyo (東京), intending to flee (다르려고) to the Trappist Monastery in Hokkaido (北海道). I also thought of seeking out Mushanokōji's (武子小路) "Atarashiki-mura" (新し*村 - New Village). It was also then that I used to take walks with B (a Japanese woman) at night in Zōshigaya Cemetery (雜司ケ谷 墓地). But then B died in the Great Kantō Earthquake (震災), the priest returned to his home country (本國), and I, due to circumstances beyond my control (내 의사로는 엇절 수 업는 경우), came to Seoul and have struggled against the fierce waves (激浪) of life to reach today. If that solitude had continued a little longer then, perhaps today's sun and moon would no longer be shining on my body.
I am lonely. Now, I feel even lonelier than ten years ago. Twisting the ginkgo leaves, must I ultimately stay awake (새이여야) through this night? Must I stay awake through this spring? No, no, must I live out my entire arduous half-life (半生) so lonely like this?

March 10th
At night, I tried writing a one-act play titled "Towards the Dark" (어두운데로). I tried to express (表白) the feelings I am currently experiencing. After writing it, it somehow felt incomplete, so I hesitated (망서리엇스나), but submitted it to censorship (검열) as is. During my life as a newspaper reporter, I acquired the habit of writing everything <118> at once, in one breath. Newspaper reporters are not allowed rewrites (推稿). Within five or ten minutes, you write one or two paragraphs of an article in one go (一氣), and 10 minutes later, the written article is already typeset (活字化) and published.
However, while knowing that creative work shouldn't be dashed off all at once like a newspaper article, I still haven't corrected this habit.
I want to write plays. Plays, where at a certain point, like a waterfall crashing down and swirling intensely, like a bonfire blazing fiercely, the focus of passionate combustion stands clearly defined, seem to suit my temperament (性情). But do I have the talent (才分) to write plays?

March 11th
Came to the office (社), roughly prepared the editing for the April issue of Samch'ŏlli (三千里), decided the contents for the spring issue of "Samch'ŏlli Munhak" (三千里文學), met several friends, and went around to 5 or 6 places for office business... And then the sun sets already. These miscellaneous tasks (雜務) repeated daily! I lose all my time to these miscellaneous tasks, and all my energy is taken by these miscellaneous tasks.
At night, read Yamamoto's (山本) "Sen-Man" (鮮滿 - Korea and Manchuria). His writing style (筆致) shows traces of effort to display fellow-traveler (동반자적) goodwill (호의). I thought that among fellow Japanese, Dr. Yamamoto Miyoshino (山本美越乃 博士) politically, Mr. Yanagi Muneyoshi (柳宗悅) artistically, and President Yamamoto of Kaizōsha (山本改造社長) emotionally and culturally, all show sympathetic attitudes towards Korea (朝鮮). Conversely, people like Saijō Yaso (西條八十) and several others seemed like unpleasant existences who treated Korea as a caricature (카리칼추어). <118>

**Summary**

This collection of diary entries from various Korean writers in March 1933 (and late February) offers intimate glimpses into their daily lives, creative processes, and emotional states under Japanese colonial rule. The entries touch upon personal events like illness and family matters, the pressures of writing deadlines and financial instability, social interactions, artistic reflections, feelings of loneliness, frustration with societal constraints (including perceived pressures of assimilation), and contemplation of life, youth, and art. Moods range from melancholic introspection and creative anxiety to moments of camaraderie and appreciation for nature or art.

From a historical perspective, the article is valuable for several reasons. It provides first-hand accounts of the lived experiences of Korean intellectuals, revealing their personal anxieties intertwined with professional struggles and the broader socio-political context. Mentions of needing manuscript fees, dealing with plagiarism, facing censorship (검열), the critique of Japanese language use in a religious setting (Tenrikyō), and discussions about writers like Na Hye-sŏk (a controversial female figure) highlight the specific challenges and cultural dynamics of the colonial period. The references to Japanese figures and publications, both sympathetic and critical, also reflect the complex relationship and awareness of Japanese cultural and political influence.

晃兒 오늘 퇴원. 집에 혼자 잇던 冕兒 반가워 형을 안고 빰을 대는 광경.

「알는 어린 것을 노래불러 재고 나면」하고 시조를 생각하엿다. 고요한 눈 날리는 밤은 깁퍼간다.
「봄이라기엔 아직도 이른 3월의 어느 어스름」
이러한 생각도 해 보앗다.
「볏에 녹앗던 땅이 다시 얼어붓는 소리도 들리는 듯한 이른 봄의 밤」
이러한 생각도 해 보앗다.
「창박게 눈 나리는 소리도 들리는 듯 하여라」
이럿케도 생각해 보앗다.
불련 듯 인생이 슬픔을 띄어 보인다.
내 일생이 가엽서도 보인다.
「하로 밧비 큰 일을」
하는 野心도 난다.
고요한 밤과 고요한 방안은 나를 일상의 밧븐 俗務와 俗된 근심속에서 끌어내어서 잠깐 인생 그것과 우주 그것을 생각하는 철학적, 종교적, 심경에 끌어 넛는다. 오래 못 찻아가던 심경이라.
「딸」, 「누이」, 「안해」, 「어머니」 4부작인 산문시 1편을 지엇다.

梅蓮
毛充淑
3월 13일
햇빗도 업는 날 유령가튼 구름떼가 남산 허리를 도라 시가까지 꽉 찻다. 언제던지 아침밥은 잘 먹히지가 안는다. 안방에 잇는 C友가 밥 적게 먹는다 걱정을 해준다. 고마운 일이다.
사무실에 오니 녯 동무 梅蓮에게서 글이 왓다. 구슬픈 글발이다. 나는 정신업시 편지를 빰에 대고 동무가 불상해 울엇다. 그러케 씩씩하고 활기잇든 梅蓮을 여지업시 타락하게 한 악마가튼 ＜108＞ 사내를 무엇으로 복수를 하나?.
나는 사랑이라는 그물에 걸녀 피여나지 못하는 梅蓮을 향해서 『굿세라. 너머 사랑을 밋지마라』하고 자조 충고를 준 일이 잇다. 그러나 梅蓮의 가슴에 박힌 끈을 수 업는 정겨운 철벽은 나의 연약한 충고로는 조곰도 동요가 되지 안엇다.
梅蓮은 高等學識을 가진 의지의 所有者엿다. 한 사람을 사랑하여 그 정으로 끗업는 哀愁에 빠질 인물은 아니엿다.
그러나 그는 동성간에 평과는 헌저히 다른 유약한 힘을 이성에게는 가시고 잇는 불행한 女兒다. 죽기를 맹세하고 梅蓮을 따라단니며 생명과 씨름을 하든 그 사나히는 지금와서 딴 여자의 유혹을 밧고 梅蓮의 심장에 칼을 꼬즌 것이다. 너머 감상적이요 애련한 梅蓮이 얼마나 가슴이 찌저지도록 압헛스면 독약을 마시고 병석에 누어잇스랴. 아모 동무도 업는 시골 병원서 뜨거운 눈물로 죽엄을 마지할 내 귀여운 동무. 개나 도야지 가튼 無道한 그 남자의 잇는 곳을 내가 안다면 힘껏 해보고 싸홈이라도 할 것 갓헛다. 마음만 압어오고 갈 수도 업는 나는 종일 머리만 뗑할 뿐이엿다. 종로로 오고가는 남자들이 다 무서웟다. 梅蓮이 가튼 동무를 속인 그 남자는 무엇이 그리 잘낫든고?
저녁에 도라와 梅蓮에게 편지를 썻다. 그러나 이 편지가 닷기도 전에 그는 줄을넌지 모른다. 카주사, 椿姬, 데쓰, 가련한 운명에 죽는 여성들이 머리를 휙휙 지나간다. 梅蓮은 걸코 약한 여자가 아니다. 그러나 무엇 때문에 그러케 긔운차든 梅蓮이 죽엄으로 이거슬 이기려는가?
사랑은  괴로운가? 청춘은 허무한 슬품인가? 웨 좀더 살아서 네의 모든 재질을 다-하여 더 발휘를 못하고 그만이야 죽단말가?
편지 쓰든 거슬 찟고 내일 아츰 梅蓮에게로 찻어가기로 결심하고 누엇다. 전동이 자버린 所舍엔 창백한 달비치 은빗 화살을 펴고 잇다. 달이 보기실타. 눈을 감엇다. 오늘은 종일 아모것도 못하고 슬푸기만 하엿다.
梅蓮아 죽지마라. 혼자 입 속에 말을 외이며 잠이 드려 버렷다.

2월  日(土曜)
눈이 하아야케 싸이다.
노는 날 일요일 외의 노는 날이란 언제든지 류달리 반가웁다. M誌의 創作欄을 닑다 그 달 잡지가 책상우에 그득히 싸여 눈 압헤 어른어른하는 동안에는 다른 일이 손에 잡히지 안는다. 月初에 그 달 잡지는― 적어도 創作欄 만은 다 닑어 버리겟다는 작뎡이 달마다 틀어저 요사이 와서는 月初는 새뤄 잡지와 씨름하다나면 언으듯 한달 30일이 다 가는 것이다.
처음보는 작가의 작품. 매우 훌륭하다. 간결한 筆致가 줄기줄기 肉迫하야 오는 佳作. 심히 익숙한 솜씨가 아마도 自傳的의 소설인 듯 하다. 그러치 안으면 이러케까지 힘차게 울녀 올 수 업겟지. 前半은 진득하고 침울하고 後半은 행복스럽고 ―. 筆致에는 輕重이 업스나 後半보다 前半이 훨신 좃다. 행복스런 인생의 面이 『이이지』에 흐르기 쉬움에 反하야 역시 괴로운 인생의 面이 더 만히 훌륭한 예술이 되는 것 갓다. 또스토옙스키의 모든 작품이 아니 古今의 위대한 예술이 모다 그러듯이.
오후에 홈자 火爐 밋 숫불을 피우고 『주리야』起稿 片紙로온 부탁을 편지로 간단히 승낙하얏스나 승낙해 놋코 보니 큰 짐을 마튼 듯한 늣김이 새삼스럽게 난다. 반년동안이나 엇더케 끌어나가노 엇삽히 피를 기우려 가며 색여갈 작품도 아니요 아직 그럴 때도 아니라고 ＜109＞ 생각하지만 그러나 그러타고 또 그것이 심심푸리의 작란이 아닌 이상 괴로운 늣김은 일반이다. 全篇의 이약이도 맨들지 못라고 구상도 서지 안은 채 원고지에 향하니 머리 속이 실오리가티 어지러워 몃 시간을 걸녀 겨우 넉 장 쓰다.

밤에 「어두운데로」라는 희곡 한 막을 써 보앗다. 내가 現에 늣기고 잇는 심정을 表白하여 보앗다. 써 노코 보니 엇전지 덜된 것 갓해서 망서리엇스나 그대로 검열에 너헛다. 나는 무슨 글이든지 한꺼번에 ＜118＞ 단숨에 써버리는 버릇을 신문긔자 생활하는 동안에 어덧다. 신문긔자는 推稿를 不許한다. 오분 십분 이내에 기사 한 段 두 段씩 一氣로 써버리고 그 쓴 기사가 10분 후이면 벌서 活字化하여 나온다.
그러나 작품은 신문긔자 식으로 한거번에 쫙 써버릴 것이 아니라 하면서도 이 버릇을 아직 곳치지 못하엿다.
나는 희곡을 쓰고 십다. 희곡은 어느 일점에 가서 폭포수 떠러저 팽팽 돌 듯 무덱불이 활활 타오르듯 정열의 燃燒의 초점이 분명하게 서 잇든 것이 내 性情에 맛는 것 갓다. 그러나 나에게 희곡 쓸 才分이 잇슬가.

3월 11일
社에 나와서 三千里 4월호의 편집을 대강 마련하고 「三千里文學」 春節號의 내용을 결정하고 그러고 몃몃 동무 맛나고 社의 일로 5, 6處로 단녀오고... 그러니 벌서 해가 진다. 날마다 번복되는 이 雜務! 이 雜務에 나는 시간을 다 빼앗기고 이 雜務에 정력을 다 빼앗긴다.
밤에 山本氏의 「鮮滿」을 읽다. 그의 筆致는 동반자적 호의를 보이러 애쓴 흔적이 잇다. 가튼 일본인이면서 山本美越乃 博士는 정치학상으로 柳宗悅氏는 미술상으로 그리고 山本改造社長은 감정과 문화상으로 다 同情者的 태도를 朝鮮에 보여주는 이라 생각하엿다. 그 반대로 西條八十이나 其外 여러사람은 朝鮮을 카리칼추어 삼어 본 사람들로 불쾌한 존재들이라 생각되엿다.  ＜118＞
