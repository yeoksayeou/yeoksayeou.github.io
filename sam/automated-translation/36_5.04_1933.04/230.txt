**Body Text Translation:**
Released from Prison After Thirteen Years
						Yang Kŭn-hwan

Why have I, having experienced various situations since the incident, clung so deeply to life (生) and survived until today? Of course, it wasn't simply because I coveted "life" itself, but because I had things I needed to do.

However, the general public probably didn't expect that I would survive and re-emerge into this world today with my life intact. It wasn't just the public; even I myself couldn't be certain, so how much less could others? Setting aside the question of whether my release today is fortunate or unfortunate for me, or for all of us, the fact is I have come out into reality.

When I was leaving prison, I had somewhat different feelings. I intend to jot them down here just as they are, calling them post-release impressions. My impressions from inside prison (獄中) are written down separately, so here I intend to limit myself only to post-release impressions.

It goes without saying, as you all know, that I was released on parole (가출옥) on February 11th. However, the issue of this parole had been talked about for a very long time. Rumors had circulated for two or three years, and whenever such talk arose during meetings, the prison officials (獄吏) told me that if I corrected my previous ideology and thoughts (主義思想) and worked towards Japan-Korea Harmonization (日鮮融和), they would let me out even today.

I have an elderly mother who is 70 years old. I heard she said she just wanted to see her son once before she passed away. As my sentence dwindled to only about a year left, I tried to forget everything.

Then, it was last December. While I was in day-and-night solitary confinement (晝夜 獨居分房), there was someone I briefly wanted to meet, so I asked the person called the Guard Chief (戒護主任) for permission to go out to work (出役). He told me to wait just a little longer, so I spent the New Year like that. When I asked again, he also told me to wait, adding cryptically that if I had to stay much longer, he would send me out to the workshop, and then left. So <48> I nodded to myself, thinking, "Does that mean they'll let me out on February 11th?" Then, on the evening of February 8th, a certain guard noisily unlocked my cell door. He was a guard I was friendly with, a good person. So I greeted him happily.

"What's up? Have you brought good news?"

When I asked, the guard smiled and said he had sweet news.

Me: What's so sweet?
Guard: You're getting out this time. On the 11th.
Me: Really?
Guard: It's true.
Me: How can you know that?
Guard: I was able to find out. That's why I came to make you happy. This is still a secret. Don't tell anyone.
Me: Okay. I understand.

And so we parted. Afterwards, although my heart was half-believing and half-doubting (半信半疑), I felt somewhat emotional (感慨). After hearing that news, the days felt so incredibly long, truly long. The three days until the 11th felt so long, it was hard to bear. I couldn't sleep well at night. I'd suddenly wake up with my eyes wide open. Three days passed like this.

The morning of the 11th arrived, and even breakfast was finished. I wondered inwardly, 'Will I really get out today?' Comparing the Guard Chief's earlier words and what that certain guard had said, getting out seemed certain, but would I really be released? Weren't they deceiving me, playing a joke on me? If so, I'd be angry, I thought, my mind racing with various possibilities. In any case, I organized my belongings, stacked my books on the shelf, cleaned the room thoroughly, and wiped the glass window. From the communal cell (雜居房) below, I couldn't tell what was going on, but there was a clattering noise, like geese and ducks landing all at once in a paddy field dike and squawking loudly, then later, the sound of low laughter, chatter, and people talking softly among themselves. A guard came, shouting angrily with glaring eyes. It seemed the prisoner was newly arrived and had been making such noise. After the guard left, I could hear them whispering among themselves again.

A: Hey, Cell 6! (Though I couldn't see his face, he was calling by the cell number as he was new.)
B: Me?
A: Yeah. How many years did you get?
B: Me? I got life (無期).
A: Eh? Life. That's too bad. What did you do anyway?
B: Robbery and murder.
A: Hmm. Really. Did you steal a lot of money?
B: It was just a name, I didn't get a single penny (피천 한푼), and when I think about how things turned out like this, I really hate it.
A: Hey. Don't worry even if it's life. If it's a long term, there might be amnesty (恩賜) during that time.
B: Well, I hope it turns out like that.
A: And besides, if you work sincerely for just 10 years, there's the special favor (恩典) of parole, so if you go out to the workshop, 10 or 20 years pass in a flash. Don't worry about anything.
B: Well, others were saying similar things. So, how long does it take before they send you out to the workshop?
A: Yeah. Usually, they send you out after you've been here for about two months.
B: Really? It'll be two months for me on the 20th...
A: It'll be two months for me on the 15th. You're five days later than me.
B: When we get out to the workshop, let's work sincerely. They say the beginning <49> is important (所重).
A: Right, right.
B: How many years did you get?
A: Me? I got 12 years.
B: Oh. 12 years? 12 years isn't really a big deal. Around here, 12 years is on the short side.

While they were having this conversation, a guard rushed over again, shouting loudly (怒號), and dragged the two talking prisoners away towards the central area. Peeking quietly through the peephole, I saw a crowd of men in dark suits gathered around the two prisoners in red uniforms.

I thought to myself. Here I am, not even sure if I'll get out or not, anxiously waiting with my bags packed. In the next cell are prisoners with life and 12-year sentences who only arrived a little over a month ago. Moreover, they were just promising to work sincerely when they get out to the workshop. They got caught while talking about it and were dragged away.

Thinking about how I too had a time as a newcomer like them long ago, I was truly overcome with emotion (慷慨無量). And I thought about how life takes all sorts (人生은 色色). After the two were dragged away, they likely endured a long lecture, got hit two or three times, and then returned to their cells. Afterwards, the neighboring cell (隣房) became so quiet you couldn't even hear breathing. Only my heart was pounding, and I paced around the room, touching the four walls, sometimes gazing at the vast blue sky (廣漠한 靑天) through the gaps in the iron bars, letting out small, unavoidable sighs. Assuming I would be released now, thinking of this place that had been my residence and place for self-cultivation (修養院) for so many years, I somehow felt a sense of regret, a sense of longing, and could only shed tears. I tried lying back on the cot, walked around, and thought to myself again how ridiculous this current state of mind would be if I didn't get out today.

Believing I would be released on the 11th, Empire Day (紀元節), I waited for the time of moral instruction (敎誨). Parole releases always happened around the time of the call for preparing for moral instruction (敎誨準備呼出). I thought that if there was no call for me by then, everything would go wrong. Just then, the sound of a guard shouting "Prepare for moral instruction!" (敎誨準備) echoed from below. My heart fluttered even more. Then, the sound of the guard's shoes echoed up the corridor. And the footsteps stopped right in front of my door. "Yang, summons for you!" he said, taking down the tag hanging above my cell door, marked with my number, 1,311.

As I left my cell, I shouted loudly to my comrades in the neighboring cells,
"Hey, So-and-so, So-and-so, stay well! I'm getting out!" peeking through each observation hatch (視察口) to say goodbye. The guard hurried me, saying, "Mr. Yang, you can't look back, hurry up and go." I asked him to wait a moment, looked at my friends' faces, and as I turned back to look at my cell, I truly couldn't suppress my overwhelming emotions (感慨無量), glancing behind me as I walked out and entered the room labeled 'Office' (교무실). Three people had already been brought there. Among them were two people I could never forget. They were people from the kitchen (취사장), who, when I said I was hungry during the cold winter, would secretly bring me an extra large rice ball and slip it to me. That was against the rules (犯則) and the guards might have made trouble, but even the guards turned a blind eye. To think that I was being released today together with such dear friends (親友), my heart (心中) filled with gratitude towards them. I experienced firsthand, intuitively, that human affection (人情) truly knows no borders or social status (貴賤). The day-and-night solitary cells (晝夜 獨居의 감방) were arranged in rows like beehives (버리둥주리) on both the north <50> and south sides, 48 cells in total.

Now, becoming free men together with those friends, I grasped their hands and said, "So-and-so, So-and-so, I've been indebted to you for so long. As long as I live, I won't forget your kindness." We shook hands firmly.

Just then, Mr. Yi (이君), with whom I was on good terms, passed by outside the window. Seeing my face through the glass pane, he said, "Mr. Yang, you're getting out," and stood there, his eyes filling with tears. I opened the door, went out to him, took his hand, and said,
"We were truly good companions for a long time."
As our eyes met, he cried and I cried too. He had a life sentence (無期). I had come a year later, and now I was leaving, while he, having been there for 13 years, still had a life sentence. His words,
"Mr. Yang. You're getting out,"
sounded to me like, "Mr. Yang, you're leaving me behind alone and getting out by yourself." At that moment, I felt truly sad. This sorrow will surely remain unforgettable throughout my prison life.

2
Turning back several times, exchanging hot tears (熱淚) with the many people seeing me off, the four of us were led out by a section chief (부장) to the office (교무실). From there, led again by guards, we entered the warden's office (소장실) on the second floor. In the warden's office, there was a sign standing on the right, so I went towards it. The Guard Chief (戒護 주임), a Head Guard (看守長), stood at attention (不動起立). On the left stood the Education Chief (교무주임), a Moral Instructor (敎誨師). In the middle, Warden Yoshida Ritsu (소장 吉田律氏) was waiting for us with something on a tray. I realized it was the parole certificates (假出獄 免狀) for the five of us. I said I had one last request for the warden. It was that since all the current inmates (수형자) would be gathered in the Instruction Hall (敎誨室), I wanted permission for about 10 minutes to go there and say goodbye to everyone. However, the Guard Chief and the Education Chief refused, saying there was no precedent for such a thing. I pointed out that the prison regulations clearly stated (明文) that, depending on the circumstances, parolees could be made to stand before an assembly to give farewell remarks, but my request was ultimately denied under the pretext of saying goodbye individually later.

Then, the Education Chief mentioned that Ch'oe Sŭng-man (崔承萬) from the youth association (청년회) and one other person had come and were waiting outside. I asked if it was only two people, and he said yes. Hearing it was only two people, I felt somewhat desolate (寂寞).

A chilling feeling suddenly spun through my heart (心中), making my feet halt and my head bow involuntarily. The desolate feeling at that moment must have dealt a chilling, fatal blow to my motivation to act.

After 12 years, I was coming out of prison, and only two people came to meet me? Could it be that only those two of my people (민족) lived in Tōkyō?

Of course, even if not a single person had come out, it wouldn't have prevented my release. There would be no change or obstacle to my ideological activities. But somehow, a strange feeling like disappointment (落憺) arose in my heart. I felt heartache and a forlornness akin to the state of rejected love (失戀). (Continued in the next issue) <51>

**Summary**

Yang Kŭn-hwan recounts his final days in prison and the moment of his release on parole after 13 years. He describes the anticipation mixed with doubt, his emotional interactions with fellow inmates (including those newly arrived and one serving a life sentence whom he leaves behind), and the procedures surrounding his release. Prison officials had previously offered early release if he renounced his ideology for "Japan-Korea Harmonization." Upon finally leaving, he is deeply moved by farewells from inmates but feels a profound sense of desolation and disappointment when told only two people have come to meet him outside, questioning the support from his community in Tōkyō.

From a historical perspective, the article offers a valuable first-person account of the psychological experience of a Korean political prisoner under Japanese colonial rule. It highlights the colonial authorities' attempts at ideological conversion ("Japan-Korea Harmonization"), the conditions and social dynamics within the prison (interactions between guards and inmates, different sentences like 'life' vs. fixed terms, the parole system), and the complex emotions—relief, sorrow, gratitude, and disillusionment—accompanying release for an independence activist. The mention of Empire Day (紀元節) as the release date and the specific names involved add concrete details to the narrative of colonial imprisonment.

十三年만에 出獄하여
						梁槿煥
내가 사건이래 여러 가지의 경우를 격거가면서 살어나자하고 生에 孰着깁히 오늘까지 살어 온 까닭이 무엇일가. 그것은 물론 「生」 그것에 단지 탐내어 그랫슬 뿐 아니라 나는 나로서 할 일이 잇서 그리함이다.
그리치만 세상 일반사람들은 내가 오늘 생명을 가지고 이 세상에 다시 사라나올 줄은 뜻하지 안엇슬 것이다. 그것은 단지 세상 사람이 그리하엿슬 뿐 아니라 내 자신조차 확신을 가지지 못하엿스니 더구나 타인이엇스랴. 오늘 내가 출옥한 것이 나에게 아니 모든 우리들에게 행복할가 행복치 못할가 하는 문제는 次置하고 엇잿은 나는 현실로 나와 온 것이다.
나는 감옥에서 나올 때에 약간 심정을 달니한 바가 잇섯다. 그것을 출옥 후의 감상이라하야 그대로 적어보려고 한다. 獄中에 잇서서의 감상은 따로 記述하여 잇슴으로 여기서는 단지 출옥 후의 감상에 끗치어 두려한다.
去 2월 11일에 내가 가출옥 석방으로서 출옥한 일은 더 말할 것 업시 여러분이 아실 일이나 이 가출옥의 문제는 대단히 녜전부터 이약이가 잇서왓다. 2, 3년 전부터 그 소문이 잇섯스며 회의때에 그러한 말이 나면 獄吏는 나에게 종전의 主義思想을 곳치어 日鮮融和을 도모하여 준다면 오늘이라도 내어보내 준다고 하엿다.
나는 70세의 고령에나는 늙은 어머니가 게시다. 老母는 생전에 다만 한번 자식을 맛나보고 도라기시고 십다고 하신다고 한다. 이럭저럭 刑期가 1년밧게 남지 안엇슬 때 나는 모든 것을 이저버리려 하엿다.
그런데 작년 섯달이엇다. 내가 晝夜 獨居分房에 잇슬 때에 잠간 맛나고 십흔 사람이 잇서 出役을 戒護主任이라고 하는 사람에게 청하여 보앗더니 조곰만 더 참으라고 함으로 그대로 설을 지내엇다. 그러고 또 한번 청하여 본 즉 역시 기다려 달나고 하면서 만일 더 길게 오래도록 잇게되면 공장에 내보내 주겟다고 「수수꺽기」가튼 말을 남기면서 자기는 나가버린다. 그래서 ＜48＞ *나는 「그러면 올치 2월 11일에는 내어 주겟다는 말인가.」하고 혼자 고개를 끄덕이엿다. 그런 뒤 2월 8일 저녁때에 엇든 간수가 나의 감방을 절칵절칵 열엇다. 그는 나와 친한 간수로서 인품이 조흔 사람이엇다. 그래서 나는 깁부게 마즈면서
「웬일이냐 무슨 조흔 소식 가저왓느냐.」
하고 무른 즉 간수는 우스면서 달큼한 이약이가 잇서 왓다고 한다.
나―무엇이 달쿰하냐?
간수―자네는 이번에는 나가네. 이 11일에는.
나―정말인가.
간수―참말이다.
나―그것을 알 수 잇나.
간수―알 수 잇섯다. 그래서 자네를 깁부게 하여주려고 왓다. 이 일은 아직 비밀이다. 남에게 이약이하면 안된다.
나―응. 알엇노라.
이리하고 갈나젓다. 그런 뒤 나의 심정은 半信半疑이면서도 다소의 感慨가 잇섯다. 그 소리를 드른 뒤 나는 하로 동안이 엇더케도 긴지 너무 길어서 참으로 길어서 11일까지의 사흘 사이가 엇더케 길든지 참기 어려웟다. 밤잠도 잘 오지 안엇다. 자다가도 눈이 번쩍 떠지엇다. 이리하면서 사흘이 지나갓다.
11일의 아츰은 와서 아츰 밥까지 끗낫다. 내심 생각하기를 정말 오늘 나가질가. 전날 戒護 主任이 한 말이나 某看守가 한 말을 대조하여 생각할 때에 나가논 일은 틀님업는 일 가트나 정말 나가질가. 나를 속이며 나를 놀니는 말이 아니엇슬가. 그러타면 화가 날 일이라 생각되면서 여러 가지로 마음이 설네엇다. 엇지되엇든 나는 짐을 정리하고 나의 책을 선반 우에 싸어올니고 방안을 깨끗하게 掃除하고 유리창을 닥것다. 아래의 雜居房에서는 무슨 일인지 알 수 업스나 덜넝덜넝하며 바로 기럭이와 오리가 한꺼번에 논드렁에 안저 소리치드시 요란거리다가 나종에 나즉하게 웃고 짓거리며 서로 나즉하게 이약이하는 소리가 난다. 간수가 怒하며 눈을 부르대고 왓다. 그 죄수은 처음 드러온 모양이 되어 그러케 떠든 모양이엇다. 간수가 간 뒤에 자긔네끼리 또 속살거리는 소리가 들닌다.

乙. 나냐.
甲. 응. 너는 몃해냐.
乙. 나 말이야. 나는 無期다.

11일 紀元節에 나는 나가지리라 하든 생각으로 敎誨의 시각을 기다리고 잇섯다. 언제든지 가출옥의 例가 敎誨準備呼出의 사이엿다. 나도 그때까지 긔다려 보아서 호출이 업스면 일이 다 빗구러 질 것이라 하엿다. 그럴 때 아래로부터 敎誨準備라고 怒號하는 간수의 소리가 들니어 왓다. 나의 심정은 더군다나 설네어왓다. 그리고는 간수의 구두발 소리가 랑하로 울니어 왓다. 그리고는 발이 나의 門前에 와 닷다. 「梁君 호출이야.」하면서 나의 番 1,311이라고 기록한 감방 우에 걸닌 패를 떼는 것이엇다.
나는 내 감방을 나서면서 큰 소리로 겻방 동무들에게
「여보게 某君, 某君 잘 잇게. 나는 나가네.」하면서 視察口를 일일히 열며 인사 하엿다. 간수는 梁君 엿보면 안된다고 어서 나가라고 졸는다. 나는 조곰 기다리라고 友人들의 얼골을 엿보면서 나는 내 감방을 돌려다 볼 때에 實로 感慨無量한 심정을 금할 수 업시 뒤로 도라보며 나와서 교무실이라고 쓰인 방으로 드러갓다. 거기에는 벌서 세 사람쯤 끄을려 와 잇다. 그 중에 두 사람은 나로서는 이즐 수 업는 사람이엇다. 그는 취사장에 잇는 사람으로 겨을 추을 때 내가 배 곱흐다고 하면 밥덩이를 따로 크다란 것을 갓다가 가만히 너허주든 사람이다. 그것은 犯則이라하여 간수도 말성을 부릴 것으로 생각되나 간수도 그것은 묵인하여 주엇다. 그리든 親友들과 오늘 가치 출옥되는가 함애 내 心中은 그이에 대한 감사의 念으로 차섯다. 人情에는 實로 국경이 업고 貴賤이 업는 것을 直觀的으로 체험한 것이다. 晝夜 獨居의 감방은 南北＜50＞兩側에 마치 *버리둥주리와 가치 列지어 지은 48房이 잇섯다.

그리고 교무주임은 밧갓헤 청년회의 崔承萬 과 또 한 사람이 와서 기다린다고 하기에 나는 두 사람 뿐이더냐고 무른즉 그러타고 한다. 나는 두 사람이란 말에 다소 寂寞를 늣기엇다.
心中에는 핑도는 차듸찬 기분은 갑작히 나를 을시켜발을 머믈느게하고 머리를 저절로 숙이게 하엿다. 그때의 적막한 심정은 적어도 나를 식혀 활동력에 치명상을 주엇슬 것이다.
내가 12년만에 감옥을 나오는데 단지 두 사람이 마중 나와 주엇든가. 東京에 나의 민족은 저네들 둘 뿐박게 살지 안엇슬가.
