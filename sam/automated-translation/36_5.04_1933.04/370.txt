**Summary**

This 1933 play/scenario by Yi Sŏ-gu depicts a modern Korean couple in colonial Seoul, Ch'ŏl and Hong, who settle everyday decisions, like paying tram fares or buying movie tickets and dinner, through inventive forms of gambling. They use the last digits of tram conductor and driver numbers, book page numbers, and even hold a bedbug race to decide who pays. Despite living together, they maintain separate finances. The story humorously culminates with Hong winning the bedbug race and "rewarding" her champion bedbug by letting it feed on her arm after Ch'ŏl pays for their Chinese meal. For historians, the piece offers a glimpse into the lives of urban, "modern" Koreans during the colonial period, highlighting changing social dynamics (cohabitation, separate finances, Western clothing, leisure activities like movies), the blending of Korean and Japanese cultural elements (gambling terms, magazines like *Fujin Kōron* and *Kaizō*), and a perhaps surprising normalization of petty gambling in everyday life, even staging a comical bedbug race. The casual mention of specific locations like the Bank of Chosŏn and Honmachi adds realistic detail.
