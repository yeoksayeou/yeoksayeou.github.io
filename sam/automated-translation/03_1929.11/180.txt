財産과 年齡
Fortune and Age

A country bumpkin (村夫子) went looking for a certain *gisaeng* house in Tabang-gol (茶坊谷) and asked a *gisaeng*, "How old are you?"
"I'm eighteen."

Several years later, he went again and asked that *gisaeng* her age once more. The *gisaeng*, not recognizing him from before, acted cute and said,
"I'm seventeen."

After another three years, he went and asked again.
"I'm sixteen."

Hearing this, the man burst into tears (쫙쫙 운다). The *gisaeng*, realizing only then that she had swindled money from him and shown dishonesty by lying about her age, regretted it.
"Oh, please forgive me. Is it because I lied about my age?"
"No. It's because your age decreasing more and more is just like my business capital decreasing more and more, so I'm upset."

處女의 心理
A Maiden's Psychology

A young man asked his beloved for a kiss for the first time.
"Oh my, I can't. What if someone sees?"
After refusing, she waited a moment and then, slyly (슴겁게도),
"My, it's so quiet here. It seems like nobody is around."

**Summary**

This article consists of two short humorous anecdotes. The first story describes a country man distraught because a *gisaeng* he repeatedly visits claims to be younger each time, reminding him of his own decreasing business capital. The second describes a young woman initially refusing a kiss for propriety's sake but quickly pointing out the lack of witnesses. These simple jokes reflect social interactions and perhaps common anxieties (money) and behaviors (courtship coyness, the precariousness of a *gisaeng*'s life) of the time. For historians, these pieces illustrate the type of light content published in popular magazines during the colonial era, offering insights into social norms, humor, and possibly underlying economic concerns presented in a non-political format.
