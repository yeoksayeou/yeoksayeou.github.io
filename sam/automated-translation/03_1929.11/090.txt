世界一週紀行(第三信), 復活하는 愛蘭과 英吉利의 姿態
						許憲
The Desolation of Ireland's Mountains and Rivers

Dear friends!
Several days after sending my second letter to you all back home from America, on the morning of January 15th, leaving behind the soot and motor sounds of New York City, I went to the pier and boarded a ship bound for Ireland.
Although I wished to stay longer in America – not only has it reached the highest stage as a capitalist nation (ellipsis - original text) but I also wanted to observe more of this country's political and economic structure, social conditions, and the like – my onward journey was urgent, so I decided to depart. Even so, during my several months' stay in America, it's not that I didn't observe the temperament of this country's populace or "Americanism," which, standing at the opposite pole from Labor-Farmer Russia, is captivating the world's culture. However, most of it pertains to current affairs and politics, so I lack the freedom to discuss it through the "Samch'ŏlli" magazine, and thus decided to pass over it. In truth, I also gave dozens of speeches until my throat was hoarse in various cities, both for resident compatriots and for Europeans and Americans as requested. Conversely, there were many times I deliberately sought out famous figures there, grasped their wrists, and conferred eagerly, but surely you can imagine even if I don't write about it.
In any case, on the 22nd of that month, I arrived at the famous port of "Queenstown" in Southern Ireland. As we crossed the Atlantic, the ship's purser pointed out various ocean spots, explaining things like, "Over there is where the steamship 'Dardanelles' sank several years ago after hitting an iceberg," or "That's where the combined Anglo-French fleet was sunk multiple times by German submarines during the Great European War." Hearing these explanations truly gave me goosebumps anew. To think that all sorts of dreadful tragedies occurred upon this very ocean, flowing so peacefully without a care, and that even now, beneath the sea our ship is currently traversing, tens of thousands of souls might still be crying out – somehow, it made my head feel cold. Moreover, as a lonely traveler wandering the ends of the earth, gazing upon the vast expanse of the Atlantic dyed by the setting sun, I couldn't suppress the nostalgic and sentimental emotions that arose in that moment.
Now, speaking of the "Queenstown" port where I arrived, it is commonly called the Queen's Village (皇后村). Until not long ago, it was a splendid city with dazzling streets. However, during the centuries under British rule, incessant wars led to its indescribable devastation. Everywhere, buildings bearing the baptism of gunfire and damaged streets evoked a feeling of wretchedness. Taking advantage of the 8-hour stopover while the ship was docked at this port, I, along with the head of the Canadian Sinn Féin Party branch and his wife who were fellow passengers, hailed a taxi. We drove through the city streets and went up to the local park. The Irish fields visible in all directions looked like battlefields, devoid of fertility; trees stood as they were burned, and roads and bridges were often destroyed. As for the dwellings, where could one find even one as well-kept as those seen in America? Truly, the phrase "a scene of utter desolation" seemed to describe this place. Nevertheless, recently, after becoming a Free State, it seems reconstruction efforts by the new government are actively underway. New<15> roadside trees are beginning to be planted along the streets, the city districts are being reorganized, and the faces of the Irish people coming and going show a glimmer of hope and passion. Seeing this sight, I thought of the mythical "Phoenix" rising from the ashes, shaking its wings. Was it fitting to associate the undying bird with Ireland and the Irish people?

The Grand Structure of the Courthouse

Dear friends!
Leaving "Queenstown," I briefly passed through the English port of "Liverpool" and, after 4 hours, arrived at the port of "Kingstown" in Northern Ireland. This Emperor's Village (皇帝村), as it's called, is the renowned port of Northern Ireland, just as the aforementioned Queen's Village (皇后村) is the famous port of Southern Ireland. These two ports, positioned like the ends of an hourglass drum on either side, are said to handle the majority of the Irish Free State's civilization and all its national economy. From here, one takes a train directly into Dublin, the capital of Ireland, which is said to be only a 40-minute ride, quite similar to the relationship between our Seoul and Inch'ŏn Port.
Now, as I placed great importance on Ireland for this world tour, I had already made many arrangements for my Irish trip while in America. Specifically, while studying language at the Peekskill summer resort in America, I became closely acquainted with Dr. M, who leads tens of thousands of Irish-Americans and serves as the head of the New York State branch of the Sinn Féin Party. I carried a lengthy letter of introduction from him, kindly addressed to Mr. de Valera. Also, when I went to the Irish Consulate General in New York to obtain a visa for my travel permit and met the Consul General, it was as if he had already heard about me and was waiting. He explained the Irish situation in detail and, furthermore, wrote letters introducing me to various prominent figures in Irish politics, which I also carried. For me, traveling to Ireland for the first time, this was indeed a great gain. Incidentally, besides the British Consulate General, there is a stately Irish Consulate General in New York, handling all matters concerning the Irish people. Since becoming a Free State, even British diplomats cannot interfere one bit in Ireland's diplomacy. Ireland was truly free. Having cast off domination, it was now independent in both name and reality!
Upon arriving in "Dublin," I immediately hailed a taxi and, around 3 PM that day, went to the Dublin National University. I had been told to go to the National University to meet Mr. de Valera. So, I entered the administrative office, but it turned out Mr. de Valera had left for Southern Ireland two weeks prior on Sinn Féin Party business. As I stood there wondering what to do, an elderly professor from the university, Dr. B, came out and asked what my business was. I explained that I was a Korean visiting Ireland and presented the letter of introduction I brought from Peekskill, America. He welcomed me warmly, led me into the reception room, and showed me affection as if we were old friends of ten years, asking how I had come from so far away. Moreover, he later expressed great regret that he couldn't personally guide me because it wasn't his teaching time. He immediately telephoned the Irish Government's Home Ministry for me. Then he told me that a secretary from the cabinet would be waiting for me in front of the Government Buildings gate at nine o'clock the next morning, and I should go there at that time. Feeling quite pleased by the sincere efforts of the old professor in arranging things, I returned to the "hotel" that day to rest my weary legs. Ireland's political environment (ellipsis - original text) might lead one to treat foreigners from similar situations<16> differently, but I hadn't expected them to be this helpful.
The next morning, as instructed, I went to the Government Buildings. Armed guards stood watch densely at the entrance, and the tumultuous atmosphere of the early days of the Free State filled the air both inside and out. A gentleman standing among the guards spotted me, asked if I was "Mr. Hŏ," and welcomed me inside the Government Buildings. After waiting there for about 5 minutes, a female official, said to be equivalent to a Vice Minister of Home Affairs, came out and greeted me kindly. She spoke pure, rustic Irish, while I barely managed English, making our conversation quite slow and difficult. Perhaps finding it frustrating, she asked if I knew Chinese. When I replied that I knew both Chinese and Japanese, she immediately called a "secretary" and summoned a Chinese student studying at the National University. (I was told there were no Japanese or Korean students studying in Ireland.) A little later, a young Chinese man, said to be from Suzhou in the south, came in. However, the Chinese interpretation through him made communication even harder, so I declined further assistance. From then on, I took out an English-Korean dictionary, and the two of us conversed while looking through the book for quite some time. We both broke a sweat, but the content of our discussion was important and uninhibited.
After that, the Vice Minister telephoned the clerks of the Irish Parliament (which was then in session), the High Court, and the Court of Appeal for me, so I left there intending to observe the Parliament and the Courts.
Thus, I immediately proceeded to visit the Courthouse. I had already heard about the grandeur of the Irish court structure, but indeed, in terms of its building and facilities, it was by no means inferior to the famous London Courts. Ireland also had a three-tiered system – High Court, Court of Appeal (覆審 - Review Trial), and Local Court (地方). Unlike the legal circles in Britain and America, Ireland uses much unwritten law, so the courtroom was filled with case precedents. Truly, from behind the Chief Justice's seat to behind the seats for the defendant and spectators, the entire area consisted of library walls (藏書壁), fully stocked with case precedents arranged chronologically. Therefore, even during a trial, the Chief Justice, defendant, or lawyers could freely open the wall cabinets, look up precedents, and proceed with the trial. Moreover, having so many domestic and foreign books readily available in the courthouse seemed rare even in Britain or America, let alone in the East. The lawyers' waiting room (辯護士控室) also had its own dedicated library (專屬圖書舘), and seeing that the judges (判事) and prosecutors (檢事) all had such excellent library facilities, the vigor of this emerging nation (新興國家), eagerly absorbing the latest knowledge (最新智識), was astonishing (驚歎).
Taking advantage of the person who bustled about for me when I went to the courthouse, three male judges, one female judge, and several Irish lawyers held a temporary welcoming banquet for me in the dining hall and treated me hospitably. (ellipsis - original text) Then, leaving the courthouse, I immediately set off for a prison tour (監獄求景). The prison was large, clean, well-ventilated and lit (採風通光), and its hygienic conditions were quite enviable. Inside, there was a theater (演劇場), a radio (라듸요), and a large-scale library. Outside of the designated factory labor hours (勞役時間), the prisoners dressed neatly in Western suits ("세비로") and moved about freely. For example, baseball tournaments (競技大會), dances (舞蹈會), and concerts (音樂會) were held inside. Furthermore, on Saturdays and Sundays, prisoners' families, especially their beloved wives, were allowed to come into the cells and spend one or two enjoyable days together before leaving. This truly made me realize the different level of magnanimity (襟度) of a civilized nation.
I, too, was invited by the prison to lecture before the inmates on one or two occasions.

The China Problem and the Parliament

Dear friends!
Leaving the courthouse and courts, I took the visitor's passes for both the Upper and Lower Houses given to me earlier by the Vice Minister of Home Affairs and went to observe the Irish Parliament. When I entered, the Lower House agenda involved some legislative debate, which was uninteresting, so I quickly left and went to the Upper House. In the Upper House, out of a total of 65 or 66 seats, only 3 or 4 were vacant (缺席); otherwise, the chamber was completely full. Everyone was agitated (亢奮), spitting fire from the tips of their tongues (舌端火), fiercely debating (激烈히 論戰) national affairs. I heard from the government secretary who accompanied me as my guide that the agenda for that day happened to be a discussion of the China problem. As you probably know, this was the time when Zhang Zuolin (張作霖) had been driven out, died on his way to Manchuria (滿州), and Chiang Kai-shek (蔣介石) had seized power, coinciding with the successive occurrences of the Nanjing Incident and the Jinan Incident, leading Britain to dispatch large numbers of troops (出兵) to China (中國). Every day, the newspapers carried articles stirring up the public, such as "Today, Britain dispatched several thousand marines and military supplies aboard several warships bound for China." At that time, the Irish Parliament was debating whether or not to bear the military expenses (軍事費) for this British troop dispatch to China. Ultimately, that day, the motion was unanimously rejected (否決), based on the reasoning that while many British people might reside in China currently, only 7 citizens of the Irish Free State resided there, thus there was no need to pay (支拂) for a useless military deployment. That day, as I sat in the upstairs visitors' gallery (階上 傍聽席), along with whispers from the parliamentary floor, many people kept glancing at me attentively. Even when members of parliament addressed the speaker and began their speeches, they would first look meaningfully towards my seat before starting. I became the center of attention in this parliamentary chamber,<17> but I could sense that their attitude was not dismissive in the slightest. Later, the secretary told me that the members of the Irish Upper House thought I was someone like a counselor from the Chinese Legation in London who had specifically come to hear Ireland's fair public opinion (輿論) on the British troop deployment issue. Afterwards, when I had the opportunity to talk with members of both Houses, they asked if I was "Chinese." I frankly explained that I was not Chinese but Korean, having a relationship with China like lips and teeth (脣齒의 關係). They seemed very pleased to hear this and said that Ireland currently holds great expectations (期待) for and does not spare its support (援助) for the newly emerging Chinese nation. They also expressed a strong desire to know more about the situation in Korea (ellipsis - original text) and asked me to send many newspapers, magazines, and books after returning to my country. This request came not only from the parliamentarians but was also eagerly sought by various universities, the courthouse, and other organizations. Upon arriving in London, I first sent Korean newspapers and materials about the Korean situation to several places. Also, using my credentials as a staff member of the Tong-a Ilbo (동아일보), I visited the office of Dublin's largest newspaper, called "The Independent" ("인덴펜스"), met its senior staff, and obtained much valuable information about Ireland's present and past.
Even calling this a superficial account (수박 것할키 - watermelon rind scratching) feels too absurdly inadequate, as I have to omit so much I wish to write, making it utterly bland (슴슴하다). I feel apologetic for continuing with such talk. Thus, I will conclude my account of the resurrecting Ireland here and now turn my travelogue's steps towards England, said to be the world's greatest power.

A Robbery Commotion?

Dear friends!
When I go to a foreign land, I customarily first buy a map of the city, then buy and carry a compass (滋石), and then seek out fellow countrymen. The map is a newcomer's precaution to understand the city's transport network; the compass is needed to know the directions—east, south, west, north—day or night, especially in places like England where heavy fog often obscures vision; and seeking our brethren is for requesting guidance and understanding our people's situation. So, I left "Dublin" city, arrived in London, and immediately took a morning train to "Cambridge" city. My purpose was to first meet an old acquaintance (故舊), Pak Sŏk-yun (朴錫胤), who was at the Law Faculty of Cambridge University. Cambridge was large. It seemed like the entire city of over 200,000 citizens lived because of the university. Grand university buildings (宏壯한 大學校舍) covered the whole city, while merchants seemed to surround them, conducting their business (營業). Both Oxford University (牛津大學) and this Cambridge University are undoubtedly world-renowned, but I hadn't realized the buildings would be this ancient and grand in scale.
Eventually, around 4:30 PM that day, I found my way to the Law Faculty where Mr. Pak was studying. The Law Faculty was located quite far out, almost in the suburbs, past the main city streets. I went to the dormitory (寄宿舍),<18> but Mr. Pak, who should have been there as I had notified him in advance (通寄), was absent. Feeling quite disappointed (失望), I waited anxiously (苦待) until seven o'clock, wandering through the university lecture halls and library. But Mr. Pak still didn't show up.
The big problem was that I absolutely had to return to London tonight. The reason was that early tomorrow morning, along with the special correspondent from the Osaka Mainichi Shimbun (大阪每日新聞), I was scheduled to visit the London Times Newspaper Company (倫敦「타임스」新聞社) in my capacity as a special correspondent for the Tong-a Ilbo (東亞日報). We had made a firm appointment (約束) with the Times, so I had to go regardless of anything else. The departure time for the last train to London was rapidly approaching (박두하다), but Mr. Pak still hadn't appeared. Unable to wait any longer, I rushed out of the university grounds. Not a soul could be seen on the dim (어듁컴컴한) street, and the electric streetlights were visible only one every four or five blocks (四五馬場).
I decided to leave for London. Facing the direction where the station should be, I clenched my fists and started running bent over. Perhaps because England is a nation of gentlemen, there were no pedestrians after sunset, and certainly no taxis or the like. So, without really seeing the path, I ran for a while when I saw a man and woman walking arm-in-arm, murmuring softly (喃喃接語). They seemed like an engaged couple or perhaps a pair of young lovers whispering sweet nothings. But was this a time to be observing others? Knowing it was rude, I went up to them and abruptly asked the way to the station and whether the last train to London might still be running. The young gentleman pointed with the tip of his walking stick (스텍기), saying if I went this way and then turned that way, I'd reach the station, but there wasn't much time left for the last train, so it was highly doubtful whether I'd make it. After bowing once, I clenched my fists again and began my "marathon" (마라손). I took off my overcoat and clutched it in one hand... When I had run perhaps two or three Korean *li*, someone behind me started shouting "Mister!! Mister!" at the top of their lungs and chasing after me. I stopped abruptly. The sound kept coming closer from the direction I had run. My heart sank. In America, robbers often approached like this in deserted places, threatened you with a six-shooter (六穴砲), ordered you to raise your hands, and then forcibly took (强奪) your money and valuables. Thinking this, I was afraid I'd lose my life if I acted rashly, and I lost all strength to flee further (逃亡). In such situations, fleeing often resulted in being shot dead. Truly, how could my spirit return home in indignation if I were to suffer a dog's death (犬死) at the hands of a robber, nameless in a foreign land thousands of miles away?
I quickly untied the money belt (錢袋) from my waist. It contained the remaining cash of over 8,000 Yen, after selling land back home for 12,000 Yen to see the world once, spending over 2,000 Yen in America and Ireland, and giving 1,000 Yen to my daughter Chŏng-suk who was left behind in America. I had wanted to deposit it in a bank and withdraw it as needed in each country I visited, but the troublesome procedures involved led me to exchange it all into high-denomination bills of 100 and 1000 dollars, put them in the money belt, and take it out to use whenever needed during my travels.
The person chasing me drew steadily closer. I quickly rolled up the money belt tightly and held it in my right hand. My plan was that if the other party threatened me to raise my hands, I would pretend to comply while quickly throwing the money belt into the grassy patch (풀밧) beside me. However, as the person got closer, I saw that the one shouting "Mister! Mister!" was none other than the young gentleman who had given me directions earlier. Panting heavily, he explained, "After sending you off just now, my sweetheart said you seemed like a foreigner who was probably lost and suffering, so I should quickly go and help you." He then walked ahead, briskly leading the way to the station. Just hearing his words, could there be anything more appreciated? However, it somehow felt too much like a miracle (奇蹟), and I couldn't bring myself to trust his words. Unable to relax (放心), feeling like I was 'eating mustard while crying' (울며 겨우자 먹기 - doing something reluctantly/suspiciously), I followed behind him for a while. After some distance, we came to a lake, and in the middle of the lake, there was a small path (오솔길). The gentleman suggested taking this path as it was much shorter. I refused point-blank in the first instance. Who knew if that fellow intended to push me into the water midway across the lake (湖水중구픔)? Brushing aside his earnest explanation (懇切叮嚀) that taking the main road would mean going three or four times farther, I insisted on walking along the main road. He reluctantly followed me. After a while, we arrived at the station. There was still time before the train departed. Only then did the gentleman seem satisfied. He said he had to return as his sweetheart would be waiting for him back where they were, and prepared to leave. It was only then that I realized the magnanimity of this English gentleman and felt ashamed of myself for having suspected him of being a robber. We had tea at a tea shop (喫荼店), shook hands several times, and parted ways. Among Englishmen, such a person was truly an honest and noble (正直高潔) gentleman. Now, I am heading to London. I am scheduled to meet "MacDonald" and also visit the Times newspaper office. My heart feels an inexplicable excitement. Well then, dear friends, stay well until I write again. Perhaps heavy snow has fallen in our Asian homeland. It is cold here too, so I put on my overcoat (外套) when I get inside the train car (車깐). (To be continued in the next issue) (19)

**Summary**

Hŏ Hŏn recounts his journey from New York to Ireland, describing the desolate state of Queenstown but also the signs of hopeful reconstruction following independence, likening Ireland to a phoenix. In Dublin, facilitated by introductions arranged in the US, he visits the National University, meets officials, and is impressed by the well-equipped, precedent-filled courthouses and modern prisons reflecting the vigor of the new state. He observes the Irish Parliament debating (and rejecting) funding for Britain's military intervention in China, noting the parliamentarians' interest in him, mistaken for a Chinese official, and their subsequent curiosity about Korea. The letter concludes with an anecdote from Cambridge, England, where, rushing for a train, he mistakes a helpful young man for a robber, only to be guided safely to the station, reflecting on the character of the "English gentleman" before heading to London.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for its firsthand Korean perspective on post-independence Ireland in 1929, drawing implicit parallels between Ireland's struggle against British rule and Korea's situation under Japan. Hŏ Hŏn's focus on symbols of Irish sovereignty (consulate, parliament debating foreign policy independently of Britain, national university, revitalized institutions) and the visible signs of national reconstruction would have resonated strongly with Korean readers yearning for their own independence. His interactions highlighting international awareness (or lack thereof) regarding Korea, contrasted with the attention given to China, and the efforts to share information about Korea abroad, are also noteworthy.

愛蘭山川의 荒凉

親愛하는 여러분이시어!
나는 美國에서 본국 게신 여러분에게 둘재번 올니는 便紙를 써 부친 뒤 수일 뒤인 1月 15日 새벽에 煤煙과 「모-터」소리에 잠긴 紐育市街를 뒤에 두고 埠頭에 나가 愛蘭가는 배를 집어탓소이다.
美國에 더 잇스면서 資本主義國家로 가장 高度의 段階에 이르럿슬 뿐더러 (中畧-원문) 이 나라의 政治와 經濟組織이며 社會事情等을 더 만히 살피고 십헛스나 압길이 급한지라, 그냥 떠나기로 한 터이외다. 그러터래도 美國에 여러 달 留하는 사이에 이 나라 民衆의 氣質이란다든지 또 勞農露西亞와 兩極端에 잇서서 世界의 文化를 風靡하고 잇는 「아몌리카이즘」을 본 것이 업는 것이 아니지만 大槪는 時事와 政治에 關係되는 것임으로 「三千里」誌를 通하야 말슴들일 自由가 업서서 그냥 지내가기로 한 것이외다. 실상 저도 여러 都市에서 在留同胞들을 爲하여 또는 歐美人을 爲하여 請하는대로 목이 쉬게 演說도 數十次하엿고 그 反對로 내가 저곳 名士를 일부러 차저서 손목을 붓잡고 열렬히 協義한 일도 만사오나 그를 아니 적는다고 여러분께서 想像도 못하여 주시랴.
좌우간 나는 그 달 22日에 南部愛蘭의 有名한 港口 「쿠인스타운」에 到着하엿소이다. 太平洋을 건널 때에 그 배 事務長이 일일이 海洋을 指摘하면서 저기는 몃 해 전에 氷山에 부드처 「다-다넬」號 汽船이 沈沒된 곳이라거니 또 저기는 英佛聯合艦隊가 歐洲大戰亂때에 獨逸潛航艇때문에 여러 번을 擊沈當하든 곳이라든지 하는 說明을 드를 때에 실노 새삼스럽게 몸에 소름이 끼치더이다. 저 아모 근심업시 洋洋히 흐르는 바다몰 우에 온갖 두러운 悲劇이 이르낫겟거니 그래서 우리 배가 지금 지내는 이 바다 밋 속에도 數萬의 生靈들이 아직 呼哭하며 잇겟거니하면 엇전지 머리가 차지더이다. 더구나 天涯에 흐르는 孤客이매 落照에 물드린 太西洋의 茫茫한 海上을 바라볼 때에 그 瞬間에 鄕愁에 아울러 이러한 感想的 情懷가 이러남을 禁할 길이 업더이다.
그런데 내가 지금 도착한 「쿠인스, 타운」港으로 말하면 俗稱 皇后村이라 하야 얼마 전까지도 市街燦然한 훌융한 都市이더니 數百年來 英國領이 되어 오는 동안에 不絶히 이러나는 戰爭때문에 그만 말할 수 업시 荒廢하여저서 處處에 銃火의 洗禮를 밧은 建物과 破損된 街路때문에 悽慘한 늑김을 가지게 하더이다. 나는 8時間을 이 港口에 배가 淀泊하는 틈을 타써 同船하엿든 「캐나다」 신펜 黨支部長 夫婦와 함께 택시를 불너 타고 市街를 지나 그 곳 公園에까지 올라가 보앗는데 四方에 보이는 愛蘭의 田野도 모다 戰爭을 치고 난 자리가치 지름끼라고 업고 樹木도 불에 탄대로 잇고 道路나 橋梁도 破毁된 것이 만헛더이다. 民家의 建物인들 美國에서 보든 것 가치 整美한 것이 하나인들 어대에 잇스리까. 實로 滿目悽慘하다 함은 이를 가르친 듯 하더이다. 그래도 요지음에는 自由國이 된 뒤에 新政府의 손으로 復興事業이 盛히 이러나는 모양으로 길가마다 새로운<15> 街路樹가 서기 始作하고 또 市區도 改正이 되며 左往右來하는 愛蘭人의 얼골 우에도 希望과 情熱의 빗치 떠오르더이다. 나는 이 모양을 보고 잿 속에서 날개를 털고 이러나는 「不死鳥」라는 새를 생각하엿소이다. 愛蘭과 愛蘭民族을 보고 죽지 안는 새를 聯想한 것이 엇재 올흔갓해서요.

結搆雄大한 裁判所

親愛하는 여러분이시여!
나는 다시 「쿠인스, 타운」을 떠나 英國 「립퍼불」港口를 잠시 거처 4時間만에 北部愛蘭에 잇는 「캉스, 타운」港口에 倒着하엿소이다. 이 皇帝村이라 함은 北部愛蘭의 名港口요 또 앗가의 皇后村이라는 것이 南部愛蘭에 엇는 名港口로 되어 이 두 항구가 장구 모양으로 량쪽에 벌러잇스면서 愛蘭自由國의 文明과 온갖 國家의 經濟를 大部分 呑吐한다 하더이다. 여기서 汽車로 愛蘭 서울인 「떠부린」市를 直入하는 터이니 겨우 車로 40分만 가면 된다 한즉 마치 우리 서울과 仁川港의 關係와 恰似하다 할 것이외다.
그런데 나는 이번 世界一週 旅行에 愛蘭에 몹시 置重하엿스니만치 美國잇슬 때에 벌서 愛蘭旅行의 만흔 便宜를 가젓섯스니 卽 내가 아메리까 「픽스킬」避暑地에서 語學工夫를 하고 잇슬 때에 數十萬 在美愛蘭人을 거느리고 잇스면서 신펜黨紐育洲總支部長으로 잇는 M博士를 갓가히 알게 되어 氏로부터 「떼벨라」氏에게 親切하게 紹介하는 長文의 書札을 지녓섯고 또 紐育에 이르러 「빗세이」 卽 旅行券의 査證을 어드려고 愛蘭總領事舘에 가서 總領事를 맛낫슬 때에도 벌서 나의 말을 들엇슴이지 기다리고 잇섯든드시 愛蘭事情을 속속드리 잘 說明하여 주며 그 우에 愛蘭政界의 여러 名士에게 紹介하여 주는 글발을 하여 줌으로 그도 또한 지내게 되엇스니 이것은 愛蘭에 初旅行인 나에게는 實로 큰 所獲이 아니라 할 수 업섯나이다. 딴 말이나 紐育에는 英國總領事舘外에 堂堂한 愛蘭總領事舘이 잇서서 愛蘭人에 對한 것은 全部 그 곳에서 處理하고 잇는데 自由國이 된 뒤로부터는 英國外交官들도 愛蘭의 外交에 對하여는 손끄락 하나 저치지 못하고 잇더이다. 愛蘭은 實로 自由롭더이다. 支配를 버서나서 이제는 名實이 모다갓게 獨立이 되어 잇더이다!
「떠부린」市에 到着한 나는 즉시 택시를 불너 타고서 그날 오후 3시경에 떠부린 民立大學에 갓소이다. 『떼벨라」氏를 맛나자면 民立大學으로 가라는 말을 들엇기에 그래서 事務室로 드러가니 마츰 떼벨라氏는 2週日 前에 신펜黨의 일로 南部愛蘭으로 出他하엿다 함으로 엇절가고 망서리는 까리에 그곳 大學老敎授 B博士가 나오면서 무슨 일이냐고 뭇기에 나는 코리안사람으로 愛蘭訪問을 왓노라 하는 말과 美國 「픽스킬」에서 가지고 온 紹介狀을 내어 보이니 그 분은 크게 반기면서 應接室에 잇글고 드러가서 멀니서 엇지왓느냐고 10年舊知가치 情을 보여 주더이다. 그 뿐더러 나종에는 敎授時間이 아니면 自己가 親히 압헤서서 案內하여 드릴것을 그만 時間때문에 못하는 것이 遺憾千萬이라고 하면서 卽時 愛蘭政廳內務省에 電話를 거러주더이다. 그리고 나더니 來日 아츰 아홉시에 政廳門 압흐로 가면 內閣의 秘書가 나와 기대리기로 되엇슨 즉 그 時刻에 가보라고 하더이다. 이러케 眞情으로 周旋하여주는 老博士의 心情에 한긋 愉快를 늣기면서 그날은 「호텔」로 도로 나와 疲困한 다리를 쉬엇소이다. 愛蘭은 政治的 環境이(略-원문) 가튼 境遇의 外國人을<16> 待함에 有別함이 잇겟거니와 이와가치 하여줄 줄은 저는 몰낫소이다.
翌日 아츰에 그 말대로 政廳으로 간즉 門前에는 武裝한 把守兵이 잔뜩 직히고 서서 自由國 創始初의 騷亂한 雰圍氣가 內外에 가득하더이다. 把守兵 사이로 엇든 紳士 한 분이 직히고 섯다가 나를 보더니 「미스터, 허」냐고 무르면서 마저 드리어 政廳內로 잇글어 가기에 그 곳에 가서 約 5分間을 기대렷슬가 할 때에 內務次官格에 該當하다는 엇든 女官吏 한 분이 나와서 親切히 마저 주는데 그 분이 純猝한 愛蘭말을 하고 나는 겨우 英語를 번지는 關係로 우리의 對話는 몹시 遲滯難通이엇슴니다. 그는 沓沓하든지 中國말을 아느냐고 뭇기에 中國말도 아나 日本말도 안다 한 즉 즉시 「색크레타리」(秘書)를 불느더니 民立大學에 잇는 中國留學生을 불느더이다. (日本人이나 朝鮮人으로 愛蘭에 留學하고 잇는 學生은 한 사람도 업다 하더이다.) 조곰 잇다가 南方蘇州에 산다는 中國靑年 한 사람이 드러왓는데 그 사람 입을 거치는 中國通譯은 더구나 말이 잘 안되기에 나는 辭退하고 그제부터는 英和字典을 끄내들고 한참 둘이서 冊보며 이약이하엿지요. 彼此에 땀이 빠젓스나 談話의 內容은 꺼림김 업는 重要한 것이엇소이다.
그런 뒤에 그 內務次官이 그 때 마츰 開會中의 愛蘭議會와 高等法院과 控訴院의 書記長에게 電話를 거러주기에 나는 議會와 裁判所見學을 할 次로 그곳을 나왓나이다.
