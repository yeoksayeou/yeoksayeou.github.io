笑話
Jokes

名談?
Famous Talk?

"This fly must be female."
"How do you know?"
"Because I see it sitting on the mirror."

李太白
Li T'aebaek

Pak: "What does the term *T'aebuin* (太夫人) mean?"
Ch'oe: "It's customary to attach the character *T'ae* (太) to indicate an elder. If you just say *Puin* (夫人), it means 'wife', but if you say *T'aebuin*, doesn't it mean 'mother'?"
Pak: "That's right! So that's why in the Book of Odes (詩經), there's the poet Li Paek (李白) and the poet Li T'aebaek (李太白). Then Li T'aebaek must have been Li Paek's father!"

엉털이
Nonsense/Muddlehead

Unable to bear watching her husband worrying anxiously as the exam date drew near,
Wife: "Honey, is writing the exam answers really that hard? It must be like me giving birth."
Husband: "That's nothing! Giving birth is still the easy part."
Wife: "Why is that?"
Husband: "A baby, you just push out what's inside your belly, but for an exam, you have to push out what's *not* in your belly, so it's tough!"<11>

**Summary**

This article presents three short jokes or humorous anecdotes typical of the era. The first relies on a simple stereotype about women and mirrors. The second plays on a misunderstanding of Chinese characters and honorifics, confusing the famous poet Li T'aebaek (Li Bai) with a hypothetical father figure based on the character "太" (T'ae - great/elder). The third joke compares the difficulty of taking an exam (especially when unprepared) to the difficulty of childbirth, concluding the exam is harder because one must produce answers from nothing. For historians, these jokes offer a glimpse into popular humor, gender stereotypes, common knowledge (or lack thereof) regarding classical Chinese figures and language, and everyday anxieties like examinations during the colonial period.
