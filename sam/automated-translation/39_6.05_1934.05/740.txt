Traces of a Great Writer
Tolstoy
Excerpts from Tolstoy

Excerpts from the Diary During the Writing of *Resurrection*

One Thousand Eight Hundred Ninety-Five

November 5th, Yasnaya Polyana. I have just returned from a walk. And I clearly understood why I am not making good progress on *Resurrection*. It is because I started it incorrectly. I realized this while rethinking the story "Who is Right?" (the children's matter). I must begin writing from the peasant's life—that should be the *main focus*, the front view, and all other things should be the clouds, the background. And so I realized the same thing about *Resurrection*. It is necessary to start writing from the woman's story. I must start over right away.
(Note)-*Resurrection* was started from Katusha Maslova, according to his initial plan. Not from Prince Nekhlyudov.

November 7th, Yasnaya Polyana. In these two days, I have newly written a bit of *Resurrection*. My conscience hurts when I think about how carelessly I began writing. What I have written so far seems to make the work somewhat complete. Many visitors came. Varuz's(?) assistant came. And also a Frenchman who brought poetry.

November 9th, Yasnaya Polyana.
I was only able to write a little more of *Resurrection*. I took a walk today. I went to where Berom(?) is. He is a very pitiful person. And I wandered around the village.

November 15th, Yasnaya Polyana.
My body has weakened, so I could only write a few letters. I went to meet Alexandronia(?). She was lying sick. Today<163> Aunt Tanya(?) and Sonya(?) came. I couldn't sleep at night. Read Schopenhauer's *Aphorisms*. Very good. Only, I wish "Service to God" were substituted for "Recognition of the Void of Life." Then I would agree.

December 7th, Moscow.
I went to the theater to see a rehearsal of *The Power of Darkness*. Art begins with play and still contains toys for adults. This is also proven by the music I know well.

One Thousand Eight Hundred Ninety-Six

January 23rd, Moscow.
It has been exactly one month since I last held a brush. During this time, I wrote a letter about patriotism and a letter from "Grotspī(?)." And here, for two weeks, I continuously wrote a play. It didn't go well, but I wrote three acts. I don't have much hope for success.
True art! Infectious work—is produced only when the artist seeks and strives. In poetry, this passion to reproduce things exactly as they are arises from the fact that the artist, after clearly seeing and possessing things as they are, hopes to understand their meaning as they are.

January 25th, Moscow.
I started writing the play. It's not going well at all. But it's getting closer.

February 27th, Niriskue(?).
I am writing the play. The brush doesn't move well at all. I really don't know if it's progressing or what.<164>

Letter to Abe Isoo

October 22, 1934 [Translator's Note: The text says 1934, but the context refers to the Russo-Japanese War (1904-05). The date 1934 likely refers to the publication date of the magazine or translation, not the letter's date. The letter itself is known to be from October 22, 1904], Yasnaya Polyana, during the Russo-Japanese War.
Dear Friend Abe Isoo,
Receiving your letter and your newspaper containing the English text (Reporter's Note - This newspaper refers to the *Heimin Shimbun*, jointly published by Abe, Kinoshita Naoe, and others) was very pleasant for me, and I thank you sincerely for it.
I am deeply grateful that there are many rational, moral, and religious individuals in Japan during this current chaotic situation between our two nations. I am deeply overjoyed to think that I have such sincere friends and collaborators in Japan.
However, just as I am with all respected friends, I cannot help but be extremely truthful with you as well.
Therefore, I must tell you that I do not agree with Socialism. I lament that the most advanced part of the intelligent and strongest people of Japan has adopted this fragile, utopian, and error-filled Socialism from Europe.
In Europe, Socialism is already being abandoned.
Socialism aims for the satisfaction of the basest part of human nature (that is, material happiness). And that happiness can never be reached by the means it guides.
True human happiness is spiritual, that is, moral, and includes material happiness within it. And this noble goal can only be reached through the religious, that is, moral perfection of all units organizing nations and humans.
By religion, I mean a rational faith in the law of God common to all humanity. To demonstrate this in practice is to love all people and to do unto all people what I wish them to do unto me.
This law may seem much less effective compared to Socialism and other fragile doctrines, but I hold this as the only truth. And all movements that attempt to realize error-filled doctrines (which, moreover, can absolutely not reach their goal) obstruct the use of this sole truth and will prevent the attainment of the rightful happiness of humanity and each individual at the present time.
Please forgive me for freely criticizing your beliefs and for my poor English. And please believe that I am your sincere friend. Leo Tolstoy.<165>

Tolstoy's Yasnaya Polyana School (November and December 1862)

I. School Outline

There are no beginner students. Even the children in the youngest class can read and write, solve basic proportion problems, and recite sacred history. Therefore, the order of our lessons was determined by the schedule below.

Mechanical and Progressive Reading
Composition
Calligraphy Practice
Grammar
Sacred History
Russian History
Drawing
Sketching from Life
Singing
Mathematics
Conversations on Natural Science
Religious Instruction
Before discussing the teaching methods, I must briefly describe the Yasnaya Polyana school and its current state.
Like any living body and entity of value, this school not only changes year by year, day by day, but has also experienced temporary crises, misfortunes, illnesses, accidental disasters, etc.
The Yasnaya Polyana school passed through one such painful crisis this past summer. There are several reasons for this.
First, as happens every summer, even the best students did not come to school.
One only occasionally encountered them working in the fields or gathering fodder for horses and cows.
Second, it was because new teachers came to visit the school using their summer vacation.
Our school has four teachers. Two are veterans who have already taught at this school for two years. They are trained<166> in dealing with the students, in their work, and in the spirit of freedom of this school.
If a teacher hasn't arrived yet, the children gather at the entrance, kicking the corridor floor with their boot heels, playing sledding on the frozen path, or some wait in the classroom.
During cold times, while waiting for the teacher, they read books, copy texts, run around, and play.
The girls do not mingle and play with the boys. If the boys plan to suggest something to the girls, they don't pick specific girls but call out to all of them collectively.
"Hey girls, why don't you come in?" or
"Look, the girls are frozen!"
or
"Come on, try to catch me together!"
they say to start a conversation.
Among the girls, there is only one girl, the daughter of a household peasant, who is very talented in many areas and often tries to leave the company of the girls.
The young boys and girls play well like their male peers. However, there is still a lingering trace of slight shyness and self-restraint.

II. Teaching Issues

Imagine, according to the schedule, we start with mechanical reading in the first, youngest class, progressive reading in the second class, and mathematics in the third class.
When the teacher enters the classroom, the students are rolling around on the floor*, wrestling each other, shouting loudly*, making noise. "Strangle him, pull his hair!"
When the teacher enters at such a time, the students lying scattered shout to each other. To get up—
"Stop it. Stop it."
The teacher brings books, places them in front of the desks, and distributes them.
"A book for me too."
"Please give me the book you gave me yesterday."
Making noise like this, they each take a book and return to their seats.
"Why are you making so much noise? I can't hear anything. Shh—"
Among them, a clever child comes to his senses and strives to establish order.
Thus, gradually, the spirit of running and mischief dissipates, and learning dominates the room.<167>
Two of the teachers are new, and both are recent school graduates, lovers of superficial etiquette, rules, bell ringing, orderliness, schedules, and all similar things, not yet as accustomed to the school's life as the first two teachers.
The school is a two-story stone building. Two rooms are used for the school, one room is the library, and the teachers occupy two rooms.
At the entrance stairs, snow is always piled up messily. The class schedule also hangs there.
The order of lessons is as follows—
At 8 o'clock, the teacher on night duty has the boy who stays with him every night ring the bell.
The villagers wake up seeing the lamplight. The school windows have already been brightly lit for a long time. About half an hour after the bell rings, students gather in twos and threes, heading up the winding slope, whether it's snowing or raining, whatever the day.
They bring nothing with them. Not a single book, not a single pencil. They have the freedom of not being nagged at home about schoolwork. However, once they enter the classroom, they apply themselves diligently to their studies.
Almost grinding their teeth, their eyes shining brightly, they study diligently as if they have no thought other than the books. At this time, it is difficult to take the books away from them.

III. The Free Development of the School

Here, I must explain a little. In describing the affairs of the Yasnaya Polyana school here, I have no thought whatsoever of providing a model of what is indispensable for a school.
I am merely describing the actual situation of this school.
This school developed freely based on principles established by the teachers and students. While the teacher's authority carried sufficient weight, the students always had the option not to attend school or obey the teacher. The teacher had the privilege of refusing students and had the power to exert his influence on the great majority of the students.
As the students progressed, the scope of education gradually widened, and the demand for order became increasingly difficult to avoid.
At my Yasnaya Polyana school, this rule has existed since its founding. Initially, it was impossible to distinguish between recitation, problems, recreation, and lessons. Everything was mixed up. And so, all classification was useless.
The students—though they are groups of boys—are human beings with the same needs as ourselves, and their thoughts flow in the same stream. They are eager to learn everything. That is the only reason they come to school. (Remainder omitted - original text)<168>

**Summary**

This article, published in the Korean magazine *Samch'ŏlli* in 1934, presents excerpts from Leo Tolstoy's writings. It includes diary entries from 1895-1896 detailing his process and struggles writing *Resurrection*, emphasizing starting with the peasant/female perspective and commenting on art as play. It features a 1904 letter from Tolstoy to Japanese socialist Abe Isoo, written during the Russo-Japanese War, where Tolstoy expresses gratitude for Japanese sympathizers but firmly rejects socialism, advocating instead for individual moral and religious perfection based on universal love as the path to true happiness. Finally, it describes Tolstoy's experimental school at Yasnaya Polyana (c. 1862), highlighting its principles of freedom, lack of compulsion, student-driven learning, and the organic emergence of order, contrasting it with conventional schooling. For colonial Korean history, the article reveals the significant interest in and dissemination of Western literary and philosophical figures like Tolstoy. Tolstoy's critique of European socialism and his emphasis on spiritual/moral solutions (contrasted with materialism) might have resonated with certain Korean intellectuals navigating ideologies under Japanese rule. Furthermore, the description of Tolstoy's free school could have informed contemporary debates about education methods and liberation within the colonial context.

安部磯雄에게 與함

1934년 10월 22일 日露開戰 당시 야스나야뽀리나야에서
친애하는 友人 安部磯雄이여
足下의 편지 及 영문을 登載한 足下의 신문 (기자註-此 新聞이라 함은 安部, 木下尙江 등이 공동발행하든 平民新聞이다)을 밧은 일은 予에 대하야 대단히 유쾌하엿고 나는 진심으로 이를 감사하오.
일본에서 허다한 이성적 도덕적 及 종교적 人士가 잇어 지금이 이 혼비한 양국민간의 ― 나는 깁히 감사하오, 予는 日本에 엇서 이러케 진실한 友人을 가지며 共働者을 가진 것을 생각하고 깁히 欣喜不已하오.
그러치만 予는 모든 존경하는 友人에 대하여 그러함과 여히 足下에 대하여도 또한 지극히 참답지 안을 수 업소.
그래서 予는 社會主義에 찬성치 안는 事를 足下에게 告하지 안을 수 업소. 予은 日本의 총명하고 그리고 가장 굿센 인민의 가장 진보된 부분이 이 脆弱한 空想的의 그리고 오류 만흔 社會主義을 歐洲에서 취하여 간 것을 슬퍼하오.
歐洲에서는 社會主義는 이제는 벌서 버림을 밧고 잇는 중이오.
社會主義는 인간 性情의 가장 천한 부분의 만족(즉 그 물질적의 행복)으로써 目的삼소. 그리하여 그 행복은 결코 그 지도하는 수단에 의하야 도달할 것이 아니오.
인간의 참다운 행복은 정신적 즉 도덕적이면서 그 중에 물질적 행복을 포함하오. 그리하야 이 고상한 목적은 국민 及 인간을 조직하는 일체의 單位의 종교적 즉 도덕적 완성에 의하여야만 도달하는 것이오.
종교라고 하면 予는 인간일체에 통하는 神의 법칙에 대한 합리적 신앙을 의미하오. 이를 실제에 나타내어 보임은 즉 모든 사람을 사랑하고 모든 사람에 대하여 나의 하고저 하는 바를 하게 함에 잇소.
이 법은 社會主義 及 기타의 脆弱한 諸主義에 비하야 심히 유효치 안은 듯이 보이지만도 나은 이것으로써 유일의 진리로 삼소. 그리하야 오류 만흔 (더구나 도저히 그 목적을 도달할 수 업는) 諸主義을 現出코저 하는 일체의 운동은 이 유일의 진리의 사용을 방해하며 現時에 정당한 인류 及 각 개인의 행복에 도달케 못할 것이오.
足下의 신앙을 자유로 評論한 事와 余의 英文이 拙한 일에 대하여 余를 宥恕하시오. 그리하여 余가 足下의 진실한 友人인 事를 信하시오. 레오 톨스토이.<165>

소녀와 색시들은 사내동무들과 가치 잘 논다. 그러치만 역시 조곰은 수저워함과 자제하는 비치 떠돈다.

二. 敎授問題

學課表에 의하여서 우리들은 제1의 가장 年少한 級에서 機械的의 독서를 第2級에서는 漸進的 독서를, 第3級에서는 수학을 시작한다고 상상하라.

「그만둬라. 그만둬라.」

「내게는 어제 주든 책을 주세요.」

「엇제서 이러케 떠드느냐 아모 것도 들니지 안는구나. 쉬-
그 중에 춍명한 아해가 정신을 차리고 질서을 정돈코저 힘쓴다.

선생 중 두 사람은 새롭고 두 사람이 다 최근 學校出로 표면의 예의, 규칙, 振鈴, 정돈, 시간표 등 기타 이와 유사한 모든 것이 愛好者로 처음의 두 사람와 갓치 학교의 생활에 잘 익숙지 못하엿다.
학교는 石造 2층집으로 되엇다. 두 간이 학교로 씨우고 도서실이 한 간 교사들이 두 간을 차지하고 잇다.
계단 입구에는 늘 눈이 어지럽게 싸여잇다. 거기 학과표도 걸니어 잇다.
課業이 순서는 아래와 갓다-
8시에 숙직하는 교사가 밤마다 그와 起居를 갓치하는 소년을 식혀 종을 울니게 한다.
이 마을사람들은 람푸등불을 보고 이러난다. 학교창에는 벌서 람푸불이 오래 전부터 밝혀 잇다. 종을 친 뒤 半時間이 되나마나 하여 구불구불 비탈길로 눈이 오든지 비가 오든지 엇든 날이든지 둘식 셋식 학생들이 모여 든다.
그네들은 아모 것도 가진 것이 업다. 책 한 권도 한 자루의 연필도 그네들은 집에서 학과공부한다고 졸니우지 안는 자유를 가지고 잇다. 그러나 교실에 드러 와서는 공부에 열심을 내인다.
거지반 이를 갈고 눈빗을 밝히면서 책 이외에는 마모란 생각이 업는드시 열심히 공부한다. 이때에 그네에게서 책을 떼자면 힘이 든다.

三. 學校의 自由로운 發達

여기에서 나는 조곰 辨解하여 두지 안으면 안되겟다. 야스나아, 뽀리나야의 학교의 일을 여기에서 서술함에 당하여서 나는 조곰치도 학교에 필요불가결한 것의 範例를 제공하려하는 생각은 업다.
다만 이 학교의 실제의 형편을 記述하는 것 뿐이다.
이 학교는 교사와 生徒와에 의하여 確在된 원칙에 基하야 자유로히 발달하엿다. 교사의 위신에 충분한 무게가 잇섯스나 生徒에는 엇제든지 학교에 출석치 안코 교사에 복종치 안을 수가 잇섯다. 교사에게는 生徒을 거부할 특권이 잇섯고 그리고 자기의 감화를 生徒의 多大數에게 보낼 힘이 잇섯다.
학생들이 향상하여감에 따라서 교육의 범위는 점점 더 널버 갓고 질서에 대한 요구가 점점 避키 어럽게 되어 갓다.
나의 야스나야, 뽀리나야의 학교에서는 그 창립 이래 이 定則이 잇섯다. 최초는 암송과 문제와 오락과 課業과을 분류할 수가 업섯다. 일체의 것이 혼란되어 잇섯다. 그래서 분류의 모든 것이 무익하엿다.
生徒들은-그들은 소년의 무리들이지만은-우리들 자신과 가튼 요구를 가지고 잇는 인간들이라 그네의 사상도 가튼 개천을 흐르고 잇섯다. 그네들은 모든 것을 배우려고 욕심내고 잇다. 그것이 학교에 오는 오직 한 가지의 이유이다. (下畧-원본)<168>
