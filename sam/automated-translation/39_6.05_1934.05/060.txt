Publication Date: 1934년 05월 01일
Article Title: 醉談 (Drunken Talk)
Article Type: 잡저 (Miscellaneous Writing)

A. We're bored, shall we have a round of drunken talk?

B. What kind of drunken talk?

A. Just like saying Pak Yŏnghyo, Marquis, should be Vice Governor-General, or Mr. Han Sangnyong should be Director of the Finance Bureau, if we were to send people from Chosŏn overseas as ambassadors, who would be the right people?

B. Let me go first. Minister to France, Mr. Yi Chŏngbyŏn.

A. Hmm, that sounds plausible. Then, for Minister to the US, would it be Mr. Sin Hŭng'u?

B. It would be Sin Hŭng'u or Mr. Sŏ Chaep'il, right? Then for Minister to Russia, would it be Yi Tonghwi or Pak O-yŏng?

A. If we were to dispatch a female minister, wouldn't Nam Manch'un's sister do?

B. A female minister would be a masterpiece! Then, would Mr. Yŏ Unhyŏng be the Minister to China?

A. Well, there are many possibilities for the Minister to China: Kim Kyusik, Sin Sŏk-u, Mr. Yŏ Unhyŏng, and so on.

B. Minister to Britain?

A. This drunken talk might turn into serious talk. Let's stop, let's stop.<36>

**Summary**

This short dialogue, titled "Drunken Talk," features two unnamed speakers hypothetically appointing prominent Koreans to high official posts and ambassadorships to major countries (France, US, Russia, China, UK) during the Japanese colonial period. They mention figures ranging from known collaborators (Pak Yŏnghyo, Han Sangnyong) to independence activists (Sin Hŭng'u, Sŏ Chaep'il, Yi Tonghwi, Yŏ Unhyŏng, Kim Kyusik, Sin Sŏk-u), and even speculate about a female ambassador. This piece is interesting for historians as it reflects a form of intellectual escapism or suppressed national aspiration under colonial rule, imagining Koreans in positions of power and international standing. The mix of personalities considered, from collaborators to nationalists, might also suggest the complex political landscape and spectrum of figures recognized within Korean society at the time.
