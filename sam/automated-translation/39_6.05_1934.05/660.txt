文學妓生의 告白
						張蓮花
(Confession of a Literary Kisaeng)
						Chang Yŏnhwa

My friends point at me and call me a kisaeng who likes literature.
Truthfully, ever since I attended the kisaeng school in P'yŏngyang, I greatly liked poetry and novels. Because of this, literary books never left my hands. Afterwards, like a willow leaf blown by the wind, even after coming all the way to Hanyang, Sŏul, I did not forget to seek out and read novels appearing in newspapers or poems in magazines.
However, to tell the truth, even though I say it's literature, my level of understanding literature is childish. There are many poems and many passages in novels that seem to hide deep meanings, but no matter how much I think about them, I don't understand them well and just pass over them.
Still, my passion for liking literature never cools down.

I don't know if writing this will bring great dishonor to my alma mater, but when I was sixteen and attending P'yŏngyang Girls' Higher Common School (평양서 녀자고등보통학교), a young teacher in charge of our class greatly enjoyed literature. When he first told us the story of Tolsŭt'oi's "Kats'yussa" (Katusha) and recounted T'ŭrgenep's "On the Eve" and "First Love," an untimely flame arose in my heart, and I experienced a refreshing feeling like seeing the half-moon rise over the mountain pass on a dark night.
That was the first time I started enjoying literature. So, from then on, I began roaming the bookstores in P'yŏngyang city, buying and reading novels.
Among the various things I read, the works of "T'ŭrukkepŭ" (Turgenev) were gentle and passionate, pleasing me greatly, so I sought out and read his works whenever possible. Although I didn't understand works like "Fathers and Sons" or "Smoke" well even after reading them, I still didn't leave out anything, reading everything including his prose poems and "Diary of a Superfluous Man."
After reading his works, I felt as if I had discovered a new world and was very happy.
To confess frankly, Mr. T'ŭrgenep is the elder who opened the multiply-shut doors of my heart. I was happy.<140>

If I had been born into an affluent family like others, perhaps by now I would have graduated from P'yŏngyang Girls' Higher Common School, gone to Tokyo as my friends dreamed back then, finished English literature at the Women's University in Mejirŏ (Mejiro), and maybe become a professor at Yihwa Chŏnmun Hakkyo (Ewha Womans College). Even if that weren't the case, perhaps by now I too would have become a female literary figure, publishing poems and novels in newspapers and magazines, receiving praise and affection from the people of the world.
However, in my third year, I was dragged by my adoptive mother to the kisaeng school near Yŏngwangjŏng Pavilion on the Taedonggang River, becoming a body that learned how to play the changgo drum and how to dance.
A pitiful girl who lost her parents early was forced by her adoptive parents' command onto the path of becoming a kisaeng in the future.
Kisaeng school! The scent of literature I caught there was sijo.

The moon hangs in the blue sky
It seems likely to fall through the winds and frosts of ages, but
For the drunken guests of the capital, it shines long.

I learned verses like this repeatedly. Sometimes I didn't know the meaning and asked the teacher, but even the teacher didn't know; it was enough just to move one's mouth this way and that, matching the intonation and the changgo rhythm.
During the year and a half I was at the kisaeng school, I gained one thing. It was the urge to read poems and novels written by Koreans. It was an impulse to seek out literary works that were not sluggish like sijo, not short like a deer's tail like sijo, and not old like sijo.
Thus, when I came out of school, I would steal away from my adoptive mother's eyes and was able to encounter the works of various writers of the modern literary scene.
What I read at that time were poems like Chu Yohan's "Beautiful Dawn," Ansŏ's "Spring Song," P'ain's "Night on the Border," P'osŏk's "On the Grass," and poems by Sowŏl, Kim Yŏsu, Chi Yong O, Ch'ŏnsŏk, Sim Hun, Pyŏn Suju, Han Yongun, and others. As for novels, centering on those by Hŏn Chin'gŏn, Kim Tongin, Yi Kwangsu, Yŏm Sangsŏp, Yi Kiyŏng, and others, there wasn't a work by various other writers that I hadn't seen at least once or twice.
Even now, guests who come to play at my house are surprised to see the literary books piled up on my desk. Indeed, the works I have read must number in the dozens.
In doing so, I came to think that literary works must indeed be records of one's own society in one's own words. While acknowledging the greatness of the works of T'ŭrgenep or Tolsŭt'oi, which I read through Japanese translations a few years ago, somehow they didn't feel real, like scratching an itch while wearing shoes.
Literature must indeed be one's own literature.

Last night, I went to a banquet at Myŏngwŏlgwan and met a novelist. This person had once come to P'yŏngyang, saw the weeping willows hanging down<141> below Pubyŏngnu Pavilion, and published many fragrant writings.
It was the first time I had actually met the face of someone I had known for a long time.
I thought he was quite old, but he was still young. As he repeatedly drank his cup, upon hearing that my hometown was P'yŏngyang, he reiterated the beauty of Nŭngnado and the Taedonggang River.
However, I showed no sign whatsoever of liking literature. In truth, often at various banquet seats, when I hear that someone is the famous So-and-so of the literary world, I blush and try to show a very pleased expression, but I end up suppressing that emotion within myself. What business does a lowly kisaeng have discussing literature? I fear they might think that.

Sometime ago, I read Kim Myŏngsun's poems in a magazine. I heard that Kim Myŏngsun also finished P'yŏngyang Girls' Higher Common School; if so, she is a senior from my alma mater. Kim Myŏngsun's poetry seems to have considerable depth. Her writing style gives a cold sensation somewhere, like seeing cold charcoal embers after everything has burned down, but it was still hot.
I also saw her novel called "Turkey" (칠면조). I thought she had a very gentle writing style.
In her collection of works, "Fruit of Life" (생명의 과실), there were many beautiful works that struck my heart. Especially the piece called "Mysterious Girl" (의문의 소녀), which was published in Ch'ŏngch'un (Youth) magazine and received high praise, was a fine short piece that captured the heart, seemingly graspable yet elusive, like a fragrant cluster of flowers blooming in the mist. Perhaps because I am a woman, I liked such writing.

When I first picked up this brush, it seemed there would be many things I wanted to write, but now that I am actually facing the manuscript paper, there is nothing much to write. If I have the opportunity in the future, I think I would like to publish works under a hidden name.
Finally, I will write down a couple of songs that I have jotted down as I felt them from time to time after returning from banquets.

My throat hoarse from the songs of the playground,
Returning home, angry, I recklessly pluck
The kayagŭm; plucking until the strings break,
Shall I stay up all night plucking?

This black hair falling to my feet, this black hair, this fine hair, if it hadn't grown on the head of a kisaeng (妓女) like me...<142>

**Summary**

Chang Yŏnhwa, a kisaeng from P'yŏngyang, confesses her deep love for literature, which began during her time at P'yŏngyang Girls' Higher Common School before she was forced into kisaeng training. Despite her limited formal education and the social constraints of her profession, she avidly reads both translated Russian literature (Turgenev, Tolstoy) and contemporary Korean writers (Chu Yohan, Kim Tongin, Yi Kwangsu, Kim Myŏngsun, etc.). She feels a stronger connection to Korean literature dealing with Korean society. Although she encounters famous writers at banquets, she hides her literary interests due to her perceived low status. She expresses a desire to publish her own writing anonymously someday and concludes with two short, poignant poems reflecting her life. For historians, this article offers a rare personal perspective from a kisaeng during the colonial period, highlighting the intellectual aspirations of women despite societal barriers, the limitations imposed by class and gender, the circulation and reception of literature (both foreign and domestic) outside elite circles, and the personal conflict between artistic sensibility and social reality.

文學妓生의 告白
						張蓮花
내 동무들은 나를 가라처 문학을 조화하는 기생이라 한다.
사실 나는 평양서 긔생학교를 다닐 때부터 시와 소설을 퍽으나 조와하엿다. 그래서 내 손에서 문예책이 떠나본 때가 업섯다. 그 뒤 바람에 날니는 버들입과 가치 한양이라 서울까지 올너와서도 신문에 나는 소설이나 잡지에 나는 시를 차저 보기를 잇지 안엇다.
그러나 실상 말이지 문학이라 하여도 내가 문학을 리해하는 정도는 유치하다. 깁흔 뜻이 숨어 잇는 듯 하면서도 아모리 쟁각하여 보아도 잘 몰나서 그저 넘겨버리는 시도 만코 소설가운데의 문구도 만타.
그래도 문학을 조와하는 열은 결코 식어지지 안는다.
이런 말을 쓰면 모교에 큰 붙명여를 끼칠는지 모르지만 내가 열여섯살 때 평양서 녀자고등보통학교를 다닐 때에 우리 반을 담당한 젊은 선생 한 분이 문학을 퍽으나 즐기어 하여 그분이 처음으로 「톨스토이」의 「갓쮸사」 이약이를 들녀주고 「트르게넵」의 「그 전날 밤」과 「첫사랑」을 이약이하여 줄 때에 내 가슴속에는 때아닌 불길이 일어 마치 어두운 밤에 고개 너머 반달이 오르는 것을 보는 것 가튼 상쾌한 감정을 맛보앗다.
그때가 문학 즐기기 시작한 처옴이엇다. 그래서 그제부터는 평양시내의 책점을 도라 다니며 소설책을 사드리어 읽기 시작하엿다.
이것저것 읽는 가운데 「트루께프」의 작품이 부드럽고 열정적이 되어 몹시 마음에 드러 되는대로 그분의 작품을 작고 읽엇다. 「아버지와 아들」이라거나 「煙氣」가튼 작품은 읽어도 잘 몰낫섯지만 그리면서도 그의 산문시라거나 「엽인일긔」까지 빼어 노치 안코 깡그리 읽엇다.
그분의 작품을 읽고 나자 나는 새 세상 하나를 더 발견한 듯하여 퍽으나 유쾌하엿다.
솔직하게 고백하자면 「트르게넵」 그 양반이 내 가슴속의 첩첩이 다친 문을 여러 준 어른이시다. 나는 행복스러웟다.<140>
