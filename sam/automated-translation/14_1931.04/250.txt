Unforgettable Foreign Women of Marriageable Age

Moscow's Russian Beauty
By Yi Chŏngsŏp

Passing through Germany and Poland, I entered Russia; the time was November 1928. Before I knew it, white snow filled heaven and earth, and the fact that Red Russia had turned white was nature's fitting irony.
I saw the sights of the capital, Moscow, and set out on the road back home via Manchuria (滿洲里). The thought of spending a whole week continuously living on the train to Manchuria was daunting, but more than that, finding the station for the train to Manchuria was the most difficult task for me, as I was not only mute in Russian but also blind to the script.

I stepped out onto the main street; the shadows of people and houses lay stretched several times their actual length on the street, and the setting evening sun urged the departure time. I asked this person and that passerby, sometimes in English, sometimes in French, for the station to the Far East, but everyone just shook their heads left and right.

Blindly, I jumped onto just any bus, mobilized all my poor linguistic knowledge, and finally, even resorting to clumsy German, asked the conductor for the station to catch the train to Manchuria, but he thoroughly failed to understand.
At this moment, a woman sitting behind me stood up.
"Excuse me, if you want to catch the train to Manchuria, you need to go back to the place you were just now and take bus number X; it will take you right in front of that station."
My ears quickly picked up the fluent French, and I turned to look; she was a young woman of 25 or 26. On her slightly longish, jade-like face, centered around a delicately formed nose bridge (鼻脉), two star-like eyes sparkled above, and below, a mouth like a peony bud (牧丹꼿보무라지) murmured softly.

Me: "Thank you so much. You are very kind."
Woman: "You understand well, right? It's very easy. Oh, wait, that place might be a bit complicated. Come with me. I'll guide you to the station." <68>
Me: "I am extremely grateful, but your path is clearly different from mine, how could I possibly let you? Please go on your busy way. If you just tell me, surely I won't fail to find the way."
The implication of my words like this was different from saying, "Well then, since it's troublesome, please come with me." The woman's kindness began to grow progressively richer.
Though the bus fare to the station was only a few sen (分), the thought that she would bear the fare for a complete stranger, someone with nearly zero probability of meeting again, was not such a common occurrence in our little human society. Especially considering this was a woman, my heart couldn't help but sweat, pressed down by this woman's heavy kindness.

Soon, we arrived at the station. Watching this woman's figure from behind as she got off ahead of me, even though her whole body was wrapped in an overcoat, the harmony of her overall body's beautiful curves (曲線美) and excellent physique (軀格) was vividly apparent. In that flesh-biting cold, her effort to reveal even a part of her physical beauty (肉體美) by covering her beautiful legs with only thin silk stockings seemed like an attempt to defend the very essence (本壘) of femininity.
She took me into the stationmaster's office, entrusted me like a piece of luggage since I didn't know the language, and came out with me.
Woman: "You stayed in Paris for a long time, didn't you? I was also in Paris for a while. I can guess that after seeing Paris, Moscow might not seem very impressive, but still, look at the dome of that church over there first. Its curved beauty is entirely different from the domes of the Panthéon (팡테온) or Les Invalides (앙바리드) in Paris, isn't it? I plan to go to Paris soon too. Wherever one goes, Paris is the best!"
Me: "Besides, the living expenses are very cheap, so pleasure seems to be developing geometrically. If by some strange chance we could meet once again in Paris..."
Woman: "Exactly!"
Me: "I feel very sad [that we are parting]."
Woman: "The feeling is mutual." "Bon voyage (봉보이야-지)!"

Having sent off this woman who blew a breath of spring breeze (春風) into the heart of a lonely traveler in a foreign land (異域孤客), I couldn't just turn away. I stood blankly in the pouring dusk, watching her retreating figure. The woman also turned her head, broke into a smile (破顔一笑하여), and waved goodbye.
Sitting down dejectedly in the train, I remembered the phrase "I saw my heart is melted" from "The Suicide Club" I saw as a child, and also recalled the passage from Anatole France's "Les Dieux out soif": "Pai senti mon eoeur se fondre comme la Cire də l'ete" (I felt my heart melt like summer wax).
It seems to be a principle of law that one can easily forget the person one loved, but the person for whom one holds a lingering attachment (未鍊) is unforgettable for a lifetime (平生不忘).

The Female Teacher at Aoyama Gakuin
By Mun Ilp'yŏng

Just when the Russo-Japanese War broke out, and the smell of ammunition suppressed even the breath of plants in the three East Asian countries, at that very time, having learned only the single phrase "Wakarimasen" (分りません - I don't understand) in Japanese, I went to Tokyo to study abroad. First, I entered Aoyama Gakuin (靑山學院). I was nineteen at the time.

I don't know how it is now, but back then, Aoyama Gakuin had a music department in addition to the religion department, and many children from noble families attended. Since I also believed in Christianity at the time, I got along well with their atmosphere day by day.
Now, there was a young female music professor (敎授) at that school named Miura (三浦). She must have been around twenty-one or twenty-two. Her face was bright like a Rugosa rose flower (海棠花꼿), gentle and graceful, and her appearance was passionate and beautiful. Her voice and conduct (行動擧止) appeared so lovely <69> in the eyes of a young boy like me. Perhaps because she saw me as a young student from a foreign land (異邦) who had intentionally come from a faraway country to study, that woman showed me kindness differently than she did others.
She would make sure I learned characters I didn't know, teaching me, and telling me things like it would be best to handle food, clothing, and shelter (衣食居處) in such and such ways during my youth.

On Sundays (主日), there was a worship service (禮拜). That woman went to the church (敎會堂) in the city streets, and so did I. Once, on the way back after the service ended, the female teacher asked me to have lunch with her, and we entered a nearby restaurant (레스트랜). And so, I ate what I now know was "lunch" (런치). Being my first time with Western cuisine (洋料理), there was no way I would know how to eat it. I tried to furtively copy (도적질하여) what the female teacher was doing, but while trying to cut the cutlet (갓즈렛즈), the knife slipped sideways, and I hurt the tip of my left ring finger (無名指). A drop of red blood fell from the cut.
"Ara, kegashitandesu no" (Oh, you got hurt?), she said, tore a piece from the cloth (제책보) that wrapped her hymn book (讚美歌冊), quickly bandaged my finger, and said,
"Mun-san, kō suru no yo" (Mr. Mun, you do it like this).
Smiling, she pulled my meat plate towards her, cut the meat neatly into several square pieces for me, and urged me to eat. It goes without saying (勿論이다) that my face must have turned red with embarrassment.

That woman sang the hymn that went "Jinsei wa ito tanoshi" (人生は いと樂し - Life is very joyful) very well. I don't know how many times I heard that song from her. After hearing it, I sang it too. We sang it together as well. While doing so, thinking of her as a trustworthy older sister, I also muttered to myself, "Later, when I grow up, if I get married, I'll definitely marry a woman like her."

After graduating and leaving, I have never heard any news (消息) of that woman. However, perhaps because she was the first woman I felt I had a significant encounter with in my youth, I cannot forget her to this day. And I realize that the song I unconsciously learned from her back then still flows past my lips even now.

Besides that, when I wandered around places in China like Hangchow (杭州) or Soochow (蘇州), visiting sites like the Chientang River (錢塘江) where Li Po (李太白) and Su Tung-p'o (蘇東坡) were said to have played, I saw many Beauties of Wu, Yue, and Chu (吳越楚姬) sitting and weaving silk. However, perhaps because I never had the chance (機會) to exchange even a single word with them, there isn't a woman who left such an impression. In contrast, I have relatively many impressions of Japanese women from my time studying in Japan. This one occurred when, after finishing my studies, I boarded a train at Shinbashi Station (新橋停車場) and was traveling on the Tōkaidō Line (東海道線). At Yokohama Station (橫濱驛), a Japanese female student wearing purple hakama (하까마) boarded, carrying a willow box (버들상자). The compartment was truly full (滿員). Many passengers who couldn't get a seat (坐席) were packed tightly like bundles (괴나리 보찜모양으로).

That female student came and stood right in front of me. I offered her my seat, urging her to sit. However, she just said,
"Ī desu no, ī desu no" (It's fine, it's fine), and continued standing without taking the seat. Several stations (停車場) whizzed by. But the number of boarding passengers was several times that of those getting off. The woman gradually seemed unable to endure her fatigue (疲勞) and fidgeted. Unable to bear it, I offered her the seat again, but she wouldn't listen. Having no choice (할 수 업시), I said, "Excuse me (失禮지만), but if you're uncomfortable, please sit on my lap." After hesitating several times, she carefully sat down on my lap (무릅). Being in my youth (靑春), my blood boiled (피가 끄를) at the sensation (感觸) of the opposite sex (異性).

That night, the woman ended up vomiting due to motion sickness (車멀미). I spread my blanket on the seat where I had been sitting and laid the female student down there.
Perhaps she was suffering too much, as the woman got off at Kyoto (京都). She had said her home was somewhere in the Hiroshima (廣島) area, and as she got off, she bowed her head many times, expressing her gratitude (感謝). That memory, too, has created one of the beautiful memories of my youth up to my forties (40靑春).

Marseille Port Departure and That Woman
By Chŏng Sŏkt'ae

A hazy white spring heat haze (아즈량이) hung over the port of Marseille (말세-유港口). Here, large ships, ready to scatter after carrying people of all races from all countries of the world, were tied up densely on the left and right of the pier (埠頭). This port, located at the crucial point (포인트) dividing Asia (亞細亞) and Europe (歐羅巴). <70>

Countless students from our country also pass through this port when they come, scattering to London, Paris, Rome, etc., and when they finish their studies in England, Germany, France, or Italy, they pack their bags, shoulder them, and return to their homeland (故國) through this port.
Marseille, Marseille – like Heidelberg (하이델, 벨히), it is a good name (名詞) that gives a fragrant impression (香그러운 印象) to travelers worldwide.

Spring 1925. White waves crashed against the breakwater (波止場), boats (뽀트) carrying boys busily went back and forth on the sea hoisting red flags (긔), and white seagulls (갈메기), like chickens drawn inside a folding screen (屛風), leisurely guarded a part of the sea surface (海面). The scenery of this port on a late spring morning.
The huge ship (巨船) M, about to depart for the East (東洋), removed the gangway (부릿지) laid across to the deck (덱기) in preparation for setting sail (出帆), and a crew member (船員) busily beat a gong (銅羅). Dong-dong-dong—

Having finished my three years in Germany, I too boarded this ship on this morning, heading for my beloved homeland, about to leave Marseille.
The pier head (埠頭머리) was packed with thousands of people who had come to see passengers off (餞送). They held one end of five-colored streamers (테브) – red, blue, yellow, white. Hundreds of strands of streamers fluttered in the spring breeze between the ship and the pier, bulging like a five-colored rainbow (五色무지게).
Among the British and French people on this ship, there might be some bearing the mission of pioneering the Eastern market (市場開拓), and some leaving to become diplomats (外交官). Though one can find a place to settle anywhere (靑山이야 人間到處에 잇겟지만), warm tears shed from the sad parting (哀別) with parents and siblings (父母兄弟) flowed between their eyebrows as they departed for the ends of the earth (天涯).

Now, amidst all these streamers, one strand was also held at my fingertips, I who had boarded the deck. Just one strand, but deeply meaningful—

The day before, having arrived at Marseille port, I had gone to a large cafe (캅페) there to relieve my traveler's sorrow (旅愁) out of fatigue. I sat at a table (卓子), nursing one or two glasses of alcohol I couldn't really drink, when a waitress (웨트레쓰) brought me a letter (便紙). The letter was written in English, saying something like: "I lived in Shanghai for several years, and I'm glad to see an Eastern gentleman (東洋어룬). Would you permit me the pleasure of your company?" I simply sent word back: "Yes (예-스)."
A little while later, smiling brightly, a young woman – perhaps 21 or 22, with a face as pretty as one from a painting, resembling an English female student – came towards my side, calling "Excuse me (엑스규스,미)". I offered her a chair, and we shared the table.

The woman said she had gone to Shanghai with her father and returned last autumn. Her father had been a professor (敎授) at Hujiang University (湖江大學) there. Perhaps because of the impressions received in her youth, she said she liked Eastern scenery and culture (東洋風物) and wanted to be friendly with Eastern people. She was also advocating Socialism (國家社會主義).
Although we had just met for the first time, we conversed very pleasantly, strolled through the city streets, and then parted. She was a very cheerful (快活한) woman.

That woman from yesterday evening was now standing over there on the pier to see me off. On the deck, there were even more men wiping tears with handkerchiefs, and on the pier too, there were even more women crying aloud.
The mountain-like huge ship M finally set sail (離陸하엿다). The streamer unwound, shortening foot by foot (한자 두자). Shorter, shorter, but there was a limit. The streamer broke and lay strewn messily across the sea surface. Behind, only seagulls frolicked about.
And thus, I parted ways with that woman, my last encounter in Europe. That was all. But somehow, to this day, that woman's refreshing (시언한) face always comes to mind before my eyes, and there is no way to forget (이즐 길이 업다). <71>

**Summary**

This article presents three separate recollections by Korean men (Yi Chŏngsŏp, Mun Ilp'yŏng, Chŏng Sŏkt'ae) about memorable encounters with foreign women during their time abroad in the late 1920s and early 1930s. Yi Chŏngsŏp recounts being kindly helped by a fluent French-speaking Russian woman in Moscow. Mun Ilp'yŏng recalls his affectionate interactions with a young Japanese music teacher at Aoyama Gakuin in Tokyo and a brief, awkward yet touching encounter with a female student on a train. Chŏng Sŏkt'ae describes meeting a cheerful English woman in Marseille the day before sailing home, who had lived in Shanghai and came to see him off. Each story emphasizes the kindness, beauty, or unique impression left by these women, making them "unforgettable."

From a historical perspective, these accounts offer glimpses into the experiences of Korean men traveling, studying, or working abroad during the Japanese colonial period. They reveal cross-cultural interactions, perceptions of foreign femininity through a Korean male lens, and the lingering impact of brief encounters experienced far from home. Historians might find interesting the specific details of travel (trains, ships, locations like Moscow, Tokyo, Marseille), the social settings (schools, cafes, stations), the mix of languages used (Korean, Japanese, French, English, German), and the emotional resonance these interactions held for Korean men navigating foreign environments and cultures.

이처지지 안는 外國의 妙齡女性
莫斯科의 露西亞美人
李晶燮
獨逸, 波蘭을 지내어 露西亞로 드러가니 때는 1928년 11월이라 어느듯 白雪이 滿乾坤하야 赤色露國이 白化한 것은 大自然의 그럴뜻한 「아이로니」엿다.
首都 莫斯科를 구경하고 滿洲里를 거처 歸國하는 길에 올넛다. 滿洲里까지 一週日동안 줄 곳 車中生活을 할 일 생각하니 이도 감아득 하지만 그보다도 露語에 입만 벙어리인 것이 안이라 눈조차 소경인 나로서 滿洲里가는 車탈 停車場 찻기가 難中難事이엇다.
제일 큰거리에 쑥나서니 人影家影은 實物의 數倍기리로 길에 가로노여 너머가는 저녁해는 發車時刻을 催促한다. 通行하는 이 사람 저 사람에게 或은 英語로 或은 佛語로 極東가는 停車場을 무르니 모도가 고개를 左右로 흔들 뿐이다.
두더펴노코 아무 「뻐스」나 하나 뒤집어 타고 나의 변변치못한 語學智識을 總動員하다 못해 마즈막에는 말똥 獨逸語까지 하면서 滿洲里가는 車탈 停車場을 車掌에게 물으니 대체 徹底하기도 알어 못듯는다.
이 때에 내 뒤에 안젓든 한 婦人이 이러나면서
「여보서요 滿洲里가는 車탈랴면요 당신이 앗가타든 그 場所에 다시 가서 아무號 뻐쓰를 타면 그냥 그대로 그 停車場 압헤가 내립니다」
流暢한 佛語에 얼는 귀가 띄어 도려다보니 25,6歲의 젊은 婦人이엿다. 白玉가튼 조금 기루스름한 얼골에 잔잔하게 달(走)린 鼻脉을 中心으로 우에는 새별가튼 두눈이 반적이고 알에는 牧丹꼿보무라지 가튼 입이 喃喃거린다.
나 「참으로 고맙슴니다. 매우 親切하심니다」
婦人 「잘아르시겟지오. 매우 쉬워요. 안이야 그 곳이 조금 複雜하 것다. 어듸 나하고 가티 가십시다. 停車場까지 내가 案內해 드리지오」＜68＞
나 「너무나 惶悚함니다. 당신이 가시는 길은 내길과 方向이 딴 것이 分明한데 그럴 수 잇겟슴니까. 어서 밧부신 길을 가서요. 그만만 일러주시면 설마 길못찻겟슴니까」
이러듯한 나의 말은 「그러면 수 고스러우니 가티 가주서요」하는 말과 말의 含蓄性이 달럿든 것이엇다. 婦人의 親切의 度는 漸漸 더 濃厚하기를 始作한다.
停車場까지 車賃이라야 몃 分일가만은 生面不知의 人이오 또한 再逢할 蓋然率조차 零에 갓가운 사람의 車賃을 自擔한다는 것은 조그마한 우리 人間社會에 그닥지 흔한일이 안이라함을 생각할 때 더구나 이것이 婦人이라함을 생각할 때 나의 마음은 이 婦人의 무거운 親切에 늘리어 땀흘리지 안흘 수 업섯다.
이윽고 驛에 到達하자 나압서 내리는 이 婦人의 姿態를 뒤로보니 비록 外套로 완몸을 에워싸슬망정 몸 全體의 曲線美와 훌륭한 軀格의 調和는 如實히 나타나보인다. 살을 어여내는 그 추위에 아름다운 두다리룰 엷으다한 씰크보선으로 가리워 肉體美의 다만 一部分이라도 보이려하는 그 努力은 女性의 本壘를 지키려는 듯이 보이엇다.
驛長室에 드러가 말모르는 나를 荷物처럼 맷기고 가티 나와서는
婦人 「당신 巴里에 오래 잇섯지오. 나도 巴里에 얼마간 잇섯서요. 巴里본눈에 莫斯科가 눈떠 보이지 안흘 것도 推側함니다만은 그래도 爲先 저 敎堂의 圓天蓋보시오. 巴里의 「팡테온」이나 「앙바리드」의 圓天蓋와는 그 曲線美가 왼통 달르지오. 나도 未久에 巴里로 가려함니다. 어데어데니 해야 巴里가 第一이얘요」
나 「게다가 生活費는 매우 싸서 歡樂은 幾何級數的 으로 發展되어가는듯합듸다. 或奇緣으로 다시 한 번 巴里에서 맛나 보일 수 잇다면..」
婦人 「그러기 말이애요!」
나 「퍽 섭섭하다는 생각이 간절합니다」
婦人 「彼此엇슴니다」 『봉보이야-지」
異域孤客의 마음에 一脉의 春風을 부러너어준 이 婦人을 보내고 그냥 도라설 수 업서 나는 펴붓는 黃昏가운데 물구럼히 서서 이 婦人이 가는 뒤를 바라보고 잇스니 婦人또한 고개를 돌리고 破顔一笑하여 잘가라고 손을 거덕인다.
기운업시 車안에 덜석 안지니 어려슬 때 본 「自殺俱樂部가운데」 “I saw my heart is melted’라는 句節도 記憶되고 아나톨.프량쓰의 “Les Dieux out soif”에 “Pai senti mon eoeur se fondre comme la Cire də l'ete”라는 句節도 聯想된다.
사량하든 그사람은 能히 이저도 未鍊둔 그사람은 平生不忘이라는 것이 法則의 原理인듯하다.

靑山學院의 女敎師
文一平
日露戰爭이 터지어 東洋三國에는 彈藥냄새가 草木의 숨까지 죽여 노힐 때 바로 그 때에 나는
「分りません」
이라는 다만 한마듸 일본말만 배워가지고 東京으로 留學을 갓섯다. 처음에 靑年學院에 들어갓다. 그 때 내나히 열아홉이엇다.
只今은 엇전지 모르겟지만 그 때 靑山學院에는 宗敎科以外에 音樂科도 잇서서 高貴한 집 子弟들이 만히 다니엇섯다. 나도 基督敎를 밋든 때니까 그네들 空氣와 잘 調和를 어더가면서 그날 그날을 지내엇다.
그런데 그 學校에 「三浦」라는 절문 女子音樂敎授가 잇섯다. 나은 수 물한둘이나 되엇슬가 얼골이 海棠花꼿가치 밝고 너울너울하고 情熱的 인 美貌인데다가 말소리며 行動擧止가 나어린 少年의 눈에 엇더케도＜69＞ 어엽부게 비최든지 그 女子 亦是 멀니 딴나라에서 일부러 工夫하려온 異邦의 靑年學生이라고 그리하엿슴인지 남과 다르게 나에게 親切한 뜻을 表하여 주엇다.
모르는 글을 일부러 기 터잇스라고 하여 가르처도 주며 靑年時代의 衣食居處는 이러저러하게 하는 것이 조켓다는 등.
主日이면 禮拜가 잇다. 市街에 잇는 그 敎會堂으로 그 女子도 가고 나도 간다. 한 번은 禮拜를 罷하고 도라오는 길에 그 女敎師는 날더러 점심먹으러 가지고 하여 그 附近 「레스트랜」으로 드러갓다. 그래서 지금 알고보니 「런치」란 점심을 먹엇다. 洋料理는 처음인 내가 먹는 法을 알 까닭이 업섯다. 그 女敎師가 하는 모양을 실금실금 도적질하여 임내 내려하다가 그만 「갓즈렛즈」를 버힌다는 것이 칼이 빗그로 다라나며 왼편손 無名指끗을 다치엇다. 빨간 피방울이 버인 자곡에서 떠러진다.
「あら, けがしたんですの」
하며 그 女子는 讚美歌冊을 쌋든 제책보끗을 찌저 얼는 손꼬락을 싸매어주고.
「文さん, こうするのよ」
