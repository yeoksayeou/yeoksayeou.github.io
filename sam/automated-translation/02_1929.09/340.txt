Article Title: 말몰이(외 2편) (The Horse Driver (and 2 others))
Author: 李殷相
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

The Horse Driver
						李殷相
The Horse Driver
So far, so treacherous
The mountain pass road to cross
The night is deep and pitch black
How can I go, oh how can I go

Carrying a mountain-like load on the horse's back
Our horse driver, holding a paper lantern
Singing loud enough to make the mountains echo
Crosses the pass in this dark night

Thoughts Towards My Beloved
Who says that the sea has a bottom?
Though the sea may have a place where it ends
Could thoughts towards my beloved have a bottom?
The more I long, the deeper they go

Like the sky that seems to exist yet doesn't
Who claims to have been to its end?
Thoughts towards my beloved are like that sky
The more I long, the more they remain

Climbing Namsan
The rain falling on Namsan in Seoul
A thousand streams, ten thousand streams, ninety million streams
In the green pine grove, it's sorrowful rain
On the worn roof of Changch'ungdan, it's mournful rain

Looking out at the distant ten-ri road of the Nodol Plain
Rain falling on the sandy expanse, rain falling on the river
The boatman turning back—who could it be?
His damp hemp jacket will get soaked again

Looking down, there's no one in the city center
Drip, drip falls the rain, oh so desolate
In this vast city, forty ri wide
Where has the master gone, leaving only the falling rain?<47>
<46-47>

**Summary**

This article presents three poems by Yi Ŭn-sang from 1929. "The Horse Driver" uses the image of a driver crossing a dark, difficult pass to symbolize endurance through hardship. "Thoughts Towards My Beloved" expresses infinite longing, comparing it to the bottomless sea and endless sky. "Climbing Namsan" paints a desolate picture of rain falling over Seoul, specifically mentioning Namsan and Changch'ungdan, evoking feelings of emptiness and sorrow in the seemingly deserted capital. From a historical perspective, these poems, written during the Japanese colonial period, are significant. The themes of enduring hardship ("The Horse Driver"), intense longing for a 'Nim' (which could be interpreted nationally), and the melancholic depiction of Seoul under rain ("Climbing Namsan") likely reflect the somber atmosphere and suppressed national sentiments of Koreans under colonial rule. Historians might find the poems valuable for understanding the emotional landscape and subtle expressions of resistance or despair during this era.

말몰이

말몰이
멀어라 험하여라
넘어가는 고개ㅅ길을
밤은 깁퍼 캄캄한데
어이 갈가나 어이 가리

산 가튼 짐을 말끼에 실고
초롱불 든 우리 말몰이
산이 울리게 놀애부르며
이 어둔 밤에 재를 넘네

님 향한 생각이야
뉘라서 저 바다를 밋이 없다노
바다는 다이는 곳 잇으렷마는
님 향한 상각이야 밋히 잇으리
그리면 그릴스록 깁허가구요

잇는 듯 잇지 안는 하늘이끗니
뉘라서 가본 이가 잇다하든고
님 향한 생각이야 저 하늘갓해
그리면 그럴스록 남아 잇구요

南山에 올아
서울이라 남산에 나리는 비는
천출기라 만줄기 구억만줄기
푸를은 솔밧에는 애굿은 비가
장충단 헌 집웅엔 구슬픈 빌세

바라보면 노돌ㅅ벌 십나라 먼 길
모래판에 오는 비 강에 오는 비
가다가 되 오는 배 사공이 뉜고
노젓은 베적삼이 다시 젓으리

굽어보면 장 안엔 아무도 없이
주룩주룩 오는 비 쓸쓸도 하이
사십리 넓을세라 이 큰 장안에
주인은 어데가고 비만 오는고＜47＞
