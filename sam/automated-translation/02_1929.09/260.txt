**Body Text Translation**
Record Without Head or Tail
Sŏl Ŭi-sik in Tokyo

Musashino (武藏野)

When I open the north window and sit, a field of grass comes into view. Across the main road where night soil carts, vegetable vendors, Western-dressed beauties, and drunkards pass, I see a grass field that holds the withered autumn all to itself. It is a corner of Musashino (武藏野), where the poet Lo Hua (盧花) listened to the crickets' song and the haiku master Issa (一荼) glimpsed the dance of frogs.

The plains of Musashino (武藏野), where the limbs of Greater Tokyo stretch out without gaps and the shouts of new civilization constantly roll in, retain only their name and change constantly. Last year and this year are different, yesterday and today are different; how different must they have become over a hundred years, over a thousand years?

When the late autumn sunset strikes the aerial wires of the wireless corps that boasts of civilization, and when the sorrow of unemployment diminishes amidst the loudspeaker of the 'radio' that sings the praises of the modern era, what use is there in speaking of the nameless grasses, trampled carelessly and plucked haphazardly? When the march of the city replaces the crickets' song and the jazz of pleasure overwhelms the frogs' dance, what use is there in speaking of the flowers that have lost their season, having lost even their fragrance and color?

The grass withers, soaked in dew. The flowers fade, scattered by the wind. The space enveloping the trampled grass tumbles headlong. Time, entwined with the scattered flowers, dashes away.<34>
Time flows. Space also expands. In this flowing, expanding time and space, how the ways of the world must have changed? As time flows and space expands, in this flowing, expanding time and space, how lives must have diverged?

The wind brushes past the old pine tree of Renge-ji Temple (蓮華寺), which silently hides speechless white bones. The chilly wind of late autumn brushes past. Shadows have fallen upon the main road where night soil carts, vegetable vendors, Western-dressed beauties, and drunkards pass. The weary shadows of the setting autumn sun have fallen.

The time and space of Musashino (武藏野), which holds the withered autumn all to itself, once again dash away. Once again, they tumble headlong. (Late Autumn, Mujin Year [1928]) <35>
<34-35>

**Summary**

Writing from Tokyo in late 1928, Sŏl Ŭi-sik reflects melancholically on the Musashino plains. He contrasts the disappearing natural landscape, once frequented by poets, with the relentless encroachment of modern Tokyo – symbolized by radios, jazz, and paved roads carrying diverse city folk. The author laments the loss of nature's beauty and tranquility, feeling the rapid, disorienting passage of time and change that erases the past. For historians, this piece offers insight into a Korean intellectual's perception of Japanese modernization during the colonial era, reflecting feelings of displacement, alienation, and nostalgia for a fading natural world amidst rapid urban expansion. It showcases a critical or at least ambivalent view towards the changes brought by "new civilization."

無頭無尾錄
						東京에셔 薛義植
武藏野
北窓을 열어 제치고 안즈면 풀밧치 보히오. 똥수레와 푸성귀장사와 洋裝美人과 酒酊꾼이 지나가는 큰길 건너로 시드른 가을을 혼자 차지한 폴밧치 보히오. 文人盧花가 지렝이 노래를 듯고 俳人一荼가 개고리 춤을 엿보든 武藏野의 한쪽이오.

大東京의 팔다리가 빈틈 업시 뻣치고 新文明의 고함소리가 쉴새 업지 굴러오는 武藏野의 벌판은 이름만 남기고 時時로 번지오. 昨年과 今年이 다르고 어제와 오늘이 다르거니 百年을 두고 千年을 두고 얼마나 달럿든고?

文明을 자랑하는 無電隊의 架空線에 晩秋의 斜陽이 부듸치고 현대를 驅歌하는 『라듸오』의 擴聲機에 실*의 설음이 자자질 때 함부로 밟히고 되는대로 뜨끼인.
名色업는 풀들이야 말하여 무엇하오. 都會의 行進曲이 지렝이노래를 대신하고 歡樂의 『째즈』가 개고리춤을 압도할 때. 香내마저 빠지고 빗갈좃차 업서진 시절일흔 꼿들이야 말하여 무엇하오.

풀은 시들어 이슬에 저젓소. 꼿은 이울어 바람에 날리오. 즛밟힌 풀을 싸고 덥흔 공간은 곤두박질을 치오. 휘날린 꼿에 휘감긴 시간은 다름박질을 치오.＜34＞
시간은 흐르오. 공간도 번지오. 흐르고 번지는 이 시간, 이 공간에 世態는 얼마나 바뀌엿든고? 시간도 흐르거니 공간도 번지거니 호르고 번지는 이 시간 이 공간에 인생은 얼마나 갈렷든고?

말업는 白骨을 말업시 감춘 蓮華寺의 老松에는 바람이 슷치오. 느진 가을의 쌀쌀한 바람이 슷치오. 똥수레와 푸성귀장사와 洋裝美人과 酒酊꾼이 지나가는 큰길 우에는 그늘이 들엇소. 점으러가는 가을해의 고닯은 그늘이 들엇소.

시드른 가을을 혼자 차지한 武藏野의 시간과 공간은 또한번 다름박질을 치오. 또한번 곤두박질을 치오. (戊辰 晩秋) ＜35＞
