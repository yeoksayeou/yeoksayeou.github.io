上海寶山路 (Shanghai Paoshan Road)
						李如星 (Yi Yŏ-sŏng)
Our home is on Paoshan Road (寶山路) in Chap'ei (閘北). My sick wife went to the IMA hospital in the British Concession (英租界). I went looking for Mr. R to Ch'unggyŏngni (崇慶里) on KC Road (KC路) in the French Concession (法界).
This day was one where, rare for Shanghai weather, the dust and smog had completely cleared, and the sun's violet rays beat down hotly. The streets bustled with yellow rickshaws (黃包車 - jinrikisha), automobiles (汽車 - jidōsha), and pedestrians, and the shops were open as usual. The advertising band music blaring from the second floor of silk shops (綢緞店) and department stores (百貨店) sounded just like yesterday.
Where could Mr. R have gone?
I had no choice but to turn back aimlessly. As I turned my steps toward the North Station (北站 - Shanghai North Railway Station) and entered the Suchou Road (蘇州路) intersection, I happened to meet my wife there, and we walked together to the entrance of 00 Road.
Ah! What? Over there!
A crowd, like a madly churning, angry tide, comes rushing towards us. Behind the crowd, white smoke is visible. Gunpowder smoke (硝烟). Gunshots, machine gun sounds are heard. The sound is deafening and heavy.
"Honey! The road is blocked!"
"Then what should we do—"
"Let's go.— Come quickly!"<11>
My wife is sick and weak. Only today did she muster the courage to walk to the hospital. Even under the imminent threat to her life, her fragile legs and heavy feet couldn't run more than a few steps before she collapsed.
The gunfire advances.
It moves toward us.
The crowd surges past like a flood, and in its wake, only three or four piles of corpses (屍體), clumped with blood and dirt, remain rolling; besides us, husband and wife, no one is left.
On the grey street, devoid of human figures, the trembling and terror have passed, replaced instead by bitterness.
An hour later, when we found some respite in the basement of a certain Chinese primary school, the joy of revival (?) burst forth in laughter.
That basement was gloomy and filled with cold air. Watermelon seeds spat out by children and peanut shells were mixed with brownish water – impossible to tell if it was tea water or urine water – strewn messily across the entire room. In the primary school's code of `săso ch'ŏngŭi chint'oe chi chŏl` (灑掃應對進退之節 - the rules of cleaning, responding, associating, advancing, and retreating), they might not know `ŭngdae chint'oe` (responding, advancing, retreating), but it was a primary school that clearly hadn't diligently taught `săso chi chŏl` (the rules of cleaning). To think this was the school's student cafeteria was astonishing!
However, that basement was precious. It supported a three-story building above it, so it would likely be unaffected by any ordinary artillery shell landing on it, and being a basement, rifle bullets posed no threat whatsoever. Furthermore, a 4-5 *chang* (丈 - unit of length, approx. 3.3m) brick wall surrounded the front, making it such a safe refuge. Indeed, if Korean houses (朝鮮家屋) are said to be designed against the cold (防寒的), it seemed fitting to say Chinese houses (支那家屋) are designed against disaster (防禍的).
As we held our breath quietly, it felt as if Shanghai was shattering; artillery sounds and shouts (吶喊聲) that hadn't been audible before now rang out!
By now, sounds like rifle fire were no longer surprising at all.<12>
Ignoring my wife's pleas, I went up to the third floor.
Despite countless bullets whipping through the Shanghai air, the warm, bright sunlight peacefully streamed through the south-facing balcony window. However, the ventilation window above that main window had a round hole pierced through it, and the ceiling bore the scar of a bullet impact. It was clearly the work of a stray bullet (流彈), serving as a powerful warning not to get too close to the window. Yet, the shouts from outside the window! The booming artillery! The countless gunshots seeming to pass by my ears! They seemed to rebuke my hesitation and mock my cowardice.
By the time I lightly leaned against the windowsill and looked out at the street scene, British Army (英國陸軍) military trucks were already racing towards the vicinity of Chiangwan Road (江灣路), packed full of Indian soldiers (印度兵). These trucks were transport vehicles, protected on three sides by canvas painted in camouflage colors stretched over an iron frame, with only the rear open. Inside, South Indian (南印度) ‘Dravida’ (드라비다) race Indian soldiers, looking like chimney sweeps, were crammed in so tightly they seemed to exceed a 2-ton weight limit. The leisurely movements and vacant expressions of the Indians, as if all tension had melted away in the fierce heat of the sun, remained utterly unchanged even on a day of killing like this. As the 8 or 9 vehicles drove off in succession, to their right, figures like rickshaw pullers (黃包車車夫), postmen (郵便配達夫), students (學生), shop assistants (店員), coolies (苦力), and amahs (阿姆 - female servants/nannies) were gesturing wildly and seemingly hurling insults, their hands holding pistols, long swords (長釰), clubs (棍棒), or even bolt-action rifles (旋條銃). The hands holding swords looked liable to strike their own ears or the head of the person in front, and the way they held the guns threatened those standing beside them and startled those behind – needless to say, even more so for those ahead. Truly a fearsome armed group (武裝群). A fearsome armed group to the enemy, perhaps, but also a fearsome armed group to themselves.<13> Amidst them, one armed youth darted out, clumsily cocked the rusty trigger, pulled a bullet from his pocket, loaded one round, aimed at the densely packed Indian soldiers in the back of the departing truck, and fired one shot. No matter how untrained the shooter, how could they miss a densely packed unit at point-blank range? One shot, then another, fired wildly!
"Ah! Pitiful. Those poor lives." "How many dark lives must have fallen bleeding inside?"
"How painfully they must have realized their stubbornness was wrong." "But realizing stubbornness is wrong is not something to grasp at the moment of death. How fortunate it would have been if they had realized it earlier in life, from the beginning." I murmured to myself, lamenting the dead like this.
However, the trucks heartlessly sped away like wind and rain, towards the battlefield, towards the battlefield. The loyalty of the Indians offered as living sacrifices for the safety of the 'Great British Concession' (大英租界)— (Omitted in original text - 中略-원문-)
At that moment, the armed group, moving perpendicularly across the path the trucks had taken, rushed like a torrent from a narrow alley on the right into the alleyway on the left. (Omitted in original text - 略-원문-)
The standard-bearer was a postman. A person looking like a shop assistant following behind had taken the postman's mailbag over his shoulder and was pulling out some kind of leaflets (『빠라』) from it and scattering them. Then students, coolies, rickshaw pullers, and amahs followed, shouting loudly. Their leaders stood on either side of the column, directing and supervising with pistols held in both hands.
'Entering the Concession' (租界侵入), 'Looting Residences' (刦掠住宅) were major prohibitions for their operations. The pistols in the leaders' hands were a precaution against the excited ragtag mob (烏合之衆) perhaps stepping out of line into licentiousness.
After 30-40 minutes, they had all passed through the Concession road, and the shouts and gunfire could only be heard distantly from the southern part of the North Station. At that point, the British troops blockaded the narrow roads on the left and right with barbed wire,<14> sandbags (沙袋), and wire mesh panels, stationing one light machine gun at each. Then, suddenly, a flood of automobiles, rickshaws, wheelbarrows (一輪車), bicycles (自轉車), and pedestrians surged forward all at once, as if a dam had burst.
I rushed downstairs quickly.
"Honey, let's go home."
My wife, with a strangely excited face, forcefully put two fingers to her lips, signaling me not to speak.
"They're making a fuss amongst themselves, saying I look like a Japanese woman!"
As I stepped down onto the basement stairs, someone resembling a gangster (流氓 - member of secret societies like the Green/Red Gangs) popped out and, with a very friendly smiling face, asked for our home address (府上).
"Paoshan Road (寶山路)—"
My reply was brief.
"You're a Korean person (高麗사람), aren't you?"
"Duìle" (對了 - That's right).
My reply was consistently brief.
Then we walked out towards the school's main gate. The 40 or 50 Chinese refugees who had brought bundles of bedding and were bustling about noisily all turned their gazes towards us.
"Hǎoxiàng Dōngyángrén" (好像東洋人 - Looks like an East Asian/Japanese).
As this murmur arose from various mouths, our footsteps seemed to grow heavier. Since the Nanjing Incident (南京事件), walking boldly through Chinese streets with features resembling those of an 'East Asian' (東洋人 - often meaning Japanese) was too daring an act.
Finally, we found our way back home. Mr. R opened the door, having waited anxiously.
Mr. R, being someone with much experience of the hardships in China (中國風塵), put aside all other talk and said,<15>
"Do you have food (飯膳)? Things looked a bit different when I came, so I brought a sack of rice."
We couldn't help but admire Mr. R's thoughtfulness (留念性).
So, after preparing and eating lunch (午飯), we planned to evacuate to the French Concession under Mr. R's guidance. As we bundled our luggage, it was already late afternoon, the day was drawing to a close, my wife began to develop a fever, and on top of that, the gunfire grew increasingly terrifying as darkness fell. The glass pane on the balcony of the house opposite shattered and fell. There was no sign of life in the houses in front or behind, but peeking through the back window of our house, a unit of plainclothes soldiers (便衣隊) was pressed flat against the base of the wall of Sunsiang Li (順祥里) opposite, firing fiercely towards Ch'ingyün Road (靑雲路). If return fire came from that direction, what would happen to our house? The closer their fierce firing moved towards Ch'ingyün Road, the greater our anxiety grew. Indeed, return fire! Return fire is coming! Roof tiles from our house fell, and the sound of bullets hitting the brick of the west wall sent chills down one's spine.
"Evacuate!" (避難!)
This word came from all three of our mouths simultaneously. But stepping even one foot outside the door would have been reckless courage (蠻勇). This was because the west wall of our house was the target (?) of the return fire, or at least within its line of sight. How thankful we were that the west wall was indeed built of brick (煉瓦築)—.
Thus, the next morning arrived. On the streets lay the corpses (遺屍) of Sun Ch'uan-fang's (孫傳芳) defeated soldiers, sprawled here and there, spilling blood. Their gray uniforms were worn out and torn, patched and re-patched, looking no different from the rags (襤褸衣) of beggars (丐乞). (One page omitted below - 以下1頁略-원문-)
The fighting still showed no sign of ending. Near the T'ient'unghuo Station (天通火站), the rifle and machine gun fire of Sun Ch'uan-fang's army, and near the Commercial Press (常務印書局) printing factory and its library, the rifle and machine gun fire of the Revolutionary Regular Army (革命正規軍),<16> chaotically echoed each other, and the second day of slaughter drew to a close amidst the gunpowder smoke.
The people of Shanghai, experienced in years of warfare (兵燹), had all evacuated to the French Concession (佛相界), the International Settlement (公共相界), or other safe zones, leaving only heavy padlocks hanging on every door. However, among them, families without money, families without relatives in the Concession areas, or people like us who would have to brave 10 *li* (里) of bullet rain just to reach a place of refuge, seemed to be lingering behind in this alley or that, hoping for sheer luck.
Tonight, there is no electricity. The lights are out. In the pitch-black street of death (漆暗한 死의 街), the rain of bullets (彈雨) pours down ceaselessly, while shouts like the curses of demons (惡魔의 呪咀) advance and retreat, rise and fall, near and far, continuing throughout the night. When the next morning came, we boiled and ate another meal of porridge and finished.
Hiding for two days (蟄伏 2일). Mr. R's depression seemed to have fermented fully. Letting out a long yawn, he said,
"I think I need to go out today. Even if a man is just watching a spectacle, he should do it outside; doing it inside the room feels a bit shameful—"
However, I absolutely lacked the courage to push Mr. R out the door. Moreover, upon hearing his words, my wife was startled and went to hide Mr. R's hat somewhere before returning.
Indeed. Cooped up eating nothing but soy sauce rice (간장밥) for two whole days, I couldn't help but feel oppressed (壓症) myself. Thinking again, just as Mr. R said, even if I couldn't take up arms like others, it felt truly cowardly (懦夫) to not even watch the spectacle from outside, but just tremble uselessly inside the room. But then again, the thought arose: perhaps this kind of extravagant talk was emerging because, after hearing gunfire day and night for two days, my ears had grown numb to it, and the bone-chilling terror (毛骨의 悚然한 맛) had completely disappeared.<17>
Presently, as the 10 o'clock bell rang, the distant cannon fire (砲響) and nearby gunshots (銃聲) began to subside, and the suffocated breath of greater Shanghai started to emerge from alleyway after alleyway.
Already, pedestrians thronged the street on Hsiehshan Road (寫山路), and rickshaw pullers were running with customers. Some customers were haggling intensely with the pullers, saying "One small silver coin" (一角小洋), "One large silver coin" (一角大洋). The wonton (『완당』) seller had also emerged somehow, surrounded by 4 or 5 crowded customers, doing great business (大『發射』 - lit. great 'firing', slang for making money). Where had these homeless people evacuated to, that their lives were all intact? And where had they hidden their crucial tools of trade (營利器具) to bring them out now so completely? Their mindset, returning to their previous way of life (生活態) in an instant when stray bullets hadn't even completely ceased, was endlessly strange. Should this be called the 'continental boldness' (大陸的 活膽性) of the Chinese? Or the unique Chinese desire for profit and wealth (謀利發財慾)? Or perhaps it was due to the worldwide poverty (世界的 貧窮) of the Chinese belonging to the fourth estate (第四階級)? It was truly a spectacle difficult for someone like me, a peninsular person (半島人), to comprehend.
In our room, a consensus (公論) emerged to buy and eat a bowl of wontons. At that time, I became the 'special correspondent' (特派員) and went out onto the street for the first time in a long while.
In front of the Chinese Relief Society (中國濟難會) Hall, the Blue Sky, White Sun, and a Wholly Red Earth flag (靑天白日 滿地紅旗) was already fluttering, replacing the old five-colored flag (5色紅旗). In front of the building stood 5 or 6 guards (番兵), among whom was a boy soldier (少年兵) of about 15 or 16 years old, standing as if about to burst into tears from the weight of his rifle. To the left, about ten hastily made wooden coffins (木槨) were laid out flat. Approaching them, I saw they were not empty; inside each lay a惨담한 victim, bloodstains (血斑) dotting their tattered military uniforms (襤褸軍服). The coffin lids were left half-open, presumably to facilitate identification (鑑別) by relatives, but it caused considerable unpleasantness (不快) for passersby.<18> Just then, I heard the sound of horse hooves (馬蹄) from behind and turned my head.
Ah, they were female cavalry! (女騎兵!)
Seven or eight women on horseback, with silent and solemn demeanor, were inspecting the ** battle-scarred area, looking around intently as they passed towards the * Station. Though they were young women who should bloom with laughter, their spirit (氣宇) seemed more *austere* than men's, and their inherent ** feminine fleshly *** charm seemed to have no time to be displayed amidst the ** dust of battle, appearing only ** solemn. (Asterisks denote illegible/missing characters in the source)
Among them, one female rider wore a Zhongshan suit (中山服) with an ammunition belt (彈帶) fastened around her waist. The long sword (長釰) slung at her left hip occasionally struck the black boots she wore, creating, along with the martial sound of the horse's hooves, a strange sense of inspiration (感激) in me.
I must have loitered on that street for about an hour.
When I returned home, the others were waiting for me, the wontons having gone completely cold. Consequently, I was busy receiving scoldings for about 10 minutes.
A little later, the landlord came, knocked on the door, and loudly inquired about our well-being.
"Shameless things! Only care about money! Don't open the door! Let them enjoy their own careful evacuation a bit longer," I suggested. But that blasted wonton seller! Like demanding payment for the chipped bowl immediately after eating, he joined forces with the landlord and caused a commotion, demanding the bowl back. Even so, we didn't go down and open the door right away.
Around sunset, the landlord came again and knocked. This time, Mr. R opened the door, seemingly raising his eyebrows slightly, but the landlord couple both came in, bowing subserviently (兢兢然 拜揖) and offering profuse apologies (百非千謝).
Then, a little later, a pair of ham hocks (南腿 - pig's hind legs) entered through the open door. (to page 29) <19>

"Qǐng kuān" "Qǐng kuān" (請寬 Please forgive us)
The three of us burst out laughing and chorused, "Bù yě zhēn" "Bù yě zhēn" (不也眞 It doesn't matter / No problem). At that point, the landlord closed the door and made a show of leaving very quickly, but then, perhaps having second thoughts about the cost, he slyly tucked the pig legs he had placed on the writing desk (寫書床) back under his arm, uttered "Xièxie" (謝謝 Thank you) once or twice, and disappeared out the door.
We burst out laughing again, louder this time.
That night, we slept soundly with pillows high (高枕安眠). Even the sound of Mr. R snoring sounded extremely peaceful.
Thus, Paoshan Road left us with an unforgettable, yet also interesting, impression. (End) <29>

**Summary**

The article recounts the harrowing experience of the narrator, Yi Yŏ-sŏng, a Korean living in Shanghai's Paoshan Road area, who gets caught in street fighting with his sick wife. They witness intense clashes involving Chinese factions (likely KMT vs. warlord troops) and British/Indian forces protecting the International Settlement. Finding temporary refuge in a school basement, Yi observes the chaos, the brutality, the resilience of ordinary Shanghainese resuming daily life amidst danger, and peculiar sights like female cavalry. The narrative concludes with a darkly humorous encounter with their landlord after the immediate danger subsides. From a historical perspective, the piece offers a valuable ground-level view of the volatile situation in Shanghai during the late 1920s (likely reflecting the 1927 Uprising), seen through the eyes of a Korean colonial subject. Historians might find Yi's observations on the different factions, the foreign presence, the attitudes towards Koreans (mistaken for Japanese), and the stark contrast between the chaos of war and the swift return to commerce particularly interesting, providing insights into the lived experience of non-Chinese residents during this turbulent period.

上海寶山路
						李如星
우리집은 閘北 寶山路이다. 병든 안해는 英租界 ima병원으로 갓다. 나는 R군을 차저 法界 KC路 崇慶里로 갓다.
이 날은 上海天氣로서는 稀罕하다 할 만큼 塵埃와 雲烟이 廓淸되여 태양의 紫光線이 따겁게 나려 쪼이는 날이다. 街上에는 黃包車(人力車) 汽車(自働車) 步行人으로 雜踏하고 店舖도 如常하게 열녓다. 綢緞店이나 百貨店 2層階 우에서 울니는 廣告軍樂도 어제가치 들닌다.
R군은 어듸를 갓슬가.
나는 할 일업시 도라서지 안을 수 업섯다. 北站(上海 北部 停車場)울 향하야 거름을 돌녓다가 蘇州路 네거리를 접어들 때 나는 우연히 妻을 그 곳서 만나 00路 들미까지 걸어 오는 길이다.
아! 무엇? 저!
군중은 뒤우치는 狂爛怒濤와 가치 우리편을 향하야 달녀 온다. 그 군중의 背後에는 뽀-얀 연기가 보인다. 硝烟이다. 총소리 기관총소리가 들닌다. 그 소리는 귀따갑고 묵직한 소리다.

우리를 향하야 前進한다.

人影이 끈어진 灰色 街路에는 戰慄, 恐怖도 다 지나가고 차라리 쓴픔이 돌앗다.
그 한시간 뒤 中國人 某小學校 地下室에서 우리들이 小康을 엇게 되엿슬 때 甦生(?)의 깁봄은 우슴으로 터저 나오게 되엿다.
그 地下室은 음침하고 냉기가 가둑 차 잇섯다. 아동들의 까먹은 수박씨 落花生 껍질은 茶水인지 尿水인지 모를 누릭찍한 물에 반죽이 되다십히 되야 왼방에 어즐녓다. 小學의 灑掃應對進退之節에 應對進退는 몰나도 灑掃之節은 알들하게도 못가르친 小學校이엿다. 그것이 그 學校 生徒食堂이라는 대야 놀나지 안을 수 잇나!
그러나 그 지하실은 고마왓다. 그 지하실은 그 우에 3층 건물을 이엿스니 여간한 砲彈은 나려 꼿처도 無關할 것이요 또 지하실인만큼 小銃 彈丸이야 何等 威嚇을 주지 못한다.  그 압헤는 4,5丈 煉瓦墻이 둘너 잇스니 얼마나 안전한 피난처일가. 과연 朝鮮家屋을 防寒的이라 하면 支那家屋은 防禍的이다 하야 적당할 것도 갓텃다.
가만히 숨을 죽이고 잇스니 上海는 부서지는 것 가튼대 악가도 들니지 안튼 砲聲과 吶喊聲!
이제 와서는 小銃聲 갓튼 것은 何等 驚異가 안이다.＜12＞

뭇 彈子가 上海의 空間을 휘치고 잇는대도 따뜻하고 밝은 太陽光線은 그 南便 洋臺 창문울 평화롭게 透射하고 잇다. 그러나 그 창문 우에 부튼 換氣窓에는 동글게 구멍이 뚤니고 天井에는 彈丸이 들어박힌 破痕이 잇다. 그것은 流彈의 所爲인 것이 분명한 것만츰 창문 각가이 接案치 말나는 有力한 警告이엿다. 그러나 창 박게서 들니는 吶喊! 轟轟한 砲聲! 내 귀ㅅ가로 지나는 듯하는 數업는 총소리! 는 나의 逡巡을 詈罵하는 것 갓기도 하고 卑㥘을 嘲笑하는 것 갓기도 하엿다.
내가 창문 턱에 몸을 가겹게 부치고 街界를 내여다 볼 때는 벌서 英國陸軍의 軍用 自働車가 印度兵을 갓득갓득 실고 江灣路 부근을 향하야 疾走하는 때이다. 그 自働車들은 三面을 鐵架에 保護色 칠한 防水布로서 둘으고 後面만 割開된 輜重用 自働車인대 그 속에는 煙筒掃除夫 갓튼 南印度産 『드라비다』族 印度兵이 2噸 重量이 오히려 넘을만치 密乘하엿다. 모든 緊張性이 劇烈한 태양열에 다 녹아버린 듯한 印度人의 悠悠한 동작과 虛心한 표정은 今日- 이 갓튼 殺日-에도 조곰도 변함이 업다. 그들을 실은 8,9輛車가 接種馳去할 때에 그 우측에서는 黃包車車夫 郵便配達夫, 學生, 店員, 苦力, 阿姆(어멈) 등이 손짓발짓을 하면서 무어라고 辱찌거리를 퍼붓는 모양인대 그 손에는 권총, 長釰, 棍棒들을 無非들엇고 혹은 旋條銃을 들엇다. 그 칼든 손은 잘못하면 제 귀나 압 사람 머리를 날닐 듯 하고 그 총 잡은 꼴은 엽해 선 사람들 견우기도 하고 뒤ㅅ 선 사람을 놀나게도 하니 압선 사람의게야 더 말할 것이 업겟나. 실로 무서운 武裝群이다. 敵의게도 무서운 武裝群이려니와 그네들 자신의게도 무서운 武裝軍이다.＜13＞ 그 가운대 一員 武裝靑年이 뛰여 나서더니 녹쓴 방아쇠를 서툴게 잭겨노코 『포케트』 속에 彈子 한 개를 끄내여 한 방을 재여 모라가는 自働車 後面 印度兵丁이 密乘한 곳을 견양하야 一發을 띈다. 아모리 훈련업는 砲手이기로 只呎에서 密集部隊를 보고 놋는 것이야 안마칠 理致가 잇나. 一發 又 一發 亂發!
『아! 가엽다. 불상한 생명들』 『數多의 검은 생명들이 그 속에서 피 흘리고 넘어젓술가』
