Magazine Title: 삼천리 제7권 제9호 (三千里 第七卷 第九號)
Publication Date: 1935년 10월 01일 (October 1, 1935)
Article Title: 獨身女性의 貞操論 (On the Chastity of Single Women)
Author: 羅蕙錫 (Na Hye-sŏk)
Article Type: 문예기타 (Literary/Other)

On the Chastity of Single Women
						Na Hye-sŏk

"Sister, write a love letter for me."

K, who is currently a working woman (職業婦人), came to ask a favor from her older sister, S. K was S’s most beloved younger sibling, and she would occasionally come to impose like this. When K talked day and night to her sister about the romance she was experiencing with her fiancé, Y, S would listen with cute interest.

"Hey, this is giving me a headache."

"What's wrong? You've gotten old too, sister."

"I have gotten old, but my mind is troubled..."

"Why is that?"

K’s eyes widened.

"Wouldn't it be so? My body is old, but my heart is not (身老心不老)."

"So you mean you're recalling the romances of your youth, sister?"

"That too, but do you think there's no romance now?"

"Oh my, how scandalous! An old person like you."

"That's why I'm worried."

"Really? Sister, do you also miss your lover like me, burning with anxiety and finding it distressing not to be able to sleep at night?"

"That's the love of youth. The love of middle age is different."

"How is it different, sister?"

K pressed closer.

"I'll tell you about that later."

"Tell me now. Please, sister..."

"It's not necessary for you now, and it would be like reading scriptures to a cow's ear (쇠귀의 경익는 격); you wouldn't understand it, so let's drop it."

"Then hurry up and write me one letter."

"To Y, you mean?"

"Of course."

"By when?"

"By tomorrow morning."

"This is quite the express request!"

"The letter that came from Y the other day, you saw it, sister? It's the reply to that letter."

"Then I'll have to write a long one."

"Since the letter received was long, the letter sent should be long too."

"But you're not old yet, and you're being foolish."

"Why?"

"Who asks someone to ghostwrite (代筆) a love letter?" <106>

"Who doesn't know that?"

"It's like falling into a pit with your eyes wide open."

"Here comes your headache-inducing theory again, sister."

"It's not a theory, isn't it true? Isn't a so-called love letter something written with those delicate hands (섬섬옥수), pouring out the blood boiling fiercely in the heart?"

"Who doesn't know that?"

"Hmph. So you know everything."

"Of course."

"What if I say I won't do it..."

"Sister. Don't be like that, just write this one for me, please."

K clung to her sister, acting spoiled.

"So you've run out of ideas (미천이 드러낫단 말이지)."

"Yes. You know best, sister. I probably don't have anything left to write."

"That's likely. With your rat's tail-sized knowledge (쥐꼬랑지만 한 學識)."

"Right. I can't measure up as Y's counterpart."

"Hey, seeing Y's letter, he seemed quite accomplished. Quite a person equipped with both human warmth (人情味) and humanity (인간애)."

"I guess he seems like that. So please write it accordingly."

"Shall I try writing it?"

S briefly looked up at the opposite wall, hesitating.

"Oh, wonderful!"

"It's quite a difficult order."

"It's difficult for me, but it's easy for you, sister."

"Well, if I were writing to my own lover, it would be easy."

"Write it with the feeling you'd have writing to your lover, sister."

"What if I go crazy doing that?"

"As expected, you're a passionate person, sister."

"Even though I'm old, the passion remains."

"That's right. Because you're an artist."

"You're quite something yourself, knowing things like that."

"You tend to look down on naive people just a little, sister."

"You shouldn't take it that way. It's because I find you cute."

S gently patted K's back.

"Well then, sister, yoroshiku tanomu yo (ヨロシクタノムヨ - Please take care of it)."

K went to the hospital, her workplace where she went daily.

S watched K leave and, leaning back slightly, smiled faintly. It was because she found the sweet whispers between K and Y adorable and lovely, and their future path, progressing step by step, flashed before her eyes like a motion picture film. <107>
And because she anticipated both comedy and tragedy in their future path, feeling as though she were watching a scene from a play. S opened her desk drawer, took out letter paper, and picked up her pen.

*My dearest Y,*

*Is it already spring? It seems spring has probably come. Spring has come, hasn't it? Indeed. Spring has arrived. Ah, it's already spring.*

*The spring of the city, the spring of the countryside, the warm spring, the beautiful spring, the spring of birdsong (鳴啼), the spring of flower fields (花田), the spring of the flute, the spring of people, the spring of beasts (禽獸), the spring of joy (喜), the spring of sorrow (悲), the spring of the long willow-lined banks (柳川長堤), the spring of Hwahongmun (華虹門), the spring of Panghwasuryujŏng (訪花隨柳亭), a complete spring has found its way here. How happy are we two, possessing both this spring of nature and the spring of life together? Seeing how nature, which seems most simple, never bores us, it seems the power of nature (自然力) must have abundant inner strength (內在力).*

*Until today, it felt like 'the heavens are vast, yet I cannot lift my head; the earth is wide, yet I cannot steady my feet' (天高萬里不擧頭 地濶千里不定足); somehow, my surroundings felt uncomfortable. But from today, my heart is at ease, strength comes naturally, and I feel firmly supported (依支). Since you, dear sir (貴公), are already a person equipped with human warmth and humanity, I believe you understand everything. I trust you will love and cherish me forever, and I wish to receive this, embrace it, and hold it to my heart with all my sincerity.*

*Reading your esteemed letter (貴緘) again and again, I feel many things. Indeed, it is so. A person cannot truly understand another's situation without knowing hardship. That is, one cannot become a person of substance. You say you have starved and even gathered firewood. That is how you became the person you are today (今日의 貴公). Though I am lacking (不及하나마), I too have experienced some hardship. Even if I may not know how to understand others, I do know how to understand what others say. Seen from this point, I believe our path ahead is a sturdy one that guarantees happiness. Please guide me well in every way.*

*...etcetera...*

*K, growing in the ninety days of spring light (90春光)*

The next morning, K stopped by S's place.

"Sister, did you finish writing it?"

"I finished, but it might not work just like that."

S, who enjoyed joking, added another joke.

"Then what should I do?"

"Who writes a love letter just like that? It's a crystallization of blood and sweat."

"You mean I have to treat you again?"

"Is there any other choice?"

"I'll do it."

"How?"

"When Y gets his salary, let's go eat temple food."

"That's a great idea."

"Now that the conditions are met, give me the letter."

"Hey. I nearly died trying to squeeze it out. Was there anything to write? I just prattled on about silly spring."

"Let me see."

K took the letter and read it.

"Goodness, it's so verbose."

"I wrote it with such effort, and all you give me are thankless words."

"No, no, sister. You wrote it charmingly well." <108>

"If that's the case, then fine."

"You wrote everything that was on my mind. Goodness, you're amazing."

"Well, I've at least grown old with writing."

"Right. Can you still write passionate letters even now?"

"Of course."

"Will I be like that too?"

"And do what?"

"Why?"

"Because it's difficult, that's why."

"It must be flavorful, perhaps."

"The state of 'body old, heart not old' (身老心不老) is something that cannot be savored unless one experiences artistic sentiments through words."

"Then such people must be happy."

"The heart suffers greatly."

"Sister. What's middle-aged love like?"

"Really, I told you to drop it. Still..."

"Tell me. Please."

"The love of youth is like a bonfire, while the love of middle age is like embers (겨불), burning gently; it's a love affair conducted after a full night's sleep."

"Naruhodo (ナルホト - I see). That seems plausible."

"Do you understand?"

"Of course, why wouldn't I?"

"Are you going to mail that letter today?"

"Yes, I have to mail it sassoku (サツソク - right away). Arigatō (アリガトウ - Thank you)."

K leaves.

On a spring day that was neither cold nor hot, on the afternoon of a day when the cherry blossoms were in full bloom at the Hwahongmun Model Farm (華虹門 模範場), at 5 PM, a taxi carrying K and Y after their engagement party stopped in front of S's house gate. K quickly got out and went inside.

"Sister, come out quickly."

S, who happened to be ready, came out. Y stood waiting at the gate.

The taxi carrying the three people sped off towards Pongnyŏngsa Temple (奉寧寺). The fragrant scent of grass carried by the wind refreshed S's head, which had been melancholic. In no time, the taxi stopped at the ridge near Pongnyŏngsa, a little less than 10 *ri* from inside the city walls. The three people climbed the stone steps, looked around the Main Buddha Hall (法堂), chose a quiet room, entered, and ordered dinner. Soon, the meal was ready. They mixed fried seaweed snacks (튀각) crushed into a gourd bowl greased with oil, added bracken (고븨나물) and bellflower root (도라지나물), poured tofu stew broth (두부전골국물) over it, and stirred.

"It's really delicious." <109>

K said, eating heartily.

"Eat a lot."

"It's delicious."

Y also remarked.

"Yes, it really is delicious."

They paid for the meal and stepped outside.

The day was ending, and the bright moon of the 15th night had risen to mid-heaven (仲天).

"Let's walk slowly and talk."

"It really feels nice."

Y smiled, satisfied.

The three people walked slowly.

Above the dark pine trees, the white moon hung, and shadows flickered. The scent of mugwort rose from the ground.

"Don't walk so far ahead."

"Westerners say they can tell which country an East Asian is from just by seeing how they walk together."

"How so?"

Y, who had been walking ahead, stopped and asked.

"If they walk side by side talking, they're Japanese. If they walk spaced apart without saying anything, they're Chinese or Korean (朝鮮人), or so they say."

"Hahaha, hohoho."

"Sister, tell a story."

"Shall I? Shall we talk so we don't notice the long road?"

"Agreed."

"When I went to see the ancient ruins of the Pompeii volcano in Italy (伊太利 뽐뻬火山古蹟), among the customs from 2000 years ago, there was a tiny gourd bottle. When someone died, they would hire people to cry, and they would collect the tears in the gourd bottle and pay them a price (갑슬)."

"Oh my goodness, that's hilarious!" K burst out laughing.

"And in another place, a piece of wall painting remained. It was covered with a lid, showing only men. Since I'm a painter, I looked at it, and what should be there but men weighing male genitals (男子生殖器) on scales."

"What do they do after weighing them?" K laughed again.

"They must be checking the weight," Y said calmly, perhaps implying some meaning.

"The Pompeii customs (뽐뻬風俗) back then were extremely luxurious and lewd (음탕해서). The dining hall had paintings of birds (鳥類畵), the dance hall had paintings of goddesses (女神畵), the bedroom had erotic paintings (春畵), the nursery had free-style paintings (自由畵), and there were rooms where all four walls were black, rooms that were entirely crimson, and rooms that were entirely deep green."

"Isn't Pompeii the place where divine punishment (神罰) supposedly fell because it was too luxurious and lewd?"

Y, possessing common knowledge, remarked.

"Not only that. During the height of Rome (露馬全盛時代), people at banquets would eat food, stick their fingers down their throats to vomit, eat again, and eat again."

"Oh my goodness!" K was startled.

"In the Museum of Ancient Customs in Paris, France (佛蘭西 巴里古風博物舘), there's something called a famous woman's chastity belt (腰帶). In the old days, when a husband went off to war (出戰), women's behavior was apparently so improper that before leaving, he would put a belt around the woman's private parts (陰部), leaving just enough space to urinate, lock it with a padlock, and take the key with him."

"Oh my goodness! What did they do? How scandalous! There are so many strange customs."

"If I were to tell them one by one, there are all sorts <110> of customs."

"That must be true. As civilization and history are long, there must be all sorts of customs."

Y said.

"Hey, K."

"Yes."

"Have you ever seen a fart?"

"How can you see a fart?"

"You haven't seen that?"

Y smiled faintly and said.

"Acting like you know everything."

"So, you don't know?"

"Then tell me."

"You should tell me first."

"No, you who said you saw it should tell first."

K and Y nudged each other, hit each other's backs, pinched each other's skin, and played playfully for a while. S, who had provided this topic, watched them sideways, just smiling faintly. The three people, each dragging their shadow, meandering, appearing white then black among the pine trees, walking towards the city walls while whispering, seemed quite leisurely and amusing.

"You're quite sly."

"Why?"

"Because you don't want to admit you haven't seen it, so you tell someone else to say it."

"Instead of pushing it onto each other like this, let's play rock-paper-scissors (장긔뽕)."

"Okay, let's do that."

"Rock-paper-scissors... Aww, five." [Likely referring to a variation or outcome]

"That's right, the man loses."

"This is a total loss."

"Hurry up and tell me," K pinched Y.

"Ouch... It's a bit awkward to say after being forced like this."

"Can you endure without saying it?"

"Alright, I'll do it."

"Hurry up and tell me." K grabbed Y's shoulder.

"Why are you grabbing someone else's shoulder for bad luck?"

"Hurry up and tell me."

"Sit in a bathtub and let out a fart. How about that?" <111>

"Right, right! That's it! It bubbles up."

"Hahahahaha, hohohoho."

"See? How could you not know that?"

"I know now."

The three held their sides, rolling with laughter. After a moment of silence, the topic shifted to views on life.

"When do you plan to have the wedding ceremony?"

S asked like an elder.

"This time right now is the happiest. The longer the engagement period (約婚期), the more we savor the taste of life."

"However, since marriage isn't the entirety of life, wouldn't it be better for Mr. Y and K not to dither (ウカウカ) needlessly, but to hold the ceremony quickly and settle down?"

"Why would there be a need for that?"

"Because boredom (염증) sets in easily. That is, it's better to make the decision before flaws become visible."

"Isn't it more dangerous if boredom arises after marriage?"

"Whether before or after marriage, everyone experiences boredom at least once."

"Why is that?"

"Love, respect, or compassion last only as long as you don't know someone well. Once you know them, feelings cool down, and flaws appear. It's like the mercury in a thermometer (寒暖計) rising to 100 degrees and then falling to 0, or even below zero in severe cases."

"Could that be true?"

"Of course, it is. Human affection is not limitless; it has limits. Its highs and lows need to strike deeper roots."

"That seems plausible, but surely it depends on the person."

"Because people have common traits (通性)."

"Then how should one live to live well?"

Y, quite interested, delved into the main issue he had been pondering alone until now.

"That's right. How many people deceive themselves with this short-lived thing called love, binding their bodies so they can't move or jump?"

"Ultimately, the goal of life is to become ordinary (평범히)."

"That may be true, but before becoming ordinary, life can be extended somewhat."

"How?"

"Isn't it marrying under the banner of love, having children, struggling to feed them, striving to please the husband, living entangled, and then dying? This is the so-called ordinary life."

"Then is there some other policy? The purpose of life is reproduction (生殖)."

"That's true. Ultimately, everyone follows such a path, but there's no need to follow it quickly, and the social system allows that much freedom."

"I'm not quite sure what you mean."

"To put it differently, when puberty (春期發動期) arrives for men and women, the love of parents or friends isn't enough, and they yearn for the opposite sex, burning with anxiety under the guise of love, <112> and end up binding themselves early, falling into a hell (地獄) where they can't move or jump by their 20s or early 30s, isn't that right?"

"Yes, that's right."

"Rather than doing that, it's necessary to first think about what one is agonizing and suffering over, resolve just that, and extend the period before constrained life begins."

"Perhaps most agonize over sexual desire (性慾方面)."

"That's why social institutions have already been established (設施) for single people, haven't they?"

"You mean brothels (遊廓), right?"

"That's right. Until one can sufficiently guarantee the livelihood of a wife and children (妻子), one should live a single life (獨身生活) and frequent brothels."

"Venereal diseases (花柳病) are scary, and one can lose self-control."

"That can be managed with considerable caution. That's why I once told a certain young man that rather than always visiting one place, he should visit different places."

"That's true. It seems unnecessary to bind oneself early just because of sexual desire."

"There's absolutely no need for that. That's why not only licensed female prostitutes (女子公娼) are necessary, but licensed male prostitutes (男子公娼) are also needed."

"They say Paris has male brothels (男子遊廓), right?"

"Not only Paris, but I've heard firsthand accounts (實談) that in Osaka, older unmarried women, military wives, and widows frequent them."

"Then wouldn't the concept of chastity (정조관념) disappear?"

"Rather than suffering from neurasthenia (신경쇠약) and becoming hysterical (히스테리) to uphold the concept of chastity, paying money to release sexual desire (性慾) and living with a cheerful disposition might be necessary even for social interactions among modern people."

"It will gradually become like that."

"That's why, as culture (人文) develops, more single people emerge, and if only the issue of resolving sexual desire is addressed, the need for a family might decrease, extending the single period (獨身時期) as much as possible."

"Then where does one get spiritual comfort (정신적 위안)?"

"For those on the front lines of life, there's no time to feel such loneliness (孤寂); their work itself becomes their spiritual comfort."

"What about when they grow weary (권태) of work?"

"That kind of thing just has to be overcome through self-discipline (克己)." <113>

"Can one continue living a single life like that?"

"That's why I'm not encouraging single life, but saying it's good to remain single for as long as one can manage."

"Couldn't one easily discard people?"

"Otherwise, don't people discard others eventually anyway?"

"That may be true, but it's a difficult problem."

"It's giving me a headache, let's stop."

"Then how can one build a peaceful family?"

Y had great and many ideals for the new family (新家庭) he would soon establish. However, he wanted to hear the opinion of S, who already had much experience.

"A Western proverb says that to build a peaceful home, 'the husband must see the wife as a flower, and the wife must be aware that she is a blooming flower.'"

"Naruhodo (ナルホト - I see). That sounds plausible."

"Westerners' 'sweet home' is by no means built solely by the efforts of the husband or wife; it lies in the freedom of social interaction between men and women (男女交際). When a husband and wife see each other day and night, boredom easily sets in. Before that happens, if they go out together (同夫人), and the husband dances or converses with another man's wife, and the wife with another woman's husband, their mood is refreshed. That's why, at any gathering, it's considered impolite (실례) for a couple to dance or converse only with each other."

"That seems plausible."

"Isn't it so? They go out, absorb a new atmosphere (새로운 기분), bring it home, and utilize that mood, so how can it not become a sweet home?"

"It will gradually become like that in Korea (朝鮮) too, right?"

"(It will take a long time)."

"The husband experiences all the bitter and sweet tastes in complex society, while the wife repeats the same tasks day after day in the narrow confines of the home. The wife fails to understand the husband's emotional cycles (感情循環), and the husband fails to understand the wife's feelings, so they drift apart endlessly. That home will be tasteless and dry (무미건조). Boredom will arise."

"That's really true."

"That's why, even with love marriages (연애결혼), curiosity is initially piqued, thinking there must be something to the woman. But soon, her depths are seen through, the woman withers just like that, while the man constantly receives social training (社會訓練) and grows. What will be the result? They end up staring blankly at each other like water and barley (물그럼이 말그럼이), and boredom sets in."

"Then it seems men mature faster (早達) than women?"

"That's right. Women mature faster biologically (生殖的), while men mature faster intellectually (智識的). Therefore, intellectually speaking, a man of 25 or 26 corresponds to a woman of 30 or 40."

"Could that be true?"

"Then, if a 30-year-old man marries a 40-year-old woman as his partner, they could build an ideal family (이상적 가정), <114> couldn't they?"

"Well, one could say that, but since there's the condition of the woman's youthful beauty (妙齡의 美), there probably aren't men who would think so transcendently."

"Didn't Renaissance master painter (才畵家) Raphael (라아엘), or the 19th-century genius painter (天才畵家) Renoir (루노아루), admire middle-aged women (中年婦人) and paint only nudes (裸體) of middle-aged women?"

Y mentioned something he had previously heard from some painter.

"If you think about it, between men and women, the beauty of mature middle age (中年의 美) is better than the beauty of youth."

"Then, for a Korean family (朝鮮家庭), what should be done to build a peaceful home?"

"That's the thing. Although they talk about gender equality (남녀평등), many conflicts arise precisely because they think in terms of gender equality. It's better for the husband to possess a sense of superiority (우월감) over the wife and handle matters himself, except for unavoidable ones; that actually leads to less conflict. As an example, you can see this by observing the frequent clashes in new families (新家庭) and the peace maintained in old families (舊家庭)."

"K, listen carefully."

Y nudged the shoulder of K walking beside him.

"A person with tiny fists can't speak, huh?"

K retorted sharply.

"You misunderstand my intentions like this."

"How could I not know? It's just being playful."

S mediated, inwardly guessing the fluctuating emotions of Y and K, who were sometimes friendly, sometimes annoyed.

"Then, rather than marrying a half-baked New Woman (新女性), wouldn't it be better to marry an Old Woman (舊女性)?"

"Still, what's better than knowing? It depends on the superior man."

"Mr. Y, listen carefully."

K nudged Y's shoulder.

"A person with tiny fists can't speak, huh? This is instantly weakening my knees."

Hahahahaha, hohohoho.

"You two play well together. It's a good time."

S spoke like an elder.

"It looks fun."

Y looked at S and said.

"Of course."

"What, sister, as if you didn't know how we were at your age."

"How do you know so well?"

"How could I not know?"

"Really, I should have asked to hear a bit about S's history (歷史)."

"Rather than talking about such trivial past matters, isn't it better to talk about things yet to come?" <115>

"Truly, I have heard many beneficial words."

Y suddenly showed courtesy. S couldn't help but reciprocate the courtesy.

"It's inappropriate for me to pretend to know things presumptuously, but I do have somewhat different views."

"I understand that."

The long, long new road (新長路) reached the East Gate (東門) before they knew it. The East Gate, mostly in ruins (廢墟), guarded the old fortress wall. Beneath the moon, listening to the sound of crooked pine trees swaying, it stood majestically, overseeing rows of thatched houses (草家).

"Oh my, we're already at the East Gate."

K exclaimed, startled, upon seeing the looming East Gate.

"You wish it were a bit further, right? K?"

Y's excited face was briefly visible in the moonlight.

"Well, home has gotten closer."

S thought of her lonely room.

Today too has passed. Life hides within time, moment by moment. Past memories hide their forms before new realities. Though 40 years of life were left flowing in time, S realized that the S of the past was vanishing like smoke from the S of the present.

The late spring evening air felt quite cool. Entering the East Gate, the Yŏnmudae (練武臺), visible high up, preserved the site of former archery practice. Only a few pillars, through which the white sky could be seen, shone in the moonlight. The fact that a motor road had been built beside it seemed indeed to await the footprints of lovers Y and K.

Turning around that road, what appeared were cherry blossoms, blooming profusely, white in the moonlight.
Through the blossoms, Panghwasuryujŏng (防花隨柳亭) and Hwahongmun (華虹門) were visible. There, only scraps of newspaper left behind from people's lunches blew in the wind; human presence was quiet. The three paused for a moment, then turned back.

It was 11 o'clock at night. When deep sleep likely fell upon them in their respective places, the souls of Y and K came and went.

Even if the flowers fall, a new spring will surely come again. Who can say that waiting is not a mystery (不可思議)? Waiting for that day. Waiting for that day.
-(The End)- <116>

**Summary**

The article, presented as a narrative dialogue, features S, an older, experienced woman (likely representing the author, Na Hye-sŏk), advising her younger sister K and K's fiancé Y. K asks S to ghostwrite a love letter, leading to conversations about youth vs. middle-aged love, marriage, and societal expectations. During an outing to Pongnyŏngsa Temple, the conversation broadens to cultural comparisons (Pompeii, Rome, Paris) and frank discussions on chastity, sexual desire, and the practicality of marriage versus single life. S controversially suggests that both men and women might use public prostitution (including advocating for male prostitutes) to manage sexual needs while single, delaying marriage until financially stable and mature. She critiques the idealization of love marriage, noting potential disillusionment, and discusses gender dynamics, suggesting men's intellectual maturity lags behind women's biological maturity, and advocates for a pragmatic approach to marriage, even suggesting a husband's 'superiority' might reduce conflict in Korean homes, contrasting 'New' and 'Old' family types.

For historians, the article is significant for its candid discussion of sexuality, gender roles, and marriage in 1935 Korea, penned by a prominent female intellectual. Na Hye-sŏk's radical views on single life, the necessity of outlets for sexual desire (including regulated prostitution for both sexes), and her critique of traditional and modern marriage ideals offer a rare glimpse into progressive, albeit controversial, feminist thought during the colonial period. The casual integration of Japanese phrases (Yoroshiku tanomu yo, Naruhodo, Sassoku, Arigatō) and the mention of Osaka also reflect the cultural milieu of the time.
