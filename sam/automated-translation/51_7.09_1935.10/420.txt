Author: Attendees: 李東民 (Yi Tong-min), 金海龍 (Kim Hae-ryong), 崔一鮮 (Ch'oe Il-sŏn), 韓鳴 (Han Myŏng); Moderators: A Reporter, B Reporter

莫斯科의 女黨員과 新興空氣, 今昔의 모스크바를 이약이하는 會
Attendees

李東民… One who lived in the Soviet Union spanning the New Economic Policy era and the 1st and 2nd Five-Year Plan eras
金海龍… The foremost expert in the arts field, currently living university life in Moscow
崔一鮮… Lived in Moscow from the time of the Russo-Japanese War until the end of the Tsarist era, knowledgeable about old Moscow
韓鳴… One who frequently entered Russia from the late Tsarist era until recently, knowledgeable about both old and new Moscow
Moderators… A Reporter, B Reporter<202>

C. Parks and Restaurants
B - Next, regarding parks, besides "Sparrow Hills," what others were there? Was Petrovsky Park on Leningradskoye Shosse there from before?
崔 - That wasn't a park before.
李 -- There's also a botanical garden in Moscow, but it seems not many people go in.
韓 - When we went, the best places were "Sparrow Hills," and then for restaurants, what is now the "Peasants' House," but there was a place called "Yar." It was truly nice.
崔 - That's right.
李 - Han, you managed to visit even places like that already.
韓 - Rather than just visiting, I was invited. (Haha)... The waiters and boys there wore white, white rubashkas like khalat-style robes. And the Hermitage Restaurant was also quite popular in the old days. The people working there, summer or winter, wore padded overcoats, put on shiny silk hats, and dressed in truly strange styles...
李 - Where was the place where Alexander carried out the emancipation of the serfs?
韓 - Ostankino on the outskirts of Moscow still remains; the park was expanded and became quite large. A famous place in the old days was "Yar." It seems it was famous as a racetrack from long ago.
李 - One drinks at "Yar" around three, and around four, goes to Sterina.
崔 - I don't know how it is now, but in the old days, they said a boy's tip (찝푸 - chip?) for a year was five thousand rubles.

7. The Beautifying Streets of Moscow
A. Asphalt
B - What is the state of Moscow's city streets? Especially recently, it seems the stone paving (鋪石道) has changed to asphalt. What was it like in the old days?
崔 - In the old days, Moscow's roads were paved with stones, and the sidewalks were extremely narrow, so when a beautiful woman walked from the other side, the path was narrow, and when a carriage happened to pass, it often happened that you'd bump into the woman's backside (궁뎅이). (Haha)
李 - Was Leningrad also paved with stones?
崔 - Leningrad wasn't like that. It was paved with wooden blocks (木煉瓦).
B -- They say the asphalt on Soviet Square was laid at an extraordinary tempo, in just forty hours, and Tverskaya<203> was done even faster, so Moscow's asphalting seems truly advanced.
李 - Asphalting Moscow's roads was impossible in the old days. Due to horses traveling, it must have been because horses found it very slippery. But recently, all of Moscow's streets have been asphalted. This seems to be because, as a result of industrialization, they abandoned 'carriages' (마차) and started using automobiles, leading to the sudden change to asphalt. Recently, one sometimes sees 'carriages' going over the asphalt, but it looks very difficult. Especially around winter when it freezes, the horses couldn't seem to run.
B - In the old days, you must have used 'carriages' for inner-city transport too.
崔 - Of course, it was carriages. Especially since the aristocrats all traveled by carriage.

B. Marble Subway
A - When did the trams appear?
崔 - That would be around Meiji 40 (1907) or 38, 39 (1905, 1906).
韓 - Anyway, although the interval wasn't long, it changed immensely. It's astonishing development. Especially when you look at things like the subway, you can see how much Moscow's transportation has developed.
B - Kim, have you ridden the subway?
金 - Yes, I rode it once not long ago. It was truly splendid, looked even more splendid than American subways. The entrances and such are decorated with marble. Though marble is abundant.
李 - Russians, even when boarding a tram, always line up (렬(列)를 지어서).
韓 - That's right. But it's because the population is smaller than Tokyo's. In Tokyo, where could you possibly line up and wait?
金 - But relatively few people ride the bus.
李 - In Russia, even when buying a newspaper, if you disrupt the line, it's considered disturbing order and becomes a big issue...
韓 - I was very impressed seeing this. In Paris, when boarding a bus, they call out numbers while punching tickets at the boarding place. When the capacity is reached, it departs. But when you come to Moscow, you just line up (렬을 지어서) and board in order, so there's no need to hand out tickets. I always think that point is indeed the best aspect regarding traffic management.
B - Kim, have you ridden the trolleybus (도로레이푸스)?
金 - You mean the trackless bus (무궤도뻐-쓰). Yes, I've ridden it. It's really nice. It has something like a tram pole (뽀-루) on top... <204>
韓 - The development of transportation isn't just in Moscow; I think it's probably global.

8. Moscow Women Past and Present, Walking the Cutting Edge
A. Formerly "Angels of the Street" B - Regarding the customs and fashions of women appearing on the streets of Tsarist Moscow, we ask Ch'oe, who knows the Tsarist era well. Recently, Soviet people are increasingly following fashion trends. How much fashion did Moscow women follow in the past?
崔 - Is that women's customs or men's customs?
B - Of course, women's customs.
崔 - Originally in Russia, though I don't know well now, as you all well know, those who followed fashion (모-단) were extremely few (小數). Therefore, it's probably better to think that Russians generally couldn't follow fashion. However, the bourgeoisie (뿌루죠아) were quite luxurious and extravagant, and the tendency to follow fashion was strongest among them. Of course, the global fashion center at the time was always Paris, France (佛), but Russia was where its cutting edge spread first to various countries. Because of that, there were women walking around in quite splendid clothes. And as you all know, Russian women are world-renowned for having many beauties, so their appearance was like that of angels. At that time, perhaps because I was young, there were frequent times when I lost my mind and was intoxicated by that beauty (美). (Hahaha…) That's enough from me, I'll pass it on to Han next.
韓 - Adding to that, it's said that the wealthy people of Moscow back then sent all their laundry (洗濯物) to Paris, so you can well imagine how extravagant they were. At that time, compared to the women of Berlin, America, or Tokyo, Russian women walked the most cutting edge. It wouldn't be an exaggeration to say that their luxury and extravagance probably reached its peak.
B - Han, you must have been quite young then, so you must have observed the various clothing styles of women well. How about it?
韓 - I was a student then, so in that area, I'm a bit...
B - The time when Ch'oe was there was when fashion (모-단) was most prevalent, wasn't it?
崔 - You could say that. As I mentioned earlier, it was Parisian fashion... On the other hand, because there were many poor people, it was particularly noticeable. When I was there, light, delicate (하르르한?) habutae (?) clothes were in fashion, and those thin (열분) clothes clung tightly to plump (포동포동한) bodies, and the physical beauty and curves revealed within were <205> truly sensual and provocative (유감적이며 조발적)...
韓 - Please refrain a little. (Hahaha, everyone laughs loudly)
A - What about the change in fashion (모-단) between just before the Russo-Japanese War and just before XX?
韓 - Before XX was likely even more extravagant (제이닥구).

B. The Beauty of Soviet Women
B - To Kim, who returned most recently, please tell us about the customs of Moscow women these days.
金 - These days, Soviet women generally appear simple (질소) and frugal (검박). If someone who had only traveled to places like Paris, London, New York, or Tokyo visited Moscow for the first time, they would be surprised and exclaim, "Indeed, Russian women live such simple lives!" and be impressed. However, as general culture gradually improves, it seems they are adding some decoration and luxury to their clothing. But it's still incomparable to other countries in general.
A - But what about the women in hotels or social venues, and the "women of the night"? Are they also like that?
金 - The women in hotels or social venues are somewhat stylish (하이카라). When it's time for dancing with the band at the Metropol, extremely stylish women gather in Moscow, but compared to the people who gather at Tokyo's "Imperial Hotel" or those appearing in American high society, it's not even comparable.
B - Next, what about opera actresses? Their costumes and such...
金 - Those people are particularly plain/simple (앗싸리하드군요). They truly look like entertainers (藝人).
李 - I also spent some time in America, but if you compare the most splendid women's clothing these days with the Russian women's clothing before XX, I definitely think the Russian side was more extravagant (제이닥구).
韓 - At that time, it wasn't just clothes, but ornaments, diamonds, rubies, and such; the first-class opera seats were amazing.
崔 - The change in the audience at Moscow's Bolshoi Theatre is strange. Compared to the past when they wore colorful attire, now they only wear black clothes; and where the smell of perfume (행수 행내) used to waft strongly (물컥물컥), now the smell of sweat (땀내) is stuffy (퀴퀴하지요). Not only what meets the eye, but even the smell has changed. This is a story from twelve years ago, though—

Female Party Members and Fashion
B - Yi, especially female party members (녀당원), Komsomol members, etc., there used to be <206> various restrictions regarding clothing, but recently that tendency seems to be weakening. What is the reason (연고) for that?
李 - That pertains to the life of Komsomol youth party members, who are central to all Soviet citizen life. Regarding clothing, even five or six years ago when I was there, it was generally utilitarian. Therefore, even female party members wore skirts made of navy blue (?) serge (감빗 사-지), made as short as possible, with cotton (木綿) on top, and flannel in winter, dressing as frugally and simply as possible, and they didn't wear elaborately decorated hats. Komsomol members working centered around factories tie their hair with red scarves (벍언 푸라독) and go out into the streets. For shoes, unlike the old days, they don't wear high heels (굽이 놉흔) but wear low-heeled (굽이 나즌) ones like men. There are no ornaments whatsoever. No necklaces (목수술?), no wrist adornments (손목치장?), and rings are unnecessary for them; they only wear watches (시게) to tell the time. That's how it was, but recently, due to the so-called development of the Soviet economy, or the improvement of general culture and arts, skirts seem to be getting a bit longer, and there seems to be some difference from before. However, you can't find women wearing makeup anywhere (어더). Only, among female X members, there's certainly an improvement in terms of wearing new things or wearing dirty things.

9. Drastic Change in Fashion Following Cultural Level
韓 - Even in Showa 3 (1928) when I was there – this was in winter – the people walking on the street looked like some kind of black bundles; everyone wore black clothes. But as one or two years passed, the streets gradually felt brighter. Three years ago, those black moving bundles (뭉뎅이) were completely gone from the streets. Gradually, clothes of different colors and styles increased. Hats and various outfits all started changing. For that reason, current Moscow shop windows (쇼-윈도-) are decorated with mannequins, displaying splendid modern beauty (모-단미). A magazine called "Modan" (모-단) also came out two or three years ago.
B - That magazine probably sells well.
韓 - Next, in the field of tailoring too, it became unacceptable to just make things without decoration as before. Also, regarding housing construction, previously they focused only on utility value without any decoration, but recently, they put carvings in architecture, and many splendid and gorgeous buildings are being built. The current subway stations have artistic architecture unseen even in Paris. I think it's an expression of the idea that efforts must be made in various fields for the improvement of the general public's cultural life. <207> Just a few years ago, there was a time when Komsomol members were told not to follow fashion. That was a time when formality was generally required. Now, it has reached the point where as long as the spirit is firm (싯카리하면), clothing doesn't matter. The most striking example is that around Showa 3 (1928) when I was there, even when an ambassador presented credentials, he attended the session of the Central Executive Committee of the Soviet Union wearing a rubashka (루바슈카). Recently, however, they properly wear suits (背廣服 - sebiro/Western suit) even when presenting credentials, and they attend other general meetings wearing splendid suits.
金 - For suits, they wear trousers (쯔봉) with black stripes (縞 - shima?).
李 - So it seems government officials are generally setting an example. To be faithful to one's duties and raise production efficiency, one must first keep one's body clean. Last year or so, Ordzhonikidze (올죠니깃세) issued such an order. It said that people coming to the factory without shaving their beards would be sent back to shave before coming. In reality, in the Soviet Union, thinking it was good to have a dirty style (風) was a transitional phenomenon, but that's already a relic of the past era. Now is the era where even women are fine wearing neat, womanly clothes. Workers should keep their bodies clean and go to work. Consequently, in the Soviet commercial sector too, quite splendid shop windows (쇼-윈도-) have appeared, various goods are sold freely, and women's clothing has become relatively liberal, so it seems many women go to see plays or operas in splendid attire. All of this can be called an improvement in cultural life. Next, speaking a bit about men, when I went to Russia, wherever I walked in any town in Siberia, people wearing Western suits with collars and ties were extremely few. Recently, conversely, people wearing rubashkas are extremely few, and many people wear suits with splendid collars and ties. This too can be seen as one aspect of the improvement in cultural life.
B - Are Homburg-style hats (한찡그) still common for hats?
韓 - For hats, fedoras (중절모자) are common.
崔 - What about women's stockings?
韓 - Rayon or cotton. Silk stockings are only worn by foreigners.
崔 - They say stockings are the most expensive. They take up 70% of a woman's clothing budget?
金 - Even in movies (활동사진), there are scenes spending a long time shaving beards; this too is presumably (컨댄) aimed at improving the body and clothing.
韓 - Ordzhonikidze's order was likely issued based on the idea that to raise factory performance, individuals must somehow cultivate habits of regular, clean living. <208>
李 - I don't know about that well, but anyway, from the "Health Committee" (건강임원회?) or other such areas, to raise production efficiency, workers' health must be maintained. For that purpose, they prevent infectious diseases, create something called Sanitarnyi Prikaz (사니타-루누이, 뿌리칵-드 - Sanitary Order?), inspect the sanitary conditions of workers, and even in factories, once or twice a week, and on trains, everywhere, they check the clothes of workers and Kolkhoz members (콜호-즈원), checking for lice (이(虱)), or whether they haven't shaved their beards. In short (요컨댄), this cannot but be called an activity towards new cultural construction and creation.
A - Tonight, thank you very much for sharing many interesting stories. We will conclude this evening's meeting here. <209>

**Summary**

This 1935 roundtable discussion features four Koreans with experience in Moscow, comparing the Tsarist era with the contemporary Soviet period under the Five-Year Plans. They discuss changes in Moscow's infrastructure (parks, paving changing to asphalt, the new subway), transportation methods (decline of carriages, rise of cars, trams, trolleybuses, orderly queues), and particularly the evolution of women's fashion and public appearance. While Tsarist bourgeois women followed Parisian trends lavishly, early Soviet women adopted very plain, utilitarian dress, but by 1935, there's a noticeable shift towards more stylish, decorated clothing and improved personal hygiene, reflecting rising living standards and a changing cultural emphasis promoted even by officials like Ordzhonikidze. The conversation highlights the rapid modernization and social shifts occurring in the USSR. From a Korean colonial history perspective, the article is interesting as it shows educated Koreans engaging with international affairs and observing the Soviet experiment firsthand, offering nuanced perspectives on its social and cultural changes compared to other world capitals like Tokyo or Paris. It provides a glimpse into how Koreans under Japanese rule perceived the outside world, particularly the ideological alternative presented by the Soviet Union, focusing here on tangible aspects of daily life and cultural expression rather than solely politics.
