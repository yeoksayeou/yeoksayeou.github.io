Publication Date: 1935년 10월 01일
Article Title: 靑山白雲帖(二) (Blue Mountains, White Clouds Folio (2))
Author: 月灘
Article Type: 기행문 (Travelogue)

Blue Mountains, White Clouds Folio
						Wŏlt'an

When I opened my eyes, the train was running against the Pogae Mountain Range (寶蓋山脈). Pogae Mountain is a large mountain in this vicinity; while the green pines and white streams in summer and the burning-like crimson maples in autumn are fine, when the white snow flutters and piles high on the mountain brow, and the north wind coldly pierces the ears, chasing wild boars and deer through deep valleys and rugged passes with a band of club-wielding beaters (모리꾼떼), drinking their fresh blood and chewing their meat is no small vigorous deed.

Passing Taegwang-ri Station (大光里驛), from here it is Kangwŏn Province (江原道). The train climbs through the low-lying terrain called the Ch'ugaryŏng (楸哥嶺) Rift Valley. The further it goes, the deeper into the mountains it becomes, where one cannot see the sky. It is extremely hot. Only the bellflower blossoms, smiling softly with noble purple hues and swaying gently on the high banks the train cuts through, comfort the languid traveler's sorrow (旅愁).

Everywhere, fragments of basalt overflow and pile up, making it clear this vicinity is a lava plateau, and it seems likely there might be a volcanic crater on some mountaintop. Since the soil decomposed from basalt is said to be best for soybeans, it is understandable why Ch'ŏrwŏn (鐵原) and Pokkye (福溪) are major soybean production areas, and Yŏnch'ŏn (漣川) is famous throughout all Korea (朝鮮) as a soybean collection and distribution center.

Passing Ch'ŏrwŏn and reaching Wŏljŏng-ri (月井里), Wŏljŏng-ri was within the castle walls during the T'aebong (泰封) period. About 3 kilometers west from the station, there is a palace site called Kogwŏl-dong (古闕洞). There stands a stone lantern boasting its ancient face amid thousand-year green moss <255>. About 1 kilometer east from the station, it is said that the turtle pedestal (龜跌) from that time still remains intact.

Leaving the stuffy rift valley and reaching P'yŏnggang (平康), it is a highland 500 meters above sea level. A vast, wide-open highland where the four mountains recede far into the distance. Truly, P'yŏnggang is a well-chosen name, though I don't know when it began to be called that. My previously heavy chest opens wide and feels refreshed.

To the east, perhaps the main mountain, a bright, graceful, beautiful, and spiritually endowed green peak seems ready to fly beyond the clouds. Where it spreads out, the fields are fertile, and large and small houses are densely scattered like teeth of a comb, forming a prosperous town. On the mountainside, the red building of a Catholic church, bearing a cross on its head, stands sacredly tall, competing in height with the green mountain. It is precisely a single, exquisite landscape painting, and one can implicitly gauge the local people's wisdom and wealth.

From here, the welcoming mountains and winding waters become brighter the more one sees them, more exquisite the more one encounters them. Is it because the world-famous Kŭmgang (金剛) Mountain is nearby? The spiritual energy (靈氣) of the mountains and rivers seems entirely gathered here.

The mountain terrain stretches boldly to the left, while on the right, it seems to hesitate and crouch as if staggering. As the front mountain approaches, the back mountain seems to respond. Looking up, the heroic spirit (英氣) is palpable, making the mind feel lofty and refreshed (飄爽); looking down, its imposing manner seems like ten thousand or a thousand figures.

It was already known that the mountains and waters of the Kŭn'gi (近畿) region and the Southern Provinces (南鮮) cannot match those of Hanyang (漢陽). Now, encountering the mountain forms and water brilliance of Kangwŏn and Hamgyŏng (咸鏡), it is a graceful beauty I had never seen before.

The steep, heroic figures of Samgak (三角) and Inwang (仁旺) mountains, and the vigorous taste of the Kwanak (冠岳) range are stone mountains, fire stars (火星). They are naturally in a different category. Except for the elegant and affectionate clear energy of Namsan (南山), which is a metal star (金星), it is difficult to mention any other place with such bright beauty and grand elegance.

Excluding these, all the large and small, named and unnamed mountains of the Southern Provinces and Kŭn'gi region have their topsoil stripped and lack undulation (屈曲). The stripped topsoil might be attributed to human ignorance and national decline, an artificial result, but the mountain forms lacking undulation are plain, dull (鈍濁), and lack charm. Consequently, they lack bright energy and elegant spirit. <256>

A mountain is precious when it has rolled and changed, shedding its old skin completely (剝換). Water is refined when it meanders and winds through nine bends (九曲). Becoming refined makes it bright and beautiful (明嵋); going and returning, flowing and turning back makes it sentimental (有情).

I had previously heard that Kangwŏn Province, with its vast mountains and rivers, produced dull (鈍濁) people born under the influence of its natural energies. I recall the "Song of the Eight Provinces' Mountains and Rivers" in the Tale of Ch'unhyang (春香傳) being written with such meaning. However, encountering these mountains and rivers this time, I realized that was a mistaken observation. In the old days, it was a mountainous region with poor transportation. No matter how much the spiritual energy of the mountains and rivers was concentrated, because access to Seoul, the center of culture, was not easy, the development of the people's wisdom lagged compared to other places. It was certainly not the fault of the mountains and rivers. The land was vast, and people were few. Just stepping outside the main gate meant mountains and fields. Since there were no plains, they didn't even think of planting grains. Not eating much rice meant side dishes weren't very necessary either. They planted potatoes and harvested beans, eating potato rice with mountain vegetables (山菜), so salt alone was sufficient. Occasionally, they caught deer and shot stags, so they didn't starve for meat either. They tilled at sunrise and drank from the well (*井而飮), so "What power has the emperor over me?" (帝力何有於我哉). This was their simple, generous, magnanimous elder-like lifestyle of the past. Of course, who could even dream of this now? Trains run, light rail cars roll, and automobiles race, so the bitter, sweet, and dizzying waves of the world must rather torment the descendants of these heavenly people (天民).

While eating lunch at Pokkye, the train heads towards the famous Kŏmbullang (劒拂浪). *Puff puff puff, phoo phoo phoo* The train begins to climb with all its might. But it was slower than a person's walk. Great nature and civilization, the tiny human power squirming before nature – it was like child's play. *Phoo phoo phoo* Only the sound of escaping steam burst out involuntarily. If this were an animal, its whole body would surely be drenched in sweat. Tumbling seven times, falling eight times (7顚8倒). Watching it crawl up, I occasionally felt the strange illusion, as if riding a sentient animal, feeling pity, and let out a quiet, bitter smile.

Kŏmbullang (劒拂浪) – striking the waves with a sword. It sounds like a name with some plausible connection to the old Sambang (三防) battlefield. <257>

As the train wouldn't move, "The warhorse won't advance, the men don't speak!" (征馬不前人不語)! Fantasy suddenly evoked this phrase. I see Sŏnjong (善宗 - Kung Ye's name when he was wandering in obscurity as a monk), chased into the Sambang Gorge (三防幽峽), his wrinkled forehead and hideous one eye glaring, facing an absurd impasse, roaring at the traitor Wang Kŏn (王建) loud enough to burst his throat, then being struck in his remaining eye by a flying poisoned arrow and falling wretchedly from his horse (蹉跎).

I see the scene of a hundred thousand troops, as if swept away by waves, screaming in agony (阿鼻呌喚), losing their way, creating a living hell in the Sambang Gorge. "Watershed Divide 603 Meters" (分水嶺603米突) – it's written like this on a white wooden post, the ink stains dripping. The train is now crossing the backbone of Korea.

Passing Sep'o Station (洗浦驛), this area is pastureland, suitable for raising sheep (緬羊) and horses. Wildflowers bloom profusely, lush green weeds abound, the air is clean, and the mountain stream water is clear. Amidst this, horses grow fat and sheep plump. The picturesque pastoral scene seems not of this dusty world (塵世). It is said that the horse ranch managed by the Office of the Yi Royal Household (李王職) and the pastures of Nangok Farm (蘭谷農場) are located here.

Again, the train winds through a mountain gorge. It is a peerless scenery (絶景) I had never seen before. Pass one mountain, and a stream flows; one stream meanders, and a tunnel appears. Just as the dark tunnel feels tedious, suddenly a bright blue mountain, like the skirt folds of a fairy maiden (仙女), wraps around. Just when you think the water has ended, beneath a bridge seemingly a thousand kil (길) high, a strong rapid surges. Rocks leap up, water breaks apart, spraying snow-like foam (as if white dragons are intertwined and fighting), sweeping down the broken banks, making a tremendous noise, forming a stream, and running away.

The children clap their hands, and I am intoxicated by the pure delight (淸興). Repeating endlessly like this, we reached the Sambang Gorge. The mountains wound twenty times, nineteen rapids roared, the darkness of tunnels occurred fourteen times – the world's wondrous sights (奇勝) gathered in one place. If one were to pass through here under the moonlight of the 15th night, one could witness the magnificent spectacle of the moon shattering and gold surging, adding one more marvel. <258>

From here is Hamgyŏng Province (咸鏡道), so it seems we traversed three provinces in half a day. After another couple of hours, passing eight stations, we arrived at Wŏnsan Station (元山驛) just past three in the afternoon. We immediately boarded the bus heading to our destination, Songdowŏn (松濤園).

Leaving the city area, after driving for a while, the bus stopped in a place where green pines grew thickly on white sandy ground. The vast, boundless sea, like spilled indigo dye, first stirs the traveler's heart. We rented a small room (貰房) and first put down our luggage (行李). Although there were a couple of inns, they were fully occupied by people who had come from various places for sea bathing.

About 50 steps from the house was the sea, so I took the children and went into the water.

I threw myself into the undulating waves of the vast blue sea (蒼波萬里). A wave came, brushed my chest, and ran away. Another wave came, embraced my waist, shimmered, and ran away. One wave passed, and another came; this wave bowed its head and retreated, then another wave raised its head and rushed forward. "One wave barely moves, ten thousand waves follow" (一波纔動 萬波隨) – I thought this was a well-crafted line, but facing these vast, surging sea waves, it felt like an utterly clumsy phrase.

The sea is like a mother's bosom. Infinite sea, infinite maternal love (慈愛)! Closing my eyes quietly, the faint feeling from childhood, as if nestled in my mother's embrace, peacefully suckling, floated into my mind.

This feeling of magnanimity (寬大), this taste of solace (憂撫) – how could one hope for it anywhere else but in a mother's embrace?

*Splash, splash* When did the East Sea (東海) get to know me? As if pleased to see me, it sends vigorous and generous waves to stroke and caress my entire body. It washes away the sweat and tickles my armpits. Like a mother's sacred hand comforting the day's fatigue (疲勞). A thousand people may come, it's fine; ten thousand sons may come, it's fine. The sea does not reject them. Sweat was gone, grime was shed. A cool sensation spread throughout my body.

The children, seemingly joyful yet fearful, hugged their floats (浮帶) and played with the waves.

The wide, wide beach was filled with naked people. Boys, young women (시악시), old men, young men, children, infants – it had completely <259> returned to a primitive, ancient era. While embraced in this sea, this generous bosom, everyone must be a sacred child of heaven (天民). Away from material desire (物慾), away from struggle, away from wickedness (無邪), merely infants embraced by the sea, the naked innocence (天眞) without a shred of falsehood or pretense (꿈임).

However, step out of the water, and a seductive (蠱惑的) beach scene unfolds. Blue, red, yellow, colorful – fin-de-siècle intense colors. Breasts and buttocks seemingly ready to burst, undulating curves flowing – a luxurious edition of decadent modern civilization that shakes the ailing senses (官能). Here, there is already jealousy (猜忌). There is lust (情慾). There is struggle. The flames of curses (咀呪) rise. Leaving the sea to eat dinner, I entered the only restaurant run by Koreans and ordered bean paste soup with rice (장국밥). A vulgar (猥藝) and base (低劣) atmosphere filled the entire restaurant. It was a place I could not bring the children to a second time. I quickly ate, barely touching the food, and returned to the rented house.

As the sun set, a cool sea breeze filled the pine forest (松林). It felt like the energy of autumn (金氣) was stirring, just like clear autumn (淸秋).

Seoul is now at the peak of the heat, about to pass the last of the dog days (末伏). It's impossible even to imagine this sensation. I wish I could transfer this cool taste (凉味) entirely, without loss, to my parents.

Night deepened. The tatami room was extremely cold. It wasn't summer; it felt like the 8th or 9th lunar month's temperature. With only one blanket to cover the two children, there was nothing left to wrap myself in. Enduring it wearing only thin cotton underwear (지지미 內衣) was difficult. I put on my Western suit and tried to sleep again, but the noise from people reveling outside in a playful mood (遊樂氣分) made it impossible to fall asleep. Base songs, the coarse laughter of drunkards, the shrill, lewd (潮淫蕩) voices of pleasure girls, the continuous, brazen sirens of cars – the tangled, vulgar commotion shattering the deep night's tranquility (靜寂) was brighter than day, ruthlessly trampling the sweet dreams the weary traveler sought. Wondering what time it was, I turned on the light and looked at the clock – how could I not be surprised? Three in the morning. There were many foreigners, yet their voices could not be heard even if one deliberately tried to listen. It was all Korean voices, Korean people. A fearful (悚懼) thought arose. A pitiful sigh escaped. Koreans who know no self-cultivation (修養). <260> Koreans who know no moderation (정도). They lack discipline (규율) and self-reflection (반성). They lack foresight (예산) and don't know the future (來頭). There is only laxity (解弛) and decadence (퇴폐). Since when did Koreans become like this? How did they become so decadent? Thinking about it, pathetic tears silently wet my pillow (潛然). We must only have self-control and self-conquest (극기극복). We must set goals ten, twenty, a hundred times higher than others and advance. Wake myself, compose myself, and fall asleep.

The next day, rose at 5 AM. Took the children to the sea.

Sunrise over the East Sea! The place where we now stand is Yŏnghŭng Bay (永興灣), the slender waist that makes the map of our three-thousand-ri (三千里) Korea most graceful and stylish. The opposite shore in view is Songjŏn (松田); slanting in from the south is Kalma Peninsula (葛麻半島). To the north, Pŏmsŏm island (범섬) stands firm, forming a star-like island (羅星) at the channel entrance (水口), while outside, small islands like Taedo (大島), Sindo (薪島), Yŏdo (麗島), and Kodo (高島) block the East Sea. There is no difference between high and low tide, so the sea is clear, deep, boundlessly clean, and a place where mighty warships (雄船巨艟) can hide – the best port on the East Sea, a naval fortress zone (海軍要塞地帶) where photos and drawings cannot be freely made.

Though one need not strive to borrow the words of the talented child Wang Po (王勃), the sky and water color have merged completely.

The eastern sky reddens. The sun rises. The crimson (씻벍것) morning sun bursts forth. The waves shimmer; crimson dye boils and surges across the vast blue expanse (蒼波萬頃). Whether magnificent (壯) or exhilarating (快), it's impossible to describe in words.

I want to quickly row a boat and float adrift in the middle of the East Sea. I stand absently (우두머니) on the beach.

After filling our bellies with soybean paste soup at the restaurant, we walked around Songha-ri (松下里), the Korean settlement on the second line, and went into the sea again.

During the day, we took a small steamship, played with the waves, and headed towards Wŏnsan Port (元山埠頭). The one-hour boat ride was incomparable to boating on Seoul's rivers (江河). Not only did the cool, vast-spirited air (浩然之氣) broaden the chest, but the sentiment of circling around the red lighthouse stirred up the sorrows of a traveler (마드로스).

After seeing the Customs House (稅關) where mooring boats (溪船) were lined up, we took a turn around the city streets. <261> Seeing only the sparse scattering of Korean shops (朝鮮商店), with a heavy heart, we returned to Songdowŏn by boat and entered the seawater bath (潮湯).

After finishing dinner at the Chot'ang restaurant, we took a walk around the wide pine forest with over 100,000 tall, luxuriant pines (長松). The clear, pure air (淸澄) felt like it would make people plump. Among the more than 40 or 50 rented villas (別莊 - rented out for 60-70 won during the summer season by the Songdowŏn Beach management), there were hardly any empty ones. Here and there, the serenade (小夜曲) from records drifted on the clear breeze (淸風) and scattered through the pine grove. Each family lived as if it were their home for the summer, bringing their families, exercising, and reading. What else could this be but earthly immortals (地上仙)? Truly a people who know self-cultivation well. But it is regrettable. Among them, there is not a single Korean. Go a little further, a few steps away, to the amusement area with cafes, bars, and pleasure houses, and it's noisy all night long – chattering, drunken behavior, commotion. What a fine contrast! What a living lesson! Could there be such a miserable irony again? Of course, they lack things too. Lack of mental余裕 (reserve), economic 余裕, cultural 余裕. But even so, can things continue like this, so lacking in principle (無節操), so low-grade (低級), so decadent? Shouldn't they be struggling to look towards the future (來頭)? Think of your children, your grandchildren, your great-grandchildren and great-great-grandchildren (曾孫 高孫). And compare yourselves to others.

In the western sky where Spica (角星) glitters, dark clouds begin to rise. The wind rises fiercely. While sitting on the porch (손밧) smoking a cigarette, the black clouds (黑雲) instantly spread north and south. The day turned completely dark. The sea also turned black (향하다 - likely a typo for '까맣다' or '험하다' - fierce/rough).

The sea grew angry. The pitch-black beach (海濱) was filled with the roaring sound of angry waves (波濤). *Crash, crash* Pitch-black, mountain-like waves roared magnificently, striking the pier (埠頭), spraying white, snow-like foam (飛沫) into the air, and collapsing. Moment by moment (刻一刻), the sound of the waves rose and fell. The fiercely leaping dark blue waves seemed intent on burying (뭇질을) the land itself. Fish and dragons (魚龍) were startled, and the lighthouse seemed to flicker and tremble. The strong sea wind whipped the hem of my clothes (옷작락) powerfully, filling my whole body with a cold chill and an apprehensive heart (송구한 마음). The whistle of a steamship coming in through the rough waves somewhere made the sea's night even more tragic (처절케). <262>

The sky and sea merged into darkness itself, and large, coarse raindrops fell heavily (우둥우둥). The entire universe seemed about to commit some great deed.

The sea, which had been so benevolent (仁慈) and gentle (상양하든 - likely 상냥하든), instantly brought about changes in wind and clouds (風雲造化), shaking the heavens and seemingly making the earth slide, raging as if mad. I couldn't help but marvel (嗟嘆) anew at the great changes of vast nature, and feel ashamed of tiny human power compared to this awesome force of nature.

Returning to the rented house, I told the children to pack the luggage (行李), guessing that the harsh weather would prevent sea bathing tomorrow. My intention was to leave Songdowŏn the next day.

Half-sleeping, I woke at three in the morning, opened the door, and looked up at the sky. Not a single star could be seen in the pitch-black heavens. At four, I woke the children, had them wash their faces, and while we were getting ready (어정버정하는 동안), the car I had arranged yesterday arrived at the door and sounded its siren.

We drove along the seashore towards the station.

Breathing in the wind carrying the full, refreshing morning energy (精氣) through the car window, gazing at the undulating, wrinkled beauty (芙姿) of Unbongsan Mountain (雲峰山), events from 500 years ago seemed visible before my eyes, and again the nostalgic feeling (感懷) of recalling the past arose gently. I see the image of Yi T'aejo (李太祖), who had lain dormant like a dragon (龍潛), cherishing the dream of carrying rafters (負椽夢) in his heart, trekking through deep mountains and remote valleys, winding through valleys and passes, trudging along (타박타박) in search of Muhak (無學). I see the scene in a dilapidated straw-thatched hut (草幕) barely sheltering from wind and rain, where the strangely clad monk (異僧) in rags (襤褸) humbly says, "What could this poor monk possibly know?" (貧道- 무었을 알리잇가). Looking up at heaven and down at earth (俯仰乾坤), what is it all but scattered fragments of a dream?

From Sŏgwangsa Station (釋王寺驛), we took a car again to Sagi-ri (砂器里) and left our luggage at an inn (旅舍). It was just eight o'clock, and they said breakfast was still a long way off.

Taking the children, I went straight up the mountain. While telling them the story of "Flowers fall, but surely bear fruit" (花飛終有實) and "A mirror drops, but how can there be no sound?" (鏡落豈無聲), I looked up at the pine tree said to have been hand-planted by T'aejo, which reportedly withered and died (故死) 70 years ago. Was it coincidence or intentional (有意)? It felt quite strange (괴이한).

Entering Tansongmun Gate (斷俗門), the pine forest was dense and dark green (蒼*하도록 - likely 蒼蒼하도록), and the stream roared down for 10 ri (里), washing away all the dust <263> from my heart, scattering all worries (煩惱).

Entering Pulimun Gate (不貳門), the valley became even deeper and more secluded (深邃), and the water, still clear, flowed abundantly (질펀하다). We climbed Sant'allu Pavilion (山嘆樓) to dry our sweat, touched the bell at Pŏmjongnu Pavilion (泛鍾樓), and looked up at the Taeungbojŏn Hall (大雄寶殿). Its scale (規模) was incomparable to any temple in Kyŏngsan (京山 - likely Kyŏngsŏng/Seoul). The calligraphy of the couplet (珠聯) attached to the East Wing (東廡)! I grabbed an old monk and asked whose writing it was, for its pure beauty (淸秀) and wondrous strength (奇逕). He said it was the writing of Ŭnyŏng (恩穎) Kim Pyŏnggi (金炳冀). I had certainly heard the name of this powerful family (權門勢家) from the Andong Kim clan (安東金氏), related by marriage to King Ch'ŏlchong (哲宗) and who dominated the era, but I never knew he possessed such hidden talent as a master calligrapher (達筆).

I caressed the brushstrokes (字劃) for a long while.

On the way down, we stopped at the medicinal spring (藥泉) and drank the water. It was clear and cool (淸冽), yet fishy (비릿하고) and slightly salty (찝찔하야), its taste difficult to describe among the five flavors (五味). It is said to be good for digestive ailments (消化器病) and effective for women's diseases (婦人病). I forced the reluctant children to drink two gourd-dippers each and returned to the inn. In the afternoon, a heavy shower (소낙비) poured down, making the stream in front quite swollen (살저), and it trickled down noisily all night. I met two or three friends who had come down from Seoul and talked late into the night before sleeping.

The next day, in the afternoon, holding a bundle of bracken (고비나물) obtained from the temple, I came out to the station. We would arrive in Kyŏngsŏng (京城 - Seoul) by ten that night.

This marked the end of this journey.
(Ulhae Year (乙亥 - 1935), August, End of Dog Days (末伏)) <264>

**Summary**

The author, Wŏlt'an, describes a train journey starting from near the Pogae mountains, traversing the Ch'ugaryŏng Rift Valley through Kangwŏn Province into Hamgyŏng Province. He details the changing landscapes, from basalt plateaus good for soybeans near Ch'ŏrwŏn to the highlands of P'yŏnggang and the spectacular scenery around the Sambang Gorge near Kŭmgang Mountain. Reaching Wŏnsan, he stays at the Songdowŏn beach resort, enjoying the sea but expressing deep disappointment and criticism towards the behavior of fellow Koreans there – their noise, lack of discipline, and perceived decadence, especially contrasting it with the quiet, orderly vacationing style of others (presumably Japanese) in nearby villas. The trip concludes with a visit to Sŏgwangsa temple, reflecting on history and nature, before returning to Seoul. From a colonial history perspective, the article is interesting for its vivid descriptions of Korea's natural beauty alongside reflections on modernization (train travel), local economies (soybeans, ranching), and particularly the author's sharp critique of Korean social behavior, lamenting a perceived lack of 'self-cultivation' and 'discipline' compared to others, reflecting anxieties and self-reflection common among intellectuals during the colonial period.

靑山白雲帖
						月灘
눈을 떠보니 車는 寶蓋山脈을 거슬려 끼고 다름질친다. 寶蓋山은 이 근처의 큰 산으로 여룸에 靑松白溪와 가을에 불타는 듯한 丹楓도 좋커니와 白雪이 흣날려 산이마에 그득히 싸이고 北風이 싸늘하게 귀를 어일 때 몽둥이든 모리꾼떼와 함께 깊은 골 험한재로 山도야지와 노루를 몰고 쪼치며 生血을 마시고 고기를 씹는 맛이 여간 씩씩한 짓이 않이다.
大光里驛을 지나니 일로부터 江原道地方, 기차는 楸哥嶺의 地溝帶라는 低陷한 地帶를 뚤코 올나간다. 갈수록 갈수록 하눌을 볼 수 없는 산골이다. 몹시도 무더웁다. 다만 기차가 뚤코나가는 높다란 두 언덕엔 高尙한 보라빗갈 웃음을 방실방실 웃스며 하느적어리는 도라지꽃들이 나렷한 旅愁를 위로해 준다.
곧곧이 玄武岩 부스럭이가 넘처쌓엿스니 이 근처가 熔岩臺地됨이 분명하고 엇던 산 꼭댁이에는 噴火口도 잇슬법하다. 玄武岩이 분해된 흙은 콩이 가장 잘된다하니 鐵原 福溪가 大豆의 大産地요 漣川은 大豆의 集散地로 온 朝鮮에 일홈이 높으니 써 그 까닭을 알 것이다.
鐵原을 지나 月井里에 다다르니 月井里는 泰封 당시에 城안이엿고 驛에서 西편으로 約3키로 米突을 거러가면 古闕洞이란 대궐터가 잇다. 千年蒼苔에 옛얼골을＜255＞ 자랑하는 石燈籠이 있으며 驛에서 東편 1키로米突가량을 가면 당시에 龜跌이 依然이 남어잇다한다.
답답한 地溝帶를 버서나 平康에 다으니 海跋 500米突의 高原이다. 一望蕩蕩 4山이 멀즉이 물너슨 平濶한 高原, 실로 平康이란 일홈을 어느때부터 불너왓는지 잘 지은 일홈이다. 훗훗하든 가슴이 활적 열리며 시원하다.
東으로 主山인가 明媚하고 아담하고 秀靈한 一座靑山이 구름밖으로 날을 듯 벌여진 곳에 전원이 기름지고 크고 적은 집이 櫛批點綴하야 탐탁한 도시를 일우었다. 山腹에 十字針을 머리에 인 카토릭敎堂의 붉은 집이 聖스런웁게 兀然이, 높이를 靑山과 다토니 바로 곳 一幅絶妙한 風景畵여니와 은연중 이 지방의 民智와 富力을 짐작할 수 잇다.
이로부터 마져주는 山 감도는 물이 볼사록 명랑하고 대할사록 玲瓏하다. 천하의 名山 金剛이 갓가운 탓이냐, 山河의 靈氣- 함빡 이곧에 모힌 것 갓다.
山勢- 왼편으를 豪氣롭게 뻐첫는가 하면 바른편으로 충충거러나니는 듯 주츰하고 업드렷다. 압山이 붙으는가하면 뒷산은 應하는 듯 하다. 처다보니 英氣어리워 정신이 飄爽한 듯하고 구버보니 우줄우줄 그 기상이 萬인가 千인 듯 싶다.
近畿와 南鮮의 뫼와 물이 漢陽의 그것을 딸을 수 없음은 일즉이 안바여니와 이제 江原과 咸鏡의 山容水光을 接하니 일즉이 보지못하든 수려한 맵시다.
三角 仁旺의 峻峭한 英姿와 冠岳連峯의 遒勁한 맛은 石山이오 火星이라. 스스로 그 部類-다르거니와 金星인 南山의 아담하고도 다정한 맑은 기운을 除한 외에 어듸 이러타할 明嵋하고 큰 맵시를 들기가 어렵다.
이들을 除한 南鮮의 近畿와 無名 有名의 크고 적은 모든 산은 토피가 벗기고 屈曲이 적다. 토피는 사람의 무식으로 국가의 衰頹로 일어난 人爲라 하려니와 屈曲이 적은 山容은 평범하고 鈍濁하고 멋이 없다. 따런서 밝은 기운 秀麗한 기상이 적다.＜256＞
산은 굴르고 박귀여 함빡 때를 버서 剝換된 것이 귀한 것이오. 물은 구비치고 감돌아 九曲된 것이 格이다. 剝換이 되니 明嵋하고, 갓다가 되오고 흘으다 다시 도니 有情한 것이다.
일즉이 江原道는 山川이 무무하야 그 山河의 情氣를 밧고 태여난 사람들은 鈍濁하냥으로 들엇다. 春香傳 비두 八道山川타령에도 이런한 의미로 씨워진 듯 기억이 난다. 그러나 나는 이번에 이 山河를 대하고 그 그른 관찰임을 알었다. 옛날엔 교통이 불편하든 山峽地帶라 아모리 山河의 靈氣 鍾集하드라도 문화의 중심지인 서울과 출입이 갓지 못하얏슴에 民智의 발달이 다른 곧에 비하야 떠러젓든 것이요. 결코 山川의 죄는 않인 것이다. 땅은 넓고 사람은 희소하니 大門만 나서면 산이요 밧이다. 平野가 없으니 禾穀을 심을 생의도 않한다. 쌀밥을 않이 먹으니 반찬도 그리 필요치 않다. 감자를 심고 콩을 거두어 감자밥에 山菜를 씹으니 소곰 한가지면 고만이다. 가끔가다 노루를 잡고 사슴을 쏘니 고기에도 그리 주리지 않는다. 日出而耕하고 *井而飮하니 帝力이 何有於我哉다. 이것이 옛날 그들의 淳厚 寬大한 長者風의 생활이다. 물논 지금에야 어듸 이것을 꿈에나 생각하랴. 기차가 달니고 輕便車가 굴느고 自働車가 쪼치니 쓰고 신 어지러운 세상물결이 도리혀 이 天民의 자손들을 괴롭힐 것이다.
福溪서 점심을 먹는 동안 기차는 저 유명한 劒拂浪을 향하야 간다. 푹 푹 푹, 푸 푸 푸 車는 죽을 힘을 다하야 올나가기 시작한다. 그러나 그것은 사람의 거름만도 못한 것이엿다. 大自然과 文明, 自然앞에 蠢動하고 잇는 조고마한 사람의 힘, 그것은 마치 어린애의 작난과 갓다. 푸 푸 푸 헷김빠진 소리만 저절로 터저나온다. 만일 이것이 동물이라면 전신엔 함빡 땀으로 물초를 하얏슬 것이다. 7顚8倒. 그 기어올나가는꼴이 마음에 마치 지각을 가진 동물을 타고 가는 양 안타까운 錯覺을 갓금갓금 늣기며 홀로 가만한 苦笑를 날려버렷다.
劒拂浪, 칼을 써서 물결에 후리친다. 三防古戰場과＜257＞ 그럴 듯 무슨 인연이 잇는 것 가튼 일홈이다.
車가 가지 않이하니 『征馬不前人不語』! 幻想은 별안간 이 글귀를 불러 일으켯다. 三防幽峽으로 쪼긴 善宗(弓裔가 草土에 무처 僧으로 잇슬 때 일홈)이 주름잡힌 이맡살과 醜해진 애꾸눈을 부릅뜨며 어이없는 기막힘을 直面하야 厲聲一喝 叛臣王建을 목통이 터져라 하고 호령하다가 날으는 毒矢에 외눈을 마져맛고 馬上에서 떠러저 蹉跎하는 꼴이 보인다.
十萬大兵이 물결에 휩싸인 듯 阿鼻呌喚 갈길을 일코 三防幽峽에 生地獄을 벌닌 모양이 눈앞에 뵈인다. 「分水嶺603米突」허연 나무에 墨痕이 지르르 흐르게 이러케 씨워잇다. 기차는 지금 朝鮮의 背梁을 넘고 잇는 것이다.
洗浦驛을 지나니 이곧은 牧場地帶, 緬羊을 길으고 말을 치기에 적합한 곧이다. 어즈러히 피인 野花 싱싱하게 푸른 잡초, 공기는 깨끗하고 산골물 맑다. 이 가운데 말은 살지고 羊은 기름지다. 그림가튼 放牧의 情景이 또한 塵世의 것이 않인 것 갓다. 李王職의 말을 치는 牧場과 蘭谷農場의 放牧들이 잇다는 데다.
다시 車는 山峽을 끼고 돈다. 일즉이 보지 못하든 천하의 絶景이다. 한 산을 지나면 한물이 흐르고 한물이 구비치면 한窟이 나온다. 캄캄한 窟속이 지리한가하면 어느듯 명랑한 푸른 산이 仙女의 치마폭인 듯 주름잡아 감돌아 돌고 물이 인제 다햇는가 생각하면 千길이나 되는 다리아래엔 살진 여울이 용소슴치니 돌은 뛰여솟고 물은 부서져 눈(雪)을 뿜는 양 (白龍이 어우러져 싸오는 듯 끌어진 언덕을 휩쓸어 어마어마한 큰 소리를 지르고 내를 일우어 달아난다.

그러나 한거름 물밖을 나오면 蠱惑的인 海濱의 풍경이 펼처져 잇다. 파러코 빨거코 노라코 달룩한 세기말적인 강렬한 채색 터질듯한 乳房과 볼기, 굼틀거려 흐르는 곡선, 병든 官能을 흔드는 頹廢的인 근대문명의 豪華版이다. 이 곧에 벌서 猜忌가 잇다. 情慾이 잇다. 투쟁이 잇다. 咀呪의 불길이 오른다. 바다에서 나와 저녁을 먹으량으로 朝鮮사람의 唯一無二의 經營인 식당으로 드러가 장국밥을 식혓다. 猥藝 低劣한 공기가 왼식당안에 가득찻다. 아희들을 다리고 두 번 다시 오지 못할 곧이다. 얼른 먹는둥 마는 둥하고 貰집으로 도라왓다.
해가 떠러지니 서늘한 바다바람이 松林에 가득히 찻다. 金氣가 움직이는 듯 마치 淸秋와 갓다.

밤이 깊엇다. 다다미방이 몹시도 차다. 여름이 않이라 바로 곳 陰曆으로 8,9월 기온이다. 담뇨 한아도  두 아희를 깔고 덥게하고나니 포개는 다시 없엇다. 지지미 內衣만으로 누어서 버틔기는 난감하다. 양복을 떼여입고 다시 잠을 청하니 길에서 遊樂氣分에 띄워 떠드는 소리에 다시 잠을 일을 수가 없다. 低卑한 노래, 술주정꾼의 너털우슴 노는 게짓의 潮淫蕩한 쇠된 목소리 계속하는 자동차의 芳姿한 싸이렌이 深夜의 靜寂을 깨트리는 얼크러진 잡된 騷동이 낮보다도 明亮하야 고단한 길손의 들려는 단꿈을 여지없이 짓밟아 놋는다. 멧시나 되엿는가하고 불을 켜 시게를 보니 엇지 않이 놀라리. 새벽 세시, 외국사람이 허다하건만 그네들의 소리는 일부러 귀를 기우려 들을래야 들을수 없다. 모도다 朝鮮말소리요 朝鮮사람이다. 悚懼한 생각이 돈다. 민망한 탄식이 나온다. 修養을＜260＞ 몰으는 朝鮮사람이다. 정도를 몰으는 朝鮮사람이다. 규율이 없고 반성이 없다. 예산이 없고 來頭를 몰은다. 다만 解弛가 잇고 퇴폐만 있을 뿐이다. 언제부터 朝鮮사람이 이 꼴이 되엿는가 이대도록 퇴폐되엿든가 생각하니 한심한 눈물이 벼개에 潛然하다. 우리에게는 다만 극기극복이 잇서야 할것이다. 남보다 열곱절 스무곱절 백곱절 이러케 목표를 잡고 나아가야 할 것이다. 스사로 나를 깨우치고 가다듬고 잠들다.

東海의 일출! 우리가 지금 슨곧은 三千里朝鮮地圖을 가장 맵시잇게 멋있게 맨드러논 잘록한 허리 永興灣이다. 마주보이는 對岸은 松田이요 남으로 비스듬이 들어온 것은 葛麻半島다. 북쪽에 범섬이 딱 버틔여 水口에 羅星을 일우고 밖으로 大島, 薪島, 麗島, 高島들의 조고만한 섬들이 東海바다를 가로막고 있다. 滿潮干潮의 차이가 없으니 바다는 맑고 깊어 한량없이 깨끗하고 雄船巨艟이 숨을만한 곳이니 東海 제일의 항구로 사진과 그림을 맘대로 못그리는 海軍要塞地帶다.

얼른 배를 져어 東海바다 한 복판에 둥둥실 떠보고 싶다. 우두머니 海濱에 스다.
식당에서 된장국으로 배를 불니고 第 2線에는 朝鮮사람은 部落 松下里를 돌아 다시 바다에 들다.

溪船이 늘어슨 稅關을 본다음 市街地를 한박휘도니＜261＞ 朝鮮商店의 零星이 눈에 띄울 뿐 답답한 가슴을 안고 다시 뱃길로 松濤園에 도라와 潮湯에 들엇다.
潮湯食堂에서 저녁을 마치고 10萬株가 넘을 長松이 落落한 넓은 松林을 한박휘도니 淸澄한 공기가 사람을 살지게 할 것 갓다. 4,50채가 더 넘을 셋 別莊(松濤園해수욕장의 경영으로 한여름철 6,70원을 받고 세를 준다.)은 뷔인 집이 별로 없다. 군데군데 레코트의 小夜曲이 淸風을 타고와서 솔밭사이로 흐트러진다. 채채이 한여름철은 내짓인양 가족들을 다리고 살림을 하며 운동도 하고 독서도 한다. 地上仙이 않이고 무엇이냐. 참으로 修養을 잘아는 국민이다. 그러나 섭섭한 일이다. 이틈에 朝鮮사람은 하나도 없다. 조곰, 몃거름 遊樂場인 카페와 술집과 게집이 잇는곳엘 나아가 보면 밤이 새도록 떠들고 짓거리고 술주정하고 야단이다. 조흔 對照가 않이고 무엇이냐. 산 敎訓이 않이고 무엇이냐. 이러케도 비참한 히닉구가 또 다시 잇겟는가. 물론 없기도 없다. 정신의 여유 경제의 여유 교양의 여유. 그러나 그러타고 이러케 無節操하고 이러케 低級하고 이러케 퇴폐만 한 대서야 될 수가 잇스랴. 來頭을 보랴고 발버둥질처야 할 것이 않이냐. 너희의 자식을 보고 손자를 보고 曾孫 高孫을 쟁각하라. 그리고 남과 견주어 대조해 보라.

아희들을 다리고 바로 산으로 올나 『花飛終有實』 『鏡落豈無聲』을 이약이 해주는 동안 지금으로부터 70년 전에 故死되얏다하는 太祖의 手植松을 발라보니 偶合이냐 有意냐 자못 괴이한 노릇이다.
斷俗門을 드러서니 松林은 蒼*하도록 탁 어울리고 시내는 소리처 10리를 몰아오니 胸中에 塵埃-＜263＞ 다 씻기워 모든 煩惱- 흐터진 듯 하다.
不貳門에 드러서니 골은 더욱 深邃할대로 으스름하고 물을 다시 맑은채로 질펀하다. 山嘆樓에 올나 땀을 드리고 泛鍾樓에 鍾을 어르만지며 大雄寶殿을 울어러보니 규모의 宏大함이 京山모든 절에 비할바 않이다. 東廡에 부친 珠聯의 筆致! 淸秀하고 奇逕하기로 老僧 한아를 붓드러 누구의 글시임을 물으니 恩穎 金炳魚冀의 글시라한다. 安東金氏로 哲宗朝의 國戚으로 一世를 風靡하든 權門勢家로 그 일홈은 익히 드럿거니와 이러틋 達筆인 숨은 재주를 가젓든줄은 전혀 몰랏다.
한참동안이나 字劃을 어르만젓다.
나려오는 길에 藥泉에 들너 물을 마시니 淸冽하고도 비릿하고 찝찔하야 그 맛을 五味중에 뭇엇이다 형언하기 어렵다. 消化器病에 좋고 婦人病에 효력이 많다한다. 먹기 실혀하는 아희들을 억지로 붓들어다 두어 표주박식 먹이고 旅舍로 도라왓다. 오후에 소낙비가 몹시 쏘다지니 압개울에 물이 제법 살저 밤새도록 졸졸 거리고 나려간다. 서울서 나려온 두세친구를 만나 밤이 늣도록 이약이하다 자다.
이튼날 오후에 절에서 어든 고비나물 한뭉치를 안꼬 역으로 나오니 밤 열점이면 京城着이다.
이것으로 이 여행의 끝을 막었다.
