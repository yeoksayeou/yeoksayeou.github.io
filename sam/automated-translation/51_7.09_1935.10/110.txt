Publication Date: 1935년 10월 01일
Article Title: 百萬長者의 百萬圓觀 (A Millionaire's View on a Million Won)
Author: 秋葉客 (Ch'uyŏpk'aek)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Discussion)

A Millionaire's View on a Million Won
						Ch'uyŏpk'aek
Mr. Pang Ŭngmo, Who Sketches Grand Plans for University Founding in His Mind

After waiting a few minutes for President Pang in the splendid yet concisely decorated reception room of the Chosŏn Ilbo President, I was guided by a female attendant (女給 아이), opened the door to the president's office, and stepped inside.
Stepping into this president's office, what caught my eye <64> was, similar to what I felt in the reception room earlier, a sense of newness and splendor, yet also a simplicity, lacking any particular decoration.
For a newspaper company's president's office or reception room, it was an excellent room that showed points superior to those of any other newspaper company. However, the absence of even a single calligraphic work (筆額) or landscape painting (山水畵) by famous figures on any corner of the walls seemed to offer a slight glimpse into the temperament of the master of this house. Only two silver cups with floral patterns visible inside a glass cabinet on one side and a single flowerpot (화분) in a corner were present.
Intending to hear in sequence about Mr. Pang's "Daily Life," "View on Life," and "View on Gold," as he was said to be busy with office work, I waited for his gaze to lift from the documents he was looking through and immediately struck up a conversation.

Me: "Sir, people say you live a very frugal (質素) life. Is that so? It's said that you get through the entire year with just two suits. Is that really true?"
Pang: "Isn't the life of a Korean naturally like that? How can one live a splendid life? This suit I'm wearing now, this is already its 8th year this fall. In summer, one wears a summer suit, in autumn and spring, a spring/autumn suit, and in winter, a winter suit, so wearing one suit for a year only amounts to a few months anyway. So naturally, one wears them for a long time. This suit will probably last another 2 years. Thus, it's not that I intentionally try to be frugal (질소) or thrifty (검박); it just doesn't wear out, so I have no choice but to wear it for a long time."
Me: "But do all wealthy people do that? Mostly, don't they smartly order new, excellent suits costing over 100 won every season?"
Pang: "Well, among wealthy people, there might be some like that. But where do I have money? And even if I had money, my temperament doesn't desire to adorn my body luxuriously (奢侈) or dress ostentatiously."
Me: "What do you enjoy most for recreation (오락), sir?"
Pang: "Recreation? I don't know anything about recreation. There are many kinds of recreation that people do nowadays, but I don't know any of them. I don't even know how to play Hwatu (화투), whether it's Songhak or Myŏngwŏl."
Me: "Ha- Then, what <65> hobbies do you enjoy the most?"
Pang: "I don't really have any special hobbies either. Since I spent about 10 years of my youth living dependently (공생), there was no time to learn anything."
Me: "Do you not play sports like golf or horseback riding (승마)?"
Pang: "Where would I find the time for such sports? Horseback riding is quite a dangerous sport. Some days, I don't even have time for my early morning walk."
Me: "It seems several prominent gentlemen frequently play golf at the golf course outside Tongdaemun these days. Don't you sometimes join them, sir?"
Pang: "It seems Mr. Pak Yŏngch'ŏl, Mr. Min Taesik, and several others gather frequently to play golf, but since I don't know how to play and have no time to play, I don't even think about it."
Me: "Then do you have time for reading?"
Pang: "I don't have time for reading either. I try to read whenever I have a moment. But even that doesn't work out as I wish. I barely manage to glance through a few newspapers and magazines published in Seoul or Tokyo; besides that, I simply have no time to touch anything else."

Planning to Establish a University Using the Yŏnghŭng Forest as Funds

Me: "Sir, wasn't there an incident last year where you received the disposal (拂下) of several tens of thousands of chō (町步) of state-owned forest land in Yŏnghŭng, Hamnam Province?"
Pang: "Yes, there was."
Me: "Regarding that Yŏnghŭng forest, recently, some people are saying that you plan to properly plant trees (植木) there and then use it as the basis, the capital, to establish a university. What are your thoughts on this?"
Pang: "Yes, I am thinking that way. When I meet my family members or close friends and talk, I mention such things. But when would that be realized? For the trees planted in that mountain to grow sufficiently, it will take 30 years from now. Who knows if I'll even be alive then?"
Me: "Of course. Won't you live for another 30 years?"
Pang: "Goodness. If I live another 30 years, I'll have to pass 80. Are there many people who live past 80?"
Me: "But sir, for the sake of establishing a university in Korea, you absolutely must live to be 80. Indeed."
Pang: "Hahaha, well. If only I could live that long... I wish I could, but can one do as one pleases? Anyway, if the Yŏnghŭng forest is well-planted (植木), it seems it could generate sufficient funds, so I feel greatly encouraged these days." <66>

Operating a Middle School with Land Producing 5,000 Sŏk Harvest from the Suwŏn Farm

Me: "And one more thing to ask, according to rumors, you've had the idea for some time to manage a middle school with the funds obtained from cultivating reclaimed tidal land (干瀉地) in Suwŏn once it yields a considerable harvest. Is this rumor not an exaggeration?"
Pang: "I have had the thought for a while of trying to run a middle school with the land in Suwŏn. It's something my family members always discuss when we gather. In any case, this is a definite plan. Now, the reclaimed tidal land (干瀉地) in Suwŏn, after investing quite a lot of money, has reached the point where the land yields a considerable harvest. What comes from the Suwŏn farm now amounts to about a 5,000 sŏk (石) harvest per year, so this alone should be sufficient to run a middle school."
Me: "Is that so? Truly admirable."

What is Your View on Gold?

Me: "How do you view money?"
Pang: "Of course, I think it's valuable. But if it's valuable, yet you just keep it locked away in your own chest (궤속), what's valuable about it? If it's valuable, it should be used valuably, shining brightly for the whole world."
Me: "Sir, do you then plan to spend more in the future on ventures other than the newspaper business you are currently running?"
Pang: "Of course, except for what I need to live on, I will donate everything to social or cultural enterprises. I will dedicate it to benefit my people. I will serve my society."
Me: "Then, will you not leave an inheritance (유산) to your descendants and donate everything for society?"
Pang: "Well, I should give my descendants enough assets (재산) to live on. How can I give absolutely nothing? Even if I give, how much money would be needed for a Korean's standard of living? And the rest, I will dedicate to some cultural enterprise. Simply passing on (遺傳) all one's money to descendants is a very bad thing. Humans must have an independent spirit to live on their own. Seen in that light, passing down wealth to descendants might even be harmful."
Me: "People say this and that, but roughly how much is your total wealth (財産), sir?"
Pang: "What money do I have now? I've put it all into this newspaper company, and now I'm empty. I have none."
Me: "Surely that can't be true? You mentioned the Yŏnghŭng forest and the Suwŏn farm earlier, and aren't you also said to own many mines (광산) in various places?"
Pang: "The Yŏnghŭng forest cannot yet be considered wealth, and considering the money invested in the Suwŏn farm, it's not yet <67> something remarkable. I do own mines (광산) in a few places."
Me: "Please tell us a little about the hardships you faced while accumulating that huge sum, said to be several million won (數百萬圓)."
Pang: "Hardship has been with me since birth. Until I was 20, my parents were still around, so it couldn't really be called hardship, but after turning 20, I suppose I struggled for about 10 years."
Me: "Have you done some farming?"
Pang: "Yes, I've done a lot of farming. Having grown up in a village (村) since childhood, in that regard, even now, I might not know the theory better than any agricultural scholar (農學士), but in practice, I'd likely be better. Then, after some time, I quit being a farmer (농군) and turned to gold mining (금광)."
Me: "What kind of people do you think can accumulate money?"
Pang: "Of course, there might be such a thing as a person's luck (運), but I believe it must be someone diligent (근면) and earnest (착실). However, it's not something easily accumulated by just anyone either—."
Me: "Overall, you must currently have about 5 million won, I suppose."
Pang: "5 million won? If I really had that much money, how wonderful would that be?"
Me: "Do you not wish to accumulate more money?"
Pang: "I wish I could, but does it happen just because one wants it? I don't try to accumulate more money. Recently, some friends came asking to start a gold mine, but I refused. From now on, I intend not to engage in any profit-making ventures (營利事業) at all. I intend to completely withdraw my hand (一切로 손을 끈으럄니다). Though I have no choice but to continue with what I already possess."

He pressed the buzzer placed on one side of the table, and in no time, the same female attendant (女給仕 아이) brought tea in white porcelain cups (白砂器 盞). While I was talking up to this point, employees from the Sales Bureau or General Affairs Bureau came several times with documents and got them approved.
Waiting for Mr. Pang to light and smoke a "Kaida" cigarette, I decided to ask just one final question.
Just then, an attendant came in and announced that a visitor had arrived in the reception room. Having finished my business (用務) for the day, I immediately rose from my seat, took another look back at the Chosŏn Ilbo building (社屋), boasting its magnificent new construction, wished for the long life of this cultural entrepreneur, and lightly stepped onto the asphalt of Kwanghwamun Avenue (光化門通), walking steadily.
The high sky and the autumn wind somehow seemed to give me energy and courage. <68>

Mr. Min Taesik, Who Intends to Proceed As Is

On the twenty-first day of September, as autumn deepened, around 5 PM, walking down the alley of Kwanhun-dong, one encounters rows of storage sheds (줄향낭) lining both sides, and in the center, a large traditional Korean gate (소슬 대문) appears. This large house, blocking the alley, retaining the old appearance of a traditional Korean building (朝鮮 古代建物), is the residence of Mr. Min Taesik (閔大植), known publicly as a great tycoon worth perhaps 10 million won, or perhaps 4 or 5 million won, and the President (頭取) of Tongil Bank (東一銀行).
Entering through the first main gate and looking around here and there, only long rows of storage sheds (줄향낭) were visible, and no sign of human presence could be detected. In the spacious yard, only small pebbles (자듸잔 쪼각돌) lay scattered; there was no adornment (치장), not a single tree or blade of grass to be seen.
Hesitantly, I moved my feet, passed through another middle gate blocking the way ahead, and entered a wide courtyard. Compared to the residential gardens of Chang'an, it was immensely spacious. One side of the courtyard was a green lawn where several unnamed pink flowering trees stood. Within that neatly swept, vast yard, only the evening breeze of early autumn quietly came and went. And inside, reminiscent of ancient castle ruins hundreds of years old, a stone wall gate covered in green ivy (츩넝쿨) stood blocking the way, and only after passing through yet another passage did the door to the reception room open. It was truly layered upon layered, like the paths of Bashu (巴蜀).
From the moment I first entered the gate of this house, and even more so upon entering this reception room, I felt as if I were experiencing firsthand the old appearance of a renowned household from the late Joseon period (韓末名門家).
Nowadays, when looking at the residences of wealthy people, it is common to see modern houses built in Western style and splendid gardens. However, visiting Mr. Min Taesik's residence, one could readily see that it has preserved the appearance of a "renowned family" (名門의 家) that was most exemplary among our ancestors of old <69>, maintaining its true identity without loss, keeping its ancient form to this day.
Inside this reception room, built in the traditional Korean style, only a few East Asian landscape paintings (東洋山水畵) were hung; apart from that, there was no other arrangement, decoration, or ostentation, displaying literal frugality (質素), thrift (儉約), and simplicity (簡潔).
After a while, Mr. Min Taesik, appearing well over 50, followed his secretary into the room.
Although he wore a Western suit, it was not particularly splendid, looking no better than the ordinary 40-50 won suits we wear.
It was clearly evident that Mr. Min prioritized "frugality and thrift" even in his attire.
A millionaire who doesn't know how to wear silk clothes, a millionaire who values frugality and simplicity above all else—what is this person's daily life, his view on gold, his way of navigating the world (處世術)?
Expecting to hear words distinctly different from what wealthy people commonly say, I prepared to launch my questions.

What is His Philosophy of Gold?

Me: "In this world, how do you view gold—that is to say, money?"
Min: "Haha... What a question you ask! Please ask worldly people such things. Anyway, it seems true that money is indeed a necessary item in this world. In reality, it is also necessary and valuable. However, regarding this, people of the world likely know better than I do even before I speak. But, money cannot be said to be the most valuable or most precious thing for us humans."
Me: "But in this world, it seems one needs money to live, and money also takes the lead (압장을 스게) in any undertaking."
Min: "Well, since the world is structured (꾸며젓스니까) that way, it can't be helped. But one cannot become a slave for money, sacrifice one's life, or trade one's loyalty and conscience for it. Humans have conscience, human feeling (인정), and besides money, possessing character (인격) is most necessary."
Me: "Currently, those known as millionaires in the world express desires to undertake certain ventures, or say they wish to try this or that project if only they had a few hundred thousand or a few million won more than their current assets. Sir, with your vast fortune, what are your thoughts?"
Min: "Yes, I too often hear such talk when I meet various people on different occasions. I always think this way: if one currently has the assets, one might undertake any public or social enterprise. But I have never thought of deliberately earning more (버어러가지고) than what I currently lack <70> to try some venture. If one has the means, one does it (하논게고); if not, one cannot."
Me: "A certain Westerner, possessing billions of won in assets, reportedly said that the money in his hands belongs to many people, and he is merely fortunate enough to be temporarily entrusted with it due to good luck. How do you view this statement?"
Min: "Certainly, it sounds plausible. However, the perspectives (관렴) of Westerners and us Easterners might differ somewhat. I don't think there are many people in Korea who hold such views."
Me: "What businesses are you involved in, sir?"
Min: "Tongil Bank (東一銀行) and the Beer Company (麥酒會社). Also, I have somewhat deep involvement with the Oriental Development Company (東洋拓植會社), Kyŏngsŏng Electric Company (京城電氣會社), and Chosŏn Trust Company (朝鮮信托會社)."
Me: "Besides these, do you wish to undertake any other businesses in the future?"
Min: "I cannot say anything definite yet. Currently, I haven't decided to start any specific new business."
Me: "Sir, how much money have you spent to date? You also studied abroad in the West for a long time, didn't you?"
Min: "I suppose I spent quite a bit of money studying, so-called studying abroad in the West. However, even my study abroad involved attending university in the United States (米國) for about 4 years, primarily studying foreign languages a little before returning, so I can't say I spent a particularly large amount of money. Other than that, the only significant spending was, as mentioned earlier, becoming involved in those few companies, which required quite a substantial investment."
Me: "How much is your wealth, sir?"
Min: "How can I possibly say?"
Me: "But people say it amounts to about 10 million won. What about that?"
Min: "Haha... 10 million won? How could it be that much? There probably isn't a Korean millionaire worth 10 million won."
Me: "But surely it's well over 1 million won?"
Min: "Well, I suppose it might be around that."
Me: "I believe it exceeds 5 million won."
Min: "How can I state an exact figure?"

What Does He Find Hobby and Pleasure In?

Me: "What are your hobbies, sir? You must have had some special hobbies when studying in the United States (米國) or now."
Min: "I neither appreciate refined leisure (風流) nor particularly enjoy recreation (오락). The only thing I enjoy, both during my school days and now, is playing golf."
Me: "Besides that, do you engage in sports like tennis or horseback riding?"
Min: "No, I don't know any of those. I did try tennis a bit during my school days, though."
Me: "Do you not enjoy calligraphy and painting (書畵)?"
Min: "I don't do those at all now either. <71> Firstly, where would I find the time (사히)? Every day, if I'm not visiting the various companies I'm involved with, I'm at the bank, so I barely have time for reading either."
Me: "Then, with which gentlemen do you enjoy playing golf?"
Min: "Whenever I meet Mr. Han Sangnyong, Mr. Pak Tasan (Yŏngch'ŏl), or perhaps the Vice Governor-General (政務摠監), or Bank President Ariga (有賀頭取), we invariably head to the golf course outside Tongdaemun."
Me: "Do you have any other particular hobbies besides that?"
Min: "I don't really have hobbies, but if I must say, it's reading from time to time when I return home."
Me: "What fields of reading do you engage in?"
Min: "I don't read books of any specific category; I browse through some Chinese classics (漢學) and also look through various newly published books (新刊書類)."
Me: "Do you not like traveling?"
Min: "I enjoy traveling too. Every year, I usually visit hot springs or Kŭmgangsan once or twice, but this year, due to a long-standing illness of my parent (家親), I've been constantly worried and unable to leave their side (엽을 떠나지 못하는 형편), so I haven't been able to travel anywhere this year."

Due to the rather lengthy conversation, the electric lights have turned on at some point.
From somewhere, the resonant chime of a grandfather clock striking seven times faintly drifts in.
Seeing Mr. Min pull out his pocket watch suggests he likely has an appointment somewhere. I decided to ask just one last question.
Me: "Will you pass on that great wealth to your descendants, or will you donate it all to social enterprises?"
Min: "I cannot say yet. My parent (家親) is still alive, so I cannot decide entirely on my own."
Me: "Do you not wish to accumulate more wealth?"
Min: "I just hold onto what I have. I don't try to accumulate more (더 모올야고는 안해요). You could call it maintaining the status quo (現狀維持), or perhaps conservative (保守的)."
I quickly exited the room, pervaded by a solemn, refined, and subtle atmosphere (분위긔), wiped the sweat from my brow, passed through the three gates (三大關門) in classical style, and set my light footsteps moving forward. <72>

**Summary**

This 1935 article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine interviews two prominent Korean millionaires, Pang Ŭngmo (President of Chosŏn Ilbo) and Min Taesik (President of Tongil Bank), about their lifestyles, views on wealth, and future plans. Pang emphasizes frugality, describes ambitious plans to establish a university funded by forestry profits and a middle school funded by agricultural land, and believes wealth should primarily serve society rather than just descendants. Min presents a more conservative view, content with maintaining his current business involvements (banking, beer, investments in colonial enterprises), enjoys golf as his main leisure activity, lives relatively simply despite his wealth, and remains non-committal about the future disposition of his assets, citing respect for his living parent.

From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into the mindset of the Korean elite under Japanese rule. It showcases contrasting approaches to wealth: Pang's focus on long-term cultural/educational projects for the Korean nation versus Min's more status-quo-oriented business activities, including participation in Japanese-led colonial ventures like the Oriental Development Company. Their expressed views on frugality, social responsibility, inheritance, and interaction with high-ranking colonial officials (like the Vice Governor-General or Japanese bank heads) provide a glimpse into the complex social and economic landscape of colonial Korea and the differing strategies employed by its privileged class.

百萬長者의 百萬圓觀

大學創設의 雄圖를 머리속에 그리는 方應謨氏

지금대로 나아가겠다는 閔大植氏
가을도 바야흐로 깊어저 가는 9월 수무하룻날 오후 5시나 되여, 寬勳洞 골목길로 거러들어 가노라면 줄향낭이 좌우로 쭉― 늘어저 있고 가운데 커다란 소슬 대문이 나타나는 조선 古代建物의 늙은 자태를 그냥 그대로 가지고, 골목길을 가로 막은 커다란 집, 이 집이 지금 세상에서 혹은 1천만원이요, 혹은 4, 5백만원이라 하는 큰 부자이자 東一銀行頭取로 게신 公集 閔大植氏의 주택이다.
첫 번 대문안으로 들어서 이리저리 살펴보아야 줄향낭만이 길게 느러저 있을 뿐 아무도 사람의 인기척이라고는 어더들을 수 없엇다. 그 넓다란 뜰에는 자듸잔 쪼각돌만이 딍구러 있을 뿐, 아무런 치장도 없고, 나무 포기하나, 풀포기 하나 어더볼 수 없다.
머믓머믓하다가 발을 옴겨, 앞에 가로막힌 中門하나를 또 지나, 넓다란 뜰안으로 들어스니, 長安의 住宅庭園하고는 한량없이 넓은 뜰안 한쪽으로는 새파랏케 잔듸밭이 되여 있어 멫대의 이름 모를 분홍꽃나무가 서있을 뿐으로, 말쑥하게 쓸여저 있는 그 넓다란 뜰 안으로는 다만 첫가을의 저녁바람만이 고요히 오락가락 할 뿐이다. 그리고 그 안은 마치 수백년묵은 古城趾를 연상식히는, 푸른 츩넝쿨이 덥힌 돌담문이 가로막아 섯는데 그리로 또 한고비 지나서야 응접실의 문이 열니어진다. 바야흐로 重重疊疊 巴蜀의 길가치 싸고 또 쌋다.
이 집 문안을 처음 들어 섯을 때 붙어도 그러하엿거니와 이 응접실에 들어와서 더욱 늣겨지는 것은 韓末名門家의 옛 자태를 그냥 맛보는 감이 들었다.
요사히 돈 많은 이들의 주택들을 보면 대개는 양옥으로 지은 샛틋한 주택과, 화려한 정원등을 가지고 있는 것이 항용 보는 일이거니와, 이 閔大植씨의 주택을 찾어 보매, 옛날의 우리 先祖들 중에서도＜69＞ 가장 모양하엿고, 넷 面目을 일치안코 직혀오는 「名門의 家」를 오늘날까지 그냥 옛모습 그대로를 가지고 내려옴을 언뜻 알어볼 수가 있었다.
在來의 우리 조선식 그대로의 건물인 이 응접실안에는 멫개의 東洋山水畵가, 걸여 있을 뿐으로, 그 외에는 아무런 장치로, 장식도, 모양도 보이지 않으랴는, 文字 그대로의 質素와, 儉約과, 簡潔함을 알어보게 한다.
얼마후 비서되는 사람의 뒤를 따라 50세가 훨신 넘어 뵈이는 閔大植씨가 나타난다.
氏는 몸에 양복은 입엇슬 망정 그것은 그리 훌융한 것이 않이고 보통 우리들이 입는 4, 50원 정도의 양복으로 밖에는 않보인다.
氏는 몸에 이르기까지 「질소와 검박」을 제일주의로 함이 역역히 나타난다.
비단옷을 입을 줄 모른다는 百萬長者, 質素와 簡潔을 제일로 아는 百萬長者, 이 분의 일상생활이며 그의 黃金觀이며, 處世術은 어떠한가,
나는 이 분에게서는 반듯이 세상의 돈 많은 사람들이 흔히 하는 말과는 별달은 말을 들어 보리라는 긔대를 가지고 질문의 화살을 던지기로 하였다.

黃金哲學은 어떤가
나 「이 세상에서 황금-즉 다시 말하면 돈이라는 것을 어떻게 보심니까.」
閔 「하하…말슴을 다 뭇는구려! 그런 말은 세상 사람들에게 물어보시구려, 하여간 이 세상에서 돈이란 반듯이 있어 필요한 물건인 것만은 사실인가 봐요. 사실에 있어서 또한 필요하고 귀한 것이라고 할 수 있지요. 허나 여긔에 대해서는 내가 말하기 전에 몬저 세상 사람들이 나보다도 더 잘 알줄 알어요, 그러나, 돈이란, 우리 인간에게 있어서 제일 귀한 것이고 제일 갑가는 것이라고는 할 수 없지요.」
나 「그러나 이 세상에서는 사라가재도 돈이고 무슨 사업하재도 역시 돈이 압장을 스게 되는가 봅니다.」
閔 「그야 이 세상이 그렇게 꾸며젓스니까 할 수 없는 일이지요만, 돈때문에 노예가 된다든지, 자긔의 목숨을 바친다든지, 의리와 양심을 밧칠 수는 없으니까요. 사람이란 양심이 있고, 인정이 있고, 돈 이외에 인격의 소유가 그 중 필요하지요.」
나 「지금 세상에서 멫 100원되는 부자라는 이들은 어떠한 사업을 허고 십다고도 하고 또 어떤이들은 지금 자긔가 가진 재산외에 멫 10만원 멫 100만원만 더 있으면 이러이러한 사업을 해보고 십다고들 하는데, 선생은 그 多大한 재산을 가지시고 어떻게 생각함니까.」
閔 「네, 나도 혹 어떤 긔회마다 여러 사람들을 대하게 되면 그런 말을 종종 듯게 되는데, 나는 늘 이렇게 생각하지요, 현재 자긔가 가지고 있는 재산이 있으면 어떠한 公共事業이든지, 社會事業이든지를 막론하고 하겠지만, 자긔에게 현재 없는＜70＞ 것을 구태여 더 버어러가지고 무슨 사업을 하여보겟다고 까지는 생각하여 본 적이 없어요, 그만한 여유가 있으면 하논게고 없으면 못하는 것이지요.」
나 「서양의 어떤 사람은 멫 十億萬圓의 재산을 가지고 말하기를 자긔 수중에 있는 돈이라는 것은 여러 사람의 돈인데 요행 자긔가 운이 좋와서 일시 마터 둘 뿐이라고 하였다는데 이말을 어떻게 봄니까.」
閔 「물론 그럴 듯한 말이구료, 그러나 서양 사람과 우리 동양 사람들의 관렴이란 다소간 달으겠지요, 우리 조선에도 그런 생각을 가진 사람이 멫 될 것 갖지 않어요.」
나 「선생이 관게하시는 사업은 무엇 무엇임니까.」
閔 「東一銀行과, 麥酒會社지요,
그러고 東洋拓植會社나, 京城電氣會社나, 朝鮮信托會社같은 데도 좀 깊은 관계를 가지고 있지요만.」
나 「그 이외에 금후 더 어떠한 사업을 하고십지는 않습니가.」
閔 「아즉 무어라 말할 수는 없지요, 현재는 이런 사업을 하겠다고 작정한 것은 없지요.」
나 「선생은 오늘날까지 얼마나한 돈을 쓰셧서요, 서양에 가서 류학도 오래 하셧지요.」
閔 「공부하노라고, 소위 서양류학을 하노라고 돈 좀 쓴 셈이지요, 그러나 류학이래도, 米國에 가서 大學에 한 4년간 단이면서 外國語의 공부를 조곰하다 돌아온 관게로 별로 크게 돈 썻다고 할 수는 없지요. 그 외에는 별로히 크게 돈 썻다는 것이, 앗까 말한 멫 군데, 회사에 관계를 가지게 되니 거기에 좀 많은 돈이 들엇다고 할 수 있지요.」
나 「선생의 재산은 얼마나 됨니까.」
閔 「그걸 내가 어떠케 말하겠서요.」
나 「그런데 세상에서는 말하기를 1,000만원 가량은 된다고들 하는데 어떳슴니까.」
閔 「하하…1,000만원이요? 그렇게 될 수가 있나요, 조선 사람으로는 1,000만원 부자가 없슬 것인데요.」
나 「그러나, 100만원이야 훨신 넘으시겟지요.」
閔 「글세요, 그렇게야 되겠지요.」
나 「500만원은 넘을 줄로 아는데요.」
閔 「그걸 어떻게 딱 얼마된다고 말하겟서요.」

무엇으로 趣味와 樂을 삼는가

나 「선생의 취미는 무엇임니까. 米國에 가서 유학하실 때나, 지금이나 무슨 특별한 취미가 있겠지요.」
閔 「나는 風流도 모르고, 오락도 즐겨하지 않어요, 다만 즐겨하는 것이라고는 學校 단일 때나 지금이나 「꼴푸」치는 것 뿐이지요.」
나 「그외에 운동으로는 테니스나, 승마같은 것을 하시지 않으십니까.」
閔 「네, 그런 것은 모다 모르지요. 학교 단일 때에는 테니스같은 것을 좀 해보기는 하엿지만.」
나 「書畵는 즐기지 안으십니까.」
閔 「그런 것도 지금은 아주 않하지요.＜71＞ 첫재 헐 사히조차 어데 있나요, 매일 관게한 여러 회사에 돌아단이지 않으면 은행에를 가게 되니 독서할 사히도 별반 없어요.」
나 「그러면 「꼴푸」는 어떤 어룬들과 벗하여 하심니까.」
閔 「韓相龍씨나, 朴多山(榮喆)씨나, 그 외에도 혹 政務摠監이나, 有賀頭取같은 이들과 만나게 되면 으레히, 東大門밖 「꼴푸」장으로 몰니게 되지요.」
나 「그밖에는 별달은 취미가 없으심니까.」
閔 「취미래야 별반 없지만, 억지로 말하자면 집에 돌아와 틈틈이 독서하는 것쯤이지요.」
나 「독서는 어떤 方面의 것을 보심니까.」
閔 「무엇 일정한 部類의 책을 보는 것이 않이고 漢學도 더러 둘처보고 새로 나오는 新刊書類도 이것저것 좀씩 보지요.」
나 「여행같은 것은 조와하지 안습니까.」
閔 「여행도 즐기는 편이지요, 해마다 한 두 번씩은 온천이나, 金剛山같은 데도 단여 오지만 금년에 와서는 오래전 붙어 친환도 게시고 해서 밤낫 마음을 못놓고, 늘 엽을 떠나지 못하는 형편이니 금년은 아무데도 못단여 왔지요.」
벌서 꽤 오랜 동안의 대화가 오고 가는 바람에 어느 사히에 電燈불이 켜진다.
어데서인지 일곱 번을 때리는 괘종시게의 우렁찬 소리가 은은하게 들여온다.
氏는 호주머니에서 시계를 끄내서 보는 것을 보니, 아마도 어데 약속한 시간이 있는 모양이다. 끝으로 한다듸만 더 뭇기로 하였다.
나 「그 많은 재산을 자손에게 물여 주시겠습니까, 혹은 전부 사회사업에 바치겠슴니까.」
閔 「아즉은 말할 수 없어요. 家親도 게시고 하니 내 마음대로도 할 수 없지요.」
나 「재산을 더 모으고 십지는 않으심니까.」
閔 「나는 있는 것을 그냥 그대로 가지고 있을 뿐이지요, 더 모올야고는 안해요. 말하자면 現狀維持, 혹은 保守的이라고나 할넌지요.」
나는 엄엄하고, 고상하고 은은한 분위긔가 떠도는 이 방을 얼신 나와 이마앞의 진땀을 훔치면서, 古典風의 三大關門을 뻐서저 가벼운 발자국을 옴겨 노왔다.＜72＞
