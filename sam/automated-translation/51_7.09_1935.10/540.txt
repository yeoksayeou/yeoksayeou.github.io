Magazine Title: 三千里
Publication Date: 1935년 10월 01일
Article Title: 팔벼개노래 (Song of the Arm Pillow)
Author: 金素月
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

팔벼개노래
						金素月

첫날에 길동무
만나기 쉬운가
가다가 만나서
길동무 되지요.

家長만 님이랴
情들면 님이지
한平生 苦樂을
다짐둔 팔벼개.

첫닭이 꼬꾸요
목노치 말아라
내품에 안긴님
단꿈이 깰리라.＜248＞

오늘은 하룻밤
단잠의 팔벼개
來日은 相思의
거문고 벼개라.

朝鮮의 江山아
네그리 좁드냐
三千里 西道를
끗까지 왓노라.

집뒤山 솔버섯
다토든 동무야
어느뉘 家門에
싀집을 갓느냐.

空中에 뜬새도
의지가 잇건만
이몸은 팔벼개
뜬풀로 돌지요.＜249＞

**English Translation:**

Song of the Arm Pillow
						Kim So-wŏl

Is it easy to meet
a travel companion on the first day?
We meet along the way
and become companions.

Is only the head of the house (家長) a beloved?
If affection (情) grows, they become beloved.
An arm pillow, pledged
to a lifetime (平生) of joys and sorrows (苦樂).

The first rooster crows, "Kkokkuyo!"
Do not cry out loudly,
Lest the beloved nestled in my arms
awaken from a sweet dream. <248>

Tonight, for one night,
an arm pillow of sweet sleep.
Tomorrow (來日), a pillow of longing (相思),
like a kŏmun'go.

Oh, rivers and mountains (江山) of Chosŏn (朝鮮),
Are you truly so narrow?
Through the three thousand ri (三千里) of the Western Provinces (西道),
I have come to the very end.

Friend with whom I competed
for pine mushrooms on the hill behind the house,
Into which family's house (家門)
did you marry?

Even a bird afloat in the air (空中)
has a place to rely (의지) upon,
But this body, an arm pillow,
drifts like floating weeds. <249>

**Summary**

This poem by Kim Sowŏl, "Song of the Arm Pillow," uses the metaphor of an arm pillow to explore themes of fleeting intimacy, inevitable separation, wandering, and nostalgia. The speaker reflects on chance encounters turning into companionship, the depth of love found outside formal bonds, and the contrast between temporary comfort and future loneliness. The journey motif culminates in a feeling of displacement and rootlessness after traversing Korea, comparing the self to aimless drifting weeds. For historians, the poem offers insight into the emotional landscape of colonial Korea. The sense of wandering ("Three thousand ri of the Western Provinces, I have come to the very end"), displacement ("Are you truly so narrow?"), rootlessness ("drifts like floating weeds"), and nostalgia for lost connections could reflect the broader societal anxieties, disruptions, and feelings of dispossession experienced by Koreans under Japanese rule.
