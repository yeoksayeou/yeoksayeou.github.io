**Body Text Translation:**
A Game Between Love and Death
						By Romain Rolland

Amidst the raging maelstrom of the French Revolution, which shook the entire European continent, the Royalist Party (王黨) and the Girondin Party (지론도黨) have fallen, and now the Jacobin Party (짝고반黨) holds absolute dominance. It is the time when Robespierre holds political power and extends his reach. Sophie, the wife of Revolutionary Council member Jerome (60), was grief-stricken, shocked by the rumor that her lover Barère had been mercilessly killed after being pursued by the government. Just then, Barère suddenly came looking for Sophie. Barère was a Girondin Party member with a bounty on his head. Sophie, a “calm fairy” who held deep love within her reason, could not help but be severely shaken by the tempest of his passion, especially upon learning Barère had endured mortal agony for six months before returning now to meet her.

The Council was like a pack of cruel beasts. Today, too, there was a new execution. Danton, who had seemed like a guardian deity of the revolution standing high above the clouds amidst the flashing lightning in the pitch-dark night, was sentenced to death. Everyone trembled in fear before the glittering eyes behind the dictator Robespierre’s glasses. Cowardly men betrayed their leader and consented to the death sentence. Infuriated by the excessively heartless behavior of these humans, Jerome did not consent to this death sentence. He willingly faced a life-threatening crisis. Jerome, who had been under suspicion for some time, could no longer escape. Returning from the Council, he found Barère had come to his house seeking a hiding place. Thus, Barère and Sophie realized their love had deepened, and Jerome resolved to give his wife freedom.

“I will not forcibly hold onto someone<283> who hopes to be free from me. Contemptible humanity is not even worth despising. Only one existence gave me reason to trust humanity again. But that person has snatched this faith away from me.”

The only thing he believed in… love… was now also betrayed. It would be a good thing if these two unfortunate people could discover the joy of life a second time. I will abandon my life… Jerome firmly, firmly resolved.

There was a house search in the street. Fortunately, however, Barère was not discovered thanks to Sophie’s help. But Jerome’s friend and member of the Committee of Public Safety (公安維持委員), Carnot, came and reported that Barère was being hidden, informing Jerome that he would fall into crisis. Carnot gave him two travel permits (旅行券) under false names (僞名) and urged him to disguise himself as indicated and flee to Paris (巴里) tonight, as quickly as possible. This was because, as a scientist who had invented outstanding machinery and could exert himself for the revolutionary army, even Robespierre would likely not dare to kill him.

Jerome gave the two travel permits to his wife and urged her to disguise herself and leave with the young man as quickly as possible. Sophie, who had sought limitless freedom in her married life, finding the freedom she sought now hers, regained the pure heart she had at the beginning and became composed. She gave one of the permits Jerome had given her to Barère and tore up her own, burning it. Sophie felt terribly afraid of a happiness that would end, of a love that would soon wither. And so, thinking of how endlessly lonely and endlessly powerless they would become when the time came for happiness and love to abandon them, she could not suppress the piercing ache and bitterness welling up in her heart and mind.

Thus, she asked to leave together with her husband.

Barère leaves to meet Sophie again, unaware that Jerome and his wife are trapped by death like fish on a chopping block.

Shortly before facing death, the 60-year-old gentleman and his young wife Sophie reminisce about the sweet old days of their marriage.

“Sophie, do you remember those long nights we spent in this room? You sat beside the desk, reading a book and watching me work. I saw that you too were dreaming all sorts of dreams. We were both deeply asleep in dreams. Thoughts, work, knowledge, love, all were hazy in dreams. When my mind, like duckweed (浮坪草) floating on water, could not find stability and drifted aimlessly, I sought refuge in your pure spirit. You spoke good words to me…”

**Summary**

This article summarizes Romain Rolland's play "Le Jeu de l'amour et de la mort," set during the French Revolution's Reign of Terror. It depicts a love triangle involving Sophie, her elderly husband Jerome (a council member), and her lover Barère (a fugitive Girondin). Facing political danger himself for opposing an execution, Jerome discovers Barère hiding in his house. He decides to sacrifice himself, offering Sophie travel permits to escape with Barère. However, Sophie, fearing fleeting happiness, destroys her permit and chooses to face death with Jerome, while Barère leaves unaware. The story highlights themes of love, sacrifice, political peril, and disillusionment against the backdrop of revolutionary chaos. For historians studying colonial Korea, the publication of this translated French play in *Samch'ŏlli* in 1935 is interesting. It reflects the engagement of Korean intellectuals with European literature and ideas, even under Japanese rule, and the themes of political upheaval, idealism, betrayal, and sacrifice might have resonated subtly with the contemporary Korean situation.
