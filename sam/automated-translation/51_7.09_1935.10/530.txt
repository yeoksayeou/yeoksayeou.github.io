**Body Text Translation:**
Sending You Off on the Night Train
						Pak Yongch'ŏl
1
Disappearing into the utter darkness,
O, a mere candle flame.
Sending you off in the snowstorm and turning back,
O, my heart.
As if aching, as if swollen, the falling snow
comes embracing me.
Underfoot, the steps prone to slipping¤
leave their traces.
The path ahead, not even far, just feels so vast.

2
You, trying so hard to gaze outside,
must be sorrowful too.
Only your own face reflected in the dark beyond the glass window, tears
must be welling up.<245>
Entering my room, those eyes hidden in every corner
would smile at me.
As if hearing your voice, as if longing, my flesh
would feel the friction.
You who are leaving, I who am sending you off, just feel so distant.

3
You (the train), pushing through the darkness like an icebreaker
on a frozen sea.
You (the person), casting off weak sentiment and merely seeking
brightness.
You (the train), would you startle at breaking? Running along
two tracks.
You (the person), would you fear death? Straightening the path
to live strongly.
You (the train) carrying, you (the person) being carried, just feel so distant.

4.
Now, in the vast winter, towards the spring day not far off,
let me gaze.
Like a spring day, smiling as it rushes in, his train<246>
let me wait for.
How could I bear the words "I forget"! Just like this, smiling,
let me try to learn.
Even try to follow his steps,
pushing through the rough path.
Finally, I shall try to become even one who follows him.<247>
<245-247>

**Summary**

This poem by Pak Yongch'ŏl describes the sorrow and longing of parting with someone on a night train during a winter snowstorm. The speaker watches the train disappear, feeling the cold and emptiness, while imagining the departing person's sadness. The train is portrayed as a powerful force, like an icebreaker, carrying the determined individual towards "brightness" and a "path to live strongly." Despite the pain of separation, the speaker resolves to endure, wait for reunion in spring, learn to smile through the sorrow, and ultimately emulate the strength and follow the path of the one who left.

For historians, the poem offers a personal glimpse into the emotional landscape of separation and longing in 1930s colonial Korea. The train, a symbol of Japanese-imposed modernity, serves as the backdrop for both departure and a potentially aspirational journey towards a better future ("brightness," "living strongly"), reflecting complex attitudes towards progress and individual resilience amidst difficult circumstances. The speaker's final resolution to follow suggests a desire for agency and strength, perhaps mirroring broader aspirations within the colonial context.
