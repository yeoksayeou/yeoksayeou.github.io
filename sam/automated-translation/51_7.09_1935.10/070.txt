Short Sketches
						by Sŏksil Kŏsa

Current stream, deep spring - Chang Tŏksu (張德秀)
Bow that doesn't shoot birds - Chŏng Kwangjo (鄭廣朝)
I carve, you polish - Ch'oe Kyudong (崔奎東)
Willow trees stirred by wind - Song Pong'u (宋奉瑀) <43>

Clouds not rising - Sŏ Chaep'il (徐載弼)
Many years your house - Yun Ikson (尹益善)
Gusu's (姑蘇) frosty sky - Chŏng Chongmyŏng (鄭鍾鳴)
Fisherman might profit - Ku Chaok (具滋玉) <44>

See that steadfast North Peak (北岳) - Yi Sŭngman (李承晩)
Samadhi's (三昧) cold lamp - Pak Hanyŏng (朴漢永)
Spring wind on the Yi River (易水) - Ch'oe Rin (崔麟)
Lotus grows in a muddy pond - Kim Ilyŏp (金一葉) <45>

Earth dragon ascends the clouds - Ch'oe Ch'anghak (崔昌學)
Pine and cypress in three springs - Sŏl T'aehŭi (薛泰熙)
Pheasant alighting on yellow - Kim Yŏnsu (金秊洙)
Peach sky, lush leaves - Ch'oe Ŭnhŭi (崔恩喜) <46>

Phoenix dances riding a rope - Pak Hŭngsik (朴興植)
Red goes to Qi (齊) - Ch'oe Tuson (崔斗善)
One boat, reed flowers - Mun Ilp'yŏng (文一平)
Moon white, clouds white - Ch'oe Ŭisun (崔義順) <48>

Leaving the gate, cold mountain - Chŏn Il (全一)
Moonrise startles birds - Yi Yunjae (李允宰)
Wild wind in a pot - Hyŏn Chin'gŏn (玄鎭健)
Thousand-league skirt wind - Yi Hyŏn'gyŏng (李賢卿) <49>

Jade hands bring forth spring - Pak Ch'anghun (朴昌薰)
Ox plowing a stony field - Yi Kŭngno (李克魯)
Dyed but not blackened (涅而不緇) - Yi Yŏng (李英)
Fragrant chrysanthemum in ninth autumn - Hwang Sindŏk (黃信德) <50>

**Summary**

This article, titled "Short Sketches," is a poem by "Sŏksil Kŏsa" presenting a series of brief, four-character metaphorical descriptions paired with the names of prominent contemporary Korean figures from various fields like politics, literature, business, and social activism. Each phrase aims to capture an essence or perception of the individual, ranging from steadfastness (Yi Sŭngman as North Peak) and resilience (Kim Ilyŏp as a lotus in mud) to potential ambiguity or critique (Hyŏn Chin'gŏn as wind in a pot). For historians, the poem offers a fascinating glimpse into contemporary perceptions and characterizations of key personalities during the Japanese colonial period, reflecting how they were viewed or perhaps stereotyped within certain circles. The use of classical allusions and natural metaphors provides insight into the cultural context and literary styles of the time.
