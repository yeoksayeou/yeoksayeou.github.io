奉天印象記
						金台俊

Despite the news that the Manchurian Incident occurred, that the Great Empire of Manchukuo was established, that the flag of another empire fluttered on the globe, and that the colors on the map of Manchuria and Korea, separated only by a narrow strip of water (一衣帶水), had changed—despite these reports striking my ears (耳朶) so intensely and frequently, I never had a chance to visit there.

Then, in the summer of Eulhae (乙亥, 1935), I happened to set foot within the territory of the newly rising Empire of Manchukuo. Nearly three years had passed since the empire's founding, and I wanted to see how much it had changed from the Manchuria I knew before the incident.

Hanyang, farewell, I shall return!
The road ahead is long, ten thousand *ri* by land and water.
Magnificent is the Yalu River railway bridge;
Reaching it, Fengtian is the old castle town of Shenyang.

The red sun rises from the sorghum fields and sets into the sorghum fields, the scenery of endless plains stretching for thousands of *ri*, the sight of blue-clothed figures returning by the riverside at sunset, driving dozens of pigs, cows, sheep<196> and horses like semi-nomadic barbarians (蠻人)—despite the flowing water and vast expanse, I felt there was something pitiful and desolate about it all.

Fengtian, which has changed its name (名色) many times—from the Imperial Capital of the Qing Dynasty, to Chang Tso-lin's capital Shenyang, to Fengtian City of the Manchukuo Empire—preserves together the various relics of the Great Qing era, the Chang Tso-lin era, and the Manchukuo era. One of Fengtian's characteristics, making it seem somewhat like a semi-pastoral city in the center of the Great Wilderness, is the horse-drawn carriage. In Manchuria, there are many horses, consequently many horse bandits (馬賊), and many horse-drawn carriages. Therefore, in the past, deficiencies in the political system and the hardships of the people's lives could potentially turn all thirty million people of Manchuria into horse bandits. It's not as if the seeds of horse bandits existed separately.

I toured the old and new city districts of Fengtian in an inexpensive horse-drawn carriage. The new city district, inhabited purely by Japanese mainlanders, seemed much more vibrant and prosperous compared to the old days. Everywhere, groups of Japanese soldiers could be seen coming and going (來征). Signs for various Western firms (洋行) and companies (公司) were lined up densely on many Western-style buildings. However, it is said that the medicine sellers (actually Opium dealers (阿片장사)) have the most substance. The several thousand Korean households living in the Sŏt'ap 10-room buildings (西塔10間房) are said to be either medicine sellers, involved in prostitution (色氏장사), or daily wage laborers (日給勞働者).

Within the walled old city district, many important buildings have been commandeered for Manchukuo's official use, and compared to the old days, there is much more coming and going of Japanese mainlanders. From the famously tall Kilsun Silk Store (吉順絲房), one can take in the entire view of Fengtian, situated in the middle of the plain.
Blue walls (푸른 壁), red pillar couplets (붉은 楹聯), swarms of rickshaws, dusty roads—while there are aspects that feel grotesque (크로데스크) and damage the reputation of a cultural city, there's also something appealing about the parts that still seem somehow abundant and artless (풍셩풍셩하고 어수룩한), compared to countries that hastily carry out poverty relief projects.

Through the kindness of Librarian Kim Kujŏng (司書 金九經)씨, I was able to view the rare books (珍籍) in the collection one by one. When he pointed out, "Here is Chang Tso-lin's funeral chamber (殯室), here is the room where Yang Yü-t'ing (楊宇霆) was shot," it felt as though the ghost of Yü-t'ing, who often threatened Hsüeh-liang (學良), was watching nearby.
Mr. Kim Kujŏng is not only deeply versed in the study of Manchu and Mongolian scripts. He published Yu Hŭi's *Ŏnmunji* (諺文誌) and Yu Tŭkkong's *Nanyangnok* (灤陽錄) in Fengtian, including them in the *Kangwŏn Ch'ongsŏ* (薑園叢書) series. He also plans to publish the *Samhaksa Chŏn* (三學士傳, Biographies of the Three Scholars) soon and will publish Sin Kyŏngjun's *Hunmin Chŏngŭm Unhae* (訓氏正音韻解).

In Fengtian, the contrast between the new and old city districts could be said to represent the difference in international characteristics between Japan and China. One shows the most advanced style (樣式) of a global capitalist nation, while the other shows the feudal style of a great nation spanning several thousand years. One is global, progressive, and aggressive; the other is particularistic and conservative. <197> <196-197>

**Summary**

The author, Kim T'aejun, recounts his trip to Fengtian (Shenyang), Manchukuo, in 1935, nearly three years after the state's founding. He contrasts the bustling, Japanese-dominated new city, marked by commerce (including opium dealing) and a heavy military presence, with the older Chinese city, which retains vestiges of the Qing Dynasty and Chang Tso-lin eras but is now partly occupied by Manchukuo authorities. He notes the harsh realities for Korean residents, many involved in illicit trades or day labor. Despite the somewhat "grotesque" elements and dust, he finds a certain artless abundance remaining in the old city. He also meets Kim Kujŏng, a scholar preserving Korean texts in Fengtian. The article highlights the stark socio-political transformation of Manchuria under Japanese influence, contrasting Japanese modernity and expansionism with lingering Chinese traditions and the precarious position of Koreans caught in between. Historians might find the descriptions of the distinct Japanese and Chinese districts, the mention of opium trade linked to Japanese businesses, the socio-economic status of Koreans in Fengtian (drug trade, prostitution, labor), and the cultural preservation efforts by Kim Kujŏng particularly interesting for understanding the complexities of life in Manchukuo and the Korean diaspora experience during the colonial period.

奉天印象記
						金台俊
滿洲事變이 생기고 大滿洲帝國이 생기고 지구상에는 다시 한 제국의 旗ㅅ발을 휘날리게 되고 一衣帶水로 相隔한 滿鮮地圖의 빗갈이 변해지도록 자조 이러한 소식이 몹시도 강렬하게 나의 耳朶를 때려옴에 불구하고 한번도 거긔에 가볼 기회가 없엇다.

때는 乙亥의 여름 偶然히 나도 新興滿洲의 帝國領內에 一步를 印하게 되엿다. 帝國成立후 벌서 근 3년 나는 사변전의 滿洲와 얼마나 달러 젓는가 보고 싶엇다.

漢陽아 잘잇거라 갓다오리라
앞길이 즐편하다 水陸十萬里
宏壯하다 鴨綠江 鐵橋다리에
다달으니 奉天은 녯날 瀋陽城
붉은 해는 高梁에서 떠서 高梁으로 떨어지고 一望無絶한 千里萬里의 들경치라든가 落日川邊에 도야지 소 羊＜196＞ 馬 각 몇십頭씩 半農牧期의 蠻人처럼 물고 도라가는 靑衣人의 행색이라든가 흘으는 물과 넓은 面積에도 어데인가 可憐하고 섭섭한 구석이 잇슴을 늣기게 되는 것이엿다.

淸國帝都로 張作霖의 都城瀋陽으로 滿州帝國의 奉天市로 여러번 名色을 變改하여가는 奉天은 大淸時代 張作霖時代 滿州帝國時代의 諸遺跡을 함께 包藏하고 잇다. 어떻게 보면 大荒原의 중앙에 잇는 半牧歌的 都市같기도 한 奉天의 특색의 하나가 마차다. 滿州에는 馬가 많고 딸어서 馬賊도 많고 마차도 많은 것이다. 그러기에 과거에 잇서 정치제도의 不備와 민중생활의 困乏은 滿州 三千萬 인구를 모다 馬賊으로 변할 수도 잇는 것이다. 어데 馬賊의 씨가 따로히 존재한 것은 아니다.

갑싼 마차를 타고 奉天新舊市街를 一週하엿다. 순전히 日本內地人만 거주하는 新市街는 넷날에 비해서 훨신 활기를 띠여 보이고 퍽 더 번성해 보인다. 간곧마다 OO군인이 떼를 지여가지고 來征하는 것이 눈에 띤다. 무슨 洋行이니 무슨 公司니 하는 간판이 櫛比한 여러 양옥에 부터잇다. 하나 내용은 약장사(실은 阿片장사)가 가장 실속이 잇다한다. 西塔10間房에 사는 數千戶의 韓僑도 약장사가 아니면 色氏장사 그러치 않으면 日給勞働者라고 한다.
城內인 舊市街의 중요한 건물은 滿洲國의 御用으로 된 곧이 많고 녯날에 비하야 日本內地人의 來往도 많다, 높기로 유명한 吉順絲房은 평야의 중앙에 잇는 奉天의 樣子를 一眸에 발아볼수가 잇는 것이다.
푸른 壁 붉은 楹聯 인력거떼 몬지 많은 길-크로데스크 늣김과 문화도시의 명예를 毁損하는 점도 잇지만 약발으게 貧民救濟工事하는 나라보담 어덴가 아즉도 풍셩풍셩하고 어수룩한 곧이 잇서 보이는 곧이 탐스럽다.

司書 金九經씨의 厚意로 舘藏珍籍을 일일히 얻어 보앗다. 여긔는 張作霖의 殯室 여긔는 楊宇霆을 銃殺하든 室이라고 가라처 줄적에 자조 學良을 威脅하든 宇霆의 幽靈이 엿보는 것 같엇다.
金九經씨는 滿蒙文字硏究에 造詣가 깊은 뿐이다. 柳僖의 諺文誌 柳得恭의 灤陽錄등을 奉天에서 출판하야 薑園叢書속에 느엇다. 또 近日 三學士傳을 간행하고 申景濬의 訓氏正音韻解를 발간하리라한다.

奉天에 잇서서 新舊市街의 대조는 바로 日本과 中國의 국제적 風物의 차이라 하겟다. 하나는 世界資本主義國家의 가장 진보된 양식을 보이고 하나는 몇 천년 大國의 봉건적 양식을 보이고 잇다. 하나는 세계적 진취적 공격적이고 하나는 특수적 보수적인 것이다.＜197＞ ＜196-197＞
