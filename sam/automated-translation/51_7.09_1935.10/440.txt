Sin Pulch'ul-ssi's *Mandam*: The Magnanimous Husband
						By Sin Pulch'ul-ssi
I deeply regret that the story of Her and Him, the "Walk with the Wife," had to be stopped due to time constraints.

"Honey! Are you leaving just like that?"
As the husband opened the door to leave for work 맞춰서 (at the right time) in the morning, his wife, wiping her hands on her apron skirt, asked. The husband, pausing mid-opening the door, turned around and looked.
Him: "What?"
Her: "Are you leaving just like that?"
Him: "Well, if I don't leave just like this, should I walk upside down? Hahahaha."
Her: "You're always too frivolous, it won't do! A person needs to be serious (마지메, *majime*) sometimes, but you're always like this..."
Him: "Alright! Let's postpone the character lecture (修身講話) until nighttime. What is it anyway?"
Her: "What I talked about last night, won't you grant me that?"
Him: "What was that? Hurry up and tell me. I'm busy heading to work."
Her: "Didn't I say I wanted to call a shaman and perform a *kut* (굿)?"
Him: (Getting angry before she finishes speaking)
Him: "*Kut*? What *kut*!"
If he had just slammed the door shut and left, thinking about it, there would be nothing more tasteless <212> than that.
This doesn't mean approving of superstitious acts (迷信行爲), but the method of admonishing it shouldn't be so rash. Also, considering the wife, who lives a pitiful existence as if trapped within the confines of a poor salaried man's household, relying solely on her one husband in the world, and furthermore, waiting all day for her husband's return – to this helpless wife, words, which cost nothing, should be spoken gently, kindly, and affectionately. Instead, just yelling thoughtlessly whatever comes to mind is like a stiff fellow who swallowed a stick and got indigestion; it's an undeniably crude and tasteless act.
He closed the door again and came closer to his wife.
Him: "Look here, honey! What is this *kut*? Haven't you seen the articles published day and night in the newspapers? Don't they scream things like 'Superstition Ruins the Nation Theory' (迷信亡國論), 'Superstition Abolition Theory' (迷信打破論), and 'Superstition's Harm Theory' (迷信廢害論)? Seriously, in today's world where we live with electric lights, what are ghosts? What kind of senseless act is this for a person of the modern era, where the globe rolls along clothed in science?"
Her mouth pouted out 쭈루룩 (like a spout).
Her: "What, what, what, what, what..."
He patted Her back several times. It seems he thought her words were stuck.

One day, after clearing the breakfast table, He called Her in a gentle voice.
"Honey!"
She replied in a sharp, needle-like voice,
"What is it?"
It couldn't be helped; after nagging day and night for so many days, her husband had flatly refused, and resentment had built up, which is why the sharp edge came out at the end of every word. The husband, thinking his beloved wife's demeanor was becoming too cold, felt he couldn't just let it continue. So, thinking, "Oh, what the heck," although not fully approving of superstition, he decided to grant her wish just this once and had called her to give her the money. But the wife, unaware of this,
"What is it?"
shot him a glare (白眼), and her upper and lower lips protruded sharply 쭈루룩 (like a spout).
The husband chuckled softly and, putting his hand into his wallet, asked,
"How much does it cost? For the *kut*."
She finally heard the word 'money,' and who knows where her sulkiness (암상) disappeared to, but a smile like Japanese soybean paste (日本된장, *miso*) spread across her face. As she rushed towards her husband's lap, her protruded lips went back in 쭈루룩 <213> and were gone.
"Well, well, well, I can't ask for much. 30 won will do."
The husband almost fainted (昏到) but regained his composure.
"30 won? What 30 won? How much is my total monthly salary, and you ask for 30 won? Here, just use 10 won."
Hearing this, She said,
"What!"
and the lips that had just retracted protruded 쭈루룩 again. If you measured Her sulky, protruding pout, it would probably fill a bowl. Angered by her husband's words, she went to the threshold over there, turned away, and started biting the ties of her apron skirt, picking at them like cherries. Seeing his wife like this, the husband, trying to smooth things over once more, made a great resolution (一大勇斷), took out another 10 won bill, and said,
"Here! Honey, why are you crying? It's 20 won. 20 won!"

A few days after the 20 won *kut* was performed, Her shadow suddenly appeared at the husband's company office.
Seeing Her attire, the husband was so shocked (大驚失色) he almost collapsed. It was no wonder: his wife had rushed in barefoot, wearing her rubber shoes (고무신) backward, still in her apron skirt, holding a fire poker (부지깽이), with her hair undone, and her face painted like a scarecrow (아웅괭이). It was impossible for the husband not to be startled.
"Fire, fire, fire! There's a fire at home!"
Hearing this, the husband, nearly losing his mind (환장) in panic, hastily called the Fire Brigade (消防組) and ran home, only to find that while there was indeed a fire, the situation was dumbfounding (기가 멕힐 지경).
Looking closely,
Since the house was originally poor, there wasn't even a sliver of space (一方寸) in the tiny yard to place a single *kimchi* pot. So, inevitably, the *kimchi* pot had been placed on top of the earthen stove (부뚜막). One side of the stove had collapsed, tilting the *kimchi* pot. To straighten it, a piece of firewood (장작개비) had been propped underneath the tilted pot. However, whenever the fire was lit, flames leaking out (솔솔새여 나오는) from the crack (틈박운이) around the cauldron's edge ignited the piece of firewood. It didn't burn well, just filled the kitchen with thick smoke. She, working outside the kitchen, discovered this and foolishly thought the house was on fire. She ran outside screaming,
"Fire!"
causing the neighborhood people to rush over in alarm without thinking and douse the inside of the kitchen with water.
In the chaos, not only was the rice being cooked ruined, but the *kimchi* pot was shattered to pieces. Seeing this, the husband <214> was so angry that he grabbed his wife who had followed him and should have yelled,
"You wench! Did you perform the *kut* hoping for a fire?"
and given her a good smack. But, unexpectedly, this husband laughed once and said,
"Hahahaha, honey! That was a really good *kut*."
At these completely unexpected (千萬 意外) words, She could only stare at her husband with wide eyes.
The husband laughed once more and said,
"The *kut* was well done because when you asked for a 30 won *kut*, if I had quickly agreed and held a 30 won *kut* as you said, it would have been fine. But because we held a 20 won *kut*, a 10 won fire broke out. Hahahaha. If I hadn't listened to you at all, it seems a 30 won fire would have occurred. Where would we have gone then in this cold winter?"
However, no matter how magnanimous (寬大한) these words, born from a generous heart, might have been, to Her listening, she couldn't tell if this was real or not, so she could only sit there, half-believing (半信半疑) and bewildered. Furthermore, the husband didn't continue speaking. He took off his suit jacket, rolled up his white shirt sleeves energetically, went to the corner of the wall where some mud had been piled up for some time, sprinkled water on it, kneaded it, and came back to repair the collapsed stove with the clumsy skill of a novice plasterer.
(Here, Sin Pulch'ul-ssi's unique way of speaking and gestures produced, truly, a divine skill (神技) difficult to fully describe in writing. Moreover, this was not limited to this scene but continued throughout the earlier scenes as well, but I cannot record it. - The Reporter)
When the husband's behavior, whom she had fully expected to scold and even hit her for her obvious mistake, turned out so unexpectedly different, the wife, overcome by startling emotion, felt large tears wetting the corners of her eyes.

That evening, the dinner table set for the husband surely had one extra delicious side dish. However, what I want to mention briefly here is that the reward for the magnanimous husband wasn't just getting to eat that extra side dish; that husband needed to understand what lay within that dish one step further. In other words, besides seasonings like chili powder, sesame salt, sesame oil, and whatnot, a husband who knows that there is one more very strange thing mixed in truly possesses the qualifications of a magnanimous husband.
So, what exactly is mixed in there <215>? It's that the wife, wanting to feed her grateful husband a delicious side dish, must have cried while preparing the seasoning (양렴). This gratitude towards her kind husband is not the joy of receiving high-end cosmetics, a piano, diamonds, or silk stockings. Rather, even amidst poor living conditions, where people's nerves become frayed, instead of dealing with each other with harsh words and blows, the husband always caresses with a warm hand of care (心手). Overwhelmed with gratitude for his kind actions (一擧一動), she cried while adding soy sauce and sesame salt to the delicious dish. It is the generosity of a husband who can imagine that his wife's overflowing tears of gratitude are mixed with the seasoning that qualifies him.

The husband, receiving his meal at a table where stew bubbled alone by the corner of a broken brazier (火爐), called his wife to sit facing him and share the meal. As she took off her apron skirt and sat facing her husband, her gaze suddenly met his. Feeling both embarrassed about the day's events and grateful, a bright red blush (紅潮), born from conflicting emotions, embroidered (繡) her face, and she lowered her head.
How joyful must the husband's heart be, gazing upon such a lovely sight? Perhaps beside them, the beloved breath of the peacefully sleeping second generation (第 2世) mingles with the harmony (團欒) of this young couple's home!
This blissful ecstasy (法悅) is not a joy felt only by those living in luxurious mansions (高臺廣室). Even before a broken wall and torn window (깨여진 벽 뚜러진 창), it is a pleasure (樂) that anyone possessing a truly magnanimous disposition can feel.

Postscript (As mentioned at the beginning, the above is only the gist (要旨) of Mr. Sin Pulch'ul's *mandam*. I once again lament that the *mandam* presented in text cannot convey even one-tenth of the fragrance of the actual *mandam* performance (漫談實演) delivered on stage. - The Reporter) <216>

**Summary**

This article recounts a humorous anecdote (*mandam*) by Sin Pulch'ul about a poor couple. The wife wants money for a shamanistic ritual (*kut*), which the husband initially dismisses as superstition but eventually gives her a reduced amount (20 won instead of 30). Later, a minor kitchen mishap involving smoke is mistaken for a major fire by the wife, leading to chaos. Instead of anger, the husband humorously blames the fire's "10 won" scale on the "incomplete" 20 won *kut*, suggesting a 30 won *kut* would have prevented it. His unexpected kindness and gentle humor deeply touch the wife, reinforcing their bond and highlighting the narrator's theme that magnanimity and understanding, not material wealth, create true happiness even in poverty.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for its depiction of everyday life and social tensions in 1930s colonial Korea. It showcases the persistence of folk beliefs like shamanism (*kut*) despite official discourse promoting modernity and decrying "superstition" (迷信), reflecting the complex negotiation between tradition and imposed modernity. The story also provides a glimpse into the economic hardships (poor salaried household) and gender dynamics (wife's domestic confinement and reliance, husband's authority tempered by affection) of the era. The mention of *mandam* itself points to popular entertainment forms of the time.
