발행일	1935년 03월 01일
기사제목	나무는 심어 놓고 (Having Planted the Trees)
필자	尙虛 李泰俊
기사형태	소설 (Novel/Fiction)

Having Planted the Trees
						尙虛 李泰俊

"What's the use of looking back at what's small? We should hurry and walk briskly while the wind has died down..."
As he said this, turning back to look at his wife, his voice was casual, but tears welled up in his eyes again. Thinking that once they crossed this mountain pass, they would never see that village again, his feet felt as heavy as a thousand pounds.

This pass, only five *ri* from home, this pass... Whenever he crossed this ridge carrying firewood, their house was always the first thing that caught his eye. Whenever he saw smoke rising from the house, he would tighten his belt, hoist the load of wood again, and stand up. From this pass, in the setting sun, he could even clearly see his wife's skirt hung to dry on their fence. This would be the last time looking at that house from this pass, that house newly thatched like a little yellow chick!

Reaching the crest of the pass, he adjusted his walking stick and turned around once more to look at the village.
There wouldn't be a village like that anywhere else. Even coming back from the town, once past the Sŏnwangdang shrine hill, it was cozy without a breath of wind. The stream in front, good for washing clothes and drinking water, and if the weather turned cold, just gathering pine needles on the back mountain could provide fuel for several days without worry... Now, it's all just a story of someone else's village!

"Let's hurry."
This time, his wife, who had fallen behind, blew her nose and wiped her tears, taking the lead.

Once over the pass, they fled as if insignificant. The man carried a bundle of bedding, clothes, a pine needle bundle, and things from across the sea, all packed tightly on his back. The woman carried their young child, wrapped so tightly in a blanket that its head wasn't visible, and held some kind of oil bottle. Sometimes ahead, sometimes behind, they jumped over ditches <258> and took shortcuts across paddy and field ridges, running so fast the wind whistled in their ears.

It wasn't market day, so there were few people on the road. Occasionally, a quail would startle and fly up from underfoot, or a pheasant would cry out "kkeok-kkeok" from a field ridge and flee towards the mountain, but otherwise, there was nothing.

"What if we take the wrong path..."
"How could I not know the way I walked every day to gather firewood..."
They exchanged only these words when passing a small fork in the road. Then, mouths seemingly sealed, they walked silently. It was well past lunchtime when they finally reached the main road to Seoul.

On the main road, the wind blew quite fiercely. The telephone poles hummed. With the winter solstice tomorrow or the day after, the wind felt sharp as ice, making their collars tremble again.
They trembled partly from the cold wind, but even more so from the long, overwhelmingly wide road, stretching out endlessly beyond sight. This road was unfamiliar to their eyes, their feet, and their hearts. While walking through the fields and paddies, they hadn't felt it as much, but stepping onto the wide new road, it truly felt like they were heading to a strange place. The unsettling anxiety of setting out to find a precarious livelihood surged within them. That's why the humming sound of the telephone poles seemed immensely more frightening than the calls of mountain birds or pheasants. Though unsaid, both the man and his wife felt the same.

All they could do was walk that road, ten *ri*, twenty *ri*. Of course, when cars passed, and even when just a bicycle came ringing its bell, they would scurry together, stepping down off the road, and just trudge onward towards Seoul.

They were a family of three: the couple, Pang Sŏbang and Kimssi, and their two-year-old daughter, Chŏngsun, carried on Kimssi's back. Until a few days ago, there had been four, including Pang Sŏbang's father. For the thirty-two years since his birth, he had lived without much want in the village they had just left. Though it was someone else's land, they had farmed the land belonging to Kim Chinsa's family for several generations, treating it like their own and farming it with peace of mind. During Kim Chinsa's time, the entire village lived without paying a penny in land tax (터세), and even after Kim Chinsa passed away, compared to other regions, the landlord wasn't particularly harsh. Kim Chinsa's son, Kim Ŭigwan, also followed his late father's virtuous example, usually reducing the rent by two <259> or three *sŏm* during the harvest season in years when tenant farmers had major life events like weddings or funerals. No one among the tenants, with their dull senses, knew exactly why this kind Kim Ŭigwan had mortgaged off all that good land. Some had heard townspeople chatter about him doing gold mining with some Japanese person or starting a company. And indeed, for a while, some Japanese people and men in Western suits frequented Kim Ŭigwan's house, making Kim Ŭigwan's two large dogs bark constantly – it felt like just yesterday.

Anyway, after Kim Ŭigwan's family left for Ansung or somewhere, and the landlord became a Japanese company, it became difficult for anyone without their own plot of land to survive. The land rent increased severalfold, they were told to use chemical fertilizer (금비) on the paddies, which was lent to them, and then in autumn, high interest was charged, the rice was taken at a low price, and they were charged for various taxes and fees they had never heard of. When they calculated everything later, they found they hadn't even earned their farming wages; instead, they were in debt. There was no other way to pay off the debt they incurred. If they had a cow, they sold the cow; if they had a house, they sold the house. That was the only way. And so, one family left, then another, and within three years, five or six households had departed.

The county office was very concerned about this. The fact that a village once considered a model village showed signs of becoming deserted was something the county rightfully needed to address. So, last spring, the county provided about two hundred *sakura* trees (사구라나무) to this village. Two trees were distributed to each household, and they were also planted along the roads and on the hillsides. The idea was that when these *sakura* trees bloomed like clouds, even the ignorant people of this village would deepen their love for their village and wouldn't recklessly leave for other places.

Most of the *sakura* trees survived; only a few died. The ones Pang Sŏbang's family planted, both in the front yard and on the back hill, grew well and vigorously. Officials from the county came, looked, and said they would all bloom next year.

However, people kept leaving anyway.
Pang Sŏbang's family also initially disliked the idea of suddenly leaving for another region. Their love for the village, their love for nature, their love for their neighbors – all stemmed from their bones, many times more earnest than those who gave them the *sakura* trees. When they planted the *sakura* trees, they worried if any might die, tending to them morning and evening. When they saw the branches gaining luster and buds sprouting, even for them, living buried in nature, <260> they had never felt the mystery of nature, the joy of spring, as intensely as they did then.

"They say the flowers will bloom next year, right?"
"Well, I wonder what the flowers are like?"
"Anyway, they say these flowers are quite something to see."
"Yes, so they say..."

But people kept leaving anyway. Entering this winter, Pang Sŏbang's family was the second household to leave.

After three days, dragging their blistered feet and limping, they finally saw Seoul shrouded in evening smoke amidst flickering snowflakes. It was completely dark when they entered the gates of Seoul.

In Seoul, it wasn't that there was no one to greet them warmly.
"Where are you from? Where are you heading? Please come to our inn."
But,
"Do we have any money..."
As soon as they said this, those seemingly kind people would flee as if stung by a bee.

It wasn't that they had absolutely no money. After selling their house and paying off debts, they had a few won left. But it wasn't enough money to comfortably stay at an inn and buy meals. Dragging their weary legs, enduring scoldings from traffic police, they wandered aimlessly from street to street. It was well past late night when they finally put down their bundles in one place. No matter how much they searched, the only place that offered shelter from the snowflakes was under some bridge – they didn't know its name.

"You should breastfeed that child."
"What's the use of nursing with empty breasts?"
This was because passersby peered down under the bridge when the baby cried.

In the darkness, they unpacked their bundles and ate hardened porridge and boiled eggs without even water. Then, without a chance to stretch their aching, throbbing legs, they sat, or stood up and shuffled around when they couldn't bear sitting any longer, passing the long night awake.

The next day, thinking it was better than being completely exposed, they bought a straw mat to wrap around themselves, set up a pot, bought rice, fetched water, and bought firewood. And the three of them rested deeply for a day.

The snow didn't stop that day either. By night, it began to fall in large flakes. Pang Sŏbang watched the falling snow <261> and thought that after this snow stopped, a terrible cold would arrive. He also thought he should have brought a broom made of bush clover.

He woke up at dawn. He searched for a long time through the snow that covered the tops of his feet and finally bought a small bush clover broom, like a squirrel's tail, paying five *jŏn* for it. Then, feeling as if he had acquired great capital, he went from house to house, knocking on doors that weren't even open yet.

"Would you like me to sweep your snow?"
"We have someone to sweep."
"Won't you sweep the snow at your house?"
"I'm worried it will freeze and then I'll sweep."

Pang Sŏbang was dumbfounded.
"Ha! These folks don't even have yards! Bought a broom for nothing!"
He returned under the bridge.

He also went to the employment agency. He looked for porter work. He tried carrying an A-frame carrier (지게) around, looking for carrying jobs, but no one called for him. A student asked him to carry a crate of fish to the station, but when he actually lifted it, he worried whether he could find his way back under the bridge alone afterwards. So,
"If I go there, will I be able to find my way back here alone?"
He hesitated. The student scolded him in Japanese and left.

One day, a policeman came looking under the bridge. He hadn't come for a household check.
"What do you think you're doing making a fire under the bridge? Huh? Smoke has been coming from under here every day... If you make a fire again, you won't be allowed to sleep under here anymore, understand?"

Indeed, from that evening on, no smoke rose. If they had something to boil, it wasn't that they couldn't boil it by going outside the bridge, but that day, their food had run out since the morning.

"What should we do?"
His wife was so disheartened she didn't even have the energy to cry. Only the child would suckle at the empty breast a couple of times and then whimper. Pang Sŏbang sat without any answer, occasionally clicking his tongue.
"Damn this world!"

The next day, early morning. The child was asleep in its father's arms. In the early evening, the mother held it, but if it wet itself, the father would then open his clothes and hold it <262>. The father, lost in thought all night and unable to sleep, had finally fallen asleep near dawn and was sleeping soundly with the child.

Kimssi felt infinitely sorry for her husband. Her husband, who never wasted money on a single drink and only bought tobacco to share with fellow workers on work days – how had he ended up in such a state? Thinking this, she could only resent the world. And she thought if only they hadn't sold the house, even if they had to starve, and only wanted to return to their hometown.

After much thought, Kimssi picked up the gourd dipper.
When leaving their hometown, a neighbor had attached it to their luggage, saying,
"They say you even have to buy these things in Seoul."
It was a new, large, well-dried gourd dipper.

This was the first time she had left the underside of the bridge since arriving in Seoul. And it was the first time in her life she had gone out holding a gourd dipper [to beg]. Her legs trembled. Having starved for a full day and night and exhausted by the child, her eyes saw stars twinkling even in the bright sky. But composing herself, she looked for rich houses. They all looked like rich houses, but all the gates were firmly shut. She wandered, looking for a house with an open gate, so focused on going forward through this alley and that alley that she didn't look back. Fortunately, there was a house with an open gate, and though not every such house gave something, by the time she had begged a gourd full of rice – some cold, some warm – from one out of ten houses, she had completely lost her way back. She tried this street, but it was a different area; she tried that street, but it was a different world. She had no idea how to get back to that stream, that bridge. She was devastated. There were many passersby to ask, but without knowing the name of the stream or the bridge, it was futile. As the sun climbed higher, the streets swarmed with people, and Kimssi's mind and legs became increasingly confused. Just then, an old woman tapped Kimssi's back with a kind hand.

"Where are you looking for?"
Kimssi burst into tears first.

"Don't worry. There's no place in Seoul I don't know. I'll find it for you..."
The kind old woman took Kimssi straight into her own house right in front of them, offering hot scorched-rice water and even breakfast.
"Don't worry, eat something. I'll just tidy the kitchen quickly, and we'll go out together." <265>

Kimssi thought that Seoul was also a place where people lived, where there was kindness, and trusting the old woman as if she were heaven itself, she shed tears of gratitude onto the dining table and ate without refusal. But with her starving husband and child in mind, the food wouldn't go down her throat. She only drank a few sips of the scorched-rice water, quickly put down her spoon (술), and followed the old woman out.

However, the kind old woman led Kimssi only to unrelated places. To Chin'gogae, to department stores, to streams that were definitely not the right one – she dragged her around for half a day and then said,
"My legs hurt today, let's look tomorrow."
Kimssi felt like her heart was tearing apart, but she didn't have the confidence to set out alone, abandoning the help of the kind old woman. She sat up all night, and gently urged her the next morning. They set out early again, but the old woman just led her to irrelevant places.

The old woman had a plan from the beginning. Seeing Kimssi's decent face and youthful flesh, she saw her as a wildcat (삵) sees a plump hen.
"How can I make some money off this..."
This was the intention the old woman formed upon discovering Kimssi.

Kimssi had no way of returning under the bridge again.

Pang Sŏbang's eyes blazed with fury.
"Damn that woman! To run away even thinking of this little child! Wicked woman! Damn her to death!"
He ground his teeth.

Pang Sŏbang couldn't bear to watch the child, who had starved for two days. He carried the child out and fed it whatever he could beg, whether spicy or bitter (*것). The weather suddenly turned cold. The child caught a cold and even developed diarrhea.

All night long, the blanket, the father's jacket lapel, and trouser legs, soaked with urine and feces in the darkness, froze stiff and rattled. Yet, within them, the child's body burned with fever, hot to the watching eye.
"What should I do? God, how can you be so heartless?"
He muttered this, but only the cold dawn wind whipped past, slapping his cheek.

Waiting for daybreak, he wrapped the child and went looking for a hospital.
"Please save this child."
"The doctor hasn't come out yet. But why did you let it get this bad? Why didn't you bring him sooner?"
"Because I didn't have money..."<264>
"Do you have it now?"
"No, I don't. Just please save him, I will earn the money and pay you back. Wouldn't I pay it back?"
"Try going to another big hospital..."

Pang Sŏbang spent half the day wandering just like this, from one hospital doorway to another, then returned under the bridge empty-handed. Pang Sŏbang was hungry again. But he couldn't leave the sick child alone for even a single step. Still, as evening approached, realizing he couldn't just stay up all night again, he shut his eyes tightly as if determined not to see, stood up, and went out.

When Pang Sŏbang returned sometime later after managing to get a few lumps of cold rice, it was completely dark. Under the bridge was pitch black. Peering intently for a while, he saw the child had crawled out of its bedding and was lying on the frozen bare (언맨) ground, neck limp, sobbing weakly (흐득흐득). He picked the child up and carried it outside the bridge. The child was having convulsions, eyes rolled back.

"If you're going to die, die quickly! Wicked woman! How well will you do after abandoning this child..."
Pang Sŏbang repeated several times,
"Die already!"
Pushing the child away, yet quickly pulling it back to look at it. Each time, the child's breathing grew shallower.

But sleep was cruel. Around what time could it have been? He had dozed off momentarily and woke with a start. It felt like only a moment had passed, but a great change had occurred around him. The day had dawned brightly, and the shallow breaths rising from the child had completely stopped (뚝 끄처). Only a slight warmth remained under the armpits; the face and limbs, once like balls of fire, had turned cold as frozen fish in the meantime.

Spring came. The winter that had treated Pang Sŏbang so coldly passed, and in its place came the spring that made Pang Sŏbang even sadder. Branches of azaleas and forsythia, like young brides (새악시), swayed on every streetcar and automobile, and on Namsan and in Ch'anggyŏngwŏn, *sakura* blossoms bloomed like clouds. Even in Pang Sŏbang's heart, woven only with dull sinews, it was a time that made him too sorrowful a poet to think of his hometown, his daughter, his wife.

One morning, luck was with him for once. Before breakfast, he carried a Japanese person's luggage all the way to the end of Namsan-jŏng and easily earned 50 *jŏn*. Hurrying down to find a tavern, he saw *sakura* blossoms blooming profusely on bent branches in the yards of Japanese houses. He stood멍하니 (blankly), gazing as if looking at a picture. <265> Suddenly, thoughts of his hometown flooded his mind.
"The *sakura* trees we planted must have bloomed like that too... The whole village must be full of flowers..."

Just then, a Japanese woman strolling under the shade of the flowers met his eyes. Pang Sŏbang flinched as if caught committing a crime and turned away. The young woman's face, radiant like a flower bed! Pang Sŏbang felt something vibrate sharply in his chest as he walked down. First, he went to his usual tavern and downed a couple of double shots (곱배기) with hearty soup. After exchanging a few jokes with the middle-aged female owner, he came out. The world seemed entirely sad if he thought it sad, yet also entirely joyful if he thought it joyful.

But once the alcohol wore off, the world was, after all, an unbearably sad world.
"Damn this world!"
He wanted to sit down anywhere, stretch out his legs, and weep.

-(Showa 8, January)- <266>

**Summary**

This story follows Pang Sŏbang and his family, tenant farmers forced to leave their beloved village due to mounting debts under a new Japanese corporate landlord. Despite the county's superficial attempt to foster village loyalty by distributing *sakura* trees, economic hardship prevails. They arrive in Seoul seeking work but find only poverty, indifference, and exploitation. They end up living under a bridge, struggling to survive. His wife disappears while begging, likely trafficked, and their sick child dies due to lack of money for medical care. The story ends with Pang Sŏbang alone, haunted by memories and despair amidst the blooming *sakura* trees in Seoul, symbols of both his lost home and the hollow promises of the colonial authorities.

From a historical perspective, the story starkly illustrates the devastating impact of Japanese colonial agricultural policies on Korean tenant farmers. The shift from a traditional landlord (Kim Chinsa/Ŭigwan) to a Japanese company led to exploitative practices (high rent, forced use of costly fertilizer '금비', unfair pricing), resulting in widespread debt and displacement – a common experience during that era. The government's response, planting Japanese *sakura* trees ('사구라 나무') to instill local patriotism, is depicted as utterly disconnected from the people's real needs and suffering, highlighting the cultural and economic imposition of colonial rule. The family's tragic fate in Seoul reflects the harsh realities of urban migration and poverty for displaced rural populations under colonialism. The specific mention of Showa 8 (1933) grounds the narrative firmly within this historical context.

나무는 심어 놓고
						尙虛 李泰俊
『작고 돌아 봔 뭘 해. 어서 바람을 졌을 때 힝하니 걸어야지...』
하면서 안해를 돌아보는 그도 말소리는 천연스러우나 눈에는 눈물이 다시 핑그르 돌았다. 이 고개마루만 넘어서면 저 동리는 다시 보랴야 안 보히려니 생각할 때 발도 천근이나 무거워지는 것 같었다.
이 고개, 집에서 오리밖에 안 되는 고개, 나무를 해지고 이 고개턱을 넘어설 때마다 제일 먼저 눈에 띄이군 하던 저 우리집, 집에서 연기가 떠 오르는 것을 볼 때마다 허리띠를 졸으고 다시 나무짐을 지고 일어서군 하던 이 고개, 이 고개에선 넘어가는 해벌에 우리집 울타리에 빨아 넌 안해의 치마까지 빤히 보이군 했다. 이전 이 고개에서 저 집, 저 노랗게 가주깐 병아리처럼 새로 영을 인저 집을 바라보는 것도 마즈막이로구나!
그는 고개 마루턱에 올라서더니 짐빵을 치키며 다시 한번 돌아서서 동네를 바라보았다.
아무댈 가도 저런 동네는 없을 것이다. 읍엘 갔다와도 선왕당 턱만 나려서면 바람 한 점 없이 안윽하고, 빨래하기 좋고 먹어도 좋은 앞 개울물이며, 날이 추우면 뒷산에 올라 솔잎만 긁어도 몇칠씩은 염녀없이 때더니... 이전 모두 남의 동네 이야기로구나!
『어서 갑시다.』
하면서 이번에는 뒤에 떠러졌던 안해가 눈물 코물을 풀어던지며 앞을 섰다.

그러나 친절한 노파는 김씨를 당ㅎ치 않은 곳으로만 끌고 다녔다. 진고개로 백화점으로 개천이라도 당ㅎ치 않은 개천으로만 한나절을 끌고 다니고는
『오늘은 다리가 아프니 내일 찾읍시다』
하었다. 김씨는 가슴이 찢어지는 것 같었으나, 그 친절한 노파의 힘을 버리고 혼자 나설 자신은 없었다. 밤을 꼼박 앉아 새우고 은근히 재촉을 하여 이튼날 아츰에도 또 일즈거니 나섰으나 노파는 그저 당ㅎ치 않은 대로만 끌고 다녔다.
노파는 애초부터 게획이 있었던 것이다. 김씨의 멀끔한 얼골과 살의 젊음을 그는 삵이 살진 암닭을 본 격으로 보았던 것이다.
『어떻게 돈냥이나 만들어 써 볼 거리가 되면...』
이것이 그 노파가 김씨를 발견하자 세운 뜻이었다.
김씨는 다시 다리 밑으로 돌아올 리가 없었다.
방서방은 눈에서 불이 났다.
『쥐길 년이다! 이 어린 것을 생각해선들 달아나다니! 고약한 년! 찍어 쥐길 년』
하고 이를 갈았다.
방서방은 이틀이나 굶은 아이를 보다 못해 안고 나서서, 매운 것 *것 할 것 없이 얻는대로 주어먹였다. 날은 갑작이 추워졌다. 어린애는 감기가 들고 설사까지 났다.
밤새도록 어두움 속에서 오즘 똥을 받은 이불과 아범의 저고리섭, 바지자락은 얼어서 왈가닥거리고, 그 속에서도 어린애 몸은 드려다 보는 눈이 뜨겁게 펄펄 달았다.
『어찌하나! 하느님 이렇게 무심합니까?』
하고 중얼거려도 보았으나, 새벽 찬바람만 윙하고 뺨을 갈 길뿐이었다.
날이 밝기를 기다려 아이를 꾸려 안고 병원을 물어서 찾어갔다.
『이 애 좀 살려 주십시오.』
『선생님이 아직 안 나오섰소. 그런데 웨 이렇게 되도록 두었소. 진작 대리고 오지?』
『돈이 있어야조니까...』<264>
『지금은 있소?』
『없읍니다. 그저 살려만 주시면 그거야 제가 벌어서 갚지오. 그걸 안 갚겠읍니까!』
『다른 큰 병원에 가 보시우...』
방서방은 이렇게 병원 집 문깐으로만 한나절을 돌아다니다가 그냥 다리밑으로 돌아오고 말었다. 방서방은 또 배가 고팟다. 그러나 앓은 것을 혼자 두고 단 한 거름이 나가지지 않었다. 그래도 저녁때가 되어서는 그냥 밤을 새일 수는 없어 보지 않으리라는 듯이 눈을 딱 감고 일어서 나왔던 것이다.
방서방이 얼마 만에 찬밥 몇 줄을 얻어먹고, 불야불야 돌아왔을 때는 날이 아주 어두었다. 다리 밑은 캄캄한데 한참 디려다 보니 아이는 자리에서 나와 언맨 땅에 목을 느려트리고 흐득흐득 느끼었다. 끄러 안고 다리 밖으로 나가 보니 경련이 일어나 눈을 뒤집어 쓰고 잇는 것이었다.
『죽을 테면 진작 죽어라! 고약한 년! 네 년이 이걸 버리고 가 얼마나 잘 되겠니...』
방서방은 몇번이나
『어서 죽어라!』
하고 아이를 밀어 던지었다가도 얼른 다시 끄러당겨 드려다 보군 했다. 그럴 때마다 아이의 숨소리는 자꾸 가뻐만 갔다.
그러나 야속한 것은 잠, 어느 때쯤 되었을까 깜박 잠이 들었다가 놀라 깨였을 제는 그 동안이 잠시 같었으나 주위에는 큰 변화가 생기었다. 날이 환하게 새이고 아이에게서는 그 가뿌게 일어나던 숨소리가 뚝 끄처 있었다. 겨우 겨드랑 밑에만 미온이 남었을 뿐, 그 불덩어리 같던 얼굴과 손발은 어느 틈에 언 생선처럼 싸늘하였다.
봄이 왔다. 그렇게 방서방을 춥게 굴던 겨울은 다 지나가고 그 대신 방서방을 슬프게 더 구는 봄이 왔다. 진달레와 개나리 꽃가지들은 전차마다 자동차마다 젊은 새악시 들처럼 오락가락하고, 남산과 창경원엔 사구라꽃이 구름처럼 핀 때었다. 무딘 힘줄로만 얼기설기한 방서방의 가슴에도 그 고향, 그 딸, 그 안해를 생각하기에는 너무나 슬픈 시인이 되게 하는 때였다.
하루 아침, 그날 따라 재수는 잊어 식전바람에 일본사람의 짐을 지고 남산정 막바지까지 가서 어렵지 않게 50전 한*이 들어왔다. 불이나케 술집을 찾아 나려오느라니 일본 집 뜰 안 마다 가지가 휘여지게 열린 사구라 꽃송이, 그는 그림을 구경하듯<265> 멍하니서서 바라보았다. 불현듯 고향 생각이 난 것이었다.
『우리가 심은 사구라 나무도 저렇게 피었으려니... 동네가 온통 꽃투성이려니...』
그때 마침 일본 여자 하나가 꽃 그늘에서 거닌다가 방서방과 눈이 마주쳤다. 방서방은 무슨 죄나 지은 듯이 움츨히고 돌아섰다. 꽃밭 같이 빛나는 그 젊은 여자의 얼굴! 방서방은 찌르르하고 가슴을 진동시키는 무엇을 느끼며 나려왔다. 위선 단골집으로 가서 얼근한 술국에 곱배기로 두어잔 드리켰다. 그리고 늙스구레한 주모와 몇 마디 농담까지 주거니 받거니 하 다 나서니, 세상은 슬프다면 온통 슬픈 것도 같고 즐겁다면 온통 즐거운 것 같기도 했다.
그러나 술만 깨이면 역시 세상은 견딜 수 없이 슬픈 세상이었다.
『정칠놈의 세상 같으니!』
하고 아무대나 주저 앉어 다리를 뻣고 울고 싶었다.
