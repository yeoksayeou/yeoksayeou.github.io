Author: Various (趙明熙, 卞榮魯, 林和, 毛允淑, 金起林, 李秉岐, 異河潤, 洪思容, 파인)
Article Type: 시 (Poetry)

Spring Lawn Field

Posŏk Cho Myŏnghŭi

Mother, please listen a moment
To the story of that twilight.
Darkness peers between the woods,
And the sound of the stream grows ever fainter.
Even the trees, every one,
It is time for them all to offer prayers.

Mother, please listen a moment,
Hold my hand and please listen a moment.
Beneath that moon, in the chestnut tree,
The sound of ripe nuts falling can be heard.
It brings news that the universe has borne a new son.
Let us walk respectfully to welcome the new guest.<186>

Nongae

Such'o Pyŏn Yŏngno

Sacred anger
Is deeper than religion,
Burning passion
Is stronger than love.
Ah, greener than the string bean flower,
Upon that wave,
Bluer than the poppy flower,
Let that "heart" flow.

Her beautiful arched eyebrows
Held high and trembling,
Her pomegranate-like lips
Kissed "death."
Ah, greener than the string bean flower,
Upon that wave,
Redder than the poppy flower,
Let that "heart" flow.

The flowing river water
Will be blue forever and ever;
Your flower-like soul,
How could it not be red?
Ah, greener than the string bean flower,
Upon that wave,
Redder than the poppy flower,
Let that "heart" flow.

The One I Cannot See While Awake

The one I cannot see while awake, I hoped to see in a dream,
Crossing dream-like green passes, I did cross,
But even the dream trembled and shook,
The longed-for you seemed near, yet far.

Ah, slipping where it is not slippery,
Between you and me, ten thousand *ri* have separated us.
Your beautiful face I cannot see again,
Fainter than a fading old dream.<187>

Suni at the Crossroads

Im Hwa

Oh, the snowstorm like a truck (*torakku*)
Runs through the streets!

Well, good, isn't this exactly Chongno crossroads?
Quickly, you and I, like lightning, grasp hands
And to plan the next task,
Let's enter the dark alley with the remaining comrades.
Seeking your boy, and also the brave youth
Who is the lover of all working women...
And so, with endless new preparations
And plans,
Spend your young days.<188>

A Wife's Wish

Mo Yunsuk

In the dense forest, the insects' chirping is careful,
In the distant sky, clear yet misty,
A voice is heard saying to mind the sandal straps,
It's hard to wake him who sleeps soundly.

But this is one of a wife's small sentiments;
What pain can compare to the thought of sending him away?
The clamorous sound rushing like smoke,
Our castle gate's evening sunlight trembles.

Oh, can there be a sending wife without heart-aching tears?
As he steps out majestically for the whole world,
His strong will, powerful force stretched across the entire sky,
Ah, how can I block his path with the affection of wife and child?

The sound of countless horseshoes splashing on the river water,
The shouts of the clamoring crowd rise high;
Rise, mount your horse, and gallop like an arrow!
Oh, I shall receive the blood-stained hem of your garment from atop the pagoda.

Firefly

A body entered the forest on a moonless night,
A single blue light upon the river of tears.
This heart, embracing a firefly and wandering,
When will this lonely flow ever end?
(July, Showa 8 [1933])

Boatman

On the clear river of my inner heart,
I float a pearl boat,
Parting the golden waves,
I serve him as the boatman.

The pearl boat on the clear heart
Was not meant for him,
But silently, he, upon my heart's river,
Without a pole, gently flows.<189>

The Fields Call Us

Kim Kirim

The frivolous birds, under the green roof of the poplar trees,
Open their eyes and sing busily.
The red breast of the sea spits up the burning sun.
The stars ride the clouds and fly away.

The river wind, herald of the morning, shakes awake the dreams of the young birds in the forest.
I throw the blood-flowing corpse of 'night' embraced in my arms onto the room floor,
And rush out towards our daylight, like infinite ambition.

(I knock on my wife's door.)
'Darling, please wake up quickly.'
Shall we not also drive the livestock to the forest?
Our good friend—the Sun—how impatiently must he be waiting by the riverside?
(My arm pointed past the wall, over the hill, across the river.)

Soon the birds will rise to the middle of the high sky and hold a concert.
The old wind, stroking the fur of the calf on the hill, will love their songs.
The small flowers will eagerly await a 'kiss' facing the Sun.

(I looked up at the sky and spread both arms.)
And darling—
Between that vast sky and earth, what small flowers shall we be?
What devoted birds shall we be?<190>

Sŏkkuram Grotto

Karam Yi Pyŏnggi

Crossing one pass, then another pass, counting the passes,
Crossing over T'oham Mountain, gazing at the East Sea,
Returning at dusk, I do not know the rush of the path, path.

Seeing and seeing again, here is Sŏkkuram Grotto,
Over the rugged pass, the winding, turning road,
With short breaths and quick steps, I come and come again.<191>

Water Mill

Yi Hayun

On the endlessly turning wheel of the water mill,
If I hang my memories, one leaf at a time,
As they submerge in the water, emerge, and turn,
Countless memories flutter on each leaf.

The wheel turns endlessly, crying out,
But my heart seeks the bygone days,
Giving only tears and sighs.
....................................

The old miller's hair is white,
What does his powerless gaze seek?
Each time the pestle pounds in the mortar,
The water flows, making a loud noise.

Song of the Blind Beggar

Over mountains, across waters, our distant countryside,
Leaving behind a one-room hut, it's been so long since I left.
Now my dream, my painful dream,
Shimmers on the hateful asphalt (aesŭp'alt'ŭ),
But I still have arms and legs, my mouth too can speak.

But my eyes, I just can't bear to keep them open,
It's been over ten years since they closed on their own.
Closing them is comfortable, but all the images that rise in my head,
Those hateful sounds that reach my ears,
My chest feels torn, my heart is tormented, I cannot live.

So I want to leave this place quickly,
But if I leave, is there a place to go? What's the use if I go alone?
On the ice-cold asphalt, I dream and pass the night.
Still, what I believe in are my limbs, my mouth too has words to say.
If only glad tidings reach my ears, my eyes too will open.<192>

I Am a King

Nojŏk Hong Sayong

That night too was a moonlit night like this,
A hazy moon hung, and on the back hill, an owl hooted, a night it was,
Mother was telling my sad story,
Then sighed deeply without reason,
And quickly bowed her face, which seemed to be smiling.<193>

Songhua River Boat Song

P'ain

In the dawn sky, cloud sheets fly.
Heave-ho, heave-ho, row quickly, let's go, this boat!
Is it only clouds that fly?
My heart flies too.

Looking back, is the homeland a thousand *ri* away?
Heave-ho, heave-ho, row quickly, let's go, this boat!
The path come is a thousand *ri*,
The path to go is ten thousand *ri*.

I left the mountains, but did I leave my affections?
Heave-ho, heave-ho, row quickly, let's go, this boat!
Though the body drifts,
Will not the spirit go?

Here is the Songhua River, the river water weeps.
Heave-ho, heave-ho, row quickly, let's go, this boat!
Does only the river water weep?
A true man weeps along too.

Blue Donkey

Why did you stop on the road you must travel?
Only the willow by the well
Is left scattered in disarray,
The blue donkey is leaving.

That cloud on Kalmi Peak,
Has it forgotten how to become rain?
The blue donkey cries and cries,
Disappearing into the mountains.

Living in the Same Castle Town

Living in the same castle town,
I can only see you in dreams,
Alas, even that dream,
Does it exist only on my side?

Romance

When the wild geese cried,
There must have been human traces,<194>
Surely they didn't cry at the moon?
There must have been romance in the reed field.

Over the Mountain to Namch'on

1.
Over the mountain to Namch'on (South Village),
Who lives there, that
Every year the spring breeze
Comes from the South?

In April when flowers bloom,
The scent of azaleas,
In May when barley ripens,
The smell of barley.

Will it not carry
At least one of these?
When the south wind blows from Namch'on,
I like it so.

2.
Over the mountain to Namch'on,
Who lives there, that
That sky, that color,
Is so beautiful?

On the wide field of golden grass,
A swarm of swallowtail butterflies,
In the willow grove's streamlet,
The song of the skylark.

Will it not bring the sound
Of at least one of these?
When the south wind blows from Namch'on,
I like it so.

3.
Over the mountain to Namch'on,
There is a pear tree,
And beneath the pear blossom,
Someone is said to stand.

With longing thoughts,
I climb the mountain pass,
But hidden by clouds,
She is not visible.

A faint song, breaking off and resuming,
Carried on the wind,
Is gently heard.<195>

**Summary**

This collection presents a diverse range of Korean poetry from 1935, published in the magazine *Samch'ŏlli*. The poems cover themes of nature ("Spring Lawn Field," "Over the Mountain to Namch'on"), love and longing ("The One I Cannot See While Awake," "Romance"), historical patriotism (Pyŏn Yŏngno's "Nongae"), exile and displacement ("Songhua River Boat Song"), social commentary reflecting urban life and hardship ("Suni at the Crossroads," "Song of the Blind Beggar"), personal introspection ("Water Mill," "Firefly"), and modernist sensibilities ("The Fields Call Us"). From a historical perspective, the collection is valuable as it showcases the varied literary landscape of colonial Korea. It includes poems expressing nationalistic sentiment (Nongae, the displaced longing in the Songhua River poem implicitly referencing Manchuria), modernist experiments influenced by Western trends (Im Hwa, Kim Kirim), alongside more traditional lyrical styles, reflecting the complex cultural and political environment of the time.
