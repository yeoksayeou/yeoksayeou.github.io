쥐이야기
						民村 李箕永 (Minch'on Yi Kiyŏng)

A chilly early winter—perhaps it was midnight? All around was silent; people were likely wandering in dreamland. This was truly the time for mice everywhere. Above the ceiling paneling of the rich Mr. Kim's house in Yangjimal, the mice also began their activity. This is what the father mouse said:
『Well! Shall we race here? Where shall we go hunting? I'm feeling a bit peckish—』
『Let's go hunting! Let's go hunting!』
Urged by the baby mice and the mother mouse, they set out for the kitchen to hunt for food.

This mouse family had recently moved to this house from Sudol's house across the village. Everyone called the father mouse Kwakchwi, meaning he was fearsome like a Kwakchwi (likely a type of hawk or fierce bird, used metaphorically). Indeed, with his protruding, sharp eyes and thick, long whiskers sticking straight out, his large body, about the size of a puppy, could leap over ordinary ditches, and his strong, embedded jade-like teeth seemed capable of gnawing through even iron. Once, he had startled his companions by grabbing the whiskers of a cat dozing in a sunny spot, but more recently, he had peed on the face of the sleeping rich Mr. Kim in broad daylight, which became a famous story. So, his companions feared him even more, and Kwakchwi's wife boasted of having found a good husband.

Kwakchwi's family consisted of his aging wife and four children, a son and daughters. Although he had married daughters and sons living separately, many among them had died, killed by cats or caught in mousetraps.

Upon moving, they immediately dug a burrow under the shed floor and tunneled everywhere—to the storehouse, the cowshed. Thus, they could steal plenty of rice and other grains, but occasionally craving tasty dishes, they embarked on kitchen hunts like this. Once, they went out before mealtime and were spotted by Mr. Kim's hawk-nosed eldest daughter-in-law<196>, nearly getting soundly beaten with a fire poker.

Not only that, but yesterday, Kwakchwi went up to the inner floor room (anmaru) in broad daylight and was discovered by Mr. Kim. Just as he was about to be caught by the tail near the inner courtyard, he let out a squeak—and bit Mr. Kim's hand. Fortunately, Mr. Kim let go, and he escaped. Hearing this, the wife mouse's eyes widened in alarm, worrying greatly, suggesting they should only hunt at midnight or else just stay here and eat grain. What would they do if he got caught? But Kwakchwi scoffed,
『What, it's fine! Just watch me live until I get caught!』
He boasted confidently. Indeed, Kwakchwi was robust, but his wife was always frail like an old woman. Thus, the son and daughter mice admired their father's bravery even more. At that moment, the daughter, smiling brightly, came and nestled on Kwakchwi's lap, saying:
『Father! When I grow up and have a daughter, I'll find a son-in-law just like you!』
The mother mouse, who had been absently chewing rice grains, heard this and laughed, "Sae! Sae! Sae!"

When Kwakchwi suggested moving from Sudol's house across the village to this house, the rest of the family had all opposed. It was daunting to think of safely traveling such a distance to Yangjimal with young ones, but more than that, they worried that such a rich house would surely have cats or mousetraps, meaning they might not live out their natural lifespan—first, the wife nagged him. But Kwakchwi stubbornly insisted.
『Being so scared is like carrying a fragile egg and not being able to cross a threshold. Who knows if this house won't catch fire tonight? First off, we can't stay in this house because those people barely have enough to eat themselves, starving as they are, so where would they get anything for us? Besides, isn't it wrong for us to deplete the food stores of a poor family? It's better to go to a rich house where grain is piled like mountains, needlessly taking from them. That way, even if we take food, it won't be pitiful, and they'll still have plenty left over after giving to us. Women just don't know their place and worry needlessly. Come on! No more talk, let's go!』
Persuaded by the words of this reliable head of the household, they finally agreed.

Indeed, after moving, the first good thing was having full bellies. True, there were scary incidents with the eldest daughter-in-law's fire poker and the recent encounter with Mr. Kim, but in return, they could get tasty dishes and bits of meat. However, one thing slightly displeased the wife mouse: this house had many companion mice bustling about, so Kwakchwi occasionally came home late from his philandering. But now, growing old, what good would jealousy do<197>? So she deliberately pretended not to know and turned a blind eye. Indeed, she now, without this thought or that, just wanted to eat her fill, sleep warmly, and watch her little ones grow plump and strong...

The children wanted meat, so Kwakchwi now went into the inner kitchen to steal some. He sensed that since people had been coming and going all day and the house was bustling inside and out, there must be some delicious food. Indeed, upon entering, the kitchen smelled strongly of meat, enough to numb the nose, but everything seemed to be put away in iron pots, making them hard to open because the heavy iron lids were in place. Thinking this trip might be in vain, Kwakchwi felt very annoyed.

About to turn back empty-handed, he somehow felt like looking into the room, so he found the hole leading from under the shelf (salgang) into the side room (kolbang) and slipped through.
The sliding door of the side room was slightly ajar, and in the inner room (anpang), a lamp burned brightly. Rich Mr. Kim sat alone, currently counting money.
Where had that gadabout wife gone wandering again? From the room across, the sound of clothes being beaten with ironing sticks went *ttokttak ttokttak*, and through it, the squealing laughter of young women, their stomachs too full, letting loose could be heard. Mr. Kim sat wearing an ox-horn-like cap (soppulgwan), and before him, piles of paper money bundles, silver coins, and copper coins were heaped high. It seemed the bustle today was because he had sold a lot of rice.

Suddenly, he coughed once, *khollok!*, and then a series of coughs followed. Unfortunately, there was no chamber pot in the lower room, so clearing his throat—*khak khak*—he opened the front sliding door and went out. While he stood at the edge of the wooden floor (maru) spitting phlegm for a while, Kwakchwi crept quietly inside, grabbed one of the larger bundles of paper money, and quickly fled.

The family, eagerly waiting, perhaps hoping he would bring back meat, saw Kwakchwi enter carrying a bundle of mere paper and their eyes widened in disappointment.
The daughter mouse popped out first and asked:
『Father! What happened to the meat? What is all that?』
『It's called money.』
『What's money for? Looking at these paper scraps?』
『Still, people believe that if they just have this, meat appears, rice appears, everything they want appears.』
『Ah? What magic do paper scraps have to make such things appear?』
『That's why it's strange; this is like the goblin's magic hat (dokkabi kamt'u) from old stories.<198>』
『Ah! So things just appear like that?』
『Yep! If you just give these, they offer up rice, meat, cloth—all those things for free.』
The daughter, who had been sitting with wide, curious eyes, still looking skeptical, asked,
『Paper scraps can't become rice or meat, so how can they exchange those things for this? Are there such fools in the world?—Giving away such goods for wastepaper scraps?』
She looked up at Kwakchwi.
『That's why they're foolish folk. Yet, fools like Sudol don't realize it. They don't know it's like a goblin's magic hat, created by cunning people to trick the simple-minded... These guys sit around stealing in broad daylight like this, yet they dare call us thieves! Isn't it funny! Giving the nickname "mouse thief" (鼠賊; sŏjŏk) to those who steal—their faces are truly shameless.』
『Really, why is Sudol's family so poor? Even though they farm every year?』
The mother mouse, who had been silently chewing rice until now, lifted her head sharply, glanced at her husband, and asked this. Kwakchwi stroked his whiskers gravely.
『It's because they lose everything to those who have this money. Because they are forced to exchange their goods for money.』
『So, now I see, we aren't the only thieves!』
The son mouse jumped up and down, pleased.
『Hey—among thieves, we are considered honest ones. And taking leftovers from a rich house like this, after they've eaten and used what they need, is not theft at all. It's because we, like them, are born creatures. But really, wife, why do you keep saying 'stealing'? That sounds bad—we don't steal, we 'take' food? Since they don't give it, we have no choice but to take it.』
『Alright, from now on, I'll say... not stealing... but taking!』
『Yes! Good. Now look at this foolish Sudol. Just this morning before the meal, he was bowing deeply to this old master (yŏnggamjangi), begging, "Sir! We're starving to death, please give us just ten mal of late-harvest rice! Surely, I won't fail to repay you?" He pleaded desperately. Honestly! Is that right? That sturdy fellow begging from a house like Sudol's? Truly! It's utterly ridiculous! Why? Can't he think of a way to take it by force? What, is he afraid of being caught? Getting caught would be better! He'd have clothes! He'd have food!』
Kwakchwi seemed to be getting angry, even though it was someone else's problem.
『Surrendering to wrongdoers only <199> encourages (助長; chojang) the wrongdoing. Expecting charity from such people is like waiting for a crow to become a white heron. If it comes to that, better to die by your own hand! Than to live such a miserable life—』
『Still, they say "no mind to steal even after starving for three days"—isn't that what Sudol said I heard sometime?—』
『Right! Right! It's good as long as you don't steal, even if you starve? How is that stealing? When you've done useful work for people but have nothing to eat, taking goods from a house that has plenty isn't stealing. It's taking the wages you earned! What? Then why go to the thief and beg for something with such pathetic words? Isn't that a filthier act than stealing? To beg for stolen goods!』
『True, Sudol's wife also came to the kitchen earlier to borrow rice!—』
『"Ma'am! We haven't been able to cook for three days now. The children and I are freezing and starving to death, please just lend us three doe of rice as an act of charity! Then just two doe—ah! just one doe..." she pleaded desperately with all her might, but the hawk-nosed eldest daughter-in-law just sharply scolded her—"Where would we get rice? Go away now—who told you to starve! Why come here and make a scene? Morning and evening, inside and out—go away now!" Chased away like that, she left crying buckets of tears, holding an empty gourd bowl. Lately, in this cold, wearing only torn thin trousers (huch'kojai)...』
Hearing this, Kwakchwi snorted 『Et!』 and fumed, hissing!
『That woman is no pushover either. Someday, I need to pee on her face too. Wife, you should pee on her too—If they can't be like us, humph... I mean, take food or do whatever, what kind of foolishness is that! Tsk! Pathetic humans! Ignorant humans! We, by nature, cannot resort to such actions, but why can't they, being fellow humans? Why not? Someone like that Mr. Kim would shatter with just one blow! If such things happened here and there, those guys wouldn't dare make a peep and would throw open their granaries... But since everyone just begs before them, such fellows only become more arrogant.』
『Truly, Sudol's family is pitiful. Ah! We are just beasts, yet we have never starved even for a season, but starving for three or four days—how can they live like that?』
『And yet, husband and wife stick together and just keep having babies. Sudol's wife is pregnant again, they say. Her belly is huge like a mountain; in her condition, why on earth would she want to have another one? Tsk! Fools!』
『Still, maybe they live for that pleasure!<200>』
『Pleasure? Who knows, maybe even hardship becomes a taste acquired!』
『Among humans, could there be families worse off than Sudol's?』
『Certainly. In Chosŏn alone, there must be several million.』
『Ah! And still they claim they are superior? Those humans?』
Suddenly, the daughter mouse jumped up and down, asking this.
『Of course. They say, "Among all things between Heaven and Earth, only humans are most noble (天地之間 萬物之衆 唯人이 最貴)."』
『What does that all mean?』
『It means between sky and earth, humans are the most precious.』
『Oh my, how ridiculous! A human hell teeming with hungry ghosts?—』
The mother mouse and son mouse again laughed, "Sae! Sae! Sae!"

Kwakchwi struck the ground once with his front paw and spoke again.
『If there were someone like that among us—one guy hoarding everything while everyone else starves! Would we let that guy last even a single day? He'd be shattered immediately!』
『True, I wouldn't leave such a guy alone either.』
『Right—Me neither!』
The baby mice also chimed in heatedly.

Kwakchwi, looking affectionate, hugged them close and gave this solemn advice. They listened intently.
『Hey—my dear little ones! When you go out into the world later, you must be alert. As human society becomes harsher to live in, our lives also become that much more difficult. To survive somehow, you must have strength. Without strength, you cannot live. If you cannot live, you are as good as dead even while alive. Sudol's house is like that. Humans call such powerless life "incompetent" (無能; munŭng). "Incompetence" may not be evil, but lacking strength is a fact. But without strength, there is no life, so ultimately, incompetence must also be seen as a type of sin. It is the sin of failing to preserve one's own life. Even if evil, if there is strength, there is life. Where there is life, there is consequently goodness, beauty, and truth. —Because this strength can give birth to good knowledge... Therefore, this strength! This strength! You must properly cultivate this strength. Look! Aren't the birds flying in the sky and the single flower blooming in the field all expressions of strength? Aren't they symbols of strength? Where there is life, there is strength. But don't misunderstand. Strength must only be used with good knowledge—it must never be selfish. Little ones! Did you listen well? Hmm?』<201>
『Yes! I want to have lots of strength like Father and become a heroine someday!』
『I want to become a brave hero too!』
Kwakchwi patted their bottoms *********. (likely affectionate gesture, e.g., 'patted their bottoms gently.')
『Right, let's count the money! How much is it! The old master's stomach must be aching. One—two—three... Ten bills in total. Well—tonight, let's sleep on money too. Smooth and shiny, this is truly a magnificent room (changgwanbang). Here! Wife, you take one to sleep on—and let's make a screen like this, and give the rest to Sudol!』
『Yes! That's a good idea! Then give mine too.』
『What, this is plenty. If we give too much, we might see him faint again. Would such a person have ever touched this kind of money in his life! Then let's forget the screen and give him six bills.』
『But how will you get it there?』
The wife seemed worried about the delivery.
『What, I'll just quickly run over and give it!』
Saying this, Kwakchwi rolled the bills tightly, held them in his mouth, and went straight out. The wife mouse urged him to be careful and return safely.

Kwakchwi dashed like an arrow to Sudol's house. Inside the room, dimly lit by a flickering lamp, the family lay sprawled out from hunger. He threw the paper money onto the floor and immediately left.
When Kwakchwi returned home, his wife and children hadn't slept, waiting for his return. They had shelled a large pile of rice for him. So, they had a good midnight snack and settled down comfortably on their new bedding made of paper money.—

The next day, an unexpected 60 wŏn appeared in Sudol's house, while at Mr. Kim's house, the entire household was turned upside down because 100 wŏn had been lost.................. -(The End)- 22, 26 At Nagwŏn-dong<202>

**Summary**

The story follows a family of mice led by the bold father, Kwakchwi, who move from a poor house to a rich man's house for better foraging. Kwakchwi justifies taking food from the rich, contrasting their own "honest" taking for survival with the exploitative nature of human wealth accumulation represented by money, which he calls a "goblin's hat" used by the clever to trick the simple. Witnessing the rich man's indifference to the starving neighbor Sudol, Kwakchwi steals money from the rich man and leaves most of it for Sudol's family. The story uses the mice's perspective to critique social inequality, the nature of theft versus necessity, and the hypocrisy of human society's values, particularly highlighting the plight of the poor under the wealthy.

From a historical perspective, this story, published in 1935 colonial Korea, is interesting for its sharp social commentary. It implicitly criticizes the vast economic disparities and the perceived injustice of the social structure, where the wealthy hoard resources while the poor starve. Kwakchwi's justification for "taking" rather than "stealing" from the rich, and his view of money as a tool of exploitation, can be read as a critique of capitalist or colonial economic systems and a veiled expression of resistance or desire for redistribution, framed safely within an animal fable.

쌀쌀한 첫겨을-아마 정밤ㅅ중이나 되엿슬가? 사방은 괴괴하니 사람들은 모다 꿈나라로 허매일 판이다. 이 때야말노 어듸던지 쥐의 세상이다. 양지말 김부자집 반자 위에도 쥐들이 활동하기 시작하엿다. 이것은 애비쥐가 하는 말이엿다.
『자! 여긔서 다름박질을 할까? 어듸로 산냥을 나갈까? 속이 좀 출출한데-』
『산양가요! 산양가요!』
하고 색기쥐, 어미쥐가 조르는 바람에 그들은 부억으로 먹이 산냥을 나갓다.
지금 이 쥐의 일가족은 건너 말 수돌이 집에서 살다가 이 집으로 요사이 이사를 왓다. 애비쥐를 곽쥐라고 모다 불으는데 그것으 곽쥐갓치 무섭다는 뜻이엿다. 과연, 그의 톡뼈지고 서긔나는 눈하고 굵고 긴-수염이 쭉-쭉-뻐친 것이라던지 강아지만한 큰 몸똥이로 여간 또랑은 껑충-껑충-뛰여 넘으며 그의 탄탄하게 박힌 옥니로는 무쇠라도 사그릴 수 잇는 그런 힘이 잇섯다. 한번은 양지쪽에서 졸고 잇는 고양이의 수염을 잡어채서 동무들을 놀내준 일도 잇지마는 요전에는 또 대낫에 낫잠자는 김부자의 얼골에다 오줌을 내깔녀서 그게 유명한 이야기꺼리가 되엿다. 그래 동무들은 그를 더욱 무서워하고 곽쥐 마누라는 남편 잘 어든 자랑을 하엿다.
곽쥐의 식구는 늙어가는 마누라하고 아들 딸의 네 식구엿다. 하긴 시집간 딸들하고 따로 사는 아들이 만치마는 그들 중에는 고양이한테 죽은 자도 잇고 쥐돗에 치여서 죽은 자도 만헛다.
