Article Title: 除夜雪 (New Year's Eve Snow)

New Year's Eve Snow
						Wŏlt'an (月灘)

Snow—is falling, the night is quiet
Following the wind, along these still night (夜) streets
Endlessly, aimlessly, I wander.
People call this night
New Year's Eve (除夜), they say.

Softly, softly, one flake, two flakes
Ten, hundred, thousand, million flakes
Enveloping the whole sky, the whole earth,
Whitely, whitely, purifying. <280>

Sacred color
Pure color!
When footsteps crunch, crunch,
My heart, oh, how subtly it flutters.
Ah, this night today,
They call it New Year's Eve (除夜).

Does the cock crow?
The sound of bells (鍾) faintly rises through the snow—
This cleanliness, this sacredness,
How can one bear it!
Forever and ever, without rest,
Night after night, I wish to tread this path.

Snow—is white, the night is quiet
Following the wind, along these lovely dawn streets
Endlessly, aimlessly, I wander.
People call this night
New Year's Eve (除夜), they say. <281>

**Summary**

This poem by Wŏlt'an describes the quiet beauty and holiness of snow falling on New Year's Eve (Jeya). The speaker wanders the snow-covered streets, feeling a sense of purification and awe at the scene. The crunching sound of footsteps, the whiteness of the snow, and the faint sound of bells create a sacred atmosphere, leading the speaker to wish for this pure experience to continue endlessly. From a historical perspective, the poem's strong emphasis on purification (淨化) and the desire for a clean, continuous path on New Year's Eve might subtly reflect a deeper yearning for national or personal renewal amidst the difficulties of the colonial period. Historians might find the poem's blend of aesthetic appreciation and potential underlying sentiment of longing for purity and change noteworthy.
