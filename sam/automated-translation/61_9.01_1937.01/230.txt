Article Title: 「劇藝術協會」에 보내는 公開狀 (An Open Letter to the "Theater Arts Association")

An Open Letter to the "Theater Arts Association"
						Sin Puch'ul

As a writer who concerns himself with the theater world in a manner akin to reading when it rains and farming when it's sunny, I intend to frankly write down a fragment of my impressions after watching the Theater Research Association's (劇硏, Kŭgyŏn) performances up to the 11th one.

It has been said that new theater troupes are art-oriented, life-oriented, guiding, enlightening, conscientious, and truthful, while commercial theater troupes are profit-oriented, pandering, flattering, deceitful, and nothing but fraudulent. In reality, while there's some truth to this, there are also many points where this isn't necessarily the case for either side.
This can be seen as the result of the deformed development brought about by the friction between the actor's own inner conscience and external control, and the disadvantages of the theater troupe's own subjective conditions and objective circumstances. This is truly a stark specimen of the times, trembling in the barren wilderness of transitional Chosŏn. This is indeed one aspect of the characteristic nature in the developmental process of Chosŏn's theatrical culture <170> that cannot be casually overlooked.
The new theater troupes laid out splendid arguments and blew trumpets of condemnation, while the commercial theater troupes, unable to contain their ill feelings, fired back with mortars.
However, no matter how much one scolds the commercial theater troupes wandering in dark nightmares, and no matter how dazzlingly the new theater troupes rose up holding torches, this merely signifies finding a stern social awakening observable in the dialectically developing historical process. In reality, how much did the theatrical effects of the plays staged by the new theater troupes, as they repeated their performances, truly contribute to the Chosŏn populace in a strictly meaningful sense?
The commercial theater troupes had practical stage experience but failed to clearly recognize social consciousness, thus failing to produce good theater. The new theater troupes grasped the ideology of the era strongly but lacked practical stage experience, thus also failing to produce good theater.
Either way, the hearts of the Chosŏn audience, lacking good theater, were exceedingly desolate.

A thousand literary theories about theater are fine, but the urgent general demand is to be shown the artistic reality of the stage, which possesses a comprehensive pressure.
This is an era of practical ability, not propaganda. Action over words. Chosŏn is now on the verge of causing a nightmare due to empty bluster consisting only of words without action. Without the emergence of great technical figures, there will be no way for the new theater troupes, still in their youth, to conquer the commercial theater troupes in their adulthood. The breakthrough to the traditional audience, the absolute majority throughout the entire country supporting the commercial troupes, will end in fantasy.
I want to emphasize once again that genuine theatrical activity must first plant its roots on a popular foundation.

There are hundreds of commercial theater troupes throughout Chosŏn, numbering over 3,000, which have never once come to Kyŏngsŏng. For those unaware of this actual situation, it is indeed a fact that cannot but cause astonishment, and they have comfortably resided outside our sphere of concern until today.
Of course, I am not someone who fully approves of the current theatrical activities of the commercial troupes. However, even if they are destined for inevitable decline due to historical development, we are not fatalists <171> who entrust all solutions to time and wait with our chins cupped in our hands. Therefore, we cannot help but eventually address the theatrical activities of the commercial troupes. The existence of these commercial troupes, spreading their dark wings over the entirety of Chosŏn, cannot but be a matter of great concern for the new theater movement activists. If their existence were to be dismissed as beneath consideration, it would truly be a careless attitude, failing even to grasp the sociological study of theater.
To silently overlook and console oneself about all their excesses is something the living trends will not forgive. Moreover, even those ruined souls overthrown by the fervent consciousness of the times fail to perceive the tragic future path of social elimination. Instead, they merely drift emotionally in optimistic illusions, pursuing social inaction and personal pleasure. This truly surpasses lamentation, making it impossible to suppress feelings of pity, and is the height of regret.
However, we must not forget the various realities surrounding them and the populace that accepts and cheers for them. The problem lies here.

If we try to find the roots of the commercial theater troupes' present state in their fundamental social environment, the sorrow and suffering of their actors, viewed from a human perspective, are truly pitiful and tragic.
We know well that whenever they discovered their own shabby appearance reflected before their sharp conscience, their breaths must have risen with painful human resentment and indignant social curses. However, whenever they felt the truth and tried to cultivate themselves, the poor society did not provide them with a nurturing institution to open a path for study. Instead, it only mocked them with merciless contempt. Where was the path for these unfortunate ones who received no benefits? Only in self-abandonment did they become extremely exhausted bodies. Since the belly is also an enemy, they became powerless humans who lost the resilience to even resist unreasonable exploitation within the commercial structures controlled by the moneyed class, which forced absolute obedience. Of course, it's not that there weren't progressive individuals among them burning with true conscientious passion and artistic desire, but should one perhaps call it a mixed brigade of truly star-crossed lives?
In a sense, one might think these commercial troupes are a mirror reflecting one aspect of Chosŏn. All this misfortune is Chosŏn's misfortune, our collective responsibility that cannot be shifted onto anyone else.
However, we must have the composure not to confuse the natural human aspect with the social aspect. No matter how much one does something for the sake of eating, we must also have the intelligence <172> to distinguish between the problem of livelihood and the problem of survival.
We always speak of Chosŏn's special circumstances, but unless we think once more about the fact that such breathtaking realities of life and society constitute Chosŏn, we cannot help but doubt the true meaning of the phrase "special circumstances."

Just as there is the misfortune in the literary world where one can often find those who, after reading only a few literary books, suffer from precocious senility and suddenly act like critics, there is also the unpleasantness in the theater world of seeing presumptuous intruders who, without even basic knowledge of stage practice, read a pamphlet or two and instantly become legislators and judges of the theatrical world.
Many cry out to break free from stagnation, but none offer concrete methods for doing so. Therefore, even if such criticisms follow one after another, there is absolutely no reason for even the slightest echo to arise. Criticism that lacks critical elements is merely idle fantasy play, nothing more than an outburst of unsound behavior. These shameless individuals lacking prudence are surely either young masters of a new ideological sect representing aristocratic, landlord psychology or outdated servants of idealism.
Theater has an artistic aspect, but it cannot be separated from its ideological aspect, nor can its commercial aspect be discarded. This three-sided form can be seen as the complete shape of theater. Of course, the commercial aspect I speak of does not mean reckless hedonism but rather principled entertainment. At the same time, regarding finances, it refers not to commercialization for profit but to the establishment of an economic enterprise.
As with literary criticism, theatrical criticism too can only become a valuable document for the field if it adopts the most generous and kind guiding attitude to broaden the general public's appreciative perspective and advance theater more socially. A preventive attitude that hinders theatrical development and leads it into a dilemma with childishly vague arguments should be cautioned against. Before emphasizing subjectivity, first calmly gaze at reality. One must know Chosŏn; one must undertake human cultivation.
A critic's single ripple can place them on the scales weighing great merit and demerit. The saying that a society without proper critique finds it difficult to achieve proper development is already pre-sociological common sense.

Although it might sound excessively vague and ambiguous, one could say the commercial troupes tried to use theater as "sugar," the New Construction (新建設, Sin Kŏnsŏl - likely another group or movement) tried to use it as "medicine," but the Theater Research Association (劇硏, Kŭgyŏn) tries to use theater as "rice (밥)." <173> (However, this word "rice" naturally does not mean professionalization.)
When considering the ultimate goal of theater from a social consciousness perspective, whether Kŭgyŏn's intention to use theater as "rice" is seen as a proper attitude is a matter to be determined by each observer's position. However, since the developmental limit of the Chosŏn new theater movement is already constrained, the result constrained within the expressed scope is that theater can be nothing more than "rice." It is an almost a priori fact that it cannot bring about any further effect.
Although inferior to "medicine" but better than "sugar," the necessity of the nutritional value of this "rice" is absolute. The crux of the matter, I believe, lies in whether one possesses the theatrical technique to cook that "rice" well.

It's already common sense, but if Chosŏn theater people have a weak understanding of the Chosŏn populace, no matter how skillfully the "rice" is cooked, if it doesn't suit the tastes and disposition of the Chosŏn people who must chew and ingest it, it will ultimately cause indigestion. Could there be anything more unkind? One must gain a clear and direct understanding of the Chosŏn populace historically, temporally, socially, and solely through the reality of life.
Precisely because it is a dark "night," one must light the "torch" of passion and desire to grasp the main current of the era, and under that bright light, the "rice" must be skillfully made. Herein lie stifling difficulties and doubts; herein lie sorrowful hesitation and lingering reluctance; herein lies the human inspiration derived from the bitter ascetic practice of the era.
Making theater—this massive entity that vitalizes life through poetry's poetry and art's art—is frighteningly difficult. Only with the outsider's detached perspective and magnanimity, knowing how to play the veritable "harp of the soul" after tasting such pioneering bitter cups, can one, I believe, produce excellent theater.

Watching the Kŭgyŏn's 11 performances up to now, I felt two great joys.
Since Kŭgyŏn's inception, I had always lamented its factually weak artistic development. Suddenly, I witnessed a leap forward in development so great it could almost be called a lie, like a miracle. Amidst the emotion flowing from the tears and sweat of the set designers and directors who toiled in the stage shadows, the passionate, conscientious, and truthful acting of the performing actors became surprisingly skilled and proficient, making it impossible to suppress a sense of wonder. This was the first joy. Seeing the packed audience and, moreover, their absolute <174> support and encouragement was the second joy.

There are many, many things I wish to write here, which I intend to address separately. However, one thing I must mention before moving on concerns Kim Kwangsŏp's (金광燮) piece titled "The Life of Translated Plays" in the theater arts publication "Kŭgyesul (劇藝術)" distributed to the public during the 11th Kŭgyŏn performance. He seems to point out that the reason the public dislikes translated plays is a lack of understanding that ignores the autonomy of foreign dramatic art. In my opinion, however, among Chosŏn people, there likely wasn't anyone who rejected the value of foreign plays' independent realm. Rather, wasn't it the generally inadequate translation techniques in the translated plays staged by Kŭgyŏn that caused the public's respect, understanding, and interest in translated plays to be obliterated?

From the beginning, I have not been one to oppose the staging of translated plays. Moreover, I doubt the mental state of those who don't hesitate to use extreme terms like "Euro-American poisoned fever patient" just because someone likes Western things. However, I could never welcome the translated plays staged by Kŭgyŏn until now. The reason, of course, lies entirely in the uncritical nature of the translation method.
It is well-known that translation is extremely difficult; there's even a saying that translation is creation. Furthermore, one cannot but say that translating emotional art belongs to the realm of impossibility.
How can the life emotions of peoples—whose every gesture, movement, word, and phrase are all historically, geographically, and culturally distinct—achieve complete identity and harmony with those of another? Especially in the art of theater, which prioritizes the extremely natural expression of life's realities?
Often, when foreign plays were translated and staged, the background, props, costumes, and makeup remained Western, while only the lines were delivered in Chosŏn language. Herein lies the contradiction. Making Chosŏn conversation happen in a Western life setting is akin to grafting a stone onto a tree. The unpleasantness caused by this irrational and unnatural pretext was beyond further description.
Moreover, the inadequacy of translated scripts arising from literal translation is a technique perfectly suited to kill the already clumsy actors' performances. The reason for what is called poor "stage breathing" (舞臺呼吸) is believed to originate here.
For example, suppose the original text contains the line:
(I hope you will live to be wiser)
If one translates this literally as:
"나는 바란다 네가 오래 살기를, 그것은 賢人이 되기까지 말이다!" <175>
No matter how much this might be called a conscientious attitude (?) trying to be faithful to the original work, such awkwardly translated words would likely not be quickly understood by the general Chosŏn audience, unless perhaps they were some clever literary youth. This is only too natural.
If one were to translate that line to be easily understood, how about simply something like:
"智覺좀 나거라!" (Get some sense!)

Borrowing the words of someone who once opposed staging translated plays: when one lacks one's own heritage, absorbing others' is an inevitable necessity and a natural act. However, considering what is gained by staging foreign plays, if it's for Kŭgyŏn's own education or training, that's one thing. But once it is publicly presented before the masses, given today's circumstances in Chosŏn, its nature changes. In other words, the general audience certainly lacks the leisure to acquire methods for appreciating foreign plays. Even if Kŭgyŏn didn't undertake such hardships, outstanding foreign works have mostly already been seen, or people will try to see them through the talking pictures shown at various permanent theaters. Who would want to experience twice the misfortune of becoming an audience member before such "translated plays," staged with immature acting on a poor stage? Consequently, the public's respect, understanding, and interest in foreign plays have diminished, becoming an insult to the original works. This is truly what is meant by unspeakable absurdity. One cannot help but think of the original authors weeping in their graves and force a bitter smile. Such harsh extreme words surely represent the circumstances of the time, do they not?

I now wish to make several requests of Kŭgyŏn.
I ask Kŭgyŏn to return to the point before theater itself. When Kŭgyŏn currently engages in theater, it truly needs to move from theoretical research to practical research, from abstract theoretical action to concrete practical action. Of course, this "practical" does not mean public performance. In other words, in the developmental process of moving from the preparation stage to the implementation stage, as a transitional procedure, I am asking you to devise the fundamental conditions for theater: a Chosŏn-style playwriting method, a Chosŏn-style directing method (including intonation and expression methods), and a Chosŏn-style set design method, etc.
One reason the new theater troupes view the commercial troupes' theater as non-theater is the point that the commercial troupes' directing methods are all in the Japanese mainland Shinpa (新派 - New School) style. If that's the case, <176> while the commercial troupes may have been doing theater with Shinpa-style directing methods, what style of directing method has the new theater troupe, Kŭgyŏn, been using to perform theater?
If Ch'ŏngch'unjwa (靑春座 - Youth Theater) is considered a commercial troupe, how much difference is there between the intonation, expression, and movement of the actors of the commercial troupe Ch'ŏngch'unjwa and the intonation, expression, and movement of the actors of Kŭgyŏn, which calls itself a new theater troupe?
I don't know how much difference there was in the social impact left by "A Doll's House" staged according to Kŭgyŏn's directing method versus "A Doll's House" staged according to a commercial troupe's directing method, but the distinction is not strikingly clear. In essence, one must realize again that theater cannot be done with conscience alone. At the same time, if Kŭgyŏn truly holds the establishment of a new theater culture in the true sense as its undying wish in a Chosŏn that lacks theater, shouldn't it have already possessed a distinct "Chosŏn-style directing method" as preparatory work planning for a hundred years of new theater, rather than boasting about the increasing number of performances?
My words may have overly focused on the formal aspect, but it is certainly not an attempt at formal reductionism in theater arts. It is merely enough, I believe, to consider the recklessness of a soldier on the battlefield who does not know how to use weapons.
The so-called 'professional's habit' (玄人の癖) or 'amateur's habit' (素人の癖) are essentially chronic, acquired afflictions created by "doing as one pleases" when there is no fixed directing method. They are "diseases" that simply cannot exist on a stage directed according to a clearly established directing method.
At this point, I harbor regret as I doubt the distance between the current new theater troupes and commercial troupes in Chosŏn.
Therefore, I trust and do not doubt that the gentlemen of Kŭgyŏn will re-examine, re-recognize, and make a fresh start regarding this.
Another point is to guarantee the livelihood of the actors. That one must eat to live is an exceedingly natural, universal fact of life common to all humankind. The saying that there is no art separate from life should not just be lines written in the plays recited by actors; it is the actors' own words. If theater is not done as a dilettantish hobby or emotional pastime, then a Kŭgyŏn member cannot have separate main and side occupations. It is not unreasonable that people call Kŭgyŏn the "Gentlemen Amateur Theater Troupe."
Enter into theater! Live in theater, and die in theater! Theater belongs to those who make theater their vocation for survival. Only by completely resolving the conflict and fetters between artistic desire and economic livelihood <177> will the fundamental problem of the hundred years of new theater be decided. This is the fateful law of theater's rise and fall, promised from the beginning.
Thus, as the most thorough artistic practitioners, they must clearly appear at the forefront of history, opening the path toward establishing a new theater culture.
The day is not far off when we will welcome the emergence of progressive new talents currently boiling with passion and waiting in Tokyo and various places. The heart of Chosŏn, possessing a Kŭgyŏn that grows promisingly, is joyful.
Please forgive these disrespectful, rambling words. Finally, hoping for the valiant efforts of the gentlemen of Kŭgyŏn, I lay down my pen (각筆한다). (Old manuscript) <181>

**Summary**

Sin Puch'ul critiques the state of Korean theater in 1937, finding fault with both profit-driven commercial troupes (lacking social consciousness) and idealistic "new theater" troupes like Kŭgyŏn (lacking stagecraft and popular connection). He argues that true Korean theater must be rooted in the lives and understanding of the Korean populace ("rice"), not just "sugar" (entertainment) or "medicine" (ideology). While praising Kŭgyŏn's recent technical improvements, he criticizes its reliance on poorly translated foreign plays that alienate audiences and calls for the development of distinctly "Chosŏn-style" acting, directing, and stagecraft methods. He concludes by demanding that Kŭgyŏn move beyond amateurism and provide financial stability for its actors to allow true dedication to establishing a lasting Korean theater tradition.

From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into the cultural debates and realities of colonial Korea. It highlights the tension between commercial art and art with a social/national purpose, the struggle to define a modern Korean identity in theater distinct from Japanese or Western models (Shinpa, translated plays), the practical economic hardships faced by artists, and the intellectual discourse surrounding translation and cultural adaptation under colonial constraints. The call for a "Chosŏn-style" methodology and grounding in popular life reflects the nationalist aspirations simmering within the cultural sphere.

「劇藝術協會」에 보내는 公開狀
						申不出
그야말노 雨讀晴耕格으로 劇界를 關心하는 筆者로서 이번 第11回까지의 劇硏公演을 보고 늣긴 바 그 感想의 한 片鱗을 率直히 적어보기로 하기로 하는 것이다.

新劇團體는 藝術本位요 人生本位요 指導的이요 啓蒙的이요 良心的이요 眞實이 있을 뿐이지만 興行劇團은 利得本位요 迎合的이요 阿諛的이요 虛僞的이요 詐欺 박게 아모 것도 아니였다고 해왓지만 實際에 있어서 어느 程度까지 그런 點도 아주 없지는 않치만 반다시 서로 그러치 않은 點도 많은 것이다.
이것은 俳優自身의 內的 良心과 外的 支配와의 軋礫과 劇團自體의 主觀的 條件과 客觀的 情勢의 不利가 가저다 주는 畸形的 發展의 結果라고 볼 수 있는 것이니 이것은 實로 過渡期 朝鮮의 荒燕地 벌판에서 떨고 있는 앙상한 時代的 標本이다. 이것이야말노 尋常히 看過할 수 없는 朝鮮演劇文化<170> 發展過程에 있어서 하나의 特徵的 性格의 一面인 것이다.
新劇團體에서는 燦爛한 論陣을 펴놓코 이러서서 辱喇叭을 불었고 興行劇團에서는 險惡한 感情을 참을 수 없다 하야 撲擊砲를 놓얐다.
그러나 아모리 시커먼 惡夢을 쓰고 헤매는 興行劇體을 꾸짓고서 눈부신 烽火를 들고 용소슴처 일어난 新劇團體라 하드라도 이것은 애오라지 辨證法的으로 發展되는 歷史的 科程에서 볼 수 있는 嚴正한 하나의 社會的 自覺을 찻었다는 것뿐이지 新劇團體의 公演이 回數를 거듭하는 동안 上演된 劇等의 演劇的 効果가 實際에 있어서 果然 얼마나한 嚴正한 意味에서의 朝鮮民衆에게 寄與하는 바가 있었든가?
興行劇團은 舞臺實際의 經驗은 있어도 社會意識을 뚜려시 認識하지 못햇든 緣故로 해서 좋은 演劇을 못하는 채로 있든 것이요 新劇團體는 時代理念은 힘있게 把握햇으나 舞臺實際에 經驗이 없었든 所以로 해서 역시 좋은 演劇을 못하는 채로 있든 것이다.
이래저래 좋은 演劇을 못 가진 朝鮮觀衆의 마음은 너무도 寂寞하였든 것이다.

演劇에 關한 천마듸 文學的 理論도 좋치만은 그보다도 한가지의 綜合的 壓力이 있는 舞臺의 藝術的 實際를 보여달나는 것이 一般의 時急한 要求다.
宣傳時代가 아니요 實力時代다. 言보다 行이다. 行이 없는 言뿐만의 虛張聲勢로 해서 朝鮮은 지금 惡夢을 이르킬 地境이다. 偉大한 技術的 人物의 出現이 없이는 少年時代에 있는 新劇團體가 壯年時代에 있는 興行劇團을 征服할 道理가 없을 것이요 興行劇團을 支持하고 있는 全鮮的 인 絶對多數의 傳統的 觀衆에의 打開는 空想에 그칠 것이다.
眞正한 演劇的 活動은 먼저 大衆的 基礎 우에다 뿌리를 박어야 된다는 것을 다시 한번 생각하고 싶다는 것이다.

일즉이 飜譯劇 上演을 反對하든 사람의 말을 빈다고 하면 무릇 나의 것의 遺産이 없을 때 남의 것을 攝取한다는 것은 必然한 事勢요 當然한 行爲라고 보겠지만 大體 外國劇을 上演함으로서 얻는바 攝取는 劇硏의 自體敎養 또는 修業으로라면 몰나도 그것이 일단 大衆앞에 公開을 하게 되는 때에 있어서는 오늘날 朝鮮事情으로 봐서 그 性質이 달나지게 되는 것이다. 다시 말하면 一般觀衆이 모두 外國劇 鑑賞法을 修得하려는 閒暇한 餘裕를 가지지 못했을 것은 勿論이요 劇硏이 그러한 受苦는 아니해 주드라도 優秀한 外國作品은 벌서 大槪는 보았고 또 各 常設館에서 上演되는 發聲映畵를 通해서 보려할 것이다. 하물며 貧弱한 舞飜에서 未熟한 演技로 上演되는 그 「飜譯劇」앞에서 觀客이 되려는 不幸을 누구라도 두번 다시 經驗하려고 아니 할 것이니 이로 말미아마 外國劇에 對한 一般의 尊敬과 理解와 興味가 殺減되고 原作에 對한 侮贖이 되였으니 言語途斷이란 實로 이를 이름이니 地下에 도라간 原作者들이 痛哭을 하였을 것을 생각하고 짐즛 苦笑를 不禁할 노릇이라고까지 慘酷한 極言을 했든 것은 這間에 事情을 여실히 代辯하는 것이 아니면 아닐 것이다.

나는 이제 劇硏에의 注文을 멫가지 말하려 한다.
劇硏은 다시 演劇以前의 地點으로 도라가 달나는 것이다. 劇硏은 시방 演劇을 함에 있어서 진실로 理論的 硏究에서 實際的 硏究에로 抽象的 理論行動에서 俱體的 實際行動에로란 말이니 勿論이 實際란 말은 公演을 意味하는 것이 아니다. 다시 말하면 準備時代에서 實行時代로 넘어 가는 發展過程에 있어서 그 過渡的 手續으로의 演劇的 基本條件인 朝鮮的인 劇作法과 朝鮮的인 演出法(抑揚法 表情法)과 朝鮮的 인 裝置法 等等을 創案하란 말이다.
新演團體가 興行劇團의 演劇을 演劇 아니로 보는 理由의 하나로 興行劇團의 演出法은 모두 日本內地 新派式이라고 하는 點에 있는 것이니 그러면<176> 興行劇團은 新派式인 演出法으로나 演劇을 해왔겠지만 新劇團體인 劇團은 대체 무슨 式 演出法으로다가 演劇을 해 왔었다는 말인가?
萬一 靑春座를 興行劇團이라고 할 것 같으면 興行劇團인 靑春座 俳優들의 抑揚과 表情 動作과 新劇團體라고 하는 劇硏俳優들의 抑揚과 表情 動作이 얼마나한 다른 差異를 가지고 있다는 말인가?
劇硏의 演出法에 依하야 上演된 「人形의 家」와 興行劇團의 演出法에 의하야 상영된 「人形의 家」가 끼처 놓은 社會的 効果가 서로 얼마마한 다른 點이 있었는지는 모르거니와 그 分別이 두드러지게도 鮮明치 못한지라 要컨대 演劇은 良心만 가지고는 못하는 것을 다시 한번 알어야 하는 仝時에 演劇이 없는 朝鮮에다가 眞正한 意味의 新劇文化樹立을 不滅의 希願으로 삼는 劇硏이라고 할 것 같으면 公演 回數가 많어가는 것을 자랑삼을 것이 아니라 맛당히 新劇 百年을 圖謀할 準備 工作으로서 하나의 뚜렷한 「朝鮮的인 演出法」을 벌서 가젔었어야 할 게 아니었든가?
내 말이 너무 形式的 側面만을 云爲하게 되였으나 決코 演劇藝術의 形式的 還元術을 꾀함이 아닌 것은 勿論 戰場에서 武器의 使用 方法을 모르는 兵士의 無謀를 한번 생각하여 주웟스면 足하다는 것뿐이다.
所謂 『玄人の癖』니 『素人の癖』니 하는 것도 要컨대 一定한 演出方法이 없을 때에 「제멋대로」가 맨드러 주는 後天的 不隨病이라 뚜려시 作定된 演出方法에 依하여 演出되는 臺舞에서는 도저히 있을 수 없는 「病」이다.
나는 이 點에서 現在 朝鮮에 있는 新劇團體와 興行團體에 距離를 疑心하게 되는 遺憾을 가진다.
써 劇硏 諸公은 여기 對한 再吟味 再認識 再出發이 있을 줄을 밋고 疑心치 않는다.
또 한가지는 俳優들의 生活保障을 해주란 말이다. 밥을 먹어야 살 수 있다 함은 너무도 自然스러운 人類共通의 普遍的 生活事實이다. 生活을 떠난 藝術이 없다고 하는 말은 俳優들이 뇌까리는 戱曲 속에만 씨여 있어야 되는 臺辭가 아니라 곳 그 배우 自身의 말인 것이다. 演劇을 好事的 趣味로나 氣分的 道樂으로써 하는 것이 아니라고 할 것 같으면 劇硏 會員으로서 本業과 副業이 따로 있지 못할 것이다. 世人이 劇硏을 일커러「紳士素人劇團」이라고 함도 無理가 아니다.
演劇 속으로 드러가라! 그래서 演劇 속에서 살고 演劇 속에서 죽으라! 演劇은 演劇을 스스로 生存의 天職으로 삼는 사람의 것이다. 藝術 意慾과 生活 經濟의 葛藤과 桎梏를 完全히 解決하는 데서<177> 비로소 新劇 百年의 根本問題가 決定될 것이요 始初로부터 約束된 演劇興亡의 宿命的 法則이다.
그리하야 가장 徹底한 藝術的 實踐者로서의 新劇文化 樹立에의 길을 열면서 歷史의 前面에 뚜려시 나타나야 할 것이다.
시방 東京과 各地에서 피를 끄리며 待機하고 있는 進步的인 新人들의 登龍을 마지할 날도 머지 않타. 탐스럽게 자라나는 劇硏을 가진 朝鮮의 마음은 깁부다.
