Publication Date: 1937년 01월 01일 (January 1, 1937)
Article Title: 나무꾼 (The Woodcutter)
Author: 李箕永
Article Type: 소설 (Fiction/Novel)

The Woodcutter
						Yi Kiyŏng

Due to a complete crop failure this year, his fifth cousin's family went bankrupt, and Myŏnggu inevitably had to leave the village.
Until today, he had grown up receiving nothing but contempt from others, like a pebble rolling by the roadside, and it was even more sorrowful that he couldn't even hold onto that and had to bid farewell to his hometown.
Hoping there might be some work in a big city, he wandered here and there and arrived here a few days ago. The further north he went, the colder the weather seemed to get. He stopped when he reached K City.
Autumn was already drawing to a close, and for Myŏnggu, who had no relatives or friends, just surviving this winter was a major concern.
Being a country bumpkin on his first journey, he managed to find the home of Okdongi, who was from the same village and barely scraping by in a gatehouse room outside the north gate. He slept there one night like a beggar, but from the next day, he couldn't impose further <271> on the family, so he went around looking for any work he could find.
However, in this unfamiliar place where he knew no one, there was nowhere that would readily hire a boy not yet twenty years old.
Having no other choice, his only asset being a single A-frame carrier (chige), he carried it around the train station and main streets, selling his labor for temporary luggage carrying. Fortunately, when he earned a few pennies, he used it to buy rice and ate his meals arranged through Okdongi's family, and for lodging, through their arrangement, he found shared lodging (setjam) for five *jŏn* a night in the servants' room of a noodle shop next door. In return for the cheap rent, he also helped the noodle shop workers when they lit the outdoor fires. Then he could also get leftover noodle strands and cold rice lumps to eat. However, when he had no earnings for several days, he often went hungry. At such times, he would pretend to go to Okdongi's house for breakfast and dinner but would just come back empty-handed. Holding his hungry stomach, he found it too sorrowful to wander the streets aimlessly, so he would often go up the back hill for a while. There, he would look down at the sprawling cityscape...

Then, right next to the noodle shop—a young woman also living in a gatehouse room—was bringing back a sackful of pine needles and twigs (solgarunamu) every evening. He didn't know what it was and paid it no mind, but later found out it was that kind of wood.
As soon as Myŏnggu realized this, his heart leaped. Because if it came to gathering wood, he wouldn't be worse than anyone else—. If a woman could do it, wouldn't he, a man, be able to do it even better? The woman not only gathered wood to burn at home but also seemed to sell it quite often to make money. He thought that if he did the same, he wouldn't have to worry about surviving in the future.

The very next evening, after eating dinner, Myŏnggu immediately dashed into the back hills. Indeed, in the gullies within the pine forest, fallen pine needles lay so thick they covered his feet. With his two rake-like hands, he continuously gathered them into his sack, and in no time, he had shouldered a full load and was coming down.
Just as he rounded the mountain slope and was descending onto level ground, someone suddenly shouted from behind him. Grabbing the leg of his A-frame carrier from behind, the person yanked it, making him drop the load of wood. Turning around, he saw it was a mountain guard (san'gam) holding a club.
"Aigo, sir (na-ri)!"
"You brat! Where did you gather this wood? Huh!" <272>
He struck Myŏnggu's back once with the club.
"Aigo, I did it without knowing..."
"You little bastard—what do you mean you didn't know! Just like a brazen thief!"
"No, really, I didn't know..."
Myŏnggu truly didn't know what to do.
"Hmph—you brat, let's see how you endure this! Do you think saying you didn't know makes it alright? Let's see you serve three or four months in jail."
The mountain guard threatened him menacingly.
"No, really, I didn't know... I did it without knowing. I won't do it again. It was my first time today."
Myŏnggu spoke like a fool and began to beg pitifully.
"Enough—where is your home?"
"I—came from down below."
"What, down below? Where is down below?"
"It's OO in Ch'ungch'ŏng Province."
"Really? When did you arrive?"
"It hasn't been many days."
Noticing the guard's demeanor soften somewhat upon hearing his answer, Myŏnggu pleaded even more pitifully about his circumstances. After listening carefully to Myŏnggu's story, the guard seemed to feel some sympathy and, after a lengthy lecture, let him go.
Myŏnggu had been terrified he would actually be dragged away and was relieved to get off safely. However, walking down with his empty carrier, he thought again and became furious that the wood he had worked so hard to gather had been completely taken away. So, after walking down a short distance, he turned back and went up again to where the wood load was. Surely the guard must have gone elsewhere by now. Then he could stealthily retrieve the wood—.
Calculating this cleverly all by himself, Myŏnggu went back up and was just about to shoulder the wood when, just like before, someone suddenly grabbed the leg of his carrier. Dropping the wood again and looking up, he saw it was the very same guard. Myŏnggu was dumbfounded. Now he really seemed to be in big trouble.
The guard, perhaps also dumbfounded, berated him as if seeing such an audacious brat, sweating profusely. This time, he was adamant. Saying he couldn't forgive him, and that a guy who comes back a second time after being let go was incorrigible, he grabbed Myŏnggu tightly by the collar and started dragging him.
Myŏnggu had no choice but to beg again. In a voice truly choked with tears, bowing repeatedly, <273>
"It was just because I was hungry that such thoughts came to me again. I was driven mad by hunger, so please, just this once, please forgive me!"
he pleaded desperately. And he pitiably recounted the story of his life up to now, the parts he hadn't finished earlier. Although the guard was annoyed by the repeated offense, the boy from another province seemed pitiful in his circumstances, and indeed seemed to have arrived recently. So this time, he warned him very sternly and sent him away with a sharp scolding.
Myŏnggu felt incredibly lucky to have been caught twice and still get away safely. Thus, he didn't have the courage to go up a third time and gave up on the wood. Walking down that path, he thought about how ridiculous and frustrating it was. The plan he thought was a good way to make a living by gathering wood turned out just like a clumsy thief getting caught on his first night, didn't it?
The more he thought about this, the more envious he felt of the woman. And he felt utterly ashamed that he, with his manly physique, couldn't manage to do what even women did quite well. He ended up lamenting his own incompetence, concluding that he was just a helpless country bumpkin.

After that, the woman continued to bring back wood just fine. Myŏnggu found this strange.
So one day, he deliberately went to see the woman and asked her how she managed to gather wood without getting caught.
At that time, the woman smiled brightly and said:
"People get caught because they do it foolishly. If you know the ropes (kolssu), why would you get caught!"
"How do you know the ropes?"
The woman, showing off her knowledge and still smiling, said:
"Look here! The mountain guard has a regular route, right? So after he makes one round of his route, you just go into that valley and do it, couldn't you? Or else, do it before he comes... But people just do it anywhere recklessly, so of course they get caught clumsily!"
And she lightly tapped Myŏnggu's shoulder.
"Ah, that makes sense."
The woman burst out laughing merrily. Indeed, listening to the woman, it seemed plausible.
"If only one could know that!"
Myŏnggu thought about asking her to go together, but felt awkward asking to go with a young woman in the dark of night, so he dropped the idea <274> and came up with a plan himself.
That very evening, Myŏnggu sat at the noodle shop and observed the woman's movements. As usual, she rolled up her sack, tucked it under her arm, and headed upwards.
Alright! Myŏnggu stealthily followed behind her. The woman climbed upwards like an arrow.
Inside the winding pine forest gully, a stream of spring water flowed down. On either side, dark rocks were scattered here and there, forming a secluded valley, and the woman entered it without hesitation.
A strange thought suddenly struck Myŏnggu! He slipped off to the side path and settled himself discreetly behind a rock from where he could look down into the valley.
Holding his breath and watching quietly, a dark shadow appeared in the gloom. Then, they met and whispered something to each other. Myŏnggu's curiosity flared, and he strained his ears.
But the voice sounded very familiar. It was unmistakably the mountain guard who had confiscated his wood the other day. Myŏnggu felt a tickle in his gut. Alright! Let's see—the words muttered involuntarily in his mouth.
Sure enough! The woman took off her skirt and spread it on the ground. Then the woman first, and then the man, lay down...
Seeing this spectacle, Myŏnggu couldn't suppress his laughter. Trying hard to stifle his giggles, covering his mouth tightly with both hands, he wondered inwardly what he should do.
Should he just leave them be? Then, he quietly urinated down from under the rock! Droplets of urine splattered onto the face of the woman below.
"Ah... is it raining!..."
The woman's uneasy voice.
"Huh! It's raining... But it was perfectly clear just now!"
"The stars are out, look at it raining!"
Hearing their conversation, Myŏnggu burst out laughing loudly, "Haha!" Startled beyond belief by the sound of laughter right above their heads, they scrambled up in panic.
"Ah! Good heavens!"
The man and woman, unable even to properly arrange their clothes, scurried away deep into the valley like scared rabbits. Myŏnggu considered chasing after them, but wanting to see how things would conclude, he stayed nearby. He kept laughing <275> for a good while afterwards.
After some time, the woman came down the path on the far hill first. He wondered when she had managed it, but she was carrying a bundle of wood nonchalantly, looking composed. Then, a short distance away, a man's loud cough was heard, followed by:
"Hey—who's that carrying wood! Leave it there—can't you leave the wood there!"
Putting on a dignified air and pretending ignorance, someone came chasing after her. It seemed that after his illicit act, he gave the confiscated wood to this woman every evening. If so, wasn't it likely that the wood taken from him the other day was also given to her? Thinking this, Myŏnggu grew angry.
He quickly climbed up the bank, blocked the woman's path, and confronted her.
"My, the secret (kolssu) to gathering wood well is quite something, isn't it! That must be the method!"
The woman's face instantly turned red.
"What method are you talking about!"
she retorted brazenly, trying to brush him off.
"What do you mean? I know everything... What were you doing getting rained on just now?"
The woman couldn't say anything and just tried to walk away, while the man following behind still pretended, yelling perfunctorily to leave the wood there. But his voice seemed to be pulled back into his throat as if afraid someone else might hear.
When he came face to face with the man, Myŏnggu abruptly grabbed him by the collar.
"Hey! You confiscated my wood the other day, didn't you! Are you really the mountain guard?"
"What?... Really the mountain guard, you say!"
Caught unexpectedly in this confrontation, the man was momentarily flustered and tried to shake off Myŏnggu's hand, staring straight at him.
"Does a mountain guard do things like that, I ask you? Not even knowing if it's raining or if the stars are out... Let's take *you* to the police station. For no good reason..."
The man, caught by the wrist, had his own vulnerability. Faced with Myŏnggu's overly firm attitude, he couldn't dismiss him as just a child. If it were just him, it might be nothing, but the boy seemed to know the woman. If he told her husband, it would be an unbearable disgrace.
Meanwhile, the woman, hearing the words "go to the police station," stopped in her tracks, unable to move forward or back, trembling uncontrollably. <276>
Seeing this, the man felt even more cornered and embarrassed. Having no other choice, he grabbed Myŏnggu's arm, nudged him hard in the ribs, and said:
"Alright—that's enough, I get it—why are you being so stubborn, young man!"
He forced a hearty laugh and patted Myŏnggu's shoulder.
"So, if I gather wood too, will you leave me alone?... Hey, you dignified man, what kind of behavior is this?"
"Ah! Alright, young man, my mistake."
Realizing there was nothing to gain from arguing, the man mumbled evasively and hurried away. Myŏnggu found the sight so amusing that he stood there laughing by himself for a while. The woman had also disappeared, nowhere to be seen.

The next day around lunchtime, as Myŏnggu was sitting near the noodle shop entrance, the woman walked past stealthily and gave him a meaningful glance. Curious about what it meant, especially after last night's incident, Myŏnggu waited a bit and then went to her house.
The woman greeted him with unusual warmth, saying no one was home and urging him to come in quickly. Myŏnggu felt a mix of curiosity and unease, unsure of her intentions. Was this some kind of trap set up as revenge for last night?—.
However, thinking she surely wouldn't harm a young, penniless boy like himself, he mustered his courage and entered the room. Indeed—her husband, who suffered from chronic coughing due to asthma day and night, was absent, as were the children.
The woman brought out an unexpected bowl of rice cake soup (ttŏkkuk) and a cup of liquor, urging him warmly:
"Really, I felt so ashamed seeing you last night, young man..."
Then she suddenly let out a deep sigh as if the ground would collapse and burst into tears.
Myŏnggu, guessing her inner thoughts, smiled faintly.
"Oh, Auntie... What did you think I would do?"
"Will you truly understand my situation? Here—please eat!"
The woman showed a look of slight relief, but still seemed half-doubting.
"Do you think I'd expose you out of malice? Really, Auntie!"
"Oh, if so, how grateful I would be..."
The woman finally stopped crying. Then, after thinking about something for a while, poking the charcoal brazier with a fire poker—she glanced sideways at Myŏnggu once more before continuing:
"Well—now that you know everything, what can't I tell you, young man? —Primarily, I'm the wicked one, but if you hear the reason why things turned out like this with him, it's not entirely my fault—Aigo, who can know my heart? Only heaven and earth know."
The woman sighed deeply once again.
"No, then how did it happen?"
Myŏnggu, feeling tipsy after one cup of liquor, asked while gulping down the rice cake soup.
"Like you, young man—I saw others bringing wood, so I went to gather wood for the first time— At that time, our food had run out, and we hadn't been able to cook for several meals. With the room also being cold, how could I survive with the young children... My husband just lies sick all day long... So I gathered a big bundle of wood and was coming down when I got caught! Getting caught doing something like that for the first time in my life, I was truly scared, just like being caught stealing. What's more, he threatened to take me to the police station and send me to prison, making a huge fuss! So I begged him, saying I did wrong! If it were now, I might have confronted him, since my fault is my fault, and his fault is his... But back then, I was especially naive and foolish, how could I even conceive of such a thing!... Then look here!... Really, it's so bizarre—I wouldn't say this if it weren't you, young man—He said... listen to me... he'd forgive me!"
"Haha, really... that's unbelievable!"
Myŏnggu burst out, unsure whether it was funny or something else. A feeling of resentment slowly crept into him. And he regretted having urinated on them the night before.
"From the start, that fellow looked indecent!"
"Coming home then and thinking quietly, this foolish woman thought—it serves me right—a desperate feeling arose. Ah well, since it has come to this, what difference does it make if I suffer once or twice!... So since then, has anything changed? Didn't I just decide to gather wood recklessly to my heart's content?... Haha, truly, living in this world, there are many strange things. If you think about it, I too, because of that..."
"Hearing your story, Auntie, it truly seems like unavoidable circumstances (sach'abulp'i). But even a long rein eventually gets stepped on (proverb meaning 'even patience has its limits' or 'wrongdoing will eventually be caught'). You should stop now!"
Myŏnggu, feeling a strange emotion, suddenly spoke in a serious tone.
"Oh! Heavens no, if I stop even that, how are we supposed to live... Especially during this winter with no income at all!"
The woman jumped as if telling him not to even mention such a thing twice. Myŏnggu couldn't help but be startled by her words. <278>
"Well then, if that incident hadn't happened, how would you have managed to live!"
Myŏnggu asked back, utterly bewildered.
"That's what I'm saying, people just live according to their circumstances at the time—if things go wrong, they live according to the wrong circumstances; if things go well, according to the good circumstances..."
The woman sighed again, seemingly without sorrow, her eyes welling up with tears. She seemed to possess a completely fatalistic view. Just live as things come—the woman continued speaking, her voice growing increasingly sentimental.
"Still, when I see my young children eating their fill with the money from selling that wood... they probably don't even know how that wood was obtained and are just happy every day that I bring back wood!—It's not just the little ones, even the grown-up..."
The woman couldn't finish her sentence and choked back a sob. Tears streamed down like rain. Suddenly, Myŏnggu also felt a lump in his throat, and something seemed to surge up inside him. The pieces of rice cake became difficult to swallow.
Truly, what is poverty?
Myŏnggu delved deep into the ignorant heart contained within his small chest. He wrung it out.
He truly found the ways of the world ridiculous.
This world is enlightened, they say. They say they make it impossible to commit crimes even at night, just like during the day.
And yet, should he gather wood again to survive? Or should he starve to death rather than do such a thing? He couldn't tell which path he should take—
Myŏnggu, who until moments ago had found the woman repulsive, suddenly lost that feeling, and instead, pity and compassion began to grow. —The End— <279>

**Summary**

Displaced from his rural home by famine, young Myŏnggu struggles to survive in K City. Initially trying honest labor carrying luggage, hunger drives him to attempt gathering firewood from a nearby mountain, a common practice but illegal. He is caught twice by a corrupt mountain guard who confiscates his wood. Later, Myŏnggu discovers the guard is having an affair with a neighboring woman, allowing her to gather wood freely (likely including Myŏnggu's confiscated bundles) in exchange for sexual favors. After confronting them, Myŏnggu learns the woman was initially coerced into the arrangement out of desperation to feed her family. The story ends with Myŏnggu grappling with the moral ambiguity of their situation and his own uncertain path forward, questioning societal claims of "enlightenment" against the harsh realities of poverty.

From a historical perspective, the story vividly portrays the economic hardships and social displacement faced by rural Koreans during the colonial period, forcing migration to cities where survival was precarious. It highlights the vulnerability of the poor, particularly women, to exploitation and petty corruption by those in minor positions of authority (like the mountain guard). The narrative implicitly critiques the gap between the supposed order and progress touted by the colonial regime ("enlightenment") and the desperate, morally compromised lives many ordinary Koreans were forced to lead simply to survive. Historians might find the depiction of survival strategies, informal economies (wood gathering), social stratification, and the themes of moral compromise under duress valuable for understanding the lived experiences of Koreans in the 1930s.

나무꾼
						李箕永
올 농사에 여지없이 흉년을 만나서 오촌집이 파산을 하는 바람에 명구도 할 수 없이 마을에서 떨려나게 되였다.
그는 오늘날까지 마치 길까에 굴리는 조악돌과 같이 남에게 천대만 받고 커왔는데, 그나마 부지를 못하고 고향을 하직하게 되는 것이 더욱 서궆은 일이었다.
혹시 대도회지는 무슨 버리가 있을가 하고 그는 이리저리 쏘다니다가 몇 일전에 이곳으로 올라왔다. 북쪽으로 올라올수록 기후는 점점 치운 것 같다. 그는 K시까지 와서 발을 멈첬다.
어느듯 가을도 저무러 가는데, 사고무친한 명구는 우선 이 겨을을 살기가 큰 일이었다.
시굴뜨기가 처음 나선 길이라, 그는 북문 밖에서 문깐방 한 간을 비러 가지고 근근히 사러나가는 한 동리 살든 옥동이네를 간신히 찾아와서 하루 밤을 신꼴치듯 자고 났으나, 이튿날부터는 그 집에 또 페를<271> 끼칠 수도 없기 때문에 사방으로 버리할 곳을 찾아 단여 보았다.
그러나 골수를 모르는 타관은 어디가 붙어야할지 모르는데 아직 스무 살도 못된 소년을 손쉽게 일손을 잡아 주는 곳은 없었다.

그 남자는 대거리를 해야 잇속이 없을 줄 알었던지, 어물어물하며 저리로 다러난다. 명구는 그 꼴이 우수워서 한동안 혼저 서서 웃었다. 여자도 어디로 갔는지 그림자를 볼 수 없다.

그 이튼날 점심때 명구가 국수집 문앞에 앉었으랴니까, 그 여자가 슬적 지나가며, 눈찟을 한다. 어제 밤에 한 짓도 있고, 무슨 일인지 궁금해서 명구는 조금 있다가 그 집을 찾아가 보았다.
여자는 전에 없이 반색을 하며 아무도 없으니 어서 드러오리 한다. 명구는 한편으로 호기심이 나면서도 남의 속을 몰라서, 위뚜름하였다. 어제밤 분푸리로 무슨 올개미를 씨우자는 수작이 아니냐고―.
그러나, 아모것도 없는 나어린 자기를 설마 어떻게 하랴고 그는 용기를 다해서 방으로 드러갔다. 방안에는 과연― 밤낫 콜록콜록 해소병을 알는 그의 남편은 물론, 어린애들조차 아무도 없다.
여자는 난듸없는 뎍국 한 그릇과 술 한잔을 갔다 놓고 은근히 권하며
「참, 어제 밤에는 총각 보기가 미안했우...」
하더니 그는 별안간 한숨을 땅이 꺼지게 내쉬며 눈물을 텀벙텀벙 쏫는다.
명구는 그 속을 짐작하고 빙긋이 웃으며
「원! 아주머니는...내가 어떻게 할줄 알고 그리우.」
「정말로 내 사정을 보아주겠어. 자― 어서 자서요!」
여자는 저윽히 안심하는 표정을 짓는다. 그러나 반의하는 눈치 같다.
「그럼 억하심정으로 누구에게 까바치겠수. 참 아주머니두!」
「아 그러면 얼마나 고마운지...」
여자는 비로소 눈물을 거두고 나서, 한참 무엇을 생각하는지 부젓갈로 화로불을 되작이다가―명구를 슬쩍 한번 다시 처다보고나서
「참―인제 다 아는 터이니, 총각한테 무슨 말을 못 하겠나. ―첫대 내가 못된 년이지마는 그이와 그렇게 <277> 된 까닭을 드러보면 나만 잘못한 것이 아니지―아이구 그 속을 누가 알까, 하늘이나 알고 땅이나 알지.」
여자는 다시 또 한숨을 내쉬인다.
「아니 그럼 어떻게 된 노릇이유?」
명구는 술 한잔을 먹고 나서 얼근한 김에 떡국을 꺼귀꺼귀 먹으며 무러본다.
「나도 총각처럼―남들이 나무를 해오는 것을 보고 처음으로 나무를 하러 갔더라우―. 그때는 양식이 떠러저서, 멫 끼를 못 끄렸는데, 방까지 춥고 보니 어린것들하고 어디 살수가 있어야지...사랑양반이란 이는 밤낫 알키만 하구...그래 나무를 한단 잔뜩 글거이고 나려오다가 그만 들켰구려! 평생 처음 그런 짓을 하다가 들키고 보니, 참말로 도적질을 하다가 들킨 것 같이 무서운데, 더구나 경찰서로 가자고 디리 을르며 증역을 보내겠다고 야단이구려! 그래 잘못했다고 개개 비렀지요! 지금만 같했서도 한번 드리대였겠지마는, 잘못은 나 잘못이고, 그 잘못은 그 잘못이니까...그때는 더구나 어리백이여서 어디 그럴 생심이나 있었나베!...그랬더니 이거보지!...내 참, 해괴망칙해서―총각이 아니면 말도 못 하겠어―그럼, 내 말을 드르라구...용서해 줄테라구!」
「하하 참...기막히군!」
명구는 우수운지 어쩐지 모르는 가슴속을 텃드렸다. 그는 실그머니 분한 생각이 드러갔다. 그리고 그는 어제 밤에 오줌을 눈 것이 잘못이라고 후회하였다.
「원체가 궐짜가 점잔치 못하게 생겼드군!」
「그대 집으로 와서 가만이 생각해 보니 미련한 년이 갈눙은 맛다구―고만 아귀둥한 생각이 납듸그려. 예라 기위 그렇게 된 몸이니 한번 당하나 두번 당하나 일반 아니냐!...그래 그 뒤로는 무에 달른게냐구. 막우터 놓고 나무나 싫것하자구 않그랬겠어?...하하, 참 사람이 세상을 살자니까, 별일이 다 많겠지. 알고 보면, 나두 그래서...」
「아주머니 말슴을 드러보니 참말로 사차불피였우. 그러나 곱비도 길면 발핀다구. 인제는 그만 두시유!」
명구는 야릇한 생각이 나서 별안간 진중한 태도로 이렇게 말하였다.
「원! 천만에 그나마 고만 두면 어떻게 살라구...더구나 아무 버리도 없는 이 겨울 동안을!」
여자가 그런 말은 두번도 말라고 펄짝 뛴다. 명구도 그만큼 여자의 말을 놀래지 않을 수 없었다. <278>
「아니 그럼, 그 일이 없었드면 어떻게 사실번했우!」
명구는 하두 어이가 없어서 되집허 물었다.
「내사 그 말이요, 그러니까, 사람이란 그때 그때의 처지대로 사는겐가바―잘못하면 잘못된 그대로 잘되면 잘된 처지 그대로...」
여자는 또 한번 실음없이 한숨을 치쉬며, 눈물이 글성글성해진다. 그는 아주 허무사상을 가진 모양이였다. 그래서 되는대로 살자고―여자는 다시 말을 잇는데 그의 목소리는 갈수록 쏀치멘탈하였다.
「그래도 그 나무를 파러서, 어린 자식들이 배불리 먹는 것을 보면...그들은 그 나무를 어떻게 해온 줄도 모르고 아주 날마다 나무 잘해 온다고 좋아하겠지!―그것은 어린것들 뿐 아니라, 애어룬까지도...」
여자는 말끝을 마치지 못하고 우름을 울컥 삼킨다. 그리고 눈물이 비오듯 하였다. 별안간 명구도 목안이 뿌듯하고 무엇이 치미는 것 같았다. 그래 떡조각이 잘 넘어가지 않었다.
참으로 가난이란 무엇인가?
명구는 그의 조그만 가슴속에 담긴, 무지한 마음을 구석 깊이 끄집어내 보았다. 쥐여짜 보았다.
그는 참으로 세상일이 우수웠다.
이 세상은 개명했다. 밤도 낫같이 죄를 못 짓게 다 한는 것이다.
그리고 그는 또다시 나무를 해다가, 살어야 할 것인가? 그렇지 않으면 굶어 죽이도 그런 짓은 말어야 할 것인가? 그 어느 편을 취해야 할는지 알 수 없었다―
명구는 아까까지도 그 여자가 더럽게 보이든 것이 어느듯 그런 생각이 없어지고, 도리여 가엽고 불상한 생각이 들어갔다. ―完― <279>
