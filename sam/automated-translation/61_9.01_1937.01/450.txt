The Fake President
						Song Yŏng
-Characters-
Kim Chae-bu (President of a certain rubber company) ꠏꠏꠈ
Ko T'ae-sik (Unemployed) (Guest at Chae-bu's house) ꠏꠏꠋ (Elementary school classmates)
O Rak-ch'ŏn (Poor writer)                 ꠏꠏꠎ
Yi Ok-yŏp (Ko's fiancée)
Yi Chu-yŏp (Ok-yŏp's younger sister)
Il-lyong (Chae-bu's house servant)
Old Woman (Rice shop owner)
Pak (Rice mill clerk)

Time:   Modern
Place:   Kyŏngsŏng
Stage: Reception room in Kim Chae-bu's house in a suburb of Kyŏngsŏng. The room is Western-style. The door on the right leads to the inner room hallway. <334> The front door leads down to the courtyard terrace.
The left door leads to the main gate.
Appropriately placed windows and numerous props suitable for a reception room.
It is now summer, around nine in the morning.
Il-lyong is cleaning the room.

-Curtain-
Il-lyong (Looking outside) Humph, here he comes again, relentlessly. (Wiping the sofa) He'll probably take another nap here. (Tidying the table) Doesn't he have any sense... Anyway, he's got guts... Humph... On such a fine day, why would he want to be cooped up in here?
(Looking out) Good grief. Hey you! Please stop coming. (Stops abruptly) Eek, my voice was too loud. What if he heard? (Wipes the glass cabinet)
Ko T'ae-sik (Enters through the left door)
Il-lyong: Welcome, sir.
Ko: Hmm, slept well? (Hangs his hat, goes and sits on the sofa)
Il-lyong: (Looks at him and smiles)
Ko: The old man hasn't woken up yet, right?
Il-lyong: Of course. Sir, why have you come so early? (Looks at the clock - 9:05) There are still two hours left, you know...
Ko: Huh... He's different because he's the company president... Sigh- Say, Il-lyong. Go gather the newspapers.
Il-lyong: (Brings a bundle of newspapers from the table) I've already gathered them like this. Please, lie down.
Ko: Hey, you rascal. Huh... That cheeky fellow...
Il-lyong: Ha... What did I do wrong? Isn't this something you routinely do every morning, sir? Ha...
Ko: Humph, that fellow is just getting cheekier. (Lies down) Alright, might as well read comfortably.
Il-lyong: (Smiles, places a small table with matches and an ashtray beside Ko's head) I've put this here too.
Ko: What? (Looks and laughs) Yes, you're quite seasoned at this, aren't you?
Il-lyong: Well then, sir, I'll go inside. (Starts to leave) Oh, sir, there's no need to tell the old man you're here when he wakes up today either, right?
Ko: Right, whenever he wakes up, just tell him I've arrived and am waiting.
Il-lyong: (Opens the door and stands half in, half out) Otherwise, wouldn't the old man know? He usually assumes you've come again when he wakes up. Ha... (Exits quickly)
Ko: Aigoo, that flippant brat—sigh— Huh... Anyway <335>
Let's read the newspaper again... -sigh- Humph... Someone died again. Newspapers are full of runaways and... humph... The world is interesting. (Reads for a while, throws it down, gets up, paces back and forth several times, then lies down again) (Sits up abruptly) Aigoo, it's stuffy. (Looks outside) Oh... The weather is indeed nice. Humph, still... (Sits down again and picks up the newspaper)

(Doorbell rings)
Ko: Someone's here quite early. Maybe a friend like me.
(Il-lyong rushes past outside the window.)
(A moment later, Il-lyong enters through the left door with a name card.)
Il-lyong: Sir! Do you happen to know this person?
Ko: Hmm. Let me see. (Startled, stands up) Mm, O Rak-ch'ŏn is here now? Tell him to come in quickly.
Il-lyong: No, sir, he didn't come to see you.
Ko: I know that.
Il-lyong: Then why are you telling him to come in, sir?
Ko: Because he's an acquaintance of mine too.
Il-lyong: But he came to see the old man.
Ko: Good grief, you rascal. I told you it doesn't matter. That gentleman, me, the master of the house—it's all the same.
Il-lyong: Then shall I ask him to come in?
Ko: Yes.
Il-lyong: Later, the old man might get angry, you know.
Ko: Right. Don't worry. I'll take responsibility for everything!
Il-lyong: Then I'll bring him in. (Exits.)
Ko: Humph, what brings this fellow here?
(Goes to sit in a chair by the table, looking towards the door.)
Il-lyong: (Entering) Please come this way.
O Rak-ch'ŏn: Yes.
Ko: Rak-ch'ŏn! It's been a long time.
O: Oh, T'ae-sik! It really has been a while.
-(They shake hands)-
Ko: Ha, it's truly good to see you.
O: Indeed, likewise.
Il-lyong: Well then, shall I inform the old man?
Ko: (Before O can speak) Leave it. What's the use of telling him? He won't come out until it's time.
O: No-. I have some urgent business.
Ko: It's still no use. Let's just talk slowly amongst ourselves until he wakes up.
O: (More agitated) No.
Ko: What, 'no'...? Hey, Il-lyong, go on inside and attend to your work. And let me know when he wakes up later.
Il-lyong: Yes, sir. (Enters the inner room) <336>
O: No... This fellow doesn't understand my situation. (Chases towards the right door) Hey! Hey!
Ko: (Blocking him) It's no use calling him.
O: Good grief. You always insist on doing things your way, just like before. Huh... This is a real problem. Please call that servant back quickly.
Ko: Hey, why are you getting so worked up? No matter how urgently you need to see him, what can you do if the person you want to see doesn't wake up readily?
O: But you didn't even try waking him.
Ko: Absolutely not... Chae-bu is completely different now from before... He's at least a company president now, isn't he? He's not a shabby lumpen like us anymore... You think he'd wake up just because you called...? It's a rule in this house not to disturb his morning sleep, no matter what happens.
O: Not even if something urgent comes up-
Ko: Yup-, that's right. The urgency is for the visitor, not necessarily for him...
O: Huh-, this is truly—sigh— Then this is a real mess.
Ko: Well, what is the matter anyway?
O: Oh— That's right. (Smacks his lips, unsure what to do.)
Ko: Anyway, just sit down first.
O: Good grief. You don't understand my predicament... (Sits down reluctantly) Huh- 참!
Ko: (Lights a cigarette and offers one) Here- have one. So, what's the story?
O: Huh... Oh dear.
Ko: Is it related to finances?
O: Yes.
Ko: Then, though I don't know for sure, brace yourself.
O: No, why?
Ko: Well, I don't know what miracle Chae-bu might perform for you, but... generally speaking.
O: No, what's that supposed to mean? Hmm, so you think <337> I came to ask Chae-bu for money?
Ko: Well, since you mentioned finances, I assumed that might be the case.
O: It's not like that. Since it's come to this, let me tell you the gist of it.
Ko: Yes, please tell me quickly. I'm getting anxious.
O: Oh dear, this is really upsetting. Look here, my friend. The truth is, the reason I wanted to meet Chae-bu wasn't to ask for or take anything, but just to ask him to say one word for me.
Ko: What word?
O: Oh, it's embarrassing. It's nothing else, really. I owe some money on credit at the rice shop in front of my house...
Ko: Go on.
O: It's been quite a while. All told, it's about 40 won. So I kept putting it off, saying this month, next month, until finally, I ran out of excuses...
Ko: No way. You seem to publish a lot in newspapers and magazines, are you really that hard up?
O: Humph, I don't write much, but if I got paid for what I write, it would be fine... Honestly, only someone who wants to starve to death would pursue this damned thing called literature.
Ko: Humph, still, I thought a famous writer like you would be living comfortably, but it seems you're also struggling.
O: Don't mention it. I'm really dying of frustration.
Even regarding this rice bill, last spring I wrote a novella for a certain magazine, and I planned to pay her when I received the manuscript fee. I acted as if I worked for a big company and managed to coax her into giving me rice on credit, and now it's turned into this mess.
Ko: Huh- That's really unfortunate. But what can you do if you can't pay? Tell her you'll pay gradually as you earn money.
O: Humph, don't mention it. It's been so long, I have no more excuses... And the owner is an old woman, extremely stubborn... Reasoning with her is useless, appealing to her sense of propriety doesn't work. She just comes every day, makes a scene, and eventually goes out into the street in front of the main gate, shouting at the top of her lungs in that ear-splitting voice, making a spectacle for the whole neighborhood—. It's so humiliating, I could just die.
Ko: Humph, try doing it like Raskolnikov once, huh-.
O: Even without that suggestion, sometimes I do have such thoughts... So, you see. Lately, I couldn't stand it, so I started sneaking in late at night <338> and dashing out as soon as it gets light. How can I sleep soundly? How can I eat meals properly at the right time? Look, friend, I wish I could just sit down and write a manuscript, but how can I possibly sit down? Oh dear...
Ko: Try bluffing your way through it once.
O: Good grief. She's an old woman, how can I even begin to argue with her—. But look, I've been avoiding her daily, telling my family to say I went on a business trip for my company... Then, damn it all, this morning I overslept and got caught by that hag.
So, what do you think happened? She demanded payment immediately, just like that. I told her I just came back from the countryside yesterday, so I needed to go to the company first to get the money. But no, that hag got suspicious, saying she's been tricked so many times, she doesn't know which company I actually work for, and insisted on coming with me. So she followed me all the way here.
Ko: No way, so where is she now?
O: Humph, it's absurd. She's standing there waiting at the main gate right now. I told her this is the house of the president of the company I work for, and she said she wants to meet him and ask why he hasn't paid my salary before she leaves. So, after racking my brains, I told her to wait a moment and came in. Like this... So I came to ask Chae-bu just to say one word, that I'm an employee of his company. Surely... he'll grant that request, right?
Ko: Humph, not a chance. Do you think the president of such a large company would say something so embarrassing, like admitting he hasn't paid his employee's salary and asking for patience? He'll surely see it as a great dishonor and refuse outright.
O: Huh, but what should I do? If I hesitate even a little, that suspicious old hag will follow me in.
Ko: Then look here, let's try a temporary fix.
O: How?
Ko: As long as we can get the old woman to leave, that's all that matters, right?
O: Then, even if we have to figure out how to handle the aftermath later.
Ko: Then let's do this. We can't wake Chae-bu now, and even if we did, it wouldn't solve much. So I'll pretend to be the president temporarily and trick her.
O: Will that be okay?
Ko: Why not? What does the old woman know? If I sit grandly in a room like this, she'll likely believe it.
O: Then let's try it.
- (Sound of the Old Woman mumbling) -
Ko: Huh-. Looks like she couldn't wait any longer and is coming in. Well then, she'll surely believe it. (Exits briefly.) <339>
Ko: (Re-enters, having adjusted his clothes, puffed out his chest, put on a grave expression, clears his throat loudly, and sits down.)
O: Madam, please come this way.
Old Woman: Here? (Looks around.)
O: This way, please. (Pointing to Ko) This gentleman is the president of our company.
Ko: (Standing up) Please have a seat.
Old Woman: No, I'm fine.
O: (To Ko) President, this madam is the owner of the rice shop in front of my house, the one I've mentioned before. She has truly been incredibly kind to my household.
Ko: Oh-. Is that so? (To the Old Woman) Thank you very much. For being so helpful even while running your business.
Old Woman: Oh, not at all. It's just that this young man is quite respectable, so I trusted him and gave him goods worth two thousand yang from my meager means.
Ko: Indeed. Truly, in this bright world where even businesses making large profits raise hell over just a few won, where else would you find someone like you, Madam, who has waited patiently for so many months?
O: That's true, President. Honestly, she hasn't raised her voice even once until now, just waiting for payment, which makes me feel even more sorry.
Old Woman: Goodness, what things you say. (To Ko) President.
Where does a person lie? It's the money that lies.
Ko: Quite right. Ahem. By the way, Madam, the fact that this Mr. O has been indebted to you all this time is actually more my fault.
Old Woman: Goodness, you say the most unexpected things. A gentleman who runs such a large company...
Ko: This isn't the company. This is my home where I live. The company is that four-story building over in Kurigae.
Old Woman: Oh? So this large Western-style house is your residence?
Ko: Yes... Indeed. By the way, Madam, due to some major issue at our company recently, we haven't been able to pay several months' worth of salaries to our employees.
That's why Mr. O ended up in this situation too.
O: Madam, I'm truly sorry.
Old Woman: Oh, don't mention it at all.
Ko: However, things have recently improved, and we plan to pay all the back wages by the end of this month.
So, could you please wait just a little longer until then?
Since you've waited this long already... huh... ahem.
Old Woman: Alright then, I'll trust your word, President, and leave. (To O) And Mr. O Chu-sa, make sure you don't fail me then, just as the President said. <340>
O: Of course, I'm a human being too.
Ko: Don't worry about anything, just go on your way.
Old Woman: Yes, then I'll trust you and go.
Ko: Yes, please go safely. (To O) Hey, you, since you seem busy today, stay here a bit before heading to the company.
O: Yes. (To the Old Woman) Well then, goodbye, Madam.
Old Woman: Right-. I'm leaving then. Mr. O Chu-sa. See you later then. Oh, I should stop by and see Officer Kim on my way. Hohoho, President, my son might not be brilliant, but he's shrewdly clever when it comes to business.
The Officer Kim I'm going to see now is also my son's friend. My son has police friends and is also friendly with the neighborhood representative. Well- goodbye. Mr. O Chu-sa, I'm off. (Exits)
- (The two men exchange bitter smiles) -
O: Phew, I'm saved now.
Ko: Good grief, she has quite the appearance. Like a demon straight out of hell.
O: Even the demon melted completely today. All her usual feistiness disappeared, and she left quite meekly.
Ko: Anyway, thanks to you, I got to be president for once. Huh... Mr. O.
O: Damn it, even when acting, I get the lower part. Ha... So, what have you been up to lately?
Ko: Me? Don't ask. This is my situation.
O: What? Even after graduating from university, you can't find a way to make a living?
Ko: Humph, no matter how hard I try, nothing works out. I'm in big trouble now too; I'm already engaged, but I need to land something, anything, before I can even think about marriage.
O: Then why did you come here?
Ko: Well, partly to ask Chae-bu for help finding a job, and also because of some circumstances.
O: What circumstances?
Ko: It's a cousin to your situation.
O: What? A debt collector?
Ko: No, actually, I got engaged a month ago. Initially, I told the woman I worked at some company. But although she's uneducated, she's quite sharp. And she's a working woman too.
But I foolishly lied about having a job, when I should have just been honest from the start.
O: What, did she find out?
Ko: It would be better if she did.
O: No? Then why? <341>
Ko: I've managed to keep it a secret until now, but coincidentally, her family recently moved near my house. So now, every morning on her way to work, she stops by and asks me to walk with her. So I have no choice but to come out with her. And then I slip into this house, telling her this is my company president's house where I stop by on my way... And so, for some time now, I've been 'commuting' to this reception room.
O: Huh... Good grief, you were born under a lucky star too, huh...
Il-lyong: (Rushes in from the left) Mr. Ko! Someone's here to see you.
Ko: What? Who?
Il-lyong: It's... a woman. (Smiles.)
Ko: What? How did she know I was here?
Il-lyong: Well... she asked, "Is Mr. Ko T'ae-sik, the Managing Director of the company where this house's president works, still here?" So I told her that gentleman is indeed here, but he's not the Managing Director.
Ko: What did you say?
Il-lyong: Then she got really annoyed.
Ko: Aigoo, you crazy fool.
Il-lyong: Why are you cursing unnecessarily? Then I'll tell her to come in.
Ko: (Just smacks his lips.)
O: Yes, please ask her to come in.
Il-lyong: Yes. (Looks back, smiles, and exits)
Ko: This is big trouble. Ok-yŏp must be here.
O: What? The fiancée you just mentioned?
Ko: Yes.
O: So you actually passed yourself off as the Managing Director?
Ko: Humph, that's right. If I'm going to lie, might as well go all the way, right? It's a lie either way... Huh, but why did she leave work so early today?
Humph, this is real trouble.
O: Hey, I have a good idea.
Ko: What?
O: Since you're already doing it, why not play the president role one more time?
If you're going to lie, you should be thorough.
Ko: Good grief. How?
O: What? How? Just like before. Here- sit like this, put your leg up on the table like this, cover your face with a newspaper, and pretend to be asleep.
Ko: Why?
O: Just do it, it'll work out.
(Il-lyong's voice: "Please come this way.")
(Ok-yŏp's voice: "Yes.")
Ko: Eek, she's coming in. No, how can I... (Does as O instructed.)
O: (Sits off to one side) <342>
Il-lyong: (Entering) Yes, please come in.
O: (Standing up) Welcome.

Il-lyong: But he's sleeping inside.
O: Huh, just go inside, I said. I'll speak to him.
Il-lyong: He's sleeping inside.
O: Just go inside quickly.
Il-lyong: (Confused, exits towards the front)
O: I've never seen such a clueless fellow. Seeing him sleeping right here, yet saying he's sleeping inside. (Looks at Ok-yŏp) My apologies. You came to see President Ko, right?
Ok-yŏp: (Hesitantly) What? President Ko? I thought he was the Managing Director.

Ok-yŏp: Oh.

Ok-yŏp: Well...

Oh my, the house is really nice. By the way, sir, are you sure you're not deceiving me again? Is this really not your house?

**Summary**

The play "The Fake President" depicts the intertwined struggles of Ko T'ae-sik, an unemployed university graduate, and O Rak-ch'ŏn, a poor writer, in 1930s Kyŏngsŏng. T'ae-sik pretends to work for his wealthy friend, President Kim Chae-bu, to impress his fiancée, Ok-yŏp. Rak-ch'ŏn, hounded by a persistent creditor from a rice shop, convinces T'ae-sik to impersonate President Kim to vouch for him. The deception unravels when the creditor returns with backup, Ok-yŏp arrives unexpectedly with her sister, and the real President Kim emerges, exposing both lies. T'ae-sik is thrown out, but Ok-yŏp chooses to stay with him, valuing the person over the pretense of status.

From a historical perspective, the play offers insights into the social and economic pressures of colonial Korean society. It highlights the desperation caused by unemployment among the educated class and the financial precarity faced by intellectuals, forcing them into acts of deception to maintain appearances or survive. The sharp class distinctions between the wealthy industrialist (Kim), the struggling educated men (Ko, O), the working woman (Ok-yŏp), the shrewd creditor (Old Woman), and the servant (Il-lyong) are clearly drawn. The emphasis on status (President, Managing Director) and the shame associated with poverty and joblessness reflect the societal values of the time under Japanese rule. Historians might find the portrayal of economic hardship, the resulting moral compromises, and the underlying critique of materialism particularly revealing about life in urban colonial Korea.
