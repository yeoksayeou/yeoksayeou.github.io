**Body Text Translation:**
Memoirs of a Famous Reporter from That Time (2), The Poet Saint of Peace Who Visited the East
						Chin Sun-sŏng
Sun-sŏng (瞬星) is the pen name of Mr. Chin Hak-mun (秦學文), a newspaperman who once published the magazine "Ch'ŏngch'un" (「靑春」雜誌) in a manner similar to a coterie with figures like Hyŏn Sang-yun (玄相允), Yi Kwang-su (李光洙), and Ch'oe Nam-sŏn (崔南善); later became a member of the weekly review "Tongmyŏng" (週刊評論 「東明」); and served as the editor-in-chief of the Sidae Ilbo (時代日報). During that time, he also produced many fine literary works. This piece being drafted here is a record of his meeting with the Indian poet-sage, old Mr. Tagore (타골-翁), when the latter visited Tokyo while the author was studying at Waseda University (早稻田大學) in Tokyo, acting as a reporter for the "Ch'ŏngch'un" magazine. Thinking back, it was already 18 years ago.

Thinking back, it was already 18 years ago, on July 11, 1916. That day, which feels distant even in recollection, at noon, a group of 23 people stood waiting for the train from Tokyo Station to Yokohama, intending to visit the Indian poet-sage Mr. Tagore, who was staying at Honmoku Sankei-en (本牧三溪園) in Yokohama City. Among them were men and women, students and teachers. Besides Japanese people, there were also Chinese, Taiwanese, and a Hawaiian woman. Among them were two Koreans: C. and myself.
Having heard beforehand that the teacher (先生, referring to Tagore) was coming to Japan, I had hoped for an opportunity to visit him. Just then, through a friend's plan, such a visiting group was formed, and becoming a member filled me with indescribable joy. However, on the other hand, I couldn't suppress my embarrassment – it wasn't that meeting such a world-renowned figure wasn't an honor for a poorly learned and ignorant person (淺學淺識의 徒) like me, but what questions should I ask, and what answers could I give! I worried secretly. Amidst this, a sliver of hope was, "C. is here!"<120>

The train carrying us left Tokyo Station and neared Shinagawa (品川). People began murmuring amongst themselves, discussing what would be appropriate to say. Should individuals ask their own questions, or should a specific topic be presented for the teacher's lecture? Discussions varied, but eventually, it was decided to request the teacher's lecture on the topic of "The New Life of Modern Youth" (現代靑年의 새 生活).
Amidst the discussions, what particularly caught my attention was how these people, meeting for the first time, various types of individuals, interacted intimately, saying "you" and "I" (네야 내야하며), appearing no different than friends of ten years. I recalled a story I once read in a book: a certain Western couple, whenever they were about to quarrel, would invariably cover the portrait of the Holy Mother (聖母의 肖像) hanging on the wall with paper before fighting. It means that there is no conflict (爭鬪) under the sacred. Teacher Tagore's intangible virtue was enough to make these people from different countries, meeting for the first time, friends as if they had known each other forever.
Getting off the train and entering the gate of Sankei-en (三溪園), to the right were lush green trees (樹木이 蒼然하고), and to the left stood a solitary peak (孤峯). Along the path climbing towards the peak, ponds on both sides were filled with fragrant lotus flowers (蓮花). At the far end opposite, there was a seaside bathing spot where naked children ran here and there. The vastness of the area (境域) and the natural beauty (景勝) made it hard to believe it was within private grounds. (Sankei-en is the villa (別莊) of the Yokohama magnate (豪客) Mr. Hara Tomitarō (原當太郞氏), who offered a part of it for Mr. Tagore's accommodation (起臥) during his stay in Japan.)
Our group, dripping with sweat, reached the mountaintop. Amidst the dense green trees stood a refined Japanese-style house (日本制의 精灑한 집), and beside it were a Chinese-style pavilion (支那式의 亭子) and an ancient-style five-storied pagoda (古式 五重塔). We all straightened our collars reverently and waited for the teacher in front of the Chinese-style pavilion. After a good while, the teacher, wearing a tea-brown velvet hat and a flowing white silk Indian robe (印度 道袍), his grey hair and white beard fluttering, came up and said, "Everyone, I am terribly sorry. I was delayed because I went to see a doctor today due to a toothache. Please forgive me." His simple greeting and momentary smile evoked infinite sympathy and respect in people.
We escorted the teacher into the pavilion. He sat down facing a desk (冊床), and we sat in rows facing him. A floral wreath expressing the group's respect was presented to him. He accepted it with a pleased expression, placed it around his neck, and offered thanks with clasped hands in the Indian style (印度式으로 合掌謝禮한다). Ancient Japanese incense (日本 古式의 焚香) was burned. A gentle breeze spread the classical fragrance throughout the quiet pavilion, intoxicating people. Each person stood up in turn to state their name and country of origin. When my turn came, feeling the utmost solemnity (崇嚴), my heart pounded for no reason.
(My name is Hakmoon chin, I came frem Corea)<121> I blurted out in a strained voice (燥音) and sat right back down. Later, thinking how overly frantic (狂的) my voice had sounded, I couldn't help but blush secretly. After the introductions, Professor Nakagiri Kakutarō (中桐確太郞氏) from Waseda University (早稻田大學) stood up and, in simple words on behalf of the group, expressed gratitude to the teacher for meeting us despite his busy schedule. He added that the young people gathered today wished to hear the teacher's lecture on the topic "The New Life of Modern Youth." The teacher closed his eyes tightly, meditated (黙想) for a moment, then calmly opened his mouth: "This question is very large and important. I don't know whether my words will satisfy you or dissatisfy you, but the question of why humans are born, for what purpose they are born, has troubled many minds for a long time. However, when we look at the Great World (大世界), what do we feel? When we see the solemn, lush forests hiding all mysteries, when we see the plains where the five grains flourish and a hundred flowers bloom, when we see the vast, free, eternal ocean of freedom, when we hear the beautiful songs of birds and the lovely breathing sounds of fish—when we see this Great World, what indeed do you feel? I, I cannot help but feel infinite love, light, and joy! Love, light, and joy! People of the world are always absorbed only in material civilization (物質文明), lacking spiritual sublimity (精神上의 崇高함). Between individuals, societies, and nations, they build ramparts (城郭), focusing only on distinguishing things (事物을 區別하기에만 焦心하나). But material life is not true life. Distinguishing things does not increase happiness. We yearn (渴求하며) for something beyond material civilization. If we cling (固執하고) only to individuals and nations, I think it's utterly difficult to taste the flavor of true life. True life... why are we born? For what purpose are we born? ... However, when I face that Great World, I always see 'suimaremeimaerson' (神, God). I believe humans are born to see this God. Born to feel the spirit of the universe (宇宙의 靈)."
Like this, for over an hour, with his beautiful, music-like voice, he spoke fluently, raising and lowering his tone. When passion arose, he clenched his fist or struck the desk with his palm. Sometimes he closed his eyes tightly, seeming to chant (吟誦) inwardly; other times he seemed to be seeing something. The entire group listened attentively (慇聽) in a quiet manner without a single cough. Afterward, three or four individuals asked personal questions, and the teacher answered politely (謹懃히) and in detail (仔細하게). After taking a commemorative photograph, we bade farewell (作別하고) to the teacher and exited the Sankei-en gate. It was 6 p.m. An irrepressible, joyful smile, as if intoxicated by sweet wine, graced everyone's lips.

The first visit ended joyfully like this. About a week later, I attempted a second visit. This was not just for personal greetings but also to request some writing from him to publish in our Ch'ŏngch'un magazine. <122> I went to Sankei-en with two or three friends and sent in my calling card (通剌). His nephew (從子) came out and invited us in. Treading carefully (操心하야), lifting my feet quietly, I followed his guidance into a spacious Japanese room. The view opened up directly to the blue sea, where the hot sunlight shattered into thousands, ten thousands of pieces, sparkling dazzlingly. Across the sea, the Miura Peninsula (三浦半島) jutted out before our eyes, seemingly close enough to answer if called. The room's decorations were simple (淡淡하고). A single flower arrangement in a vase (甁揷花) in the tokonoma alcove seemed to welcome the guests (來客). On a desk at the edge of the veranda lay a typewriter (Tyimaewriter misspelling of typewriter?) and Western manuscript paper. A tiger skin (虎皮) draped over a pair of chairs (雙椅子) fluttered softly in the gentle breeze blowing from the pine forest to the left, subtly evoking (誘發한다) feelings of my homeland (故國의 情調) within me. We knelt on the designated cushions and waited for the teacher. Shortly after, the teacher, with the same profound smile on his white-haired, youthful face (白髮童顔), wearing a Japanese yukata (日本浴衣) with a Hyōgo-obi (兵庫帶) hanging long in front, calmly came out, sat on a cushion, and stroking his white, abundant beard (鬚髥), said, "It's very kind of you to visit like this in the heat." His words didn't sound like mere polite greetings. I respectfully lowered my voice, "I am extremely happy and honored to have this opportunity to see you often, Teacher. I hear that you will be going to America (美國) soon. Around when might that be?"
"Well, I'm thinking of going around the end of August."
"Then, when might you return to your home country (本國)?"
"That cannot be predicted (預期할 수 업소)... Is Korea (朝鮮) also this hot?"
"Yes. Because Korea's climate is continental, it gets very hot when it's hot."
"On this trip, I really wanted to visit Korea and China (支那), but due to lack of time, I couldn't manage it, which I find deeply regrettable (遺憾)."
"Teacher, I know you are busy, but would it be possible for you to write something, anything, for the Korean youth who yearn (渴求하는) for a new life? If you could write for us, our gratitude would be beyond words, and I believe the response (反響) would be many times greater than that received by Western philosophers (西歐哲人) or writers (文人) who have written for us."
"Yes. For which magazine is this **?"
"Yes. It is to be published (揭載하랴 함니다) in the only magazine ** for all of Korea, called 'Ch'ŏngch'un' (「靑春」)."
"That must be a Korean-language (朝鮮文) magazine, I presume?"
"Yes, it is."
"I am very busy writing the drafts (草案) for my lectures in America here, so I cannot write anything long, but if a short piece is acceptable (無妨하다면), I will write it for you."
"Yes, length doesn't matter at all."
Thus, the Teacher readily agreed to write for our Ch'ŏngch'un.
Just then, a friend who came with me asked, "Teacher, I intend to engage in education (敎育에 從事코저 함니다) from now on. Could you please tell me <123> what method would be most appropriate?" The teacher closed his eyes tightly for a moment, then said, "When I was in my home country, young people often came to ask about their future paths. Each time, I told them to go to the countryside, establish a boarding school (塾舍를 設始하고), and engage in education. Whether the students number two or three doesn't matter. I do not believe true human life exists in the city. Many people drive cars or carriages through complex city streets, looking proud and triumphant (意氣揚揚하게), but thinking again, isn't the wealth and glory (富貴榮華) of the floating world truly empty? Rather than living a life without fruit or seed, go to the countryside, sing of nature with simple (純朴한) children, praise (稱頌하야) God, gradually realize the beauty of nature, see God, feel the infinite eternal (無限 永遠), and connect (連하는) with the spirit of the universe – isn't that where true life is found? If one person guides (引導하고) five or ten people, and those five or ten people each guide another five or ten, how can that be considered a small thing? However, the qualification (資格) for the person guiding must be limited to those with high virtue, great, long-lasting, and universal (普遍的) love. You say you want to become an educator now, but if you truly want to become an educator, you must first become a child yourself. You must become purely a child and run and play with them. In doing so, instill in those pure and innocent children the notion that you are a companion, not the concept (觀念) that you are their teacher. Then, gradually teach and edify (敎化시겨야) them. Do you understand? It is easy for a young child to hear their parents' words and repeat them, but things taught by setting times at school, making them sweat, are easily forgotten. I also run a school (學校를 經營하오) in my home country, but it's true freedom. In that large forest, those who want to run freely run, those who want to climb trees climb trees, playing as they please, perhaps meditating, perhaps singing, learning what needs to be learned without realizing it (不知不識 中에). I hope you do not forget this meaning." Thus, he earnestly (諄諄히) explained the fundamental principle (本旨) of education. We stood up and asked the teacher for a photograph. He said, "A photo? Let's take one. Do you have a good camera? But let me change my clothes first. I want to be photographed wearing Indian clothes." He went inside, came out changed into an Indian robe (印度 道袍), and said, "Alright, let's take it." We went up to the rooftop terrace (屋上露臺) and took a commemorative photo. Afterward, the teacher turned to me and said, "I will send the writing I promised earlier (악가 約束한 글) within a few days."

It was a cloudy evening. The sky, low as if about to weep, looked like rain would fall soon. On the deck (甲板) of the small ship Canada-gō (카나다號) moored at the Yokohama pier (橫濱埠頭), standing forlornly and watching the cargo (卜物?) <124> being loaded, was the Indian poet-sage, old Mr. Tagore (타쿠로 翁), who just a couple of months prior had been deafened by the enthusiastic cheers (熱狂한 幾萬 群衆의 탄성) of tens of thousands at Tokyo Station. When the departure bell rang, he, accompanied by a group of Indian guests, the family of Mr. Hara Tomitarō (原富太郞氏) with whom he had stayed for a long time, and barely fewer than 10 other people bidding farewell (餞別), set off on the long sea route (海路) towards America. He looked towards the desolate pier with apparent sadness and bowed with clasped hands (合掌叩頭한다). What must have been in Old Man Tagore's heart at that moment!
Ah, the eventful journey of the poet-sage! I stood watching him until the ship disappeared upon the ocean waves. The poet-sage continuously expressed his gratitude and also watched me from the deck for a long time. Amidst the lightly falling drizzle, the Canada-gō, floating far out at sea, vanished into the mist and clouds.
Taking this opportunity to introduce this great poet once again: Our Mr. Rabindranath Tagore (라빈드라나드 타쿠로), a great prophet (預言者) of the 20th century disseminating the spiritual civilization of the East to the world, comes from a prominent and noble family (名門巨族) for generations. His ancestors (祖先) included distinguished figures (著名한 士) in politics, law, music, art, and religion. Mr. Tagore's grandfather, Mr. Dwarkanath Tagore (드와가나드 타쿠로), was a leading figure in Calcutta (칼커타), managing various companies (諸種 會社를 總攝하고), engaging in diverse businesses (各種 商業에 着手하야), and commanding considerable respect (人望) in the commercial and industrial world. Furthermore, he was a generous (寬大하고) and compassionate (慈悲한) large landowner (大地主). In this sense, he was revered (尊崇을 밧앗) as a savior (救世主) of the poor and unfortunate. Mr. Tagore's father, Mr. Debendranath Tagore (데빈드라나드, 타쿠로), made significant contributions (多大한 供獻) to the Indian religious reform movement (印度宗敎 改革運動) called the Brahmo Samaj (부라하마, 사마지 / 上天敎會), later receiving the venerable title (尊號) "Maharshi" (마하루시). Our Mr. Tagore inherited this noble lineage (高貴한 血統) and let out his first cry (呱呱의 聲) in 1860 in the city of Calcutta on the banks of the Ganges River (간디쓰江畔). Possessing the disposition (素質) of a poet, philosopher, and religious figure from the time he was in his mother's womb (母親의 胎內), Mr. Tagore, from his childhood, held a burning admiration (불붓는 듯한 景仰) for his homeland's great art and sublime religion. He also shed fervent tears (熱淚) drawn from his heart over the vast difference (絶大한 殊異) between the past and present. His passionate devotion (熱烈한 精誠) to history, art, and religion eventually led him towards the infinite called Nature.
The Teacher harbored extreme aversion (非常한 反感) towards stifling school education. He often rebelled (反抗) against teachers, asked dissatisfied questions (不平한 質問), and refused to answer (答述을 拒絶), consequently always ranking last (恒常 末席에 잇섯스나). However, he consistently achieved the top score (首席) in written exams. His father, knowing his disposition (性癖), left Calcutta where they had lived for many years and went with the young Tagore to the Himalayas (히마라야) to live in the forest, teaching him Sanskrit (梵語), English, Bengali (벤갈語), astronomy (天文學), etc. Indeed, this Himalayan forest life provided the most fitting influence (適當한 感化) for him later to become a great poet of nature worship.
The Teacher lost his mother at age 11<125> and spent his days in tears of grief (悲歎). At that time, he was living with his father in a garden pavilion (園亭) on the banks of the Ganges (간디쓰江畔). In front of it was a small ferry landing (渡船場). As evening fell, a few people, weary (疲困한) from the day's labor (勞働), stood by the slowly flowing river. The sound of them calling for the ferryboat shattered the twilight tranquility (黃昏의 靜淑을 께치여), echoing (反響을 일희키고) off the distant mountains opposite. As the setting sun silently passed over the horizon (地平線上), clouds like torn cotton dyed purple in the clear blue sky (맑은 蒼空). Returning yellow cows lowed mournfully (슬믜우는 소리) in the distance, as if bidding farewell to the day. Young Tagore, living in solitude (孤寂히) after losing his mother, would always open the window facing the mountain at such sunset times (夕陽 때마다), stare blankly at the blue waters of the Ganges, and let out deep sighs. What always came to his mind at such times was the scene (光景) of his mother's deathbed (臨終할 때): "Why did mother have to go? Why did she go to such a faraway land!" he would cry out at the end of his grief. But there was no answer, only the great Ganges River, veiled in dim light, flowing slowly onward in silence.
At age 17, the Teacher studied abroad in England (英國), diligently researching English poetry, toured the European continent (歐洲大陸), and after returning to India, devoted himself entirely (全心으로) to the study of Nature.
Like his grandfather and father, the Teacher also had experiences of passionate love (熱烈한 慈愛의 經驗) in his youth, tasting the fierce agony (激烈한 懊惱) of passion (情熱) and instinct (本能) day and night. His poetry collections like "Gitanjali" (기단쟈리) are echoes (反響) of that love, and his play "Chitra" (치트라) also expresses his fervent thoughts on love. However, he was resurrected (復活되야) from blind love, and the wreaths he offered to the beloved person came to be offered to the absolute God (絶對한 神). That is, he entered love from love, emerged from love, and entered a life of pure, harmonious faith (純一和融한 信仰).
In 1912, he traveled again to England and America. When he first toured Europe at age 17, he was treated everywhere as an Indian troublemaker, a subject of the British Empire (屬英의 人稱이라 하야), met with contempt (侮蔑) and ridicule (嘲弄). This time, however, he was welcomed as a great thinker of India, a great poet-sage, a great man from an ancient nation (老大國 偉人), a representative of Buddhists (佛徒의 代表者), and especially as a saint produced (産出한 聖徒) by the primeval forest. Yet, the Teacher was extremely humble (謙讓하고) and modest (恭遜하얏스니). This humble attitude and elegant, gentle demeanor (優雅溫良한 풍채) truly amazed (驚異케 하야) Westerners. Leading critics of the time joined their pens to exclaim (絶呌하얏더라), "His voice should not be heard in the streets of London; his profound (玄妙한) voice is truly the voice of God to be heard in Heaven."
The Teacher now resides in Santiniketan (압부로 平和鄕 - *likely referring to Santiniketan*), established by his father, dreaming a life of contemplation (黙想). He established an open-air school (露天學校) in the primitive forest, engaging in the education of village children. In the open air, there are only two old houses (老屋), and under an old tree lies his father's stone seat for meditation (黙想하는 石座).
World literature's judgment (世界文學이 栽判), for reasons of racial difference, would certainly never ignore (黙殺할리 <126> 萬無하야) a world-great poet-sage. Our Indian saint, Mr. Rabindranath Tagore (라빈드라나드,타구르), received the most prestigious Nobel Prize (노벨償金) in 1913. This was truly the first time (효시라) an Eastern person received the Nobel Prize. Not only did India's standing (面目) renew thanks to the Teacher, but it should be called an honor for all of us Eastern people.
Among the Teacher's many works (著作), the most famous plays (戱曲) are "The King of the Dark Chamber" (闇室의 王), "The Post Office" (郵便局), and "Chitra" (치트라). His poetry collections (詩集) include "Gitanjali" (기단쟈리), "The Crescent Moon" (新月), and "The Gardener" (園丁). In philosophy (哲學方面), there is "Sadhana: The Realisation of Life" (生의 實現).

An excerpt from "Gitanjali" (기단쟈리)

I am going on a long journey. Farewell, brothers! I bid you farewell many times.
Here I leave the key to my door – and I renounce all rights to my home. I only wish to hear kind words of farewell (餞送) from you all.
We have lived together as neighbors and friends (동내 친고) for a long time. It seems I have received more than I have given. Ah, the day has already dawned, the lamp lit in the dark corner is extinguished (꺼지고), and the sermon (說敎) begins. Well then, well then, I am going. Farewell, brothers.

An excerpt from "The Gardener" (園丁)

Hey! Don't hide the secrets of your heart.
Tell them only to me, just to me.
You who smile sweetly (방긋이), hey, whisper softly.
I will listen with my heart, not my ears.
The night is deep, the house is quiet (종용하오).
The little bird's nest (보금자리) has awoken from its dream.
With hesitant (躊躇하는) tears, flickering (너른너른하는) smiles, shame (치욕), and pain as intermediaries (중매삼아),
Tell me the secrets of what follows.

An excerpt from "The Crescent Moon" (新月)

'When evening comes and that round full moon gets caught in the branches of the Kadamba tree (가담樹), can't anyone catch that moon?' I asked absently (무심코). My elder brother looked at me and laughed.
'Hey, you're the biggest fool I know. Isn't the moon always so far from the earth like that? Who could possibly catch it?' he says.
'Brother, you are the fool! When mother watches us play from that window and smiles, do you think mother is far away?'
Again, my brother says, 'You really are an idiot (天痴). Where will you find something big enough to catch that moon?'
I say, 'Brother, you can definitely catch it with your own hands.' My brother laughs and says, 'You really are the biggest fool among the people I know. If that moon came close, do you know how big it would be?'
I say, 'Brother, brother, why do they teach such nonsense (믓생긴 소리) at your school! When mother bends down (수기실 때) to kiss (입마추시랴고) us, does mother's face look that big?'
But my brother still says, 'You really are an idiot.' (End)<127>

**Summary**

This memoir by Chin Sun-sŏng (pen name of Chin Hak-mun) recounts his meeting with the Indian poet Rabindranath Tagore in Japan in 1916. As a student reporter for the Korean magazine "Ch'ŏngch'un," Chin joined a diverse group visiting Tagore at Sankei-en in Yokohama. He describes Tagore's presence, his lecture on "The New Life of Modern Youth" emphasizing spirituality over materialism and universal connection, and a subsequent personal visit where Tagore agreed to write for "Ch'ŏngch'un" and discussed his educational philosophy centered on nature and becoming childlike. The article concludes with Tagore's quiet departure for America and a brief biography highlighting his family background, aversion to formal schooling, spiritual journey, international recognition (including the Nobel Prize), and major works, ending with excerpts from "Gitanjali," "The Gardener," and "The Crescent Moon."

From a historical perspective, this article is significant as it documents a direct encounter between a Korean intellectual and a major global figure like Tagore during the Japanese colonial period, offering a rare glimpse into transnational intellectual exchange involving Koreans at the time. The discussion themes—the spiritual crisis of modernity, the role of youth, and alternative education—resonate with the concerns of Korean intellectuals seeking new paths under colonial constraints. The specific mention of requesting and receiving a contribution for "Ch'ŏngch'un," a key Korean literary and cultural magazine, underscores the efforts of Koreans to engage with world thought and assert their cultural presence despite limitations.

기사제목	名記者 그 時節 回想(2), 東洋來訪의 平和의 詩聖
필자	秦瞬星
기사형태	회고·수기

名記者 그 時節 回想(2), 東洋來訪의 平和의 詩聖
						秦瞬星
瞬星이라 함은 秦學文氏의 雅號로 일즉 玄相允, 李光洙, 崔南善氏 等과 同人制 비슷하게 「靑春」雜誌를 刊行하엿고 그 뒤 週刊評論 「東明」의 同人이엇고 時代日報 編輯局長까지 歷任한 新聞人이라 그동안 文藝에 대한 佳什도 만엇다. 여기 草하는 一編은 氏가 일즉 東京 早稻田大學에 在學時 마츰 印度 詩聖 타골-翁이 東京來遊가 잇서 그때 「靑春」 雜誌記者의 資格으로 會見한 記錄이니 생각건데 벌써 18年 前 일이엇다.

생각하면 그때가 벌서 18年 前인 1916년 7월 11일의 일이엇다. 回想하기에도 아득한 그날 正午 橫濱市 本牧三溪園에 체류하야 잇는 印度 詩聖 타골 선생을 訪問코자 東京驛에서 橫濱 가 는 車를 기다리고 섯는 23인의 一團이 잇스니 그 중에는 남자도 잇고 여자도 잇다. 학생도 잇고 敎師도 잇스며 日本사람은 물론이어니와 支那人도 잇고 臺灣人과 하와이婦人도 잇다. 그 중에 朝鮮 사람도 두 사람이 잇스니 C君과 나라.
나는 前부터 선생이 일본에 오신다는 말을 듯고 한 번 기회를 어더 방문코자 하든 次에 마침友人의 계획으로 이러한 訪問團이 成立되야 그 中의 一員으로 參與케 되니 깃븜은 이로 말하기 어렵다. 하나 한편으로 부끄러움도 禁할 수 업다-나와 가치 淺學淺識의 徒가 그와 갓흔 世界的 偉人을 맛나 보는 것이 光榮이 아닌 바는 아니나 무슨 말을 무르며 무슨 말을 대답할고! 하고 남몰래 애를 썻다. 하는 中에도 한 줄기의 밋븜은 『C君이 잇스넛가 !』<120>
우리를 탠 車는 東京驛을 떠나 品川近傍에 오니 各其 수군거리며 무슨 말을 해야 올을가 或 個人으로 自己의 疑問을 뭇는 것이 올을가 또는 一定한 問題를 提供하야 先生의 講話를 듯는 것이 조흘가 하고 議論이 不一하다가 결국은 『現代靑年의 새 生活』이란 문제로 선생의 講話를 듯기로 決定되엿다.
서로 議論하는 中에도 특히 나의 注意를 끄는 것은 처음 만나는 이들의 各種 사람이 서로  네야 내야하며 親近히 이 야기하는 것이 10年 舊交와 다름업시 뵈인다. 내 일즉이 어느 冊에서 본 이야기가 생각나니 그는 西洋 엇더한 夫婦가 內外 싸홈을 하랴 하면 반다시 벽에 걸닌 聖母의 肖像을 조희로 가리고 싸홈을 한다 하는 이야기라. 거룩한 아래에서는 爭鬪가 업다함이니 타 先生의 無形할 德은 이 各나라 처음 만나는 사람들로 하야금 足히 써 一面如舊의 벗을 만드는도다.
車에서 내려 三溪園門에 드러서니 오른편에는 樹木이 蒼然하고 왼편에는 兀然한 孤峯이 웃둑섯다. 그 峯을 向하야 올라가는 길에는 左右편 못에 香氣로운 蓮花가 가득 찻고 마즌편 끗헤는 海水浴場이 잇서 벌거버슨 아해들이 이리 뛰고 저리 뛰니 그 境域의 넓음과 자연의 景勝이 아모리 보아도 門戶안이라고는 생각할 수 업다. (三溪園이라 하는 곳은 橫濱 豪客 原當太郞氏의 別莊으로 타 先生이 日本에 滯在하야 게실 동안 그 一部를 先生의 起臥에 提供한 곳이라.)
우리의 一團이 땀을 뻘뻘 흘리고 峯上에 올라가니 창울한 樹木속에서 日本制의 精灑한 집이 한 채 잇고 그 졋헤는 支那式의 亭子와 古式 五重塔이 잇다. 一同이 정숙히 옷깃을 발니하고 支那式 亭子 압헤서 先生을 기다린지 良久에 선생이 茶褐色 우단 帽子에 흰 綢屬 印度 道袍를 기름이 입고 灰色 머리와 흰 수염을 푸르르 날리면서 올라와 「여러분 大段 未安햇소. 내가 오늘 齒痛으로 해 醫師에게 갓다 오느라고 조곰 느젓소. 용서하오」하는 簡單한 인사와 순간의 微笑는 사람으로 하야금 無限한 同情과 尊敬의 念을 生케 한다.

아아 變化 만은 詩聖의 旅行이여. 나는 그 배가 洋上에 떠 보이지 아니할 때까지 그를 바라보고 섯섯다. 詩聖은 줄곳 感謝하다 하는 뜻을 表하고 自己 亦 오래동안 甲板 우에서 나를 바라보고 잇다. 가는 비가 부실부실 가로날니는 中 海上에 멀니 뜬 카나다號가 雲霧 속으로 몸을 감춘다.
이 機會에 이 大詩人을 거듭 한 번 紹介하건대 20세기의 一大 預言者로 東洋의 精神的 文明을 世界속에 傳播하는 우리 「라빈드라나드 타쿠로」선생은 代代 名門巨族으로 그 祖先에는 政治, 法律, 音樂, 美術, 宗敎 各 方面에 著名한 士가 輩出하얏스니 타先生의 祖父 「드와가나드 타쿠로」氏는 칼커타 第 一流의 人物이라. 諸種 會社를 總攝하고 各種 商業에 着手하야 商工業界에 相當한 人望이 잇슬 뿐 아니라 또 寬大하고 慈悲한 大地主니 이러한 意味로 그는 貧寒하고 불상한 사람의 救世主로 尊崇을 밧앗또 그 타先生의 父親되는 「데빈드라나드, 타쿠로」氏 는 「부라하마, 사마지」(上天敎會)라 하는 印度宗敎 改革運動에 多大한 供獻이 잇서 後에 「마하루시」라 하는 尊號를 밧앗는데 우리 타先生은 이러한 高貴한 血統을 이어 1860年에 간디쓰江畔 컬커타市에서 呱呱의 聲을 질넛더라. 母親의 胎內에 잇슬 때부터 詩人, 哲學者. 宗敎家의 素質을 가진 타先生은 幼年時代로부터 故國의 偉大한 美術과 崇高한 宗敎에 대하야 불붓는 듯한 景抑을 가젓고 또 今昔의 絶大한 殊異에 대하야 가슴에서 끄러나오는 熱淚를 뿌렷섯는데 歷史, 美術, 宗敎에 대한 熱烈한 精誠이 필경 先生으로 하야곰 自然이라 하는 無限한 것 *向하게 하엿더라.
先生은 거북하 學校敎育에 對하야 非常한 反感을 가젓슴으로 각금 가다가 敎師에게 反抗도하얏고 不平한 質問도 하얏스며 答述을 拒絶한 일도 잇서 恒常末席에 잇섯스나 筆記試驗에는 언제든지 首席을 占하얏섯더라. 先生의 父親은 그의 性癖을 알므로 多年 居住하든 캘커타市를 떠나 선생과 함께 히마라야에 가 森林生活을 經營하면서 先生에게 梵語, 英語, 벤갈語, 天文學 等을 가르치니 實로 이 히말라야 森林生活은 後年에 선생으로 하야금 自然崇拜의 大詩人이 되기에 가장 適當한感化를 준 것이더라.
先生이 11세에 母親을 여의고<125> 悲歎의 눈물로써 날을 보냇더라. 그때에는 父親과 함께 간디쓰江畔 園亭에 잇슬 때니 그 압헤 적은 渡船場이 잇서 날이 점을면 하로 勞働에 疲困한 小數 人들이 천천히 흘러가는 내가에 서서 나루ㅅ베 부르는 소리가 黃昏의 靜淑을 께치여 멀니 건너 山에 反響을 일희키고 석양이 말업시 地平線上에 넘어갈 대에 맑은 蒼空에는 솜을 찌즌 듯한 구름이 보라빗흐로 물드리며 도라오는 누른 소가 그날을 作別하는 듯이 먼데서 슬믜우는 소리가 구슬프다. 母親을 열코 孤寂히 지내는 타 선생은 恒常 이러한 夕陽 때마다 山에 向한 窓을 열고 간디쓰江의 푸른물을 우두커니 바라보고 기픈 한숨을 깁히 쉬엿더라. 이러한 때에 늘 그의 머리에 생각나는 것은 自己 母親의 臨終할 때 光景이니 「웨 어머니는 도가 가섯나? 웨 멀고 먼 나라로 가섯나!」하고 悲歎 끗헤 소리처 울더라. 그러나 그곳에는 아모 대답도 업고 어둔 빗에 섀인 간디쓰의 大江이 입담은 채 천천히 흘러갈 뿐이더라.
先生이 17세時에 英國에 遊學하야 熱心으로 英詩를 硏究하고 歐洲大陸을 巡歷하야 다시 印度에 도라온 後 全心으로 自然을 硏究하얏더라.
先生은 또 自己 祖父나 父親과 가치 靑年時代에 熱烈한 慈愛의 經驗이 잇스니 日夜로 情熱과 本能의 激烈한 懊惱를 맛보앗더라. 그의 시집「기단쟈리와 갓흔 것이 그 慈愛의 反響이오 戱曲 「지트라」에도 熱烈한 그의 慈愛에 對한 思想이 表顯되여 잇도다. 그러囑나 그는 盲目的 慈愛로부터 復活되야 그리하는 사람에게 바치든 花輪을 絶對한 神끠 바치게 되엿더라. 卽 그는 慈愛로부터 慈愛에 드러갓고 慈愛에서 나와 純一和融한 信仰의 生活에 드러갓더라.
1912년에 그가 다시 英美諸國에 旅行하니 처음 17세에 歐州에 巡遊할 때에는 印度의 작란군으로 간 곳마다 屬英의 人稱이라 하야 侮蔑과 嘲弄으로 대접밧든 선생이 이 때는 印度의 大思想家 大詩聖 老大國 偉人 佛徒의 代表者 特히 原始的 森林이 産出한 聖徒로써 歡迎을 밧앗다. 하나 先生은 極히 謙讓하고 恭遜하얏스니 이 謙讓한 態度와 優雅溫良한 풍채가 實로 歐美人을 驚異케 하야 當時 一流의 批評家들이 붓을 合하야 「그의 소리는 한 론돈 市街에서 드를 것이 아니라 그의 玄妙한 소리는 實로 天國에서 드를 神의 소리라」 絶呌하얏더라.
先生은 지금 自己 父親이 開拓한 압부로 平和鄕에 잇서 黙想의 生活을 꿈이고 原始的 森林속에 露天學校을 設立하야 村落 子弟의 敎育에 從事하니 露天에는 단지 二個의 老屋이 잇슬 뿐이오 老木 下에는 그 父親의 黙想하는 石座가 잇더라.
世界文學이 栽判은 人種相異의 理由로써 결코 世界的 大詩聖을 黙殺할리<126> 萬無하야 印度聖者 우리 「라빈드라나드,타구르」先生이 1913년에 가장 榮譽잇는 「노벨償金」을 밧앗스니 實로 東洋 사람으로「노밸償金」을 밧은 것의 효시라. 先生으로 말미아마 印度의 面目이 一新한 것은 勿論이어니와 우리 東洋人 全體의 名譽라 할지로다.
先生의 著作이 만흔 中 가장 有名한 것은 戱曲에 「闇室의 王」, 「郵便局」, 「치트라」가 잇고 詩集에는 「기단쟈리」, 「新月」, 「園丁」이 잇스며 哲學方面에는 「生의 實現」이 잇나니라.

「기단쟈리」의 一節

나는 먼 旅行을 감니다. 兄弟여 安寧히 게십에오! 나는 여러번께 하직을 엿줌니다.
내가 여긔 내 방문 열쇠를 두고 감니다-그러고 나는 집에 對한 모든 權利를 다 버리고 감니다. 단지 나는 여러분께 親切한 餞送의 말슴을 듯고 십습니다.
우리는 서로 오래동안 한 동내 친고로 지냇섯습니다. 내가 들인 이보다 밧은 것이 만흔 듯하외다. 아아 발서 날이 밝아 어둔 구석에 켯든 燈불이 꺼지고 說敎가 始作됨니다. 그럼 그럼 감니다. 兄弟여 安寧히 게십시오.

「新月」의 一節

『저녁이 되여서 저 둥그런 만월이 가담樹 가지에 걸킬 때 누구든지 저 달을 잡을 수 업나?』하고 내가 무심코 말하닛가 兄님이 나를 보고 우스면서.
『얘 너는 내 아는 사람 중 바보다. 달은 언제든지 저러케 땅에서 멀니 떠러저 잇지 아니하냐 누가 저것을 잡을 수가 잇겟니』한다.
『兄님이야말로 바보지! 어머니께서 저 窓에서 우리를 노는 것을 보고 우슬 때 兄님은 어머니께서 멀니 떠러지 계시다고 생각하오?』
또 兄님의 말이 『너는 참 天痴다. 네가 그 달을 잡을 만한 큰물을 어듸서 차저낼테냐?』한다. 나는 『兄任이 自己 손으로 잡을 수가 꼭 잇소』하닛가 兄님이 우스면서 『너야말로 참 내 아는 사람 중 그중 바보다. 만일 저 달이 갓갑게 오면 얼마나 클른지 모르니?』한다.
나는 『兄님. 兄님 學校에서는 웨 그리 믓생긴 소리만 가라치오! 어머니께서 우리들에게 입마추시랴고 수기실 때 어머니 얼골이 그러케 크게 보입니가?』
