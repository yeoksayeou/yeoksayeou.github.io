A Divorce Confession, To Mr. Ch’ŏnggu
						Na Hye-sŏk

Having approached the age of 40 or 50, received specialized education, undertaken a tour of Europe and America that others cannot easily do, and being in a position to guide juniors, failing to unify one's character and failing to unify one's life is not only something both individuals should be ashamed of, but also something for which we have no face before society in general, feel ashamed, and apologize.

Mr. Ch’ŏnggu!
This shock, encountered for the first time in my life, is too deeply wounding and fatal.
Lamentation, weeping, agitation, anguish—while wandering through life on all these paths since then, I call out again to you, Mr. Ch’ŏnggu—, who has been cast to the bottom of the abyss.
Mr. Ch’ŏnggu! As I call your name, my eyes fill with tears. Will the world call me "weak" for this?

How could we know this reality, this fact, this experience of a wound fated in destiny, something we never even dreamed of, between you and me, who face each other daily, who prided ourselves on deep understanding and knowing each other well?
It was all a dream, all a nightmare; I deliberately want to call the past tragedy thus, this is my undisguised truth.
"A good husband"—wasn't this at least the posture<84> that appeared in the path of our past life together? "A good husband"—how much I tried to deny it since the incident, but ultimately, that posture is the Mr. Ch’ŏnggu reviving in my wounded heart now.

In my heart, struck by the blow since the incident, the impressions and recollections of our 11 years of married life flicker on and off. Was there not the slightest dissatisfaction, complaint, or anxiety in anything? Was there anything in your daily life that caused me, your wife, suspicion or displeasure? In the evening, you always returned at the closing time, didn't you? You were a man who showed an affectionate smile to me and the children. You smoked tobacco only in small amounts and had no tolerance for alcohol at all. In this sense, one cannot but say you were a rare "good husband" in the world. Being such a husband, I could not help but trust you. No, I trusted you completely. Who could have dreamt that such a man concealed a fearsome decisiveness, a cruel tendency to reject, on his hidden side? Weren't you the one who gave me not a shred of time, not a shred of leeway, to reflect or repent? Did not foolish I still beg and plead, hoping perhaps for forgiveness?

Having suffered an unprecedented misfortune, lost all credibility in the world, faced universal public anger and criticism, been abandoned by parents and relatives, and lost old, good friends, I am of course unhappy, but you, who carried this out, must also feel considerable lamentation and despair. I can only wander in the wilderness, gaze at the emptiness in the dark night, and lose myself.
Holding a paintbrush and palette in trembling hands, am I heading towards darkness, or am I seeking a moment of brilliance? Having received the shock of a wound too large, too heavy, my heart feels like it's bursting from the impulse to cry and collapse, hearing the desperate cries of life pressing in moment by moment.

Was our marriage a "false marriage," or did our marriage, while joined by mutual understanding and love, fall onto the "crossroads of falsehood" following the flow of that life? I do not want to call our marriage, our life, "false." This is because all preparations, all vows, were already established at the time of marriage, and we had already put them all into practice.

Mr. Ch’ŏnggu!
Having lost both light and darkness, I stand still in this empty state of self-loss and think it necessary to reflect deeply once more. Thinking this way, I stand before a painful resolution. Bearing all the world's ridicule and censure, I intend to carry this cross on my back and proceed silently. While listening to the quiet whispers of life flowing beneath obedience and absolute agony, not knowing if it is light or darkness, I am resolved to continue marching towards rebirth once more.

History Up to the Engagement

It was already long ago, when I was nineteen.<85> My fiancé, whom I was engaged to, died of a chronic illness. The wound in my heart at that time was so severe that I temporarily went mad, and subsequently, nervous exhaustion reached a chronic state. That summer, during the school break, I returned home from Tōkyō. At that time, a visitor came to our detached guest house (사랑), looking for my older brother and also to see me; that person was you, Mr. Ch'ŏnggu. It was already three years since you had lost your wife, so it was a very lonely time for you. I was playing with my niece in the guest house when I ran right into you. Taking this opportunity, my older brother introduced us. A few days later, you went to Kyŏngsŏng and sent me a long letter. It was written frankly and passionately. First, due to your circumstances and the loneliness of mind and body, you needed to remarry, and you hoped I would be that partner. Of course, I did not reply. I did not have the mental space for that. A second letter came. I sent a brief reply. A few days later, you came down again, bringing pineapples and fruit. This time, I did not see you. As you went down to your hometown, you asked me to write when I went to Tōkyō. Later, when I went to Tōkyō, I unconsciously sent a postcard. Passing through Ōsaka at midnight, a student wearing a square cap bowed to me. I did not recognize him. He came with me as far as Kyōto, but I had four or five companions, so I went straight on. It was when I was living a self-catering life with my companions in Higashi-Ōkubo, Tōkyō. You came looking for me, carrying local Tōkyō sweets, yatsuhashi. You had come as a speaker to the Tōkyō Imperial University Youth Association Oratorical Contest. During the day, you would always work on your manuscript at my desk, and in the evening, you would return and always write a letter. One night, as you were leaving, I extended my hand at the tram stop. You shook it warmly and then suggested we go to a nearby grove, where you offered a prayer thanking God. In this way, your letters, your words, your actions were all emotion transcending reason, just heat. Whenever I received this heat, I was happy. Unconsciously, I felt myself melting into that heat. Thus, while you were in Kyōto and I was in Tōkyō, we sometimes met once a day, sometimes got warned by policemen while strolling, sometimes spent a pleasant day boating, and sometimes traveled seeking snowy landscapes. During these six years of back and forth, you urged me to marry several times. However, I did not want to go through with it. More than anything, the scar remaining in a corner of my heart, unknown to others, had not yet healed; and second, I suspected that your love, transcending reason, was merely unconditional love, that is, love no more than rationalized instinct, and doubted if there was any understanding of me as an individual personality. Thus, if it were instinctual love, it wouldn't matter if it were another woman besides me; I felt there was no need to demand me specifically.<86> Among all humanity, why must you seek me, and I pair with you? Unless we find that something that makes you indispensable to me and me indispensable to you, that married life cannot be permanent, nor can it be happy—this I realized early on. Nevertheless, I did not want to let you go, and you did not let me go. We just couldn't go through with it. Then, due to the persuasion of relatives on both sides and my own sense of responsibility, we chose a date and got married. The conditions I demanded at that time were these:
Love me just like now for my entire life.
Do not interfere with my painting.
Let me live separately from your mother and the daughter from your previous marriage.
You agreed unconditionally.
The fact that you sought out the grave of my deceased lover in a remote mountain village for our honeymoon trip, just as I requested, and even erected a tombstone, is something I cannot forget for my entire life. In any case, it must be a certain fact that you loved me with your whole being.

Ten Years of Married Life

Living as a married couple for three years in Kyŏngsŏng, six years in Andong Prefecture, one year in Tŏngnae, and one and a half years in Europe and America, we had four children, one daughter and three sons. We lived a life of leaps, from this life to that life, from that life to this life: as a lawyer, as a diplomat, as tourists, studying abroad for our son, as a painter, as a wife, as a mother, as a daughter-in-law. We were financially comfortable, did everything we wanted to do, and achieved what we strove for. This could be called a happy life. Your personality, Mr. Ch'ŏnggu, was emotional, departing from reason in every aspect, unable to foresee even an inch ahead. I wanted to live better as a member of society, as a housewife, as a human being. To do so required finances, time, effort, and diligence. Although I had many shortcomings, the motive was rooted in the impertinent ideal of living well like a human being, which couldn't be uprooted. Perhaps because of this, after conflicts arose between us as a couple, a child was invariably conceived each time.

Life as a Housewife and Painter

When my submitted works won special selections and prizes, you rejoiced just as I did. Everyone praised me lavishly, saying it was thanks to having a good husband. I was satisfied and happy.
Understanding from those around and one's husband is necessary, but making them understand is also necessary. The starting point for everything lies within oneself. No one would object to using leftover time after managing household affairs efficiently. I never neglected housework to paint. I never wore silk clothes on my body and never played around even for a minute. Therefore,<87> the most precious things to me were money and time. Thinking now, perhaps it was my art that took away the happiness of my home. But without this art, there was nothing else to make my emotions happy.

Tour of Europe and America

Among the supporters who enabled the tour of Europe and America, there were, of course, those who wished for your success, Mr. Ch'ŏnggu, but also those who wished for my success. Thus, our tour of Europe and America was surprisingly easy. When a person sees one more thing, their own life extends and becomes richer by that much. After the tour, you developed a political perspective, and my view of life became somewhat more ordered.
One, how should one live well? While Eastern people admire the West and envy the lives of Westerners, if you go to the West, they admire the East and envy the lives of Eastern people. So, no one is satisfied with their own life. It just depends on one's mindset. In the midst of earning much money, accumulating much knowledge, and doing much business, one attains satisfaction in the heart only by acquiring the knack. That is, satisfaction is felt only when one sees the communion of the divine between people and things. Two, how can a couple live harmoniously? Since one personality and another personality have united, one cannot insist only on oneself. The key point is simply not to forget self-control. And it seems there are three stages in married life. First, during the love stage, there is no time to see the partner's flaws; only strengths are visible. Everything is idealized and beautified. Second, the boredom stage. If, after 3 or 4 years of marriage, children are not born to alleviate boredom, the symptoms of boredom intensify. The partner's flaws catch the eye, and one begins to feel weary. Statistics show that the number of marriages is highest at this time. Third, the understanding stage. By now, the husband and wife know each other's flaws and strengths, affection deepens, a new love arises, and one will want to overlook the flaws and promote the strengths. When a couple reaches this point, they likely cannot part, no matter what obstacles arise. Only then do beauty and goodness appear, and married life finds its meaning. Three, what is the status of women in Europe and America? The general spirit in Europe and America respects the small rather than the large. It yields to the weak rather than the strong. In any gathering, without women, there is no focal point, and the atmosphere cannot be harmonious. She is the protagonist in society, the queen in the family, the subject as an individual. This is not just because so-called big and strong men protect them, but because women themselves possess that much great charm and mystery. Therefore, there is no need to demand equality and freedom anew; equality and freedom inherently coexist. We Eastern women have just not realized this. The power of us women possesses great *ryŏk (力 power/strength) and holds mystery. Therefore, there is no need to demand equality and freedom anew; equality and freedom inherently coexist. We Eastern women have just not realized this. The power of us women is great.<88> The more civilized we become, the more those who will rule that civilization will be none other than us women. Four, what is the other key point? It is *dessin* (뗏상). That *dessin* does not merely mean outline but combines *couleur* (칼나), that is, color, *harmonie* (하모니), that is, tone. Therefore, being able to competently draw a model whose *dessin* is clear becomes one's lifelong task. Though ignorant, I was able to resolve the above four issues to some extent. Therefore, it felt as if my life's catalogue was unfolding from now on, and I thought my starting point would be from here. Accordingly, my ideals were large, and I had concrete plans. I was infinitely optimistic about the future path, but what kind of result has indeed come about is something I cannot help but be ashamed of myself.

Life in Confrontation with Mother-in-law and Sister-in-law

After marriage, I lived with my mother-in-law for one year, then, to guarantee the future of the young couple living thoughtlessly, she went down to her hometown, Tŏngnae, prepared a house, and saved money sent every month to buy plots of land. Her only wish was for her son and daughter-in-law to return to the hometown in their old age, live surrounded by relatives, and for her to bequeath all the property, saved penny by penny, to her son whom she raised without a father. Thus, this property (though not much) was accumulated through the joint efforts of three people. One person earned, one person saved and sent, and one person gathered and bought. Thus, the finances of the two households were meticulously managed and tasteful. Into this harmonious family, an event causing turmoil occurred.
One month after we returned from our tour of Europe and America, the third younger uncle (셋재偲三寸), who had quit farming in another region, returned to his hometown without any preparation, relying on the main house (큰宅), that is, us, where his nephew, the head of the family (長足下), lived. Before a few days passed in bewilderment, the second younger uncle (둘재 偲三寸) also arrived with five family members. Since I hadn't found a job after returning home, I couldn't help them, yet seeing them was pitiful; it was truly an awkward situation. Inevitably, we housed the two uncles in the lower room for a year and found jobs for all the cousins. Seeing this, naturally, talk among close relatives grew, and unfounded rumors began to arise. A major incident was enrolling a cousin, who was always around day and night, into high school without any budget, with us taking responsibility for his tuition.
We couldn't even properly entertain the acquaintances and friends coming from various places in the capital and countryside to hear stories about the tour. Pretending to have what we didn't was vanity, but it was unavoidable social interaction for career advancement. They were not ones to understand this. I reluctantly requested a one-year suspension of studies until my husband found a job. The uncle flew into a rage. This way, we had no money; that way, we lost goodwill; truly, there was nothing we could do.
At that time, Mr. Ch'ŏnggu refused the transfer from the Ministry of Foreign Affairs (外務省) to become an administrative official (事務官) at the Government-General (總督府),<89> rejecting the telegram twice (telling him to become an official), stubbornly started practicing as a lawyer (辯護士), became a guest at some inn (旅舘) in Kyŏngsŏng, was tempted by dressed-up kisaeng (妓生) and rich prostitutes (갈보), and using a letter I sent to Mr. C as a pretext, went around publicizing his intention to divorce at this restaurant and to that friend. I, innocent of any wrongdoing in the motive, unaware that the divorce rumor was now public in Seoul, further provoked Mr. Ch'ŏnggu's anger, saying, "You simpleton, you fool who can't foresee even an inch ahead, what will you do later, taking on the tuition fees?"
There was someone who indirectly held absolute authority over our household affairs: my sister-in-law. Not only did she act as a coach (코취) to my mother-in-law in all matters, but even if I went to Haeundae with guests from Seoul, the next day, my mother-in-law would inevitably scrape together money she didn't have and go too. One might say it was all the result of my lack of virtue, but having learned more than others, how could my human feelings be less than others? However, there was no room whatsoever for us to rise from this adversity.
When I returned from my tour of Europe and America, I bought some local products (土産物) for various relatives and friends. However, I did not bring anything for my mother-in-law, sister-in-law, or other close relatives. This was not because I was complacent, but because there were no suitable items for them. My intention to buy them something after returning to Korea fizzled out. When they saw that the two crates of luggage (짐) arriving from France (佛蘭西) contained nothing but posters (포스타), picture postcards (繒葉書), records (레콧트), and art supplies (畵具), they felt disappointed and mocked me. Truly, the world we live in is the same, but the world of the heart is different, so there were many painful things. Due to this, a gap formed in the feelings of my mother-in-law and sister-in-law, though unspoken.
Mr. Ch'ŏnggu has two sisters among his siblings (同復男妹). One is simple-minded (千痴), and the other is the sister-in-law I am talking about now, an excessively clever woman who handles matters meticulously without any gaps. A young widow (靑春寡婦), she remarried but has no blood relatives, raising a daughter from somewhere as if she were a cherished treasure (金枝玉葉). Her remaining affection is poured onto her mother and older brother (오래비), and the money she saved penny by penny was also for her older brother. Thus, she intended to live as close as possible to her older brother in their hometown and spend the rest of her life there. Once, I said, "I dislike Tŏngnae. I really must go live in Seoul." Gathering all these various points, the conclusion was reached that the older brother's wife (오래비댁) is unfilial (不孝) to her mother, discordant (不睦) with relatives, and a fickle person who dislikes her hometown. Who besides God could have known that this would surface at some opportunity and serve as support for the divorce argument? Indeed, the emotions of a narrow-minded woman are fearsome, and the man who overlooks this and moves on is limitlessly foolish.
With two housewives in one family, the mother-in-law saying "my household," the daughter-in-law having a separate budget (豫算), the sister-in-law interfering (干涉), the housekeeping wife (마누라) being cunning, and brothers and relatives bustling about front, back, left, and right (前後左右 兄第親戚),<90> the position of a single housewife who was not overly affectionate, not clever, had no money, no plan, was young, and had no connection to old customs (舊習) was difficult. People all look the same on the outside, but how complex are their inner workings, and how tangled are the movements of emotions beyond reason?

Relationship with C

I had heard of C's fame early on, but our first meeting (初對面) was in Paris (巴里). As I was preparing food (料理) to entertain him, his first greeting, "Hello," was intentionally forceful. Since then, my husband went to Germany (獨逸), and C and I, due to our lack of French (佛語), hired an interpreter (通辯) and always went around as a group of three to restaurants (食堂), theaters (劇場), boat trips (船遊), and sightseeing outside the city (市外 求景). In discussing past, present, and future matters, we found many points of resonance (共鳴) and came to understand each other. He toured Italy (伊太利) and left Paris before me, heading to Germany. Later, we met again in Cologne (콜논). At that time, I said something like this: "I love you, Sir. But I will not divorce my husband." He patted my back firmly and said, "Indeed, those are words worthy of you. I am satisfied with those words." In Geneva, I said to a friend from home (故國親舊), "If one gets along well with another man or woman, conversely, one can get along even better with one's own husband or wife, right?" He resonated with this. Having such thoughts, though ultimately deceiving oneself, I certainly did not intend to deceive my husband and love another man, namely C. Rather, I believed my affection for my husband would deepen. Seeing that such open secrets (公然한 秘密) exist between ordinary men and women, husbands and wives, in Europe and America, I thought it was a natural thing, and behavior within the bounds of not losing the central husband (本夫) or wife (本妻) was neither a sin nor a mistake, but rather an emotion that should properly exist in the most progressive people. Therefore, when clarifying such facts, laughing it off is the best approach (우서두는 거시 수); there is no need to deliberately name it. Jean Valjean (쩐발잔) comes to mind. Unable to bear seeing his young nephews/nieces (족하) starving, he took a piece of bread (빵) from a neighbor's house, which became the cause (原因) for him to be in and out of prison (監獄出入) for nineteen years afterwards. How beautiful was that motive? Did not morality (道德) and law (法律) exist, deceiving his conscience (良心)? Do cause and effect not arise separately? Because of this morality and law, how many wrongful deaths (怨痛한 죽음) are there, and how many harbor resentment (怨限)?

Family Fortunes in Adversity

The modest surplus (餘裕) we had during the so-called official life (官吏生活) was spent building a house in the hometown, buying land, and spending over 20,000 won during the Euro-American tour. The 2,000 won received as an Imperial Gift (恩賜金) all went into the expenses for opening a law practice (辯護士開業費用), there was no income (收入) whatsoever, and the economic downturn (不景氣) grew harsher day by day. With no plan (方針), I had no choice but to enter the professional front (職業戰線). However, the demon (魔) of fate (運命)<91> blocked even this path. Eight months after returning home, I became weakened due to mental and physical exhaustion. Moreover, my stage (舞臺) is Kyŏngsŏng. Due to financial (經濟上) reasons, I couldn't set up a household in Seoul. Also, I couldn't leave the young children and neglect the household. Powerless, caught in a pressing crisis (危機切迫), I could only worry inwardly. If only, at that time, I didn't have a nursing baby (젓먹이) and had found a job to make a living (生計), such a tragedy (悲劇) would not have lain across our path. It was at this time. The so-called letter incident (片紙事件). The only person who could help me seemed to be C. So, I asked him to come down, wanting to try starting some business (經營). And I expressed my hope to meet and associate with him again. Through misrepresentation (誤傳) by wicked people in between (中間惡漢輩), this became "I entrust my whole life (平生) to you," incurring Mr. Ch'ŏnggu's great anger (大怒). To the extent that he believed their words more than mine, the affection between husband and wife had tilted, and Mr. Ch'ŏnggu's heart began to change (變).
The struggle for existence (生存競爭) and survival of the fittest (弱肉强食) have become severe in Chosŏn too. On top of that, people have minds (심사) that prefer seeing others fail rather than succeed. Since Mr. Ch'ŏnggu had already publicized the divorce (離婚) by his own mouth, and the letter incident occurred, the wicked people (惡漢輩) who idly engage only in gossiping added insult (치욕), saying things like, "Why live with such a woman?" and calling him a simpleton and a fool. Among them was a group (구룹) of three or four influential instigators (코취자) who, from a so-called ideological viewpoint (思想家的見地), strongly urged (强勸) the divorce out of curiosity (好奇心) to see me live alone, found a replacement candidate (後補者), and concocted plans (考案) for before and after. In their minds (心思), rather than human feeling (人情味) sympathizing with the rupture (破裂) of a family and the future (前道) of the children, they wanted to spectate (求景) how the relationship between C and me would turn out after the divorce, and wanted to watch the cruel future path of a tough and tenacious bitch like a play (演劇求景). Just as one's own happiness is known only to oneself, one's own misfortune is also known only to oneself. Asking this person and that person about the intention to divorce and revealing (發露) the flaws (缺點) of his former beloved wife (愛妻) of 10 years' cohabitation (同居) cannot be called the behavior of an ordinary person, nor can firming up one's resolve (決心이 굿어저가다) while playing along with the instigation (추김) to "do it, do it" be called the behavior of an ordinary person.
In any case, just as Mr. Ch'ŏnggu's entire family (一家) faced misfortune (悲運), his personal adversity (逆境) reached its peak (絶頂). He had cases but couldn't start (着手) due to lack of money; staying at an inn (旅舘), he couldn't pay the lodging fees (宿泊料) for three or four months, losing face (面目) to the owner daily; from society's side (社會側), criticism (批難) about the divorce rumor was rampant, so he had no standing (體面) to maintain appearances (行勢); lacking judgment (判斷力) due to his personality (性格上), he hesitated (躊躇) in matters. Mr. Ch'ŏnggu's cheekbones (뺨뼈) protruded as he grew thin (말느고), and his eyes sank as he suffered from insomnia and anguish (煩悶) at night. During sleepless nights, Mr. Ch'ŏnggu thought deeply. First, the anger (忿함) rising from jealousy (嫉妬) flushed his face (얼골). Then, thinking about himself and having tasted the world (世上맛), he realized there was nothing harder than earning money.<92> He regretted (後悔) his extravagance (濫用) during the Andong Prefecture days, and felt regretful about his wife (안해) buying art supplies (畵具) to paint. A person's mind is like a sailboat's mast; once it catches the wind, it runs with the wind. Similarly, once a fundamental thought (根本生覺) takes hold, all thoughts run in that direction (便). The more Mr. Ch'ŏnggu thought like that, the more the emotion (感情) arose like fire, unwilling to keep that woman under his wife's name (名義) even for a moment. At the same time, he saw one of his friends (親舊) living comfortably as a kisaeng's kept man (妓生서방). When he thought of this too as a strategy (方策) to revive himself from his adversity, the divorce rumor became public, and many rich prostitutes (갈보) here and there pleaded (請願) to be candidates (後補). He chose (取하엿든 거시외다) one among them. It was at this time that he demanded a divorce from his wife, threatening (威脅) to file a lawsuit (告訴) for adultery (姦通罪) if she did not consent (承諾). Ah! Men (男性), in times of peace (平時 無事), fully enjoy (充分이 亨樂) the affection (愛情) offered (밧치는) by women (女性), but once bound by formal constraints (形式的 束縛) like law (法律) or reputation (體面), they turn away from their indulgent (放恣) and pleasure-seeking selves of yesterday and become gentlemen (君子) of today, putting on airs (점잔을 빼는)—are they not cowards (卑怯者) and tyrants (橫暴者)? Let all us women rise up, curse (呪咀), and denounce (저하노라) men!

Divorce

It was when I was staying in Tŏngnae with the children. A telegram arrived saying Mr. Ch'ŏnggu, who was in Kyŏngsŏng, would arrive. I went out to the main gate (大門) to meet him (出迎). Seeing me, Mr. Ch'ŏnggu averted his gaze (反目不見) and looked startled (실즉함니다). His complexion (顔色) was pale (蒼白), and his eyes were bloodshot. I was taken aback. And my heart pounded (두군거렷나이다), sensing some misfortune (不詳事). Mr. Ch'ŏnggu went into the room across and called me.
"Honey, come here a moment."
"I went across. I sat silently, just watching his expression."
"Honey, let's get a divorce."
"What are you talking about, so suddenly?"
"Didn't you write a letter to C?"
"I did."
"Didn't you write, 'I dedicate my whole life (平生) to you'?"
"I did not write that."
"Why are you lying? Anyway, let's divorce."
He forcefully pulled out important documents (重要文書) and insurance policies (保險券) tucked inside his clothes (내장속), distributed them respectively, then went to the inner room (안방) and entrusted them to his mother.
"Hey, tell auntie (고모어머니?) to come! Tell uncle (三寸) to come!"
"Soon (未久), they gathered one by one, two by two."
"I am going to get a divorce."
"Hey, what are you talking about? What about the little children?"
His mother-in-law, who had been lying down as if sick since receiving a letter from Kyŏngsŏng the day before, tried to dissuade him (만류하엿사외다).
"Oh, that man talks nonsense (쓸대업는 소리)."
His older brother said.
"Brother, what are you saying?"
"How can I live with someone who commits adultery (서방질하난 것)?"
Everyone fell silent.<93>
"If you prevent the divorce, I will die."
At this point, everyone put their heads together and whispered. The sister-in-law took charge, and the matter was decided.
"Do as you please. They say you are unfilial (不孝) to mother and discordant (불목) with relatives."
I jumped into the midst of those seated (坐中에 뛰여드럿슴니다).
"If you want to do it, let's do it. There's no need for many words this way or that, and no need to find fault (허물을 잡아낼) where there is none. But I built this house, money from selling paintings went into it, and you can't say you earned the money alone, so let's divide the entire property (全財産) in half (半分)."
"This property is not my property. It's all mother's."
"Do you think I'm a living corpse (산송장)? You just don't want to give it, right?"
"What brazenness (뻔뻔) from a guilty woman?"
"What guilt? It's guilt because you make it up (맨드니 罪지)!"
"I will only give you this, sell it and go."
Mr. Ch'ŏnggu handed over one paddy field document (논문서), worth about 500 won.
"I am not one to take such a thing."
Mr. Ch'ŏnggu stood up, saying he was going to Kyŏngsŏng. He went straight to his sister's house, discussed (議論) things, and left.
At night, unable to sleep, I thought deeply.
"No, no. I should apologize (謝罪). And I should explain that my motive (動機) was not evil (惡). It's unpleasant (滋味업다) for things to get bigger. Let's yield (屈하자) for the sake of the children's future (前程)."
I suddenly (不然듯) headed for Kyŏngsŏng. I went to the inn (여관) and met (맛나보앗사외다) him.
"I was wrong about everything. But my motive was definitely not evil."
"What's the point of saying this now? Just stamp (도장이나 찍어) it quickly."
"What about the young children?"
"I will raise them well, so don't worry."
"Let's not do this. If we can't live by our own strength, let's believe firmly in religion (宗敎) and live by the power of religion. Didn't Jesus get nailed (못박히지) to the cross (十字架) on behalf of (대신하야) the sins (罪) of all mankind (萬人)?"
"I don't want to hear it."
Tears came to my eyes, but inwardly I laughed. What was the point of twisting the world so askew (빗두로)? If he just laughed it off heartily like a man, wouldn't everything be fine? I knew Mr. Ch'ŏnggu was unshakeable (搖地不動). I ran away (다라낫사외다) to Mr. So-and-so (某氏).
"Oppa (읍바), he wants a divorce, what should I do?"
"Do it. You don't know hardship yet, so you need to experience some."
"I can't do it, thinking of the children's future."
"Didn't Ellen Key (에렌케이?) say that children raised by parents in discord (不和한 夫婦) are better off than those raised in a new family after divorce?"
"That's just theory (理論). Maternal love (母性愛) is noble (尊貴) and great (偉大). The mother who loses maternal love is unfortunate, and the child not raised with maternal love is also unfortunate. Knowing this, I cannot get divorced. Oppa, please mediate (仲裁를<94>시켜주세요)."
"Then, will you become an absolutely wise mother and good wife (賢母良妻) from now on?"
"I have never not been a wise mother and good wife myself until now, but I will do as Mr. Ch'ŏnggu requests."
"Alright, I'll try to mediate."
Mr. So-and-so picked up the telephone (電話器) and called the company president (社長) and the business bureau chief (營業局長). It was to request mediation. The phone reply (電話答) came. There was no hope for compromise, so give up (斷念), it said. Mr. So-and-so said, "Just do it. What need is there not to listen when he requests it so much?" Being a novelist (小說家), Mr. So-and-so was more interested (好寄心) in the progression of the event (事件進行) than in the inner suffering (人生內面에 苦痛) of life. I returned, unsatisfied here too. That night at the inn, while I tossed and turned (업치락뒤치락), unable to sleep, the sound of laughter drifted in from the men's quarters (사랑) where kisaeng (妓生) had been called for merrymaking (興이냐 興이냐놀며). What a contradiction (矛盾) for this time! If one is to discuss the partner's misconduct (不品行), one's own self should naturally (當然) be clean (淸白), yet under the name of being male, he has the bold right (當當한 權利) to play and sleep with the opposite sex (異性) without consequence. Social customs (社會制度) aside, his senseless (沒常識) attitude (態度) made me laugh. It was no different than the behavior (行動) of children acting up (作亂모양), saying, "If you do it, I'll do it too." As someone who had never directly experienced (直接經驗) nor could easily imagine (想像) the complexities (內幕) of life's inner workings, I guessed he would regret (後悔) it soon (未久), but since he was infatuated (熱中) with his kisaeng lover (愛人) and stubbornly insisted (固執不通) on divorce using past events as a pretext (口實), there was no way (方針) to change his mind.
Reluctantly, I left for Tŏngnae. Should I run away to Mukden (奉天)? Should I run away to Japan (日本)? I was confident that if I could just get past this crisis (요곱이), things would be fine. But unfortunately, I did not have enough travel expenses (旅費) in my hands (手中). Unable to bear the pain (苦痛), I got off in Taegu (大邱). I visited Mr. Y's house; he welcomed me gladly, and we went to theaters (演劇場) and restaurants (料理집), drank alcohol (술), smoked tobacco (담배), the three of us with his wife (夫人), staying up all night. Mr. Y worried about my haggard appearance and asked me to find him talented people (人材?). My pain, known only to me, relentlessly spun round and round inside my heart. Having no choice, I went down to Tŏngnae. From Mr. Ch'ŏnggu, demand letters (督促狀) continued to arrive as before, once every two days.
"Stamp your seal on the divorce papers (리혼장). If you don't stamp it within 15 days, I will file a lawsuit (告訴)."
My reply (답장) was as follows:
Just as it is natural reason (當然한 理治) for strangers to unite, it is also natural reason for them to part, but we have four conditions (條件) that prevent us from parting. 1 is the 80-year-old mother (老母), it would be unfilial (不孝); 2 is the four children (子息四男妹), they are school-age children (學齡兒童) and must be protected (保護); 3 is that a family (一家庭) is the joint life (共同生活) of a couple, so not only was production (生産) done jointly (共同), but upon separation (分離), there must naturally be provision (生計) for one household becoming two. Isn't providing this the duty (義務) of a human being? 4 is that, considering our age (年齡), experience (經驗),<95> or timing (時機), we should live not just by pure feeling (純情), that is, love, but by understanding (理解) and duty (義). I have already apologized (謝過), my motive (動機) was not entirely (專혀) evil (惡), and I have said I will become the wise wife and good mother (賢妻良母) Mr. Ch'ŏnggu demands.
Mr. Ch'ŏnggu's reply was as follows:
"I am not a person who thinks about the past or future. I just live for the present (現在). If you really can't bear to be without the children, it's fine to live with them (同居) after the divorce, and it doesn't matter (無關) if you live just like before."
Whether these were words to trick me or about the details (始未) of the divorce, they were, as expected (亦是), senseless (沒常識) words. The back and forth of "please do it" and "I won't do it" lasted nearly a month. One day, the uncle (三寸) whom I had asked to suspend studies, harboring resentment (怒心), took the lead (압장을 시고), and my husbands' brothers (시숙들) and sisters-in-law (시누이들) gathered and pressed me hard (肉迫).
"Stamp your seal as a sign (표) that you admit fault. We will handle everything smoothly after that."
"Marriage was done by two people, so divorce should also be done by two people. Please don't worry and leave."
At night, unable to sleep a wink, I thought.
It's already ruined. He has another woman, the relatives agree; it's useless (쓸대업난 일) for me to refuse alone. Suddenly thinking of this plan, I wrote two pledge documents (誓約書).
Pledge (誓約書)
Husband OOO and Wife OOO pledge not to remarry (再嫁 又는 再娶) for a full two years, agreeing that reunion (復舊) is possible depending on observation of each other's conduct.
Above Husband OOO Seal (印)
Wife OOO Seal (印)
The younger uncle (偲叔) who had gone to Kyŏngsŏng to mediate returned with the seal (圖章) stamped. He spoke thus:
"Look here, Auntie (아주머니), let's stamp it. What does that piece of paper (종이) matter? With four children, where would your rights (權利) to this house go? And my older brother just talks; surely he won't go through with the procedures (手續)?"
The mother-in-law sitting beside him also said,
"Isn't that just how it is? I'm very worried you'll get sick (病날가). Stamp it, and whether he gets another woman and lives with her or not, you live with me and the little ones."
I laughed inwardly (속으로). And I felt displeased (아니꼽고) and hurt (속傷햇다). Quickly (얼는) pulling out my seal, I gave it.
"What's the point of hesitating? I'll stamp it ten times if you want."
Indeed, how a single piece of paper moves a person's mind (心事), things unforeseen arose one by one, two by two, and the way things changed with time (때)—should one view it with tears or laughter? Adopting an attitude of absolute non-resistance (絶對無抵抗主義), silently enduring the emotions and events carried by time (타임?), I could only grit my teeth and overcome them one by one. **Summary**

This article is Na Hye-sŏk's public "divorce confession" addressed to her ex-husband, Kim U-yong (referred to as Ch'ŏnggu-ssi). Writing in 1934, she recounts the history of their relationship, marriage, and the events leading to their separation. She defends her character and actions, particularly her relationship with "C" (Choe Rin) encountered in Paris, framing it within a 'modern' perspective gained from her Western travels. She contrasts her husband's initial passionate love with his later perceived cruelty and irrationality, details conflicts with his family (mother-in-law, sister-in-law, uncles), financial hardships after their return from Europe, and condemns his hypocrisy and the societal double standards that allowed him to pursue divorce while engaging with kisaeng. The piece is a mix of personal anguish, self-justification, social critique, and a declaration of intent to endure and find rebirth despite public condemnation.

From a historical perspective, the article offers a rare, intimate glimpse into the lives and struggles of elite, Western-educated Koreans during the colonial period. It highlights the tensions between traditional family structures/expectations (filial piety, family obligations, gender roles like 賢母良妻) and modern ideals (individualism, romantic love, women's aspirations beyond the domestic sphere, evolving views on relationships influenced by the West). Na Hye-sŏk's articulation of her desire for personal fulfillment through art and her critique of patriarchal hypocrisy, published openly in a popular magazine, is particularly revealing about changing gender dynamics and the nascent feminist consciousness among some Korean women. The public airing of such personal conflict also underscores the role of the press as a forum for debate and the formation of public opinion in colonial Korea. Historians would find the detailed accounts of family conflicts, financial struggles linked to social status maintenance, the impact of overseas travel on perspectives, and the direct challenge to societal norms valuable for understanding the social and cultural landscape of 1930s Korea.

잡지명	삼천리 제6권 제8호
발행일	1934년 08월 01일
기사제목	離婚告白狀, 靑邱氏에게
필자	羅蕙錫
기사형태	문예기타

離婚告白狀, 靑邱氏에게
						羅蕙錫
나이 4, 50에 갓가 왓고 專門敎育을 밧앗고 남들의 容易히 할 수 업는 歐米漫遊를 하엿고 또 後輩를 指導할 만한 處地에 잇서서 그 人格을 統一치 못하고 그 生活을 統一치 못한 거슨 두 사람의 自身은 물론 붓그러워 할 뿐아니라 一般社會에 대하여서도 面目이 업스며 붓그럽고 謝罪하는 바외다.
靑邱氏!
난생처음으로 當하는 이 衝擊은 넘오 傷處가 甚하고 致命的입니다.
悲嘆, 動哭 進燥, 煩悶- 爾來이一切의 軌路에서 生의 彷徨을 하면서一便으로 深淵의 밋바닥에 던진 氏를 나는 다시 靑邱氏-하고 부름니다.
靑邱氏! 하고 부르는 내 눈에는 눈물이 긋득 차집니다. 이거슬 世上은 나를「弱者야」하고 불를가요?
날마다 當하고 지내는 氏와 나 사이는 깁히 理解하고 知悉하고 自負하든 우리 사이가 夢想에도 生覺지 안튼 傷處의 運命의 經驗을 얻어케 現實의 事實도 알 수가 잇스릿가.

結婚後 1年間 시어머니와 同居하다가 철업시 사러가는 젊은 內外에 將來를 保障하기 爲하야 故鄕인 東萊로 내려가서 집을 작만하고 每朔 보내난 돈을 節約하야 땅마지기를 작만하고게섯슴니다. 그의 오직 所願은 아들며누리가 늘게 故鄕에 도라와 親戚들을 울을삼고 살나함이오 自己가 分分錢錢이 모든 財産을 아버지 업시 길니운 아들에게 遺産하는 거시외다. 그리하야 이 財産이란 거슨 三人이 合同하야 모은 거시외다. (얼마되지 안으나) 한 사람은 벌고 한 사람은 節約하야 보내고 한 사람은 모아서 산 거시외다. 그리하야 두집살님이 물샐틀업시 째이고 滋味스러웟사외다. 이러케 和樂한 家庭에 波亂을 일으키는 일이 생겻사외다.
우리가 歐米漫遊하고 도라온지一朔만에 셋재偲三寸이 他地方에서 農事짓든 거슬 집어치고 一分準備업시 長足下되는 큰宅 卽 우리를 밋고 故鄕을 차자 도라온 거시외다. 어안이 벙벙한지 몃칠이 못되여 둘재 偲三寸이 또 다섯食口를 데리고 왓슴니다. 歸家後 就職도 아니된 때라 도읍지도 못하고 보자니 딱하고 實노 亂處한 處地이엿사외다. 할 수 업시 三寸 두 분은 一年間 아랫방에 뫼시고 四寸들은 다 各各 就職케 하엿슴니다. 이러고 보니 近親間 自然的은 말이 늘어지고 업는 말이 生기기 시작하게 되엿고 큰 事件은 朝夕이 업는 四寸아들을아모 預算 업시 高等學校에 입학을 식이고 그 學資는 우리가 맛게 된 거시외다.
漫遊後에 感想談드르러 京鄕 各處로 붓허오는 知人親舊를 待接하기에도 넉넉지 못하엿다. 업는 거슬 잇는 체하고 지내난 거슨 虛榮이나 出世方針上 避치 못할 社交이엇사외다. 이거슬 理解해 줄 그들이 아니엇사외다. 나는 不得已 남편이 就職할 동안 一年間만 停學하여 달나고 要求하엿사외다. 三寸은 大發怒發하엿사외다. 이러자니 돈이 업고 저러자니 인심 일코 實로 엇절 길이 업섯나이다.
때에 氏는 外務省에서 總督府事務官으로 가라난 거슬 실타하고 電報를<89> 두 번이나 拒絶하고(官吏하라고) 固執을 부려 辯護士開業을 시작하고 京城 어느 旅舘客이 되어서 입분 妓生 돈 만흔 갈보들의 誘惑을 밧으면서 내가 某氏에게 보낸 片紙가 口實이 되여 이 料理집 저 親舊에게 離婚意思를 公開하며 다니든 때이엿슴니다. 動機에 아모 罪업는 나는 方今 서울에 離婚說이 公開된 줄도 모르고 氏의 분을 더 돗앗스니「一寸의 압길을 헤아리지 못하는 이 千痴 바보야 나종 일을 엇지하랴고 學資를 떠맛핫느냐」하엿사외다.

一家庭에 主婦가 둘이어서 시어머니는 내 살님이라 하고 며누리는 따로 預算이 잇고 시누이가 干涉을 하고 살님하는 마누라가 꾀 사실을 하고 前後左右에는 兄第親戚이 와글와글하니<90> 多情치도 못하고 약지도 못하고 돈도 업고 方針도 업고 나이도 어리고  舊習에단 연도 업는 一個 主婦의 處地가 難處하엿사외다. 사람은 外形은 다 갓흐나 그 內幕이 얼마나複雜하며 理性 外에 感情의 움지김이 얼마나 얼키설키 얽매엿는가.

C의 名聲은 일즉 붓허드럿스나 初對面하기는 巴里이엇사외다. 그를 對接하랴고 料理를 하고 잇는 나에게「안녕합쇼」하는 初인사는 有心이도 힘이 잇는 말이엇사외다. 以來 夫君은 獨逸노 가서 잇고 C와 나는 佛語를 모르난 關係上 通辯을 두고 언제든지 三人同伴하야 食堂, 劇場, 船遊 市外 求景을 다니며 놀앗사외다. 그리하야 過去之事, 現時事, 將來之事를 論하는 中에 共鳴되는 點이 만핫고 서로 理解하게 되엿사외다. 그는 伊太利求景을 하고 나보다 몬저 巴里를 떠나 獨逸노 갓사외다. 其後 콜논에서 다시 맛낫사외다. 내가 그 때 이런 말을 하엿나이다.「나는 公을 사랑합니다. 그러나 내 남편과 離婚은 아니하럄니다.」그는 내 등을 뚝뚝 뚜듸리며「과연 당신의 할 말이오. 나는 그 말에 만족하오」하엿사외다. 나는 제네바에서 어느 故國親舊에게「다른 男子나 女子와 조와 지내면 反面으로 自己 남편이나 안해와 더 잘 지낼 수 잇지요」하엿슴니다. 그는 共鳴하엿슴니다. 이와 갓흔 生覺이 잇는 거슨 必竟 自己가 自己를 속이고 마는 거신 줄은 모르나 나는 決코 내 남편을 속이고 다른 男子 卽 C를 사랑하랴고 하는 거슨 아니엇나이다. 오히려 男便에게 情이 두터워지리라고 밋엇사외다. 歐米 一般男女夫婦사이에 이러한 公然한 秘密이 잇는 거슬 보고 또 잇난 거시 當然한일이오, 中心되는 本夫나 本妻를 엇지 안는 範圍內에 行動은 罪도 아니오 失守도 아니라 가장 進步된 사람에게 맛당히 잇서야만 할 感情이라고 生覺합니다. 그럼으로 이러한 事實을 判明할 때는 우서두는 거시 수요, 일부러 일홈을 지을 必要가 업느 거시외다.  쩐발잔이 生覺남니다. 어린족하들이 배곱하서 못견대는 거슬 참아 볼 수 업서서 이웃집에 가  빵 한조각 집은 거시 原因으로 前後 十九年이나 監獄出入을 하게 되엿사외다. 그 動機는 얼마나 아람다웟든가. 道德이 잇고 法律이 잇서 그의 良心을 속이지 아니하엿는가. 原因과 結果가 따로 나지 아니하난가. 이 道德과 法律노 하야 怨痛한 죽음이 오작 만흐며 怨限을 품은 者가 얼마나 잇슬가.
