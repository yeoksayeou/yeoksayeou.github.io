Sowŏl Poem Collection
Life, Money, and Death

1. If sad, I should weep; if there's laughter, I should laugh,
But the tears I try to cry are withheld, the heart that tries to laugh is insipid,
This restless heart, I fear there is no one who knows it.

To the beloved who sleeps on one pillow, eats from one pot,
Even if I try to convey this restless heart,
There are no fitting words, and I fear it too would become a burden.

What is it about this burdensome sentiment that is innate?
Even in the middle of the night in April or May, that croaking frog,
I rather envy its circumstances.

2. Sadness and pain and joy and pleasure,
Even love and hate, are they not dreams once passed!
Then what is it, that I call living?

If dreams were alive, then life too would be a dream!
If sleep were death, then dreams of death would likely vanish too.
Holding fast, insisting it is so, yet what can be done about this?

If memories of being alive were to exist after death,
Why are there no memories of being dead while alive?
Since I do not know what happens after death, how can I claim to know while alive?

3. Is this all there is to being alive? Is it all over when one dies?
Have I crossed the sea of oblivion on the boundary of life and death?
Even assuming that, if it is only about being alive, I know it is not so.

Can one not die while living? Can the dead not live?
This world, no matter how much one lives, one cannot know,
Even if one died and lived again, I know I still would not know.<174>

This thing called living in this world, I too know nothing.
Where did I come from? What will become of me when I die?
In this utter ignorance, I wonder why I am like this.

Money Song

1. Who should I give these five *don* to? This heart.
With five *don*, I cannot cut a silk *tanggi* ribbon,
Cannot buy a silver ring. Ah!
Let's buy ten firecrackers, and light them. This heart.

2. The thought that it might work out.
Shall I go to Manchuria (滿洲)? Shall I go to the mines (鑛山)?
Will it work out, will it not, yesterday, today,
The thought that doing this or that might somehow make things work out this way or that way.

3. When I had it, I didn't know,
Now that it's gone, I see it was you.

When I had it, I didn't know,
Now that it's gone, I see it was you.

There's nothing valuable left on my body,
My remaining capital is my true heart (本心).

Having things was the source of illness,
Not having is better than it was.

That's the way things go,
It took youth along with it.

You didn't know I'd want to eat rice,
Trying not to eat 술고기 (sul-gogi, meat dishes eaten with alcohol).

You didn't know there'd be no right to chase away,
Saying wife and friend are stuck together.

You didn't know what made you so great,
Saying your character was the best.

"A thousand gold pieces scattered return again" (千金散盡 還復來)
Is not true after it's gone.

Even if one becomes His Majesty the King,
Treading the right path is better.

"Human life cannot regain youth" (人生不得 更少年)
Is something to say when I still exist.

Han River water (漢江水), Pedestrian Bridge (人道橋),
Did you know high and low?

That's the way things go,<175>
It takes courage along with it.

Do you know who I am?
I am indeed your world.

I am leaving, so your world is gone,
Try living without a world.

You inflict contempt upon me,
Let's see whose contempt you receive.

Serving me again
Is serving your world.

Just look at me, I will come again,
Just look at me, I will come again.

Saying I won't come, saying I won't come,
Could there be a reason (理) I wouldn't come?

I follow because I must have it,
But having it, I see it was you.

I follow, saying I must have it,
But looking back, I see it was you.

Naggonggok (囉嗊曲)

Pleasant to hear, the Huai Shui (秦淮) water,
Quaint, the ferry boat.
It carries my love away,
And knows not the passing of time.
不喜秦淮水 生憎江上船 載兒夫婿去 經歲又經年 Liu Ts'ai-ch'un (劉采春)

Yijuga (伊州歌)

Guarding the border by the Huang Ho (黃河) River,
He cannot come this year either.
Shine brightly, my moon,
Specifically on my love's military camp (營門).
聞道黃河水 頻年不解兵 可憐閨裏月 偏照漢家營 Anonymous (無姓氏)

Don't chase the orioles
And make them cry.
If I wake from my dream at their sound,
I cannot reach Liao-hsi (遼西).
打起黃鶯兒 莫敎枝上啼 啼時驚妾夢 不得到遼西 Anonymous (無姓氏)

Changganhaeng (長干行)

"Where do you live?
I live in Hengtang (橫塘).<176>
Stop the boat and let me ask,
Aren't we from the same place?"
君家住何處 妾住在橫塘 停船暫借問 或恐是同鄕 Ts'ui Hao (崔顥)

"My home is by the Jiujiang (九江) River,
Coming and going, I pass through here.
We are both from Changgan (長干), yet
Somehow we never knew each other!"
家臨九江水 來去九江側 同是長干人 生少不相識 Ts'ui Hao (崔 顥)

Seeing off Yuan Er on his Mission to Anxi (送元二使安西)

Wei-ch'eng (渭城) morning's drizzling rain
Dampens the road dust.
Green, green, the tavern yard,
The color of the willow branches.
Look here, my friend,
Have another cup.
Once you leave Yangguan Pass (陽關),
What friends will there be?
渭城朝雨浥輕塵 客舍靑靑柳色新 勸君更進一盃酒 西出陽關無故人 Wang Wei (王維)

J. M. S.

You, the man of character born in P'yŏngyang (平壤), J. M. S.
You hated me who lacked virtue (德),
And loved me who had talent (才操).
J. M. S., who was at Osan (五山),
After ten springs, I think of you this morning.

Waking up after sleeping dreamlessly for the first time in recent years.
A ruddy face, small stature, and lean figure,
A will like tempered steel seemed ready to burst forth,
Only the burning pupils shone exceptionally bright.
That passionate one who knew nothing more than for the sake of the Nation (民族),

With his simple appearance (風采), benevolent (仁慈) like his old self,
However, ah— tangled in alcohol, women (게집), and greed (利慾),
Me, desolate after fifteen years,
Why is it that you, sir,
Seek me out in your mind? This morning.
Beautiful, great love never dies,
Remembered, it always hides within my breast,
To calm my conscience whenever it strays,
Until I leave this painful world.<177>

**Summary**

This collection, titled "Sowŏl Poem Collection," presents a series of poems by Kim Sowŏl published in 1934. The poems explore themes of existential angst ("Life, Money, and Death"), the pervasive influence and lack of money ("Money Song"), longing and separation (adaptations of Chinese poems like "Naggonggok," "Yijuga," "Changganhaeng," "Seeing off Yuan Er"), and a poignant remembrance of a respected figure, "J. M. S." The overall tone ranges from philosophical despair and cynicism about economic hardship to nostalgia and reflections on love, friendship, and past ideals. From a historical perspective, the poems are revealing of the socio-economic conditions and psychological landscape of Koreans during the colonial period. The explicit mention of potentially migrating to Manchuria or mines for economic reasons ("Money Song"), the reference to national passion ("J.M.S."), and the pervasive sense of loss, displacement, and existential questioning resonate strongly with the historical context of Korea under Japanese rule. The poem "J.M.S." remembering a figure from Osan School, possibly a missionary, adds a layer about encounters with Western influence and education in that era.

生과 돈과 死

1. 설으면 우올 것을, 웃업거든 우슬 것을,

한벼개 잠 자거든, 한솟밥 먹는 님께,
허거픈 이 심사를 전해볼까 할지라도,
맛찹은 말 업거니와, 그 亦 누될까합니다.

누된달 心情만이 타고날께 무엇인고-,

차라로 그 身勢를 나는 부러워합니다.

2. 슬품과 괴롭음과 깁븜과 즐겁음과
사랑미움까지라도, 지난뒤 꿈아닌가!
그러면 그 무엇을, 제가 산다고 합니까?

꿈이 만일 살앗서면, 살미 亦是 꿈일게라!
잠이 만일 죽엄이면, 죽어꿈도 사라듯하리.
잡고 끗끗내 이럿타해도 이를 또 어찌합니까?

사랏든 그記憶이 죽어 만일 잇달진댄,

3. 사라서 그만인가? 죽으면 그 뿐인가?

살아서 못 죽는가, 죽엇다는 못 사든가?
암만이 살지라도 알지못할 이 세상을,
죽엇다 살지락도 또 모를 줄로 압니다.<174>

이 세상 산다는 것, 나도 무지 모르갓네.
어듸서 예 왓는고? 죽어 어찌 될 것인고?
도무지 이 모르는데서, 어쩨 이러는가 합니다.
