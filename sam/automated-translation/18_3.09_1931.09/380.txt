**Body Text Translation:**
When the Sweetbrier Blooms, at Myŏngsa Simni
						Ch'osa

Bloomed, bloomed
The sweetbrier has bloomed
On that wide field of Myŏngsa Simni
Blood-red flowers have bloomed so beautifully

A grown maiden from Hamgyŏng Province
When going out to the pollack grounds
Plucks an armful of flowers
And scatters them upon the vast sea

How could flowers scattered on the vast sea
Bloom upon the waves?
Young boatman, oh Third Son,
As if seeing me, please pick them up

Do you see her now, the Hamgyŏng maiden picking blood-red sweetbrier flowers on that sandy field? How is it that she is so lost in picking flowers that she doesn't even notice me approaching beside her?
Perhaps this maiden is the same woman I met this morning at Kalma Ferry Crossing (葛麻津), where fishing boats dock, while I was strolling along the coast. If so, I think I know the reason she is picking flowers. A little while ago, that maiden was clearly gathering seaweed at the base of a rock, holding a basket made of bush clover. Just then, a fishing boat came in from the distant sea. From that boat, a young boatman with a turban tied tightly around his head and a nose bridge standing straight like the T'aebaek Mountains leaped ashore. The maiden blushed and quickly hid herself in the shadow of the rock, while the man, whether he saw the maiden or not, whistled nonchalantly and strode off towards the pine-forested fishing village shrouded in the smoke of breakfast cooking.
The man will probably come out again at dusk to cast his nets in the distant sea. The maiden, lacking the chance to speak to him, must have been agonizing alone, thrown the seaweed she had gathered back into the sea, and come straight to this sweetbrier field to pick flowers. Isn't she picking these flowers to float them on the shore when the boat departs, hoping that the young boatman might chance upon them and pick them up? Even if he doesn't pick them up, doesn't she wish they might at least touch the tip of his oar as he rows?
Her heart is simple and deeply pitiful. If that boatman acts as if he doesn't notice, how lonely will the heart of this young maiden seeking love become, burning endlessly, lonely, lonely, and small? How resentful she will be towards the sea where he rowed away, and how she will sigh when facing the sweetbrier flowers! Perhaps not just sighs; who can say she won't come to this flower field every morning, rain or shine, twelve months a year, glare out at the vast, boundless ocean, trample one sweetbrier stalk in the morning, break another in the evening, and eventually turn this lovely flower field into a desolate white sand plain?
I know her feelings well. Don't I also have such sad memories? No matter how much I try to laugh loudly, ha-ha-ha, isn't there always a corner somewhere from which an empty laugh escapes? Isn't there a kind of sorrow that makes me hesitate and sit down just when I'm preparing to charge forward in all matters?
Who is to say that the flowers she was picking are not the flowers I picked in some bygone day? If not, the only difference would be that she picked the sweetbrier of the Southern Provinces<90> and I picked the bellflowers of the Northern Provinces.
I went up beside her. I went and broke off a flower stalk and asked to place it in the bouquet she intended to scatter on the sea. Since she too was weeping over a lonely love, and I too had such times, are we not truly compatriots, brothers and sisters, comrades, and also like an older brother and younger sister?
At that moment, the maiden, as if noticing for the first time that a strange man had come beside her, rounded her mouth like a gong, opened her eyes brightly, showing clear surprise, and lightly uttered,
"Oh, how embarrassing!"
Leaving behind that one phrase, she abruptly scattered the flowers she had gathered in her skirt hem and darted away, stumbling this way and that. I exclaimed,
"Oh dear!"
And watched her retreating figure. Her black, long braid undulated like waves over her skirt hem, and her bare white feet, without even socks, left only the soft "sabak-sabak" sound as they tread upon the white sand.
After running a short distance, she stopped abruptly, turned around gracefully, and looked this way. Like a "kat-chu-sa" (a type of traditional singer or entertainer, maybe referring to a pose?), she tilted her head, slender like a willow leaf, slightly to the left and stood there delicately. Indeed.
Only then did I realize she had forgotten her basket, hence her behavior, and quickly held up the basket filled with sweetbrier. I couldn't quite see if a smile played on her lips then, but I clearly saw her head nod lightly up and down, like a clock's pendulum moving silently.
I quickly moved away from that spot, went to the seashore, let the waves wet my legs up to the knees, and then quickly turned back. The maiden had flown back like a swallow, quickly grabbed her basket, and this time ran off in the direction she had gone before without looking back. Seeing her like that, I noticed her indigo skirt had marks from the splashing waves. It must have been from when she was gathering seaweed at the base of the rock this morning, so engrossed in watching the young boatman that she didn't even notice the waves crashing.
I mentally named the man "Setchae" (Third Son) and then, alone, walked along the Myŏngsa Simni coast, singing this song to myself. Discovering the shadow of my own失神した (shisshin shita - lost/fainted, likely meaning 'lost' or 'despondent' here) memories from a certain time, feeling desolate, desolate—

During my studies in Tokyo, there was a time when I discovered Nietzsche, and my whole body felt like a fireball with his ideas. Especially when, during his youth, while crossing the Alps on his way to Italy, he saw the white snow, accumulated over millions and hundreds of thousands of years, covering the silent mountains and rivers, and heaven and earth seemed dead quiet. Seeing the endless Nirvana (寂滅) of all things in the universe, how much did this famous German pessimistic philosopher wander through the snowfields, intending to commit suicide?
The impulse to commit suicide! This is an immortal feeling hidden deep, deep in the far corner of my heart. It was Nietzsche who drew this out and lit a fire in its wick. Because of this, how much did I wander through the pine forests of Yoyogi (代代木)? Then, wanting to flee to a monastery in Hokkaido (北海道) and spend my life calling on Mary Magdalene (막달나마리아) to become a monk, how many times did I secretly knock on the door of a Western missionary in the dead of night? After the earthquake disaster (震災-) when all property and spirit turned to ash, picking up the thread-like, torn life and returning to Korea, how fondly did I envision a hermitage deep in the uninhabited Kŭmgang Mountains?
However, all the realities attached to me demanded a few more grains of rice from my life, a few more yards and inches of clothing. So, I became a person who wanders around and works like an ox from morning till night. People around me call this brave, and also a good tendency.
If I continue like this, I feel the temptation that I might surely discover light for myself. Light in the darkness! It feels like that light will shine through if I just break down this wall blocking my path a little more.
On the day I find this light, I will sprawl out at the peak of life and laugh loudly, loudly, indeed. The cry of a crane would be insufficient, the roar of a bear in the deep mountains and remote valleys would be insufficient, the thunder in the sky would be insufficient—that's how loudly, loudly I will laugh and live. If I fail to find that light, I will again be captured by the old pessimistic thoughts, either flee to a monastery or cast this body<91> into the rocks of the nine-bend treacherous path within the vast blue waves. Weren't there times when the sea and mountains where all earthly creatures struggle, or the monastery, all felt like the longed-for hometown I always wanted to go to?
I feel now that I am situated in a great crisis. Light and darkness! Light and darkness!
To discover the light, perhaps I must become a demon. But does my weak nature possess such courage and determination? Will my character, having tried and failed for seven or eight years to become a demon, ever become strong?
Lately, I enjoy walking alone in quiet places, and whenever I walk alone, I am preoccupied with thinking about the light and darkness laid out before me.
Even now, after that maiden ran off with the sweetbrier basket, as I walk along the endless stretch of Myŏngsa Simni, befriended only by the sea waves, my heart is filled with this thought, and I don't know what to do.
There flies a white gull (白鷗). Seeing the white gull, I compare it to myself, and seeing the islands standing distant and withdrawn, my mind cannot find peace.
Every time I see my shadow cast sideways on the sandy field, my heart aches, but there seems to be no way to cure this pain. Go on and on, keep going, yet it seems absent. Will it truly be absent forever?

Oh-hŏ-ya, let's go, let's go
Even if there's nowhere to go, we must go
All the places walked are desolate
Even if there's nowhere to go, we must go

Oh-hŏ-ya, let's go, let's go
Beyond the mountain, there will be the sea
Beyond the sea, there will be another mountain
What if I make mountains and sea my friends for life?

Oh-hŏ-ya, let's go, let's go
If the deep water flows away
New water will come again
Even if this body goes, won't others come after?

However, Myŏngsa Simni is delightful. Look at that blue sea. Look at those waves energetically surging forward. Among those waves, might there not be the waves carrying letters floated by our brothers in America (美洲), sitting on the Pacific Coast (太平洋岸), longing for their homeland? Might there not be the passionate tears of the Komsomol pioneers (꼼소-몰 피오낼) of the Maritime Province (沿海州), charging forward, again and again, for the construction of a new world? And in the wind that pushes the waves from east, west, south, and north, I seem to hear the refreshing folk song melodies of the Jeju Island (濟州島) fisherwomen (海女) and the spirited singing voices of middle school students from all over the peninsula, crossing mountains after mountains.
Here, there is only openness. Only joy and freedom exist. There is only the unrestrained freedom like that of the flock of seagulls, settling on this island if they wish, or settling on that rock if they wish. I too must break free from this confined life.
That's right, that's right! I must quickly come out of the secret chamber, stand on that sandy field, and wash away all the gloomy thoughts of yesterday, when even seeing a utility pole humming in the sea breeze, I imagined it carrying coded telegrams saying "Catch my brother-in-law," "Catch Lyŏ Un-hyŏng and Kim Ch'an." Instead, I must think of it as a utility pole sending congratulatory telegrams like, "Mr. Kim's daughter and Mr. Lee's son are getting married, how delightful!"
I must cast away the sad thought that all that dark land over there is just land waiting to be taken away, and think instead, let's make it richer fertile land through our efforts.
Seeing that pine branch sticking out slanted in the pine forest, I must abandon the thought of looping my belt over it and wondering if it would break if I hanged myself, and instead think that it looks sturdy enough to hang a swing (추천) on and play. Thinking these thoughts, I take a deep breath facing the distant sea. I try shouting military commands loudly until my throat is hoarse, and just shout "Wŏ-i, Wŏ-i" like a boatman's call. Then, truly, my heart feels as broad as the sea off Wŏnsan, and a beautiful landscape and fragrance like a sweetbrier field seem to envelop my whole body. In the end, I feel the thought arising that I should neither go to a monastery nor die, but live cheerfully on this earth. Wouldn't that be right? I must think it is right.<92>

**Summary**

The author, Ch'osa, describes an encounter at Myŏngsa Simni beach with a young woman from Hamgyŏng Province who is gathering sweetbrier flowers, seemingly to express her unspoken feelings for a young boatman. This observation triggers the author's own reflections on loneliness, past sorrows, and a profound existential crisis marked by suicidal thoughts influenced by Nietzsche and the desire for monastic escape, contrasted with the demands of reality. He contemplates the struggle between despair ("darkness") and the hope for a meaningful life ("light"). Ultimately, the open expanse of the sea and beach inspires a fragile resolution to embrace life, reject gloomy thoughts, and find joy and purpose on "this earth," symbolized by the beauty and freedom of Myŏngsa Simni.

From a historical perspective, the article is revealing for its candid portrayal of intellectual despair and existential angst among Korean intellectuals during the colonial period, grappling with personal struggles alongside the implicit backdrop of national subjugation. The references to Nietzsche, escape to monasteries, coded mentions of political figures like Lyuh Woon-hyung and Kim Ch'an potentially being targeted (in the imagined telegram), Komsomol pioneers in the Soviet Far East, and emigrants in America, provide glimpses into the diverse ideological currents, anxieties, and transnational connections shaping Korean consciousness in the early 1930s.

지금 저 모래벌에서 피빗가튼 海棠花 꼿을 따는 함경도 색시를 여러분은 보심니까. 엇지하야 그는 내가 겻헤 가는 줄도 모르게 꼿 따기에만 넉을 일코 잇슴니까.
