Clues to the Discovery of a Major Incident (Seen 20 Years Ago)
						By Yu Kwang-yŏl

Receiving a request from the Samch'ŏlli editorial department to write about the Kimi [1919] events, I take up my brush and feel anew how fast time flies.
Now, looking at the paulownia leaves falling from the tree outside my window in the autumn wind, let me reminisce about those events that flowed away twelve years ago.

In June 1918, as the incredibly disastrous Great War in Europe (구라파대전난), which had wiped out tens of billions in assets and tens of millions of lives, finally came to an end, a peace conference was set to open in Paris. American President Wilson (윌손) emerged as its protagonist, and his announcement of the fourteen basic conditions for peace with Germany greatly stirred the world's attention.
Among them, the clause on "national self-determination" (민족자결) gave an extraordinary impulse to the world's weak and small nations.
The people of a certain place in the East also began to buzz with activity. Thus, in January 1919, Koreans in America first initiated a national movement, and soon after, compatriots in various places overseas also rose up. In Russia, the XXXX Council [likely Korean National Council] convened, and in Tokyo, the international students (류학생들) issued the XX Declaration [likely February 8 Declaration].

At this time, within Korea itself, under the atmosphere created by General Hasegawa (長谷川將軍), only an inscrutable silence prevailed.
As February of that year, with its falling snow, arrived, the quiet waves began to stir. Students in classrooms, teachers in staff rooms, believers in churches, and the populace in drawing rooms (사랑방) and main halls (대청마루) somehow started whispering.<38>
During this time, the Christians proceeded with their movement plans, as did the Buddhists, eventually joining forces.

At this time, Mr. O and Mr. K of the XX religion [likely Ch'ŏndogyo] were employed as non-regular staff (촉탁) at a certain fearsome place. They continued to report to work there until two days before signing [the Declaration]. Then, perhaps sensing something, someone there asked Mr. K.
"Will a XX movement arise in Korea?"
"Well... I don't know about the future, but as things stand now, the populace isn't even dreaming of it."
Indeed, a simple conversation.
Around this time (portion omitted - original text), he too asked,
"What is the situation among Korean figures?" S still spoke of the continued tranquility.

Thus, at Posŏngsa (普成社) in Susong-dong, which has now become the kindergarten of the Buddhist Affairs Office (佛敎敎務院), the printed document XXX [likely the Declaration of Independence], which Mr. R stayed up all night printing, was released on March 1st.
As people of purpose might notice if they look closely now, it was in that pavilion which became the T'aehwa Yŏjagwan (泰和女子舘) across from Sadong from Chongno, where the thirty-some signatories gathered and announced it before a meal resembling a last supper.
Around this time (portion omitted - original text), five or six thousand people began (portion omitted - original text).
The signatories telephoned, "We thirty-three people are announcing the (portion omitted - original text) through (portion omitted - original text), and a celebration banquet is being held, so how about sharing a drink together?"
The Metropolitan Police Headquarters (경무총감부) was startled. A flood of police officers came rushing in cars! In cars! To T'aehwagwan (泰和舘). They surrounded the banquet site three or four layers deep. In the meantime (portion omitted - original text).
At that moment, Mr. P of the Christian faith, Mr. S of the Ch'ŏndogyo faith, and Mr. H of the Buddhist faith calmly continued eating, while the others sat stiffly upright (remainder omitted - original text).

Now it is autumn.
The wind that brushes past my ears makes me recall old events anew.<39>

**Summary**

This 1931 memoir by Yu Kwang-yŏl recounts the atmosphere and events leading up to the March 1st Movement in 1919. Triggered by Wilson's "self-determination" principle after WWI, Korean movements began overseas (US, Russia, Tokyo). In contrast, Korea under Governor-General Hasegawa was outwardly calm but filled with hidden activity among students, teachers, and religious groups (Christian, Buddhist, Ch'ŏndogyo implied). The author notes the secrecy maintained by key figures, even those working in sensitive positions, right before the event. He describes the printing of the Declaration of Independence at Posŏngsa and the final gathering of the 33 signatories at T'aehwagwan on March 1st, which ended with their arrest after they boldly notified the police of their "celebration." The most revealing aspects for historians are the firsthand description of the tense, secretive atmosphere preceding the March 1st Movement, the explicit mention of the confluence of different religious groups in planning, and the anecdote about the signatories' deliberate call to the police from T'aehwagwan, highlighting their non-violent, declarative strategy and resolve. The use of initials (O, K, S, P, H, R) also hints at the continued sensitivity surrounding the event even in 1931.

						柳光烈
삼천리 편즙국으로부터 긔미때 이약이를 써보라는 부탁을 밧고 붓을 잡으니 세월이 새삼스럽게 빨은 것을 늣기게 된다.
이제 가을바람에 떠러지는 문압 오동나무입을 바라보면서 멀니 열두해전에 흘너가버린 그때 일을 회상하여 보기로 하자.
1918년 6월 그러케도 참담하든 구라파대전난이 수백억의 재산과 수천만의 생명을 업새고 겨우 끗나자 파리에는 강화회의가 열니기로 되어 그 주인공노릇으로 미국대통령 「윌손」이 나섯고 그가 대독강화(對獨講和)에 대한 십사개조의 긔본조건을 발표하야 세계의 이목을 자못 소동식혓다.
그증에 「민족자결」이란 한 조목은 세계약소민족들에게 비상한 충동을 주엇다.
동방의 엇던 곳 백성들도 자못 수선수선하기 시작하엿다. 그래서 일천구백십구년 일월에 미국에 잇는 조선사람들이 몬저 민족운동을 이르키고 뒤이어 해외각처에 잇는 동포들이 또한 이러섯다. 로령에서는 XXXX회의가 열니고 동경에서는 류학생들이 XX선언을 발표하엿다.

이때에 조선내지에서는 장곡천장군(長谷川將軍)의 공긔속에 알 수 업는 침묵에 고요히 잠겨 잇슬 따름이엇다.
그해 눈내리는 이월이 되면서 고요하든 파도는 움실움실 물결치기 비릇하엿다. 학생들은 교실에서 교사는 직원실에서 교인은 교회당에서 민중은 사랑방 대청마루에서 엇전지 수군수군하게<38> 되엇다.
이사이에 야소교는 야소교대로 불교는 불교대로 운동방침을 진행하고 잇다가 필경 합류하게 되엇다.

이때 XX교의 O씨와 K씨는 엇던 무시무시한 곳에 촉탁으로 잇섯다. 그네는 서명하기 전 이틀전까지 여전히 그곳에 출근하엿다. 그때 그곳에서는 무슨 눈치를 채렷는지 K씨더러 물엇다.
「조선에서 XX운동이 이러날가요.」
「글세요. 장래는 엇덜는지 지금 가타여서는 민중이 꿈도 아니꿈니다.」
실로 간단한 대화이다.
