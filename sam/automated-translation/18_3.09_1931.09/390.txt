The Watermill
						To Hyang (Posthumous Work)

*Clatter, clatter*—the water entering the flume, then pouring out again, lifts the heavy watermill wheel high, then *thump*, throws it down into the trough. Inside the mill house, thick with white grain dust, the humming sighs of the farmhands sound mournful.

*Swish, swish, swish*—water becoming beads, then silver powder, stretching out like bamboo stalks, then *crash, crash*, pouring down, becoming blue dragons and white dragons, surging like rising dragons, the water flows. After winding around the mountain corner ten *ri* away on that side, and then cutting through the fields on this side for about five *ri*, it washes past the edge of the village where this Pangwŏn (芳源) lives, and there sits a watermill upon it.

Looking out from the watermill, to the northeast lies a sizable village. In this village, the richest and most powerful man is called Sin Ch’igyu (申治圭). This man named Pangwŏn lives in his servant's quarters (막실사리) and cultivates his land, getting by day to day with his wife.

One autumn night, an unusually bright moon shone tranquilly upon this quiet village. Near the watermill, the sound of a woman and a man talking could be heard.

The woman was Pangwŏn's wife, now twenty-two years old, a young woman at the age when her heart should be burning with passion, the age of greatest happiness. The man was over fifty-five, having lived most of the path life offers, already nearing the pit of decline, an old man.

His voice sounded as if coaxing the woman. "Hey, isn't there nothing wrong with what I'm saying? I'm sure Old Lady Swenae explained the details, but think about it. If you just agree, I'll give you anything you say you want. How many centuries would you have to live with that fellow Pangwŏn to ever escape the corner of the servant's quarters? Heh heh, if a person doesn't experience luxury when young, they'll die without ever having known it, isn't that right? What I'm saying isn't wrong at all! I heard the gist of your thoughts from Old Lady Swenae, but still, it's not the same as hearing it directly from you, so I asked to meet you here. What's on your mind? Come on, heh heh, don't feel awkward in front of me, just tell me, eh?"

This old man was, without a doubt, Sin Ch’igyu. He stared at Pangwŏn's wife with greedy eyes, patting her back with one hand.

Her aloof face was pale blueish, with long eyebrows and dark bluish eye rims, powdered lips, pouting cheeks, and a sharp nose bridge. Combined with her slender height and broad hips, she looked frighteningly rational (理智的) and, at the same time, like the prostitute type (娼婦型).

The woman stood without a word, deliberately feigning shyness, flashed a seductive smile, and turned her head away. How much that smile must have satisfied the beast-like Sin Ch’igyu, how it must have stirred his heart; his graying beard came closer, almost touching the woman's cheek.

"Hey? Why no answer? Are you shy? It's not something to be so shy about."
He took the woman's hand.
"I never knew until now that your hands were this pretty.<93> Truly like powder. Isn't it too pitiful and wasteful for such a gracefully formed girl like you to become the wife of a lowly fellow like Pangwŏn and rot away your whole life like this? Hey."

The woman made no move to turn away, letting the old master do as he pleased, just looking down at the ground with her eyes, then finally seemed to part her lips with difficulty.
"My words... Old Lady Swenae told you everything. What you say is too much for my station."

"Nonsense, you're talking utter nonsense. What kind of talk is that? As you know, I'm not doing this just for fun, and it's because I have no heir (後嗣), so please, bear me a son. Then, wouldn't everything I have become yours? Come now, don't be like this, agree today. Then, even tomorrow, I'll kick that fellow Pangwŏn out and bring you in."

"How can you kick him out?"

"Heh, what's so difficult about that? Do you think he won't leave if I tell him to get out? Do you think he'll endure a hundred days!"

"But wouldn't that be too much?"

"What? Because you think like that, you've stayed in this state until now. What does it matter? Don't worry about such things at all, come on! Let's go inside quickly before your husband finds out."

"You go in first."

"Why?"

"People might find it suspicious if they see us."

"What's suspicious about you going with me?"

"Let's go quickly."
The woman slowly followed a few steps behind, then stopped hesitantly.
"Master!"

"What is it?"
The woman stood silently again, then said,
"It's nothing."
And added,
"Go in first," turning back. The old master, impatient, grabbed the woman's hand.

"Let's go. Let's go into the house."
His chest seemed to pound, his breathing quickened. The woman tried to pull her hand away.
"You're a respectable elder, what is this behavior?"
Yet, her body language showed consent to everything. The old master pulled the woman's body close and turned behind the mill house. The woman, held in the old master's arms, looked at him with eyes full of desire.

"Master," she said one word and swallowed once.
"You won't lie, will you, Master?"

"No."
His voice trembled. The woman grasped the old master's arm with one hand and pointed inside the mill house with the other.
"Go in there."
The old master and the woman came out of the mill house again after twenty or thirty minutes.

Three days later, Sin Ch’igyu summoned Pangwŏn to the front yard of his main house's detached building (sarangmadang).
"Hey."
Pangwŏn bowed his head to his master.
"Yes."
He answered respectfully.
"While I appreciate your diligent work at my house thus far..."
Sin Ch’igyu began, putting on airs of dignity and authority. Pangwŏn's heart seemed to anticipate the words following "...but," as if all the blood in his body rushed to his chest, and then the hair on his head seemed to stand on end.

"Starting today, due to circumstances at my house, don't stay here anymore; go find a good place elsewhere."
There were no conditions. Nor was there anything to say here. If told to die, one had to at least pretend to die. The master could buy and sell people with money.

Pangwŏn felt suffocated. If he were alone, he could survive somehow, somewhere, even by begging, but the path to providing for his beloved wife was bleak. He bowed his head, bent his waist, and finally bent his spirit, trying to plead and beg. But it was futile. The master's heart was harder than iron or stone.

Having no choice, he told his wife the story. He then asked his wife to plead with the mistress of the house to let them stay a little longer. But his wife was unlikely to listen to Pangwŏn's words. Instead<94>,
"Then what are we supposed to do? How are you going to feed me from now on?"

"Are you that scared you won't be able to eat and live?"

"Of course, I'm scared. Think about it. Aren't we completely doomed now?"

"Doomed?"

"Well, when you brought me here, what did you say? Didn't you say you could somehow manage to feed at least one person like me?"

"Yes."

"So, how well have you fed me and given me luxury? For two whole years now, all you've done is drag me around to live in someone else's servants' quarters, right?"

"Hey, are you saying this without knowing? Did things turn out this way because I didn't try? As we live on, something will surely come up. We won't just grow old and die like this, will we?"

"I don't want to hear it! 'We'll eat the horns when they fall off'—when will that ever happen?"

Pangwŏn, already kicked out and angry, felt a fire surge within him as his wife spoke like this.
"You wretched woman, deserving of death six times over! Why do you stir up a man's heart?"

"Why are you cursing at me?"

"You worthless wench, what if I curse a little?"

"Why are you cursing?"
The woman's face turned yellow as she confronted him.
"Is this wench throwing a fit?"

"Who's throwing a fit? A man who can't even support one woman just curses at her, what else has he done? Have you ever bought me even a silver ring or a silver hairpin? Is there anything I haven't done as you asked!"

"You wench, do you want silver rings and hairpins that badly? You filthy wench."

"What's filthy? How clean are you, you bastard?"
The word "bastard" (놈) started coming from the woman's mouth.

"Look at this wench. Who are you calling a bastard?"
His hand grabbed the woman's chignon (낭자), lifted her by it, and punched her back several times.
"You wench who deserves the leg-squeezing torture (주리때)!"
A couple of kicks landed on her buttocks, and the woman fell backward before getting up again. Her undone hair hung messily, and tears mixed in her rice-water-pale eyes.

"Why are you hitting me? You bastard! Kill me. Go ahead, beat me to death. You bastard, let's both die, you first!"
She lunged at him, but he struck her down.
"Is this wench trying her hardest to die!"

When Pangwŏn hit his wife, it was a kind of rough play (롱담) done with fists. When his fist or foot struck her body, Pangwŏn felt a pain piercing his own chest, sharper than the pain felt by the wife receiving the blow. Hitting her in anger was, in reality, no different from biting his own arms and legs with his teeth. For him, hitting her carried intense pity and wretchedness. However, the only person who accepted his venting of anger so far had been his wife. More than her being the easiest target, she was the one he could most freely vent his anger upon. When, not even a day after the fight, the two lay with pillows side by side, holding each other tightly, there was no comfort so precious, so moving again. After hitting his wife and venting his anger, the comfort received through regret that seemed to tear his chest and a warmer embrace gave Pangwŏn, not just the two of them, a strong and fervent trust unlike any other.

The wife deliberately raised her voice and wept loudly.
Though almost everyone in the village listened, they thought,
"Ah, they're having a lovers' quarrel again!"
And rather envied the fight. A young person from the neighboring house came over, smiling broadly, looked in, and pretended to mediate, saying,
"Stop it now."
Only the village children lined up in front of the yard, watching with wide eyes.

That evening, Pangwŏn returned home, tipsy from alcohol. The anger from hitting his wife earlier had somehow dissipated, and in his alcohol-fueled excitement, he yearned deeply for his wife's embrace, even feeling the urge to apologize to her. Fundamentally good-natured, soft-hearted, and affectionate, his ignorant upbringing led him to commit foolish acts, but this foolishness did not define his character.<95>

Stumbling along the path home, he closed his drowsy eyes slightly and muttered to himself,
"Damn bastard! If he says get out, I'll get out, am I scared? He must think there's nowhere else to live but his house! Humph, isn't everything pointless? Is having money the most important thing? You bastard, if you keep this up, you'll taste these fists someday, do you think you'll die peacefully?"
After jumping over a stream,
"Money! What is money?"
After thinking for a moment,
"Sigh."
He let out a sigh, then,
"Money kills people! Money! Money! Humph, people came first, then money, right? Did money come first, then people?"
Stumbling across the stepping stones again,
"That damn woman (고 배라 먹을 년), why did she throw such a tantrum (포달) and scratch at a man's heart!"
His voice held an indescribably affectionate tone. When he thought of his wife, all his complaints seemed to melt away. Lifting his previously bowed head, he looked up at the sky.
"Well, she's suffering too."
He lowered his head again.
"I went too far, too far. I shouldn't have done that."

He reached his house, grabbed the door handle, and shook it.
"Hey! Are you asleep! Hey!"
But nobody was there, only darkness.
"Where did that wench go!"
He slammed the door hard enough to break it, then went back out to the street and to the house next door.
"Hey, Auntie! Have you seen my wife? Do you know where she went?"
The neighboring couple, who were eating, said,
"Looks like you got drunk somewhere again! Oh dear, she did her hair earlier and went towards the mill (방아께)." (Continued on page 59)<96>

"Towards the mill?"
"Yes."
"Damn wench! What did she go to the mill for!"
He headed towards the mill alone, muttering to himself again.

Just as he turned around the back of the mill house, he saw Sin Ch’igyu and his wife coming out of it.
"Ah."
It was so unexpected that he couldn't say anything, just stood there dumbfounded for a long while, watching.

His eyes blazed with anger (쌍심지가 걱구로 섯다). Heat rose within him, making his eyes red as if painted with vermilion, and a fierce glint like a dog's flashed in them.
He trembled all over for a long time. His teeth chattered, clattering together. His fists clenched so tightly they felt like they would break.

The wife (계집) and Sin Ch’igyu, seeing Pangwŏn standing there, felt a chill run down their spines at first, but then calmly composed themselves. Now that things had come to this, their attitude was 'do what you will'.
Pangwŏn rushed forward and grabbed his wife's wrist. (Continued on page 85)<59>
He clenched his teeth and trembled violently.
"I never thought you'd do this."
The wife (계집) retorted,
"What did you never think I'd do?"
She glared with her blueish eyes.
"I'm seeing all sorts of things in the end. Did you only just realize it would obviously be like this? Let go! Why are you holding someone else's arm like this? From today, you can't treat me carelessly! Filthy fellow! If a woman is greedy, a man should just step aside, what kind of unmanly behavior is this! Let go."
She tried to shake off his arm, but filled with fury, he didn't release his grip so easily.
"Hey? Are you serious about this?"<85>

"Am I not serious? Would I lie after eating expensive rice?"

"Have you truly gone mad?"

"What? Who are you saying has gone mad? It's utterly suffocating! Let go! Let go! Let go, clinging like this?"
She wrenched her hand away forcefully, and it slipped out. The wife rubbed her wrist and turned away resentfully.

Sin Ch’igyu, who had been watching this scene from a distance, took a couple of steps forward, cleared his throat awkwardly, and said,
"Hey! If you're drunk, you should go home early and sleep, what is this behavior? Do your eyes (눈갈) not see anything? Your quarreling, you wench and bastard, is something you should do anywhere else, don't you see who's here and who isn't?"
He deliberately raised his voice and shouted authoritatively.
"Tsk, despicable fellow!"
He glared fiercely. Pangwŏn looked up at him for a long time without speaking. If he acted on impulse, he would knock him down with one punch, but the notion that Sin Ch’igyu was his master until just moments ago still lingered in his mind. Like lightning, that notion suppressed his mouth and arms. His mind, having only served others from childhood until today, had deeply ingrained the trait of fearing anyone considered a master. However, starting today, Sin Ch’igyu was no longer his master, and he was no longer Sin Ch’igyu's servant. They were merely equals (뚝가튼 사람), facing each other. No, from now on, Sin Ch’igyu was Pangwŏn's enemy. (Continued in the next issue)<86>

**Summary**

The story depicts the plight of Pangwŏn, a poor tenant farmer, and his young wife. Pangwŏn works for the wealthy and powerful landowner, Sin Ch'igyu. Sin Ch'igyu, desiring an heir and attracted to Pangwŏn's wife, conspires with her to become his mistress, promising her wealth and luxury. He then dismisses Pangwŏn without cause. Facing destitution, Pangwŏn confronts his wife, leading to a violent argument where she scorns his poverty and inability to provide. Later, Pangwŏn discovers his wife and Sin Ch'igyu together at the watermill. A tense confrontation ensues, where Pangwŏn's ingrained deference to his former master conflicts with his rage and sense of betrayal, setting the stage for future conflict.

From a historical perspective, the story vividly illustrates the stark class divisions and the power imbalance between landowners and tenants in colonial Korea. Sin Ch'igyu's exploitation of Pangwŏn and his wife reflects the economic vulnerability and lack of recourse for the lower classes. The wife's decision, driven by poverty and desire for a better life, highlights the limited choices available to women in that society. The simmering resentment and eventual confrontation between Pangwŏn and Sin Ch'igyu symbolize the underlying tensions and potential for rebellion against oppressive social structures.

솰 솰 솰 구슬이 되엿다가 은가루가 되고 대줄기가치 뻐치엇다가 다시 쾅 쾅 쏘다저 청룡이 되고 백룡이 되어 용소슴처 흘으는 물이 저쪽 산모통이를 십리나 두고 돌고 다시 이쪽 들북판을 오리쯤 꾀뚜른 뒤에 이 방원(芳源)이가 사는 동리 압 기슬글 시처 지내 가는데 그우에 물레방아 하나가 노여 잇다.
물레방아에서 듸려다 보면 동북간으로 큼직한 마을이 잇스니 이 마을에 가잠 부자요 가장 세력이 잇는 사람으로 일홈을 신치규(申治圭)라고 불은다. 이 방원이라는 사람은 그 집의 막실(幕室)사리를 하야 가며 그이 땅을 경작하야 자긔 안해와 두 사람이 그날 그날을 지내간다.
엇더한 가을 밤 유난히 밝은 달이 고요한 이 촌을 한적하게 비초일 때 그 물레방아깐 엽헤 엇더한 녀자 하나와 엇던 남자 하나가 서서 이야기를 하는 소리가 들리엇다.
그 녀자는 방원의 안해로 지금 나히가 스물 두살 한참 정열의 타는 가슴으로 가장 행복스러울 나히의 젊은 녀자이오 그 남자는 오십이 반이 넘어 인생으로서 살아올 길을 다 살고서 거의 거이 쇠멸의 구렁이를 향하야 가는 늙은이다.
그의 말소리는 마치 그 녀자를 달래는 것과 가치 『얘 내 말이 조곰도 글을 것이 업지? 쇠네 할멈에게도 자세한 말을 드렷슬 터이지만은 너 생각해 보아라. 네가 허락만 하면 무엇이든지 네가 허고 십다는 것은 내가 전부 해줄 터이란 말야. 그까짓 방원이 녀석하고 네가 몃 백년 살어야 언제든지 막실 구석을 면치 못할 터이니 허허 사람이란 젊어서 호강해 보지 못하면 평생 호강 한번 하야보지 못하고 죽을 것이 안이냐. 내가 말하는 것이 조곰도 잘못하는 것이 업느니라! 대강 너의 말을 쇠네 할멈에게 듯기는 들엇스나 그래도 너의게 한번 바로 대고 듯는 것만 못해서 이리로 맛나자고 한 것이다. 너의 마음은 엇더냐? 어듸 허허 내 압히라고 조곰도 엇더케 알지 말고 이야기 해봐 응?』
이 늙은이는 두 말할 것 업시 신치규다. 그는 탐욕스러운 눈으로 방원이 게집을 듸려다 보며 한 손으로 등을 뛰다린다.
새침한 얼골이 파루쭉쪽하고 기다란 눈섭과 검푸른 눈가장자리에 입분 입 뾰루통한 뺨이며 콧날이 옷똑한데다가 후리후리한 키에 떡 버러진 엉등이가 아모리 보드래도 무서웁게 리지적(理智的)인 동시에 또는 창부형(娼婦型)으로 생긴 녀자이다.
게집은 아모 말이 업시 서서 짐짓 북그러운 태를 자우며 미혹적의 우슴을 생긋 웃고는 고개를 도리켯다. 그 우슴이 얼마나 즘승가튼 신치규의 만족을 사게 되엇스며 또는 마음을 충동식혓는지 히끗한 수염이 거의 게집의 뺨에 닷도록 더 갓가히 와서.
『얘? 왜 대답이 업니? 붓그러워서 그러니. 그러케 붓그러워 할 일은 안인데』
하고 게집의 손을 잡으며
『손도 이러케 입분 줄은 여태까지 몰낫구나.<93> 참 분길갓다. 이러케 얌전히 생긴 애가 방원가튼 천한 놈의 게집이 되어 일평생을 그대로 썩는다는 것은 넘어 가엽고 앗갑지 안으냐? 얘.』

『에ㅅ 괘심한 놈!』
눈갈을 부라리엇다. 방원은 한참이나 치어다 보고서 말이 업섯다. 생각대로 하면 한 주먹에 때려 눕힐 것이지만은 그래도 그의 머리 속에는 앗가까지의 상전이라는 관념이 남어 잇섯다. 번개불가티 그 관념이 그의 입과 팔을 억거 노앗다. 어려서부터 오늘날까지 남을 섬겨 보기만 한 그의 마음은 상전이라면 모두 두러워 하는 성질이 깁히 깁히 뿌리를 박어 노앗다. 그러나 오늘부터는 신치규가 자긔 상전도 안이오 자긔가 신치규의 종도 안이다. 다만 뚝가튼 사람으로 서로 마주 섯슬 뿐이다. 안이라 지금부터는 신치규는 방원의 원수엇다.(以下 次號)<86>
