On the Road to P'aesŏng
						Sin Puch'ul

Is my mind adrift because my body flows?
The sun and moon deceive me, going on their own.
Where might my grave be, that I wander searching like this?

As time, wrinkling the path to the grave, flows and spring comes,
Youth is sickened; I hate to see the spring where flowers bloom.
Child, sweep away the spring itself, even before the falling blossoms.

With the coming spring and the departed beloved, am I the only one sad?
How many can be cheerful with the faint songs of the ninety days of spring light (90春光)?
At the old site, crows cry, flying towards the mountain behind.

Climbing high on Moran Hill (牡丹峰), I embrace the spring breeze,
To release the waters of the Taedong River (대동강), dress Nŭngna Islet (綾羅島) in new clothes,
And with the temple bell of Yŏngmyŏng Temple (永明寺), I wish to awaken the winter dream.
-(32, 3) <38>

**Summary**

This poem by Sin Puch'ul expresses a deep sense of melancholy, transience, and displacement. The speaker wanders, feeling disconnected from the passage of time and the arrival of spring, which only highlights their sorrow and "sickened youth." Visiting P'aesŏng (P'yŏngyang), the speaker observes signs of decay but ultimately expresses a desire for renewal, wishing to awaken the city from its "winter dream" by invoking its famous landmarks like Moran Hill, the Taedong River, Nŭngna Islet, and Yŏngmyŏng Temple. For historians, the poem reflects a common sentiment of lament and disillusionment among Korean intellectuals during the colonial period, juxtaposing personal sorrow with the state of the nation, symbolized by the ancient capital. The concluding stanza's faint hope for rejuvenation offers a glimpse into the complex emotions of the era.

浿城 갓든 길에
						申不出
흐르는 몸이라서 마음마저 흐르는가,
일월이 날 속이고 저 홀노 가는 것을,
내 무덤 어듸라기에 이리차저 헤매노.

무덤길 주름잡는 세월 흘너 봄이 오니,
청춘이 병들어서 꼿 피는 봄 보기 실타,
아이야 낙화보다도 봄을 먼저 쓰르렴.

오는 봄 가신 님이 나만 홀노 슯흘소냐,
90春光 엿흔 노래 흥겨울 이 멧멧인고,
녯터에 가마귀 울며 뒤 山으로 나느니.

牡丹峰 놉히 올나 봄바람을 안어드려,
대동강 물을 풀고 綾羅島에 새옷 닙허,
永明寺 쇠북으로다 겨을 꿈을 깨과저.
-(32, 3) ＜38＞
