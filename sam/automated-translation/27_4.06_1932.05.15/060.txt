기사형태	논설 (Editorial/Argumentative Essay)

Three Elements of the National Economic Movement
						By Sŏl T'aehŭi

Given our current circumstances, rather than indulging in aspirations or ideals, the urgent task is first to escape the immediate line of death. Among such movements, the economic movement, indispensable for sustaining life, is the foremost and most pressing task. The reason we initiated the Native Products Movement (土産運動) in ordinary times was, of course, because we considered it a crucial measure for this purpose. However, until now, that movement has merely served as a propaganda organ, and not a single practical project has yet been accomplished. Even if, in the future, under the principle of self-sufficiency (自作自給), industries centered on Koreans—especially various manufacturing industries—were to spring up everywhere, under the current economic system of free market economics, that is, capitalism, even if there comes a time when all industries develop in a state of the strong devouring the weak (强食弱肉的 상태), this would not be an industry based on peace conforming to the great trend of the world. Furthermore, with the current financial power and intellectual capacity of Koreans, there is absolutely no advantageous method (利法) to compete and win against imported goods from overseas. Therefore, we must examine the opportune moment (機宜). The locus of that opportune moment is the rural villages, the greatest source of Korean industry. To speak of industry while forgetting the rural villages, especially for Koreans, would be a delusion akin to seeking fish from a tree (緣木求魚). Then, what indeed is the plan for the revival of rural villages? Originally, means and methods are not just one or two, but it can be asserted that the most multifaceted and dynamic approach would be the organization of the following three types of institutions first: 1. Organization of production cooperatives at the *myŏn* (township) level throughout all of Korea. 2. Organization of one village financial cooperative (鄕村金融組合) per two combined *myŏn*. 3. Organization of one store at the *tong* (village) or *ri* (hamlet) level. Now, to briefly explain their organization and management methods:

First, regarding the production cooperative, although there are general precedents, if we take one *myŏn* as the unit, it does not mean that all means of production—materials unique to that *myŏn* or raw materials purchased from elsewhere to make processed goods—are handled solely by one cooperative office. Rather, it is an organization intended for the joint use of all methods, such as standardizing product quality and price for sales, joint machinery and equipment, and all other means. The profit and loss calculation will inevitably have to be based on the number of investment shares (出資口數) according to the current system, but it is most appropriate to operate with the spirit of ensuring that all residents within the *myŏn* benefit equally (均被其益). The organization of the Village Financial Cooperative will also likely have a standard profit and loss based on investment shares according to the current situation, but this must be operated as a semi-sacrificial (半犠牲的) profit-making venture out of the responsibility of the propertied class (有産階級) in the villages. Since this cooperative is an institution responsible for backing the circulating funds of the production cooperatives and the *tong*/*ri* stores within its jurisdiction, and engaging in remittance business (爲替營業) between cities and villages, both internal and external, it will naturally require permission from the authorities for its initial establishment and will need to establish connections with various banks and semi-official financial cooperatives. The organization of the *tong* or *ri* store must absolutely be based on the equal standing (均勢) of the residents. Therefore, investment should be uniform per household, whether it be 10 won or 20 won. For the portion of the poor, it would be good for the propertied to provide loans upfront (先貸), to be settled later from future profits. Thus, households added later will gradually join, and the share rights (股權) related to moving in or out will also be slightly different from the usual income-based gains and losses of the current system, rather granting a benefit-sharing aspect (亨受的 利樣) equally distributed among resident households, much like a village company (鄕村會社). The profit potential of this *tong* or *ri* store is clearly immense, needing no further explanation. Generally, the *tong*/*ri* store avoids the losses incurred through 5 or 6 intermediaries (middlemen and brokers) in both purchasing and selling, thereby becoming an institution that seeks the profit of its own *tong*/*ri*, i.e., the profit of the consumers themselves. For example, it arranges direct transport (直輸移入) for purchases from the manufacturing source and direct outward transport (輸移出) to cities for sales, becoming a vital engine (活機關) for enriching the *tong*/*ri*. In that it handles the consumption of an entire *tong*/*ri* single-handedly, it resembles a consumer cooperative or purchasing cooperative. In that it handles the sales of an entire *tong*/*ri*'s products single-handedly, it resembles a production cooperative. In that share rights are utilized for this, it resembles a credit cooperative. In that it awakens people to general market prices, it becomes an institution that instills important economic concepts through practice. In that it resists the absorptive power of the cities, it becomes the foundation (張本) for the urbanization of rural villages. In that the interests of a village are shared, there are signs (兆朕) that it will promote the moralization of community psychology (部落社會心理). In that it purchases only what is needed based on the village's interests and refrains from purchasing disadvantageous items, it has the power of rural customs control (농촌통관). Furthermore, it is certainly worth proclaiming (貢言) that it will become a favorable institution serving directly and indirectly as an introduction center for rural culture. Moreover, if such an institution naturally encourages household side-businesses (부업장려) in farming families, thereby preventing the departure of farmers from villages (農民離村) and checking the harm of increasing urban laborers, its effect in promoting peace by shortening this turbulent transitional period (動搖的 過渡期) will be immensely great.

However, realizing this requires expecting the spontaneous initiative of each rural sibling, but given the current situation, the cooperative efforts of our general brethren who possess a considerable sense of responsibility—be it business-related, national, or social—are even more necessary. To achieve this, there must also be an organization. For this, how about launching a Promotion Society (獎産社) as a foundation (財團)? For instance, with a plan to organize all of Korea, divided into four stages: In the first stage, select 50 counties (*kun*). Encourage (督勵) the organization of production cooperatives in the approximately 600 *myŏn* under the jurisdiction of these 50 counties, establishing 600 locations. If village financial cooperatives are established in this area at a rate of 300 locations, then 200 personnel must be trained to take on the roles of managers or directors to oversee the operations after establishment, following encouragement (慫慂) for their founding. The remuneration for these personnel should be borne by the Promotion Society for at least the first two years after establishment. The Promotion Society will bear this responsibility, and on the other hand, it will establish branch offices in key hubs (要樞) nationwide and engage in commissioned brokerage for the purchases and sales of the central production cooperatives and central *tong*/*ri* stores. As there should be considerable income from this engagement, it will be sufficient to cover the total burden of expenditures.

To establish the Promotion Society with such management, it is thought best to set the total capital at a minimum of one million won, divide this into four stages, and devise a plan to raise 250,000 won in the first stage. It is earnestly hoped that the propertied class and the intellectual class, with sincere devotion (衷情), will agree with this humble opinion (管見) and initiate the establishment of the Promotion Society. I am convinced that the realization of this plan is almost the only strategy (謀猶) for the Korean nation to escape the crisis. I implore the members of our brethren, the propertied and intellectual classes: for the sake of our ancestors, for the present, for our descendants, to avoid becoming the scapegoat (干板) for all curses, and for the highest honor, you must show sincerity in this one endeavor by making a bold investment (黃金一擲). Ability is not precious if not used well, and money, if not spent well, can instead become a source of calamity (禍端). Please deeply contemplate the principle (理諦) that the opportunity to spend well and achieve good lies in this single matter. <12>

**Summary**

The author, Sŏl T'aehŭi, argues that Korea's immediate priority is economic survival, surpassing mere ideals. He critiques the ineffectiveness of the Native Products Movement and the harsh realities of capitalism (free competition favoring the strong). He proposes a concrete, three-tiered cooperative system focused on rural revitalization as the key to national economic strength: *myŏn*-level production cooperatives, two-*myŏn*-level village financial cooperatives (run semi-altruistically by the local elite), and *tong*/*ri*-level stores acting as combined purchasing, sales, and credit unions. This system aims for self-sufficiency, direct producer-consumer links, community benefit, instilling economic awareness, strengthening rural areas against urban drain, and fostering community spirit. He calls for the creation of a national foundation, the "Promotion Society" (獎産社), funded by the wealthy and educated elite (initially requiring 1 million won, raised in stages), to organize, fund the initial management of, and coordinate these cooperatives nationwide. Historically, the article is interesting as it reflects a form of grassroots economic nationalism during the colonial period, seeking self-reliance outside the dominant colonial economic structure. It blends cooperative ideals with a pragmatic reliance on the existing social hierarchy (propertied class leadership) and offers a detailed organizational blueprint, revealing contemporary concerns about rural impoverishment, capitalist exploitation, and the desire for national economic autonomy, albeit framed within the constraints of the time. Historians might find the specific cooperative models, the critique of capitalism alongside a proposed alternative structure, and the direct appeal to the Korean elite for funding and leadership particularly noteworthy.
