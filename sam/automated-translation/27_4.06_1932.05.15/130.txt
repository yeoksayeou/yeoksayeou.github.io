手標稿畔吟
						파인

Buyŏ
The desire to visit Buyŏ is especially keen this season. I wish I could climb Panwŏlsŏng Fortress on the banks of the Paengma River, where the spring grass grows thick, gaze out at the mountains and rivers of Paekche stretching far north and south, whistle a long tune, and then come down.
My longing for the old capitals has become especially unbearable lately. What meaning could there be in thousand-year-old stones or hundred-year-old tree roots? Yet, the empty feeling of not having a new city of culture built by our own hands forces me to seek out even the ancient capitals.
I visited P'yŏngyang last year, I think. Wandering through Ch'ilsŏngmun Gate, Kija's Tomb, Nŭngna Island, and Ŭlmil Pavilion, I felt as sorrowful and desolate as if I had come to the grave of a past lover. I wanted to dig up all the sand on the banks of the Taedong River with my ten fingers and weep. If I had long hair like a Koguryŏ boy, I would have wanted to wrap it tightly around the pillars of pavilions like Yŏngwang Pavilion and Pubyŏk Pavilion and thrash about wildly. I only had 12 hours to wander due to lack of time, but I have never felt so stirred up as I did then. Even the flight of a single dove that had nestled in Taedongmun Gate was a marvel to me, and the one or two scattered fragments of Nangnang roof tiles by the river filled me with endless attachment.
Since then, after seeing Sŏnjuk Bridge in Kaesŏng and Ch'oksŏng Pavilion in Poju (Chinju), my love for the ancient capitals has become even more relentless. And when spring and autumn arrive, this feeling becomes even more intense.
Among the ancient capitals, Buyŏ is the one I wish to visit most. Kyŏngju has been excessively 'made up' for the times with museums and historical site preservation. Somehow, encountering it feels like meeting a courtesan powdered and rouged (油紛의 妓女), and my steps falter, but Buyŏ alone seems like it would be pleasant, showing its ancient life just as it is, down to every blade of grass and tree growing carelessly on the roadside, increasing my longing all the more. Who knows if I will get to tread the soil of Buyŏ this summer?

Kim Ch'an
Kim Ch'an, the communist leader who seems to be shaking the newspapers these days, attended elementary school (普通學敎) with me. He was, I think, two grades ahead of us.
His father was a leading figure in the Ilchinhoe and served as the magistrate (郡守) of our county. Back then, the son of a magistrate was regarded with considerable awe.
During his elementary school days, Kim Ch'an was an incorrigible troublemaker (作亂). He often hit classmates like me, was good at imitating the teacher, and excelled at sports.
Among the 500 or so students, Kim Ch'an was the only one who wore Western clothes and shoes. When he came wearing a new Western suit, he would play jump-over and roll around, getting covered in mud the same day, and nobody knew which ditch his hat had fallen into.
Despite this, he was really smart. In subjects he put his mind to, he invariably scored 100 points. Conversely, in subjects he disliked, he couldn't even get 10 or 20 points. His strengths seemed to be mathematics and history.
He also fought frequently with other children, mostly taking it upon himself to fight for friends who were being beaten up by others. Even now, I can almost see him disappearing into a corner of the playground, nose dripping blood (콧피를 흘니며) after being hit by someone, shouting something.

Komusō (虛無僧)
On a dark night, walking along the wall of Kyŏngbok Palace, I reached the vicinity of Kwanghwamun Gate when I saw a Japanese man and woman wearing large hats made of straw, sitting by the stone wall and playing bamboo flutes (퉁소). They were likely a pair of komusō (虛無僧), itinerant monks, who for some reason had renounced the world and were living a solitary, wandering life.
Unseen by them, I sat behind them and, until late at night, listened to the mournful, intermittent sounds of their flutes, thinking about many things.
Then it suddenly occurred to me: in the 30 or 40 years of my own future life, might there not be a scene where I myself become that man, and my wife becomes that woman, seeking out a stone wall on a deep night, becoming wandering people playing the flute? Whether this will be after I turn 30, or after 40, or perhaps when I am near death at 60 or 70, I cannot know the time. Nor do I know if it will last for a month or continue for a year, but it seems likely that such a time will surely come.<20>

**Summary**

The author, "P'ain," expresses a deep longing to visit the ancient Korean capital of Buyŏ, contrasting its perceived natural state with the "made-up" restoration of Kyŏngju. He recalls a moving visit to P'yŏngyang and other old capitals, feeling a profound connection to the past amidst a sense of contemporary emptiness. He then reminisces about Kim Ch'an, a childhood acquaintance who became a prominent communist leader, describing his rebellious yet intelligent nature and complex family background (father in the pro-Japanese Ilchinhoe). Finally, the author reflects on encountering a pair of Japanese komusō (itinerant monks) playing flutes late at night, pondering the possibility of a similar wandering, melancholic fate in his own future.

For historians, the article offers insights into the Korean intellectual sentiment during the colonial period, particularly the yearning for a lost national past and cultural identity represented by the ancient capitals. The description of Kim Ch'an provides a personal glimpse into the background of a significant figure in the early Korean communist movement and the complex social dynamics of the time, including connections between pro-Japanese collaborators and their leftist offspring. The reflection on the komusō subtly touches upon themes of displacement, world-weariness, and shared human vulnerability that might have resonated deeply in the context of colonial subjugation.

扶餘
扶餘에 가보고 십흔 생각은 이 계절에 더욱 간절하다. 봄풀이 욱어진 白馬江畔半月城에 올나 멀니 남북으로 줄줄이 버더나간 百濟山川을 바라보면서 길게 휘파람 불다가 내려왓스면 조켓다.
녯 都邑을 그리워하는 내 심정은 근래에 와서 더욱 참울 길이 업다. 천년 묵은 돌 백년 묵은 나무리에 무슨 이 잇스리요만은 우리 손으로 건설된 문화의 새 도시를 가지지 못한 공허한 심정은 古都라도 찻지아니치 못하게 하더라.
나는 작년엔가 平壤을 가보앗다. 七星門, 箕子陵과 綾羅島, 乙密臺를 거닐면서 녯날의 애인 무덤에 온 듯 애닮고도 허금허서 열손까닭으로 대동강가에 잇는 모래란 모래를 모조리 파헤치면서 울고 시펏다. 高句麗의 童子가치 내게 긴머리이 잇섯더리면 練光亭이고 浮碧樓로 하는 樓臺의 도리 기둥에 팅감고 한바탕 몸부림 치고 십헛다. ㅏ이간이 업서서 겨우 12시간을 거닐엇지만 나는 그때와 가치 마음이 들복기워진 가 업섯다. 大同門에 깃드럿든 빌둘기 한 마리 나는 것도 내게는 驚異엇고 강가에 樂浪의 어진 기와이 한두개 흐터저 잇는 것도 업는 애착을 주엇다.
그뒤 개성의 善竹橋를 보고 普州의 촉石樓를 보면서부터 나의 古都에 대한 사랑은 더구나 칠 길이 업섯다.그리고 봄과 가을이 되면 이 생각은 더구나 간절하여젓다.
古都중에도 扶餘는 더욱 가보고 십흔 곳이엇다. 慶州는 博物館이요 古蹟保存이요 하는 시대의 粉裝을 지나치게 하엿다. 전하는 것이 엇전지 油紛의 妓女를 대하는 듯 거름이 선 듯 나서지를 안치만은 扶餘만은 길바닥에 무심히 난 一草一木까지라도 녯생명을 그냥 모여 즐것갓해서 그리운 정이 일층 더하여진다. 아지 못게라 금년 녀름에나 扶餘의 을 밟게 될는지요.

金燦
요지금 신문지상을 흔들다 십히 하는 共産黨의 首領 金燦은 나와 普通學敎를 다치 다니엇다. 그는 우리보다 두 學級이 압섯든가.
그의 아버지는 一進會의 指導級人物로서 우리 고을 郡守로 재직하고 잇섯다. 그만해도 郡守의 子弟라면 퍽으나 두럽게 보엿다.
小學校 時代의 金燦은 손을 대일 수 업는 作亂이엇다. ㅏ가튼 學友를 잘 리고 선생의 숭내를 잘내고 그리고 운동을 잘하엿다.
500여명 학생중에 洋服 입고 구두 신은 者라고는 金燦 혼자이엇는데 그는 새 洋服을 입고 나오면 건너기 나딩글기를 하기에 당일로 진흙 투성이 되고 帽子도 어느 개굴창에 쓰려백힌지를 물낫다.
그리면서도 그의 머리는 정말 조왓다. 着心만 하는 學課이면 依¾‹히 100점이다. 그대신 제 마음에 업는 學課는 10점 20점도 못어덧다. 그의 長技란 아마 수학과 역사이엇든 듯.
그리고 아해들과 잘 싸우는데 그것은 대개 남에게 어더맛는 동무를 구하느라고 代싸홈을 가르마터 함이엇다. 지금도 그가 남에게 어더마자서 콧피를 흘니며 무에라고 소리치면서 운동장 한모퉁이로 사라지는 모양이 눈압에 보이는 듯 하다.

虛無僧
어두운 밤에 景福宮 담장을 고 光化門 附近에 이르럿슬 엇든 일본인 남녀가 집흐로 만든 크다란 갓을 내리쓰고 돌각담에 안저 통소를 블고 잇섯다. 아마 무슨 사정으로 세상을 버리고 외로히 漂泊生活을 하는 한쌍의 虛無僧이리라.
나는 그네들이 모르게 그 동뒤에 안저서 밤이 깁도록 엇다 이엇다하는 그 구슬픈 퉁소소리를 드르면서 여러 가지를 생각하여 보앗다.
그러다가 문득 나의 압날의 3,40년 生涯중에도 내 자신이 저 사내가 되고 내 안해가 저 여자가 되어서 깁흔 밤 돌각담을 차저 퉁소부는 漂浪人의 斷面이 잇지 안을가. 그것이 30이 지나 잇슬른지 40이 지나 잇슬는지 죽을 임박인 60, 70에 잇슬는지 그 시기는 아지 못하겟느나  그것이 한달이나 게속할는지 1년을 繼續할는지 몰라도 반드시 그런 時代가 잇슬 듯 생각된다.＜20＞
