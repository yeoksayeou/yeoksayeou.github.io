Let's Go to the Lakeside
						Sin Sŏkchŏng

Let's go to the lakeside
Mother—
The day is quite hazy
On such a night, in the distant woods, in a nest lined with moonlight
A mountain bird's blue dream blossoms,
And in the wind-calm lake, small twinkling stars
Will make a long journey.

Mother—
On the blades of grass, tears of meaningless dew form,
And the distant stars are submerged in deep whispers.
—Only on such hazy nights by the lakeside,
Do fairies in white mourning clothes descend to dance—
Didn't you tell me that story once, Mother?

Mother— Hurry, let's go out to the lakeside
And there, in the brightly shining reed field,
Won't you let me watch the dance gathering
Of the fairies in white mourning clothes on a blue moonlit night?

Do You Know That Distant Land?
Mother—
Do you know that distant land?

Circling around the deep forest belt,
On the tranquil lake's white fire path, wild** berries (野**) redden,
Where fawns run freely in the distance,
That distant land where no one lives, do you know it?
When you go to that land, please don't forget,
Let's raise doves together in that land.

Mother—
Do you know that distant land?

Gently descending the mountain slope,
In the sunny spot, a white goat leisurely grazes,
In the cornfield where stalks shoot up, the sun sets,
The sound of the distant sea waves is heard mournfully,
That distant land where no one lives, do you know it?
Mother— Please don't forget,
Let's drive the young sheep home then.

Mother—
Do you know that distant land?<19>

**Summary**

These two poems by Sin Sŏkchŏng express a deep yearning for an escape to an idealized, peaceful, and untouched natural world, often presented as a "distant land" or a mystical lakeside. The speaker addresses their "Mother," evoking a sense of nostalgia, comfort, and shared longing for this utopia, free from the intrusions of reality. From a historical perspective, the poems, written during the Japanese colonial period (1932), are significant. Historians might find the strong escapist theme and the idealization of a pristine, uninhabited natural realm reflective of the desire to find solace away from the oppressive colonial reality or perhaps a subtle assertion of a pure, untouched Korean spirit and landscape. The invocation of the mother figure could also symbolize a longing for the lost homeland or traditional security.

湖畔으로 갑시다
어머니-
날이 무척 자욱 함니다
이런밤 먼-수풀에는 달빗으로 깐 보금자리에
산새의 푸른 이 피여나고
바람 잔 湖水에는 깜박이는 적은 별이
먼- 旅行을 하겟습니다.

어머니-
풀립페는 뜻업는 이슬의 눈물이 지고
먼-별은 기픈 속새임에 잠겻습니다
-湖畔에는 이러케 자욱한 밤이래야
素服한 仙女들이 나려와 춤춘다고-
그 언젠가 어머니는 이야기 하지 안엇습니까?

어머니- 어서 저-湖畔으로 나어갑시다
그리하여 히게 빗나는 갈대바테서
푸른 달ㅅ밤 素服한 仙女들의
舞蹈會를 구경시켜 주시지 안으시렵니까?

그 먼-나라를 알으십니까?
어머니-
당신은 그 먼나라를 아르십니까?

기픈 森林帶를 고 돌면
고요한 湖水에 힌불ㅅ길에 野** 열매붉어
멀리 노루색기 마음노코 여다니는
아무도 살지안는 그 먼 그나랄를 알으십니까?
그 나라에 가실에는 부대 잇지마서요
나와 가치 그나라에서 비둘기를 키웁시다.

어머니-
당신은 그 먼나라를 알으십니까?

山비ㅅ탈 넌지시 타고 나려오면
양지바테  힌염소 한가히 풀고
길 솟는 옥수수바테 해는 점으로
먼-바다 물ㅅ소리 구슬피 들려오는
