Magazine Title: 삼천리 제13권 제7호
Publication Date: 1941년 07월 01일
Article Title: (戱曲) 放浪詩人 金삿갓(完) ((Play) The Wandering Poet Kim Sakkat (Conclusion))
Author: 宋影
Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

Part 2 (Continued from previous issue)

Kyehyang: (Receiving and putting it on) Thank you.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Kyehyang, does Lord Kim P'ansŏ still come to visit these days?
Kyehyang: As they say, "An uninvited guest comes on their own," he visits occasionally.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Hey, don't say that. No matter how old that lord is, isn't he a *yangban* (兩班, aristocrat)?
Kyehyang: So what about it?
Wŏn Saengwŏn: By all means, try to please that Excellency.
Kyehyang: Forcefully?
Wŏn Saengwŏn: If not forcefully, then how? But hey, (confidentially) maybe around now, that Excellency would listen to anything you say—he seems completely smitten.
Kyehyang: Why do you say that?
Wŏn Saengwŏn: What do you mean, why? You know perfectly well.<186>
Kyehyang: What?
Wŏn Saengwŏn: What do you mean, what—really, please grant my request (*ch'ŏng*, 請). Even though it hasn't been long since that Excellency left his official post, he probably still has influence in the Royal Court (*chojŏng*, 朝廷). So, somehow, you skillfully make a request (*ch'ŏng*) and get me an official position.
Kyehyang: Ho... ho...
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Don't just laugh, listen carefully. Truly, living only as a *Saengwŏn* (生員, holder of the lowest state examination degree) until this old age has become a deep regret (*han*, 恨). I'll offer my rice paddies, I'll offer my fields, anything will do, just plead with him to give me any official position. That lord will surely listen if it's your words.
Kyehyang: Have you finished speaking?
Wŏn Saengwŏn: I have. Only you know the reason why I visit you so often like this. Even your mother probably thinks I come here because I adore you. But my single-minded devotion (*ilp'yŏndansim*, 一片丹心) is not for you, but for my official position. Kyehyang, please grant my request (*ch'ŏng*).
Kyehyang: (Takes off the ring and throws it. In a sharp voice) Tch, you pathetic thing.
Kim Sakkat looks in from outside the fence and smiles faintly.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: What?
Kyehyang: What do you mean, what? You can't even think of succeeding with your own talent (*chaejyo*, 才操) and strength, but seek advancement (*palsin*, 發身) by borrowing the power of a mere *kisaeng* (妓生, female entertainer)—tsk! Hey, are you even a real man (*sanae taejangbu*, 사내大丈夫)?
Kim: (Loudly) When afflicted with the madness for office, one would bow not just to a *kisaeng*, but even to a puppy. Why make a fuss?
The two turn around, startled.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Feeling ashamed amidst his anger.
Kyehyang: Looks at him strangely.
Kim: Heh...
Kyehyang: Who are you?
Kim: Someone looking for a drink because I'm hungry. (Enters.)
Kyehyang: Why are you coming in?
Kim: I won't ask you to get me an official post, so don't worry. Just warm up a cup of liquor for me, please. And a bowl of rice instead of side dishes—oh, I'm so hungry. (Sits down on the edge of the wooden floor, *maru*.)
Wŏn Saengwŏn: What kind of fellow is this?
Kim: A fellow who wanders around begging for food. Heh... What kind of official post is obtained by asking a *kisaeng*? It must be a *Pyŏlgam* (別監, low-ranking official) or a *Saryŏng* (使令, servant) who frequents the *kisaeng* house. If you're going to frequent such places anyway, why bother with an official title? Heh...
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Aren't you going to leave?
Kim: If you tell me to leave, I'll leave, but I'll shout it out as I go. I'll wander through the whole village shouting exactly what I just saw. (Starts to leave.)
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Hey!
Kim: Why are you calling?<187>
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Since you've come anyway, have a drink before you go.
Kim: *Kosowŏn pulgamch'ŏng* (姑所願不敢請, I wished for it, but dared not ask). (Sits down again.)
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Kyehyang, please prepare a table quickly, however you can.
Kyehyang: What, do you think this house is a place that serves beggars?
Kim: It must be a house that wears *sakkat* (삿갓, conical bamboo hat).
Kyehyang: What did you say?
Kim: Originally, *kisaeng* gain fame the better they are at putting *sakkat* on spineless men. But hey, miss, I came already wearing a *sakkat*, so what other *sakkat* would you put on me?
Kyehyang: He really seems like a madman.
Kim: Heh... Perhaps a madman only appears in the eyes of a madwoman. Sir Saengwŏn, isn't that so?
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Please, that's enough.
Kyehyang: Oh dear. (Cannot bear the indignation.)
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Stop it. (Winks.)
Kim: Sir Saengwŏn, did dust get in your eye? Why do you keep blinking so often?
Kyehyang: He really must be mad.
Kim: *Kakp'a nan tongjo / Hwanwi ilsŏk ch'in* (却把難同調 / 還爲一席親, Though hard to harmonize at first / We become close sitting together). Heh...
Kyehyang: (Looks somewhat taken aback.)
Owŏl: Miss, has someone arrived?
Kim: A guest has arrived.
Owŏl: Who is that?
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Watch your words.
Owŏl: Yes.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Hurry inside and prepare a medicinal liquor table (*yakchu-sang*, 藥酒床). (Winks.)
Owŏl: Sŏ Chinsa hasn't arrived yet.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Hurry, this guest seems to be in a rush.
Kim: I'm not in that much of a hurry.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Hurry.
Owŏl: Yes... (Goes inside.)
Kyehyang: (Gradually taking an interest in Kim) Who are you, really?
Kim: (Recites)
*Chusiŏn kyo siŭn* / 酒仙交市隱 (A drinking immortal mingling in the hidden market)
*Yŏhyŏp si munin* / 女俠是文人 (A female knight-errant is a literary person)
Kyehyang: (Guesses he is no ordinary person.) You recite poetry well.
Kim: It's borrowed scenery and moonlight (*p'ungwŏl*, 風月).
Owŏl brings out a poorly set table, glances repeatedly, and exits.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Here, let's have a drink.
Kim: Let's eat.
Kyehyang: (Curiously sits to one side and pours the liquor.)
Kim: I'll drink first.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Please, drink a lot.<188>
Kim: Well, the moon should be bright tonight. (Drinks.)
Kyehyang's Mother enters, bringing Sŏ Chinsa and Mun Ch'ŏmji.
Kyehyang's Mother: (Making a fuss, feigning innocence) Please come in. Sir Saengwŏn, guests are arriving.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Welcome, welcome. We've been waiting.
Sŏ Chinsa: Sir Saengwŏn, you look well.
Mun Ch'ŏmji: Thank you again for inviting us. Heh...
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Now, stop teasing and please come up.
Kyehyang: Please come up, everyone.
Sŏ Chinsa: Your face has brightened even more.
Mun Ch'ŏmji: It seems to be her fate to only attend Sir Saengwŏn.
Kyehyang: You're just saying that unnecessarily.
Sŏ Chinsa: Who is this gentleman?
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Uh... (Hesitates.)
Kyehyang's Mother: Indeed, who is he? Dressed exactly like a secret royal inspector (*ŏsa*, 御使). Sir Saengwŏn, who is it?
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Uh—he is a distant relative of mine.
Kim: Yes, although my clothes are like this, my family rank (*hangnyŏl*, 行列) is high, making me this Wŏn Saengwŏn's paternal uncle's cousin's peer.
Kyehyang's Mother: Oh, is that so? Oh my, then what should I do? Your Excellency, I am so sorry. I spoke carelessly.
Kim: It's alright. You surely weren't talking about me. You were talking about my shabby clothes. Heh...
Kyehyang smiles faintly, developing more goodwill towards Kim.
Sŏ Chinsa: Is that so? I was too rude. It is my first time meeting you.
Kim: Likewise.
Sŏ Chinsa: I am Sŏ Pyŏngdŏk (徐丙德), holding the rank of Chinsa (進士, licentiate degree holder).
Kim: I am just Sakkat, living while wearing a *sakkat*.
Sŏ Chinsa: Pardon?
Mun Ch'ŏmji: That fellow is too flippant. Where is the propriety in responding to a first meeting greeting like that?
Kim: If that's the case, didn't the saying go, *No o no hayŏ igŭp inji no* (老吾老하여 以及人之老, Respect my elders, and extend it to others' elders)? Isn't your friend's uncle the same as your uncle? Yet, this is the kind of greeting you offer to an elder (*chonjang*, 尊長)?
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Please, calm down.
Kim: Are you joining them too?
Wŏn Saengwŏn: No, sir.
Mun Ch'ŏmji: I apologize.
Sŏ Chinsa: Please calm down. We were wrong.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Please stop. Sirs, let's get to know each other's ranks later.
Kim: Heh... Alright, let's drop it.
Owŏl: (Entering) Ma'am, shall I really bring out the table?
Kim: Whatever table comes out, make sure to put a bowl of rice on it.
Owŏl: Oh my, that gentleman...
Kyehyang's Mother: You wench! That gentleman is Sir Wŏn Saengwŏn's uncle.<189>
Owŏl: Oh, is that so? (Goes inside.)
Kyehyang's Mother also goes inside.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: (Smacks his lips.)
Kim: Saengwŏn, is this table a fake?
Kyehyang: It's not like that. It was brought out first as a simple offering (*ch'oryŏm*, 初廉) because you seemed hungry.
Kim: As a simple offering table, it's not too bad. For kimchi, instead of solid pieces, there's only watery brine, reflecting the white clouds floating in the empty sky. *Ch'ŏn'gwang unyŏng kong p'aehoe* (天光雲影共徘徊, Heavenly light and cloud shadows wander together). Heh...
Kyehyang's Mother and Owŏl come out carrying a large table together.
Kyehyang's Mother: It was prepared hastily (*cholji*, 卒地), so there isn't much. Please accept this small courtesy (*sorye*, 小禮) as a great courtesy (*taerye*, 大禮).
Kim: Then I'll drink from a large cup. (Turns over a fruit bowl.)
Kyehyang: (Pours liquor.)
Kyehyang's Mother: Oh my, you drink medicinal liquor well too.
Owŏl: Oh my— (Words about to come out.)
Kyehyang's Mother: You wench, don't make noise and hurry inside.
Owŏl: Hmph. (Goes inside.)
Kyehyang's Mother: You wench, are you also afflicted with the hmph-sickness (*hŭng-pyŏng*, 흥病)? (Exits.)
Kim: It's much better than being afflicted with the ruin-sickness (*mang-pyŏng*, 亡病). Now—let's each have a cup of this.
Sŏ, Mun: Oh dear, how can we drink that?
Kim: What's the big deal with just this much—here—pour.
Kyehyang: (Pours.)
Kim: Then shall I drink one more cup before passing it around? Hey, how about singing a drinking song (*kwŏnjuga*, 勸酒歌)?
Kyehyang: I will. (Recites what Kim recited earlier.)
*Kakp'a nan tongjo* / 却把難同調 (Though hard to harmonize at first)
*Hwan'yo ilsŏk ch'in* / 還如一席親 (We become like close friends sitting together)
*Chusiŏn kyo siŭn* / 酒仙交市隱 (A drinking immortal mingling in the hidden market)
*Kaeghyŏp si munin* / 客俠是文人 (A guest knight-errant is a literary person)
Kim: You just changed the character for woman (*yeoja*, 女) to guest (*kaek*, 客). Good, good. (Drinks.) (Gives to the two men) Now, it's your turn, nephews.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: How can I drink so well?
Kim: What, don't be reserved in front of your uncle. Although there's seniority (*changnyu yusŏ*, 長幼有序), isn't there also shared joy between old and young (*noso tongnak*, 老少同樂)?
Sŏ Chinsa: Please accept it. It's a cup given by an elder.
Mun Ch'ŏmji: Please accept it quickly. It's impolite not to accept.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: (Accepts reluctantly. Smacks his lips. He has a habit of smacking his lips when upset.)
Thus, one round is completed.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Since we've gathered like this, shall we each compose a regulated verse (*yul*, 律) to further enhance the mood?
Sŏ, Mun: Excellent.
Kim: Then shall I compose first?
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Will you compose?
Kim: Well...
Kyehyang: (Brings paper and brush.)<190>
Kim: Hold on. How many characters are in one line of a regulated verse?
Sŏ Chinsa: There are seven-character verses (*ch'irŏn*, 七言).
Mun Ch'ŏmji: And five-character verses (*oŏn*, 五言).
Kim: Depending on the situation, there can even be fifteen-character verses (*siboŏn*, 十五言). Heh...
(Writes.)
*Ilch'ul wŏn saeng wŏn* / 日出猿生原 (Sun rises, monkeys appear on the plain)
*Myogwa sŏ chin sa* / 猫過鼠盡死 (Cat passes, rats all die)
*Hwanghon mun ch'ŏm chi* / 黃昏文簷至 (Twilight, mosquitoes gather under the eaves)
Throws down the brush. Alright, then I'll be going first.
Kyehyang: Please stay and enjoy yourself a bit longer.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: (Glares at Kyehyang.)
Kyehyang: (Follows him down.)
Wŏn Saengwŏn gets up abruptly.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Where are you going?
Kim: To my nephew's house to spend one more night.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: What?
Kim: I'll make a round trip and then leave.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Are you really coming?
Kim: Is there truth or falsehood in going somewhere? Only the liquor table at this house might be true or false.
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Go safely.
Kim: See you again later.
Sŏ, Mun: Go safely.
Kim: Let's meet often. (To Kyehyang) Stay well.
Kyehyang: Please come again.
Kim: We'll see. (Puts on his *sakkat*. Exits reciting a poem: *Pupu a rip tŭng hŏju* / 浮浮我笠等虛舟, Floating, floating, my hat is like an empty boat.)
Sŏ Chinsa: Sir Saengwŏn, why is your elder relative wearing a *sakkat*?
Wŏn Saengwŏn: Let's stop talking about him.
Kyehyang: Let's all read how he composed that poem.
Sŏ Chinsa: Shall we? (Recites) *Myogwa sŏ chin sa* (猫過鼠盡死)... Haha... that makes sense. *Myogwa*, the cat passes, then *sŏ chin sa*, all the rats die. Yes. That's right. The cat passes, so the rats all die.
Kyehyang: *Myogwa sŏ chin sa*.
Wŏn and Mun: (Laugh loudly.)
Mun: *Sŏ chin sa*? Sŏ Chinsa!
Sŏ: What did you say?
Wŏn: Isn't Chinsa Sŏ Chinsa? And aren't all the rats dying also *sŏ chin sa* (鼠盡死, rats all die / Sŏ Chinsa)?
Sŏ: (Finally understands and throws the paper down) Ungh, what a truly senseless fellow. If it weren't for Sir Saengwŏn's presence, I would have said something.
Wŏn: Well, it's because he colored it plausibly.
Mun: Indeed, that's true. Ho... (Picks it up and reads) *Hwanghon mun yŏm chi* (黃昏蚊簷至, Twilight, mosquitoes gather under the eaves). Yes, that's right. *Hwanghon*, dusk, then *mun yŏm chi*, mosquitoes gather under the eaves (*ch'ŏmha*, 簷下).
Sŏ: (Claps his hands) Heh...
Mun: Why are you laughing?
Wŏn: Aren't mosquitoes gathering under the eaves, *mun yŏm chi* (蚊簷至), and Ch'ŏmji, *Mun Ch'ŏmji* (文僉知),<191> the same sound?
Mun: What, so you're calling me a mosquito? That fellow has truly gone senile. (Throws it down.)
Kyehyang: *Hwanghon mun yŏm chi*.
Mun: Don't read it.
Kyehyang: (Smiling) I should read the rest. (Picks it up and reads herself.)
*Ilch'ul wŏn saeng wŏn* (日出猿生原).
Wŏn: What's that?
Kyehyang: *Ilch'ul*, when the sun rises, *Wŏn saeng wŏn*, doesn't it mean the creature called monkey (*wŏnsungi*, 원숭이 / 猿) crawls out onto the plain (*tŭlp'an*, 들판 / 原)?
Mun, Sŏ: (Clap and laugh loudly.)
Wŏn: Why are you laughing?
Mun: Saengwŏn is Wŏn Saengwŏn (*Wŏn Saengwŏn*, 元生員), and a monkey crawling out is *wŏn saeng wŏn* (猿生原, monkey appears on the plain)! Heh...
Sŏ: That writing is truly妙 (妙,妙, marvelous). Heh...
Wŏn: (Snatches it and tears it up) Damn brat!
Sŏ: Who are you insulting?
Wŏn: That *sakkat*-wearing bastard (*hurejasik*, 후레자식)!
Mun: Sir, what kind of talk is this towards your elder relative?
Wŏn: What elder relative—no, where did that beggar brat come from to be my uncle?
Mun: Then why did you call him uncle earlier?
Wŏn: (Flaring up) Oh, it feels like a fireball is leaping up in my chest. Since things have come to this, I'll swallow my shame and tell you everything. That brat is a beggar brat I've never seen before (*saengmyŏn puji*, 生面不知). (Lowering voice) Actually, I wanted to get a damn official position, so I asked this Kyehyang to make a request (*ch'ŏng*) to Lord Kim P'ansŏ. But then that *sakkat*-wearing bastard (*nomŭi hurejasik*) overheard from outside the fence and came in to tease me. So, although I found it unpleasant, I thought it would be embarrassing if he went around blabbing, so I said he was someone I knew. That fellow called himself my uncle to insult me. Oh, I'm so furious! After being treated condescendingly by that brat all this time, in the end, I'm even called a monkey—
Mun: What? If I had known that, I wouldn't have let him be either. What, just a mosquito?
Sŏ: And I'm a rat—the more I think about it, the more furious I get.
Kyehyang: Why didn't that gentleman insult me?
Wŏn: You're a woman (*ajanyŏja*, 兒女子).
Kyehyang: Ho... If that gentleman had done so to me, perhaps he would have said this.
Wŏn: What?
Kyehyang: *Ŭisi Wŏl'gung kang Hang'a* (疑是月宮降姮娥, Suspected to be Chang'e descended from the Moon Palace).
Wŏn: I don't want to hear it. What makes the likes of you Chang'e (*Hang'a*, 姮娥) from the Moon Palace (*Wŏl'gung*, 月宮)?
Kyehyang: Ho...
Mun: Wŏn Saengwŏn, the more I think about it, the more furious and detestable it is.
Sŏ: It was Sir Saengwŏn's mistake to ask anyone, blinded by ambition (*kongmyŏng*, 功名).<192>
Mun: That's right. Although I only reached the rank of Ch'ŏmji (僉知) at best, I never asked others for help to climb higher.
Sŏ: Why would you ask?
Wŏn: Stop it. You're making me out to be the only bad guy. (His face flushes.)
Outside the fence, Kim P'ansŏ, wearing an official hat (*kwan*, 冠), clears his throat loudly.
The group falls silent, startled.
Kim P'ansŏ: Why is it so noisy? (Enters.)
Wŏn: No, sir. We were just feeling gloomy and sharing a drink.
Mun: We apologize.
Sŏ: (Only bows slightly and mumbles.)
Kyehyang: Please come up here. (Acts coyly, *kyot'ae*, 嬌態.)
Kim P'ansŏ: Stop acting coquettishly. (Sternly) What is this behavior? Just wasting time (*ch'ilt'ang*, 迭湯) every day— Haven't you heard the ancient sage's words that frivolous idling (*ch'ilt'ang yuyu*, 迭湯優遊) and gambling with excessive drinking (*pag'yŏk hoŭmju*, 博奕好飮酒) are the pleasures of petty men (*soin*, 小人)?
Kyehyang: (Turns away and smiles faintly.)
Wŏn: I apologize.
Kim P'ansŏ: You are not young children anymore. Aren't you people in this village known for literacy and etiquette (*yeŭi*, 禮儀)? Then, shouldn't you think of setting an example (*ŭip'yo*, 儀表) for all the common people (*paeksŏng*, 百姓) in everything you do, instead of acting like young profligates (*t'angja*, 湯子) every day like this—Tch, strange things abound.
Sŏ: Today, this Wŏn Saengwŏn invited us after a long time, so we were just summoned here.
Kim P'ansŏ: Whether someone invited you or not, isn't the result ultimately the same? It's not that men shouldn't enjoy themselves. However, aren't we people who should act and behave in ways that serve as a living lesson (*kyo'hun*, 敎訓) to others, no matter what? So, be careful from now on. And try to avoid excessive extravagance (*pŏnhwa*, 繁華) and dissipation (*pangt'ang*, 放湯).
Wŏn: Yes, that's right.
Mun: We will be careful from now on.
Sŏ: (Winks) Then, Your Excellency, we will take our leave.
Kim P'ansŏ: Since you were already enjoying yourselves, stay and enjoy some more. I was speaking generally.
Wŏn: No, sir. We were just about to break up (*p'a*, 罷).
Kim P'ansŏ: I'm leaving too. (Hesitates.)
Wŏn: We will leave. (Winks at Kyehyang and exits, *t'oe*, 退.)
Sŏ and Mun also bow (*kuk'kung*, 鞠躬) and exit outside the fence. (Exit, *t'oe*.)
Kim P'ansŏ: I'm leaving too, really. (Pretends to follow, then hesitates.)
Kyehyang: If you're just saying you're leaving, don't just say it, please go quickly if you intend to. Why would a young profligate (*t'angja*) care about a *kisaeng* house at twilight (*hwanghon*, 黃昏)?
Kim P'ansŏ: You impudent wench! Heh... (Sits down on the edge of the *maru*.)
Kyehyang: Ho... You only seek the pleasure of petty men (*soin*) when admonishing others; what will Your Excellency seek for yourself?
Kim P'ansŏ: Don't be impertinent. When an elder shows affection, you should behave yourself properly.<193>
Kyehyang: Ho... Why are all the respectable elders like this?
Kim P'ansŏ: What do you mean, 'all like this'?
Kyehyang: Ho...
Kim P'ansŏ: Don't laugh.
Kyehyang: Should I not even laugh?
Kim P'ansŏ: Hey Kyehyang, won't you really bear me a son?
Kyehyang: You already have grandchildren, what need is there for another son?
Kim P'ansŏ: That son is that son, and your son is your son.
Kyehyang: Please stop being senile (*nomang*, 老妄).
Kim P'ansŏ: I'll come around the third watch (*samgyŏng*, 三更). When you hear a cough outside, quietly open the door.
Kyehyang: Do as you please.
Kim P'ansŏ: Don't pretend to be asleep like last time and make me waste my trip.
Kyehyang: If I fall asleep, I wouldn't know even if heaven and earth were overturned (*ch'ŏnji kaebyŏk*, 天地開闢).
Kim P'ansŏ: Don't push it too far unnecessarily. If you provoke me needlessly, not only you, but your entire family (*ilmun*, 一門) will be utterly ruined.
Kyehyang: That's enough.
Kim P'ansŏ: (Standing up) Tormenting an old *yangban* too much is a sin (*choe*, 罪). It's a sin. Heh...
Kyehyang: Then, Your Excellency.
Kim P'ansŏ: Hmm?
Kyehyang: How will you come?
Kim P'ansŏ: What do you mean, how?
Kyehyang: If you come like this, I won't open the gate (*mun*, 門).
Kim P'ansŏ: Why?
Kyehyang: If you come just like this and are seen by the villagers (*tongnisaram*, 洞里사람), wouldn't you be embarrassed?
Kim P'ansŏ: That's true.
Kyehyang: Therefore, come in disguise (*pyŏnjang*, 變裝).
Kim P'ansŏ: How?
Kyehyang: Come wearing a *sakkat* like a fisherman (*ŏbu*, 漁夫). Then, even if you happen to meet someone on the road, no one will know who is who.
Kim P'ansŏ: That's true—Tsk, you wench!
Kyehyang: Why are you doing that again?
Kim P'ansŏ: No matter how important you are, how can a *yangban* wear a *sakkat*? It affects my dignity (*ch'emo*, 體貌).
Kyehyang: It's the middle of the night, what does it matter?
Kim P'ansŏ: Still, I don't want to.
Kyehyang: Then forget about it.
Kim P'ansŏ: (After thinking) Alright, do I really have to come wearing a *sakkat*?
Kyehyang: Only then will I open the gate.
Kim P'ansŏ: I don't know. Well, I'll come later.
Kyehyang: Yes.
Kim P'ansŏ: I should leave quickly. Lest someone sees me. (Leaving) Don't sleep, wait for me.
Kyehyang: Yes.<194>
Kim P'ansŏ: (Exits, then resumes his figure-eight walk) Ahem! (Exits stage left, *sangsu*, 上手.)
Kyehyang: (Stands blankly for a moment, looks up at the empty sky) Ho... ho...

**Summary**

This conclusion of the play "The Wandering Poet Kim Sakkat" depicts the consequences of societal corruption and the enduring sorrow of its protagonist. Act 2 shows Kim Sakkat exposing the hypocrisy of aspiring officials (Wŏn Saengwŏn, Sŏ Chinsa, Mun Ch'ŏmji) and the powerful Kim P'ansŏ at a *kisaeng* house, aided by Kyehyang's manipulation, leading to public humiliation for the elites. Act 3 jumps 20 years later, finding a weary Sakkat in the mountains. He encounters Ŭmjŏn, a woman whose family was destroyed due to defending him decades prior, and briefly meets his estranged son, Ikkyun, who vows to avenge his father's suffering caused by the rigid and unjust social system. The play critiques the ambition-fueled corruption of the ruling class and portrays the tragic, wandering life imposed upon Kim Sakkat as a consequence of challenging societal norms, ending on a note of lingering resentment and a vow for future retribution.

For historians, this play offers insight into social critique during the colonial period. While ostensibly set in the Chosŏn Dynasty, the themes of corruption among the elite, the suffering of the marginalized (Kim Sakkat, Ŭmjŏn), the misuse of power (Kim P'ansŏ), and the yearning for justice resonate with potential contemporary frustrations under Japanese rule. The portrayal of figures like Kim Sakkat, a historical poet known for his satire against the establishment, could serve as a veiled commentary on the injustices and rigid hierarchies of the colonial era itself. The emphasis on *han* (恨, deep-seated resentment) and the vow of *sŏrwŏn* (雪怨, avenging resentment) reflects a potent emotional undercurrent in Korean culture, perhaps amplified during this period.
