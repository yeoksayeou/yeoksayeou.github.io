雅號의 由來!
						閔泰瑗
牛步 閔泰瑗

10餘年 前 내가 每日申報 記者를 다닐 때 하로는 第一高普의 스켓팅 大會에 探訪을 간 일이 잇섯다. 그래서 불붓는 「運動會 뉴-스」를 어더 가지고 急히 歸社하니 正히 編輯이 끗난 뒤 30分 后엇다. 추운 때고 또 場所가 멀엇든 關係로 그리 되엇건만 겻헤 안젓든 何夢, 方台榮君 등이 「牛步的」이라고 놀니더라.

그 뒤는 機會잇는대로 牛步牛步的하고 한 두 사람씩 그 榮光스럽지 못한 別號의 使用 範圍가 날로 擴大되어 가기로 그럴 바에는 그 別號를 買收?하여 버린다고 雅量?을 보이어 結局 「牛步」라는 두 글자를 나위 雅號로 삼엇든 것이다. 雅號의 由來-이 우와 가틀 뿐.<23>

The Origin of My Pen Name!
						Min T'aewŏn
Ubo Min T'aewŏn

Over 10 years ago, when I was working as a reporter for the *Maeil Sinbo* (每日申報), one day I went to cover the skating competition at the First Higher Common School (第一高普). I gathered the hot-off-the-press "Sports Meet News" and hurried back to the company, only to arrive exactly 30 minutes after the editing had finished. It happened because it was cold and the location was far, but Ha Mong (何夢), Pang T'aeyŏng (方台榮), and others sitting nearby teased me, calling me "Ox-like" (牛步的).

After that, whenever the opportunity arose, one or two people would call me "Ubo" (牛步, Ox-step) or "Ubo-like" (牛步的), and the scope of use for that inglorious nickname expanded day by day. Since it had come to that, I showed the magnanimity(?) to "buy out"(?) the nickname, eventually adopting the two characters "Ubo" (牛步) as my pen name. The origin of my pen name – is just as described above.<23>

**Summary**

The author, Min T'aewŏn, recounts how he acquired his pen name "Ubo" (牛步), meaning "Ox-step." Over ten years prior, while working as a reporter for the *Maeil Sinbo* newspaper, he returned late from covering a skating event at the First Higher Common School due to the cold and distance. His colleagues, including Ha Mong and Pang T'aeyŏng, began teasingly calling him "Ubo-like" for his slowness. As the nickname spread, Min T'aewŏn humorously decided to embrace it, adopting "Ubo" as his official pen name. From a historical perspective, this short piece offers a glimpse into the daily life and camaraderie within a Korean newspaper office (*Maeil Sinbo*) during the colonial period. It mentions a specific colonial-era educational institution (First Higher Common School) and casually uses Japanese-influenced terms like "뉴-스" (news), reflecting the linguistic environment of the time. The self-deprecating humor about adopting the nickname provides insight into personal interactions among intellectuals/journalists.
