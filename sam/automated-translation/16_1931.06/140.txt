Outlook for the New China, the Current Plight of Korean Farmers in Manchuria, Its Causes and Damage Situation
						Kim Sam-min (金三民)

The number of Koreans in Manchuria (在滿朝鮮人) is said by some to be 800,000, by others 1,000,000, and by still others 1,300,000 or even 2,000,000, but it is difficult to determine which is the accurate figure. In the first year of Shōwa (1926), the Consulate side reported 520,000 people, the Tōyō Kyōkai (東洋協會) 740,000, the Mantetsu (滿鐵 - South Manchuria Railway) 780,000, and a survey by the Taedong Minbosa (大東民報社) reported 1,370,000. However, generally speaking, surveys in the interior (奧地), where anti-Japanese elements and bandits (馬賊) exist, are impossible for Japanese consulate-affiliated organizations to conduct. Considering the Korean relationships in the six zones mentioned above, not only from the current political ruling relationships but also from the differences in economic foundations, there is certainly a sense of being in different countries. Furthermore, it can be seen that for some time in the future, they will likely develop in different forms. It is a principle of all ages that politics follows the people, not that people exist for politics. In other words, the rulers (治者) are not necessary for the sake of the ruled (被治者); rather, rulers exist only after the ruled people are present. Persecution by Chinese Officials and Civilians

Recently, Korean farmers in Manchuria have fallen into a plight never experienced before. The return of about 15,000 people to the Korean interior and along the South Manchuria Railway line from the end of last year until late March of this year proves this dire situation. Considering the recent plight of general farmers within Korea, it would seem natural for migration from Chŏsen (朝鮮) to Manchuria to increase sharply, but the fact that people are instead returning from Manchuria to the Korean interior has significant reasons behind it. One major reason for the current hardship of Koreans in Manchuria is the persecution of Korean farmers by Chinese officials and civilians (中國官民). Their oppression of Korean farmers can be divided into the following three categories:
(1) The widespread major oppression incident across all of Manchuria from September of Shōwa 2 (1927) to January of the following year.
(2) Since Taishō 15 (1926) until today, the rampant actions of Chinese military police in various locations, using the Mitsuya Agreement (三矢協定) as a reason and under the pretext of suppressing Korean independence groups (朝鮮人獨立團取締), inspecting farmhouses, extorting money and goods, and enriching themselves.
(3) Since last autumn, county governors (縣知事) with strong anti-Japanese sentiments in places like Hŭnggyŏng (興京), Yuha (柳河), Ch'ŏngwŏn (淸源), T'ieh-ling (鐵嶺), Tunhua (敦化), Ponghwangch'ŏng (鳳凰城), etc., using military police, landlords, etc., to unconditionally oppress Korean farmers under their jurisdiction to expel them, and obstructing the leasing of land and houses. <46>
Due to these oppressions, many have had the foundations they built through years of hard work overturned from the roots, returning either to their homeland or to the South Manchuria Railway line, or moving to other zones; such cases were not few (不尠). Particularly from August last year to late March this year, the number of returnees and migrants was even greater than during the major oppression of Shōwa 2 (1927). Because this occurred partially in various regions and was not easily noticeable, there were few reports about it in the papers, and public opinion (輿論) was not aroused. However, as the following figures show, the number was extremely large, and the damage was even more severe than the Shōwa 2 incident.

2. Methods of Oppression
Chinese officials and civilians use various means to oppress Korean farmers. The main ones include:
1. Refusal to lease land and houses (土地家屋의 貸借拒絶)
2. Unfair increases in rent for tenant farming and housing (小作料家賃의 不當引上)
3. Dissolution or closure of schools (學校解散 又는 閉鎖)
4. Eviction orders (退去命令)
5. Refusal to accept rent payments for tenant farming and housing (小作料家賃의 受領拒絶)
6. Forcing naturalization or forcing the carrying of residence certificates (歸化强要 或은 僑居證明書携帶强要)
7. Unfair taxation or unreasonable fines (不當課稅 又는 無理한 罰金處分)
8. Personal extortion of money and goods by military police (軍警의 個人的 金品奪取)
9. Beatings or violent insults (歐打 或은 暴行侮辱)
10. Searches of homes or persons (家宅 又는 身體搜索)
Among the above, some are carried out directly by officials, while others are done by ordering homeowners, landlords, etc., and imposing strict punishment policies on those who do not comply, thus persecuting indirectly through homeowners and landlords. Items 1, 2, and 5 mentioned earlier are examples of this indirect oppression.
However, their methods of oppression are gradually evolving. To avoid becoming seeds for Sino-Japanese negotiations (日中交涉) as occurred with previous unconditional eviction demands (i.e., to cleverly prevent grounds for protest from the Japanese), they claim it's necessary for suppressing "troublesome Koreans" (不逞鮮人) connected with communists, or they impose unfair taxes or raise tenant farming rents under various pretexts (in the Yuha County and Ch'ŏngwŏn County areas, they impose poll taxes and other strange new taxes, making each person's burden reach more than five times the previous amount<47>; in the Hŭnggyŏng area, tenant farming rent was doubled compared to before), thus cleverly expelling them from the region.

3. Causes of Oppression Against Korean Farmers
There are various reasons for the oppression of the Korean people by Chinese officials and civilians, aligning with the views of various gentlemen:
1. Due to the yearly increase of migrating Korean farmers and the fostering of ethnic animosity.
2. Erupted due to the fervor of the Chinese people for recovering national rights (國權恢復熱).
3. Due to the increase of immigrants and migrant workers to the Three Eastern Provinces (東三省) (North).
4. Seeing Koreans as the vanguard (先鋒) of Japan's aggressive policies (侵略政策).
5. Due to the threat of communization plans (赤化計劃) and so-called "troublesome Koreans" (所謂 不逞鮮人).
6. Due to the burgeoning enthusiasm among Chinese for paddy field farming (水田熱). (Others omitted - original text)<41>

**Summary**

This 1931 article by Kim Sam-min details the severe plight of Korean farmers in Manchuria, estimating their population at around 1.5 million. It argues that recent persecution by Chinese officials and civilians, driven by factors like rising Korean migration, Chinese nationalism, economic competition (especially over paddy fields), and viewing Koreans as agents of Japanese expansionism, has surpassed previous hardships, including the major oppression of 1927. The author outlines various methods of oppression, such as eviction, exorbitant rent hikes, unfair taxes, denial of leases, school closures, and violence, noting a shift towards more subtle tactics designed to avoid direct diplomatic incidents with Japan. The article emphasizes the significant number of Koreans forced to return to Korea or relocate within Manchuria due to this pressure. For historians, this piece offers a contemporary, albeit likely biased (given the source and era, potentially reflecting Japanese colonial interests or framing), perspective on the escalating Sino-Korean tensions in Manchuria shortly before the Manchurian Incident. It highlights the complex interplay of migration, nationalism, economic factors, and geopolitical maneuvering involving Koreans, Chinese authorities, and underlying Japanese influence in the region. The specific details on methods of persecution and the attempt to quantify the resulting displacement are particularly noteworthy.

中國官民의 迫害

(1) 昭和2년 9월부터 翌年 1월까지의 全滿的 大壓迫事件.

2. 壓迫方法

1. 土地家屋의 貸借拒絶

3. 學校解散 又는 閉鎖
4. 退去命令
5. 小作料家賃의 受領拒絶
