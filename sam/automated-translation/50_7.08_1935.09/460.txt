Longing for the Beloved (and 4 other poems)
						P'ain

Longing for the Beloved

Chased, chased, chased, the waves of the vast sea come
Pushing, pushing, pushing them back out again, that strength—
That strength, though it seems about to turn back and fall a hundred times,
Is my longing for the beloved, rushing out again with a cry of "Uaah!"

Splitting, splitting, and splitting again,
Even if the rock is ground into powder,
Jade is still jade.

If large, a large light,
If small, a small light.
Who can block the beloved's light?<253>

蒼天有* (*The Blue Sky Has...)

Unable to bear this stifling heart,
I take off my hat and fling it toward the sky.
As if the sky understands,
My mind becomes light.

不勝淸怨 (Unbearable Clear Resentment)

On the willow tree,
Hang the *kŏmun'go*,
Shall I let it cry in the wind?

Letting it cry,
On a moonlit night,
Shall I listen alone?

Thinking of the departed beloved,
This autumn night, quietly,
Shall I listen to the *kŏmun'go*?<254>

My Epitaph

『Had he been just a little better looking,
He wouldn't have suffered heartbreak.
Had he possessed just a little more courage,
He might have gained the world.

Herein lies the fool 『P'ain』,
Who, failing to meet the right time,
Lived a whole life without lover or hometown...
Died after rambling senselessly alone like a mad dog.』

Please write it like that, on my gravestone when I die—<255>

**Summary**

This collection of five short poems by P'ain explores themes of intense longing for a beloved, the enduring nature of the beloved's essence, personal frustration and despair, lonely melancholy, and profound self-deprecation culminating in a bleakly humorous epitaph. The poems express raw emotion, from the relentless force of longing compared to ocean waves, to the feeling of suffocation relieved only by a gesture towards the vast sky, and the quiet sorrow of listening to a *kŏmun'go* alone while remembering a lost love. The final poem presents a harsh self-assessment, lamenting missed opportunities and a life perceived as wasted. From a historical perspective, the poems, particularly "蒼天有*" with its "stifling heart" (갑갑한 이 가슴) and the epitaph's mention of "failing to meet the right time" (때를 못맛나) and living without a "hometown" (고향), might offer historians glimpses into the personal anguish and sense of powerlessness potentially felt by Korean intellectuals under Japanese colonial rule. The intense focus on personal sorrow and failure could be interpreted as an indirect expression of broader societal frustrations and limitations of the era.

님 그리는 정

쫏겨, 쫏겨, 쪼겨서 오는 한바다 파도를
밀어, 밀어, 밀어 도로 압흐로 내보내는 그 힘
그 힘이 일백번 되도라 지려다도
다시 으아!하고 내닷는 나의 님그리는 정일네.



쪼개고 쪼개고 또쪼개여
바위를 가루만든들
玉은 옥일네라

크면 큰빗
적으면 적은빗
누가 님의 빗 막으리<253>

蒼天有*

갑갑한 이 가슴 참을 길 업서
모자 버서 하늘에 휘 둘느다
하늘이 뜻잇는 듯
마음 겁분 하여지네

不勝淸怨

버들낭게
거문고 거러
바름에 울닐가

울려서
달 밝은 밤
혼자 드를가

가신님 생각하며
이 가을 밤 고요히
거문고나 드를가<254>

『조곰만 더 잘 생겻던들
