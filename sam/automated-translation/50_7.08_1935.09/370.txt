Magazine Title: 삼천리 제7권 제8호
Publication Date: 1935년 09월 01일
Article Title: 莫斯科의 新女性과 新文化, 今昔의 모스크바를 이약이 하는 會 (New Women and New Culture of Moscow, A Meeting Discussing Moscow of Today and Yesterday)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Roundtable Discussion/Interview)

Attendees

Yi Tong-min (李東民)…Lived in the Soviet Union from the New Economic Policy era through the First and Second Five-Year Plan periods.
Kim Hae-ryong (金海龍)…Currently a university student in Moscow, a leading figure in arts research.
Ch'oe Il-sŏn (崔一鮮)…Lived in Moscow from the time of the Russo-Japanese War until the late Tsarist period, an expert on old Moscow.
Han Myŏng (韓鳴)…Frequently visited Russia from the late Tsarist period until recently, an expert on both old and new Moscow.
Moderators…Reporter A, Reporter B

Reporter A - Allow me to offer a brief word of greeting. Thank you very much for attending tonight despite your busy schedules. Well, I won't give stiff formal greetings, so please understand generously. Tonight, I hope you will speak enjoyably in soft words about Moscow, shrouded in the medieval mood of the distant past, or Moscow, pulsating with the new ideology there – freely dissecting this world-renowned distinctive metropolis on this occasion for our readers would be wonderful. Fortunately, tonight, authorities on both old and new Moscow have gathered in one place, so I would be grateful if you would speak freely without any hesitation (忌憚) to make this a meeting rich in content.
For proceeding with this, I will entrust it to Mr. B (Reporter), who is also an expert on Moscow.<210>

Reporter B - As Mr. A just mentioned, present here are those who saw Tsarist-era Moscow, those who were there until very recently, and also those who saw both the old and new Moscow, so I believe this roundtable will be most well-rounded.
First, what catches the eye initially upon arriving in Moscow is perhaps the street scenery, the people walking the streets, and all such things must be vastly different between the Tsarist era and now.
First, please tell us about the women who wander the night streets – the "women of the street."

1. The Throngs of Young Women Appearing on the Night Streets

B - I'm referring to the "women of the street" during the Tsarist era. Did such women also do things like dance with customers?
Ch'oe - What are "women of the street"? I don't really know. Perhaps Han or Kim can explain.
B - Han - How about it? Where were they most numerous?
Han - Mostly in places with grand-sounding names. For example, just like Marseilles' Street of Paradise (極樂街), Moscow also had the "Flower Tree Street" (꼿나무 거리), which was it.
Yi - I'm not sure if it means flowers or sensual pleasure (色).
Ch'oe - Street of Paradise (極樂街) is indeed profound in meaning.
Han - Isn't it just like our Chosŏn? (Laughing)
B - Perhaps "Flower Tree Street" was third-rate (第三流) as a "night street," wasn't Tverskaya (쩨루스카야) or Petrovka (뻬도로푸카) first-rate?
Ch'oe - There were various sorts in such places.
B - Didn't the "women of the night street" mostly carry yellow licenses (監札)?
Ch'oe - You mean the licenses (감찰). Perhaps they did carry them? Of course, women in the licensed quarters (遊廓) all had to have them—.
Yi - In Russian, it's called *pardak* (빠루닥크).
Kim - Just this year, I had the chance to observe a hospital that accommodates prostitutes (매음부). They treat venereal diseases, teach them work skills, and next, there's a library (라이푸라리-) to instill literary and artistic interests.
B - Around the 6th or 7th year of Shōwa (1931-1932) when I was there, there were four such places. But by the time I returned, there was only one left.
Kim - And the women there are mostly 18 or 19 years old, though occasionally there are madams (매담) aged 25 or 26.<211>

2. Scenes of Nudity (裸軆風景)

A. Men and Women in Hotel Baths

B - Moscow's hotels must have changed significantly now. The current Metro-pol (메트로포-ㄹ) Hotel is where the Ministry of Foreign Affairs (外務省) used to be; people like Karahan (카라한) lived there.
Kim - That hotel has a splendid bathhouse.
Han - There have always been many interesting stories about Moscow bathhouses from the old days, but now even married couples entering together has been forbidden.
Yi - Nowadays, *normal* (노-멜한) people enter as couples, but the baths are separate.
Ch'oe - In the old days, if you went there and just asked them to call a woman, they would bring one right away.
B - Then, women must have frequently accompanied men to such places back then?
Han - Of course. And one thing I found strange about Russian bathhouses is, why do Koreans or us East Asians cover our fronts whenever we enter a bathhouse? But Russian people don't seem to think about covering their fronts at all.
Yi - That's why when I first went and saw such a scene, I was so embarrassed... hahaha (Laughs).
Ch'oe - It seems to be the same in places like America. Perhaps Westerners are generally like that. Just letting *that thing* (그 눔) dangle...
Han - Still, the Eastern – especially our Chosŏn – customs seem better.

B. Nude Women at Bathing Beaches (海水浴場)

Yi - In Russia, such things happen frequently at bathing beaches too.
Ch'oe - What was the name of that street by the Moskva River (모스크바 江)? You know, where the bathing beach is. Many men and women swim there completely naked (흘닥 발가벗고). Then there are people who get in boats and watch the figures of those nude women (라체녀) with binoculars.
Han - But recently, it seems to have become stricter. In Moscow, people bathing mostly wear swimsuits (수영복). Those who don't wear them have become quite few. However, if you go out to a village not far away, everyone does it without wearing anything. But they do press down on their fronts. (Hahaha)
Yi - When I was right in Moscow, which summer was it... I don't even know where he is now, but Cho Tong-sŏn (趙東鮮) and I once accidentally entered a women's bathing beach <212> without knowing. We were walking along the riverbank in the suburbs, just the two of us chatting, when somehow we suddenly arrived right in front of the women's bathing beach. About ten women were lying on the white sand, completely naked, drying their bodies. But having stumbled upon it unexpectedly, we couldn't just turn around and leave, nor could we enter among them, so we just stood there, not knowing what to do. Eventually, having no choice, we walked forward, and they laughed, saying, "Aren't you ashamed to come here?" We got cursed at like that once.
Han - The style of bathing suits is gradually becoming one-piece (완, 삐-스하게), barely covering the chest and waist. In that respect, Russia seems a bit ahead in fashion.

C. Nude Painting (裸體畵) and Sculpture

Kim - Not only at bathing beaches or bathhouses, but photographs and nude paintings (라체화) also depict *that part* clearly. They don't cover it with things like grape leaves. Truly, Russian nude paintings are depicted boldly—both men and women.
Ch'oe - In the Hermitage (에루미타-쥬) in Leningrad (레닝그란드), there is a bronze statue (銅像) with an X attached. They say there are only two like it in the world.
Han - There was a Rodin (로단) piece there. That was truly remarkable. Very splendidly done.
Kim - Have you seen it?<213>
Kim - Yes, I have seen it.
Han - The two titled "Spring" and "The Kiss" weren't that large—.
Kim - And there's a considerably old painting where a man and woman are embracing each other, about to taste pleasure instantly. Even the women boldly grasp wrists... It was quite something.

3. Anniversaries and Dance

B - Don't Russians like dance? Dancing on the streets, dancing in the villages. Anyway, it's a fact that Russians greatly enjoy dance. Please share some interesting stories about that.
Yi - In the new Russia, radio and jazz (짜-즈) are particularly encouraged. They even dance on the street pavement.
Kim - That's right... radio and dance.
Yi - During events like May Day (메-데-), when one's turn in the parade was far off, previously they used to have recreational activities, but now they have dance and jazz instead.
Kim - As they march in procession, if something breaks down ahead and they can't proceed, they immediately dance right there on the street pavement among the passersby.
Han - For example, on commemorative days like May 1st and November 7th, there's a huge commotion everywhere. There are officials controlling the processions, but when somewhat high-ranking officials pass by, the halt can last not just ten or twenty minutes, but even over two hours. At such times...
Kim - At such times, they play accordions and dance.
Han - Among them, there are also those who clap their hands, form a circle (圓陣), and dance.
B - Weren't dances very popular in the old days too?
Ch'oe - Dances were quite popular... though perhaps only the so-called bourgeoisie (뿌루죠아) did them.
Han - No. Not just the bourgeoisie; the proletariat (푸로레타리아) also customarily danced when they gathered.
Kim - You mean the kind where they stomp their shoes loudly and jump up? (Hahaha)

4. Leaping Forward: New Sports

B - Next, regarding sports, in the sense of brightening life, it seems they are considerably encouraged these days. How is it?
Yi - Sports have developed considerably. However, being that kind of country, collective sports are fervent... <214> Previously, setting records was rare, but nowadays, records are pursued, and competitive matches against advanced countries are also held quite actively. This ultimately seems to be a reflection of all policies of the contemporary Soviet Union itself. Not just sports, but if they intend to leap forward in all aspects, isn't it necessary to recognize individual, what should I call it, initiative (이니시아치-푸) to some extent? This is reflected in sports, leading to much talk about breaking records or jumping so many meters. Also, recently, newspapers have started carrying many articles about sports. This might be to encourage physical education for the general public, but in any case, individual sports have also developed considerably recently.
Han - That's quite true. Newspapers mostly dedicate a large portion to sports articles. Also, recently, chess (將棋 - *Note: Likely referring to chess, though written as Shogi/Janggi*) has been particularly promoted, with international competitions being held.
Ch'oe - Sports were entirely absent during the Tsarist era, but now sports, like general transportation, have truly developed.

5. City of Art

Literature, Theater, Playhouses

B - Next, let's roughly trace the past and present of Moscow as a city of art.
What theaters existed during the Tsarist era? The Bolshoi Theatre (보리쇼-이·데아톨) and others have existed since the old days...
Ch'oe - The Maly Theatre (마-루이·데아톨) also existed since the Tsarist era.
Yi - When was the current "Revolution Theater" built? The building is truly magnificent. When I first saw that theater, I admired Russia, thinking it truly is a country of art.
B - Since Meyerhold (메-ㄹ포리드) built it, it must be post-revolution.
Ch'oe - Perhaps when I was there, the Russian arts scene had reached its peak... Thinking back now, there was Shalyapin (샤리야삔), Smirnov (스미-ㄹ놉흐), Saubinov (사우비놉흐), and in literature, it wasn't long after Tolstoy (톨스토이), Chekhov (체호흐), and Dostoevsky (도소도엡흐스키-) had passed away.
Han - Regardless, that time was indeed the peak.
Kim - The subsequent development in literature, art, theater, etc., has advanced astonishingly. The recent downfall that shocked the world's attention, like the "Gorky Incident" (고리-키-호 - *Possibly referring to events surrounding Gorky*), is perhaps a first in world literary and artistic history. Starting with world-renowned veteran writers like Gorky (고리-키), new emerging writers are being produced from factories and workplaces.<215>
Han - Furthermore, theater and ballet (바레-) developed, while movies don't seem to be as liked by Russians. It's mostly students and those who can't afford theater who watch them.
Kim - However, the film sector has also developed considerably now. There are world-famous directors like Pudovkin (푸도푸킹), and the development of talkies (토-키-) is probably world-class. But what's more surprising is the field of theater. This has likely developed more than in any other country in the world.

A. Recent Trends in Soviet Cinema

B - Regarding the recent Soviet film scene, I especially want to hear from Kim. You must have seen this year's "Film Festival" too.
Kim - Yes, I just returned after seeing it.
B - What did you see? You must have seen "Chapayev" (챠바-엡흐).
Kim - I saw "Chapayev" and also "Peasants" (쿠네스챤 - Krestyane).
B - How was "The Youth of Maksim" (막심의 청년시대)?
Kim - Yes, I saw that too. And I also watched "Jolly Fellows" (명랑한 동무들). But Russian films somehow always give the impression of being influenced by theater. Since stage actors mostly take the roles, the techniques purely for cinema seem somewhat lacking. The protagonist in this "Chapayev" was like that too. Besides that, I always watch movies, but unless real "film actors" appear, this drawback seems unavoidable. If there's a flaw, it would be this.
Yi - Nowadays in Russia, it's said that not only theaters but also movie theaters are permanently established even in any rural village.
Kim - Yes, it seems so.
B - Recently, Russia is said to be producing many humorous works and comedies (히극). How is that?
Kim - Yes, but since theater and film reflect the national character, cheerfulness is hard to find. Therefore, one could say there are no true comedies. However, it's true that recently much attention is being paid to comedy. But I believe that truly "light" (가루이) comedies like those in America will never emerge.
Yi - Therefore, the reviews for "Jolly Fellows" are not very good either.
Kim - Russians, engaging in XX [censored/revolution?] and other things, have tasted profound human suffering. Just as we East Asians, influenced by Buddhism, don't feel like we've watched a play unless we cry, Russians seem unable to appreciate theater without tasting Maxim Gorky (막심·고-리키-). This might be the agony of seeking comedy yet being unable to have it. They are producing extraordinary comedies, but they don't become comedies; <216> at best, they elicit a smile or perhaps just that.
However, cheerful comedies like "Dzhunya" (쥬니아-?) have been quite successful. Also, they are handling comedies from Dickens' (듸켄스) works and achieving considerable success.
Yi - It seems considerable effort is being put into this area in literature as well. The question of whether borrowing a cheerful mood from Dickens' works would be suitable seems to be quite debated.
"Russians are people without laughter. Of course, we also lack laughter, but it's true that laughter is scarce in your country too." I once struck up a conversation with a Russian university student. Then the student said, "You say there's no laughter, but my friend, even if we try to laugh, we can't, can we? After all, XX [censored/revolution?] has just barely ended. Haha..." I heard him say that. There's truth in those words too.
Han - Anyway, Russians are indeed a nation without laughter.

6. Seeking Moscow's Famous Places

A. Changes in the Kremlin (쿠례무린) Palace

* - If a foreigner comes to Moscow, what place would be good to show them first?
* - Han: The Kremlin (쿠레문린) and Sparrow Hills (참새의 언덕).
Yi - The Lenin Mausoleum (레-린廟) is good too.
B - Aren't many of the Tsarist-era temples (사원) destroyed now?
Ch'oe - Is the largest temple next to the Kremlin still remaining?
Han - That has been destroyed now. In its place, something called the Palace of Soviets (토-ㅁ·사베-트 - Dom Sovetov) has been constructed.
A - Please tell us about the Kremlin before XX [the revolution?].
Han - In the old days, it was like a park for Moscow citizens, open for strolling. However, there were various buildings inside... museums, detached palaces (리궁), the high court, etc. Entry to the detached palace was forbidden even in the Tsarist era, but the entire Kremlin wasn't restricted like now. Currently, the government resides inside. That is, the Central Executive Committee is there. And there are residences where Kalinin (카리닌) and Molotov (모로톱흐) live. There's also a museum displaying weapons (병긔) and such, and temples, which are shown to the public (파푸릿크 - publik?). In the old days, next to the Kremlin, there was a square where the statue of Alexander II (아렉산드2세), who liberated the serfs, stood. Beside it flowed the Moskva River, and there was also a lawn, so citizens often took walks there.
Ch'oe - I also often took walks there at night.
Han - Generally, the Kremlin has three entrance gates. The gate through which foreign envoys enter to present credentials (국서봉정), the gate entering from Red Square (적색광장) <217>, and another one, I forget its name, but there were three. They were usually kept closed, and there was a device set up so that when a car approached within a certain distance, a bell (베-ㄹ) would ring... Then a guard (守兵) would come out, order a stop... and only after checking the permit would one be allowed to enter.
A - But recently, people seem to be touring the inside of the Kremlin.
Han - That's possible with a letter of introduction from the Ministry of Foreign Affairs.
B - Not just the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, but with an introduction from VOKS (폭스?) or Intourist (인쯔-리스트), anyone can see it.

B. World-Class Museums

B - Let's leave the Kremlin and look into museums. Currently, there are many post-XX [revolution] ones like the Revolution Museum, the Anti-Religious Museum, or the Tolstoy Museum, but were there any large museums in old Moscow?
韓 - There were several famous museums in the old days too. First, the "Museum of European Fine Arts and Sculpture" (구주의 미술조각 박물관) was quite large, displaying models of art from Greece (기리샤), Rome (모-마), etc.
Besides that, in Red Square, there has been the Historical Museum since the Tsarist era. And the place that is now the Lenin Library (레-린도서관) was also a museum in the old days.
B - And on Ilyinka Street (이링가街), in front of Red Square, there's a museum of a 17th-century nobleman (귀족의 뮤-제-).
韓 - Yes, there is one there. It's quite a small museum.
金 - I saw the Museum of Oriental Art (동양박물관). There were many Japanese artworks, Chinese artworks, and Persian (페류샤) artworks. (To be continued in the next issue) <218>

**Summary**

This 1935 roundtable discussion features four Korean intellectuals sharing their experiences and observations of Moscow, contrasting the Tsarist era with the Soviet period under the Five-Year Plans. They discuss social changes, particularly regarding women ("street women," prostitution reforms), public nudity (in bathhouses, beaches), the growing popularity of dance (jazz, folk dancing), the rise of organized sports, and the state of arts and culture. The participants touch upon Moscow's famous landmarks like the Kremlin and museums, noting changes like the destruction of religious sites and restricted access to the Kremlin. They also delve into theatre, the influence of stage acting on Soviet cinema, the perceived lack of genuine comedy despite efforts, and the enduring influence of authors like Gorky.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting as it reveals Korean perspectives on the Soviet Union during Japan's colonial rule. It shows awareness of both the Tsarist past and contemporary Soviet life, including its social experiments, cultural policies (promoting sports, arts), and perceived national character (serious, lacking laughter). The discussion about nudity, changing gender roles, and the comparison between Western/Russian and Korean/Eastern customs offers insight into cultural encounters. The mention of "XX," likely referring to the revolution or politically sensitive topics, hints at self-censorship or caution even when discussing a foreign power. Historians might find the participants' backgrounds (some lived through Tsarist times, others in the NEP/Five-Year Plan era) and their specific observations on Moscow's transformation valuable.
