荊軻, 五幕歌劇
						中國 歐陽予倩(作), 梁白華(譯)
(Jing Ke, A Five-Act Opera)
                        (China, Ouyang Yuqian (Writer), Yang Baekhwa (Translator))

First Act

Characters
Ching K'o (荊軻, commonly called Ching Ch'ing 荊卿)
Ko Chŏm-ni (高漸離)
Dog Butchers (狗屠), four or five men
Serving Women (酌婦) A, B, C (3 people)
Sword Dancer Woman (舞劍女)
Yŏn General (燕將)
Retinue (騶從)

Setting: A tavern, facing diagonally onto a street. Inside, several round tables are placed, with several jars of liquor (솔) and large amounts of food and drink (酒肴) spread on the tabletops. Ching Ch'ing (卿), Ko Chŏm-ni (高漸離), and several Dog Butchers (狗屠) are sitting here and there, drinking. Serving women (酌婦) move back and forth, running errands. The curtain opens with a chorus.

Chorus: What shall we do with this sorrow!
How can we look upon the chaotic heroes! (Many drink.)
Drink and drink, yet this sorrow cannot be killed.
Let the tears of blood of a great man (大丈夫) wash this land (江山)!
Let the tears of blood of a great man wash this land!

Ching: Everyone, brothers, I cannot stand this anger! Let's have some drinks!

Ko: Ching Ch'ing's words are right. Let us drink deeply and sorrowfully (痛飮).

Ching: Ko Chŏm-ni, if you want to drink a lot, keep strumming that *ch'uk* (筑, a stringed instrument) for us.

Several Dog Butchers: Let's all drink together.

Ching: We have dog meat. We have good liquor. Why aren't we drinking quickly?

All: Cheers!

(They drink. Suddenly, the sound of voices clearing the way (呵道) is heard, and a couple of warriors appear, ordering everyone to kneel and bow. The people reluctantly obey the command. A general<275> with a large retinue (騶從) passes by the front door. After he passes, Ching K'o chases out, looks once, and returns.)

Ching: Who was that person, acting with such authority?

Ko: That was the Great General of Yŏn (燕)...

Dog Butcher A: Those fellows are going hunting again!

Ching: Those fellows know how to go hunting, but why don't they know how to go out and fight when the Chin (秦) army comes? And they only show off here, pushing the common people around! Let me go teach them some manners! (Ching K'o draws his sword and prepares to go. Ko holds him back.)

Ko: Ching Ch'ing, if you can't endure small things, you'll ruin great plans.

Ching: Speaking of those military commanders, they are like the hunting dogs and falcons (鷹犬) raised by the feudal lords (諸侯) of a nation. They live in mansions (高堂大厦), wear silk clothes (綾羅綢緞), eat fine delicacies (珍羞盛饌), have wives and concubines (妻妾) surrounding them, and the sounds of pleasure (風流ㅅ소리) fill their ears. What mind do they have left to love the country and love the people? If we common folk were to trust them, our lives would be over. Now is a time of critical danger (危急存亡之秋), yet those fellows still only go hunting. How can I possibly endure this?

Dog Butcher B: As long as those guys have troops, horses, money, and grain (兵馬錢糧), they don't care about us common people.

Dog Butcher A: Aren't the troops, horses, money, and grain all from the common people (百姓)?

Ching: Why don't we settle accounts (셈가르지) with those fellows?

Ko: That's our fault for being foolish. We should settle accounts with them—but they likely don't know how to calculate detailed accounts (細細한 셈)!

Ching: With people like that around, how can we possibly resist the invasion of that violent Chin (秦) state? Everyone, we must all rise up!

All: Right!

Ching: Aaargh! How frustrating!

All: (Chorus) Let's all rise up and resist the strong Chin (强秦). Let's all rise up and resist the strong Chin!
Gather the hero's spirit, let's drain a thousand cups of fine liquor like the sea!
(Ching K'o draws his sword and dances. Ko Chŏm-ni strums the ch'uk, and the Dog Butchers join in.)

Ching: (Singing) What's the point of talking about that insignificant strong Chin?

All: (Chorus) Hurry, let's sharpen our swords!

Ching: (Singing) What's the point of talking about those insignificant Chin soldiers?

All: (Chorus) Let's form ranks (隊伍) and advance!
If the violent ones attack, we cannot live. Let's seize today and not speak of tomorrow!<276>
(Many sing a sad song while continuously drinking liquor. Again, the sound of clearing the way (呵道) is heard.)

Ching: What corrupt official (탐관오리) is coming to ruin our mood now?

Dog Butcher A: I'll go see... It's Master Chŏn Kwang (田光先生) coming this way in a four-horse chariot (駟馬高車).

Ching: Oh! What has that old man done? Just leave him be. When he comes, let's not acknowledge him and just watch what he does.

(Many nod. Chŏn Kwang enters.)

Chŏn: (Singing) Holding onto grand ambitions in vain, only letting the years pass by.
A hero grows old. How sorrowful!
The youths of Yŏn (燕) land, their spirit is truly strong. May heaven not forsake these youths.

All: (Drinking) Cheers, everyone!

Chŏn: (Continuing to sing) I step forward to speak, asking the brave warriors to eliminate the wicked ones. Are you all well?

All: The liquor is good!

Chŏn: Are you all well?

Ching: I wondered who it was, but it's Lord Chŏn (田大人)! Are you well?

Chŏn: What are you all doing here?

Ching: Sir (先生), you said you wouldn't take office, so why are you riding in a four-horse chariot (駟馬高車) and passing by here so ostentatiously?

Chŏn: I came to meet you, Ching K'o.

Ching: Oh! Sir, you go pursue wealth and honor (富貴). What concern is that of mine? Currently, the royal court (廟堂) is full of corrupt officials (탐관오리). As for me, Ching K'o, I am a man who stands tall between heaven and earth (頂天立地). How could I possibly associate with such a crowd (무리)?

Chŏn: If you don't want to serve in office, are you planning to go die?

Ching: What do you mean by that?

Chŏn: I came this time to take you with me so we can die together.

Ching: Those words...

Chŏn: Everyone, please close the door and step aside for a moment. I have something to discuss with Ching Ch'ing.

Ko: Ching Ch'ing! Master Chŏn (田先生) is a righteous man (義士) of this world. His visit this time must surely be about something important. There's no need to be suspicious like that. Hey everyone, let's go drink over there.

All: (Chorus) Drink and drink, yet this sorrow cannot be killed.
Let the tears of blood of a great man wash this land!
(Several people close the door and exit with their cups.)

Ching: Master Chŏn, if you have something to say, please say it quickly. You only hint at things, making me anxious!<277>

Chŏn: My dear younger brother (賢弟)! It's nothing else but this: King Ying Chĕng (嬴政) of Chin (秦) is committing atrocities throughout the land, causing everyone under heaven to resent him, yet they feel powerless. However, Crown Prince Tan (太子 丹) of Yŏn (燕), though residing deep within the palace (深宮), possesses considerable ambition and seeks to eliminate the tyrant (强暴) for the sake of the world. He is recruiting brave warriors (勇士) from all directions to go assassinate the King of Chin.

Ching: Ah—

Chŏn: But despite the vastness of the world (天下之大) and the breadth of the nine provinces (九州之廣), absolutely no brave warrior has volunteered (應募). Therefore, the Grand Tutor Kuk Mu (大傅鞠武) recommended this foolish elder brother. Ah! If it were 10 years earlier, I would have undertaken (担當) this task myself! But now I am old! Though I have the will, my strength fails me (힘이 붓치니). I fear I might bungle the great task. And based on what I've seen throughout my life, there is only you. That's why I recommended you, my dear brother (賢弟), to the Crown Prince. The Crown Prince, admiring you as a hero and wise man (英賢), immediately ordered his court officials (朝臣) to prepare a four-horse chariot (駟馬高車), gold (黃金), and white jade (白璧) to come and welcome (迎接) you.

Ching: Yes. I wish to offer my single body to the world and die once. However, this body is not the kind to become the private property (私有) of Crown Prince Tan. How can you speak of selling my body for a four-horse chariot, gold, and white jade?

Chŏn: Ching Ch'ing, what kind of talk is that? Going to assassinate the King of Chin this time is to eliminate the tyrant for the sake of the world. It is not entirely for avenging Yŏn (報仇). A tyrant like the King of Chin is someone the people of the world all want to capture and kill. Eliminating the tyrant is our lifelong aspiration (素志); it is by no means the affair of the Crown Prince alone... And think about it, without Crown Prince Tan, we would have absolutely no way to get close to the King of Chin. Isn't it acceptable to borrow this opportunity to achieve our long-held goal? Hesitating (망서리고) and failing to decide due to trivial formalities (苟苟小節) is not the mark of a great man (丈夫)!

Ching: (Clenches his fist, looks at himself as if gauging his own strength)

Chŏn: Ching Ch'ing, in the past, I regarded myself too highly and made light of deeds. Now, my hair has turned white, and I have accomplished nothing! Ching Ch'ing, youth does not come again. Do not follow my example!

Ching: Sir, your words are correct! I will follow you right now, meet the Crown Prince, and discuss the plan (計校) to destroy Chin.

Chŏn: Wait. Crown Prince Tan of Yŏn entrusted the grave responsibility (重任) of assassinating the King of Chin to me, Chŏn Kwang. But because I, Chŏn Kwang, am old and unable to go, I recommended you, Ching Ch'ing. Therefore, <278> I implore you before the people of the world. If you assassinate the King of Chin, the people of the world will rise up because of this, saying that tyranny (强暴) cannot be trusted and justice (公道) is not extinguished. How could this not be an immense merit (莫大한 功德)? However, Ching Ch'ing, if you go this time, you will certainly not return alive. You and I have a life-and-death bond (生死之交). How could I let you go die alone? I want to die with you and live with you!

Ching: Master Chŏn, I understand your heart well.

Chŏn: Not only that, but earlier, when the Crown Prince saw me leaving the palace (出宮), he called me and told me not to leak (漏洩) this matter, seeming somewhat suspicious of me. I will die once to dispel his suspicion.

Ching: Oh!

Chŏn: Ching Ch'ing, when you enter the palace this time, the Crown Prince will surely have you stay in a grand mansion (高堂大厦), provide you with fine food (조흔 음식), and present beautiful women (아름다운 계집) to you. Do not change your initial resolve. Ching Ch'ing, conduct yourself with dignity (自重)!

(Chŏn Kwang commits suicide by cutting his throat (自刎). The Dog Butchers and others run out. Ching K'o embraces Chŏn Kwang.)

Ching: (Singing) Suddenly seeing Chŏn Kwang pierce his own throat,
Making this day one of unbearable sorrow.
He solidified my resolve with his death;
If I fail to kill the King of Chin, I am not human!
I take off my outer coat to cover the body;
Such righteous fervor (義烈) deeply moves people.
Bowing my body in respect, I express my sincerity;
May you and I, Sir, never be parted in life or death!

All: Why did Master Chŏn commit suicide (自刎)?

Ching: (Singing) The Master lamented his old age and lack of strength, entrusting (맷기) the heavy task to us—
The Master's death was not for private reasons (私情); it urges us to cherish our youth.
Though I, Ching K'o, may be untalented (不敏), I will borrow the Crown Prince's power to enter West Chin (西秦).
Everyone, lend your strength and be my rear shield, lest Chŏn Kwang's death be in vain! (Pours liquor) Pouring a full cup of liquor, I offer it before his spirit.
(Pours half before Chŏn Kwang, half before the others.)

Ko: Ching Ch'ing, we understand your words well. Secrecy (秘密) is paramount in matters like this; no need for many words. If you die, we will all go die together!

All: Right!

Dog Butchers: (Chorus) As for us, our bodies are separate, but our hearts are one. Let's strive for the sake of the people!<279>

Ching: (Singing) The mission (使命) is truly great.

Chorus: Life and death (生死) are light.

-(Curtain)-

Second Act

First Scene

Characters
Civil Official (文臣) A, B, C, D

Outside the Palace Gate (宮門박)

Four Civil Officials: (Speaking together) Each person sweeps the snow from their own doorstep; pay no mind to the frost on others' roofs! How about it, how about it!

Official A: Today, because Ching K'o is visiting, the Crown Prince is holding a great banquet for all the officials. We must also attend (伺候).

Official B: We are useless people!

Official C: All the learning filling our bellies is now worth less than some vulgar fellow! It's absurd!

Official D: Exactly. I heard that Ching K'o is just a pig butcher (돼지잡는 白丁).

Official A: No, a dog butcher (개 白丁)!

Official D: Pig butcher!

Official A: Dog butcher!

Official D: Pig butcher!

Official A: Dog butcher! Definitely a dog butcher!

Official B: In my view, he's neither a pig butcher nor a dog butcher, but simply a murderer (사람 죽이는 者).

Official C: Right. We will all suffer harm (害) from that man in the future!

Official A: There are many of us and only one of him. Are you saying we should fear such a thing?

Official B: If we find some way to send him to Chin (秦), he will die.

Official A: If he actually succeeds and returns, we will have no way to live.

Official B: Success isn't something easily achieved!

Official A: Never mind that fellow; let's just finish our business. Let's go attend (伺候) at the court (殿上) and go drink!

Second Scene

Characters
Crown Prince Tan (太子 丹)
Ching K'o (荊軻)
Official (臣) A, B, C, D<280>
General (將) A, B, C, D
Eunuch (太監)
Ok Hŭi (玉姬)
Dancers (舞女)
Kuk Mu (鞠武)
Palace Women (宮女)

Setting: The Palace of the Crown Prince of Yŏn (燕太子), where a grand banquet (盛宴) is laid out.
Curtain opens with soft music (細樂). Singing and dancing are in full swing on stage. The dance ends.

Tan: Ha ha ha!

Tan: My lords (卿)! I have long heard the name of Master Ching K'o (荊軻先生) but was unable to meet him. To meet Master Ching K'o so easily today is truly great fortune (萬幸) for the state. We should rightly toast this with three cups of liquor. Come, Master Ching K'o, drink!

All: Master Ching K'o is truly a nobleman from heaven (한울兩班)! Come, drink!

Tan: Master Ching K'o's great courage (勇猛) and great wisdom (智慧) should rightly have long been employed at court (朝廷) to devise benefits (福利) for the state. That Master Ching K'o has long remained in humble circumstances (알엣도리) is my, Tan's, fault. I should rightly drink three large cups as a penalty (罰酒)!

Officials A, B, C, D: That is the fault of us subjects (臣) for not recommending him (薦擧)! You should rightly punish us!

Tan: My lords are truly loyal subjects (忠臣)! Come! (Drinks liquor)

Ching: Your Highness (太子)... (Singing)
Sitting proudly in the grand hall (高堂), drinking fine liquor,
Beauties (美人) and delicacies (山珍海錯) are spread out before me.
I thank Your Highness (殿下) for the generous treatment of this subject (臣);
Thinking of Chŏn Kwang (田光), tears choke my throat!
This humble subject (小臣) wishes to eliminate the thieves for the world's sake;
It is not about avenging Yŏn's (燕) private grudge (私事怨讐).
Yŏn is in peril (危急), facing a life-or-death (存亡) crisis;
How can one man hold up a collapsing boat?
Many people must exert their strength to save it;
We must rightly advance step by step!

Tan: (Singing) Sir's words are profound (意味가 기프구야); each phrase is etched onto my heart.
Sir, eliminate the tyrant (强暴) for the people's sake.
I, the unworthy one (寡人), am not merely seeking private revenge (私事怨讐).
I hope you drink the liquor poured full in the cup. I pray that your great ambition (큰 뜻) is achieved swiftly (하로밧비)!
(Speaking) Alright, let's stop talking about state affairs (國事) and have some drinks. Eunuch (內侍), go call Ok Hŭi (玉姬) to come pour liquor for the Master.<281>

Eunuch: Yes! An order from the Eastern Palace (東宮)! Ok Hŭi is summoned!

Ok: (From offstage) Yes! (Singing) Hearing the royal command (傳旨), I hastily ascend to the hall (殿上).
Moving my steps before the jade stairs (玉階),
I look from the side of the hall (殿).
What brave warrior (壯士) sits side-by-side
Like a king (王)?
Adjusting my attire (옷매무새),
I step forward to pay respects (拜謁),
Wishing you long life and boundless health (萬壽無疆).

Tan: Ok Hŭi, greet Master Ching K'o!

Ok: Master Ching K'o, are you well?

Tan: Her name is Ok Hŭi.

Ching: Ok Hŭi! Ha ha ha, truly beautiful!

Tan: Ok Hŭi, bring your zither (거문고) and play a tune to accompany Master Ching's drinking.

Ok: Yes. (A Palace Woman brings a zither. Ok Hŭi plays.) Ya!
(Singing)
Strumming the strings with my hand, I turn my head to look at Ching K'o; Heroic spirit (英氣) fills his brow.
Alas! The world is now descending into chaos; if the tyrant (强暴) is not eliminated, the brave warrior (壯士) will lose face.
Sorrowful is this body of mine, confined in the deep palace (深宮), unseen by anyone, unseen by anyone.
Year after year, the beautiful spring was merely wasted,
How could I so easily get to see the face of a hero? The face of a hero of this age (著世)?
Meeting him without difficulty, my settled heart stirs
like ripples on water!
I ask, who in this world, like me, would step forward in national crisis (國難)
And command the winds and clouds (風雲) in an instant (指顧間)?
Within my box (匣) lies a pair of treasured swords (雙股寶劍);
With this treasured sword (寶劍), I shall perform a dance
To stir the heart of the brave warrior!
(Performs a sword dance. Ching K'o also draws his sword and dances opposite her. Ching K'o, drunk, cannot support himself. Ok Hŭi supports him.)

Tan: Truly妙絶千古 (unrivaled through the ages)! Attendants, escort the Master to the Palace.
(Palace Women and Eunuchs turn and help Ching K'o exit. Officials A, B, C, D and Generals A, B, C, D smile strangely and exit to both sides.)

-Curtain- (To be continued next issue)<282>

**Summary**

This text presents the first two acts of a five-act opera titled "Jing Ke," translated into Korean in 1935. Act 1 introduces the protagonist Ching K'o (Jing Ke), a patriot frustrated with the inaction and corruption of Yŏn (Yan) officials in the face of the rising threat from Chin (Qin). Along with his friend Ko Chŏm-ni and some commoners (dog butchers), he laments the state's condition. The elderly righteous official Chŏn Kwang (Tian Guang) arrives, reveals Crown Prince Tan's plan to assassinate the King of Chin, and recommends Ching K'o for the mission. To prove his loyalty and urge Ching K'o, Chŏn Kwang commits suicide. Act 2 shows Ching K'o being welcomed at the Yŏn palace with a lavish banquet, where he meets the scheming court officials and the beautiful courtesan Ok Hŭi, who performs for him. Ching K'o reiterates his mission is for the world, not just Yŏn's revenge, while the Crown Prince lavishes attention on him.

From a colonial history perspective, the translation and publication of this Chinese story in 1935 Korea are noteworthy. The themes of resisting a powerful, expansionist state (Chin/Qin) and the internal decay hindering effective resistance (corrupt Yŏn officials) could easily be interpreted allegorically by Korean readers under Japanese rule. The emphasis on heroic sacrifice (Chŏn Kwang, Ching K'o) for a greater cause, transcending personal or even nationalistic revenge (as Ching K'o claims), might have resonated with nationalist sentiments or revolutionary ideals prevalent during the period. Historians might find the choice of this particular story, its specific translation nuances (preserving older Korean forms), and its reception among contemporary readers of *Samch'ŏlli* magazine interesting for understanding cultural production and coded political expression under colonialism.
