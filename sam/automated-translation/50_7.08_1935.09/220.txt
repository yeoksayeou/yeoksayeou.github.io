歌謠四篇
						파인

담바꾸야 (Oh, Tobacco)

Oh ya, oh ya, tobacco ya
Flaring, burning tobacco ya
When I take a puff of you
The narrow world seems broader
My stifled heart bursts open
No less than clear winds and bright moon
My friend in this world
Tobacco ya, it is you

Oh ya, oh ya, tobacco ya
Flaring, burning tobacco ya
If not for you, who is there
To soothe a man’s heart?
Pressed by money, pressed by power
Everywhere is desolate
My friend in this world
Tobacco ya, it is you <116>

Oh ya, oh ya, tobacco ya
Flaring, burning tobacco ya
You burn and become a cloud
Vanishing into the blue heavens
I burn and become the wind
Throughout the peninsula’s rivers and mountains (반도강산)
I will seek the unfortunate heroes
I will seek the unfortunate men

半月城 (Panwŏlsŏng Fortress)

Below the ruined ancient fortress site of Panwŏlsŏng (半月城)
A single wild chrysanthemum bloomed alone
Then scattered in the frosty autumn wind
Was the thousand-year dream (千年의 ) so fleeting?

The flock of water birds by Anapji Pond (雁鴨池 蓮못가)
Having lost their home, wander crying through this cold night
Beneath the moonlight shining on the old capital
Shall I too, follow the water birds, weeping through the night?

Lying in the field overgrown with autumn grass
I close my eyes dreaming of the old palace
The chime of wind-bells, *deng-geureong*, again *deng-geureong*
Echoes from the distant sky as if it were yesterday

漢陽學生歌 (Hanyang Student Song)

On the asphalt streets of Hanyang (漢陽城) castle town
Rosy-cheeked youths (紅顔少年) in uniforms (制服) and caps (制帽)
In their hands, Carlyle’s (카이랄) *On Heroes, Hero-Worship*
On their lips, Byron (빠이론) and Tu Tzu-mei’s (杜子美) poetry <117>

We are the talents (秀才) from the Thirteen Provinces
Gathered in Hanyang (漢陽城) castle town to cultivate our aspirations
Entering through the East Great Gate (東大門) to pursue our studies
We are the workers who will go out the South Great Gate (南大門) to labor

Seoul is a fine place, a garden of dreams ()
When flowers bloom in early spring, we are romantics (로맨티스트)
On autumn nights when wild geese cry, we are sentimentalists (센치멘스트)
Youth grows up amidst this

By the Mediterranean Sea (池中海), at Heidelberg (하이델벨히)
There, the youth of Prussia (푸로시아) play
Below the clouds of Paegundae (白雲臺) peak, in Hanyang (漢陽城) castle town
We, the very flower of flowers of this nation (이 겨레의 꼿에꼿), play

放浪歌 (Song of Wandering)

Shall I go to the sea? I long for the sweet brier roses (해당화)
Shall I return to the mountains? I’ll miss the seagulls (갈메기)
When night falls, I'll weave a light dream (여튼 꿈) at the Kuksadang shrine (國師堂)
When day breaks, shall I just sing upon the lawn (잔듸우에)?
This body dislikes flattering gold (黃金) and power (權勢)
Becoming a bird of freedom, I drift east, west, south, north

If I wish to get drunk, in the wide fields of the Tuman River (두만강)
I stop my raft and seek out a tavern girl (주막접)
If I wish to dance, on the southern isle of Chejudo (제주도)
I grab the sleeve of a camellia-oil maiden

As time flows and flows (흘너흘너) and white hair flies (白髮이 날닐제)
Shall I seek my old hometown (녯고향) and let out a sigh?
But for a wandering body, can there be a hometown (고향인들 잇스랴)?
There go the white clouds, let's go quickly <118>
<116-118>

**Summary**

This article presents four poems by P'ain (Kim Tong-hwan). "Oh, Tobacco" personifies tobacco as a comforting companion against worldly hardships. "Panwŏlsŏng Fortress" laments the lost glory of the Silla kingdom, contrasting past dreams with present desolation at the historic site near Kyŏngju. "Hanyang Student Song" portrays the modern Korean student in Seoul (Hanyang), educated in both Eastern and Western classics, embodying national hope and youthful energy. "Song of Wandering" expresses a yearning for freedom from societal constraints like wealth and power, celebrating a rootless, wandering existence across the Korean landscape.

For historians, these poems offer glimpses into the Korean psyche during the colonial period. "Oh, Tobacco" reflects a sense of individual struggle and the need for solace. "Panwŏlsŏng Fortress" touches upon nationalist sentiment through mourning a lost past. "Hanyang Student Song" is particularly interesting for its depiction of modern education blending Western influences (Carlyle, Byron, Heidelberg, Prussia) with Korean identity (Hanyang, Thirteen Provinces, Paegundae) and aspirations, suggesting the complex cultural landscape and emerging elite of the time. "Song of Wandering" hints at alienation and a desire for escape, potentially reflecting unease with the political or social realities. The use of older Korean spellings and vocabulary is also linguistically significant.
