Article Title: 我觀 「蔣介石, 간듸, 트로츠키」, 이 세 人物은 이제는 過去史上의 人物이 되엇는가, 또는 再起가 기대되는가? (My View on "Chiang Kai-shek, Gandhi, Trotsky": Have these three figures become figures of past history, or is a comeback expected?)
Article Type: 설문 (Survey/Questionnaire)

My View on "Chiang Kai-shek, Gandhi, Trotsky": Have these three figures become figures of past history, or is a comeback expected?

Cho Man-sik (曺晩植)

Regarding the question about the future prospects of a comeback for the above three individuals, as someone without any particular definite views, it would be presumptuous to say this or that, but since you have asked, I offer the following, although it is merely a superficial observation.
I believe Chiang Kai-shek has much room to be active in the future.
The reasons are: First, the position, power (especially military strength), and background (supporting financial conglomerates) that Chiang currently possesses<38> are quite solid and strong. Second, there seems to be no greater figure than him in present-day China. Therefore, whether viewed from Chiang's ambition or China's situation, unless there is extreme anti-Chiang interference from other third forces or a special incident concerning Chiang's personal safety, he will neither voluntarily retire nor should he. Third, although age is not a major condition for someone active on the political front, Chiang, being just over 50, the age of "knowing Heaven's mandate" (知天命), neither young nor old, is at an age full of energy and ambition to be active.

I believe Gandhi will not actively enter the political fray unless a special opportunity arises.
The reasons are: He was originally not in robust health, and now he is an old man of 65. Additionally, there are many disadvantages in the struggle. Therefore, as he is planning to return to the countryside and spend his remaining years (餘年) in the cultural movement of farmers, it seems unlikely he will make a comeback unless a special opportunity arises.

It seems Trotsky has almost no hope of a comeback. The reasons are: Although his age is still in the late 50s (born in 1878), making him capable of activity age-wise, more importantly, as long as Stalin is in power, there will be no room for activity within the Soviet Union. Moreover, being in exile abroad and facing restrictions on residence wherever he goes, it seems he will not be able to find opportunities for activity even abroad, thus I think there is no hope for a comeback.

Hŏ Hŏn (許憲)

Chiang Kai-shek seems to be falling into a period of disappointment, pressured by the tide of events, but besides Chiang, there is still no visible talent capable of resolving the tangled state of current Chinese politics. Therefore, I believe Chiang's regime will be maintained for a while longer.
Gandhi has now gained influential leaders like Nehru (넬), so he should quietly retire to the countryside, protect his principles (志操), and focus on concluding his later years (晩節) cleanly.
Trotsky - His time will not come again before another World War breaks out.

Sŏl T'ae-hŭi (薛泰熙)

Regarding your question about the three figures Chiang, Gandhi, and Trotsky, I have no particular interest or view. However, looking at the general trend and considering the level of their respective countries' people and political situations, if Chiang and Gandhi have ambitions, perhaps there might be some interest in whether they would formulate a grand plan to connect with the Soviet Union, but otherwise, all their myriad strategies (千術萬策) will likely just end up as prey for the strong (强者의 魚肉). Ho ho ho?

Yi Yŏng (李英)

1. Trotsky was a new type of figure. Due to his departure from the organized camp stemming from his vague worldview and embracing isolation, he has completely become a figure of past history.<39>
2. When Gandhi transitions from his unique non-resistance principle to a humanitarian compromise, he and India are already severed (絶緣되엿고), and consequently, no political significance can be expected.
3. Chiang Kai-shek remains in the final stage (最後階段) of cooperative work (協同工作) for the realization of Great Asianism (大亞細亞主義) as a means to extend his own political power.

Wŏn Ik-sang (元翊常)

Regarding your question, considering the reality and the circumstances of these individuals, I reply as follows.
1. Chiang Kai-shek seems to have a bright future (前途洋洋) by grasping Great Easternism (大東洋主義) in light of reality.
1. Gandhi seems unlikely to have a chance for a comeback, considering his abandonment of non-military civil disobedience last year and his advanced age.
1. Trotsky faced opposition from comrades worldwide in the Third International (第3인터내수낼계) from the beginning when he confronted Lenin, and considering his current circumstances, a comeback seems impossible.

Yun Ik-sŏn (尹益善)

1. Chiang Kai-shek, since rising to prominence, seems to follow the trends of the times, his will swayed left and right, and appears lacking in foresight regarding the future course of events. Therefore, it is presumed (推想된다) that it would be best to consider his achievements up to the present as his peak record.
2. Gandhi possesses abundant courage and resilience (강인력). If we consider a phase (一期) in his work, it is recognized that he has reached the midpoint (只今中分). Therefore, it is thought (想覺된다) that the final period (終期) of his work's success, meaning one more phase, remains.
3. Trotsky, due to his profound lifelong study, has thorough foresight into future trends. Therefore, it is conjectured (추측된다) that when the time comes, he holds the hope of becoming a pioneer (선구자) in a great enterprise by carrying through his established principles and political views (旣定한 주의정견).

Kim Hang-gyu (金恒圭)

1. Judging from Chiang Kai-shek's actions over the decade since the Northern Expedition, more than anything, he desperately strives sacrificially to extend his own political life, disregarding any humiliating actions or insulting circumstances (受侮적 사정), leaving no room for other considerations (他顧의 片隙). Therefore, I believe (思하나이다) there is absolutely no expectation (毫無하다고) from the populace, not just in the past but also in the future.
2. While Gandhi has abundant influence as a religious figure, his qualities as a politician are questionable. Simultaneously, his observation of domestic and international situations seems somewhat mistaken and lagging (착각낙오한 감이 불무하니). Consequently, I believe (思하나이다) future expectations are weak (박약하다고).
3. While Trotsky is a thorough revolutionary and theorist, and his contributions to the domestic revolution are widely known (세인이 주지하는 배나), his attempt to realize his ideals too rapidly led him inevitably to face adversity and stormy attacks (역경이 풍랑습격 未免하여), severing his political life. At the same time, it is questionable whether popular expectations will continue indefinitely.

An Chae-hong (安在鴻)

Since the North China issue this time, Chiang Kai-shek's<40> prestige (威望) has considerably fallen, but his political life hinges on the success or failure of the Communist suppression campaign (共軍征伐成敗) in Sichuan (四川). Thus, he is still an active figure. No one capable of replacing him has yet appeared. Gandhi and Trotsky are primarily figures of the past; popular support (人望) in India is said to have shifted from Gandhi to left-leaning figures like Jawaharlal Nehru (야와하르·네르). Trotsky continues his life in exile, banished due to his political view of the 'Possibility of Socialism in One Country' (一國 社會主義의 可能이란 政見) distinct from Stalin's faction and their gradual self-defense policy (自衛政策). As long as the current international situation is maintained, his comeback will be difficult. If the much-talked-about Second World War breaks out and a major upheaval (一大變局) reoccurs, one cannot predict their futures (前途들을 逆睹할 수 업스나), but even so, one cannot definitively say their emergence will be necessary (要하리라고). The future of the latter two is even more unknown (未知).

Kim Myŏng-sik (金明植)

1. China currently aims for a modern military state organization (군국조직). In this military state process (군국과정의), Chiang Kai-shek is an indispensable figure. Currently, one cannot find anyone in China surpassing his military reputation (聲譽) and actual power.
2. I believe India's Gandhi has already become a figure of a bygone era (先世之人物).
3. The Soviet Union is currently in the process of constructing a classless democracy (무계급 떼모구라시). Thus, the destructive genius and constructive heretic Trotsky cannot find a role in the Soviet Union.

Kim Kyŏng-chae (金璟載)

Regarding Trotsky, who is currently living a wandering life (유랑생활), human sentiment makes one sympathize with him. However, this is merely an emotion, and in reality, the possibility of Trotsky returning to the political stage someday and having any kind of expectation placed upon him is hopeless. Gandhi, too, once stood at the forefront of the Indian masses and was the focus of public attention, but he fell from compromise to non-compromise, and then just completely declined from non-compromise. Now, he receives no support whatsoever (하등의 지지) from the Indian people, so he too is one of the people of the past. Chiang Kai-shek is still the head of the Nationalist Government, and though nominally its ruler, he too has no hope of becoming a pioneer of the newly emerging China.
Recalling their past efforts and struggles, it is sad to relegate them now to actors of a past era, but since that is the reality, it cannot be helped.

Pae Sŏng-nyong (裴成龍)

The three gentlemen you asked about were all ousted long ago, either by the masses of their countries or the world masses. Any activity now would be nothing but reactionary. What can be expected of them? If they were to appear when it's not their stage, their efforts would be inferior even to those of clumsy, unknown newcomers, and they would only bring harm (해독이나 끼치게 되겟지요) to popular activities.<41>

Yi In (李仁)

1. For China, facing a tangled present situation and showing signs of concentrating the brewing global turmoil (風雲) into one place, expectations still remain for Chiang Chung-cheng (蔣中正 - Chiang Kai-shek), who can be seen as the one holding the most real power among many figures. Chung-cheng's stage of activity lies in the coming era, and the creation of epoch-making facts for China will also happen at this time.
2. Gandhi, the embodiment (權化) of overturned feudal heroism, carrying an outdated and baseless signboard lagging behind the times and life, is too distant (태원하여) from the times and the masses, and there will be nothing more to expect.
3. Trotsky, gazing only at the moon of his place of exile (配所) and controlling his indignation, possesses a spirit (패기) brimming throughout his being. There will be a time when this aligns with some of the remaining turbulent aftereffects (軌轢餘波) that the Soviet Union itself contains (포장하고 잇는).

Kim Ch'ang-che (金昶濟)

As I was away for a period and only returned home today, I am only now replying respectfully (仰答하오니) to your inquiry. Might there be lament for the lateness? Please forgive me (노경하옵소서).
I have little knowledge about the three figures you inquired about. Therefore, judging what their future might hold is too bold an undertaking, I think. Merely speaking intuitively based on the present, their work does not seem particularly bright (광명스럽게). However, Gandhi alone has some unique appeal (이채가 잇습니다). This is because his saintly life itself can be seen as "strength" (힘).

Chu Ik (朱翼)

Trotsky, harboring indignation and enduring the hardships of wind and dew (풍찬노숙하는) in a distant foreign land thousands of miles away, no matter how much he plots a comeback (捲土重來를 꾀하나), has already lost the support of the masses and will find it difficult to appear once more. Gandhi, who shifted from non-compromise to compromise, no matter how much he attempts a comeback (재기를 圖한들), is already just a figure of past thought, abandoned (이반을 당한) by the masses. Chiang Kai-shek, seeking to dominate the 400-some provinces (400여주의) of China, may act somewhat as a tool (장괴로) of the world capitalist powers, but in the near future, he will not escape the final misfortune (말운) of a setting sun (서산낙일).

Yi Sŏn-gŭn (李瑄根)

I reply to your question.
1. Chiang Kai-shek - I see him as a figure who still has much work left to do. Success or failure is a separate issue, but in eventful China, he is still the figure with the greatest controlling power...
2. Gandhi - I don't know what words or actions he might leave behind at the final moment of his last breath, but now he appears only as an aged saint who has lost his active capacity.
3. Trotsky - Unless the Stalin regime collapses... anyway, he seems destined to spend the rest of his life (여생을) as an old revolutionary unable to make a comeback, perhaps dedicating himself to reading and research. Occasionally making "show"-like predictions (預언이나)...<42>

Sŏ Yŏn-hŭi (徐延禧)

Trotsky, Gandhi, Chiang Kai-shek are already people of bygone days. One wants to sympathize with Trotsky's wandering life and Gandhi's decline from a human perspective, but my personal sympathy and their historical mission are separate paths. Although Chiang Kai-shek is still the ruler of the Nationalist Government, he's already lost the support (지지 밧기는 글넛소) of the Chinese populace.
Thinking about it now, they were heroes of a certain time. They were great men. But now they have become fallen straw men (草人). Heh heh, the ways of the world are all like that.

Yi Kŭng-no (李克魯)

Chiang Kai-shek and Gandhi have more work to do in the future, and the masses will expect more from them. However, I think Trotsky has become a figure of past thought.

Ham Sang-hun (咸尙勳)

Applying the term "comeback" (재기) to Gandhi and Trotsky, who have retired or been expelled from political life, is appropriate, but using the term "comeback" even for Chiang Kai-shek, who currently holds political power, must be because he is almost in an era of disappointment. Gandhi fights when the political tide, the so-called high tide (만조기), arrives, and retreats to focus on spinning wheels (물레둘르기와) and other political education when the tide recedes (퇴조기). If he lives another 10 years, there might be a chance for a comeback. Trotsky has lost his stage, so a comeback is likely impossible. Chiang Kai-shek will probably maintain the status quo until a great war breaks out.

Anonymous (失名氏)

No one can know human affairs and world affairs.
Especially concerning the future, and furthermore, predicting the future of people in this world means predicting the future of the entire world, so it is difficult for anyone to clearly foresee. However, depending on how times change, might Gandhi not regain trust from the masses through a unity of religion and politics (교정일치로써의)?

Pak Ch'ang-hun (朴昌薰)

The three figures you asked about seem to be, to a certain extent, figures of past history.<43>

**Summary**

This 1935 article from the magazine *Samch'ŏlli* surveys various Korean intellectuals and public figures on their views regarding the future prospects of Chiang Kai-shek, Mahatma Gandhi, and Leon Trotsky. Opinions vary significantly. Chiang Kai-shek is generally seen as still having a role to play, primarily due to his existing power base in China and the lack of clear alternatives, though some criticize his perceived opportunism or predict his eventual decline. Gandhi is mostly viewed as a figure whose political influence has waned, likely to retire or focus on spiritual/cultural matters, although a few hold out hope for a future role. Trotsky is almost universally considered a figure of the past with little chance of returning to power, mainly due to Stalin's control in the USSR and his own exile. From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article is interesting as it showcases the engagement of Korean intellectuals with major global political figures and events, despite being under Japanese rule. Their diverse opinions reflect different political leanings and analyses of international affairs, offering a glimpse into the intellectual climate and geopolitical awareness within colonized Korea. Historians might find the range of perspectives, the specific reasoning provided (e.g., linking Chiang to Pan-Asianism, noting Gandhi's shift, analyzing Trotsky's theoretical vs. practical struggles), and the very act of posing such a question in a colonial context particularly revealing.

잡지명	삼천리 제7권 제8호
발행일	1935년 09월 01일
기사제목	我觀 「蔣介石, 간듸, 트로츠키」, 이 세 人物은 이제는 過去史上의 人物이 되엇는가, 또는 再起가 기대되는가?
기사형태	설문

我觀 「蔣介石, 간듸, 트로츠키」, 이 세 人物은 이제는 過去史上의 人物이 되엇는가, 또는 再起가 기대되는가?
曺晩植

以上 3人의 장차 再起의 期待有無에 관한 설문에 대하야 하등의 확실한 견해가 없는 자로서 무엇이 어떠어떠 하다고 말하기는 너머 주제 넘은 일이오나 已往 물으섯스매 다만 피상적 관찰에 불과하오나 左와 같이 기하옵니다.
將介石은 장래에도 활동할 여지가 많으리라고 봅니다.
그 이유로는 一은 蔣氏가 지금 가지고 잇는<38> 그 지위 세력(특히 군력), 배경(지지하는 재벌)이 그 만큼 確固强大한데다가 二는 現下 중국에서는 其以上 大인물이 없는지라 그래서 蔣氏의 야심으로 보던지 중국의 정세로 보던지 다른 第3勢力의 극단의 排蔣干涉이나 蔣氏 신변의 특별한 사변이 없는 한 자진하야 引退하지도 아니할 것이며 또는 해서는 아니될 것으로 보며 三은 정치전선에서 활동하는 사람으로서 연령은 그리 큰 조건은 되지 아니하지마는 蔣氏는 젊도 늙도 안은 知天命의 50이 갓 넘은 점으로 보아서 한참 활동할 기력과 야심이 만만한 年紀임으로써 이외다.
간듸는 특별한 기회가 아니면 政戰에 積極進出 없슬 것으로 봅니다.
그 이유로는 본래 그리 健康體가 안인데다가 지금 65세의 老體이오 兼하야 투쟁상 여러 가지의 불리한 점이 많은지라 그럼으로 농촌으로 도라가서 농민의 문화운동으로 餘年을 보내려 하는 중이매 특별한 기회가 이르지 안이하는 한에는 재기치 아니할 것 같습니다.
