**Body Text Translation:**
Watermill
						稻香
It has been exactly 10 years this autumn since Do Hyang passed away. His friends are preparing to hold a 10th-anniversary memorial service this autumn, and anyway, we publish this posthumous manuscript of his to once again commemorate his writing style (Editor).

Clatter, clatter. The water pouring into the flume and spilling out again lifts the heavy watermill wheel and slams it down into the mortar pit with a thud. The grunts of the farmhands echo mournfully from within the mill shed, thick with fine white powder.

Swish, swish, swish. Turning into beads, then silver powder, stretching out like bamboo stalks, then crashing down again, roar, roar, becoming blue dragons and white dragons, the water surges and flows. It curves around the mountain corner ten *ri* away, pierces through the middle of the fields here for about five *ri*, and then washes past the front slope of the village where this Pangwŏn lives. A watermill stands upon it.

Looking out from the watermill, there is a large village to the northeast. The richest and most powerful man in this village is named Sin Ch'igyu. This man named Pangwŏn lives in the outbuilding (막실) of that house, farming his land, passing the days with his wife.

One autumn night, an exceptionally bright moon shone tranquilly upon this quiet village. Beside the watermill shed, the voices of a woman and a man talking could be heard.

The woman was Pangwŏn’s wife, now twenty-two years old. A young woman at the age when the heart burns with passion, the age of greatest happiness. The man was over fifty-five, having lived most of the path life offers, almost heading towards the pit of decline—an old man.

His voice sounded as if coaxing the woman. “Hey, isn’t there the slightest thing wrong with what I’m saying? You must have heard the details from Old Woman Swaenne, but think about it. If you just agree, whatever<283> you want to do, I’ll do it all for you. Living hundreds of years with that fellow Pangwŏn, you’ll never escape the corner of the outbuilding. Haha, if a person doesn’t experience luxury when young, they’ll die without ever having experienced it once, won’t they? There’s nothing wrong with what I’m saying! I heard the gist of your thoughts from Old Woman Swaenne, but still, it’s not as good as hearing it directly from you. So I asked to meet you here. What’s in your heart? Come on, haha, don’t hold anything back just because you’re in front of me. Tell me. Hmm?”

This old man was, without a doubt, Sin Ch'igyu. He looked down at Pangwŏn’s wife with greedy eyes, patting her back with one hand.

Her prim face was pale and smooth, with long eyebrows and dark blue eyes. Her pouting cheeks were powdered, her nose sharply defined. Tall and slender with broad hips, she looked frighteningly intellectual (理智的) and at the same time, like the prostitute type (娼婦型).

The wife stood silently, feigning shyness, then smiled faintly with a seductive smile and turned her head away. How that smile must have satisfied the beast-like Sin Ch'igyu, how it must have stirred his heart! His graying beard came closer, almost touching the wife’s cheek.

“Hmm? Why? No answer? Are you shy? It’s not something to be so shy about.”

He took the wife’s hand.

“I never knew your hands were so pretty until now. Truly like powder. Isn’t it too pitiful and regrettable that such a demurely formed girl should become the wife of a lowly fellow like Pangwŏn and rot away her whole life like this? Hey.”

The wife didn’t try to turn away, letting the old master do as he pleased, merely looking down at the ground. Finally, she seemed to force her lips open.

“As for me… Old Woman Swaenne told you everything. What you say is far beyond my station.”

“Nonsense, what are you talking about? As you know, I’m not doing this just for fun, and it’s because I have no heir (後嗣), so please bear me a son. Then wouldn’t everything I have become yours? Come now, don’t be like that. Agree today. Then tomorrow, I’ll kick out that fellow Pangwŏn and bring you in.”

“How can you kick him out?”

“Ha! What’s so difficult about that? If I tell him to leave, do you think he won’t leave without putting up a fight?”

“But wouldn’t that be too much?”

“What? Because you think like that, you’ve stayed in this state until now. What does it matter? Don’t worry about such things at all! Come on! Let’s go inside quickly before your husband finds out.”<284>

“You go in first.”

“Why?”

“If people see, they’ll think it’s suspicious.”

“What’s suspicious about going with me?”

“Let’s go quickly.”

The wife slowly followed a few steps behind, then stopped abruptly.

“Master!”

She stood hesitantly.

“What is it?”

The wife stood silently again for a moment, then said,

“It’s nothing.”

She turned back.

“Please go in first.”

The old master, impatient, grabbed her hand.

“Let’s go. Let’s go into the house.”

His heart pounded; his breathing grew shallow. The wife tried to pull her hand away.

“What is a respectable elder like you doing?”

Yet, her body language showed consent to everything. The old master pulled the wife’s body close and turned behind the mill shed. The wife, held in the old master’s arms, looked at him with eyes full of desire.

“Master.”

She uttered one word and swallowed once.

“You won’t lie, will you, Master?”

“No.”

His voice trembled. The wife grasped the old master’s arm with one hand and pointed towards the inside of the mill shed with the other.

“Go in there.”

The old master and the wife emerged from the mill shed twenty or thirty minutes later.

Three days later, Sin Ch'igyu summoned Pangwŏn to the front of his main courtyard.

“Hey.”

Pangwŏn bowed his head before his master.

“Yes.”

He answered respectfully.

“It’s commendable that you’ve worked diligently at my house all this time, but…”

Sin Ch'igyu began, drawing out his words with dignity and gravity. Pangwŏn’s heart seemed to anticipate the words that would follow "but," feeling as if all the blood in his body rushed to his chest, and then every hair on his body seemed to stand on end.

“Starting today, due to circumstances at my house, don’t stay here anymore. Go find a good place elsewhere.”<285>

There were no conditions. Nor was there anything to say here. If told to die, one had to at least pretend to die. The master could buy and sell people with money.

Pangwŏn felt suffocated. If he were alone, he could survive somehow, somewhere, even by begging. But the path to saving his beloved wife was uncertain. He bowed his head, bent his back, and finally bent his heart, trying to plead and beg. But it was futile. The master's heart was harder than iron or stone.

Having no other choice, he told his wife the news. He then asked her to plead with the mistress of the house to let them stay a little longer. But the wife was in no position to listen to Pangwŏn. Instead, she retorted,

“So what are we supposed to do? How are you going to feed me from now on?”

“Are you that scared you won’t be able to eat and live?”

“Of course, I’m scared! Just think about it. Aren’t we completely doomed now?”

“Doomed?”

“Yes. When you brought me here, what did you say? Didn’t you say you could somehow manage to feed at least one person like me?”

“Yes.”

“Right. How well have you fed me and given me luxury? For two years now, all you’ve dragged me around to is the servants’ quarters (행랑) of other people’s houses, right?”

“Hey, are you saying this because you don’t understand? Did things turn out this way because I didn’t try? As we go on living, something will turn up. Surely we won’t just grow old and die like this, will we?”

“I hate hearing that! ‘We’ll roast the horn when it falls off’—when will that ever happen?”

Pangwŏn was already furious about being kicked out, and now with his wife behaving this way, a fire surged within him.

“You damned woman, deserving worse than death! Why do you grate on a man’s nerves?”

“Why are you cursing at me?”

“What’s wrong with cursing a bit, you bitch?”

“Why are you cursing?”

The wife’s face turned pale as she confronted him.

“Are you throwing a fit, you bitch?”

“Who’s throwing a fit? A man who can’t even support one wife just curses at her, what else does he do? Have you ever bought me a silver ring or a silver hairpin? I’ve done everything you asked me to do!”

“You bitch, do you want a silver ring and silver hairpin that badly? You filthy bitch.”

“What’s filthy? How clean are you?”<286>

The word “놈” (fellow/bastard) began to escape the wife’s lips.

“Look at this bitch. Who are you calling a ‘fellow’?”

His hand grabbed the wife’s topknot (낭자), lifted her up, and punched her back several times.

“You bitch, deserving the rack (주리때)!”

A couple of kicks landed on her buttocks (웅등이), and the wife collapsed, then got up again. Her unbound hair hung disheveled, and her sharp eyes glinted with malice.

“Why are you hitting me? You bastard! Kill me. Kill me. Go ahead, try and kill me. You bastard. Let’s die together, me first, then you!”

He struck the charging wife, knocking her down.

“This bitch is trying her best to die!”

When Pangwŏn hit his wife, it was a kind of rough play done with fists. When his fist or foot met her body, Pangwŏn felt a pain pierce the center of his chest, sharper than the pain felt by the wife who was hit. Hitting his wife in anger was, in reality, no different from biting his own heart with his own teeth. For him, the hitter, there was intense pity and compassion. However, the only person who could receive his venting anger was still his wife. More than her being the easiest target, she was the one with whom he could most comfortably vent his anger. When, less than a day after the fight, the two lay side by side, pillow to pillow, holding each other tightly, there was no comfort so precious, so deeply moving. After hitting his wife and venting his anger, when he received solace through remorse that felt like his heart was being gouged out and through a hotter embrace, for Pangwŏn, not just for the two of them, there was no stronger, hotter trust than that.

The wife deliberately raised her voice and wailed loudly.

Almost everyone in the village listened, but thought,

“Ah, they’re having a lovers’ quarrel again!”

And rather envied their fight. A young man from the neighboring house came over, grinning, looked in, and said,

“Stop it now.”

Making a gesture of stopping them. Only the village children lined up in front of the yard, eyes wide like marbles, watching the spectacle.

That evening, Pangwŏn returned, tipsy from alcohol. The anger from hitting his wife earlier had somehow dissipated. In his alcohol-fueled excitement, he intensely longed for his wife’s embrace and even felt inclined to apologize to her. Fundamentally good-natured, weak-hearted, and affectionate, he committed ignorant acts because he grew up uneducated, but this was by no means the ignorance that defined his character.

Staggering towards home, he closed his blurry, drooping eyes and mumbled to himself,

“Damn beggar! If he tells me to leave, I’ll leave. Am I scared? He must think there’s no place<287> to live outside his house! Humph, what nonsense is this? Is money everything? You bastard, if you keep this up, you’ll taste this fist someday. Do you think you’ll just get away with it nicely?”

After jumping over a small stream,

“Money! What is money?”

After thinking for a moment,

“Aigh…”

He sighed, then,

“Money kills people! Money! Money! Humph, people came first, then money, right? Did money come first, then people?”

Staggering across the stepping stones again,

“Why did that damned woman have to provoke me like that and scratch at a man’s heart!”

His voice held an indescribably affectionate tone. When he thought of his wife, all his discontent seemed to fade. Lifting his bowed head, he looked up at the sky.

“Haah, she suffers too, it’s suffering.”

He lowered his head again.

“I went too far. I shouldn’t have done that.”

He arrived at his house, grabbed the door handle, and shook it.

“Hey! Are you sleeping! Hey!”

But there was no answer, only darkness.

“Where did this bitch go!”

He slammed the door hard enough to break it, then went back out to the street and to the neighboring house.

“Hey, Auntie! Did you see where my wife went?”

The neighboring couple, who were eating, replied,

“Got drunk somewhere again, I see! The young miss did her hair earlier and went towards the mill.”

“Towards the mill?”

“Yes.”

“Damn bitch! What did she go to the mill for!”

He muttered to himself again as he headed alone towards the mill.

Just as he rounded the back of the mill shed, he saw Sin Ch'igyu and his wife coming out of it.

“Ah…”

It was so unexpected that he couldn’t say anything, just stood there blankly for a while, staring.

Twin flames seemed to leap upside down in his eyes. Heat rose, making his eyes red as if painted with vermilion, and a lightning-like glint flickered within them.

He trembled from head to toe for a long moment. His teeth chattered against each other. His fists were clenched so tightly they felt like they would break.

Seeing Pangwŏn standing there, the wife and Sin Ch'igyu<288> felt a chill run down their spines at first, but then calmly composed themselves. Since things had come to this, their attitude was: do what you will.

Pangwŏn rushed forward and grabbed his wife’s wrist. He ground his teeth and trembled violently.

“I never thought you’d do this.”

The wife retorted,

“What do you mean, ‘do this’?”

She glared with cold eyes.

“Now I’ve seen everything. Did you only just realize it would naturally come to this? Let go! Why are you grabbing someone else’s arm like this? From today, you can’t treat me so carelessly! Filthy fellow! If a woman says she dislikes you, you should just back off. What kind of unmanly behavior is this! Let go.”

She tried to shake off his arm, but filled with fury throughout his body, he didn’t let go so easily.

“Hey? Are you really serious about this?”

“Of course, I’m serious. Did I eat expensive rice just to lie?”

“You’ve truly gone mad, haven’t you?”

“What? Who are you saying has gone mad? I’m utterly dumbfounded! Let go! Let go! Why are you being so clingy? Let go.”

She pulled away with all her might, and her hand slipped free.

The wife rubbed her wrist and turned away resentfully.

Sin Ch'igyu, who had been watching this scene from a distance, took a couple of steps forward, cleared his throat awkwardly, and said,

“Hey! If you’re drunk, you should go home early and sleep. What’s all this commotion? Do your eyes see nothing at all? You two can fight wherever you want, but don’t you see who’s here? Do your eyes see nothing?”

He deliberately raised his voice and bellowed.

“Eh, despicable fellow!”

He glared fiercely. Pangwŏn stared up at him for a long time without speaking. If he followed his thoughts, he would knock him down with one punch, but the notion of him being his master until just moments ago still lingered in his mind. Like a flash of lightning, that notion bound his mouth and arms. His mind, having only served others from childhood until today, had deeply ingrained the trait of fearing anyone called ‘master’. But from today, Sin Ch'igyu was no longer his master, and he was no longer Sin Ch'igyu’s servant. They merely stood facing each other as equals. No, from now on, Sin Ch'igyu was Pangwŏn’s enemy. An enemy whose liver he could chew and still have hatred left over.

Sin Ch'igyu met Pangwŏn’s direct gaze and stared back.

“What are you going to do, staring straight at me like that? The whole world must be going to ruin, seeing all sorts of strange things happen. What now, you bastard?”

“You bastard?”<289>

Pangwŏn took a step forward. As his tree-strong leg strode forward, Sin Ch'igyu’s hair stood on end. As his iron-like fists thrust forward, Sin Ch'igyu’s heart plummeted.

“Does the phrase ‘you bastard’ come out of your mouth? You bastard who deserves to have his limbs torn apart and still wouldn't be satisfying enough! You told me to leave today so you could steal my wife, didn’t you?”

“Oh dear! This fellow’s eyes must be crooked. Hey, I’ll go in first. You come in later with your husband!”

Seeing the situation turn dangerous, Sin Ch'igyu tried to sneak away, turning to leave. Pangwŏn grabbed the turning Sin Ch'igyu’s collar tightly, lifted him up with one arm, and said,

“Where are you going, you bastard? You haven’t learned your lesson yet, have you?”

He slammed him onto the ground, then sat astride him and pressed down on his throat. Like a snake catching a frog, Sin Ch'igyu made croaking sounds, unable to utter a single word.

“You bitch, if you die and I die, that’s the end of it, right?”

Pangwŏn rained down blows with his fists indiscriminately. Eventually, his fists weren’t enough, so he picked up a nearby stone pestle and struck with all his might, aiming to kill. The rage boiling in his arms, his entire body, reached its peak, and the innate cruelty hidden in the human heart emerged without reserve. His eyes glittered terrifyingly, like those of a wolf or hyena sitting astride its panting prey, seeming only satisfied upon seeing hot blood. A supernatural, terrifying strength surged into his arms and legs.

Seeing this spectacle, the wife was terrified. A dreadful thing was about to happen before her eyes. Her legs, weak with fear, wouldn’t move as she wished.

“Ah! Help! Someone save him! Help!”

In the desolate night, in the lonely village, a woman’s tragic voice echoed eerily. Hearing this sound, Pangwŏn exerted even more force, tightly shut his eyes, and struck down relentlessly. The sound of bone hitting stone mingled with the sound of tearing flesh, thud, thud. Blood-soaked stones scattered here and there, and pieces of flesh clung to the tattered clothes.

From the direction of the village, there was murmuring, then the sound of shoes and the clatter of a sword. Something flashed like lightning in Pangwŏn’s mind. Still gripping the weapon in his hand, he paused, collected himself for a moment, and listened intently in that direction.

“Policeman…”

Sitting astride Sin Ch'igyu’s stomach, hearing the policeman’s shoes, he finally realized what he had done.

He stood up like a madman. Then he went towards his wife, who stood nearby, trembling.

“Hey! Let’s go! Let’s run away! You and I, let’s go together! Come on!<290> Hurry! Hurry!”

The wife, fearing something else might happen to her, tried to run away. Pangwŏn followed her.

“Hey! Hey! Can you really not understand me like this? Don’t you know how I feel about you? Come on! Let’s run away quickly. Hurry, hurry, the policeman is chasing us from behind.”

The wife stood still, taking small, hesitant steps.

“No! You go alone! I don’t want to. I don’t want to.”

“Let’s go! Huh? Let’s go!”

Like a madman, he grabbed the wife’s arm and pulled. At that moment, someone seized both his arms from behind, pinning them back tightly as if clamping them in stocks.

“Where do you think you’re going, you bastard?”

Without looking back, he knew who it was. All the strength drained from his body, and as he was about to fall backward, a fist like a plank struck his cheek mercilessly.

“Get a hold of yourself.”

“Yes.”

Unconsciously, his head bowed, and his voice became respectful.

On the ground, Sin Ch'igyu twitched and rolled around. A mournful scream (悲鳴) could be heard.

Pangwŏn, bound with rope (포승), and the wife were dragged away to the police station as they were. Sin Ch'igyu was carried away by the farmhands.

Three months passed. Pangwŏn, who had served time in prison for the crime of inflicting injury (傷害罪), was released upon completing his sentence. However, Sin Ch'igyu recovered at his home without consequence and lived with Pangwŏn’s wife. After his whole body healed, Sin Ch'igyu thought to himself:

“I thought I was going to die, but here I am, still alive!”

He touched the scarred place on his face.

“That fellow doing that was actually fortunate for me. My face hurt quite a bit, though! Haah.”

“I was worrying about how to get rid of that fellow anyway, so it turned out well. I hope that fellow eats prison beans for a good ten years.”

In prison, Pangwŏn thought that as soon as he got out, he would kill the pair of them and then either die himself or meet some other fate.

Being kicked out of the house and having his wife stolen—thinking about it made his teeth grind and his body tremble with rage. Realizing it was all because he had no money made him feel even more resentful.

“Eh, filthy bitch!”

Even while working in his red prison garb and chains, he would sometimes spit on the ground and mutter to himself.

“What’s the point of living like this? A perfectly healthy man gets his wife stolen and eats prison beans for no reason…”<291>

When he came out of prison, he looked back once more at the prison building, thinking, ‘Either I lose my life here for the last time, or else I stab myself in the throat with my own hand,’ resolving that some fate awaited him. He tensed his whole body again and gave a bitter smile.

He walked the two-hundred-*ri* road back to the village where his wife lived.

However, no one acknowledged him. People he used to be close with saw him and avoided him.

He was treated like a leper. Since coming out of prison, the world had become even colder. Colder and more heartless than he had imagined. Having no choice, he wandered in the nearby mountains until nightfall. Then, deep in the night, he came down to the village. He passed by the mill shed again. Thoughts of three months ago returned. Remembering he was captured here made the feeling of injustice and resentment surge up even more intensely. He stood there for a long time, thinking about that time, shuddered, and then looked for that house again.

The weather had turned very cold, and snow lay on the ground. The clothes he wore were the autumn clothes he had entered prison in, so the cold should have felt like it was carving his flesh, but lost in resentment and excitement, he didn't even notice it.

“Should I just dispose of both of them?”

He pondered inwardly.

“Right, what’s the use of keeping such worthless lives alive?”

He touched the long dagger hidden at his side again. He stroked it with a thrill.

He climbed over Sin Ch'igyu’s wall. His feet moved with the familiarity of previous visits. He peeked into the master's quarters, then circled around back and stood beneath the window of the inner room. He pressed his ear against it. Nothing could be heard. He drew the knife in his hand. Then he deliberately rattled the back window.

“Who’s there?”

The wife’s head popped out as the window opened. He quickly stepped aside. The window closed again, and the wife went back inside.

Pangwŏn’s heart wavered strangely. Hearing the lovely sound of his wife’s voice after such a long time felt ethereal and enchanting, like when he dreamed in prison, seeming to tempt his heart. It felt like meeting her again in a dream, and seeing her after so long, all his resolve seemed to melt like ice. He thought, surely the wife couldn’t have completely forgotten him, and when he considered their past affection, the thought arose that perhaps her betrayal was a lie.

Even though she had caused him to end up in prison, the courage to raise the knife and kill her did not come instantly, and he began to hesitate.

“No. Let me ask her just one more time.”<292>

He put the raised knife back and thought.

“It’s a lie. It’s a lie! It can’t be true.”

He was half-convinced, half-doubting.

“Right. Let me ask just once more, and then decide whether to kill or spare her!”

He rattled the window again. This time, the wife opened the window again, looked around, put on worn-out straw sandals, and came out.

“Who is it?”

As she was about to turn the corner of the house where Pangwŏn stood,

“It’s me!”

He clamped his hand over her mouth and pressed the knife to her chest.

“Make a sound and you die!”

Pangwŏn gagged the wife with a handkerchief, tied her up, threw her over his shoulder, and ran like lightning. In no time, he carried the wife to the front of the watermill, put her down, and untied her bindings. Then he took a deep breath.

“Don’t you know me?”

In the pitch-black night, he brought his face right up to the wife’s nose. The wife looked closely at his face, then,

“Ah!”

She cried out and stepped back.

“There’s nothing to be surprised about. If you listen to me today, I’ll let you live. If not, it’s this!”

He thrust the gleaming knife forward. The wife, composed again, said,

“Listen to you? If I were going to listen to you, I would have done so already. Would I still be here? You know my heart too. When you and I ran away here two years ago, my former husband tried to kill me, stabbing me in the side with a knife—you touched that scar every night, didn’t you? Do you think I’m afraid of a little knife like that, that I can’t do what I want? Ha! What kind of cowardly act is this for a man? Go on! If you’re going to stab, stab me. Go on, go on.”

The wife bared her chest and confronted him. Pangwŏn was so stunned by the wife’s bold attitude that the knife he held actually recoiled slightly. He unconsciously asked,

“Are you serious?”

And took another step closer.

“Am I not serious? Though I am a woman, I am not a coward like you! What on earth is this?”

Perhaps still afraid, the wife knocked the knife from Pangwŏn’s hand, sending it clattering to the ground.

As the knife fell, Pangwŏn, seeing the wife who had seemed so brave moments before suddenly appear intensely cowardly and dirty, picked up the knife again and advanced.

“Yes! You cunning bitch! What now? Won’t you go far, far away with me right now? Come on! Let’s go!”

He tried pleading with tears in his eyes, begging<293> her.

“Come on, hurry, let’s run far, far away together like before! I truly cannot kill you with my own knife.”

Malice rose in the wife’s eyes. A glint flashed like lightning in the dark night.

“No. I’d rather die than go. I’m sick of living such a wretched and lowly life again. I’m tired of it.”

“Do those words truly come from your mouth? You made it impossible for me to return to our hometown, made me lose everything I had, and finally, even sent me to prison, which they call hell on earth! And still, you won’t grant my very last wish?”

“I always knew I would die at your hands someday! Well—whether I die today or tomorrow, death comes eventually. Since it has come to this, kill me.”

“Really? Really?”

“Really!”

The wife showed her determined will. Pangwŏn’s hand trembled. Then he squeezed his eyes shut tight!

“Eh, you fox-like bitch!”

He aimed the knife point at the wife’s side and thrust with all his might. The wife clenched her teeth.

“Murderer!”

With one cry, she collapsed on the spot. His hand holding the knife hilt trembled uncontrollably as blood rushed to it, then blood seeped out.

Pangwŏn pulled out the knife, collapsed onto the wife, stabbed himself in the chest, and died.
(End)<294>

**Summary**

The story "Watermill" depicts the tragic fate of Pangwŏn, a poor tenant farmer, and his wife. Pangwŏn's wife is seduced by their wealthy and powerful landlord, Sin Ch'igyu, who desires an heir. Promised a better life, she agrees to become his mistress. Sin Ch'igyu then dismisses Pangwŏn, leading to a violent confrontation where Pangwŏn brutally attacks Sin Ch'igyu near the watermill but is arrested. After serving three months in prison for assault, Pangwŏn returns to find his wife living with the recovered Sin Ch'igyu and himself ostracized. Consumed by betrayal and despair, he confronts his wife again at the watermill, ultimately stabbing her when she refuses to run away with him, before killing himself. From a historical perspective, the story starkly illustrates the extreme power imbalance and exploitation inherent in the landlord-tenant relationships prevalent in colonial Korea, where wealth and status allowed figures like Sin Ch'igyu to act with impunity, while the poor like Pangwŏn were left powerless, driving them to desperate violence. The narrative highlights the brutal social realities and lack of recourse for the disenfranchised under the existing system.

물레방아
						稻香
稻香이 간지 올가을로서 꼭 10년이 된다. 그의 友人들은 이 가을에 10年祭를 지내려 準備中인데 아무튼 그의 遺稿一篇을 실어 그의 作風을 다시 추모하려 하노라(編者)



덜컹 덜컹 홈통에 들엇다가 다시 쏘다저 흘으는 물이 륙중한 물레방아를 번쩍 들엇다가 쿵 하고 확속으로 내던질 제 머슴들의 콧소리는 허연계 가루가 케케안즌 방아간 속에서 청승스러웁게 들려나온다.
솰 솰 솰 구슬이 되엿다가 은가루가 되고 대줄기가치 뻐치엇다가 다시 쾅 쾅 쏘다저 청룡이 되고 백룡이 되어 용소슴처 흘으는 물이 저쪽 산모통이를 십리나 두고 돌고 다시 이쪽 들복판을 오리쯤 꾀뚜른 뒤에 이 방원(芳源)이가 사는 동리 압기슬글 시처지내가는데 그 우에 물레방아 하나가 노여잇다.
물레방아에서 듸려다 보면 동북간으로 큼직한 마을이 잇스니 이 마을에 가장 부자요 가장 세력이 잇는 사람으로 일홈을 신치규(申治圭)라고 불은다. 이 방원이라는 사람은 그 집의 막실(幕室) 사리를 하야가며 그의 땅을 경작하야 자긔 안해와 두 사람이 그날그날을 지내간다.
엇더한 가을밤 유난히 밝은 달이 고요한 이 촌을 한적한게 비초일 때 그 물레방아간 엽헤 엇더한 녀자 하나와 엇던 남자 하나가 서서 이야기를 하는 소리가 들리엇다.
그 녀자는 방원의 안해로 지금 나히가 스물두살. 한참 정열의 타는 가슴으로 가장 행복스러울 나희의 젊은 녀자이오 그 남자는 오십이 반이 넘어 인생으로서 살아올 길을 다 살고서 거의 거의 쇠멸의 구렁이를 향하야 가는 늙은이다.
그의 말소리는 마치 그 녀자를 달래는 것가치 『얘 내 말이 조곰도 글을 것이 업지? 쇳네 할멈에게도 자세한 말을 드럿슬터이지만은 너 생각해 보아라. 네가 허락만 하면 무엇이든지<283> 네가 허고십다는 것은 내가 전부 해줄 터이란말야. 그까짓 방원이 녀석하고 네가 몃백년 살어야 언제든지 막실 구석을 면치 못할터이니 허허 사람이란 젊어서 호강해보지 못하면 평생 호강 한번 하야보지 못하고 죽을 것이 안이냐. 내가 말하는 것이 조곰도 잘못하는 것이 업느니라! 대강 너의 말을 쇳네 할멈에게 듯기는 들엇스나 그래도 너의게 한번 바로 대고 듯는 것만 못해서 이리로 맛나자고 한 것이다. 너의 마음은 엇더냐? 어듸 허허 내 압히라고 조곰도 엇더케 알지 말고 이야기 해봐. 응?』
이 늙은이는 두말할 것 업시 신치규다. 그는 탐욕스러운 눈으로 방원의 계집을 듸려다보며 한손으로 등을 뚜다린다.
새침한 얼골이 파루 쪽쪽하고 기다란 눈섭과 검푸른 두 눈 가장자리에 입분입 뾰루통한 뺨이며 콧날이 옷똑한데다가 후리후이한 키에 떡버러진 엉등이가 아모리 보드래도 무서웁게 리지적(理智的)인 동시에 또는 창부형(娼婦型)으로 생긴 녀자이다.
계집은 아모 말이 업시 서서 짐짓 북그러운 태를 지우며 미혹적의 우슴을 생긋 웃고는 고개를 도리켯다. 그 우슴이 얼마나 즘승가튼 신치규의 만족을 사게 되엇스며 또는 마음을 충동식혓는지 히끗히끗한 수염이 거의 계집의 뺨에 닷도록 더 갓가히 와서
『응? 왜? 대답이 업나? 붓그러워서 그러니. 그러케 붓그러워 할 일은 안인데.』
하고 게집의 손을 잡으머
『손도 이러케 입분 줄은 여태까지 몰낫구나. 참 분길갓다. 이러케 얌전히 생긴 애가 방원가튼 천한 놈의 계집이 되어 일평생을 그대로 썩는다는 것은 넘어 가엽고 앗갑지 안으냐? 얘.』
계집은 몸을 돌리랴고 하지도 안코 령감이 하는대로 내버려두며 눈으로 땅만 내려다 보고 섯다가 갓가수루 입을 떠이는 듯 하더니
『제말야 모두 쇳네 할멈이 엿주엇지요. 저에게는 넘어 분수에 과한 말슴이닛가요.』
『온 천만에 소리를 다하는구나. 그게 무슨 소리냐. 너도 아다십히 내가 너를 작난삼아 그러는 것도 아니겟고 후사(後嗣)가 업서 그리는 것이닛가 네가 내 아들이나 하나 나주렴. 그러면 내 것이 모다 네것이 되지 안늬. 자아 그러지 말고 오늘 허락을 하렴. 그러면 래일이라도 방원이란 놈을 내쫏고 너를 불러듸릴터이니.』
『엇더케 내쫏칠 수가 잇세요.』
『허어 그것이 그리 어려울 것이 무엇잇니. 내가 나가라는데 제가 나가지 안코 백일줄아늬!』
『그러치만 넘어 과하지 안을가요?』
『무엇? 저런 생각을 하닛가 네가 이 모양으로 여태까지 잇섯지 엇더탄 말이냐? 그런 것은 조곰도 념려하지말구자! 또 네 서방에게 들킬라 어서 드러가자.』<284>
『먼저 드러가세요.』
『왜』
『남이 보면 수상히 알게요.』
『무얼 나하고 가는데 수상히 알게 무어야.』
『어서가자.』
게집은 천천이 두어거름 따러가다가
『령감!』
하고 무춤하고 서잇다.
『왜 그러니.』
게집은 다시 말이 업시 서잇다가
『안예요.』
하고
『먼저 드러가세요.』
하며 도라슨다. 령감이 간이 달어서 게집의 손을 잡으며
『가자. 집으로 드러가자.』
그의 가슴은 두근거리는지 숨소리가 자저진다. 게집은 손을 빼랴하며
『점잔으신 어른이 이게 무슨 짓이세요.』
하면서도 그에 몸짓에는 모든 것을 허락한다는 뜻이 보엿다. 령감은 게집의 몸을 끄러안더니 방아간 뒤로 도라드러섯다. 게집은 령감 가슴에 안키어서 정욕이 가득한 눈으로 그를 보면서
『령감.』
말 한마대 하고 침 한번 삼키엇다.
『령감이 거짓말은 안하시지오.』
『안이.』
그의 말은 떨리엇다. 게집은 령감의 팔을 한 손으로 잡고 또 한 손으로는 방아간 속을 가리켯다.
『저리로 드러가세요.』
령감과 게집은 방아깐에서 이 삼십분 후에 다시 나왓다.



사흘이 지난 뒤에 신치규는 방원이를 자긔의 사랑마당 압흐로 불럿다.
『얘』
방원은 상전이라 고개를 숙이고
『녜』
공손하게 대답을 하얏다.
『네가 그간 내집에서 정성스러웁게 일을 한 것은 고마운 일이지만은...』
점잔과 주짜를 빼면서 신치규는 말을 끄내엇다. 방원의 가슴은 이 「만은」이라는 말뒤에 이어질 말을 밀이 깨달은 듯이 온전신의 피가 가슴으로 모혀드는 듯 하더니 다시 터럭이라는 터럭은 전부 까구로 이러스는 듯 하얏다.
『오늘부터는 우리집에 사정이 잇서 그러니 내집에 잇지 말고 다른 곳에 조흔 곳을 차저가 보아라.』<285>
아모 조건도 업다. 또는 이 곳에서도 할 말이 업다. 죽으라고 하면 죽는 신용이라도해야 하는 것이다. 주인은 돈 가지고 사람을 사고 팔 수도 잇는 것이다.
방원은 가슴이 답답하얏다. 자긔 혼자 몸 가트면 어듸가서 엇더케 비러 먹드래도 살 수가 잇지만은 사랑하는 안해를 구해갈 길이 망연하다. 그는 고개를 굽히고 허리를 굽히고 나종에는 마음을 굽히어 사정도 하여보고 애걸도 하야보앗다. 그러나 그것은 헛된 일이다. 주인의 마음은 쇠나 돌보다도 더 구덧다.
그는 하는 수 업시 자긔 안해에게 그 이야기를 하얏다. 그러고 안해더러 안주인 마님께 사정을 좀하야 얼마간이라도 더 잇게 하야 달라고 하야 보라 하얏다. 그러나 안해는 방원의 말을 들을 리가 업섯다. 도리혀
『그러면 엇더케 한단말이오. 이제부터는 나를 엇더케 먹여 살일 터이오?』
『너는 그러케도 먹고 살 수 업슬가봐 겁이 나니?』
『겁이 나지 안코. 생각을 해보구려. 인제는 꼼작할 수 업시 죽지 안엇소?』
『죽어?』
『그럼 님자가 나를 데리고 이곳까지 올 때에 무엇이라고 하얏소. 어더케해서든지 너 하나야 먹여 살리지 못하겟느냐고 하얏지오.』
『그래.』
『그래. 얼마나 나를 잘 먹여 살리고 나를 호강식혓소. 여태까지 이태나 되도록 끌구 도라다닌다는 것이 남의 집 행랑이엇지오?』
『얘 그것은 네가 몰으고 하는 말이냐? 내가 허랴고 하지 안어서 그러케 된 것이냐? 차차 사라가는 동안에 무슨 일이든지 생기겟지. 설마 요대로 늙어 죽기야 하겟니?』
『듯기실소!  뿔떠러지면 구어먹지 어느 천년에』
방원이는 갓득이나 내여 쫏기고 화가 나는데 게집까지 그리하닛가 속에서 열화가 치밀려 올너왓다.
『이 륙시를 하고도 남을 년! 왜 남의 마음을 글컹거리니.』
『왜 사람에게 욕을 해.』
『이년아 욕 좀 하면 엇더냐.』
『왜 욕을 해.』
게집이 얼골이 노래지며 대든다.
『이년이 발악인가?』
『누가 발악야. 게집년 하나 건사 못하는 위인이 게집보고 욕만 하고 한게 무엇야? 그래 은가락지 은비녀나 한벌 사주어 보앗서? 내가 임자하자고 하는대로 하지 안은 것은 업지!』
『이년아 은가락지 은비녀가 그러케 갓고 십흐냐. 이 더러운 년아.』
『무엇이 더러워. 너는 얼마나 정한 것이냐.』<286>
게집의 입 속에서는 「놈」소리가 나오기 시작한다.
『이 년 보게. 누구더러 놈이래.』
하고 손길이 게집의 낭자를 휘여 잡더니 그대로 집어들고 두어번 주먹으로 등줄기를 우리엿다.
『이 주리때를 앙길년!』
발길이 웅등이를 두어 번 질르닛가 게집은 그대로 걱구러젓다가 다시 이러낫다. 푸러 헛드린 머리가 치렁치렁 끄을리고 쌀눅한 눈에는 독기가 석기엇다.
『왜 사람을 치니? 이 놈! 죽여라. 죽여. 어듸 죽여보아라. 이 놈. 나 죽고 너 죽자!』
하고 달려드는 게집을 후려처서 꺽구러트리고서
『이 년이 죽으랴고 기를 쓰나!』
방원이가 게집을 치는 것은 그것이 주먹을 가지고 하는 일종의 롱담이다. 그는 주먹이나 발길이 게집의 몸에 다을 때 거기에 으더맛은 게집의 살이 압흔 것보다 더 찌르르하게 가슴 복판을 찌르는 압흠을 방원은 깨닷는 것이다. 화김에 게집을 치는 것이 실상은 자긔의 마음을 자긔의 닛발로 무러 뜻는 것이나 다름이 업는 것이다. 따리는 그의게는 몹시 애처로움이 잇고 불상함이 잇는 것이다. 그러나 자긔의 화푸리를 바더주는 사람은 아즉까지도 게집밧게는 업섯다. 제일 만만하다는 것보다도 가장 마음 노코 화푸리할 수 잇슴이다. 싸홈한 뒤 하로가 못되어 두사람이 벼개를 나란히 하고 꼭 끼고 잘 때에는 그러케 고마웁고 그러케 감격이 일어나는 위안이 또 다시 업슴이다. 게집을 치고 화푸리를 하고 난 뒤에 다시 가슴을 어이는 듯한 후회와 더 뜨거운 포옹으로 위로를 바들 그 때에는 두 사람 안이라 방원에게는 그만콤 힘잇고 뜨거운 미듬이 또다시 업는 까닭이다.
게집은 일부러 소리를 놉혀서 꺼이꺼이 운다.
온마을 사람이 거의 귀를 기우렷스나
『응. 또 사랑싸홈을 하는군!』
하고 도리혀 그 싸홈을 부러워하얏다. 엽헤집 젊은 것이 와서 싱글실글 우스면서 뒤려다 보며
『인제 고만두라구.』
