**Body Text Translation:**
The Philippines, Nearing its First Presidential Inauguration Ceremony
(Head Office Special Contributor) O Yŏng-sŏp, Manila · Philippine Union College

Dear XX Hyŏng,

Right now, the Philippines (比律賓) seems feverishly excited. It's because the Presidential Inauguration Ceremony is drawing near. That is, preparations for the independence of the Philippine Commonwealth (比島聯邦) are steadily progressing, and the historic first presidential inauguration ceremony is scheduled to be held grandly in the new capital, Manila (마닐나), on November 15th. All organizations and individuals within the Philippines are busy with preparations. On the other hand, newspapers report that congratulatory envoys are expected not only from various countries along the distant Pacific coast but even from the major powers of Europe (歐羅巴 列强). The envoys confirmed so far include 47 members from the United States, including current Vice President Garner (가-나-) and members of both the Senate and House. From Japan (日本), it is said that either the President of the House of Peers, Prince Konoe (近衛公爵), or Prince Tokugawa (德川公爵) will come. From China (中國), former Foreign Minister Wang Zhengting (王正延) is set to come. Besides these, envoys from related countries such as Great Britain (英吉利), Spain (西班牙), and the Netherlands (和蘭) are also expected, so it is not difficult to imagine the national significance of this event. (Photo shows members of the Philippine Independence Party (比島獨立黨員)).

Therefore, in the streets of the capital, Manila (마닐다), there are already many foreign guests dressed in splendid attire. Many new buildings are being constructed in the city, and even the roadside trees on the central thoroughfares have been newly pruned, showing the hustle to enhance the city's beauty.

As this is the report on the local situation here that I deeply felt the need for when I parted ways with you, Hyŏng, at the gates of Hanyang (漢陽 城頭),<59> I, your younger brother (弟), will sincerely provide further reports, Report No. 2 and Report No. 3, detailing the scenes of the Presidential Inauguration Ceremony.

By the way, according to what I hear, it seems Upper House Speaker Quezon (커즌) is considered more likely to become the President this time than General Aguinaldo (아기날드 장군).

Dear XX Hyŏng,

Forgive me, the order of my writing has become mixed up. After parting with you, Hyŏng, that day, I went to the Osaka Shosen Kaisha (大阪商船) Moji (門戶) branch office on the morning of July 5th to buy a ship ticket. They told me to go to the Water Police Station (水上警察署) and get a physical examination certificate first. So, I went to the Water Police Station, but they said that people going abroad must complete their examination beforehand in Yokohama (橫濱), Kōbe (神戶), or Nagasaki (長崎) to be boarded at Moji (門司); otherwise, they couldn't allow boarding. This is useful knowledge for future travelers going abroad to know in advance.

So, I went back to Nagasaki port (長崎港), completed the examination, and returned to Moji. Although I was delayed by a day, there was still some time before the ship's departure. I went up to Mekari Park (매까리공원) and was looking over the whole of Moji city. Intending to write a letter home, I sat on a bench in the park's hexagonal pavilion (公園六角) and started writing when a man in plain clothes approached me and said that this place is a fortified zone (要塞地), so taking pictures or making any kind of record is prohibited. I realized this too was an experience that first-time travelers should be cautious about beforehand. Anyway, after going through various twists and turns (層折), I finally boarded the ship bound for Manila.

Once aboard the ship, I felt as if I had already reached my destination. The name of the steamship I rode was the Shikago Maru (シカコ丸). Although the ship wasn't very large, its facilities were complete and clean. This ship only had first-class and third-class cabins. In first class, there were 7 passengers: 2 Americans and 5 Japanese. In third class, there were 75 passengers: 74 Japanese and 1 Korean. The crew numbered 96. This ship was a world inhabited by 178 people. More than half of the third-class passengers were immigrants (移民), and the rest were merchants (商人). I was the only one going to study, and also the only Korean. Aboard the ship floating on the sea, people were very kind, and their hearts seemed good. Sharing food and helping each other with conveniences was much better than on land. Although it was my first time experiencing life at sea, it was extremely interesting (趣味). I ate the well-prepared meals served three times a day. When bored, I read books, slept, gathered in small groups for conversation (談話), looked out at the vast, distant sea from the deck, and sometimes played golf (콜푸) prepared on the deck. Particularly worth seeing at sea were the round, red sun slowly rising from the eastern sea in the morning, and the scene in the evening where the round sun gradually sank into the sea not too far away – it was truly a spectacle.

Dear beloved Hyŏng,

On the morning of the 9th, I arrived at Keelung Port (基隆港) in Taiwan (臺灣). When the ship docked, about 20-30 Taiwanese people in small, fish-shaped <60> boats (魚形), wearing dark blue clothes and strange farmer's hats (農笠), circled around the steamship waiting to take passengers onto their boats. This sight left a truly strange impression. I disembarked at Keelung Port and explored the city streets. The streets and shops of Keelung (基隆) were completely different from those in Korea (朝鮮) or Japan. The buildings were all large and made of brick. Back in Korea, people were still wearing lined clothes (겹옷) in the mornings and evenings, but when I went up to the famous Kōshaku Park (高沙公園) in Keelung, the sound of cicadas was incredibly loud. Coming down from the park, I heard that bananas (빠나나) in Taiwan were very cheap, so I spent 10 sen (錢) to buy bananas and papayas (빠바야). They were indeed cheap. No one could possibly eat them all alone. I sat on a bench set up under a nice-looking coconut palm tree (椰子樹), ate my fill, and still had leftovers, which I ended up giving to a passing Taiwanese girl. At 10 AM, I took the train from Keelung Station (基隆驛) bound for Taipei (臺北). Arriving in Taipei, I took a bus (빠쓰) and went up to the park where the Taiwan Shrine (臺灣神社) is located (I forgot the name) to view the entire cityscape of Taipei (臺北市街 全景). Then I entered the Taiwan Zoo (臺灣動物園). Indeed, as this is a tropical region, there were many tropical animals I hadn't seen at the Changgyeongwon Zoo (昌慶苑動物園). In one place, there was a Korean wolf (朝鮮産 승량이(狼)). Afterwards, I visited the Taipei Botanical Garden (臺北植物園) and the Taipei Products Exhibition Hall (臺北 商品陳列舘) located within its grounds, where I saw beautiful tropical plants and all sorts of Taiwanese products. I then visited the Taiwan Governor-General's Office (臺灣總督府) and the nearby Taiwan Museum (臺灣博物舘). It seemed like there was nothing that wasn't in that museum. The living conditions and figures of Taiwanese indigenous people (臺灣生蕃) were recreated exactly as they are, giving the impression of seeing the indigenous people firsthand. In one section, models representing people from various regions under Japanese rule (日本 관활 아래) were displayed, including a Korean couple (朝鮮 사람의 한 부부). The man wore a large gat (갓) and official robes (朝服) with haenggeon (행건) and straw shoes (집신). The woman wore an extremely short jeogori (저고리), exposing her breasts (乳房) outside the garment. It was truly embarrassing (창피하엿습니다) to look at.

After that, I went to the largest department store (백화점) in Taipei to look at current Taiwanese products and tasted some tropical food. Wanting to understand a bit about the lifestyle and thoughts (心理思想) of the Taiwanese people, I decided to spend a night at a Taiwanese inn (臺灣人의 여관). Since I couldn't find a Taiwanese inn by myself, I hailed a rickshaw (人力車) and asked to be taken to the largest Taiwanese inn. The driver took me to a large inn called the Tainan-kan (臺南舘). Spending a night at this inn, I briefly learned about the psychology and thoughts of the Taiwanese people.

Dear beloved Hyŏng,

On the morning of the 10th, I visited fellow Koreans (조선동포) in Taipei (臺北). Here, there were Ginseng Trading Companies (人蔘商會) called Koryŏ Kongsa (高麗公司) and Sŏnhŭng Sa (鮮興社), and also restaurants (料理屋) named Chosŏn Nu (朝鮮樓) and Chosŏn Chŏng (朝鮮停). I found out that there are about 200 fellow Koreans in Taipei city alone. In Taiwan, the main livelihoods for fellow Koreans are ginseng trading and the restaurant business, and besides that, there are many medicine peddlers (藥行商). I then took a train to Taichung (臺中). The Taichung line (臺中線)<61> had been damaged in the recent earthquake (地震) and was not yet reopened, so I had to take the coastal line (海岸線). Arriving in Taichung, I spent 3 hours exploring the city streets* and visiting fellow Koreans there. Here too, there were several large restaurants (料理屋) run by fellow Koreans. Then I went to Tainan (臺南) and spent the night at a Japanese inn called Koyanagi Ryokan (小柳旅舘). On the 11th, I visited a Japanese missionary (日本人 使役者) who was starting our church's work in Tainan; he treated me very kindly. Together, we toured the famous sites (名勝地) of Tainan, and I received a generous lunch reception (占心對接) at the restaurant of Tainan's largest department store (데파트). Due to time constraints, I couldn't visit the fellow Koreans in Tainan. At 11 o'clock, I took the train bound for Kaohsiung (高雄).

Dear beloved Hyŏng,

As I had heard in Korea, the climate in Taiwan (臺灣氣候) is much hotter than in Korea. When I left Korea, the rice seedlings (벼모(稻苗)) were just starting to grow in the seedbeds, but when passing through southern Taiwan, the rice harvest (벼 추수) was already in full swing there. The Taiwanese farming villages (대만 농촌) along the railway line (鐵路沿邊) appeared much more developed than Korean farming villages (조선농촌). Most farmhouses (農家) visible from the train window were brick houses. Sights worth seeing in the countryside included dozens of water buffaloes (水牛) bathing together in streams, hundreds of domestic ducks (집오리) moving in flocks, and groups of 20-30 pigs (도야지) as large as cows gathered in one place – these were rare sights not seen in Korean villages. Arriving in Kaohsiung (高雄), I didn't have enough time, so I couldn't visit fellow Koreans and only briefly toured the city streets before boarding the Shikago Maru again for Manila. According to what I heard, there are 2 or 3 wealthy fellow Koreans (조선동포 재산가) in Kaohsiung Port who are doing well in everything. In Taichung City (臺中市), there is an association of fellow Koreans called the Chūtai Shin'yūkai (中臺親友會) organized with 25 members, and there is also an association of fellow Koreans organized in Kaohsiung Port. The East China Sea (東支那海) from Nagasaki to Taiwan was calm and very pleasant, but from the time we left Taiwan, the waves became somewhat rough, and being unfamiliar with life at sea, I was quite scared. A sight worth seeing in this South China Sea (南支那海) was flying fish (飛魚). Like flocks of swallows on an autumn day, hundreds of them would leap out of the sea in groups, fly for a while, and then dive back into the sea.

Dear beloved Hyŏng,

When I left Taiwan, I had only 1 won (圓) and 50 chon (錢) in cash. I wanted to send a telegram (電報) to the school from the ship, but the minimum telegram fee (電報料金) was 5 won, so I couldn't send one. I had already sent a letter, but in that letter, I had mentioned that I would inform them of my arrival time in Manila by telegram. What if no one from the school came out to meet (맛기) me? What if I encountered difficulties in that place where I don't know the language or customs (風俗), and without travel expenses (旅費)? Amidst such worries (念慮), the ship arrived in Manila on the morning of the 14th. After getting off the ship, no matter how many times I called out the name of the person I had written to earlier, he wasn't there. After paying 1 won <62> for unloading my luggage from the ship, I only had 50 chon left. While waiting for the luggage inspection at customs (稅關), not knowing what to do, an unfamiliar Western man noticed the Youth Volunteer Mission Society (靑年義勇宣敎會) emblem pinned to my suit and gladly shook my hand. I also gladly shook his hand. Oh, the Lord who loves me helped me. Riding in the car he brought, I arrived safely at my destination, this school. I'll end here for today and will write again later with news about this region and the school.

July 15th
Manila, Philippines
Philippine Union College
From the Men's Dormitory
Your younger brother, O Yŏng-sŏp

Dear beloved Hyŏng,

You must have been waiting eagerly for my letter (弟의 편지). I too have been thinking of writing to you since the day I arrived here, but I couldn't find the time to write. Thank you for the July issue of Samch'ŏlli (三千里) you sent; I received it thankfully. As you requested, Hyŏng, I am sending the travelogue (여행기) recorded from Kyŏngsŏng (京城) to here. Please read it, and if you wish, I will also record and send details about the customs here and the current state (現狀) of my life at this school.

At this school, Americans, Filipinos (比律賓사람), Chinese (中國사람), Malays (馬來사람), Japanese (日本사람), Spanish (西班牙사람), and Koreans (朝鮮사람) all study together. In the dormitory where I am staying, people from various countries are all together. Since arriving here, I haven't yet met (맛나 보지 못하엿습니다) any other Koreans. Currently, I face many difficulties (골란합니다) because I cannot communicate well in the language, but I am gradually improving.

Publisher's Note (社告)

The Philippine Presidential inauguration ceremony (比大統領의 취임식) is approaching in November (11월). Articles about this grand, historic event will be contributed by Mr. O (吳氏) and published in subsequent issues (號), so please look forward to them.<63>

**Summary**

This 1935 article by O Yŏng-sŏp, a Korean student traveling to the Philippines, is a personal letter detailing his journey from Korea via Japan (Moji, Nagasaki) and Taiwan (Keelung, Taipei, Taichung, Tainan, Kaohsiung) to Manila. He describes the bureaucratic hurdles of travel, life aboard the ship "Shikago Maru," vivid impressions of Taiwan's cities, landscape, and encounters with fellow Koreans residing there. The letter concludes with his arrival in Manila, initial financial anxiety solved by a chance encounter, and anticipation of the upcoming Philippine presidential inauguration, which he promises to report on further. From a Korean colonial history perspective, the article offers a rare firsthand account of a Korean traveling abroad, highlighting the procedures imposed by Japanese authorities (physical exams, restricted zones), observations on Japanese colonial rule in Taiwan (development, museums depicting subjects including an embarrassing portrayal of Koreans), and the presence and livelihoods (ginseng trade, restaurants) of Korean diaspora communities in Taiwan. The author's sense of identity as a Korean ("조선 사람," "동포") amidst diverse nationalities is palpable.

						(本社特別 寄稿員) 마닐나·빌립빈유니온大學 吳永燮
親愛하는 XX兄
지금 比律賓은 마치 熱病에 걸닌 것 가치 亢奮하고 잇슴니다. 그것은 大統領 就任式이 갓가워온 까닭이외다, 즉 比島聯邦의 독립준비가 작고 작고 進捗되어 역사 잇는 초대 대통령 취임식이 11월 15일 신국도 마닐나에서 성대하게 개최하기로 되야 比島내의 모든 단체와 인사는 준비에 분주하며 또 한편 멀니 太平洋浴岸의 각국은 물론, 歐羅巴 列强에까지 축하사절이 오리라고 신문은 보도함니다. 오늘까지 判明된 각국사절은 米國으로부터 현대통령 가-나-씨 이하 上下 兩院議員 47명이 오기고 되엇고 日本서는 貴族院議長 近衛公爵이나 德川公爵이 오리라 하며 中國으로서는 前外交部長 王正延씨가 오기로 되엇고 그 박게 英吉利 西班牙, 和蘭등 관계 각국에서 오리라 한 즉 실로 이번의 국가적 성의는 상상하기에 어렵지 안슴니다.(사진은 比島獨立黨員들)
그러기에 요지간의 首都 마닐다 市街에는 벌서부터 화려한 의복을 입은 외국 손님들이 만코, 시가의 건축물도 새로 짓는 것이 만흐며 中央通의 가로수는 새로 손질까지 하여 市街의 美觀을 놉게 하기에 분주함니다.
漢陽 城頭에서 兄으로 더보러 訣別 할 때 깁히 늣길 이곳 事情의 보도인지라<59>弟는 心誠을 하야 장차 제2보 第3報로써 大統領就任式 光景을 보도하여 드리 오리다.
그런데 들니는 말에 아모래도 이번 大統領은 아기날드 장군보다 上院議長 커즌씨가 有力하다고들 함니다.
親愛하는 XX兄
그만 執筆의 順序가 박기워 젓슴니다. 저는 그날 형과 작별하고 7월 5일 아츰에 大阪商船 門戶支店에 이르러 배표를 사려하엿더니 水上警察署에 가서 신체검사를 마터 가지고 오라기에 다시 水上署로 갓더니 외국 가는 사람은 橫濱이나 神戶, 長崎에서 미리 검사를 마터야 門司서 태이지 그러치 안으면 乘船 식힐 수 업다고 하더이다. 日后 외국 가려는 여행자는 미리미리 아러 둘만한 지식이더이라.
그래서 弟는 다시 長崎港에 가서 검사를 마터 가지고 다시 門司에 오니 하로를 느진 대신 배 떠날 시간까지는 여유가 잇기에 弟는 「매까리공원」에 올너가 門司全市를 구경하다가 故土에 편지 한 장 쓰려고 公園六角을 폔취에 안저 펜을 날닐때에 엇든 사복한분이 겨톄오더니 이곳은 要塞地이니까 사진이나 무엇이나 기록하는 것을 금한다고 하더이다. 이것도 초행자에게는 미리 조심할 경험인 줄 알엇나이다. 엇잿든 여러 가지 層折을 격고난 뒤 마닐나행의 배를 탓소이다.

사랑하는 兄이여
9일 아츰에 나는 臺灣基隆港에 倒着하엿는대 배가 항구에 도착될 때 2, 30 명의 臺灣사람들이 魚形으로 된<60> 적은 배를 타고 靑黑色의 의복에 이상한 農笠들을 쓰고 자기 배에 손림을 태위기 위하야 汽船가로 도라 단니면서 손림을 기다리는 광경은 참 이상한 感想이 이섯습니다. 基隆港에서 상륙하야 市街를 구경하엿는대 基隆市街와 상점들은 朝鮮이나 日本식과는 도모지 갓지 안습니다. 건물은 모다 벽돌노 큼직큼직하게 지엿습디다. 朝鮮에서는 아즉 아츰 져녁으로 겹옷을 닙어섯는대 基隆에서 유명한 高沙公園에를 올나 가니가 매암이 소래가 굉장히 요란하엿습니다. 公園에서 내려 오다가 臺灣에 빠나나가 하도 싸다고 하기에 10전을 내여 빠나나와 빠바야를 삿더니 과연 싸더이다. 혼자서는 누구던지 다 먹을 수 업슬 것입니다. 보기 조흔 椰子樹 밋헤 설비해 노흔 의자에 거러 안자서 실컷 먹고도 남아서 지나가는 臺灣 소녀를 불너서 주고 말앗습니다. 오전 10시에 基隆驛에서 臺北행 기차를 탓습니다. 臺北에 도착하야 니여 빠쓰를 타고 臺灣神社 잇는 公園(일흠이젓슴)으로 올나가서 臺北市街 全景을 바라 본 후 臺灣動物園에를 드러갓습니다. 과연 이곳은 열대지방이니 만큼 昌慶苑動物園에서 보지 못하든 열대지방동물이 만핫습니다. 한 곳에를 가니가 朝鮮産 승량이(狼) 한 마리가 잇더이다. 그 후에는 臺北植物園과 그 區內에 잇는 臺北 商品陳列舘을 구경하엿는대 보기 조흔 열대지방식물들과 臺灣産의 모든 물품들을 구경하엿습니다. 다시 臺灣總督府와 그 근처에 잇는 臺灣博物舘을 구경하엿는대 그 박물관 안에는 업는 것이 업는 것 갓햇습니다. 臺灣生蕃의 生活狀態와 인물을 그대로 다 만드러 노핫는대 生蕃들을 친히 보는 것과 갓튼 感想이엿습니다. 한 곳에를 가니가 日本 관활 아래 잇는 각지방 사람들이 模形을 만드러 노핫는대 朝鮮 사람의 한 부부도 이섯습니다. 남자는 큰 갓을 쓰고 朝服을 닙고 행건과 집신을 신흔 사람이엿고 여자는 심히 짧은 저고리를 닙어서 乳房이 저고리 밧갓흐로 나온 것을 만드러 노핫는대 보기에 참 창피하엿습니다.
그 후에는 臺北에서 제일 큰 백화점으로 가서 臺灣現商品들을 구경하고 열대지방음식을 조곰 맛본 후에 臺灣人의 生活狀態와 心理思想을 좀 알아 볼 마음이 이서서 臺灣人의 여관에서 한 밤을 지나기로 생각한 후 혼자서 대만인 여관을 차즐 수가 업슴으로 人力車를 불너타고 대만인의 제일 큰 여관을 가자고 하니가 臺南舘이라는 큰 여관으로 인도하더이다. 이 여관에서 한밤을 지나면서 대만인의 심리 사상을 잠간 알아 보앗습니다.
