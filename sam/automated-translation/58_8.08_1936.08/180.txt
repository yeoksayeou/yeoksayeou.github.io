Secrets of a Summer Night Sleeper Car
Beauty and White Bones
Yi Sŏ-gu

It was on the way back from Tokyo. Having finally arrived in Shimonoseki (下關), I had already bought a sleeper ticket for the Kanpu ferry (관부 연락선). As there were still three hours to spare before the ship's departure, I went into a pufferfish restaurant across from the Sanyō Department Store to eat the famous Shimonoseki pufferfish, which I always enjoy.<152> The habitual coquetry of the familiar waitress was welcoming enough to embrace this morning.

"You are the only Chosŏn (朝鮮) gentleman customer we have," she said, praising me as if I were representing the entire Chosŏn people or an international envoy visiting her establishment. Pufferfish sashimi arrived, pufferfish stew arrived, and pufferfish sake (ヒレサク - hiresake) arrived. My tolerance is less than one bottle. The stew came with a bowl of rice.

"Sir... today is an interesting day. Right next door, there are Chinese guests."
The kind-hearted old woman was busy all by herself. Indeed, Chinese speech drifted from the next room, a duet of soprano and baritone.
Father and daughter –
That would be nice.
Young man and beauty –
I hoped it wasn't so. A baseless thought.
"Are they husband and wife?"
When I asked, the old woman waved her hand dismissively.
"Heavens no. A man over 40 and a beauty of 18 or 19."
I smiled faintly.
Me: "Pufferfish for them too?"
Her: "No, just the standard meal."
Me: "Why not recommend the pufferfish?"
Her: "Goodness, do you want someone killed?"
Me: "Then, just slyly place the pufferfish on their table."
Her: "What would you say?"
Me: "Isn't the exquisite taste of pufferfish melting in a beauty's mouth precious?"
Her: "Stop it, ho ho..."

That was all that happened at this place. Then, using the remaining two hours, I rushed to a hotel and took a nap. I asked to be woken 20 minutes before the ship's departure, but they woke me 10 minutes before. Without time to get angry, I dashed onto the ship. "No. 8," second-class lower berth, a seat facing the open window. As I headed to the berth, I saw a Chinese gentleman and a lovely, Western-dressed beauty sitting on the seat installed opposite berth No. 8. I immediately thought,
"Oh, those must be the people from before."
She wore a black hat with a veil lowered, but through the veil <153> could be seen a jade-like face, large sparkling eyes, a sharp nose, and red, lovely lips. Perhaps because she had applied red rouge to both cheeks, she gave the impression of a doll. Her cut hair was curled up tightly; I guessed this must be the "electric perm" (전기지짐) that costs several tens of won at a time. The moment I saw her, I felt my heart tighten. I felt ashamed to approach her. I wanted to respect her. No, I already respected her. My heart felt itchy with curiosity.
"Born a daughter of nobility in a large, old, ailing country. Endless hardships, fortunes rising and falling like lightning. What business brings her from her homeland to Tokyo or Ōsaka and back...?" I grew inexplicably curious. She looked like the protagonist of some tragedy. Her eyes seemed ready to cry at any moment, her desolate figure looked exhausted from weeping, spiritless. The old gentleman sat like a prince of silence, his large mouth firmly shut, only his eyes rolling. The secret hidden in the hearts of these two people – a secret to me – I wanted to know that secret.
"Sir. What should I do now?"
If this young lady were to collapse onto my lap, weeping and confiding her sorrows, I would sell my name. I would sell the world. I would even sell my body.
"Elder brother (老兄 - rohyŏng), our family of two trusts only you."
If that closed mouth were to open and such a request were to emerge from the old gentleman's heart, I would have shouted "OK!" right then and there, whatever it took. The ship departed. It seemed they had bought the upper and lower berths opposite mine. First, the young lady took off her gray overcoat and, still wearing her hat, got into the berth. I briefly went to see Mr. *, whom I had met on the ship, where he was lying down, then immediately returned and got into my berth.

About two hours must have passed. I opened my eyes and looked out; the ship seemed to be in the middle of the Genkai Sea (현해). All around was quiet. On the opposite seat, the young lady, wearing only her nightclothes, sat cautiously reading a magazine. At first, I thought it was a magazine, but it was a Chinese Buddhist scripture. The old gentleman – I looked for him – was lying in the lower berth where the young lady had been, having switched places. The upper berth was empty. Why did they only use one, taking turns? The old man having the good fortune to lie where the young lady had slept. More than that, the unpleasant thought occurred that the lovely young lady would later lie where the old man had lain. No matter how I looked, she was 18 or 19, my age <154> was 38. If I had started early, I could have had a daughter that age.
"Well, I'm so curious, let's try talking to her."
Adopting the attitude of a benevolent father towards a young daughter, I abruptly kicked off my covers and stepped out of the berth. Our eyes met. She didn't quite give a radiant smile (쌩끗-생끗). It was more of a slight, shy smile (빵끗-) as she composed herself. I stretched once, feigning utmost nonchalance, and struck up a conversation:
"The passage is very smooth."
The young lady glanced up again.
"Yes, it's very quiet."
Her speech was clearly learned in Tokyo. Thus, surrounded by the sound of waves and snores, sitting on the narrow seat in the middle of the berths where no sound could be heard, we continued talking in her not-so-fluent Tokyo Japanese until the old gentleman woke up. Her father was the famous nobleman Mr. OOO from Peking (北京), who had taken refuge in a separate villa (별부?) and recently passed away. The daughter, who attended an Anglo-French girls' school (영불 화녀학교?) in Tokyo, met her uncle who was waiting at the villa, and though unwillingly, she had to perform the cremation (화장) and was now heading to Peking to bury the bones in the ancestral burial ground (선산).
Me: "Your father's remains (백골)..."
Her: "They are in the bag placed in the upper berth."
That was why the curtain on the upper berth remained drawn and untouched, and they only used the lower berth in turns. If they had bought three berths, the porter (뽀이) might have become suspicious and questioned them, so the two of them bought two berths and placed the bag with the remains in the upper one. Who would know that in berth No. 8 [Translator's note: The text says 18 here, but 8 previously. Likely a typo.], the remains (백골) of a fallen hero who once influenced the destiny of China were enshrined? <155>

The Secret-Laden Night Sleeper Car
Pok Hye-suk

Sleeper cars (寢台車), you mean? I've ridden sleeper cars quite a lot. I tend to travel relatively often, but usually, it's for filming or other group activities, so many people ride the train together. However, it has become a habit for me to always go to the sleeper car alone, stretch out my arms and legs wide, and spend the night.

But once, I saw something so funny. It was so amusing that I remember being astonished, thinking, "Was the sleeper car this kind of place too?" It's still vivid before my eyes.

Once, I had occasion to go towards the Kantō region (關東) and boarded a train. As usual, I couldn't sit up all night, so I went to the first-class sleeper car. My body was very tired, and as I stretched out my limbs on the seat and was about to drift off to sleep, quite unexpectedly, from the adjacent berth, the strange sound of a man and woman whispering affectionately reached my ears!

My eyes, which had been drowsily falling into a sweet sleep, popped open at that moment. Suppressing my slightly pounding heart and holding my breath, I listened intently, straining my ears in that direction, when suddenly a porter (뽀이) stealthily approached their berth. It must have been well past midnight. It was a very quiet time, with only the sound of people snoring deeply.

"...Excuse me! Uh, just a quick question..."
Before the porter could even finish his sentence, another voice said,
"Aigoo, I'm sleepy! I was just about to fall asleep..."
This voice clearly belonged to a young woman.
After a short pause, again,
"Sorry! Yes? Yes?"
the porter replied, almost confrontationally, as if half-asleep. Then he just seemed to stand there.

Being so curious, I stealthily raised myself from my sleeping position and peeked through the gap in the curtain (휘장).
Sure enough! From the other side, through a gap in their curtain, a woman's pale hand emerged, holding a 5-won bill. The porter glanced around quickly, snatched it, put it away, mumbled something under his breath, barely acknowledging it, <156> grinned, and left.

I was utterly baffled for a moment, not knowing what was going on. Because I was so curious and couldn't sleep due to the whispering sounds, I stayed awake all night. No matter how much I thought about it, there hadn't seemed to be anyone traveling with the woman. Wondering what was happening, I got up early in the morning, found the porter, and, casting aside decorum regarding yesterday night's "incident," asked him who the woman was. The porter hesitated slightly, then smiled faintly and said,
"She's a regular customer on this train..."
Then he scurried away evasively.

So, I couldn't ask any more questions and just kept wondering about it inwardly. Later, I told this story to a gentleman I knew, mentioning that I had only seen the woman's wrist and heard her voice on the night train and hadn't seen her at all, even when getting off the train. That adult replied,
"There are women who 'work' the sleeper cars!"
Only then did I realize what kind of "woman" she was.

Another time, something like this happened.
I was taking the train bound for Fengtian (奉天) for an overnight journey. I couldn't sleep, so I went out between the cars to get some night air. I saw the conductor arguing with a Manchukuo (滿洲國) woman. Pretending not to notice, I listened from nearby. It turned out the woman had fallen asleep and missed her stop, and the conductor was telling her to pay extra and get off at the next station to go back. The woman argued that she didn't have a single penny on her, and it was escalating into a fight when a seemingly very sympathetic gentleman stepped forward, saying,
"...Isn't it enough just to give her the money!....."
He then gave the woman money for her return fare. While his giving the money was very kind, what happened next was quite a spectacle! After the conductor left, the two whispered something to each other, then held hands and went into the sleeper car.

It makes one think, "So this is what a night sleeper car is like!" It seems plausible, doesn't it... ho ho... <157>

An Intimate Social Venue
Chŏng Sŏk-t'ae

As someone who particularly enjoys traveling, I often take trains and have ridden in sleeper cars many times. However, Asian sleeper cars are American-style, with multiple berths in one car, making them communal. While they might be convenient, they lack an intimate and quiet atmosphere.

In contrast, European-style (歐羅巴式) sleeper cars typically accommodate only 2 or perhaps 4 people per compartment at most, as they are all separate rooms, making them exceptionally quiet and intimate.

Moreover, if the 2 or 3 passengers in one compartment agree, they can simply close the door from the inside and lock it (쇳대). Inside, they can do whatever they please, and it doesn't matter what kind of gambling they engage in. Truly, the "European-style" sleeper car can be called akin to a quiet inner room (안방) of a home, and it also serves as an intimate social venue for young people.

Therefore, in Europe (歐羅巴), there are indeed many romances that begin in sleeper cars, and many secrets unfold within them.
The secrets within the sleeper cars rushing through the pitch-black night (漆黑暗夜) every night are known only to the parties involved.
Who could possibly spy on the songs, dances, and whispers of love happening inside the tightly shut sleeper car doors?

During my long stay in Germany (獨逸), I rode sleeper cars several times. They were indeed quiet and intimate places.
Westerners, regardless of age, rarely travel alone and solitarily. Except for the sick or in special circumstances, it is the norm to travel as a couple or with a loved one. If there is a man or woman traveling alone, they inevitably arouse the curiosity of others. Furthermore, it is an iron rule for Westerners to wear a ring on the ring finger of their left hand after marriage. If a woman or man lacks that ring, they are subject to receiving romantic advances from the opposite sex. They must also be prepared for such possibilities beforehand.

When I was studying in Germany, I took a sleeper car on the night train from Paris (巴里) to Berlin (伯林). The sleeper car I boarded was for four people, but as it happened, there were only two of us. Across from where I sat alone, a woman who appeared to be married (부인) was sitting alone.

Of course, being an Oriental (東洋人), I had no thought of engaging this Western lady (?) in conversation and was lying down alone in my berth reading a book.
The woman (who seemed married) sat gazing blankly for a while, then quietly got up, securely locked the sleeper car door, and returned. Some time later, I turned my eyes from my book and looked towards the opposite berth where the woman was. She was also reading a book, and by chance, our eyes met.

At that moment, I clearly detected a faint smile crossing her face. I could instantly perceive that this smile spoke of melancholy and loneliness.
The night was probably at its deepest point. Outside the curtain (카-텡), only the flickering lights of drowsy electric lamps flashed past in the pitch darkness. All around, there was only the rumbling sound of the train running on the rails (네-루? rails/레일); the earth seemed to have ceased breathing.

Some time later, I was surprised to notice the absence of the ring that should have been on either her left or right ring finger. (In many countries, it's worn on the left after marriage, but in Germany (獨逸), it's a characteristic feature to wear it on the left during engagement and the right (ring finger) after marriage.)
"A strange woman!" I inwardly exclaimed at that moment.
That night, I was able to pass the time engaging in pleasant conversation with her. Early the next morning, guided by her, we went to the dining car and had breakfast together.
At that time, I committed an extremely unexpected discourtesy towards her.
I mishandled my knife (나이푸), and liquid from the food splattered onto the back of her hand. <159> She merely smiled faintly once and quickly wiped it off with her handkerchief, but for me, it was a terrible blunder. At that moment, I couldn't even utter a single word of apology; I just blushed deeply.
As she wiped the droplets off the back of her hand, I could faintly discern the mark where a ring had clearly been worn on her right ring finger (無名指).
I was startled once again.
I realized the woman was trying to treat me with excessive kindness (好意). However, at that time, I was rather quite cold (冷情).
After that, whenever I rode a train, I would always think of that woman.
Even now, the one thing that remains regrettable, almost to the point of feeling sorry (未安), is that I failed to say even a single word like "Excuse me (失禮). I am truly sorry (未安)," to her who had been so kind. It still weighs on my mind.

Sweet Dreams Found in Solitude
Kim Yŏn-shil

I like solitude. Always, in any situation, spending time quietly all by myself is what I enjoy most and find most pleasant.
Perhaps it's because I grew up lonely from a young age, always desolate amidst solitude-.
I have ridden trains very often. Due to my involvement in the film world (영화계) and theater world (劇界) since childhood, and more recently entering the recording industry (레코-드계), I often travel by train or ship with many people when going on location (로케이숀) or for recording sessions (취입).
However, perhaps because I have a habit of liking solitude, I always avoid places with many people and seek out quiet spots. Although I am a woman of weak constitution, I rarely get headaches or feel nauseous on trains or ships, but I habitually lean against the sleeper car (寢台車), gaze at the scenery unfolding outside the window, and always pass the time quietly.
At such times, I naturally find myself reviewing all the tangled matters of my past life, and I also spread the wings of endless imagination about the future. This is always the most enjoyable and quiet moment in my tiring life. Just having such quiet and leisurely time is incredibly welcome.
Leaning my head sideways against the train window, lost in endless thought, I somehow gently walk into the land of dreams before I know it.
Dreams dreamed in a sleeper car! Those dreams are truly sweet dreams. I don't know how many times I've had such dreams. They aren't disgusting, skin-crawling dreams like seeing dreadful snakes (구렝이); they were always happy dreams of whispering about youth's joys under the deep shade of lush green trees.
Just as I like solitude, I also have a nature that enjoys dreams. The night in a sleeper car is truly desolate (살풍경). Perhaps I'm not the only one who thinks so. People sprawling here and there, snoring; people with pale faces from fatigue, full of drawn-out yawns; people sighing heavily, unable to sleep, tossing and turning this way and that, furrowing their brows – it looks like a world filled only with crowds who have suffered a great war or calamity. Yet even amidst this, gazes are constantly exchanged, and sly smiles appear amid peculiar flirtatious glances (추파) – this too is a strangely desolate scene (얄구진 살풍경).
If a close friend were to see me sitting alone (욋드름이) amidst such a desolate scene, that friend would surely regard me with puzzlement.
It's probably natural that I often hear comments that my appearance, as seen in a sleeper car, seems too cold, like an autumn lake. <161>
I have received countless gifts of inexplicable smiles from unknown young men sitting beside me, and I have often experienced warm affection that I haven't even found from my parents or siblings, but each time, my emotions, unbeknownst to me, turn cold. Sometimes, faced with excessive kindness, I feel so apologetic that I inwardly scold my mummy-like (미이라/木乃伊) attitude.
Will I follow solitude forever? And will I only enjoy sweet dreams?
I want to love solitude and "dreams" throughout my life.

A Single Bright Blue Jade Bead
Kim Sŏl-bong

A single flaw-free, bright blue jade (翡翠) bead, like an autumn sky without a single cloud!
This one small jade bead makes me unable to forget that unknown man, even now.
It happened when I followed my older brother (옵바), who was attending technical college (전문학교), up to Seoul and was attending the third year at a certain girls' high school (녀자고보). So, I think I was 17 years old.
When summer vacation started, I boarded the Kyŏngbu line train, but there were so many passengers that finding a seat was impossible. So, inevitably, I went to the sleeper car (寢台車) to spend the night.
Of course, that was the very first time I had ever ridden in a sleeper car. I wore a white Chosŏn dress (朝鮮 옷) with the school's emblem (마-크) neatly attached above my left breast (젓가슴). My face and <162> figure (몸 맵세) were truly innocent back then.
The sleeper car was also crowded, so I barely found a spot in a corner and leaned back. Right next to where I sat, a young man was leaning back alone, reading a book.
Even now, I sometimes hear words like this, almost reproachfully, from men:
"...There's a somewhat icy (차듸찬) look about your face."
In fact, sometimes when I face the mirror and look at my own face, I notice an excessively cold aura and feel frustrated, even worrying myself sick (가슴을 조바서 보기까지 함니다), wondering if I can't project a more affectionate and gentle fragrance towards men.
However, on the other hand, when my mood brightens, there are also times when I am excessively warm and affectionate towards people. I am well aware that this dual personality of melancholy and cheerfulness, coldness and affection, exerts an overly strong influence on my mind at times. Perhaps it's an inescapable nature inherited from my mother's womb (배ㅅ 속).
It's like that even now, so what more needs to be said about back then (my 18-year-old schoolgirl days)?
Anyway, that man was a graduate of a university in Kyoto (京都), returning home after a study trip to Manchuria (滿洲). He expressed great regret that he couldn't stop in Chosŏn (朝鮮) to see various aspects of the country, and he asked me, sitting next to him, various questions in detail. <163>
As a young schoolgirl, I initially felt somewhat awkward and even blushed when addressed by a strange man, but after exchanging words for a while, I realized, before I knew it, that the man was becoming quite friendly towards me.
He was neatly dressed in a Western suit (양복/背廣) and looked gentle; even with my young judgment at the time, I could easily tell he was likely the son of a wealthy family.
Although I wasn't particularly tired, the man must have been extremely fatigued from his long journey, yet he stayed beside me, seemingly unaware of the deepening night, talking throughout the night without showing any sign of leaving.
The next morning, as I was preparing to get off at Samnangjin (三浪津), that unknown man came over to my side and pressed a slender (개룸한?), bright blue jade bead into my hand. I was so taken aback by this unexpected event that my heart pounded fiercely.
After that, the man sent a picture postcard to my hometown address from Kyoto once, I think.
After that, I forgot about him, and it seems he completely forgot about me too. Now, even his name has vanished from my memory.
Later, after graduating from school, I became someone who traveled around Fengtian (奉天) and Dalian (大連). Dalian is famous for its abundance of jewels. Once, when I entered the Dalian department store, I discovered there a beautiful, flawless, bright blue jade identical to the one I received from the unknown man I first met on the sleeper car. Beside the jewel, I clearly saw a price tag of 200 won attached. At that moment, I finally realized how valuable that jade bead he had affectionately pressed into my hand that day was.
Now, I don't know where that man is, nor can I tell if I gave the jade bead he gave me to some friend or if I lost it.
However, even now, when I pass by a jewelry store and gaze at jade beads as neat and vividly green as sprouting lawn grass, the image of that unknown man who was so kind in the sleeper car that day fleetingly passes through my mind. <164>

**Summary**

The article compiles five short reminiscences by different authors (Yi Sŏ-gu, Pok Hye-suk, Chŏng Sŏk-t'ae, Kim Yŏn-shil, Kim Sŏl-bong) about their experiences in sleeper cars during the 1930s. The anecdotes range from Yi Sŏ-gu's poignant encounter with a young Chinese noblewoman transporting her father's ashes, Pok Hye-suk's observations of suspected prostitution and ambiguous encounters on trains, Chŏng Sŏk-t'ae's reflections on the contrasting intimacy of European versus American-style sleepers and a mysterious interaction, Kim Yŏn-shil's appreciation of the sleeper car's solitude for introspection despite the surrounding "desolation," to Kim Sŏl-bong's nostalgic memory of receiving a valuable jade bead from a kind Japanese stranger met on her first sleeper car journey as a student.

From a colonial history perspective, the article offers valuable glimpses into the lives of the Korean elite and middle class, showcasing their mobility within the Japanese Empire (travel between Korea, Japan, Manchuria) and their interactions across nationalities (Korean, Chinese, Japanese). It subtly reveals social dynamics influenced by the colonial context, touching upon class, gender relations (solicitation, unwanted attention towards women), cross-cultural encounters, and the unique atmosphere of travel infrastructure like sleeper cars that connected disparate parts of the empire. Historians might find the detailed portrayal of these personal experiences, social observations, and the casual backdrop of inter-regional travel useful for understanding everyday life and social nuances during the period.

동경 갓다오는 길이엇다. 마침내 하관(下關)에 도착한 나는 관부 연락선의 침대권은 미리 사노홧겟다. 배 떠날 시간까지는 세시간이나 여유가 잇기로 항상 조화하는 하관 명물 복어를 먹으랴고 산양백화점 마즌 편 복어 요리집으로＜152＞ 드러갓다. 낫익은 하녀의 상습적 애교도 이 아츰에 만은 가슴에 숨일 만치 반가웟다.

나는 지금도 각금 사나히들에게 이런 꾸지람 비슷한 말을 듯는 일이 종종 잇슴니다.
「... 그 얼골에는 어뒨지 차듸찬 빛이 흐르는데요.」
사실 나도 때때로 거울을 마조하고 내 얼골를 듸려다 보면, 넘우나 쌀쌀한 빛이 떠도는 것을 엿보고 좀 더 사나히들에게 다정스럽고 부드러운 향긔를 던저 줄 수 업슬가 하고 안타가우리만치 가슴을 조바서 보기까지 함니다.
그러나 그런 반면에 마음이 명랑스러워질 때에는 넘우나 지나치게 사람에게 따뜻하게 굴고 정다웁게 구는 때가 또한 잇담니다. 아마도 나는 우울과 명랑, 냉정과 다정의 이중성격이 때때로 내 마음을 넘우나 심하게 지배하고 잇슴을 잘 알고 잇슴니다. 아마도 어머님의 배ㅅ 속에서 타고난 어쩔 수 없는 천성인가 봄니다.
지금도 그러니 그 때(18세의 여학생 시절)야 더 말해 무엇하겟서요.
그런데 그 사나히는 경도(京都) 어느 대학을 졸업하고 滿洲에 시찰로 갓다가 다시 고향으로 돌아가는 길인데 朝鮮에 들여서 朝鮮의 여러 가지 사정을 구경 못하는 것이 퍽 유감스러운 일이라고 하며 겻헤 앉은 나에게 여러 가지를 차곡차곡 뭇는 것이엿슴니다.＜163＞
아직 나 어린 여학생의 몸으로 처음에는 웬 아지도 못하는 사나히에게서 말을 받을 때 얼마간 서먹서먹 하기도 하며 얼골도 붉혀 보앗지마는 얼마동안 말을 주고 받고 하는 동안 아지 못하는 사이에 그 사나히는 나에게 퍽도 갓갑게 구는 것을 깨달엇슴니다.
양복(背廣)을 말숙하게 입엇고 얌전스럽게 보-이는 그 사나히는 분명히 어느 돈 많은 집 아드님이라는 생각이 그 때의 내 어린 판단력으로도 넉넉히 알 수가 잇섯슴니다.
나는 별로히 피곤치 않지마는 그 사나히는 오랜 여행에서 몹시 피로할 텐데 밤이 깊어가는 줄도 몰으고 내 옆에서  떠날 쭐을 몰으고 밤새두록 이야기하고 잇섯슴니다.
이튼 날 새벽 내가 三浪津에서 내릴 준비를 하든 때 그 미지의 사나히는 내 엽흐로 오드니 개룸한 샛파란 비취알 한 개를 내 손에 꼭 쥐워주는군요. 나는 넘우나 뜻박게 일에 가슴만 몹시 두군거리엿슴니다.
그 뒤 그 사나히는 京都에 가서도 내 고향집으로 그림 엽서를 보낸 적이 한 번인가 잇섯슴니다.
그런 뒤에는 나도 그를 잊게 되엿고 그도 나를 영영 잊은 모양임니다. 지금은 그의 일홈 조차도 기억에서 사라저 버리엿슴니다.
그뒤 나는 학교을 졸업하고 奉天으로 大連으로 돌아단이는 몸이 되엿슴니다. 大連은 보석 많기로 유명한 곳임니다. 언젠가 大連 백화점에 들어가보니 그 곳에는 내가 침대차에서 처음 만난 그 미지의 사나히에게서 어든 한알의 비취와 똑 갓흔 틔하나 없는 새파란 아름다운 비취를 발견하엿슴니다. 그 보석 옆에는 분명이 200원의 가격이 붙어 잇는 것도 보앗슴니다. 그 순간 나는 전날에 그가 다정스럽게 쥐여주든 그 비취 알이 얼마나 값가는 것인가도 비로소 알게 되엿슴니다.
지금은 그 사나히가 잇는 곳도 모르지마는 그 사나히가 주던 비취알도 어느 동무를 주엇는지 그러치 많으면 잊엇는지 조차 알 수 업게 되엿슴니다.
그러나 지금도 보석상 앞을 지나다가 샛파라케 돋아 오르는 잔듸풀 같이 말쑥한 비취알을 듸려다 볼 때면 불현듯이 그 전날 침대차 속에서 다정스럽게 굴든 미지의 그 사나히의 면영이 지나처 버리곤 함니다.＜164＞
