잡지명	삼천리 제8권 제8호
발행일	1936년 08월 01일
기사제목	湖心 (Heart of the Lake)
필자	申不出
기사형태	시 (Poem)

湖心
						申不出
늙은 湖水로다 어허늙은 湖水로다
네일즉 어느바다 뛰여노든 물이드니
지금에 뜨내기손의 구경감이 됏느냐

Old lake, alas, old lake!
Which sea were you the water that once leaped and played in?
Have you now become a spectacle for fleeting visitors?

달빛을 등에지고 溪谷도 흘렀드니
배전을 부드치며 大河도 흘렀드니
지나일 꿈만역이고 편이예와 쉬노라

Carrying the moonlight on my back, I flowed through valleys;
Striking against the boat's side, I flowed through great rivers.
Considering past days but a dream, I came here easily to rest.

흘너야 물이겠나 고였다면 물아니리
길만 툭터지면 浩浩蕩蕩 않흐르리
저물도 날과같아야 때오기만 긔두네

Must it flow to be water? If stagnant, is it not water?
If only the path were wide open, wouldn't it flow vast and boundless (浩浩蕩蕩)?
That water, like me, only awaits its time to come.

익끼야 꼇을망정 물빛이야 變할소냐 ＜273＞
바람불면 波濤일고 靑山明月 빛이는법
오히려 거울같아서 節介더욱 맑구나

Though moss (익끼) may have grown, how could the water's color change (變할소냐)? <273>
When the wind blows, waves (波濤) rise; it's the way blue mountains (靑山) and the bright moon (明月) are reflected.
Rather, it is like a mirror, its integrity (節介) all the clearer.

시럽슨 나그네가 더진돌두 받었노라
돌마저 아푼가슴 주룸으로 웃었노라
한밤에 찬달을품고 우는속을 뉘아노

A senseless traveler received even the thrown stone.
Even the stone struck a painful heart, which smiled through wrinkles (주룸).
Who knows the weeping heart that embraces the cold moon in the middle of the night?

流
						
險한 돌길이라 急한 물소린저
휘돌아 구비치고 스며가는 길이로되
바다로 향하는맘은 變할줄이 없느니

Flow
						
A perilous (險한) rocky path, oh the sound of rushing water!
Though the path twists, turns, and seeps through,
The heart aiming for the sea knows no change (變할줄이 없느니).

쏘다저 용소슴처 감돌다 갈너지고
부드처 깨젔다도 다시엉겨 흐르느니
앞섰다 뒤를슬망정 서로끌고 미드라＜274＞

Gushing, soaring like a dragon, winding, splitting,
Crashing and breaking, yet gathering again to flow.
Though one goes ahead and another lags behind, they pull and push each other. <274>
＜273-274＞

**Summary**

This document presents two poems, "Heart of the Lake" (湖心) and "Flow" (流), by Sin Puch'ul, published in the Samch'ŏlli magazine in August 1936. "Heart of the Lake" uses the metaphor of a stagnant lake, once part of a dynamic sea, to reflect on a state of forced stillness and being observed by others. Despite surface changes like moss, its essential nature remains clear, holding inner sorrow while awaiting the chance to flow freely again. "Flow" depicts the persistent journey of water towards the sea, overcoming obstacles through twists, turns, breaks, and reunification, emphasizing relentless forward movement and mutual support. From a colonial history perspective, these poems are potentially rich in allegory. The lake's condition could symbolize Korea under Japanese rule – its past dynamism contrasted with present stagnation, its enduring spirit beneath the surface, and a suppressed longing for freedom ("awaiting its time"). The relentless "Flow" towards the sea despite hardships might represent the aspiration for national liberation or survival, highlighting perseverance and collective struggle. The author's name, Sin Puch'ul (申不出 - "Sin Will Not Emerge/Go Out"), could itself be a poignant reflection of the constrained circumstances of intellectuals during that era.
