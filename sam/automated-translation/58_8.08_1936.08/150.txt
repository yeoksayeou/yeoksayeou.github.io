Magazine Title: 三千里 제8권 제8호
Publication Date: 1936년 08월 01일
Article Title: 畵家가 「美人」을 말함 (Artists Speak of "Beauty")
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Roundtable Discussion)

**Body Text Translation:**
Artists Speak of "Beauty"
Topic
1. Where do painters find beauty?
2. Which author's works are good for creating illustrations?
3. Are contemporary settings or historical settings easier for drawing illustrations?
4. Tales of hardship in finding a beauty "model".
5. Words you wish to say to general society and authors.

Attendees
Yi Sangbŏm (Tong-a Ilbo)
An Sŏgyŏng (Chosŏn Ilbo)
No Suhyŏn (Chungang Ilbo)
Yi Sŭngman (Maeil Sinbo)
Our Company's Kim Tonghwan
Pak Sanghŭi

July 7th, 4 PM to 6 PM at the rear terrace of the "Chosŏn Hotel" in Hasegawa-chō (長谷川町), Seoul <116>

Where do painters find beauty?
Kim Tonghwan (Our Company) - I am happy to have gained this opportunity to gather four painters, who are most distinctly active in our art world, in one place like this and fully discuss various issues regarding our art scene, especially the world of illustrations in newspapers and magazines. As you see, the four gentlemen who have come here are all people who entered the newspaper and magazine illustration scene quite a long time ago, and it is no exaggeration to say that they are the ones who have pioneered virgin territory as illustration painters in Chosŏn. Therefore, I know that within your hearts (胸中), there are many things you wish to say to our society as artists, particularly as illustration artists, and accordingly, I believe there must be countless unique hardships and sorrows (悲哀) as illustrators. However, as there has never been an opportunity in our society to gather the highest authorities of illustration painting like yourselves in one place and hear various beneficial and interesting words, now that we have created such an opportunity, though it may be modest, please speak frankly about whatever you wish to say. First, I believe painters often think about beauties; where do you truly seek beauty? For example, do you find true beauty among the uniformed girls coming out of the school gate? Or do you find true beauty among professional women like kisaeng who stay up all night amidst red lights and green wine (紅燈綠酒), or perhaps among the noblewomen (令夫人) deeply hidden within the inner chambers (閨中)?
Yi Sangbŏm - Asking where one finds true beauty is essentially asking to state the standard of <117> modern beauty, but the ways of seeing beauty all differ, and the standards are all different. For instance, a kisaeng possesses beauty as a kisaeng, a female student as a female student, and a married woman as a married woman; each has her own unique beauty, I would say.
An Sŏgyŏng - That's right. It's not like there's some fixed standard. If a woman, somehow even just looking from the outside, appears to have character in her face or bearing, seems knowledgeable, constantly thinking about and judging matters, and has sharp intellectual power, she would be called the most beautiful woman. A woman possessing such noble beauty naturally reveals that kind of beauty somewhere in her eyes or face. We illustrators too, when walking down the street or in some situation, if we discover a beauty who seems to possess such qualities, we observe her two or three times and keep her in mind, inevitably using her as a subject when we need to draw a certain female protagonist.
Kim Tonghwan - Sŏgyŏng! So, where can such beauties be found?
An Sŏgyŏng - Well, they can occasionally be discovered in places like tea rooms or bars, and seen on the streets, but generally in Chosŏn, perhaps because true beauties don't roam the streets, if you want to find the kind of beauty I mentioned, you probably have to go stand in front of a school gate and look for them among the uniformed girls carrying their book bags (책보) and walking lightly (삽분삽분).
Yi Sangbŏm - Then Sŏgyŏng must often visit girls' school gates looking for beauties? (All laugh)
No Suhyŏn - To find beauties in Chosŏn, you still really have to look among the kisaeng to discover true beauties.
Yi Sŭngman - That's right. There are indeed true beauties among the kisaeng. Where else are there beauties who catch the eye? Even on the streets, the beauties who first catch one's eye are usually kisaeng... But this is a slightly different story, but Chosŏn women, regardless of their class, have one point where they fall short of becoming beauties compared to women of other societies. The other day, I went to Kyŏngsŏng Station (京城驛) because a guest was arriving from Tokyo (東京), and many female students from various city schools came out, seemingly as a welcome. Among them were Japanese (日本) female students from the mainland and also Chosŏn female students, but the most noticeable thing was that, generally, the Chosŏn female students seemed to have absolutely no radiance (윤택) in their faces. They all looked rough (꺼-츨하고) and lacked oiliness (기름끼). Of course, this might be due to growing up in constrained living conditions related to living standards, housing systems (가옥제도), nutrition issues (영양관게), <118> and various other factors, but in that aspect, Chosŏn women's skin (살결), one of the biggest conditions for becoming beautiful, was the most disappointing to observers.
No Suhyŏn - That is true. Not just female students, but generally, the faces of Chosŏn people, compared to people from other places, lack healthy color (혈색) and radiance, which is unavoidable due to livelihood issues. However, instead, one thing that stands out compared to female students from other places is the abundance of beautiful leg lines (각선미).
An Sŏgyŏng - That's right. Chosŏn women's leg lines are much better. It would probably be fine to say they are the best among East Asian women. Looking at women from other places, it's truly laughable. If five women walk down the street, four of them have legs that are thick then thin, which is quite off-putting.

Which author's works are good for creating illustrations?
Kim Tonghwan - Well then, I can guess roughly where you all find beauties. Next, since you are all individuals who have worked at newspaper companies for over 10 years each and have strived to illustrate many works by various authors, I believe you now know well which author's work is easy to illustrate or difficult. If you could pour it all out without reservation on this occasion, I believe it would be a great help to general authors and also provide much realization for the readers. First, Mr. Yi Sŭngman, please speak. How are Yŏm Sangsŏp's (廉想涉) novels?
Yi Sŭngman - Yŏm Sangsŏp's works are <119> quite good for illustrating. Of course, it's partly because we meet often and coordinate, and also because he doesn't write the novel in short snippets but writes long pieces, giving ample time to fully read the novel once and have leisure to draw the illustration, allowing me to grasp the appearance and character of the protagonists appearing in the work to some extent, so it's very easy. I've drawn quite a lot for his works, relatively speaking, but they were all mostly easy to draw. Among them, I also did the illustrations for "Morangkkot Toel Ttae" (『모랑꽃 될 때』 - When the Peony Blooms), published years ago, and that was particularly easy. Well, since I get to meet him often, whenever there's a chance, he frequently tells me how he wants the characters in the work drawn.
An Sŏgyŏng - That's right. If the novelist, after writing the work, could just tell the person drawing the illustration roughly what kind of people the characters appearing in the work are, it would make finding the unifying point of the entire work possible, and knowing what kind of character and type of human they are would make drawing much easier. But how many authors understand that point? In my view, Tokkyŏn (獨鵑) is the one who has the most understanding towards illustrators. Even now, there aren't many authors who show understanding towards illustrators, but Tokkyŏn, even from the time he wrote "Sŭngbang Pigok" (「僧房悲曲」), frequently talked to the illustrator about the protagonist, making it very easy to draw. Besides him, Ch'unwŏn's (春園) works are also quite easy. Since he is such a veteran author (老大作家), he tends to understand the illustrator's hardships, and also, the novel seems to provide suitable material for illustrating each installment. The illustrations for "Kŭ Yŏja ŭi Ilsaeng" (「그 女子의 一生」), which was published some time ago, I think turned out quite well myself.
No Suhyŏn - Tokkyŏn's works are indeed easy. Although "Yŏnae Pisangsŏn" (「戀愛非常線」), currently being published, is the first of his works I've encountered, it's not difficult like those of other authors. However, perhaps because I've drawn them often, Yi T'aejun's (李泰俊) works are the best among those I've illustrated. His works have soft lines and few appearing characters, giving a concise feel, making them very easy. I've dealt with his works several times, and they are mostly like that.
Yi Sangbŏm - I drew for Pinghŏ's (憑虛) work "Chŏkto" (「赤道」), and the <120> male protagonist in it had to be drawn like someone who came out of prison, but I couldn't find a model and had no experience, so I just roughly guessed in my mind, thinking if I drew like this, he might look like someone out of prison, and I drew it. It seems the protagonist's face was unified from beginning to end. Besides that, for Ch'unwŏn's "Yi Sunsin Chŏn" (「李舜臣傳」) too, although he's an ancient figure, I just drew him like a famous general, and in that case too, I managed not to lose the unity of the protagonist's face until the very end.
Yi Sŭngman - Everyone has only mentioned the easy aspects, but I'll talk about the difficult ones. Without specifying which author, generally in scenes depicting the countryside, there are many instances where the author's description is distinctly different from the actual rural scenery. Seeing only what appears in the work, it differs from reality, making such cases quite awkward. Recently, while drawing illustrations for works by Chosŏn female authors serialized in the Osaka Mainichi (大阪每日) Chosŏn edition, the actual rural scenery and the described facts appearing in the works were somewhat different, making it quite difficult. Such cases occur frequently.
An Sŏgyŏng - By the way, looking at novels published in newspapers and such in the Tokyo area, the portion published each day provides suitable material for drawing an illustration. This shows that newspaper novels there have reached a much higher standard. People writing novels for Chosŏn journalism should also have that much consideration and understanding for the convenience of the illustrator.

Are contemporary settings or historical settings easier for drawing illustrations?
Kim Tonghwan - Lately, among the novels published in Chosŏn newspapers and magazines, there's a growing trend of historical pieces (時代物). I believe there must be many difficulties in illustrating these historical pieces. So, generally, are contemporary settings or historical settings easier to illustrate? Ch'ŏngjŏn (靑田 - Yi Sangbŏm's art name), please speak first. I believe you must have quite a lot of experience with historical illustrations. When drawing illustrations like those for Yun Paengnam's (尹白南) "Hŭktugŏn" (「黑頭巾」) or Ch'unwŏn's "Yi Sunsin" (「李舜臣」), <121> where did you find models to look at, or did you draw entirely from imagination?
Yi Sangbŏm - Illustrations for historical pieces are mostly created from the artist's imagination. For "Yi Sunsin," I saw a portrait (초상), but he didn't appear to have the face of a general with the type of great commander (명장) that contemporary people imagine. If I drew only according to that portrait, would he look like a great general to people today? So I added flesh to the face, attached a powerful beard (수염), and crafted it in various ways. For illustrations of historical pieces, reference materials for that era are unobtainable, and one doesn't know the customs and habits of that time, so there are really quite a lot of difficulties.
No Suhyŏn - That's certainly true. To draw a historical piece, first, to gain common knowledge (상식), there's no choice but to go to elderly people and directly ask about the common knowledge of that era. But some people don't even try to explain it well. Therefore, an illustrator intending to draw historical pieces must, to some extent, professionally research all the common knowledge about ancient things themselves.
Yi Sŭngman - What's even more dumbfounding is when an author brings a historical piece and asks me to draw for it. When I asked about the time period (연대), the author themselves didn't know well and vaguely (막연하게) said to draw it as about a thousand years ago. Is that all? Looking at the scenes appearing in the work, there's a scene where a sword (劒) is used to cut a body wearing a topo (도포) into two or three pieces (동갱이). But were old Chosŏn swords daggers? It's impossible to cut a person wearing a topo into two or three pieces, truly a pitiful situation (딱한 일). Cases like this are quite troublesome. Looking at historical pieces coming out of the Tokyo area, the authors have abundant knowledge of ancient things and describe them meticulously, down to the smallest detail (촌분).
An Sŏgyŏng - Compared to historical pieces, contemporary pieces are much easier. Firstly, materials are easy to find, and the artist drawing them generally knows things through everyday common knowledge. Even if there are occasional instances where that's not the case, one can always hear about it from others. For historical pieces, if there's something you don't know while drawing, there's no choice but to run out right then and go ask someone knowledgeable <122>, so it's quite a pain. Moreover, I've drawn quite a few historical pieces, but I utterly lack confidence. So, from now on, I've decided not to touch historical pieces at all. People who draw historical pieces should be specialists who only do historical pieces.
Kim Tonghwan - When you couldn't obtain reference materials like that or drew something you weren't confident about from imagination, weren't there any protests (항의) from readers?
Yi Sangbŏm - Occasionally there are. But frankly, even those who protest don't seem to know much more than the illustrator's common knowledge.
An Sŏgyŏng - Right now is the pioneering era for historical illustrations, so it's quite a great hardship. However, if current historical illustrators make an effort to establish certain standards and forms take shape, it will become much easier in the future.
Yi Sŭngman - Well, that may be true. But illustrators don't directly know about ancient things, so it's quite a hardship. Even if we ask knowledgeable old people, they are unwilling to tell us, which is a big problem.
No Suhyŏn - The Yi Dynasty (李朝) period isn't the issue, but going back before Koryŏ (高麗), even historians are vague, so how can this work?
An Sŏgyŏng - Also, even when the author knows about illustrations, they seem unwilling to explain well.
Yi Sŭngman - Another tricky point in drawing historical pieces is that in the old days, there was no custom of cutting hair or shaving beards, so even men just over 20 would have beards. But if you draw a person around 20 with a beard for today's readers, they'll see him as a 40- or 50-year-old man, not a 20-year-old youth. Also, in the past, a chief mourner (喪재) wore a black hat (갓), but in the eyes of people today, putting a black hat on a chief mourner would look inappropriate. Such cases are also quite difficult.
Kim Tonghwan - Then, have there been no completed works among historical illustrations up to now?
Yi Sŭngman - Probably not a single one.
Kim Tonghwan - Then, regarding illustrations for contemporary pieces, when drawing the male and female protagonists appearing in the novel, don't you <123> generally use specific men and women as direct models?
An Sŏgyŏng - Even if it's a contemporary piece, can one really use models yet in Chosŏn? Primarily because it's not economically feasible. However, when I occasionally see a person with a character I like, I always keep them in mind and essentially use them as a model anytime.
Kim Tonghwan - But in the case of roman à clef novels (모텔 소설), wouldn't you know the person who is the direct model? Don't you actually use that person as a model? I assume there are quite many such examples...
Yi Sŭngman - "Pangnang ŭi Kain" (「放浪의 歌人」 - The Wandering Singer), published years ago, was purely a roman à clef based on the vocalist An Kiyŏng (安基永) and Kim Hyŏnsun (金顯順). However, because the newspaper company repeatedly requested (부탁이) me not to use An Kiyŏng and Kim Hyŏnsun as models and to avoid them, I inevitably couldn't use their faces as models (살니지 못 하엿지요). So, having no choice, I essentially created and drew characters of a certain nature conceived in my head.
An Sŏgyŏng - Also, Tokkyŏn's "Ch'ŏngch'unbo" (「靑春譜」 - Chronicle of Youth), published sometime in the Maeil Sinbo (每日新報), was apparently a pure roman à clef.
Yi Sŭngman - Right, "Ch'ŏngch'unbo" was also a roman à clef based on Pak Indŏk (朴仁德) and Sin Hŭng'u (申興雨). For this novel too, out of curiosity, I intended to draw the two individuals directly as models and even obtained their photos, but I was told to avoid the models as much as possible. So, I tried my best to avoid the models, but readers were chattering (떠듭데다) that even the pictures appearing in the novel were of Sin Hŭng'u and Pak Indŏk.

Words You Wish to Say to General Society and Authors
Kim Tonghwan - As I mentioned briefly earlier, among artists, you are the ones who pioneered the barren land (황무지) as illustrators, being the very first in Chosŏn. Thus, up to today, you are indeed pioneers (선구자) who have strived for the Chosŏn illustration world for a truly long time. From such long experience, you must surely have many feelings about illustrations, and as illustrators, many sorrows <124> and hardships. Therefore, I believe you must have many things you wish to say to society, authors, or the general readers.
An Sŏgyŏng - The hardships of an illustrator are truly countless. Firstly, it would be great if we could at least know the outline – what kind of personality the protagonist in the work has, how old they are, what class and type of person they are. But the novelist just lays out the work, writes and sends one installment after another, and says nothing to the illustrator about the characters in the work (作中), then later complains that the protagonist's face is wrong, or this or that. If there were at least some time, it might be different, but most novelists bring in each installment just as the printing time is imminent, so where is the time to fully read the novel? Consequently, the text and the picture naturally lose their unity. In any case, novelists need to understand the illustrator's hardships a bit more.
No Suhyŏn - That's also true, but frankly, newspaper companies and general society seem to disregard the existence of illustrators too much. When you say illustrator, it's not just drawing illustrations; one is naturally expected to be active as a reporter, and also handle statistical charts, sketches, and whatnot, leaving no time to breathe, the burden is just too much. So, how can one confidently draw the illustrations that are the lifeblood of newspaper novels? A greater respect for illustrators is desperately needed.
Yi Sangbŏm - That's right. From now on in Chosŏn too, illustrators should be employed exclusively for that role. And there's a need to have separate specialists for contemporary and historical illustrations. In short, there needs to be the magnanimity to treat illustrators as skilled technicians. Also, another thing is that printing technology in Chosŏn is still lagging behind, so even if a picture is drawn well, the illustration that actually appears on the page often isn't very clear.
Yi Sŭngman - In my opinion, it seems newspaper and magazine companies don't insert illustrations because they feel a pressing need, but rather because general readers strongly demand illustrations, they are reluctantly dragged into (끄을여서) doing it as they have no choice. In Chosŏn, illustrators are treated with too much contempt. The novel is considered primary, and the illustration is seen as an appendage (부속물).<125>
An Sŏgyŏng - Yes. That tendency is prevalent. But in reality, if you want to fundamentally bring a work to life, the effect of illustration is tremendous, but it's true that society and novelists don't understand that well... By the way, in mainland Japan, excellent artists all advance into the illustration field because they are paid 20-30 won per illustration, which solves livelihood problems first, and allows them to directly spend money to find and use models. There's a need to treat Chosŏn's illustrators better too. It's a fact that learning this skill costs more money than other skills. They say Chosŏn artists drink well, but where else can artists release their melancholy and sorrow? Naturally, they end up just drinking.
Kim Tonghwan - Showing the true colors of the drinking party... (All burst into laughter)
Yi Sangbŏm - Frankly, regardless of the novel, the first thing that catches the reader's eye is the illustration. One sees the illustration first before reading the text content. Looking at this alone, one can understand what an important role illustration plays.
Yi Sŭngman - Exactly, they don't recognize (인식치) such an important existence and impose many various burdens, making one's head so muddled that it's impossible to concentrate. Yet, readers and authors tell illustrators off, criticizing (비난) that they can't draw realistic depictions of scenes and only know how to draw large faces of characters. Naturally, it can't be helped but turn out that way.
An Sŏgyŏng - Indeed, newspaper companies don't even have a single reference book that could be helpful to illustrators, so what more is there to say?
No Suhyŏn - The most dumbfounding (기맥히는) time for an illustrator is when a picture turned out well, but unfortunately, due to a printing worker's mistake, it gets omitted, or due to editing constraints, there isn't enough space, and it has to be removed. That's truly dumbfounding.
Yi Sangbŏm - Is that all? It's also common for the frame to be arbitrarily cut off sideways (엽흐로) or turned upside down (꺽구로).
An Sŏgyŏng - Logically (이치로), if they can arbitrarily remove pictures and cut them arbitrarily, they should also be able to arbitrarily chop up the novel. (All laugh)
Kim Tonghwan - Despite your busy schedules, thank you very much for providing so many valuable words. I believe today's meeting has concluded smoothly with this. <126>

**Summary**

Four prominent illustrators (Yi Sangbŏm, An Sŏgyŏng, No Suhyŏn, Yi Sŭngman) discuss their profession in 1936 Chosŏn. They debate where ideal beauty is found (kisaeng, schoolgirls, noblewomen), with differing opinions, but note Korean women's complexions suffer due to living conditions, though their 'leg lines' are superior. They find illustrating easier when authors communicate character details and provide longer text segments, praising Tokkyŏn (Ch'oe Ch'ansik), Ch'unwŏn (Yi Kwangsu), and Yi T'aejun for being cooperative. Historical illustrations pose significant challenges due to lack of references, historical inaccuracies in novels, and difficulty depicting historical appearances for modern readers. They lament the lack of professional respect, low pay compared to Japan, excessive workloads beyond illustration, poor printing quality, and arbitrary editorial decisions that damage their work. They feel undervalued despite illustrations being crucial for reader engagement.

This article offers historians insights into the professional lives and struggles of artists in colonial Korea, highlighting the nascent state of newspaper/magazine illustration. The discussion reveals contemporary aesthetic standards and social commentary (e.g., observations on Korean women's appearance linked to living standards), the challenges of representing historical subjects accurately in a colonial context lacking resources, the complex relationship between authors and illustrators, and the socio-economic pressures faced by artists, including comparisons with Japan. The mention of specific authors, works, and real people used as models (An Kiyŏng, Kim Hyŏnsun, Pak Indŏk, Sin Hŭng'u) provides valuable context for literary and cultural history.

畵家가 「美人」을 말함
話題

2.어느 作家의 作品이 揷畵 그리기에 조흔가?

李象範(東亞日報)

7월 7일 오후 4시부터 동 6시까지 서울 長谷川町 「朝鮮호텔」
後園路臺에서＜116＞

畵伯은 엇던데서 美人을 구하나?
金東煥-(本社)-우리 畵壇에서 가장 뚜렷하게 만흔 활약을 하시는 네 분의 화백을 이러틋 한 자리에 모시고 우리 화단 더구나 신문 잡지 등의 삽화계에 대한 제반 문제를 충분 토의할 이 기회를 엇게 되엿슴을 깁부게 생각합니다. 보시다십히 여긔 오신 네 분은 모다 신문 잡지 등의 삽화계에 나선지도 퍽 오랜 분들로서 朝鮮에 잇서서 삽화 화백으로서는 처녀지를 개척하여 노흔 분들이라고 해도 과언이 아닌 줄노 암니다. 그럼으로 여러분들의 胸中에는 화가로서의 특히 삽화 화가로서의 우리 사회에 향해서 하고 십흔 말슴도 만흐신 줄 아오며 따라 삽화가로서의 남달은 고심, 비애 등도 수두룩할 줄로 아옵니다. 그러나 우리 사회에서는 아직껏 삽화 화백의 최고권위 되시는 여러분을 한 자리에 모으시게 하고 여러 가지 이로운 말슴 재미로운 말슴을 들을 기회가 한번도 업섯든 차에 비록 변변치 못하나마 이런 기회를 짓게 되엿스니 하시고 십흔 말슴은 기탄업시 말슴하여 주시기 바람니다. 먼첨 화백은 미인을 자주 생각하실 줄 주로 아는데 참말 미인은 엇던 데서 구하심니까? 가령 예를 들면 교문을 나오는 制服의 처녀들 한데서 참말 미인을 구하겟서요? 그러치 안흐면 紅燈綠酒에서 밤을 새우는 기생과 가튼 직업여성이나, 혹은 閨中에 깁히 파뭇처 잇는 令夫人들 가운데서 참말 미인을 구하겟슴니까?
李象範-참말 미인을 엇떤데서 구하겟느냐? 하는 말슴은 즉 현대 미인의 표준을＜117＞ 말하라 하는 말슴인데 미인을 보는 방법도 모다 달고 그 표준도 모두가 달으니까요. 가령 기생은 기생으로서의 미를 가지고 잇다고 하겟고 여학생은 여학생으로, 부인은 부인으로서의 제 각금 독특한 미를 가지고 잇다고 하겟지요.
安夕影-그러치요. 무슨 일정한 표준이 서 잇는 것도 아니니까요. 어듼지 모르게 거트로 보아서도 그 얼골에나 몸 맵시에 인격이 잇서 부이고 늘 사물을 생각하고 판단하는 지식이 풍부하고 이지력이 날카로운 여자라면 가장 아름다운 여자라고 하겟지요. 그러한 고결한 미를 소유한 여자는 으레히 그 눈에나 얼골에나 어듼지 모르게 그런 미가 나타나는 것이니까요.
우리 삽화가들도 길을 가다가든지 혹은 어떤 경우에서 그러한 품성을 가진 듯한 미인을 발견할 때에는 두 세 번 보아 두엇다가 어떠한 여주인공을 그리랴 할 때에는 반듯이 화제로 삼게 되지요.
金東煥-夕影! 그러니 그러한 미인은 어떤데서 구할 수가 잇겟서요.
安夕影-그야 喫茶店이나 빠-갓흔데서도 간혹 발견하게 되고, 거리에서도 보게 되지만는 대체로 朝鮮에서는 참말 미인이란 거리로 쏘단이지 안허서 그런지는 몰나도 내가 말한 그런 미인을 구할야면 아마도 교문 앞에 가 섯다가 책보를 끼고 삽분삽분 거러 나오는 制服의 처녀들 가운데서 차저 보아야 하지요.
李象範-그럼 夕影은 미인을 구하려 여학교문 앞으로 자주 차저 단이겟군요?(일동笑)
盧壽鉉-朝鮮에서 미인을 구할나면 그래도 아직까지는 기생 가운데서야 참말 미인을 발견하게 되지요.
李承萬-그러치요. 기생들 가운데에 참말 미인이 잇습데다. 그 박게야 어듸 눈에 걸여 보이는 미인이 잇습데가? 거리에서도 눈에 제일 먼저 띄이는 미인이라면 으레히 기생이드군요... 그런데 이것은 좀 딴 이야기 갓지만는 朝鮮 여자들은 이떤 계급의 여자들을 물론하고 미인되기에 다른 사혀Œ 여자들보다는 한 가지 부족되는 점이 잇습데다. 내가 일전 京城驛에 東京에서 어떤 손님이 온다고 해서 나갓섯는데 시내 각 여학교 학생들이 퍽 만히 환영하는 의미로 나왓드군요. 그 중에는 日本 내지인 여학생들도 잇도 朝鮮 여학생들도 잇섯는데 제일 눈에 띄이는 것은 대채로 朝鮮 여학생들은 얼골에 윤택이라고는 조금도 업서보입데다. 모다가 꺼-츨하고 기름끼가 업서요. 그야 생활 정도라든가 가옥제도라든가 영양관게＜118＞ 등 여러 가지로 여유 업는 생활 속에서 자라나는, 탓도 잇겟지마는 그런 점으로 朝鮮 여자들은 미인될 여러 가지 조건 가운데서 가장 큰 조건의 하나인 살결이 제일 보는 사람에게 실망을 주드군요.
盧壽鉉-그건 그럿습데다. 비단 여학생뿐만 아니라 일반적으로 朝鮮 사람의 얼골은 다른 곳 사람들에게 비하면 혈색이 업고 윤택이 업서 보이는 것은 생활 문제로 할 수 업는 일이지요. 그러치만는 그 대신 한 가지 다른 곳 여학생보다는 빼난 것은 각선미가 풍부한 점이지요.
安夕影-그러치요. 각선미는 朝鮮 녀자가 훨신 낫지요. 아마 동양 여자들로는 제일이라고 해도 조흘걸요. 다른 데 여자들은 보면 참 우습지요. 다섯 명이 길을 간다치면 네 명은 그 다리가 굵엇다 가느러젓다 한 것이 정이 뚝 떠러집데다.

어느 作家의 作品이 揷畵 그리기에 조흔가?
金東煥-그러면 여러분이 대체로 어떤데서 미인을 구하나 하는 것은 짐작할 수 잇슴니다. 다음에는 여러분이 10년 이상을 각각 신문사에 게시면서 여러 작가의 만흔 작품을 삽화하기에 힘써 오신 분들임으로 지금에 안저서는 어느 작가의 작품은 삽화 그리기에 쉬웁다든지 혹은 힘든다든지 하는 것을 잘 아실 줄로 암니다. 이번 기회에 숨김 업시 쏘다노와 주웟스면 일반 작가들에게도 퍽으나 도음이 될 줄 아오며 독자들에게도 만흔 깨닭음이 잇슬 줄로 암니다. 먼저 李承萬씨부터 말슴해 주서요. 廉想涉씨의 소설이 어떳습데가?
李承萬-廉想涉씨의 작품은 삽화 그리기에＜119＞ 퍽 조트군요. 물론 서로 자주 맛나 연락을 취하는 때문도 잇고 또 소설을 짤막― 써주는 것이 아니고 길게 써 주기 때문에 한번 소설을 충분히 읽고 삽화 그릴 시간의 여유를 가지는 관계로 작품 속에 나오는 주인공의 面影, 성격 등을 어느 정도까지 짐작하게 되니까 아주 쉽드군요. 그 분의 작품은 비교적 만히 그려본 편이지마는 모다 그리기가 쉬운 편입데다 그 중에서도 年前에 실닌 『모랑꽃 될 때』도 내가 삽화를 하엿는데 그 중에서도 더구나 쉬운 편이드군요. 그야 그 분과 자조 맛나게 되니까 긔회 잇는 대로 작품 속에 나오는 인물에 대하야 자주 어떠케 그려달나고 이야기 해주니까요.
安夕影-그래요. 소설가가 작품을 써 가지고는 즉 삽화 그리는 사람한테 이 작품 가운데 나오는 인물들은 어떠한 사람이라는 것을 대략이나마 이야기 해주웟스면 작품 전체의 통일점을 찻게되고 어떤 성격과 부류의 인간이라는 것을 알게되니까 그림 그리기가 퍽으나 쉬울텐데. 어데 그런 점을 이해해 주는 작가가 멫 되나요. 내 보기에는 獨鵑이 가장 삽화가에게 대한 이해성을 가진 분으로 알지요. 지금에도 삽화가에게 대해서 이해를 갓는 작가가 멫 안되는데 獨鵑은 「僧房悲曲」을 쓸 때부터도 퍽 삽화가에게 주인공에 대해서 자주 이야기도 해 주고 해서 퍽 그리기가 쉬웟지요. 그 박게도 춘원의 작품도 퍽 쉬운 편이지요. 이 분은 원악 老大作家이니까 삽화가의 고심도 이해하는 편이고 또 소설이 한회  한회 삽화 그리기에 적당한 재료를 제공해 주는 듯 십드군요. 얼마전이 실니엿든 「그 女子의 一生」 삽화는 나로서도 퍽 잘 된 편이라고 생각되여요.
盧壽鉉-獨鵑의 작품은 참 쉬운 편이드군요. 요즈음 실니는 「戀愛非常線」이 이 분의 작품으로서는 처음 대하는 것인데도 다른 작가의 것처럼 어럽지 안허요. 그러나 자주 그려 보아서 그런지는 몰라도 李泰俊씨의 작품이 내가 삽화 그리든 중에서는 가장 조트군요. 이 분의 작품은 선이 부드럽고 등장하는 인물이 적어서 간결한 맛이 나는 작품임으로 아주 쉽지요. 이 분의 작품은 여러번 대하여 보앗지마는 거개가 그래요.
李象範-나는 憑虛의 작품으로 「赤道」를 그렷 보앗는데 그 속이 나오오는＜120＞ 남주인공은  감혹에서 나온 사람 갓치 그려야 할텐데 모텔을 구할 수도 업고 경험도 업고 해서 내 생각으로 대강 짐작해서 이러케 그리면 감혹에서 나온 사람 갓해 보히려니 하는 생각으로 그려 보앗는데 처음부터 끝까지 주인공의 얼골이 통일된 듯 합데다. 그 박게도 春園의 「李舜臣傳」도 물론 고대 인물이라 유명한 장군 갓치만 그렷는데 그 것도 맨 나종까지 주인공의 얼골이 통일된 점을 일치안노라고는 한 셈이지요.
李承萬-모다 그리기 쉬운 편만을 말슴하엿는데 나는 또 힘든 것을 이야기 하겟서요. 어느 작가 어느 작가 할 것 업시 일반적으로 농촌 묘사의 장면 갓흔데는 작가로서 실제적 농촌 풍경과는 판이하게 묘사된 것이 만허서 작품에 나오는 것만을 보아서는 사실과는 달으기에 이런 경과에는 여간 거북스럽지가 안허요. 이번 大阪每日 朝鮮版에 연재되는 朝鮮 여류작가들의 작품의 삽화를 그리는데 실제적인 농촌 풍경과 작품 속에 나오는 묘사된 사실과는 다소 다른 점이 잇서서 여간 힘들지가 안허요. 그런 경우는 얼마든지 잇지요.

李承萬-글세 그런 중요한 존재인 것을 인식치 못하고 여러 가지 부담을 만히 지워노흐니 도모지 머리가 뒤숭숭해서 정신을 차릴 수가 업지요. 그런데 독자나 작가들은 삽화가에게 말해 오기를 장면―의 사실적인 묘사을 그리지 못하고 거저 인물의 얼골이나 커다라케 그릴 줄만 안다는 비난을 듯게 되는데 자연 그럴 수 박게 업지요.
安夕影-글세 신문사에는 삽화가에게 대하야 참고될 만한 참고서 한 책이 업스니 더 말해 무엇하겟서요.
盧壽鉉-제일 삽화가로서 기맥히는 때는 그림이 잘 되엇슬 때 공교로히도 인쇄 직공의 잘못으로 빠저 버린다든가 편집 관계로 지면이 모자라서 부득이 빼여 버릴 때는 참으로 기맥힙데다.
李象範-그 뿐인가요. 마음대로 화면을 엽흐로 짤너 버린다든가 꺽구로 뒤집어 놋는 경우도 중중하지요.
安夕影-이치로는 그림을 마음대로 빼여버리고 마음대로 짤너버릴테면 소설도 마음대로 뚝뚝 짤너버려야 하겟지요.(일동一笑)
