Article Title: 제고장서 듯는 民謠 情調, 濟州島 멜로디 (The Folk Song Sentiment Heard in My Hometown, Cheju Island Melody)

The Folk Song Sentiment Heard in My Hometown, Cheju Island Melody
						Kŭmnŭngin (金陵人)

It is rather curious that the editor should ask me, of all people, to write about the folk songs of Cheju Island (濟州島), while I am convalescing in my hometown cradle, Kŭmnŭng (金陵) in Haesŏ (海西), after a long absence.
Among those who have recently visited Cheju Island is the novelist Yi Muyŏng (李無影), and more pertinently, those who call Cheju Island home include the playwright Kim Chinmun (金振門), the musician Sŏ Sangsŏk (徐祥錫), and the critic Hong Yangmyŏng (洪陽明); there are certainly many suitable candidates who could write on this topic. Amidst these, although this clumsy writer did indeed visit Cheju Island years ago, it was merely a cursory glance, like viewing mountains from a galloping horse (走馬觀山), just scratching the surface. And even though I collected several folk songs and legends, due to certain circumstances, I currently do not possess even one complete piece.
However, lacking the qualifications to refuse, I will attempt to describe my humble opinions about Cheju Island folk songs, focusing solely on the mood and sentiment as suggested by the title.

Cheju Island is undoubtedly a land particularly<102> rich in folk songs. Just as Cheju Island has many legends, it also has many folk songs, and Cheju Island folk songs are characterized by their earthy smell and natural growth.
Indeed, regardless of the region, folk songs and legends are the most precious documents a place possesses. Folk songs arise when there is much sorrow (恨), yet also a certain degree of leisure in life. If life is too poor and desperate, the populace forgets how to laugh, and even their tears dry up; when would songs emerge then?
(How pitiful a canary that has forgotten its song must be!)
Cheju Island is a place where life's hardships are particularly abundant, yet, to some extent, it is also a land of leisurely living.
This becomes the element that gives birth to so many legends and folk songs.
Cheju Island is a lonely island surrounded on all four sides by a vast, boundless sea.
One might think that its wide landmass would diminish the sense of loneliness, but because it is situated so far from the mainland and surrounded by a desolate sea lacking even a single nearby island, the poignant feeling of being a solitary island at the ends of the earth (天涯孤島) is inescapable. As soon as someone from the mainland sets foot on Cheju Island, they are immediately struck by the sorrowful feeling of having arrived in a foreign land thousands of miles away.
Nowadays, transportation has become relatively convenient, shortening the round trip between Cheju Island and Mokp'o (木浦) to less than 20 hours, but in the old days, this sea route took a month or even two for just a one-way journey. More severe than that, traveling this route meant risking one's life. However, due to the necessities of life, people had no choice but to traverse this perilous waterway, and often undertook even more dangerous and distant trading voyages—even to China (中國). Consequently, separations became as common as daily meals, and these separations frequently left behind eternal sorrow (恨).
Thus, many folk songs imbued with sorrow and resentment came into being. Next, Cheju Island is a land of self-sufficiency. Cheju Island is called the land of Three Abundances (三多)—abundant wind, abundant stones, and abundant women—but I discovered it is also a land of Three Absences (三無). Firstly, there are no thieves, no beggars, and no ferocious beasts, making it Three Absences; indeed, it's not Three Fewnesses but absolute Three Absences. Some say this is because Cheju Island, being a remote island (絶海孤島), offers no escape for thieves or wild animals, but I see the absence of thieves and beggars as evidence of the islanders' leisurely life.
Indeed, the scenery of Cheju Island is leisurely, and the atmosphere of Cheju Island is tranquil (悠閑). Cheju Island is a place where trifoliate orange blossoms bloom in the villages, and pasture horses (木馬) grow fat on the mountain slopes. Anyone seeing the sight of Cheju Island's pasture horses might feel they have arrived in a storybook land. The horses of Cheju Island<103> are horses without reins. These unbridled horses leave their stables in the morning and leisurely make their way out, careful not to step on or damage the vegetable gardens. They then gather in herds of several hundred on the mountainsides, grazing all day long. When dusk falls, they leisurely wander back, each finding its own home. What need is there for a shepherd (牧者)? This scene of pasture horses alone sufficiently represents the lifestyle of Cheju Island. Also, Cheju Island is a land where women's labor predominates. During the day, the women of the village all go out to the sea, like seabirds or frogs, harvesting freedom and livelihood from within the water.
Meanwhile, the men stay at home, looking after children, guarding the house, and taking naps.
Thus, the villages become endlessly quiet. Only chickens and dogs roam through the villages, not even fighting. Occasionally, one sees the sight of a young girl carrying water (different from carrying it on the head).
At night, the moonlight seems exceptionally bright. Most village men seek out the moonlight by the seashore, cleansing their bodies and minds, swallowing the clear, distant sounds of the weeping tide.
How could folk songs not exist in such a leisurely land? Therefore, on this not-so-large island (Yes, it's not very large. Although Cheju Island is called a large island of 70 *li* square, unlike Kanghwa Island (江華島), it is not flat terrain; the entire island is mountainous, centered around Halla Mountain (漢拏山), and people live only in the low-lying areas near the sea, so in terms of population, it cannot be called large), when I visited, I managed to gather about 20 folk songs and 10 legends. Furthermore, a certain Buddhist woman (佛信者인 여인 某氏), who serialized articles on Cheju Island folk songs in the literature and arts section of a newspaper some years ago, reportedly collected an astonishing 60-odd Cheju folk songs.
Now, in order, I will transcribe the opening lines of a few representative folk songs and add my foolish impressions about them.

Iŏ— Iŏdo hara
Thinking of Iŏ makes my tears fall

Raindrops falling on green onion leaves
Teardrops mixed with sighs
Ah—Iŏdo hara, cross over Iŏdo
Green onions, green onion leaves

This is a sorrowful song (悲曲) of island life, woven with sorrow and resentment. Not knowing where Iŏdo (이어島) is, the island wives, having sent their husbands off on trading journeys to places like China, pray choked with tears for their husbands to safely cross Iŏdo. This is because of a legend that no matter how perilous the sea route, it becomes calm once Iŏdo is passed. However, nobody knows where Iŏdo actually is. Both those sending off and those departing merely wish to pass this dream island, this island of yearning (憧憬), safely.<104>
The existence of Iŏdo in Cheju Island is akin to the existence of Arirang Pass (아리랑 고개) on the mainland (which islanders call the Chosŏn (朝鮮) interior).
Just as Arirang Pass on the mainland is a pass of yearning as well as a pass of tears, Iŏdo for these islanders is also an extremely sorrowful pass. Even if one safely crosses Iŏdo, the long separation is unavoidable. A wife who has bid farewell to her husband at the tearful port (浦口) returns home and sits by the window. Just then, the sound of raindrops falling on the onion (葱) leaves planted in the backyard resembles the teardrops mixed with sighs emerging from the wife's heart.
It is a natural sentiment of island life filled with many separations. However, without personally setting foot on Cheju Island and gazing at the breathtakingly vast horizon, it would be difficult to fully appreciate the profound, sad mood of this folk song.

Cold water, chilled water swirls in the mouth
But alcohol goes down smoothly, smoothly
Erandagarigamsil negeurimasora

Sparrows, small birds nest in grass or groves (고지)
But our love nests only in my embrace
Erandagarigamsil negeurimasora
Note—Erandagarigamsil has no particular meaning, koji—grove/forest

This too is a song of wives, and in this folk song, one can glimpse to some extent the island's custom of female dominance (女尊男卑). The scene of women indulging in alcohol (溺愛) is amusing, but the blatant expression of their burning desire to monopolize men is a truly surprising and novel sentiment and mood.
Speaking of alcohol, this is true: the alcohol of Cheju Island, which the women love more than water, is truly a famed liquor (名酒) of Chosŏn, no, a world-class famed liquor. In Cheju Island, the freedom and generosity to brew alcohol as sung in folk songs still remain, and I believe the Cheju Island local brew (土酒), with its strong millet scent, is something that every drinker under heaven (天下酒客) should taste at least once in their lifetime.

Tunggwidi tangsil—
Mortar pestle, yŏdu tangsil
Light the candle in our master’s room
Our master will ride the moon home

As mentioned in the introduction, after the Cheju Island wives go out to work by the sea, the men generally stay home doing leisurely tasks. This song depicts that scene.
Before the wife, the head of the household (家長), returns from work, the husband performs the easy task of pounding grain in a mortar (방아찟는) while waiting for the head of the household to return. However, the sun sets in the west and the moon rises in the east (日落西山 月出東嶺),<105> yet the wife, the head, has not returned. The husband, anxious and deferential, lights a lamp in his wife's room and waits for her to return riding the moonlight. Aren't they remarkably devoted (賢淑) husbands?

Sea route a thousand *li*, land route a thousand *li*, how far it is
The gentleman (양반) who went to Hanyang (漢陽) shows no sign of returning.

I do not view this folk song as an import from the mainland Kangwŏn Province (江原道) Ŏrang T'aryŏng's lyrics: "How far is Hanyang from here—once gone, never seems to return." This is likely a coincidental similarity in phrasing. Ŏrang T'aryŏng is Ŏrang T'aryŏng, and the Cheju Island folk song is a song born from its own circumstances and sentiments. Furthermore, I believe this Cheju song originated in a much earlier era.
I interpret the "yangban" (양반) in the song lyrics not as a general term of respect, but as referring to the yangban class of old. In the past, the royal court (조정) treated Cheju Island as the farthest and harshest place of exile (귀양사리). High-ranking officials (양반) like Royal Secretaries (承旨) and Vice Ministers (叅判) were sometimes exiled to this remote island (絶海孤島). While some were guilty of serious crimes, among them were also fair-faced young noblemen (貴公子) and talented individuals (才人) who were victims of slander by petty men. Over the long passage of time, these men, often out of loneliness, seduced young island girls. However, the habit of these yangban was generally to make promises firm as Halla Mountain (漢拏山) when exchanging affections, but once pardoned and returned to the Hanyang court, they would completely cut off contact.
Thus, sad songs and sad legends arose on the island. Legends arose even more frequently than songs.
Seeing the surprising number of heart-wrenching legends on Cheju Island born from such sorrow, and moreover, seeing the eternal, unwashable恨 (han) and blood of the island women of old embedded in those legends, I could not help but tremble.
This folk song, too, is a song where the blood and tears of the island women of old are knotted into every phrase (마듸마듸).
How could it be limited only to the past? Similar sad romances surely exist today and will continue to be woven.

Father (아-방)—Mother (어-멍), just gather fish
What's the use of planting potatoes (감제)?

Not even half a day passes
Before seaweed and mussels fill a basket (한구덕이)

This girl (이비바리), spread your legs wide
Would you die in ten fathoms of water?

What's the use of fish and seaweed?
Exchange them for money and build a tile-roofed house (지왜간)
Note—a-bang ŏ-mŏng—father<106> mother, kamje—potato, kudŏgi—basket, pibari—girl, chiwaegan—tile-roofed house.

From this one folk song, one can glimpse the scenes of Cheju Island's *haenyŏ* (sea women) life, the leisurely mood, and the romantic sentiment all at once.
As seen in the first verse, from birth, they dislike farming and yearn for the *haenyŏ* life.
The second verse is their boast about their labor efficiency; the women (彼女들) are all sea goddesses (化神).
The third verse also shows the *haenyŏ* taking pride in their sea labor, feeling ashamed even at a young age if they are not skilled in the sea:
"Would you die in ten fathoms of water?"
This line reaches a level that astonishes mainlanders.
The fourth verse is the constant prayer (祈禱) they hold.
Why gather mountains of fish and seaweed? To exchange them for gold on the mainland, build a tile-roofed house, become wealthy, and live peacefully for a lifetime on this leisurely island. Don't you feel envious of their life, where they value their own labor, do not seek luck (僥倖), and enjoy self-sufficiency?

With this, I believe I have offered at least a glimpse (片鱗) of Cheju Island folk songs. That it is insufficient was mentioned at the beginning of this writing, so I offer this to fulfill my obligation (塞責). Finally, I wish to point out a regrettable fact concerning Cheju Island folk songs that I have always harbored.
It is the phenomenon that the destiny of these precious documents, the Cheju Island folk songs, to disappear is not far off.
Years ago, when I went to Cheju Island to collect folk songs and legends, besides Mr. P (P씨), the branch chief of the C newspaper (C신문 支局長) in the local town (邑內), Mr. K (K씨), the owner of a musical instrument shop (악기점주), and Dr. C (의사 C씨), more than ten other aspiring young men all provided me with extreme assistance. The assistance these gentlemen (氏등) provided involved introducing me to elderly fishermen who knew the old songs and grandmothers who were near death's door. Despite being natives of Cheju Island themselves, these gentlemen could not recall a single island folk song.
The *pibari* (비바리 - young women/girls) in the street taverns belt out Arirang and Toraji T'aryŏng, while records (레코-드) in the shops play the Yangsando melody.
Of course, these gentlemen also knew bits and pieces of such mainland songs.
I think that as transportation between the island and the mainland becomes more convenient, and further spurred by the arrival of records and radio (라듸오) on the island, the island's songs are naturally being conquered.
Furthermore, the residents, constantly yearning for and<107> admiring the mainland, seem to take pride in singing mainland songs and feel ashamed to sing the island's unique folk songs.
Moreover, the new youth seem eager to memorize popular songs introduced by records and radio, while showing absolutely no inclination to look back upon the island's traditional folk songs, these precious documents.
Because this is an extremely lamentable phenomenon, I asked Messrs. P and K if they had considered any measures to preserve them.
However, both gentlemen (兩氏) remained silent. Recently, several record companies have taken an interest in Cheju Island folk songs, attempting to introduce and preserve them, but this only applies to a few pieces deemed practical, and even those are being adapted for nationwide dissemination (頌布).
If things continue this way, genuine Cheju Island folk songs will likely vanish from the island itself, melodies and all, within a few years.
The legends will probably meet the same fate. It is a terrible waste.
June 19
From Muksa Study (黙思寮), Kŭmnŭng Village (金陵村), Haesŏ (海西)<108>

**Summary**

The author, Kŭmnŭngin, writing from his hometown Kŭmnŭng in 1936, reflects on Cheju Island folk songs despite feeling unqualified compared to natives like Kim Chinmun or Sŏ Sangsŏk. He describes Cheju as a land of sorrow (due to separations caused by dangerous sea travel) but also leisure (peaceful villages, self-sufficiency, dominant female labor), which fostered rich folk song traditions. He presents examples lamenting separation (Iŏdo), expressing female boldness, depicting unique gender roles, and recalling historical sorrows (exiled officials). However, the author expresses deep concern that these unique folk songs and legends are rapidly disappearing due to the influx of mainland culture via improved transport, radio, and records, and a tendency among islanders, especially youth, to prefer mainland songs and neglect their own heritage. From a colonial history perspective, the article is interesting for its snapshot of regional culture facing homogenization pressure from the (Korean) mainland, accelerated by modern media, even before considering direct Japanese assimilation policies. It highlights internal cultural dynamics, the perceived exoticism of Cheju by a mainland Korean intellectual, and early awareness of cultural endangerment driven by modernization and centralization trends within Korea itself during the colonial period.

제고장서 듯는 民謠 情調, 濟州島 멜로디
						金陵人
오래간만에 搖籃의 鄕地 海西 金陵에 靜養 중인 필자에게 하필 濟州島의 민요에 대하야 쓰라시는 편집자씨의 부탁도 야릇합니다.
최근에 濟州島를 단여온 이로는 소설가 李無影씨 같은 분도있고 그보다도 濟州島에 고향을 갖은 이로 극작가 金振門씨 음악가 徐祥錫씨 평론가 洪陽明씨 등이었으니 이 문제에 대하야 쓸 수 있는 적임자가 얼마든지 잇읍니다. 이런 중에 拙劣한 필자야말로 年前에 제주도를 보라갓든 일이 있다하여도 走馬觀山격이요 수박것할끼였으며 또 몃가지 民謠나 傳說을 採取한 것이 있엇다 하여도 어떤 사정상 현재 한편이라도 그 완전한 것을 수중에 갖고 있지 못합니다.
그러나 또한 사양할 자격도 없어 다만 題目에 뵈여지는대로 그 기분과 정조에 대하여서만 濟州島 民謠에 대하야 愚見을 敍述해 볼가합니다.

제주도는 모름직이 특히 민요가＜102＞ 많은 고장임니다. 제주도에는 민요와 한가지 전설도 많거니와 제주도의 민요는 그 가장 흙 냄새가 나고 자연생장적인데 특색이 있읍니다.
무릇 어느 고장을 물론하고 민요와 전설은 그 고장이 가진 가장 보배로운 文獻이거니와 가장 한이 많으면서도 어느 정도까지 생활의 餘裕가 있을 때 민요가 생겨집니다. 너머나 생활이 가난하고 악착하면 민중은 우슴을 니저 버리고 눈물까지 말너지나니 어느 사이에 노래가 나어지겠읍니까?
(노래를 니저 버린 카나리아는 얼마나 불상한 새이겠습니까?)

그리고 주민들도 항상 육지를 그리고＜107＞ 높이는 남어지 육지의 노래를 부름을 자랑으로 녁이고 섬의 고유민요를 부름을 붓그러 하는 모양입니다.
더욱 신청년들은 레코-드 라듸오에서 소개하는 유행가를 외여 둘지언정 섬의 재래 민요인 이 고귀한 문헌에 대하야서는 도모지 도라볼 생각을 안 두는 모양입니다.
이것은 극히 개탄할 현상이기에 P씨 K씨에게 이것의 유지책을 생각함이 없는가고 무러보았읍니다.
그러나 兩氏는 묵묵히 대답이 없었읍니다. 그런대 근래 몇 레코-드 회사에서 濟州島 민요에 유의하야 그 소개와 유지를 꾀하나 이것은 실행가치 있는 其中 수편에 불과하며 그것조차 改作하야 全鮮的으로 頌布케 되는 현상입니다.
이 모양대로 가면 진정한 濟州島 민요란 몇 해 안 가서 섬 안으로부터 그 멜로듸가 소멸되고 말 것입니다.
전설조차 같은 운명을 맞나겟지요. 몹시 아까운 일입니다.
