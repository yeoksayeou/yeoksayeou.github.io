Want to Go to Manchuria and Make Money? Authorities Gather for a Roundtable

Our Compatriots and the Industrial Relationship with Manchuria
Kim Tong-hwan (Chief Editor, Samch'ŏlli): First of all, we thank you all for putting aside your busy schedules, rushing about in various directions, coming from faraway Kaesŏng and even distant places within the city, putting everything aside to attend this meeting. We, the organizers, feel how deeply significant and important this gathering is. The attendees here have generally experienced various industrial sectors—be it farming, mining, or manufacturing—through management or practical experience in South and North Manchuria, Outer Mongolia, North China (Huabei), and other parts of China, so we know you must have many aspirations regarding this. Therefore, we ask you to speak about all these things. Especially since nearly two million compatriots have already migrated there from Chosŏn proper, and every year 60,000, 70,000, or 80,000 men, women, and children leave their <128> homeland for the wilds of Manchuria without a clear destination, we also hear that they face multiple threats such as persecution by local bandits (土兵), extortion (苛斂誅求) by local officials, and local oppression.
Then, this situation with Manchuria, which is geographically and industrially like one household, extremely close and intimately connected to Chosŏn, will surely cause great harm. If so, what should be done about the relationship between these two parties, which are linked in such a chain-like (聯環的), critical, and intimate way? Please speak frankly about various aspects of this issue. Furthermore, as this year marks the 8th anniversary of our magazine's founding, we have firmly resolved to undertake something that other newspapers and magazines have not done first, and we hope that those who speak will also share this intention and speak meticulously and candidly. The moderation will be shared between Mr. Kang Ch'on Chŏng Su-il and myself.
Chŏng Su-il (Managing Editor, Samch'ŏlli): The first thing we'd like to hear from everyone today concerns the industrial issues related to Manchuria, which has the closest <129> relationship with Chosŏn proper. Since the topic is industry, please set aside political matters and speak purely about industrial relations. Industry refers to manufacturing, commerce, agriculture, mining, forestry, animal husbandry, and various other fields, making the scope quite broad. Moreover, if we break all these down, they can be classified into many different areas. However, here, please focus primarily on matters related to Chosŏn compatriots, especially those most closely related to Chosŏn compatriots residing in Manchuria or those within Chosŏn. Thus, if we were to set foot on that land or invest there, which areas should we primarily focus on? We hope for insightful guidance on this.
(All tense, contemplating...)
Kim Tong-hwan: Mr. Kong (Kong Chin-hang), who has invested heavily in the North Manchuria region, is currently managing a farm, and has inspected South and North Manchuria, West and North Kando, and thus has extensive experience in this field, perhaps you could speak first?
Kong Chin-hang: Generally speaking, the lives of compatriots not only residing in Manchuria but across various parts of Manchuria are, inevitably, precarious (浮虛). That is, there are no real entrepreneurs to speak of. Economically or in terms of business acumen, there's little worth mentioning. Now, if we were to pick out a few businessmen, there's Kang Chae-ho in Kando (managing Kando Industrial Co., Ltd. and four other companies), Hong Sŭng-gyun in Kae'wŏn (rice milling, consumer cooperative), both playing leading roles with capital of 600,000-700,000 won. There are a few others in Harbin, Hsinching, etc., but before the Incident (事變; Manchurian Incident), there were generally no entrepreneurs worthy of the name. However, things changed drastically after the Incident, and now investors and entrepreneurs are emerging, with the advance of financial capital becoming more prominent day by day. Therefore, looking ahead, there will be much to see industrially, and there is great hope. However, if the security situation, that is, the political instability, were resolved further, it would attract even more financial capital, and the promotion of enterprise would surely be vastly different (懸殊).
Yi Sŏng-hwan: I would like to answer the question posed like this. Since industry is a general term covering all sectors, I will focus only on the industries that have an intimate and inseparable relationship with Chosŏn people. Manufacturing (heavy industry) is out of the question, and forestry is also not feasible due to capital constraints; timber and pulp industries are promising but impossible. Fisheries (Bohai Bay, Jilin, Songhua) existed somewhat, but were insignificant. From my perspective, only mining and agriculture remain. <130> Regarding mining, since the promulgation of the Mining Law on September 1st last year, the number of applications (出願數) has been largely from Japanese mainlanders and Chosŏn people. This even led to talk of mining-related immigration, so expectations are indeed high. The reason is that our compatriots have long had experience in gold mining and other areas, possess considerable skills, and moreover, the gold yield (産金率) is higher than in Chosŏn territory, so I believe it is very promising. Next is
agriculture, which includes crop farming (耕種農業) and livestock farming (牧畜農業). Livestock farming can be postponed to the future, leaving only crop farming. Considering past facts or various future relationships, this is seen as the most intimate and significant area. I believe the most crucial meaning lies latent here. Therefore, what I want to recommend to our compatriots is that our path to survival lies in actively advancing into this area.

With Kando Extensionism
Kim Tong-hwan: Hearing Mr. Yi Sŏng-hwan speak, it seems you have a rather optimistic view of advancing into Manchuria. However, the prevailing sentiment among compatriots in Chosŏn proper is still one of unease regarding Manchuria due to issues of security, education, communication, etc. If this is the case, should we fundamentally encourage emigration?
Yi Sŏng-hwan: First, we must discard one delusion we have traditionally held. <131> That is the thought we've had until now: "People go to Manchuria because they can't survive in Chosŏn, so they just wander into the Manchurian plains, on a path of vagrancy, aimlessly embarking on an uncertain journey." We have sent people off and gone ourselves with this mindset. However, from now on, we must completely root out this thought from our minds. Instead, from now on, each of us must adopt the thinking that "going to Manchuria is returning to the ancient land (古土) where our ancestors lived." We must go with the idea that we can live there together with neighbors and relatives. In other words, we should go with "Kando Extensionism" (間島 延長主義). Because our compatriots have lived collectively in Kando for hundreds of years, institutions for education, security, communication, exchange, and all other aspects are no different from the homeland. Therefore, going to Kando was considered no different than someone from Kyŏngsang Province moving to Hamgyŏng Province. As such, anxiety and fear did not accompany it. It is with this same mindset that I urge people to advance into South and North Manchuria.
Kong Chin-hang: I share Mr. Yi's sentiments. Indeed, we must abandon the old way of thinking, "going on a path of vagrancy because we cannot survive," and adopt a most sincere attitude, a psychological correction (시정), thinking "we are going back to reclaim the land where we lived." And what I want from the Chosŏn financial powers (재벌) is to encourage them to invest as much as possible from now on. My assertion is that they should invest even as a duty.
Yi Sŏng-hwan: Moreover, the reason we encourage advancing into Manchuria is that the land is vast, fertile plains are everywhere, and there's even a famous saying by geologists that the land is fine even without fertilization for 200 years. If we only provide the labor, developing these God-given resources is possible. Furthermore, considering the population distribution, rather than being confined (跼踳) within Chosŏn, venturing out to live in a vast, newly opened territory is something modern people should do.
Also, looking at the tenant farming system, it's 4:6 in South Manchuria and 3:7 in North Manchuria (landlord:tenant ratio), which is more advantageous than Chosŏn. Historically, as mentioned earlier, it's no different from our homeland, so we should focus on this. The only issue is security, but this too can be resolved if people live collectively (like in Kando). In this sense, I advocate Kando Extensionism. That is, I believe the most urgent issue is for us to embrace the intimacy of "Manchurian soil and our flesh becoming one dough."
(At this moment, Mr. Pang T'ae-yŏng arrives, apologizes to everyone for being late due to prior engagements, and takes his seat.) <132>
Kim Tong-hwan: Many of you have spoken so far. Mr. Pang, since you've traveled extensively, even to Harbin, perhaps you could share some valuable insights? Specifically, regarding the relationship between us and Manchurian industry, based on what you saw and heard during your travels...
Pang T'ae-yŏng: I haven't been there recently. A friend in Harbin introduced me to another friend who was starting a company, and I just helped out a bit. After that, because I'm in the book printing business, I went for textbook sales matters and came back after only 2 or 3 days, so my knowledge of Manchurian affairs is a blank slate (白紙). And today, hearing that your company is discussing a topic most relevant to us, I rushed over to listen for future reference, not to speak. Haha...
Kim Tong-hwan: Sir, I heard you made a huge investment in Manchuria recently?...
Pang T'ae-yŏng: No, sir. That must be Mr. Pang Kyu-hwan (方奎煥). He's a distant relative. This seems to be a misunderstanding (訛傳).
Haha... Everyone: Oh? Really? Haha… (Everyone laughs loudly)
Kim Tong-hwan: Roughly how much has been invested in Manchuria from within Chosŏn since the Incident?
Kong Chin-hang: As it's still the early stages, it's not that much, but since the Incident, it seems to be around 4 million won.
Yi Sŏng-hwan: That's right. Roughly 4 million won, but it will increase rapidly from now on. I hear that from Kyŏngsŏng, Messrs. Kim Yŏn-su (金䄵洙) and Pang Kyu-hwan (方奎煥); from Kaesŏng, Kong Chin-hang (孔鎭恒); from Taegu, Kim T'ae-wŏn (金台源) have invested. From P'yŏngyang, Mr. Kim Tong-wŏn (金東元), and others from various regions <133> seem to be following suit.
Chŏng Su-il: Then, to be more specific, comparing Chosŏn with South and North Manchuria, what are the land prices, climate, and other profit-related factors? Could you give examples to make it easier for the general public to understand?
Yi Sŏng-hwan: Before speaking on this, we first need to consider two things. That is the security issue: first, the bandit (匪賊) problem, and second, the Japan-Soviet (日蘇) war potential. However, considering the current Japan-Manchukuo relations, the bandit problem is not an issue. Second, although there might be minor regional conflicts, given the current situation in the Soviet Union, it doesn't seem to be a significant concern. Therefore, the "key" to investment lies in these two problems. If these are not issues, then we don't need to be overly afraid of investing. Therefore, looking at the capital trends or the current situation in North Manchuria, I believe now is the time to go. It seems the most opportune time for advancement, possessing ample potential. If you buy land, you can recoup the capital within that year (當年). That is, you can recover the land price (土價) with the harvest of the first year, and from the second year onwards, it can be considered profit. Because it is so advantageous, the fervor for investment is being encouraged.
Kong Chin-hang: I bought land the year before last and am now managing it in North Manchuria, and have continued to purchase more. For the reclaimed (開墾) land, I recovered the capital with the income from that land in the same year. Last year, although there was some flood damage (水敗), I still saw considerable profit.
Chŏng Su-il: Then, comparing land prices with Chosŏn, or the harvest comparison, what kind of ratio can we see? Also, which region—South Manchuria, North Manchuria, or Huabei—do you consider the cheapest and most promising?
Yi Sŏng-hwan: Generally, in China during the feudal era, under the "yuanzhang" (莊園) system, large areas were held by imperial relatives, nobles, and wealthy merchants. Unlike Chosŏn where land was already subdivided and fully privately owned, after the Incident, many owners were absent, or the gentry and merchants who fled became ruined. Therefore, there are many absentee landlords. If you find such land, it costs 2.7 or 2.8 *chŏn* per *p'yŏng* (坪; approx 3.3 sqm), the expensive ones are around 8 *chŏn*. Even in places like Fengtian (奉天; Mukden), which are sufficiently safe in terms of security, etc., you could buy land for 60 *chŏn* per *p'yŏng* (last spring). Even where transportation is convenient and the land is already well-developed, 1,500 *p'yŏng* <134> can be bought for 50 to 80 won. Considering this, in remote areas of North Manchuria like Sajagu (獅子口), 1,500 *p'yŏng* can be bought for 60 *chŏn* or 1 won 40 *chŏn*. So, the talk of rising land prices in Manchuria is limited to areas near cities; in such places, prices have doubled or tripled (騰貴), but elsewhere, they haven't risen much. Even recently, land was traded at 2.7 or 2.8 *chŏn* per *p'yŏng*.
Kong Chin-hang: Anyway, to put it simply, although the situation before and after the Incident is vastly different, even now, if you go to remote areas, you can buy land for 5 *chŏn* per *p'yŏng*. This refers to land of the same grade as land costing 1 won per *p'yŏng* in Chosŏn. So, for the price of 1 *p'yŏng* in Chosŏn, you can buy 20 *p'yŏng* in Manchuria. You can buy 20 times the amount of land. Look how advantageous this is, and shouldn't our compatriots take significant interest? I believe now is indeed the time to invest.

Huabei and the Situation of Migrant Compatriots
Kim Tong-hwan: Mr. Kim U-gŭm, you have spent over 20 years in Huabei and other parts of North China, gaining much experience in various businesses and seeing the reality firsthand. Could you please share your thoughts?
Kim U-gŭm: My opinion differs somewhat from those who spoke earlier. I will mainly talk about the situation of our compatriots in Huabei. Huabei refers to the area around the Great Wall, the demilitarized zone (非戰區). In Beiping (北平), Tianjin (天津), and elsewhere, about 1,000 households of our compatriots live. The Chosŏn people in this area mostly arrived empty-handed before the Incident and settled down. However, there are now 7 people with 200,000 won, and those who have lived there for over 20 years almost all have over 10,000 won. So, with the idea of "let's do our business with their money," we raised 500,000 won to establish the Huabei Industrial Co., Ltd. (華北産業株式會社). We plan to engage in agriculture under a 10-year plan and intend to initially bring 100 households as immigrants. The climate in Huabei is similar to that of Chŏlla Province, and fortunately, the Taihang Mountains (大幸山; likely Taihang Mountains 太行山) run north-south, making it very mild. Therefore, I believe immigration to Huabei is most suitable for us.
Kim Tong-hwan: Your words are very good. The idea that our compatriots, who lack capital, can conduct business using the money of the local people...
Yi Sŏng-hwan: I hold a slightly different opinion from Mr. Kim (U-gŭm). From a security standpoint, I believe Huabei immigration is premature.
Kim U-gŭm: Theory and practice are different. As long as people have strong determination <135> and abundant practicality, even if they lack skills and are uneducated, they can accumulate wealth. So, I think Huabei is good. Selling opium or running restaurants to make money is not necessarily impure. Even if we remain upright and don't engage in such businesses, it doesn't mean those activities won't flourish there. Thus, accumulating money this way and enabling our compatriots, who lack their own funds, to start businesses seems perfectly fine to me.
Yi Sŏng-hwan: While there's some logic to it, firstly, ownership rights are not recognized, and secondly, the North China issue remains unresolved. Therefore, considering the instability in terms of security and other circumstances, I oppose it.
Kim U-gŭm [Note: Seems speaker might still be Kim U-gŭm, despite name change in original text]: Excluding the security issue, the most advantageous aspect for farmers is that the local inhabitants lack skills in wet-field rice cultivation (수전). So, firstly, farming, and secondly, P'yŏngyang chestnuts (平壤栗) (called Yùtián 玉田 there). The output from here reaches 1.21 million won, yet they only receive 20 *chŏn* per *mal* (斗; unit of volume) there. Next, as sidelines, there are persimmons (柿), chicken farming (養鷄), and duck farming (鴨). The consumption of these chickens and ducks in Beijing (北京) and Tianjin alone amounts to 50,000 *jok* (a pair or unit). As for me, I believe Huabei immigration is the most advantageous. And regarding the wealth of the Chosŏn compatriots living here, during the silver export period some years ago, it was revealed they possessed 7 million won.

Views on Planned Immigration vs. Free Immigration
Kim Tong-hwan: Mr. Hong Yang-myŏng, you recently traveled through various regions. Please share your thoughts on this issue, particularly comparing the planned, collective immigration currently being encouraged by the authorities, with the free immigration that has been occurring. Also, regarding the current debate point, which immigration—to South/North Manchuria or Huabei—do you think is more viable?
Hong Yang-myŏng: First, I looked at the Yingkou (營口) and Hadong (河東) areas. They said there were fewer than 300,000 people there. Generally, free immigrants seemed to have low competitiveness for survival. As for planned immigration, or collective immigration, while it has some political protection (斗護), in a word, it's a failure. Regarding the question of whether Huabei or Manchuria offers more security for immigration, although Mr. Kim U-gŭm has spoken, I tend to agree with Mr. Yi Sŏng-hwan's view. The reason is that economic investment requires political guarantees. However, political stability is questionable everywhere, and factors like climate and customs cause general hesitation. But in the future, if these <136> doubts (疑訝) are resolved, people will naturally follow the profits. Therefore, it's probably correct to assume that people will lean towards South and North Manchuria rather than Huabei, which has considerably more political instability.
Yi Sŏng-hwan: I also view planned immigration as a failure. The reason is that organizations like Tōa Kangyō (東亞勸業; East Asia Development Company) and Mantetsu (滿鐵; South Manchuria Railway Co.) have brought immigrants collectively and systematically, but the results have generally been failures. The cause is that the immigrants belonging to these schemes become essentially agricultural serfs (農隷化). That is, looking at their plans so far, it would have been better to quickly establish them as owner-farmers to foster attachment to the land. Instead, they ignored the farmers' psychology and needlessly provided farming tools and other support over long periods. However, the costs of managing and supervising this, especially the expenses for the company executives, increased significantly, thereby wiping out the profits that should have gone to the farmers. This led to failure. We don't know what policies the newly established Chō-Man Takushoku Kaisha (朝滿拓殖會社; Chosŏn-Manchuria Colonization Company) will pursue, but if they repeat the past mistakes, it too will likely fail. Therefore, from our perspective, it would be better to encourage free immigration, provide assistance with travel expenses or other funds, and guide and encourage them to spread into every nook and cranny.
Kong Chin-hang: I also believe free immigration is more advantageous than planned immigration. Small farmers come to my farm asking to cultivate land. These are people who couldn't get farmland in Chosŏn and came freely, seeking the fertile Manchurian land they could cultivate as they wished. Our farm accommodates them as much as possible, the condition being that they intend to settle permanently with their wives (妻帶永住).
Wet-field rice cultivation (수전사업)—in this, Chosŏn farmers' skills and techniques are absolutely superior, unmatched by any other ethnic group in Manchuria. Also, profits are higher and more favorable to farmers than in Chosŏn. If one intends to start, doing so at this time would be much more advantageous. The reason is, even from an investor's perspective, now is the time for Chosŏn people to invest. If we wait a bit longer, once news spreads that security is completely resolved, large financial conglomerates from Osaka (大阪), Tokyo (東京), etc., will advance, making it impossible for us to enter then, and there will be no profit. Farmers too should migrate now; otherwise, Chinese and Mongolians will learn wet-field cultivation methods. As they gradually become proficient, we will definitely be unable to compete in terms of labor and diligence, leading to losses. Just last year, a Manchurian came to sell 1,200 *sŏk* (石; unit of volume) of unhulled rice (정조). In any case, whether for migrants or investors, now is the most suitable time to advance. <137>
Chang Yŏng-sun: Of course, there's the security issue in Manchuria, but if you're going to advance, you must do it now. Wait a little longer, and we'll end up regretting our tardiness again. Profits are high when the risk level is somewhat high; once the risk disappears, various parties gradually divide the share, so it won't be much. In any case, if you intend to advance, doing it right away is very advantageous.
Kim Tong-hwan: Large-scale farming methods, like using tractors ("추럭터") as done in America or the Soviet Union for cultivation—is that feasible?
Hong Yang-myŏng: It might be advantageous for dry-field farming (田作), but for wet-field rice cultivation (전수경작), tractors don't seem very beneficial.
Kong Chin-hang: Wet-field cultivation might be possible in the first year, but after 2 or 3 years, the soil in wet fields becomes compact, making it impossible to use tractors ("추럭"). They just sink in.

Appeals and Demands to the Governing Authorities
Kim Tong-hwan: Now then, let's speak boldly (大瞻) and frankly (卒直) without hesitation about the various conditions regarding advancement into Manchuria and Mongolia (滿蒙), addressing our appeals or hopes to the Manchukuo government, the Kwantung Army, various immigration authorities, the Government-General of Chosŏn, and other governing authorities (爲政當局).
Kong Chin-hang: On this point, more important than what ordinary investors like us want to say is the issue that Chosŏn people are not allowed ownership of more than 15 *chō* (町; approx. 15 hectares). If such a restriction is placed on the ownership rights recognized from July 1st, it's a huge blow to the advancement of investors.
Hong Yang-myŏng: Individuals are restricted to 15 *chō*, but does this also apply to companies and large financial groups? If so, that's a major problem.
Kong Chin-hang: They say companies or farm managers can get loans from Tōtaku (東拓; Oriental Development Company), Sen-Gin (鮮銀; Bank of Chosŏn), or the newly formed Sen-Man Taku (鮮滿拓; Chosŏn-Manchuria Colonization Co.). Otherwise, they have to locate their headquarters in Manchuria. Anyway, there are double and triple burdens.
Yi Sŏng-hwan: The first thing we should boldly propose to the authorities is the abolition of "discriminatory treatment" (차별대우). Second would be to ask them to establish a firm, unwavering (確乎不拔) fundamental policy on how they think Chosŏn compatriots should be guided and managed after advancing into Manchuria.
Kim U-gŭm: That's right. Discrimination is always the issue. It's true in Chinese territory (華地), and also in places like Manchuria. It always seems like there's no established policy regarding Chosŏn compatriots. <138> Besides that, we wish the nationality issue could also be resolved.
Pang T'ae-yŏng: As Mr. Yi Sŏng-hwan said, if we were to make one request, establishing a fundamental policy would be the prerequisite. Opinions still seem divided and inconsistent, but perhaps a policy will be established in the not-too-distant future.
Hong Yang-myŏng: When establishing that policy, it's crucial that they don't just favor their own position but carefully consider (斟酌) the genuine cries of our compatriots residing there and those in Chosŏn proper. That's what's most needed, but whether they'll actually do that is the question.

Mr. Kim Chŏng-hyŏng, you recently inspected various regions from the perspective of medicine and pharmaceuticals. Do you have any impressions or requests related to this field?
Kim Chŏng-hyŏng: I have many impressions and saw many things, but since the previous speakers have covered almost everything, I'll briefly speak only about the medical field. Generally, there's a lack of knowledge about medical treatment, and the related institutions are insufficient. There's a funny story: a certain person opened a clinic, but the ordered medicine hadn't arrived yet. A Manchurian came seeking treatment. Since this person couldn't say the medicine hadn't arrived, they examined the patient and, having no other choice, wrapped up some tooth powder (齒磨粉) as if it were medicine. The patient who took it strangely recovered and came back to express gratitude. Haha...

Kim Chŏng-hyŏng: This is just a funny story, but one can imagine how low their medical knowledge is. Hearing that there are only 4 hospitals and 2 patent medicine dealers (賣藥商) for the 2 million compatriots in Manchuria, I don't know how pathetic it feels. Will this be enough? If we focus on this area and advance, it seems very promising. Also, on the one hand, while our compatriots did commit wrongdoings in the past by dealing opium, using that as a pretext (憑藉) to deny permits for pharmaceutical businesses (藥種商) is going too far. The authorities need to reconsider this (省察). I believe there will be considerable advancement once the legal permit system comes into effect from July this year.
Chŏng Su-il: Chosŏn traditional pharmacies use a lot of medicinal herbs (당재). Did you find any herbs worth importing into Chosŏn?
Kim Chŏng-hyŏng: There's a lot of licorice (감초), angelica root (當歸; Tanggwi), Cnidium officinale (葺; likely 川芎 Ch'ŏn'gung), and other medicinal herbs. Moreover, their low prices are astonishing. If the tariff barriers were lowered somewhat, I believe it would be a very promising trade.
Yi Sŏng-hwan: Licorice used in Chosŏn is almost entirely sourced from there. <139> If such items could be traded, it would be very advantageous since they are used in large quantities...

Advancement into Manchuria and Future Outlook
Kim Tong-hwan: You have shared many valuable insights over several hours. Finally, one more thing I'd like to ask you to speak about: as you know, immigrant trains are still running frequently, meaning hundreds, even thousands, are heading to Manchuria daily. Regardless of whether this is free advancement or planned advancement, do you have an optimistic or pessimistic outlook for the future?
Yi Sŏng-hwan: I am absolutely optimistic. Broadly, there are five ethnic groups (五族) in Manchuria: Manchus, Mongols, Hans, Yamato (大和族; Japanese), and Chosŏn (朝鮮族; Koreans). Among these five, the Manchus are not even a question, the Mongols are a very small minority, and the Han Chinese are mostly descendants of the large migration of "Shandong coolies" (山東苦力) during Sun Yat-sen's time, making them the most numerous. However, when they migrated, they did so with the backing of gentry or large financial powers and undertook various activities. But after the Incident, a major change occurred, causing them to lose that backing. Now, they are drifting like migratory birds ("渡り島"), constantly moving. Therefore, they always harbor thoughts of returning to their homeland, whether they manage to save money or not. Due to this psychological tendency, their influence will gradually decrease. Then, the remaining issue involves the Yamato and Chosŏn groups. Regarding the Yamato group, due to differences in lifestyle, culture, and various other circumstances, their living standards don't match, so they have no intention of settling permanently in Manchuria. If they succeed, fine, but even if they fail, they return. This fact is well understood by looking at the past and present situation of immigration to Chosŏn. So, this too is not really an issue. What remains then is our Chosŏn group. Considering climate, customs, geography, and all other aspects, they are the rightful masters of that land. Moreover, historically, Manchuria was our ancient homeland, so the only thought that arises is living in one's own homeland. Furthermore, concerning agricultural immigration, our compatriots are the most superior. In terms of skills, wet-field reclamation techniques, and everything else, they are excellent, so it's definitely positive. A nation's two main elements are land and people. Since Chosŏn people have the greatest suitability for that land, I believe they will prevail, leading me to conclude with an optimistic outlook for the future.
Kong Chin-hang: As mentioned during the demands section earlier, if the unreasonable restrictions and discrimination against the advancement of Chosŏn people are abolished, it meets the primary condition for our compatriots' advancement. Psychologically and realistically, <140> I believe we can be optimistic. In any case, I believe it's best for investors, especially those investing in land enterprises, to advance significantly, establish all necessary facilities, and prepare to welcome our great march forward.
Hong Yang-myŏng: Mr. Yi Sŏng-hwan mentioned "Kando Extensionism." If we trace our roots, West and North Kando are merely neighboring provinces. For someone from Kyŏngsang Province, South and North Manchuria evoke the same psychological reaction as moving to North Hamgyŏng or North P'yŏngan. If we advance with Kando Extensionism, I believe the primary inhabitants (살님 사리꾼) of that land will inevitably be our compatriots.
Kim Tong-hwan: It's now 11 o'clock. Thank you for sharing such valuable and beneficial insights during this long session. On behalf of our company staff and readers, I express my gratitude. Furthermore, I sincerely thank you for attending despite your busy schedules. With this, we conclude today's roundtable discussion.

Impression of Meeting High Officials of the Manchukuo Government
Yi Sŏng-hwan
My most vivid memory of meeting a high official of the Manchukuo government is meeting Yuan Jinkai (袁金凱). I recall meeting Mr. Yuan around the time Chosŏn people were massacring Chinese people (likely referring to anti-Chinese riots in Korea, 1931). At that time, he was the chairman of the local maintenance committee and a midwife to the founding of Manchukuo; he is now the Minister of the Privy Council (尚書大臣; equivalent to Lord Keeper of the Privy Seal). He doesn't wear Western clothes, always Chinese attire, and is now in his 60s. He is slightly built, always wears glasses, and upon meeting me back then, his magnanimous and composed demeanor, the air of a respected elder, leaves a good impression on anyone. When we met, I couldn't speak Chinese, and he couldn't speak Chosŏn language, so we ended up communicating through writing (筆談). I first wrote,
"The massacre of your countrymen that occurred recently within Chosŏn proper was not the collective will (總意) of all Chosŏn people. Know that it was retaliation by misguided mobs," adding, "What are your future thoughts regarding the Chosŏn people issue?" He barely understood and replied,
"Based on my conjecture, after our country is stabilized, there will surely be special measures (瓣法) for the Chosŏn people."
That's what he said. Then, not long after, what I had said was immediately leaked somewhere, causing me countless troubles and distress. Seeing that our private written conversation was leaked elsewhere, I cannot trust that so-called prominent Chinese officials and political figures necessarily uphold good faith. <141> Doesn't this one incident vividly reveal the true face of those who revere so-called empty formalities ("커취" 虛禮)?...

Baptism by Pistol and "Me"
Yi Sŏng-hwan
While traveling throughout North and South Manchuria and other parts of China, I never encountered horse bandits (馬賊), but I did experience a couple of incidents that were thrilling and still make me shudder to think about.
It was right after the Incident. It was the third day after Zhang Xueliang (張學良) fled from Jinzhou (錦州) Castle and Japanese troops entered the city. Martial law ("계엄령") was declared in the city, forbidding outings after 10 PM, and suspicious interrogations ("불심심문") were conducted. If you hesitated, anyone could be shot dead – notices ("布令") were posted inside and outside the city walls, creating a truly terrifying atmosphere. However, overcome by the melancholy of travel (旅愁) and unable to contain my feelings, I casually went out after dinner for a walk. My feet led me outside the city walls, and by chance, I stepped into "Café Suzuran" (카페-一スズラン). Drinking and chatting, I lost track of time and stayed until 1 AM. I needed to get back to my lodging, but the security was strict, and I couldn't just break through. Hesitating, I decided to risk it, hailed a rickshaw (양차), and headed towards the city center. Sure enough, shouts of "Dare ka?" (誰レカ?; Who goes there?) erupted from all sides, and four pistols were pressed against my neck. I felt utterly doomed. Hesitation meant losing my life, so I first identified myself as "Chosŏn person" and confessed the truth. They reprimanded me severely for roaming around in such danger despite seeing the proclamation, but then let me go. One reason they released me, I later found out, was that officers happened to be staying at the inn where I was lodging, and this was confirmed.
Another time was in Fengtian (奉天; Mukden). At that time too, people out late were checked, and anyone slightly suspicious was shot. I was enjoying myself at the "International Café" (국제 카-페) there and again lost track of time. On my way back to the lodging, three soldiers suddenly appeared – who knows from where, they were very quick – pressed pistols right against my throat, and questioned me. There was nothing to hide, nor could I hide anything, so I frankly explained the situation. After repeated shouting and detailed investigation, they let me go. Thinking back now, my behavior was quite reckless. After receiving this baptism by pistol (拳銃洗禮) twice, the terrifying thought keeps me from going out late at night whenever I go to Manchuria. <142>

I Barely Escaped Death
Kim U-gŭm
Though I wandered through various parts of China for over 20 years, I never actually encountered horse bandits (馬賊). I occasionally heard news of attacks by bandits. However, I have one story about barely escaping death, which I will tell now.
It was when I was in Jinan (濟南), Shandong (山東) province. Zhang Zongchang (張宗昌) was at the height of his power, flexing his muscles. At that time, there were only 18 households of our compatriots there. One day, three friends and I, four of us in total, went out for a walk. My three friends were all heavy drinkers (酒客). Being unable to drink much myself, I was left aside while they drank heavily. All three became completely intoxicated, truly "oblivious" (망지불사), reaching a state where the sky seemed as small as a coin. In their drunken state, useless bravado surfaced, and they headed towards the training ground (련병장) where Zhang Zongchang was drilling his troops. A large notice was posted there: "Anyone entering the area without permission will be shot dead (Šƒ殺), regardless of who they are." But my friends, emboldened by alcohol, thought, "What's the big deal?" and entered the training ground. I desperately tried to stop them, shouting "Oh no!", but the three of them pulled me along, and being outnumbered, I was dragged inside instead.
Well, things having come to this, more than thinking "This is trouble," the thought "Now I'm dead" made my vision go dark. Sure enough, dozens of soldiers rushed over shouting, tied the four of us up tightly, and threw us into the military court (軍法廳), where we spent the night. The next day, we were dragged out. Plaques inscribed with our crime (罪名) were hung around our necks, just like dog tags, and we were taken to the military court. There, we were interrogated. After stating the facts and identifying ourselves as Chosŏn people, they must have immediately inquired with the Japanese consulate, because shortly after, an interpreter arrived. He scolded us for our illegal actions, gave us a stern lecture, and then released us, so we survived. We narrowly escaped certain death, saved by sheer luck.

Fragmentary Jottings (餘錄片片)

While traveling through North and South Manchuria, the thing that most often caught my eye and evoked the thought, "Ah, our ancestors once lived on this land," the sign that brought forth feelings of reminiscence, was the painting of a dragon (용) and tiger (호) pasted above the main gates. Visiting villages in various regions, one can find traces of such paintings <143> stuck on old houses. Considering that the colors (彩色) are exactly the same as those in Chosŏn, without the slightest difference (일호 차착이 업는 것), and the brushstrokes (필치) are identical, it makes me think this land must surely be ours. (Yi Sŏng-hwan)

If you go to the Maritime Province (沿海州) or other parts of West and North Kando, you can discover that the lifestyle, housing structure, bedding (침방), and other customs of the "Sago" people (?) living there are completely identical to those of our compatriots. Viewed historically, this also shows that they were descendants of the same people as us. (Yi Sŏng-hwan)

In remote villages of North Manchuria, Sajagu, Tieling (鐵嶺), and other areas, one can find stone mortars (돌절구 石臼). Seeing that the Manchu people living there now don't even know what these were used for and have left them discarded in the dirt suggests they are relics (遺跡) of our ancestors. (Kong Chin-hang) <140>

**Summary**

This 1936 roundtable discussion features prominent Korean businessmen and journalists debating the opportunities and challenges for Korean migration and investment in Manchuria (Manchukuo) and North China. Participants discuss the economic potential, particularly in agriculture (esp. rice cultivation) and mining, citing cheaper land and higher yields compared to Korea. They advocate for "Kando Extensionism," framing migration not as fleeing poverty but as reclaiming ancestral lands. While optimistic about future prospects due to Koreans' suitability and skills, they express serious concerns about security, political instability (especially in North China), discriminatory policies by Japanese and Manchukuo authorities (like land ownership restrictions), and the failures of state-sponsored planned immigration compared to free migration. The discussion provides a fascinating window into Korean colonial-era perspectives on expansion into Manchuria. Historians might find the explicit articulation of "Kando Extensionism," the candid discussion of leveraging local capital in China, the critique of Japanese/Manchukuo policies and planned migration failures, and the raw accounts of life under occupation (like Yi Sŏng-hwan's encounters with Japanese military) particularly insightful regarding Korean agency, nationalist sentiment, and the complex realities of settler colonialism within the Japanese empire.

우리 同胞와 滿洲와의 産業關係
金東煥(本社主幹) 여러분께서 모다 밧부신 데다가 각 방면으로 동분서주하시는 터에 멀리 開城서 또 市內에서도 먼 곳에 게시면서 이 가티 만사를 제페하시고 이 모힘에 參席하여 주신데 첫재로 감사함을 마지 안사오며 主催者側인 저의 一同은 이 모힘이 얼마나 의미가 깁고 또 중대함을 스스로 늣기는 바이올시다. 여기 와 주신 여러분으로 말삼하면 대체로 南北滿洲, 또는 外蒙, 華北, 其他 中國 各地에서 或은 농장, 광업, 공업 할 것 업시 모든 산업부분에서 경영 혹은 실제상황을 골골루 체험하신 터이라 이에 대한 포부가 만흐실 줄 암니다. 그래서 이에 대한 모든 것을 말삼하여 주십소사 하는 것이외다. 더구나 朝鮮 內地에서 그곳으로 이주한 동포가 이미 2백만명에 달하고 또 해마다 6, 7, 8만명식이 남부녀대로＜128＞ 고토를 떠나 滿洲 荒野로 정처업시 간다는 것과 또 토병(土兵)의 박해, 지방 관헌의 가렴주구(苛斂誅求), 지방의 압박 등 이중 삼중의 위협을 밧고 잇다는 것도 듯습니다.
그러면 이 가장 우리 朝鮮과 밀접하고 또 긴밀하기가 격장한 한집가튼 만주와 우리와의 지리적 또 산업적으로 큰 해(害)가 될 줄 압니다. 그럿타면 이와 가튼 련환적(聯環的)으로 중대 긴밀한 관계를 가진 피차(彼此)의 관계를 여하히 하여야 할지 이 여러 가지 점에 대하야  이모저모로 툭 터러노코 말삼하여 주시옵소서. 더구나 今年이 본사 창간 8주년인 만큼 이번에 단연히 뜻을 決하여 다른 신문 잡지사에서 하지 못한 것을 先着하려하는 의미도 여기 잇고 또 말삼하여 주시는 분들도 이 가튼 뜻을 가지시고 알알 삿삿치 말삼하여 주섯스면 합니다. 그리고 사회는 江村 鄭秀日君과 本人이 번가러 하겟습니다.
鄭秀日(本社編輯長) 제일 먼저 여러분과 이 자리에 말삼을 듯고저 하는 것은 우리 朝鮮 內地와 가장 긴밀한＜129＞ 관계를 가지고 잇는 滿洲와의 산업문제인데 문제가 산업문제인만큼 정치문제 가튼 것은 떠나 純 산업관계에 대하여 말삼하여 주시기를 바랍니다. 산업이라고 하면, 공업, 상업, 농업, 광업, 임업, 목축업 기타 여러 가지를 指摘한 것이 되어 퍽 범위가 광범합니다. 그뿐 아니라 이 모든 것을 세별한다면 그 부분이 퍽 여러 방면으로 분류가 됩니다만은, 여기서는 주로 朝鮮同胞와의 關係되는 것 그 中에도 在滿 朝鮮同胞와의 관계 또는 朝鮮 內에서의 관계가 제일 긴밀한 것을 들어 말삼하여 주십시오. 그래서 우리가 그 땅에 발을 듸듸든지 또는 투자를 한다면, 어떤 방면에 주로 착목을 하여야 올흘지 이에 대한 발근 敎示가 잇기를 바랍니다.
(一同緊張, 黙考...)
金東煥 北滿 방면에 만흔 투자를 하시고 또 농장을 방금 경영하실 뿐 아니라 南北滿洲, 西北間島  등지를 시찰하서서 가장 이 방면에 만흔 체험을 가지신 孔先生(孔鎭恒氏)께서 말삼하여 주섯스면  조켓습니다.
孔鎭恒 개괄적으로 말하면 在滿 동포뿐 아니라 만주 각지에 잇는 동포들의 생활은 일체로 浮虛하다고 할 수밧게 업습니다. 즉 기업가다운 기업가가 업다는 것이외다. 경제적으로든지 사업경륜으로든지 보잘 것이 업다는 것인데 이제 그 중에서 몃분 사업가를 추려내자면 間島에 강재호(間島興業株式會社 其他 四個會社 經營) 開原에, 洪承均(精米業, 消費組合) 모다 6,70만원의 자금을 가지고 지도적 역할을 가지고 잇는 분이오, 哈爾賓, 新京 등지에도 몃몃 분이 잇스나, 事變前으로 論하면 대체로 기업가다운 기업가가 업섯습니다. 그럿튼 것이 사변후에는 격변하야 현재로는 투자가도 생기고 기업가도 생겨 날로 재벌 진출이 현저하여 감니다. 그래서 압흐로는 산업적으로 볼만한 것이 만허 질 것이오 또 가장 희망을 갓게 됩니다. 그러나 좀더 치안방면 즉 정치적 불안이 해결된다면 더욱 재벌 진출을 유치하야써 기업의 진흥이 懸殊할 줄 압니다.
李晟煥 나는 무르신 말에 대하야 이러케 대답하랴 합니다. 즉 산업이라고 하면 모든 부문을 총칭한 말이니까 그 중에서 나는 가장 朝鮮 사람과 밀접 불가분의 관계에 노혀 잇는 산업만을 들추어 보기로 합니다. 공업(중경)은 모다 말이 못 되고 임업도 자금관계로 참여할 자격이  업스니 재목, 팔푸 공업이 유망하지만 할 수 업고 수산업(勃海灣, 吉林, 松花)도 좀 잇섯스나  보잘 것 업고 오직 내가 보는 점으로는 광업과 농업뿐인데＜130＞ 광업은 작년 9월 1일 광업령 발포이래 그 出願數는 日本 내지인, 朝鮮人의 것이 다수인데 이로 인하야 일시는 광업 이민설까지 생겨난 만큼 참으로 기대가 큼니다. 그 까닭은 우리동포는 예전부터 금광기타에 경험이 만코, 또 기술도 상당하며 더욱 朝鮮지대보다 産金率이 높흠으로 가장 유망한 줄로 생각합니다. 그 다음은
농업인데 즉 耕種農業, 牧畜農業 두 가지 중에 목축농 등은 장래문제로 미룰 수밖에 업고 오직 경종농업인데 이것은 과거사실에 비춰보든지 또는 장래할 제종관계로  보아 가장 긴밀하고 또 중대성이 잇다고 봄니다. 우리는 이점에 가장 중시하여야 할 의미가 잠재하엿다고 봅니다. 그래서 나는 동포에게 권할 것은 우리 살길은 이곳으로 적극 진출함에 잇다는 것을 제의하고 십습니다.

間島 延長主義로써
金東煥 李晟煥씨의 말삼을 들으면 퍽 滿洲진출에 대하야 낙관주의적 색채로 말삼하시는 모양인데 아직도 朝鮮내 동포의 심적 경향은 滿洲는 치안, 교육, 통신, 기타로 보아 불안을 늣끼는 감이 잇는데, 만약 이러타고 하면, 근본적으로 이민을 권하여야 조흘까요?
李晟煥 우리는 먼저, 종래로 품고 잇든 망상 한 가지를 버려야＜131＞ 하겟습니다. 그것은 잇때 것 우리는『滿洲로 가는 사람은 朝鮮 내에서 살 수가 업스니까 그저 滿洲 벌판으로 방랑의 길, 유랑의 발로, 정처 업시 막연한 길을 떠난다』라는 생각을 가지고 보내고 가고 하엿습니다. 그러나 이 생각은 지금부터 아조 머리속으로부터 뽑아 내 던져야 합니다. 그 대신으로 우리는 지금부터는『만주로 가는 것은, 우리 선조가 살든 古土로 돌아간다』라는 생각을 각각 가져야 합니다. 거기를 가면 이웃에 살든 사람, 친척들과 가티 살 수 잇다는 생각을 가지고 가도록 하여야 합니다. 즉 밧구어 말하면 「間島 延長主義로써」가는 것이외다. 間島는 일즉부터 우리 동포가 집단하여 몃 백년을 살어오기 때문에, 교육기관, 치안, 통신, 교류 기타 모든 방면이 고토와 조곰도 달음이 업는 만큼 間島로 간다는 것은 맛치 慶尙道 사람이 咸慶道로 살너 가는 폭밧게 아니 녀겻습니다. 그러니만큼 불안이나 공포가 따르지 안엇습니다. 똑 그와 가튼 마음으로 남북만으로 진출하라는 것이외다.
孔鎭恒 나도 李선생의 말삼과 동감임니다. 사실로 우리는 「살 수 업서 방랑의 길로」라는 예전의 생각을 버리고 가장 진지한 태도로 「우리가 살든 땅을 도로 차저 간다」는 심리상 시정을 바라는 바입니다. 그리고 朝鮮 재벌에 원하는 것은 금후는 될 수 잇는대로 투자하라는 것을 권하고 십습니다. 의무로라도 투자를 하라는 것이 나의 주장입니다.
李晟煥 더구나 우리가 滿洲로 진출하기를 권하는 의미는 지대가 광활하고 도처에 옥토가 잇스며, 200년 간을 시비치 안터래도 좃타는 지질학자의 명언도 잇는만큼, 우리는 노력만 제공하면 이 天賦의 부원개발이 가능한만큼 또 더구나 인구의 분포 상태로 보아 朝鮮 내에서만 跼踳하지 말고, 광활한 신개척지로 나아가 살어 보는 것도 근대 신인의 할 바라고 봅니다.
또 소작 제도로 보아서 南滿은 4대 6, 北滿은 3대 7이니, 지주와 소작인의 비율로 보더래도 朝鮮보다 유리한 처지요, 역사적으로도 앗까 말한 바와가티 고토와 달음 업슴으로 이에 착목할 것인데, 다만 문제는 치안이라고 하나, 이것도 집단적(間島와 가티)으로 살게되면 해결 될 것이니 이런 의미에 잇서서 나는 間島 延長主義를 제창하는 바이외다. 즉 우리는 「滿洲의 흙덩이와, 우리 살(肉)과 반죽이 되어야 한다는 친밀을 가짐이 가장 간절한 문제인 줄 암니다.
(잇때 方台榮씨 출석, 대우관계로 遲하얏슴을 일동에게 謝하고 着席)＜132＞
金東煥 잇때 것 여러분이 만히 말삼을 하섯습니다. 方선생께서도 哈爾濱까지 두루 단녀 오섯다고 하니 조흔 말삼좀 들여 주섯으면 조켓습니다. 즉 여행하시든 중에 보시고 들으신대로, 우리와 滿洲 산업과의 관계되는 것을...
方台榮 나는 최근에는 아니 갓섯고 哈爾濱에 잇는 어떤 친구의 소개로 어느 친구가 회사를 맨드는데 좀 도어 주엇슬 뿐이고 그 다음에는 내가 서적 인쇄를 하는 관계상 교과서판매 관계로 갓다가 2,3일 만에 온 것밧게 업서 滿洲 사정은 白紙입니다. 그리고 오날 貴社에서 우리에게 가장 적절한 문제를 가지고 말삼하신다기에 저도 후일에 참고로 들으러 뛰어왓지 말을 하러 온 것이 아닙니다 허허...
金東煥 선생이 최근 滿洲에 거대한 투자를 하섯다는데요?...
方台榮 아니올시다. 方奎煥씨인 게지로. 먼 친척입니다. 이것이 아마 訛傳이 된 것 갓습니다.
허허...일동 네? 그래요 하하…(일동 大笑)
金東煥 대체 사변 후에 朝鮮내에서 滿洲로 투자한 금액이 대체 얼마나 됩니까?
孔鎭恒 아직은 초기인 만큼 그다지 만치 못하지만 사변 후 대체로 4백만원은 되는가 봅니다.
李晟煥 그럿치오. 대체 4백만원인데 금후로는 가속도로 늘 것입니다. 이제 드르니까 京城에선 金䄵洙, 方奎煥, 開城엔 孔鎭恒, 大邱에선 金台源 이러한 諸氏가 하엿고 平壤에선 金東元씨 其外에도 각 지방에서＜133＞ 뒤를 이여 나스는 것 갓습니다.
鄭秀日 그러면 좀더 구체적으로 꼭 집어 말한다면, 대체 朝鮮과 南北 滿洲에 대비하야 土價라든지 또는 기후라든지 기타 수익관계가 어떠한지 그것에 대하야 예를 들어서 말삼하섯스면 일반이 알어듯기 쉬울 것 갓습니다.
李晟煥 이것에 대하야 말하랴는 전제로 먼저 우리는 두가지를 생각할 필요가 잇습니다. 즉 치안 문제인데 제1은 匪賊 문제와, 제2는 日蘇 전쟁 그것입니다. 그런데 匪賊 문제, 지금 日滿 관계로 보아 문제가 되지 안코 제2는 이것 역시 다소의 지역적 분쟁은 잇슬지 몰라도 蘇聯  현하 정세로 보아 문제 삼을 것이 업슬 것 갓습니다. 그럼으로 투자 관계의 「열쇠」는 이 두 문제인데 이것이 문제가 되지 안흐면 우리는 투자에, 그다지 겁낼 것이 업습니다. 그럼으로 나는 자본 동태로 보든지 또는 지금의 북만 사정으로 보든지 지금 건너가야 할 것으로 봄니다. 지금이 가장 진출하기에 편리한 가능성을 다분이 가지고 잇나 봄니다. 토지를 사 가지면, 當年에 그 자본을 빼낼 수 잇습니다. 즉 당년 수확을 가지고 土價를 빼내고 제 2년 채부터는 수익으로 볼 수가 잇습니다. 그만큼 유리하기 때문에 투자열이 고취되는 터입니다.
孔鎭恒 내가 토지를 재작년에서 사서 지금 北滿에서 경영하고 또 이어 매수하엿는데 개간한 것은 그 당년에 그 토지 수입으로 자금을 건젓고 작년에는 다소 水敗를 보앗스나 상당한 수익을 보앗습니다.
鄭秀日 그러면 토지가로 보아서 朝鮮과의 대비 또는 추수의 대비는 얼마나한 비율로 볼 수가 잇스며 또는 南滿 또는 北滿,  華北 등 어떤 지역이 가장 저렴하고 유망하다고 보심니까.

꼭 죽을 걸 살엇소
金友琴
나도 20여 년을 中國 각지에 돌어 단엿서야 실지로 마적(馬賊)을 만난 일은 업다. 간혹 마적에게 습격(襲擊) 당햇다는 소식은 들엇지만. 그런데 내가 꼭 죽을 것을 사러난 이약이가 한가지 잇기로 지금 말한다.
山東성 제남(濟南)에 잇슬 때인데 장종창(張宗昌)이가 한참 득세하야 팔을 뽑낼 때에 그때 그곳에는 우리 동포가 겨우 18호 밖에 업슬 때다. 어느 날 친고 3인과 나와 합 4인이 산책을 나섯다가 친고들 三인은 모다 큰 주객(酒客)인지라, 못 먹는 나는 저처 노코 술을 잔뜩 먹어 3인이 모다 대취하야 그야말로 「망지불사」로 하눌이 돈 짝만큼 보힐 지경까지 일으럿섯다. 그래서 취중에 고만 쓸데업는 객긔가 나서 장종창이가 군대를 거느리고 鍊兵하는 련병장에를 갓섯다. 그 곳에는 대서 특서로 「허가 업시 구역내에 침입하는 자는 누구를 물론하고 총살(Šƒ殺)한다」고 써붓첫다. 그런데 이 친구들은 술낌에 「그까짓것」하며 련병장에 침입을 하는데 나는 어마 뜨거라 하고 못들어 가도록 붓잡는다는 것이, 세 사람이 끄는 바람에 중과부적으로 돌이어 끌여 드러갓다.
자- 이리되고 보니 큰 일이란 것 보다 「인제는 죽엇다」하는 생각에 눈이 캄캄합듸다. 아니나 달을까 수십명 군인, 소리를 치고 달려들어 네사람을 고대고 꼭꼭 묶어 군법청(軍法廳)으로 몰어다가 넛터니 그대로 하로밤을 지냇다. 그 이튼 날 꺼집어 내는데 네사람 「목」에는 죄명(罪名)을 쓴 패를 맛치 개목아지에 패를 채우듯 채우더니 군법청으로 끌여갓다. 거기서 심문을 당하는데 사실대로 말을 하고 朝鮮 사람인 것을 말하엿더니, 즉시 日本령사관으로 조회를 하엿넌지 얼마 잇다가 통역관이 와서 불법한 행동을 힐난하더니 엄중히 훈계를 한 후 노아 주기에 살어 왓다. 하마터면 꼭 죽을 것인데 천행으로 살어낫다.

北滿, 獅子口, 鐵嶺 기타 오지의 촌락에서 돌절구(石臼)를 발견할 수 잇는데, 지금 거기 사는 만족은 그것이 무엇에 쓰는 것인 줄도 몰으고 그대로 흙속에 던저 둔 것을 보아도 우리 선조들의 유적(遺跡)인 것을 말하는 것 갓습듸다. (孔鎭恒)＜140＞
