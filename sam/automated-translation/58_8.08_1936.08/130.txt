Folk Song Sentiments Heard in My Hometown, Menariya, Menariya
						Han Chŏng-dong

Folk song sentiments heard in my hometown!
Having received a letter requesting something under this title at noon on the day before the deadline, just one day away, I couldn't help but hesitate for a moment. As someone possessing nothing, wouldn't writing something like this carelessly risk misrepresenting the unique pride of my hometown, its folk songs, elsewhere? This concern crossed my mind.
However, the topic itself was deeply inspiring to me, and since I had always wanted to write something about my hometown's folk songs, I decided, perhaps presumptuously, to take up the pen out of a desire to convey even a ten-thousandth part of the uniqueness and beautiful sentiment of my hometown's folk songs (especially those of Yonggang and Kangsŏ) on such an occasion. Therefore, I simply wrote as the pen moved, without the leisure to revise even once, and I ask you to please overlook this point.
Although I know well this is not the place to discuss what folk songs are, I wish to borrow a few lines for convenience. That is, <108> a folk song is likely the natural music of a certain people, the outpouring of their ethnic poetic sentiment. In other words, it is poetry and music accumulated spontaneously wherever the seven emotions—joy, anger, sorrow, pleasure, love, hate, desire—are stirred, as a comprehensive expression of that people's customs, life, and environment. Therefore, I want to see folk songs as both a complete record of that people and a distinct historical trace.
So, do we Chosŏn people all know Chosŏn's folk songs well? Not necessarily. People from the Southern Provinces (南道) don't know Northern Province (北道) folk songs well, and people from the Northern Provinces don't know Southern Province folk songs well. This is naturally different from other ethnic groups, but even within one people, it is likely because customs, life, and environment differ slightly. This is where the term 'hyangt'o' (hometown, native place) arises. Consequently, the sentiment of folk songs inevitably differs from one hometown to another. 'Hyangt'o' means hometown. When a person is born, they already have a hometown. The songs that sing of the scenery and sentiments of that hometown would be 'hyangt'o minyo' (hometown folk songs). Therefore, folk songs are natural poetry, natural music, born from the soil.
So, what are the natural poems, natural music, the folk songs born from the soil of my hometown, generally like?

T'aryŏng (Ballad): Simpleton
Your daddy, my daddy, both stepdaddies,
You threw a tantrum, demanding a piggyback ride.
Wiped your nose, fed you rice, raised you up,
Now you grab the shafts [of the cart] and want a divorce.
The sin of having a stepdaddy deserves death,
For the price of raising you, just take me away.

Oh, the evils of early marriage! Oh, how anguished must her heart be to curse her parents and even resolve to be beaten to death, volunteering to be taken away? She was indeed the source of ruin. What more is there to discuss when such practices were prevalent, especially among the middle and upper propertied classes?

"Yellow head, topknot like a spinning wheel spindle,
When will it grow long enough to live with my beloved?"

Compare this with the 'Kinari' song. The latter is a bit more scathing. Consequently, that evil custom was gradually rejected and eventually eradicated.

(T'aryŏng) Numb Heart
This body is a sold body, just mowing grass,
Yesterday two stone-loads, today how many loads.
If I mow for a season, it'll be hundreds of loads,
With every load, only sorrow increases.
A dull sickle blade can be sharpened and used,
But what cannot be sharpened is this heart of mine.

Tears of sympathy flow. A poor bachelor! He is another's hired hand! Although he too has great ambitions, the seasons pass, age increases, and nothing goes as intended, so his heart can only grow heavier and more troubled! Thus, the only place for him to pour out his woes is <109> the song that comes out with his sighs!

"Is this heart the Taedonggang River, at ebb tide?
Confine it, confine it, there's no end."
Just like this 'Kinari' song, this person has endless time like hair, and ceaseless anguish (惱悶) like a waterway follows him. Ah, this unbearable state of his heart!

(T'aryŏng) Enemy
Parting in death, lower than an enemy,
Parting in life, cannot be lower than an enemy.
An enemy in death, an enemy that cannot be made,
An enemy in life, an enemy that cannot be made more so.
In a world of enemies, enemies are many,
Living or dead, enemy, yet again enemy.

That enemy! It is truly a song of desperate parting, a truthful expression that could not but emerge from such a desperate heart. Why does the demon of separation by death and life cling so persistently to this person?
One cannot help but recall the 'Kinari': "Startled awake deep in the night, I wept even at another's parting." As the proverb goes, "My own nose is three measures long" [meaning one worries about oneself first], so why care about another's sorrow! But his desperate situation makes another's parting seem like his own sorrow. Let him borrow another's situation to weep out all his sorrow. And let's see if, even for a brief moment, a feeling of relief might arise.

(T'aryŏng) Reed Flute (Kalp'iri)
Irisŏm islet, Torungsŏm islet, Ch'ŏngsanp'o estuary (Place names in Kangsŏ, near the Taedonggang River)
When the moon rises, come by boat from Konyanggae (River name on the opposite bank east of Irisŏm - Konyanggang)
Saying you can't find me because the reed field is deep,
If you cut a reed leaf, it can become a flute.

It is a promise, both innocent and kindly meticulous (親切叮嚀). And thinking of that reed leaf flute, it seems like a signal meaning "I've arrived" if they come, or perhaps, if they cannot come, the flute sound itself serves as sufficient consolation.

"Outside the door, the rustling of dried greens (시라리타례),
Even the blowing wind deceives me."
Might this not be the owner of the 'Kinari'? Waiting and waiting, that anxious heart is deceived even by the dried greens rustling in the wind. Knowing it's the rustling of dried greens, still, perhaps, perhaps, they keep being deceived. Ah—might they not be making promises here and there while writing letters like this? Oh, the heart of that person possessed by the fiercely burning flame of longing (憧憬)!
After reading the few examples of 'T'aryŏng' and 'Kinari' above, one might offer a commonplace assessment that they are just so-so. However, that is not the case; without actually visiting the place and experiencing it, one can never truly taste its deep flavor. <110>
How much more so for the strange sentiments of those who have worn clothes, eaten food, and lived there for decades, even centuries? There is definitely a unique taste embedded within, surpassing the imagination of others. In other words, there can be nothing else possessing a greater magic than one's own song, singing about one's own surroundings, known only to oneself. Therefore, when I listen to or sing my hometown's folk songs (especially T'aryŏng and Kinari), and reflect upon my hometown's social modern values, customs, culture, and sentiments, I simply become absorbed in them, losing the capacity to collect my恍惚 (hwanghol - entranced) state of mind.
And not just the lyrics, but when it comes to the melody's tune being vexingly beautiful and subtle (微妙) (especially 'Kinari' is even more so), what can one say but stand agape and stunned? I have scribbled down thoughts as they came, whether they make sense or not, but it is truly a small part, and I cannot deny a sense of dissatisfaction in my heart. However, there may be future opportunities, so I hope you will view this with potential in mind, and I too shall conclude by singing a verse of my hometown's folk song.

This year's harvest
Is abundant, so what (Kinari)
The blood and sweat in every grain
Is others' pleasure (亨樂).

Though I planted it,
A morning glory's heart. (Kinari)
Over the wall, at the neighbor's house,
It blooms its flowers.
6, 14 <111>

**Summary**

Han Chŏng-dong discusses the nature of Korean folk songs (minyo), emphasizing their role as expressions of a people's specific sentiments, customs, life, and environment, differing significantly by region (hyangt'o). Focusing on his native region of Yonggang and Kangsŏ (near Pyongyang), he presents examples of local folk song types, 'T'aryŏng' and 'Kinari', illustrating themes such as the suffering caused by early marriage, the hardships and sorrows of laborers (mŏsŭm), the pain of separation, and longing. He stresses that the true depth and unique flavor of these songs can only be appreciated through direct experience of the place and its culture, concluding with two poignant 'Kinari' verses reflecting on the farmers' toil benefiting others. For historians, the article offers valuable insight into regional cultural identity and social commentary during the colonial period. The specific examples of song lyrics provide glimpses into lived experiences and social issues like forced early marriage and the exploitation of tenant farmers or laborers, expressed through the culturally significant medium of folk song. The final verses explicitly lamenting that the fruits of their labor ("blood and sweat") become "others' pleasure" could be interpreted as a subtle critique of the colonial economic structure or landlordism.
