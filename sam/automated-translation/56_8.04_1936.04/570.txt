자장가
						張德祚 (Chang Tŏk-cho)


"Come on, just a few more times, push just a few more times."
The midwife, squinting her seemingly bloodshot eyes, first strained, "Ugh, ugh."
With each effort, oily sweat beaded on her finely wrinkled forehead. However, Inhwa, who had already gone limp, lacked the strength to muster new energy. She merely hugged the low writing desk pushed to the upper part of the room and stared blankly into the air. Only when terrifying contractions surged did she twist her body, gasp for breath, and suffer.
"Ah, I'm dying. What sin did I commit—"
Blood-tinged cries repeatedly tumbled from her parched lips.
"Now, now, don't worry. In my nearly thirty years as a midwife, having assisted hundreds, thousands of births, I've never seen a baby pop out <344> without this much trouble,"
The midwife wiped the tears and sweat, like beads, from the mother's face with a towel, trying her best to calm the young mother's heart.
"Why, for a first birth, ten hours is progressing so easily it's almost unusual."
But the mother still couldn't regain her clear senses and kept complaining of the astonishing pain. From somewhere, the loud clatter of a sock-knitting machine echoed, making the night air of this newly developed area (新開地) feel particularly desolate.
After the tearstains were wiped clean, the color instantly drained from the mother's face; even the movement of the blue veins (靜脈) on her pale brow was clearly visible.
Nine o'clock, ten o'clock—the anxious hours ticked by, but the mother's waning strength became increasingly palpable.
"Is it still the same?" Oksun'ŏmŏm, who was boiling water in a basin placed on a charcoal brazier (風爐), peeked through the sliding paper door (미다지), seemingly worried by the quietness in the room.
"Shouldn't we call a doctor before it's too late?"
She tossed her legs out onto the quilt, breathing heavily and rapidly. Glancing at the sleeping mother, she lowered her voice again and whispered.
"Well," the midwife tilted her head,
"The baby's head is quite low. If the mother could just regain her senses and push a few more times, it would all be over."
She forced a bitter smile (苦笑), looking troubled.
"But she's already lost her strength like this?"
"Anyway, the baby is big... Well, I took responsibility for things in the room, so don't worry and just boil the water."
The midwife tossed out the words as if annoyed and moved closer to the mother, who was tossing and turning anew, groaning. <345>
"Hhu-heung, heung-heung."
Oksun'ŏmŏm, about to rush in unconsciously at the mother's agonizing cries, stopped short, turned back, closed the door, and sighed.
"Oh my. At that age, with those looks."
Having moved to Seoul with her family just four months prior, renting a room in this tenement-like house, she still retained the simple-heartedness of a country person. Seeing Inhwa, who she heard had even been a female teacher, struggling alone like this in a corner of a dirty rented room to give birth to a fatherless child, she felt tears welling up with pity.
"Truly, the wicked ones in the world are men."
Oksun'ŏmŏm clicked her tongue.
Before her eyes rose the image of her eldest daughter, who had married at fifteen and was already widowed at seventeen with a child in her belly.
Her son-in-law had been a servant at the village rich man's house. Though uneducated and lacking resourcefulness, he was apparently handsome enough to be swayed by a temptress from the town and run off with her.
The daughter, who cried day and night holding the prematurely born baby after nine months, one night suddenly ran out as if in a frenzy, grabbed a sickle, and tried to kill the child.
Though prevented by family members, the incident became known even to the local police station (刑廳), and after spending several days in detention, she abruptly came up to Seoul and was now working as a housemaid somewhere.
The mother, whenever she visited once or twice a month, would curse and abuse the child, who was growing up remarkably healthy without any illness under the care of the poor grandmother.
"If only you didn't exist, my fate would improve. Die, just die." <346>
The young girl sometimes wailed aloud.
"Since I get a salary, can I even buy myself clothes? It goes here and there taking care of you. When you grow up, you'll leave me and run away just like your father."
Yet, unable to bear the sight of her jobless father and the weakening child, she would still visit with sweets or bread in hand whenever she received her salary.
Each time, unspeakable curses and abuses rained down upon the young child.
"That's enough. You can fix your fate now. What sin has that child committed?"
Oksun'ŏmŏm, unable to stop her daughter who kicked and hit the child like a madwoman, often ended up crying herself.
Her sympathy and heartfelt care for the lonely Inhwa stemmed from this lingering, deep resentment regarding her daughter. Oksun'ŏmŏm also knew the man from this room well.
The morning after Inhwa moved in, around noon, he emerged from the room with a toothbrush in his mouth, his pale appearance seeming sinister.
"But where did he go that he hasn't returned?"
Inhwa's reserved and sharp nature had never revealed her heart's secrets to this simple-hearted neighbor wife, so Oksun'ŏmŏm still didn't know the man's whereabouts.
"He probably doesn't even know she's giving birth."
She was so engrossed in thinking about the man, who hadn't been seen for some time, that she didn't even notice the fire in the brazier burning hot enough to melt the bottom of the basin.

<347>

As Inhwa's physical pain intensified, the ache of resentment towards the man in her heart grew tenfold, a hundredfold, seeming to drain even the last thread-like bit of strength from her already frail body.
"It's all my fault. I was blind."
She clutched her disheveled hair, wringing her uncontrollably anguished chest, thrashing and weeping.
Each time a wave of overwhelming agony washed over her, Inhwa saw visions of the man in various situations amidst fresh tears.
The way the man blinked his eyes as if in disbelief when she first confessed her pregnancy. From that night on, the peace they had maintained, at least superficially, shattered instantly.
"Don't worry too much. If this is really a pregnancy, I can arrange for you to give birth secretly at a friend's house I know."
When Inhwa, unable to bear watching the man's distressed state any longer, said this,
"I wouldn't have the face to look at the child born," the man averted his gaze.
Indeed, their union itself, to put it bluntly, was a mistake.
A female teacher at a common school and a night school teacher—he, a literary youth whose submissions to magazines and newspapers went unanswered, was still enrolled in some professional school.
His family, claiming to be a prominent household (豪家) in a certain village in Ch'ungch'ŏng Province, didn't provide very ample monthly tuition fees.
In fact, perhaps unable to find a way to supplement his insufficient tuition, he might have started tending to the runny-nosed children at the A Public Common School (公普) night division.
In any case, less than three months after the man started frequenting the school, Inhwa, the model female teacher of the day division, received a thick love letter from him.
"When I graduate, some path will surely open up for me. Moreover, being the eldest son, I will inherit the family's considerable <348> fortune then. Come leap into my arms with the relaxed feeling of boarding a mothership (母船)."
It wasn't unreasonable for the twenty-year-old maiden, who could be called utterly alone with parents gone to Manchuria three years ago without news and a younger sibling studying on scholarship at some government school in the countryside, to be drawn to the sweet words (甘言) of this pale young man tinged with a shadow of melancholy.
Some time later, their cozy life together began in a room of a tenement house under Naksan hill.
It was a dreamlike life. During the day, they longed for each other while apart, and the nights passed sleeplessly until dawn broke.
The sweltering summer vacation passed without boredom, and though reminders from his elderly mother (老母) urging him to visit piled up on his desk, the man showed no sign of moving.
"Go ahead. Go down and get your parents' consent..."
Inhwa felt rather sorry for him, but the man
"Graduation is just a few days away,"
he trailed off, smiling.
"Even if the form is technically illegal, society should naturally permit us, united by true love. Anyone who points fingers behind our backs would be in the wrong."
The man's tone overflowed with passion as he spoke thus.
"It's all premature. If we just endure a little longer, happiness will come. Waiting for the right time to leisurely get my father's consent is actually more interesting than doing it right now."
However, their livelihood, where even the man's meager remittances (送金) had ceased, grew increasingly precarious.
"Darling. Even if your parents don't approve, will you stay buried in this nest?" <349>
One day, when they couldn't make breakfast because the rice ran out and there was no charcoal (숫), Inhwa pressed him like this.
"This is the most difficult time in the countryside now. Perhaps by autumn, there will be some news from home."
Without reason to doubt the man's reply, Inhwa's heart, recalling their marriage formed while turning their backs on the world amidst others' contempt, would flush momentarily as new passion flared.
July, August, September. As autumn approached, one humid day (하로) when silvery fine rain had continued for several days, Inhwa suddenly felt nauseous during class (교수), vomited (구토), and collapsed at the lectern (교단).
"Your complexion has been quite poor these past few days."
A colleague worriedly pointed this out, so she returned home early by rickshaw (인력거) that day.
When the man came home in the evening, she held onto him and
"I think I might be pregnant," she said with a smile, but the man denied (부정) it to the end, saying,
"How could that be?"
In truth, they knew well how frightening a threat pregnancy was in their current living situation and had tried various methods to avoid it.
"But we were originally ignorant in that area, so couldn't something have gone wrong?"
Inhwa found the man's attitude amusing, as he seemed to have ultimate faith in their clumsy (슈치한) methods.
However, as signs increasingly pointed to nothing but pregnancy, the man's heart grew colder.
Her food preferences changed (달나지고), and nausea (구역질) immediately followed meals.
Even standing at the lectern became a daily torment.
"As things are now, there might be a way to handle it."
One day, he came home slightly drunk on alcohol he couldn't usually handle, spoke like this, and watched her reaction. Inhwa <350> silently choked back tears.
"Since it has happened, let's get your parents' permission and raise it properly."
When Inhwa confronted (아러케 따지고 대여들엇슬 때) him one day, the man became serious and, for the first time, confessed (설토하엿다) that he had a fiancée chosen for him in childhood (어렷슬 때 정혼한 처녀).
"Hmph. And you used graduation, graduation as a shield every time you spoke," Inhwa thought of how the man, when first pleading for her love, had brazenly shown her his family register (호적등본) to prove he was a 'bachelor' (총각).
"So what are you going to do?"
The man, head bowed silently, tried to excuse himself by elaborating tediously on how difficult it was to break off a childhood engagement with a prominent family (大家) in the village, and even mentioned his own family's recently declining finances.
"Shouldn't I consider my parents' face too?"
His tone mixed a desire for the fiancée's family wealth and a vile mockery (조롱이 석겨 잇섯다) of Inhwa's situation, having no one to even provide a single blanket.
Inhwa was furious.
The man's greedy and selfish attitude was detestable, but when he worried about wedding expenses (혼인 치례를 바라고 결혼비용을 념녀하는 대), she was left speechless.
Misinterpreting Inhwa's renewed silence, the man said,
"Well. First, let's somehow take care of the thing in your belly, and then we can slowly arrange the next steps."
He shifted the topic for the first time.
"Just carrying it around like that, won't you look less human day by day (나날이 사람꼴이 백혀가지 안아)?"
That night, the man brought home some unknown pills (환약) and urged her to take them in front of him, but
"What trick is this?" Inhwa pretended not to hear. <351>
"It probably hasn't even formed proper features yet, what's the big deal?"
The man persistently (질깃질깃하게) pressed her, but
"Even if it's not a human baby (사람색기) but an animal cub (짐승색기), it's fine. It's natural for me to love what's in my belly."
Inhwa, who was already feeling faint fetal movements (태동) then, thought, "Does this man have any idea how I feel?"
"By what mistake did you find me? Making both you and me unhappy."
With a mother's emotion, she caressed her belly, trying to suppress the overflowing tears, but couldn't stop the wave of sorrowful thoughts welling up from below her navel. Everywhere she turned, her past of mistakes and abuse, and the pitiful, heartbreaking circumstances of this blood-clot clinging to her, seemed endlessly tragic.
"Then instead of taking it, I'll keep it as a memento."
Inhwa wrapped it in several layers of paper and stored it deep in the desk drawer.



"That medicine must be right here in this desk drawer."
As Inhwa's thoughts (공상) reached this point, she instinctively tried to lift her head slightly, bracing against the desk leg she was holding.
Simultaneously, the world seemed to turn upside down, her vision went black (캉캄하여지며), and as some force surged, she collapsed backward again.
"Alright, just one more time—"
The midwife's voice sounded faintly, and new strength surged again.
"Uwaa—" <252> [Note: Page number seems out of sequence, likely a typo in original, should probably be <352>]
A crying sound erupted.
However, the baby, whose head was elongated and features distorted from nearly a full day and night of hardship (신고), cried out only a few times upon hitting the cold outside air (외기) for the first time, then went limp again.
As the terrifying pain receded, the mother (산부),
seeing the midwife searching for scissors to cut the umbilical cord (배꼽 끈흘) while saying, "Such a big baby girl," felt an indescribable joy wash over her like a wave.
There was no resentment towards the baby's father.
Not even a trace of sad thoughts about her lonely situation arose.
"I've become a mother."
Under this immense emotion, tears flowed endlessly.
Hearing the baby's cry, Oksun'ŏmŏm, who had gone over to her own room, rushed back startled.
"Really, if everyone's like this, the midwife teacher won't be able to stand it," the midwife, who had expertly washed the baby, looked up at Oksun'ŏmŏm standing stock-still in the upper part of the room and laughed.
What time was it? Even the sound of the sock-knitting machine had long ceased. The appearance of the humble room, lacking even a clock, felt rather eerie after the terrible tension had passed.
Having finally regained her clear senses, Inhwa felt embarrassed by the worn-out cotton quilt, the dirty birthing tools (산구), and the sour smell they emitted.
"Thank you for your hard work," she said, earnestly bowing her head to the midwife.
"Well, first births are naturally difficult. That's why someone should come and help watch. By the way, isn't your mother here (안게시우)?"
The midwife lit a cigarette, seemingly not expecting an answer. <353>
"For women, this is a great trial (대역). A great trial."
After the rooster crowed several times at dawn and the eastern sky brightened, the midwife examined the mother's belly and body thoroughly with a worn-out stethoscope (청진기), gave some instructions, and left with the words,
"Get a good long sleep (한숨 흠벅 주무시우)." The room felt even quieter.
"Baby, I am the one who saved you. Now it's just you and me, so I am the one who will raise you (살녀갈 것두 나지)." Inhwa reached out and pulled the packet of dark pills (색감안 환약) from the desk drawer.
"The world is truly scary, you know. Look at this. Here is the demon that tried to devour you and me."
Caught up in pointless excitement, she poked her face out from the dirty quilt edge, touched the cheek of the deeply sleeping child, and felt the roughness of her own hand, which seemed to have suddenly lost all its oiliness. But when Oksun'ŏmŏm, who had been bustling about outside the door, brought in something called rice soup, she had drifted into a faint sleep.
Inhwa, having fallen into a painless world of sleep for the first time in ages, found everything bothersome, but Oksun'ŏmŏm insisted on helping her sit up.
"They say sleeping on an empty stomach after childbirth is terrible," she clucked her tongue, adding,
"Is there anyone who knows and cares enough to look after you?"
Unable to refuse her earnest urging, Inhwa, still lying down, took a couple of spoonfuls of the bland broth, which contained no meat. Oksun'ŏmŏm hesitated for a long moment as if about to say something difficult, then suddenly whispered,
"Doesn't the baby's father know?"
"Know what?"
"That this was the month for childbirth (산월)."
"What good would it do if he knew?"
Inhwa threw down the spoon, turned away, and closed her eyes.
Oksun'ŏmŏm, unable to persuade her further, gathered the dishes with a clatter. <354>
"Still, that's not right. Calm your heart and at least let him know by letter (편지루락두 알녀 들입쇼)."
Not knowing the full story, she seemed to assume the two were separated after some lover's quarrel.
"Letters? I've sent letters, fifty times over, but there's no news."
As Inhwa said this, she thought that the letters, postcards, and telegrams she had sent during the nearly three months since the man went straight home in March after finishing his graduation exams indeed probably numbered around fifty.
"I'll go settle things. I'll break off that engagement, arrange for the child's registration, and first of all, arrange for the childbirth expenses."
The man had comforted the anxious Inhwa, holding her hand, and left right at the beginning of March.
During the preceding months, they hadn't even looked at each other's unpleasant faces, but that day, they went out on the street for the first time in a while and even had lunch at a department store restaurant.
"As soon as I get down there, I'll send at least a hundred won, so don't be too anxious, just wait."
The man calmly added these words.
"No matter how run-down our family is now, would they not spare a few hundred won for their precious first grandchild? I'll go explain things well to the family, make preparations to set up housekeeping, and come right back up. Okay?"
Inhwa, enduring the discomfort of her growing belly, had finally been expelled from school once everything became known. At a time when, apart from occasional sewing work and the small amount of tuition money she practically begged for again from the man's family after it had stopped, she hadn't seen any money and was practically starving, the man's words couldn't help but sound appealing.
Still unable to feel at ease, she followed him to the station.
"You're not running away and abandoning me, are you?" she casually tested him.
"If I were, you could sue me," the man laughed nonchalantly.
"You wouldn't make me go so far as to sue, would you?" <355>
Inhwa laughed along, but indeed, once he left, the man's whereabouts became utterly unknown (묘연하엿다).
Letters went unanswered, telegrams received no reply. Eventually, she inquired at the school, but the response was that his graduation certificate had been mailed (부첫고) and they knew nothing else.
She wanted to rush down there immediately but hesitated countless times, clinging to the hope of 'surely not, surely not'. Now, not only had her hope and expectation vanished, but even her resentment and curses towards the man had exhausted themselves, and it had been half a month since she stopped trying to contact him.
"What's the use of doing pointless things?"
As Inhwa muttered to herself, lying turned away, Oksun'ŏmŏm cleared the table without another word.



A considerable amount of time passed before the newborn shed its initial water weight and the flattened back of its head filled out.
Though the wrinkled eyes and nose hadn't fully smoothed out (사리 정돈이 되지는 안앗스나), Inhwa, nursing the baby, felt this blood-clot, wrinkling its face like an old person, was a treasure beyond measure.
Only the long torso, pointed ears, and broad forehead, features seemingly inherited from the father, made her heart ache with sorrow (비감하게 해 주엇다) for no reason.
Though no one congratulated her and not a single gift (례물) of fabric for a baby jacket (저고리감) arrived, the child was healthy. Contrary to Oksun'ŏmŏm's worries, her milk supply (젓) wasn't too scarce, the navel (배꼽) healed smoothly, and the baby reached its first month (첫 일헤).
After Oksun'ŏmŏm bundled up the messy belongings and made several trips to the pawnshop (전당포) messenger (사환), the payment due to the midwife was also settled without issue. But Inhwa couldn't stay in bed for long.
As soon as her limbs regained strength, she had to personally wash the postpartum laundry (해산 서답들) stored away in the attic and frequent the employment agency (직업소개소) as if it were her own home to find a way to feed two mouths (두 입의 플칠할 길). <356>
However, jobs like diplomat, office worker, or saleswoman were impossible while carrying a baby, and the pay wasn't sufficient to afford childcare.
Besides those, jobs suitable (맛갓지가 안코) for someone with a baby were fundamentally unsuitable, and eventually, even the agency clerk grew annoyed.
"Are you here looking for a job? Or are you here to disturb busy people?" he wouldn't even glance her way.
Small debts and large debts piled up, the number of people pressing her daily increased (나날이 졸으러드는 사람만 늘어가나), but there was no way to earn income. While nursing the baby,
"Baby, at this rate, I'll die, and you'll starve to death too," such days continued.
But at least while the milk (젓) flowed, it was still fortunate.
As the redemption deadlines at the pawnshop approached and items like clothing and cheap rings (반지 부스럭이), pawned because she couldn't even pay the interest (리자), were forfeited one by one, Inhwa's milk ducts (젓줄) also began to dry up like a drought-stricken stream (가믈 맛난 시내처럼 말나들기).
On particularly hot days, the baby, looking like it was fished out of a water jar (물독에서 건저 낸 것), would gasp (할닥이며) and suckle desperately. Each time, her breasts ached as if being torn away, making her resentful (원랑스러울만큼) – where did such strength come from in that tiny body? But the baby, oblivious, burrowed into its mother's thin chest, not even sleeping through the night.
Inhwa, weeping, poured all her effort into feeding the baby rice gruel (암죽) made from rice laboriously acquired under the hot summer sun, or boiled rice-water (뜨물), but the unsatisfied baby's once-plump bottom grew thin (홀죽해 갓다).
"Poor thing. Why were you born to someone like me?"
Seeing mother and child lying exhausted, even Oksun'ŏmŏm couldn't bear it.
"Still, shouldn't you go visit the baby's father once?"
she urged.
"It's Ch'ungch'ŏng Province, so it's not that far. Just go see, consider it a wasted trip if need be." <357>
Having now roughly grasped Inhwa's situation, she stroked the back of her own grandchild, who was just learning to take steps, and repeated her suggestion.
Inhwa, noticeably gaunt (수척하고 여윈) from the recent childbirth ordeal (산고) and ever-increasing mental anguish, still felt the joy (깃븜) of having survived the life-threatening childbirth crisis (해산의 액난), but
"I don't mind dying. If only this one weren't here," she said, turning to Oksun'ŏmŏm, her attitude seemingly devoid of falsehood or exaggeration.
"Well, even an ignorant old woman like me finds it infuriating. Go and see. I'll look after the baby."
"What good would meeting him do (맛나면 뭘 허우)? It would only hurt more."
Remembering that the man hadn't replied even to the notice of childbirth she sent, everything felt like a futile effort. But then, she simultaneously recalled the attitude of a friend who had been toyed with by a rich man's son and eventually even filed a lawsuit (고소사건), and her own inclination to just give up seemed incredibly foolish.
"But doesn't it feel dirty, like selling the child or blackmailing (협박) him?"
"Dirty? Really, baby's mother (애기 어머니). My daughter, though still young, says if she ever meets her man, she'll throw the child down right in front of him and watch him die."
Seeing Inhwa's interest piqued, Oksun'ŏmŏm said this, but then immediately looked embarrassed, as if her words had been too harsh, and gave an awkward smile.
Inhwa, having seen the young woman herself several times lift the child upside down and throw it from the room into the yard, couldn't help but shudder at Oksun'ŏmŏm's story.
"How strange. Even though the baby still knows nothing, I only feel that it's precious and dear beyond measure."
"That girl, she's like an animal. Still, sometimes she actually nurses it and even sheds tears," <358>
"Well, it's the child that came from her own belly."
But what immediately troubled Inhwa more than such stories about others was her own pressing future (전정).
"I really need to make a decision somehow."
Suddenly, a scene from a Western movie she had seen long ago unfolded vividly before her eyes.
The mother who sent her beloved daughter to be adopted by a rich family, then hid herself for the sake of her daughter's happiness!
That woman's sublime expression, smiling with satisfaction as she watched her happy daughter through a rain-streaked glass window on a rainy evening... As Inhwa recalled it, she jumped to her feet.
"Oksun'ŏmŏm, I'll go. I'll go see, and if the situation seems right, I'll leave this one there."



Inhwa, holding the baby, arrived at Kyŏngbu Line's B station late one morning, already well into August.
The baby, whose hundredth day (백일) was the day after tomorrow, was nevertheless dressed in a turquoise artificial silk one-piece garment (옥색 인조견 내리다지).
Having made the final decision late last night, Inhwa sold off everything remaining—pots (남비), brazier (풍로), even spoon scraps (숫가락 나부랭이). After budgeting for travel expenses (차비), she used the remaining money to buy two *ja* of artificial silk.
Sewing the garment (내리다지) on a neighbor's sewing machine (자봉침), she dressed the baby's thin body and wept, looking at Oksun'ŏmŏm.
"On this journey, perhaps our last, the clothes a mother makes cost only 68 *chŏn*."
But the baby, having spent the whole summer in two cotton bloomers (무녕 두룽치) made before birth, perhaps felt the different texture of the artificial silk, and suddenly cried out, "Uwaa—" <359>
"Oh, my pitiful daughter. Please forget your mother and grow up well."
Oksun'ŏmŏm, sweeping the room with a worn-out broom, sniffled along.
"Yes, be well. Country people are often kind-hearted. You won't starve. You won't go naked."
Indeed, in the man's hometown, still a 20-*ri* ride by car from the station, ripening grains undulated like waves, and the people's faces overflowed with simple smiles.
At a Japanese (내지사람) shop that also served as the bus stop (정류소), Inhwa didn't even need to give the man's address; just his name was enough to find the house.
It was, contrary to Inhwa's suspicions, an impressively large old house (구가), making her hesitate. Only the thatched roof (초가 집웅), seemingly unchanged for years, overgrown with nameless weeds (잡초), and the empty expanse of the large courtyard (광장) in front of the gate seemed to speak of the declining state (령락해가는) of this house.
After hesitating for a while, Inhwa started to step inside the gate, then turned back again and approached a group of children playing by the stream in front of the house, catching loaches (미꾸리를 낙그며).
"Could one of you tell the young master of that house that a guest has arrived? Please?"
But the children, who had been staring blankly at this unfamiliar woman from the start, remained standing like dazed people.
"Baby, you'll become like this too. You'll become ignorant like these children."
Inhwa readjusted the baby held in her tired arms.
The discovery that a mother's heart leaving her daughter behind was by no means as romantic as depicted in that movie saddened her.
"Hey kids, could someone run and tell him?"
Among them, boys wearing only trousers (고의), boys in just shirts (저구리), ranging from five or six to perhaps ten or twelve, even including a girl covered in scabs <360> (덕지가 더덕더덕 달나부튼), didn't answer but whispered amongst themselves.
"The young master of that house is over there."
An older boy pointed with a crooked finger towards the fields (두던길).
Inhwa glanced in that direction and suddenly her head felt intensely hot, her mind reeling (정신이 앗질해젓다).
It was him.
Wearing coarse hemp trousers (굵은 베고의) and still a cotton undershirt (무명적삼), stumbling along carrying an awkward water A-frame (물지개 지고 비씰그리며), the man's appearance was unmistakably that of the child's father, unforgettable even for a moment.
He seemed extremely startled.
Like a pheasant shot mid-flight, he first flinched as if to run away, but then, with a resigned look, tears welling in his eyes, he threw down the water carrier and ran towards her.
"Oh, oh, uh, what brings you here?"
The man waved his hands as if swimming, then quickly looked around, flustered.
"Wait for me under the zelkova tree behind that house, on the rock. I'll come right away. Definitely."
"Let's just go into the house. I didn't come to make trouble (야료ㄹ하러 온 것두 아니니)."
Inhwa's head was still throbbing, but words came out of her mouth with a coolness that surprised even herself (랭연한 대답이 나왓다).
"Here, take your child for a moment."
The man seemed to continuously plead about something, but ultimately, seemingly overwhelmed by her demeanor, he unconsciously extended trembling hands, took the baby, and walked towards the house.
Following silently behind, Inhwa observed the deserted, spacious inner courtyard, the dusty old furniture (헌 세간들), and the stacks of barley covered with straw mats (엽댕이 뀌여진 보리 우댁이들). Seeing the eldest son, a graduate of a professional school, carrying water, she felt she could thoroughly grasp the family's circumstances. <361>
"Whose child is that?"
From the main room (안방), which seemed easily four *kan* (칸) wide by Seoul standards (서울 간살노 친다면), the mistress (마나님), who had been sewing (바누질) looking over her glasses, saw her son entering looking crestfallen and the young woman following behind. She shrieked and rushed out onto the wooden veranda (마루).
"Mother, she found me after all."
The son snapped brusquely.
This mistress, who remarkably resembled her son in every feature, seemed to have already guessed the situation.
"Come on up," she addressed Inhwa informally (반말을 건넷스나), but her expression clearly revealed an undisguisably difficult situation.
"I heard the details from this boy, but anyway, you must be tired from the long journey."
After seating arrangements were made in the room—the mistress in the warmest spot (아래ㅅ목), then her son, then Inhwa—the mistress spoke first.
"But his marriage (혼담) is already arranged, and he's supposed to get married (성례케 되엿는대) this month. What do you mean by showing up like this?"
Her voice held sharp needles.
Inhwa tried to retort (말대ㅅ구 하려 하엿스나), but only her pale (해ㅅ쓱해진) lips trembled (부루루 떨니고), and no sound came out.
"Well, it's embarrassing if the villagers find out. Since you've endured this long, couldn't you have endured a few more days?"
As the mistress's attitude grew increasingly haughty (기고해 가는), Inhwa finally ground her teeth in fury (이가 아드득 갈니도록 분해젓다).
"I have something to discuss with you alone."
This was the sentence that finally escaped her lips (끗끗내 그의 입에서 흘너 나온 말이란 이것이엇스나), but the man, who had been merely wiping tears beside her, became flustered instead.
"As you know, I'm a weak person, so if we met alone, I'd surely be overwhelmed by you. Instead, Mother is saying everything that's in my heart, isn't she?"
"Did you consult your mother when you first wrote letters to me? Were you planning to come with your mother to meet under the zelkova tree earlier <362> (아까 느티나무 밑에서 맞나잘 *도 어머니허구 올 작정이엇소)?" [*typo likely meant 만나자 할 때도*]
Inhwa, who had intended to negotiate peacefully until the end, became enraged (악이 밧처 올낫다) at their exceedingly selfish and base mentality.
"There's no such rule. Speaking rudely and confronting someone you once served, even briefly, as a husband is unacceptable behavior in this land."
The mistress, her face flushed with anger, veins standing out, lectured (훈게나 하듯이) her.
"I am not someone to be lectured (훈책받을 사람이란 업소) by you people. You are too, too despicable (비열들하오)."
Inhwa snatched the sleeping baby from the man's arms and stood up abruptly.
"I gave up on you long ago too. But I felt pity that the child would go hungry and naked—"
Her words were cut off by tears.
"Actually, I came to entrust the child to you. But I can't entrust her even for a moment to people with such a base character (저열한 근성)."
As she pushed open the door to leave, she glimpsed a blanket folded seemingly preciously in a corner of the warmest spot. Even the sight of the blanket edge (담요자락) triggered unpleasant memories.
It was the morning the man left for the countryside.
"The quilt is dirty, the mattress is like this... leave that blanket behind. I can use it as a blanket cover (이불보 삼아 쓰게)."
After packing everything—his watch, fountain pen (만연필), even used envelopes and stationery scraps (편지지 나부랭이까지)—when he finally tried to stuff that blanket (담여) in too, Inhwa had tried to dissuade him, but the man, without a hint of apology,
"It might still be cold on the train. I'll send money as soon as I get down there. Get yourself a thick, soft quilt first."
had stubbornly taken that too as he left.
"Disgusting people. Disgusting people!" Inhwa felt nauseated by everything she saw, pressing her handkerchief to her eyes. <363>
As she hurried to exit the house gate, she heard footsteps quickly approaching from behind. The man, breathless, caught up and grabbed her sleeve.
"Here, come in and at least have dinner before you go."
The man trembled like a frightened deer, practically kneeling before her.
"Mother is very angry, so please apologize. If you stir up her feelings like this now, it will hinder our future (우리들의 전정에도 장애가)..."
"After deceiving people so much, you still speak of 'us' and 'the future' with that dirty mouth?"
Inhwa shot a contemptuous look at the man's face, cutting him off.
The man, adopting an even more dying voice, pleaded,
"Still, I realize my responsibility towards the child too. Today, for the first time, I feel a father's love."
"You? The one who bought medicine to kill us both?"
Inhwa was beyond furious; she was dumbfounded (기다 맥히엇다).
"Don't concern yourself (알은 체두 말우) with the child's affairs ever again. I'll raise her somehow if I can, and if I can't, I'll abandon her, but I will never let her step over your family's threshold (당신네 집 문지방은 안 밟히우께)."
"Is that really true?"
The man's attitude suddenly changed.
"Swear it. If you promise that's exactly how it will be, I can live with peace of mind too."
He pressed again, as if dubious.
"Oh, don't worry," Inhwa thought, "Your true nature (本性) is finally revealed," and replied with a sarcastic sneer (냉소),
"Just make sure you don't show up later claiming to be the father. So she doesn't have to feel the shame of having received dirty blood." <364>



As Inhwa and the baby rushed back late that evening, Oksun'ŏmŏm rubbed her sleepy eyes, startled.
"You came back with her just like that (그냥 되 다리고 왓소구레)?"
"So, is a human child the father's property? It originally belongs to the mother." Inhwa snapped this reply, but Oksun'ŏmŏm, unable to understand (리해못한) her words, kept trying to ask about the man's family situation and what had transpired.
"What's the point of talking about that? The child belongs only to the mother. She is the mother's property to possess."
Oksun'ŏmŏm still couldn't understand these words either.
That night, Inhwa slept with the child on an empty stomach (뷘 속에) in the old room, now devoid of any furniture (세간 한개 볼 수 업는).
"Baby. What good is social standing? What good is education? I have you, and you have me. This blind love, beyond reason or principle, is all that matters."
Indeed, not a speck of vanity or sorrow (애상) could be found in her heart.
The poignant sentiment (애연한 정서) of turning away in the rain, praying for her daughter's happiness, the sad sacrificial spirit (서글푼 희생심) hoping the child would be treated as the young lady of a village magnate's house—even the trace (자최조차) of these had vanished.
"Life is not a dream. It's not sentimentality (감상). Baby, everything is reality, a pressing situation right before our eyes."
She stroked the baby's head and whispered into its sleeping ear.
Dawn broke before she knew it.
It was a clear day.
Oksun'ŏmŏm peeked through Inhwa's door, which she had opened slightly after staying awake all night. She was wearing clean outing clothes, seen for the first time.
"Where are you going?" <365>
Before Inhwa, who was nursing, could ask,
"That girl finally caused trouble (기어히 일을 저질넛세요)," Oksun'ŏmŏm stepped into the room.
According to her, news arrived yesterday evening that her daughter, who had been working as a housemaid (어멈살이하든), had run off without direction with a neighborhood fishmonger (생선장수).
"She fell head over heels for a man who already has a wife and child."
She finished speaking and wiped her nose.
"So I'm on my way now to that house outside Saemun Gate to see if she caused any mischief. My goodness, the master and mistress were so kind-hearted, they treated that beastly girl just like their own daughter!"
While listening to Oksun's lament, a strange plan gradually formed in Inhwa's mind.
"Then that household must be really short-handed (손이 째이겟네) now."
"Of course. With an elderly person and two school-going children, how difficult must it be?"
"Then," Inhwa suddenly moved closer.
"Put in a word for me at that house. Tell them I don't mind any kind of hard work. Recommend me as a housemaid (어멈으로 천거 좀 해달난 말이오)."
Oksun was dumbfounded for a moment, then finally,
"The job my daughter did? You mean as a housemaid (어멈을 말슴이오)?"
she asked again after a pause.
"Why can't I work as a housemaid (어멈노릇은 못허우)? If it's a job where I don't have to be separated from this child, what couldn't I do?"
Perhaps drawn by the passion in Inhwa's forceful words, she thought for a moment, nodded, then lamented,
"Still, for someone who was even a teacher to become a housemaid..."
"No, don't say anything else. Just say I'm a young woman with one child."
As if resigned, Oksun'ŏmŏm <366>
"Alright," she promised.
"Oh my. I have to walk all the way to Saemun Gate. Will I get there before the master of the house leaves for work (출근할 시간)?"
Unable to endure even a minute on trams or trains, she quickly got up.
That evening, as anticipated.
"That girl, perhaps receiving the blood of her innocent mother, didn't steal or waste anything, thankfully."
Smiling happily as she entered, the wife relayed the good news that followed.
Since the household was large and couldn't manage even a day without help, the master said that even with a child, if the person was trustworthy, she should come to work immediately.
"Really, in this world, how hard it is to find one person. Old women just aim for the high-class houses (진고개집만 노리고), and the young ones all go to those darn bars or cafes (술집 가회)—or whatever they're called, my goodness."
Oksun'ŏmŏm rambled on nonsensically, trying to comfort Inhwa, but Inhwa, having found a place of refuge (안식처), felt both happy and choked up.
"Anyway, thank you for your trouble. Then, I should go right away."
Excited about this adventurous new beginning, tears even welled in her eyes as she bowed her head very humbly before Oksun.
"Finally, I've found a place where I can earn food without having to abandon you (너를 떼치지 안아두 밥 어더 먹을 자리가 생겻구나)." Kissing the baby's soft cheek, Inhwa saw her daughter tumbling and playing in a dim servant's room (어둑한 식모 방에 딩굴며 놀 딸), and her own love enveloping the surroundings like a radiant halo (원광).
And within it, the sacred song she would hear every night:

Hush, hush, little baby, our baby sleeps so well.
In our baby's sleeping room, light dawns without a lamp. <367>

**Summary**

This short story, "Lullaby," follows Inhwa, a former teacher who finds herself pregnant and abandoned by her lover, a student from a formerly wealthy family who is engaged to another woman. Facing social stigma and extreme poverty after giving birth alone in a rented room, Inhwa initially seeks help from the child's father's family but is cruelly rejected by him and his mother due to their concern for social standing and his impending marriage. Resolved to keep her child despite the hardship, Inhwa learns of a housemaid position vacated by her neighbor's runaway daughter and decides to take the job, finding solace in the fact that she can raise her daughter herself, viewing her child and her maternal love as the ultimate reality and treasure, transcending societal judgment and past sorrows.

From a historical perspective, the story vividly portrays the precarious situation of unmarried mothers and the limited options for women, even educated ones, in 1930s colonial Korea. It highlights the clash between emerging modern relationship ideals and deeply entrenched traditional family structures, patriarchal values, and the importance of lineage and arranged marriages. The economic struggles, the detailed depiction of poverty (selling possessions, lack of food, poor living conditions), and the limited job opportunities (housemaid as a last resort) reflect the harsh realities faced by many during this period. The casual mention of Japanese presence (the shop owner) and the social hierarchy also offer glimpses into the colonial context. Historians might find the portrayal of female agency (or lack thereof), social attitudes towards illegitimacy, class differences, and urban poverty particularly revealing.

저녁 늦차로 달녀드는 인화와 아기를 마즈며 옥순어멈은 졸든 눈을 부비고 놀낫다.
「그냥 되 다리고 왓소구레」
「그럼 사람자식이란 애비 것이랍딧가. 본래 어미 물건이지」 인화는 배앗듯이 이러케 말하엿스나 그 말을 리해못한 옥순엄마는 련해 사나희의 집안 사정이며 그동안의 진전을 캐여 물으려 하엿다.
「그런 건 말해 뭣허우. 아이는 어미만의 물건이지. 어미가 소유할 재산이지」
그러니 옥순어머니는 역시 그 말도 알아들을 수 업섯다.
이 밤 인화는 뷘 속에 어린 것을 끼고 세간 한개 볼 수 업는 옛방에서 잣다.
「아가. 체면은 해서 뭘하니? 교양은 해서 뭘하니? 내게는 네가 잇고 네게는 내가 잇고 리치도 도리도 붓흘 수 업는 이 맹목적 사랑 하나면 그만일게다」
과연 그의 마음에는 털끗만한 허영도 애상(哀傷)도 찾을 수 업섯다.
딸의 행복을 빌며 비를 맞고 도라서는 애연한 정서도 어린 것을 마슬의 대가ㅅ집 영양으로 위함을 밧게 하려든 서글푼 희생심도 자최조차 살아젓다.
「삶은 꿈이 아니다. 감상(感傷)이 아니다. 아가 모든 것이 현실, 눈앞에 닥친 절박한 사정이구나」
그는 어린 것의 머리를 쓸며 잠든 그 귀에 속삭이엇다.
어느듯 동이 터 왓다.
맑은 날세엿다.
밤새 잠 못잔 인화의 방문을 방긋이 열며 드려다 보는 옥순엄마의 몸에는 처음보게 깨끗한 나들이 옷이 감겨 잇섯다.
「어델 가시우」<365>
젓을 물니고 잇든 인화가 뭇기도 전에
「그년이 기어히 일을 저질넛세요」하며 옥순엄마는 방안에 들어선다.
그의 말노는 어제 저녁 어멈살이하든 딸이 동네ㅅ집 생선장수와 함께 방향업시 다라나 버린 통지가 왓다는 것이엇다.
「게집까지 자식까지 잇다는 놈과 그만 눈이 마자서」
그는 말을 맺으며 코ㅅ물을 훔첫다.
「그래 무슨 지랄이나 낸 것이 업나하고 새문박 그댁까지 지금 가는 가는 길이예요. 글세 주인 어룬들도 어찌 인심이 조흐시고 그 짐승 같은 년을 꼭 친딸같이 돌봐주시든대!」
이 옥순네의 탄식을 듯는 동안 인화의 가슴에는 점점 이상한 게획이 이루어저 갓다.
「그럼 그 댁에서두 지금 퍽 손이 째이겟네」
「암이요. 늙은이가 게시고 학교가는 아이가 둘이나 잇는데 오작 어려우시겟세요」
「그럼」 하고 인화는 갑자기 압흐로 닥아 앉앗다.
「그 집에 날 말좀 해주. 어떤 고단한 일도 상관치 안는다구. 어멈으로 천거 좀 해달난 말이오」
옥순네는 기가 맥혀 한참을 벙벙하다가
「딸년하든 노릇을요? 어멈을 말슴이오?」
하며 이윽이 되뭇는다.
「왜 어멈노릇은 못허우. 이 어린 것을 떼놋치 안을 일이면 뭔 못 허겟수」
힘잇게 말하는 인화의 정열에 끌니엇든지 그는 함참동안 무엇을 생각하고는 고개를 끄덕이드니
「그래도 선생님까지 지내신 이가 어멈으루야 어멈으루야」하며 탄식한다.
「아니 다른 말은 할 것 업고, 그저 아이 하나 딸닌 젊은이라고만 말해주」
옥순엄마도 할 수 업다는 듯이<366>
「그럽쇼」하고 장담하엿다.
「아이구. 새문박까지 걸어 가얄텐대. 그집 나으리 출근할 시간까지 가 달찌 몰나?」
전차 기차간에 차라면 단 일분을 못견데는 그는 급히 자리를 털고 일어낫다.
기다리든 저녁 때
「그 년이 그래도 죄업는 어미 피를 받아 아무것도 훔치거나 축낸 것은 업섯답니다」
다행한 듯이 웃고 들어서는 마누라의 입에서는 뒤니어 반가운 소식이 전갈되엿다.
원체 많은 식구라 하로라도 사람업시는 견댈 수 업는 그집 주인이 아이가 잇서두 조흐니 사람만 신실하면 즉 일노 곳 와달나는 것이라 한다.
「참 세상두, 사람 하나 구하기가 얼마나 어렵운데요. 늙은 여편네들은 그저 진고개집만 노리고 젊은 것들은 모다 그 놈의 술집 가회-라나 하는 곳 아이구우」
옥순엄마는 공연히 인화의 마음을 위로코저하는 엉터리를 부리엇스나 위선 안식처를 얻은 인화는 반가웁고 한편 목이 메엿다.
「아무튼 수고하섯소. 그럼 곳이라도 가보지」
그는 이 모험적 새 출발에 대한 흥분으로 눈물까지 글성해지며 지극히 겸손하게 옥순네의 앞에 머리를 숙이엇다.
「기어코 너를 떼치지 안아두 밥 어더 먹을 자리가 생겻구나」하고 어린 것의 보드라운 뺨에 입살을 갓다 대이며 인화는 어둑한 식모 방에 딩굴며 놀 딸과 그 주위를 오붓이 휩싸고 잇슬 자기의 사랑이 원광(圓光)처럼 빗나는 것을 보앗다.
그리고 그 속에서 밤마다 들녀올 거륵한 노래소리
