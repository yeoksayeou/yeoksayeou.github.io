Article Title	白衣椿姬哀歌 (Elegy of the White-Clad Camille)
Author	李瑞求
Article Type	소설 (Novel/Fiction)

An Elegy of the White-Clad Camille
						Yi Sŏgu
1. Ten Years Without Spring

Ch'ŏl stood blankly, holding Hun's wrist. Hun, whose wrist was held, couldn't speak either, her head bowed. The two people met unexpectedly after ten years in an unforeseen place! However, Hun, at least, had thought they would meet someday, and if they couldn't meet, she had intended to find Ch'ŏl's whereabouts and at least call him once. But for Ch'ŏl, it was truly unexpected. Hun's fine hand, held by Ch'ŏl, was gaunt and cold.
"You've become quite thin."
He asked softly, as if unable to bear the pity.
"..."
Instead of answering, Hun shed tears. With the hand that had been limply held until now, she grasped Ch'ŏl's<176> hand firmly in return.
Hun: "Anyway, I'm glad to see you."
Ch'ŏl: "Which room did you come to?"
Hun: "I came to room three over there."
Ch'ŏl: "Have you been here long?"
Hun: "Yes. I think I'll be leaving soon."
Ch'ŏl: "Let's talk quietly for a bit. Shall we?"
Hun: "Yes. I was just hoping for that chance too."
Ch'ŏl: "Then will you come where I ask you to?"
Hun: "Yes. Of course, I'll go."
Ch'ŏl: "Then, Yŏnsu-gak outside Tongdaemun Gate!"
Hun: "Then go ahead and wait. I'll definitely be there within an hour."
They were two people who met after ten years in the narrow corridor of Ch'ŏnhyang-wŏn. Being a busy place, they couldn't talk at length. Ch'ŏl went ahead to Yŏnsu-gak, and just as he was about to pour a glass of beer after taking a bath, the sound of a car was heard.
The person getting out was Hun.
Ch'ŏl: "Ah! You're here already."
Hun: "Guests were milling about, so I made an excuse that I was sick and came."
It wasn't untrue. Anyone looking would have seen her only as a sick person. Didn't her fine face have a pale, bluish tint, and weren't her clear eyes pitifully sunken?
Ch'ŏl: "Well—anyway, let's have a drink."
Hun: "Let's."
Hun let out a quiet sigh.
Ch'ŏl: "Why are you sad?"
Hun: "It's lamentable."
Ch'ŏl: "Are you the only one who feels lamentable?"
Indeed, it must be lamentable. Since parting like a dream ten years ago, due to unfortunate mismatches, they had been anxious, wanting to meet but unable to fulfill their wish. Having missed the good times, they met after ten years only to find that Hun's body was being eaten away by pitiful-looking tuberculosis.
Hun: "What's the use of meeting only after I'm about to die?"
Ch'ŏl: "Hmph. I'm not the man I was back then either. My heart has roughened as much as it could,<193> and my body has weathered as much as it could."
Hun: "Still, your body is healthy, isn't it? ... As for me, I couldn't bear waiting passively for the day I die, so I became a kisaeng. I intend to play to my heart's content, roam around to my heart's content, and collapse and be abandoned somewhere."
Tears constantly flowed from Hun's eyes. Warm tears began to overflow from Ch'ŏl's eyes as well.
Ch'ŏl: "So, you went to Tokyo and were living well, they said. How did you end up becoming a kisaeng again?"
Hun: "Please don't ask about the past. Just know me as a fool who ran out of the hospital into the kisaeng registry! As an idiot who doesn't fear death."
Ch'ŏl: "Was it because you didn't have money?"
Hun: "I have no money, no love, and now my own body has just become bothersome even to myself."
Ch'ŏl: "Why must you view the world so grimly? If the right path is blocked, shouldn't you think about taking the left path?"
Hun: "Right or left, for me, only the path to death remains."
Ch'ŏl: "My dear! After making someone pine for ten years, is it right to meet gladly only to talk about dying?"
Hun: "Hmph, hmph... Since you're sitting with a living corpse, what important words could there be besides talk of death?"
Ch'ŏl: "If you intend to die... let's die together."
Hun: "Wh-what!"
Ch'ŏl: "Isn't it good? It wouldn't be unexpected for us, who longed for each other for ten years, to die holding each other."
Hun: "Don't you have a family and a career?"
Ch'ŏl: "I will abandon them for your love. I have the courage to abandon both family and career."
Hun: "Ho... Isn't it too pitiful to offer your family, career, and even your life to a woman who might die today or tomorrow?"
Ch'ŏl: "My dear... Are we in a position to confront each other with such logic? Come now, let's not talk anymore. Either we die together, or else we live together. Let's choose one of the two paths."
New tears welled in Ch'ŏl's eyes, and an immovable resolve showed on his lips.
Hun: "Aren't both difficult? To die together, your life is too precious; to live together, my health is fading away..."
Ch'ŏl: "Then let's try to cure your illness. Shall we?<194>"
Hun: "Hmph, how can an illness that needs clear air, warm sunlight, and peace like a spring breeze be cured while living as a kisaeng in the middle of the city?"
Ch'ŏl: "Then let's go to the countryside. Let's find a village with good air, good scenery, and a good climate and go there."
Hun: "What fate allows that?"
Ch'ŏl: "If I were to prepare accommodations for you in the countryside and come to fetch you, what would you do then?"
Hun: "Why, I would just throw open my arms, follow you, and welcome the days of gratitude and love. But what about your family? What about your career?"
Ch'ŏl: "I will abandon them. Didn't I say I have the courage to abandon them?"
Hun: "Mr. Ch'ŏl!"
Overcome with emotion, Hun could only bury her face in Ch'ŏl's lap and weep.

2. The House of Love

Hun's face, having spent a night embraced in Ch'ŏl's generous arms, brightened with hope and seemed to show a color unseen before. As soon as the day broke, the two climbed the back hill to see the tomb of Princess Myŏngon. Ch'ŏl, reading the epitaph, turned to Hun and said,
Ch'ŏl: "It seems the couple buried in this tomb also shared considerable love."
Hun: "Who passed away first?<195>"
Ch'ŏl: "It says Princess Myŏngon passed away first, and Tongnyŏngwi followed her."
Hun: "Ho—that must be a lie..."
Ch'ŏl: "Then shall we die on the same day, at the same hour?"
Hun: "No. It's a wife's greatest fortune to be buried by her husband's hand."
Ch'ŏl: "And perhaps it's a man's greatest fortune to be buried by his wife's hand."
Hun: "Then let's neither of us die."
Ch'ŏl: "Yes, that's better."
Ch'ŏl plopped down onto the golden grass.
Ch'ŏl: "Come, sit here."
Hun: "If people saw us, they might think we're courting."
Ch'ŏl: "Ha—What courtship is there for an old groom and old bride who've waited ten years? It's worries about living."
Hun: "So, where in the countryside have you decided on?"
Ch'ŏl: "Let's go to Yŏngjong-do in Inch'ŏn."
Hun: "Isn't that where the villa from 'The Cuckoo' (불여귀) is?"
Ch'ŏl: "That's right. The scenery there is good, and while it's close to Seoul, it's also far, so it's the most suitable place for us to live."
Hun: "Do you know anyone there?"
Ch'ŏl: "I have a bit of land there, though not much, so it won't be confining or tiring."
Hun: "When will the house be built?"
Ch'ŏl: "Let's plan to start right away, even tomorrow."
Hun: "So easily?"
Ch'ŏl: "My dear. What could be more urgent for us than this? But once the house is finished, will you be able to quit being a kisaeng right away?"
Hun: "Quit being a kisaeng..."
Ch'ŏl: "Yes, is there something conflicting?"
Hun, composing her clouded expression, said,
Hun: "Absolutely not. I'm all alone without parents or relatives, so what is there to hold me back?"
Ch'ŏl: "Then let's take this road together to Yŏngjong-do, decide on the site for the house, and come back."
Hun: "Yes. Of course, let's go."
Hun still hadn't quite believed Ch'ŏl would rush things like this. But now, overcome by his sincerity, she couldn't utter another word and was simply swept along as Ch'ŏl led. Moreover, she came to believe that doing so was the unparalleled path to happiness for her. The two immediately went to Kyŏngsŏng Station and took an express train to Inch'ŏn. As there was no regular boat to Yŏngjong-do, they hired a small steam launch and reached Yŏngjong-do. After looking here and there, they chose a hundred-pyeong plot on a hill facing the sea where a large ginkgo tree stood. The design, of course, was done by Ch'ŏl and Hun together, sitting right there. They planned a cozy and elegant house: the inner room (anpang) with ondol heating, the side room (kŏnnŏnpang) as a Western-style room, and an outer room (pakkatpang) furnished as a guest room, even considering the lodging<196> needs of occasional visitors from Kyŏngsŏng. The Western-style side room was designated entirely for Hun's illness; to receive warm sunlight, it would have a glass roof, a soft bed, and even a ping-pong room, which Hun particularly enjoyed—the main focus of the house was concentrated on this Western-style room. Drawing the blueprint on paper, the two looked at it for a while.
Ch'ŏl: "Ah, shoot. There's no bathroom."
Hun: "More importantly, your study."
Ch'ŏl: "The study? I can just borrow your room."
Hun: "Then let's build the bathroom right behind this Western-style room."
Ch'ŏl: "Yes, that would be good."
Hun: "I feel like if I just came to live in this house, I'd become a new person within a year."
Ch'ŏl: "Of course, you would. Flowers in spring, sea bathing in summer, autumn foliage, skating in winter—how wonderful would it be if we built our nest in this house and never left throughout the four seasons?"
Hun: "Ah—"
Hun closed her eyes, let out a faint sigh, and shed tears. It must have been a sigh overflowing from a heart bursting with too much joy. Perhaps they were tears shed trembling with the joy that made her wonder if she wasn't in a dream.
That day, Ch'ŏl visited an acquaintance in Inch'ŏn to arrange for funds. He was eager to start the construction the very next day. If delayed any further, it would be the rice-planting season. He knew then it would be extremely difficult to hire workers for construction, even if they were his own tenants. Looking at Hun, who had followed him to Inch'ŏn, Ch'ŏl told her to go back to Kyŏngsŏng first.
Hun: "Then when will you come up?"
Ch'ŏl: "Well, after I buy all the lumber and see the construction begin, I'll come up."
Hun: "So how many days will that take?"
Ch'ŏl: "Well, it will probably take a little over a week."
Hun: "Will you be alright alone during that time?"
Ch'ŏl: "Hmph. We were apart for ten years.<197>"
Hun: "Ho— Then exactly one week. Yes?"
Ch'ŏl: "It might take longer than a week, but I definitely won't be able to go earlier."
Hun: "Then, since I'll be bored, I'll continue working as a kisaeng until the house is completed."
Ch'ŏl: "That's fine too, but don't stay out too late."
Hun: "Don't worry."
Thus, Ch'ŏl went to Yŏngjong-do with carpenters and lumber, and Hun returned to Kyŏngsŏng.

3. A Shattered Dream

"Where did the young mistress say she was going when she left?"
Ch'ŏl asked impatiently.
"I don't know exactly. She went out early this morning."
The housemaid mumbled evasively. Having worked at a kisaeng house before, she certainly wouldn't reply that the mistress had gone out last night and simply hadn't returned.
"What time did she come back last night?"
Ch'ŏl, sensing something suspicious in the maid's demeanor, stared intently, waiting for an answer.
"Uh- uh- What time was it? Anyway, it wasn't that late."
Her manner of speaking, her complexion, everything seemed suspicious.
"So she went out last night and just didn't come back."
"No. She went out this morning."
The maid hurriedly tried to cover up, almost interrupting herself with excuses.
"If she went out last night, where would she have slept and not come back yet? Oh well! Since I told her it would take about a week, I suppose she felt free to go out and have fun."
Ch'ŏl went into the room and rummaged through Hun's things. Inside the dressing table drawer, he found a summons slip from Myŏngwŏl-kwan dated May 9th. May 9th was yesterday. So, she went to Myŏngwŏl-kwan and then disappeared somewhere? Looking at the back of the slip again, it read:
Please come. Kim is waiting. Ung - Nongson -
It was written there.
"Kim is waiting!"
"Nongson!"
So the woman who lured her out was Nongson. What need does Hun have for money now, and why stay up all night to earn it? Ch'ŏl regretted his own impatience, having returned earlier than planned before the week he had promised was up, simply because he missed her too much.
"Ah—if I had just stayed in the countryside, I would have been deceived and none the wiser."
Ch'ŏl immediately went looking for Nongson's house. Of course, Nongson wasn't there either. After probing Nongson's mother, he learned that she had gone to Onyang Hot Springs with a client the previous night. At the same time, he learned that Hun had also gone, paired with<198> a certain Mr. Kim who ran a cloth store in Chongno.
"Ah—I was foolish. A kisaeng can only be a kisaeng after all. Where should I turn these feet that stepped out, regarding the world's ridicule and family turmoil like mere straws in the wind?"
Like a madman, Ch'ŏl drank that night. He called all the kisaeng he knew.
"Ah—What's all this fuss about, what happened with Hun?"
Nan, who knew the situation with Hun well, asked worriedly.
Ch'ŏl: "Hmph, Hun, you say? She got married off to Ch'ungch'ŏng Province."
Nan: "How many places can she marry into with just one body?"
Ch'ŏl: "Could that be? I became a widower, and Hun found a new husband. Ha-ha, so it's all over. It's not the woman's affair that's lamentable. It's pitiful that a life that tried to live ran away towards the path of death. For this body that stepped out of the family and withdrew from work, nothing remains now but the world's ridicule and laughter. What is a house? What are possessions? Everything is just fragments of a shattered dream. Ah—let's run away. Let's run away endlessly."
That night, Ch'ŏl took the 3 a.m. train and left for Tokyo. With the money he had prepared to build the house...

4. The Fallen Camellia Flower

Hun finally returned the night before Ch'ŏl was scheduled to arrive. Rushing home frantically, she found that everything—ah—had gone wrong. Ch'ŏl had already come and gone long ago. Only a postcard filled with Ch'ŏl's anger, mailed from Kyŏngsŏng Station, awaited her.
Hun! This is too much. Mockery reaching this point is akin to murder. Live well. What more can I say? Leaving on the path of wandering. Ch'ŏl
The world instantly went dark before Hun's eyes. It was the end she had been dreading all along. What had to happen had finally happened. Nongson, peering at the postcard from behind her, was also too stunned to speak. Hun, standing blankly holding the postcard, could only let tears stream down like rain.
Nong: "Sister. This is terrible."
Hun: "Hmph. What can be done about what's already happened? I must bear the consequences of my own actions."
Nong: "Where could he have gone?"
Hun: "With his temper, he probably went far away."
Nong: "So what are we going to do?"
Hun: "I'm such a pathetic woman. Why couldn't I speak plainly?"
Nong: "It's all my fault, I'm sorry."
Hun: "Am I of an age to do everything you tell me to? I did it because<199> my heart wanted to."
Hun threw her handbag down as if it were a dirty object and sank onto the floor. The handbag fell half-open, and from its open mouth, a bundle of 100-won bills protruded like the tip of a viper's tongue.
Hun: "Oh, my heart aches. It was all because of that wretched money."
Nong: "Sister, pull yourself together."
Hun: "What's the point of living this wretched life instead of dying?"
Nong: "Let's try to find him and explain the situation. He seemed open-hearted, so maybe he'll understand."
Hun: "How can we find him when we don't know where he went? He's probably far away by now."
Nong: "Still, maybe there will be some news."
Hun: "If there were hope for news, wouldn't I be fortunate? But, but it's probably hopeless. What I did was too grave... He must hate me as much as he trusted me; a nail must be driven into his heart."
That night, Hun checked into the Ŭijŏn Hospital, claiming chest pains. A first-class room on the second floor, facing west! This was the room Hun had stayed in until a month ago after returning from Tokyo. When she left this room, she had resolved never to return. It was a decision to face the end, living or dying, out on the streets. She had buried thoughts of love and health, determined only to play to her heart's content for as long as she lived, for every hour she lived. She had thought she might meet her old love, Ch'ŏl, but she had never imagined it would turn out like this. However, in Ch'ŏl's heart, the flames of love she thought extinguished were still burning fiercely. Hun, who had only blamed heaven until now, offered thanks for the first time. The life she was about to throw away suddenly became precious again.
'How wonderful. A husband who cares for me like a little child, good air more vital than medicine, scenery embracing only peace and happiness, a new life on a beautiful island—let's raise rabbits, keep chickens, grow vegetables, plant flowers!'
Just thinking about it felt like her suffocating chest was opening wide!
'I too will abandon everything. So you too, forget everything.'
When she heard these words, Hun didn't hesitate.
'Yes. Of course, I'll abandon it. What is there I can't forget?'
She answered readily, but one shadow lingered in her heart! It was the debt Hun incurred five years ago when she went to Tokyo. One thousand won wasn't a small amount.
Considering Hun's current popularity, she could probably earn that much in less than two months. But according to Ch'ŏl's urgent rush, he would drag her off to the countryside in less than a month. Of course, Hun wanted to go<200> too. But this time, she had to repay the debt before going. Since she was entrusting her entire life to her husband and going to the countryside, if she couldn't repay it at this opportunity, she would inevitably end up worrying her husband. However, Hun, with her upright character, hated the idea of owing Ch'ŏl even a penny.
'Hmph. As if I'm going after receiving a bride price.'
Being a kisaeng, such thoughts wouldn't leave her mind. After much thought, she consulted her closest friend, Nongson. Nongson boasted that if Hun just went to the hot springs with Mr. Kim, the cloth merchant, and stayed for two or three days, she could extract one thousand won in cash.
"My dear, I don't want to. How can I go with that bear-like fellow? And is it something I could possibly do to Mr. Ch'ŏl?"
At first, she refused. But Nongson urged her again and again.
"Look, just last night at Kugil-kwan, I met Mr. Kim, and when I told him my sister desperately needs a thousand won and asked if he could lend it, he said he'd give it right away if only she'd go to the hot springs once. He's completely smitten, head over heels. If you just grit your teeth this one time, get the money, pay off your debt, won't you be able to get married honorably too?"
At these words, Hun gradually began to listen. Since she was already a woman who couldn't serve one husband, what difference would one more man make? If she just took the money and went off to the countryside, wouldn't the troublesome issue disappear? It would be a sin against Ch'ŏl, but it seemed lighter than the awkwardness of burdening him with her debt. It seemed less painful than the suffering of getting married while owing money, so she ended up agreeing. She deliberately arranged for Ch'ŏl to stay in the countryside for a week, and went to the hot springs as if committing theft. She got the money, but her husband had left. What use was regret now? What use was throwing the money away? There was no way to find Ch'ŏl, who had run off in anger, and even if she met him, what face could she show him? Just when it seemed oil was being added to a dying lamp, this time a hole opened at the base of the lamp, and the oil began to leak out. The games of fate were too cruel.
'Alright, this body was meant to die anyway. A body that won't live long even if it tries, so let's just commit suicide.'
Making this decision, Hun checked into the Ŭijŏn Hospital. She didn't want to die in someone else's house, nor did she find it pleasant to throw her tenacious corpse onto the street. Let's check into the hospital and commit suicide in the room. Then the body would be handled neatly, and there would be no one to dislike it. Where else besides a hospital is death treated as commonplace?
Hun: "My dear Nongson, you can go now.<201>"
Seeing Nongson sitting by the bedside, shrouded in worry, Hun spoke softly.
Nong: "Somehow, sister, your actions seem suspicious, so I don't want to go."
Hun: "You crazy girl. What's suspicious?"
Hun forced a smile. But that unnatural smile made Nongson even more suspicious.
Nong: "Sister, until there's some news from Mr. Ch'ŏl, you mustn't think of doing anything else."
Hun: "Honestly, why are you being like this... You're the one acting suspicious. Hurry up and go. You need to get to your gambling game. Mother is waiting."
Nong: "Then I'll come back even if it's dawn when the game ends. Just lie down quietly."
Hun: "Alright. I'll wait."
After Nongson left, a desolate wind blew through the room again.
'Was I mad? Why did I do such a thing? If only I had just closed my eyes and told him I had a thousand won debt, it would have been fine... Though regretting it a thousand, ten thousand times is futile, the remorseful feeling is uncontrollable.'
A nurse came in. She came in to take the last temperature reading for the night. It was a nurse whose face Hun recognized from her previous hospitalization.
Nurse: "How are you feeling?"
Hun: "My chest hurts terribly."
Nurse: "Have you coughed up blood?"
Hun: "No, not yet..."
The nurse placed the thermometer under Hun's armpit and sat respectfully before the bed.
Nurse: "Your complexion is very pale."
Hun: "Perhaps this time I'm really going to die."
Nurse: "Why do you say such things?"
Hun: "I have no parents or relatives, so if I die, please at least pity me."
Tears flowed from Hun's eyes. It sounded like a joke, but the words carried weight. Coming from the mouth of someone who had made the heartbreaking decision not to live past this night, they were no different from a last will. The nurse didn't have any further words of comfort. She merely looked at her with a pitiful expression and said,
Nurse: "Please don't cry. Getting yourself worked up like that only makes your spirits gloomier."
Hun: "It's good that I can cry. For a body that has reached a state where even crying is impossible, the tears still flow—tears are common indeed."
Nurse: "Is no one coming to care for you this time?"
Hun: "Yes, I'll be sick alone. I've had enough of seeing people who aren't even close relatives forced by duty and sentiment to sit watch, doing nothing but yawn."
Nurse: "Ho— Why are you saying only such lonely things this time? Please don't, calm your heart and try to sleep.<202> Tomorrow I'll bring flowers to put in a vase for you."
Hun: "Thank you."
Nurse: "Well then, sleep well."
Hun: "Then I'll be imposing on you again."
The nurse left too. The room became empty and desolate once more. The calendar hanging on the wall pointed to May 10th. Hun closed her eyes, wondering if it indicated the final day of her turbulent twenty-six years. To die after living only this long, why had she struggled so hard? Trying to save money, she failed; trying to tread the true path, that too did not go as planned; and this time, rushing down the final path of love, she ultimately couldn't pass the final moment and collapsed, did she not? Looking back, her half-lived life seemed like the tearful record of a blood-soaked, defeated loser.
Oh well, let's die. If Nongson rushes in again, even the final path to death will be cut off, and only shame will be added to this body that has no value in living.
Hun opened her handbag, took out the money, wrapped it neatly in paper, and wrote:
"To Nongson"
And below that:
Use this money for my funeral expenses. And on my tombstone, be sure to carve the words "Ch'ŏl's wife".
Now there was nothing left unforgotten, nothing left to cling to. Since Ch'ŏl, her last hope, had gone, what lingering attachments, what affections could there be? She quietly took out the sleeping pills and the razor she had hidden in her handbag. Hun had been an orphan since birth. She grew up without knowing her mother's or father's face. She called out to the
"Mother!"
whose face she didn't know. Now her heart calmed, and not even a fingertip trembled. She opened her bag and took out a set of white clothes. These were clothes she had made to wear when setting up house in the countryside: an oksyangmok (cotton) jeogori top and a mosijinsol (fine ramie) chima skirt. She carefully changed her clothes. Though her heart wasn't sad, tears flowed ceaselessly. After changing, she neatly arranged her hair, letting it down, sat on the edge of the bed, and swallowed five packets of sleeping pills at once. Collapsing onto the bed as if swept down, she held the razor firmly in her right hand and pressed it hard against the pulse on her left wrist. Red blood spurted out, flowing down and beginning to soak the bedding. Hun immediately lost consciousness. Thus, a single camellia flower sadly fell.<203>

**Summary**

A tragic story of reunited lovers, Ch'ŏl and Hun. Hun, now a kisaeng dying of tuberculosis, meets Ch'ŏl after ten years. He offers to abandon everything to care for her in a house he'll build in the countryside (Yŏngjong-do). While he's away making arrangements, Hun, desperate to clear an old debt before starting anew, secretly accepts money from another man (Mr. Kim) in exchange for accompanying him on a trip, arranged by her friend Nongson. Ch'ŏl returns early, misinterprets her absence as betrayal after finding evidence, and leaves in anger for Tokyo. Devastated upon her return, Hun checks into Ŭijŏn Hospital and commits suicide, requesting only to be buried as Ch'ŏl's wife.

From a historical perspective, the story vividly portrays the limitations and despair faced by women, particularly kisaeng, in colonial Korean society, highlighting their economic vulnerability and societal constraints. It also reflects the era's realities like the prevalence of tuberculosis, the mixing of traditional and modern lifestyles (kisaeng houses, hot springs, Western amenities, modern transport), and the fatalistic undertones potentially mirroring the sense of restricted agency under colonial rule.

白衣椿姬哀歌
						李瑞求
1. 봄업는 십년

哲이는 訓의 손목을 잡은 채 멍멍히 서 잇다. 손목을 잡힌 訓이도 고개를 숙인 채 말을 못한다. 뜻밧게 생각지 안은 곳에서 십년만에 맛난 두 사람! 그러나 訓이 만은 의례히 한번은 맛나려니 맛나지 못하면 哲의 잇는 끗을 차자서 뎐화라도 한번 걸니라 생각은 햇섯다. 다만 哲에게는 정말 뜻박의 일이엇다. 哲의 손에 잡힌 訓의 고흔 손길은 앙상하고 싸늘하얏다.
『퍽 수척하얏구료
앗가워 못견대겟다는 듯시 나즉히 무럿다.
『…
訓이는 대답대신 눈물을 흘녓다. 이 때까지 열 업시 잡혀 잇든 손으로 哲의<176> 잡은 손을 마조 굿게 쥐며
訓 『엇잿든 반갑슴니다.
哲 『어느 방에 왓소
訓 『저 삼방에 왓서요.
哲 『온 지 오래요.
訓 『네. 곳 가게 될가 보와요.
哲 『좀 조용히 이약이나 합시다. 그려.
訓 『네. 저도 벼르든 차임니다.
哲 『그럼 내가 오라는 곳으로 오겟소.
訓 『네. 가다마다요.
哲 『그러면 동대문밧 延壽閣!
訓 『그럼 몬저가 기다리서요. 한 시간 안에 꼭 갈게
텬향원 좁은 복도에서 십년 만에 맛난 두 사람이다. 번한 곳이라 더 장황히 이약이 할 수는 업섯다. 연수각으로 압서 나간 哲이가 막 목욕을 하고 맥주를 한잔 기우리랴니 자동차소리가 낫다.
나리는 사람은 訓이엇다.
哲 『아! 발서오.
訓 『손님들이 어름거리길내 몸이 압흐다고 핑게를 대고 왓지요.
안인게 안이다. 누가 보든지 몸압흔 사람으로 밧게는 볼 수 업슬 것이다. 고흔 낫에 푸른 긔운이 도도록 낫빗이 햇슥해지고 맑은 동자가 가엽도록 매알녀 잇지 안은가.
哲 『자- 엇젯든 한잔 합시다.
訓 『먹지요.
訓은 한숨을 가만히 쉰다.
哲 『왜 설소.
訓 『원통함니다.
哲 『원통한 사람이 당신 뿐이겟소.
안인게 안이라 원통하기도 할 것이다. 십년 전에 꿈결가치 손을 난호인 이래 공교로운 상치로 서로 맛나랴 하면서도 뜻을 이로으지 못하고 조바심만 해오든 두 사히이다. 조흔 시절은 다 노치고 십년 만에 맛나보니 訓의 몸에는 보기에도 딱한 페병이 좀먹고 잇지 안은가.
訓 『다 죽게 된 뒤에야 만나니 무엇을 하오.
哲 『흥. 나 역시 그 때의 나는 안이요. 마음은 것칠대로 것치러지고<193> 몸은 험할대로 험해젓소.
訓 『그래도 몸은 근강하시지 안아요. … 나는 나는 죽는 날을 안저서 기다리기가 안탁가워서 기생이 되얏담니다. 마음것, 놀고 마음것, 도라다니다가 어듸서든지 너머저 바릴 작정임니다.
訓의 눈에서는 눈물이 작고 흐른다. 哲의 눈에서도 더운 눈물이 넘처 흘느기 시작을 햇다.
哲 『그래 동경가서 잘 산다드니 엇저다가 또 기생이 되얏소.
訓 『지난 일은 뭇지 마라주서요. 병원에 누엇다가 기생 권번으로 뛰어나온 바보! 죽는 것을 무서워 역이지 안는 천치로나 아라주서요.
哲 『돈이 업서서 그랫소.
訓 『돈도 업고 사랑도 업고 인제는 내 몸동이가 나부터 귀찬키만 해젓서요.
