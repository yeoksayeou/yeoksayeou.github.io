Spring Nostalgia
Bygone Old Days

Son Ch'o-ak
Sometimes my mood turns quite bright and cheerful, yet other times, when a gloominess that feels like falling into a pit thousands upon thousands of fathoms deep in the earth presses down, I reach a point of suffocation where I almost lose consciousness. Today too, waking from such a nightmare, I threw open the front sliding door and looked up at the blue sky, when my eyes fell upon a rose bush by the wooden fence, looking dead with only its gaunt frame remaining. I was startled. Seeing a hint of green on the branches, I sighed deeply, thinking, "Spring must already be here." The loneliness of my heart creeps along every branch reaching towards the sky. Is it sorrow? Frustration? Longing? A desire to go somewhere? I cannot know what it is, but born from my heart…<254>
Even ghosts cannot fathom the transience of this Saha world (娑婆). Where can one inquire about the sorrows of a thousand ages? The tenacious life of humanity, how long will we live that we suffer such deep pain (沈痛)!
I turned my eyes again and looked towards the southern sky, where my hometown, which gave birth to me and raised me, lies. The sorrows of my gentle girlhood days seep into my heart like the tide. I vividly recall the garden of my old home where I used to lie on the clover-covered lawn, tracing the skyline, counting the flying swallows before falling asleep. And how I miss those mountains and rivers (山川) of that day, roaming the wide fields with a basket beside me until the sun set on the West Mountain (西山) with those childhood friends, whose whereabouts I now don't even know, digging shepherd's purse, wildflowers, and mugwort. Ah, the friends of that day, dear friends, the hearts of that day, pure hearts. Stretching my sorrowful voice long, I call again to those bygone old days. Though I try to forget, try to forget, those times become new again, and today especially, I long for them so much. Floating upon the vast horizon of the sea of tears from old memories, seeming to sink, seeming to rise, the green sorrow of nostalgia moistens my pupils (瞳*) again today.
Spring, spring, spring! Greasing the frozen axis of the Earth (地軸) with spring rain, using the warm wind from the South Country (南國) as a drive belt (皮帶), the motor (모-터) of life accelerates its rotation. Oh, people of this land! Farmers of the countryside! The light of spring flows over the earth, and the time to brush the rust off the hoe blade in the fertile fields is not far off. Will you not roll up your sleeves and step forward! To embrace the soil…
But how much must you curse the coming of this spring? The contradiction of cursing while eagerly awaiting (苦待)! And so, unable to cry or laugh, within your breasts must boil a lump of thick resentment (悲憤) like the great mountain ranges (長山). The incarnation of the devil that rides in every spring! The formless vampire called "Spring Poverty" (春窮) begins to threaten the starving village people (鄕民). Escaping the cold, they face oncoming famine (饑饉) and debt collectors (債鬼). How many tragic stories (悲話) of lives turning their backs on their hometown mountains and rivers, packing bundles (봇다리) in the dead of night seeking a new path to life, will arise this spring?

Hometown's White-Clad Maiden

Hong Hyo-min
Thirty years have passed since I left my hometown, and I have no memories left of it.<255> That's understandable. Since I came to Seoul (上京) at age four, there's no way any memories could remain.
However, when our family had fallen considerably in status after coming to Seoul, my mother went down to settle our family property (家産). At that time, there is one distinct, impressive memory.
I was 14 years old then, and compared to the powdered and pomaded (油頭粉面) young ladies I saw in the city (都會), I discovered a truly white-clad maiden (白衣處女).
When the morning sun rose red, I climbed the village hill, partly to see the village scenery, and partly because my childish heart wanted to gather the chestnuts that had fallen during the night.
At that time, quite a few womenfolk were gathered at the well. Among them, what caught my eye was a maiden in plain white clothes (素服), truly a white-clad maiden.
A White-Clad Maiden (白衣處女)! This was clearly one impressive being to me at that time. Perhaps my eyes for the opposite sex were beginning to open then.
Compared to the sophisticated city womenfolk with their overly heavy makeup, seeing a maiden wearing white, with her simple hair as it was, carrying a water jar (물동이), my excitement (感興) was incomparable.
So, forgetting even the chestnuts I was gathering, I sat on the fallen leaves and absentmindedly watched that woman walking along the winding village path. And for a moment,
"When I get married, I will marry that maiden!" I thought with childish fullness (稚氣滿滿) and smiled to myself. That maiden, unaware that there was an audacious young man full of such wild ambition (野心滿滿) looking down from the hill, filled her jar and came out again.
I watched her fetch water 4 or 5 times just like that.
"What are you staring at like that!"
Sŏktol (石乭), my wet nurse's son, spoke to me as if it were strange. I just said,
"Oh, just… nothing…"
and trailed off vaguely, then came down, still gathering chestnuts or doing morning exercises (朝起體操).
After that, claiming I was doing morning exercises every day, I went up the hill and watched the living landscape painting of that white-clad maiden carrying her water jar, walking along the winding village path.
However, this "romantic" game lasted less than 10 days. I was too young and timid (多㥘) then to find out her background (來歷). And that lady in my heart remained an object of my longing (憧憬) until I grew much older.<256>

By now, that maiden must have become someone's mother! But the White-Clad Maiden in my heart has not faded, and I think it was three years later, at age 17, that I published my first practice poem (習作詩) in some paper (某紙) titled "Pure Maiden." Truly, thinking back now, time is like water. Such clean thoughts, such clean phantoms (幻影) no longer dwell (기ㅅ드리지) in me. I long for such a surge of childlike heart (童心) again. (February 12th)

That Scenery and Those People

Sin Im
Just like the song "When the swallows that went to Kangnam (江南) return, spring comes to the land again," whether Kangnam is the swallows' hometown or a place that's warm all year round, I have never set foot there, nor do I know which corner it's attached to, but it is a fact that spring reliably finds its way to this land along with the swallows returning from this place called Kangnam. Perhaps because the time is near for these messengers of spring news, the swallows, to return, one can guess the gentle spring breeze is seeping in by watching the silk skirt hems of women walking the streets flutter energetically, and seeing the thick sheet of ice (어름장) on the riverbank, the remnants (殘骸) of winter, cry out as it breaks apart with cracking sounds, it is undeniably certain that spring has truly come.
Yes. Spring has come. It whispers the news (잇사) of its arrival in my ear too. The spirit of spring flows evenly (골골히) over the great Earth (大地). Within my chest (가삼), within my blood vessels too, the spirit of spring undoubtedly flows. Indeed, how shall I greet this spring? Shall I greet it with joy (깃뿜) or with sadness (슲음)? Surely joy is better than sadness, so it should be a spring greeted with joy, yet rather than feelings of joy, the thread of bitter, stinging, painful memories that makes my brow (이마쌀) furrow takes hold of my brush, what can I do?
What is it? It is the painful feeling that arises in my heart as I trace back to the mountains and rivers and friends of an old village (not my current hometown) from days now dimly distant.
Seeing even a single blade of grass sprouting green with the spring, I intensely miss the back hill of my house <257> overgrown (욱어진) with old pine trees; seeing the gently (潺潺히) flowing river currents, I again recall the clear and lovely stream in front of my house, like silk. More than that, whenever I hear the sound of a trumpet (喇叺) flowing on the spring breeze, the longing for my childhood friends (죽마의 옛벗), with whom I grew up blowing willow-leaf whistles, becomes earnest. Among them, the image of Poknam (福南), deeply imprinted (印직혀) on my young soul (넉), vividly comes to mind. With the intention of resolving all these feelings, I visited the old village one spring day several years ago, but all the sentiments of those old days had become extremely sparse (히박하여젓든). The thought of turning back with the tears of sorrow for the crumbling (허무려저간) old village in my heart surfaces in my mind pointlessly during this spring season.

There is a lesson, "He who diligently strives for good is a follower of Shun (舜之徒也); he who diligently strives for profit is a follower of Robber Chih (跖之徒也)." It truly used to be a beautiful and peaceful village where people loved and helped each other. But after the men in Western suits (양복쟁이) started coming and going (들낙날낙한) in this land, changes occurred where this person's land became that person's land, and then that beautiful village became filled with the evil (惡) of deceiving and stealing from one another.

When I looked for (차저슬 때) the old friends with whom I grew up eating roses in the courtyard of the village school (書堂), some among them had already unfortunately become departed souls (不歸客), some had scattered (홋터젓고) east and west seeking a crust of bread due to life's hardships, and even (좃차) the few (몃) remaining did not give a pleasant impression. Though I wanted to share even a moment's drink with them affectionately, there was a foolish simpleton (바버) who seemed afraid of me, and also an idiot (얼간) who tried to conduct diplomacy (外交) with reckless, idle talk. I cannot understand (아지 못게라)! No matter how heartless this world may be, what fear could there be between them and me, and what is all this talk of diplomacy? Whose fault (탈이든가) was all this? It wasn't their fault, nor was it mine (안이엿만) – unlike some friends nowadays whom one befriends following money, or power, or knowledge, or profession, or fame, we were truly (眞字의) friends who grew up calling each other 'hey' and 'you' without even a hair's breadth of distance, setting aside all calculations of gain and loss (利害打算), yet it was endlessly unpleasant that I couldn't find the true flavor in them now (업섯든). I just forced a smile (선우슴치고) and let it be (말엇든). What was most sorrowful, piercing (사못치는) to the bone, was when, to find the whereabouts (잣최) of Poknam (福男), who had made my heart race as a child, I turned my steps towards the alley where his house used to be, harboring anxious (조마조마한) thoughts,<258> but regrettably (안탓가웁게도), now even the shadow of that time was gone, and only village children were playing (작난만) in the empty lot. If only Poknam had been there, even if ten million changes had occurred in this land, he would surely have welcomed me with the same warm heart as before, but distressingly, his whereabouts were now completely unknown (杳然하엿든). Poknam, where have you gone and what are you doing now? When I visited (차저슬 때) the old village, the water flowing clear as silk in the river in front seemed to cry out (소리처), telling (아뢰는 듯) of your traces, yet in the end (종시), there was no way (바이 업섯다) to know your whereabouts. The Munp'il Peak (文筆峯), standing with a large pine tree on its forehead as unchangingly as before, seemed to bow its waist and embrace me warmly with welcoming hands, and the turf grass in the back field seemed to bow respectfully to its old master, swaying this way and that in the wind, yet the memories of the bond formed (매저진) between you and me back then welled up (북밧처 올나) in my heart, making a bitter taste rise in my mouth. My heart felt like choking (메여지듯이), constricted and painful (압헛다).
Poknam, this is a fragment of the sorrow from when I visited what I called my hometown several years ago. If you too are still alive (살어), surely every spring, every spring, you must think of the grassy field (잔듸밧흘) below the back hill— The sky (하날) is still that sky, the land (따) is still that land, the mountains and rivers are still those mountains and rivers, yet why (웨) is there so much (만튼가) change among people? This is a piece of my heart's thoughts (心思), sorrowfully yearning for my old hometown this spring.<259>

**Summary**

These three essays, penned under the title "Spring Nostalgia" in 1936, explore the authors' complex emotions evoked by the arrival of spring. Son Ch'o-ak contrasts the season's beauty with the grim reality of "Spring Poverty" (춘궁) driving rural folk from their homes. Hong Hyo-min fondly recalls a fleeting, youthful encounter with a symbolic "White-Clad Maiden" in his rarely visited hometown, representing lost purity. Sin Im laments the changes in a beloved former village, noting the erosion of traditional communal bonds and the displacement of friends like Poknam due to modernization ("Western suits") and economic hardship, finding his nostalgic return filled with sorrow and disillusionment. Collectively, they express a deep yearning for an idealized past juxtaposed against the harsh socioeconomic realities and cultural shifts of colonial Korea.

For historians, these pieces offer valuable insights into the lived experiences and sentiments of Koreans during the colonial period. They reveal the pervasiveness of rural poverty (specifically the phenomenon of 춘궁), the deep sense of loss and displacement felt by individuals witnessing rapid social change and modernization under colonial rule, and the potent symbolism attached to hometowns and figures like the "White-Clad Maiden" as representations of a purer, lost past. The subtle critique of external influences (symbolized by "양복쟁이") altering village life is also noteworthy.

그 江山과 그 人物

申琳
「江南 갓듣 제비가 돌아오면은 땅에도 또다시 봄은 온다네」하는 노래와 갓치 제비公의 고향인지 사시장철 더웁기만한 곳인지 내 일즉이 발버본 적이 업고 또한 어느 구석에 붓허잇는 줄도 몰으지만은 이 江南이란 땅에서 도라오는 제비와 한가지로 이 땅에도 봄은 학실히 차저오곤 하는 것이 사실이다. 멀지 안어서 이 봄소식의 使者인 제비가 돌아올 때가 되여서 그런지는 몰으나 길단이는 여인들의 비단 스까트자락이 활기잇게 휘날이는 것을 보와 보드라운 봄바람이 슴여드는 것을 가히 짐작할 수 잇고 겨울의 殘骸인 江가에 두터운 어름장이 우작우작 풀이는 비명을 떨치는 것을 보와 봄은 정영코 왓슴이 틀임이 업다.
그러타. 봄은 왓다. 나의 귓가에도 봄은 왓노라는 잇사를 속삭여준다. 大地 우에는 봄뜻이 골골히 흐르고 잇다. 나의 가삼 속에도, 혈관 속에도 봄뜻은 틀임업시 흐르고 잇다. 과연 나는 이 봄을 엇덧케나 마즐 것인가. 깃뿜으로 마즐 것인가 슲음으로 마즐 것인가. 응당 슲음보다 깃뿜이 나을진댄 깃뿜으로 마저야 할 이 봄이엇만 깃뿜의 感懷보다도 그만 이마쌀이 찝흐려지는 쓰고 맵고 쓰린 추억의 실마리가 나의 붓대를 사로잡고야 마는 것을 엇지하랴.
그것은 무엇인고 하면 벌서 감아 아득하게 지나간 날의 옛마을(지금 고향은 안임) 산천과 벗들을 더듬어 보게 됨에 따라 앗픈 늣김이 가삼 속에 떠오르는 것이다.
봄따라 파름파름 움돗는 풀 한포기 보와도, 老松이 욱어진 나의 집<257> 뒷동산이 몹시도 그리워지고, 潺潺히 흐르는 강물결을 보와도 비단결갓치 맑고 어엽뿐 나의 집 압 개천이 다시금 생각히워지든 것이다. 그보다도 봄바람을 타고 흘너오는 喇叺소리를 들을 때마다 버들입 피리를 불며 가치 자라든 죽마의 옛벗을 그리는 정이 간절하여 지든 것이다. 그 중에도 나의 어린 넉에 깁히 印직혀 잇는 福南의 모양이 그윽히 머리 속에 떠오르든 것이다. 나는 이모든 심정을 풀려는 뜻으로 멧 해 전 어느 봄날 옛마음을 차젓것만 그 옛날 모든 정서는 몹시도 히박하여젓든 것이다. 나는 허무려저간 옛마을의 슲음의 눈물을 가삼에 담고 돌아서든 생각이 이 봄철이면 부질업시 머릿 속에 떠올은다.

「孜孜爲善者는 舜之徒也요 孜孜爲利者는 跖之徒也」라는 교훈이 잇는, 이야말로 서로 사랑하고 도와주는 아름답고 평화스러운 마을이엿섯다. 그러나 이 땅에 양복쟁이 들낙날낙한 후 부터는 차차로 이 사람 토지가 저 사람 토지가 되는 변동이 잇슨 다음은 그 아름답던 마을도 서로 속이고 빼앗는 惡으로 차진 것이다.

나는 書堂 마당에 장미꼿을 따먹으며 갓치 자라든 옛벗을 차저슬 때 벌서 그들 중에는 불행하게도 不歸客이 된 사람도 잇섯고 혹은 생활난으로 빵쪽을 차저서 東에 西에 홋터젓고 남어잇는 몃 사람 좃차 快롭은 감상을 주지안튼 것이다. 瞬間이나마 그들과 한잔에 술이라도 정답게 난우려 하엿스나 나를 두려워하는 못난 바버도 잇섯고 함부로 헛튼 수작으로 外交하라는 얼간도 잇섯다. 아지 못게라, 이 세상이 아모리 야속타 한들 그들과 나 사이에 두려움이 무엇이며 外交가 다 무엇이라. 이것이 모다 누구의 탈이든가. 그들의 탈도 안이요 나의 탈도 안이엿만- 나는 요사이 친우라는 누구누구와 갓치 돈을 따라 사괸 것도 안이고 권세를 따라 사괸 것도 안이고 지식이나 직업이나 명예를 따라 사괸 것도 안이고 그야말로 모든 利害打算을 떠나서 터럭끗만한 간격도 업시 천진난만하게 야, 자, 하고 자라든 眞字의 벗이엿것만 지금의 그들에게서는 참다운 맛을 발견할 수 업섯든 것이 끗업시 불쾌하엿든 것이다. 나는 그저 그런 것이든가 하고 선우슴치고 말엇든 것이다. 무엇보다도 서름이, 뼈에 사못치는 것은 어릴 때의 나의 가슴을 뛰게 하든 福南의 잣최를 알기 위하야  그이 집이든 골목으로 조마조마한 생각을<258> 품으면서 발길을 돌엿스나 안탓가웁게도 지금은 그 때의 그림자좃차 업고 빈터에는 마을아이들이 작난만 치고 잇섯든 것이다. 만약에 福男이 잇기만 하엿다면 잇땅에 천만변화가 잇섯드라도 그는 예와 갓치 뜨거운 심정으로 나를 반겨 마저주엇스렷만은 애달게도 그는 지금엔 종정이 杳然하엿든 것이다. 福男아, 너는 지금 어데 가 무얼하고 잇는고. 나는 옛마을를 차저슬 때 압 강가에 비단결갓치 맑게 흐르는 물결은 소리처 너의 자최를 아뢰는 듯 하엿것만 종시 너의 종적을 알길이 바이 업섯다. 예나 지금이나 변함업시 이마에 큰 소나무를 이고 섯는 文筆峯은 허리를 굽혀서 뜨거운 손으로 반갑게 포옹하여 주는 듯 하엿고 뒷벌판의 잔듸풀은 옛주인을 맛나 반갑게 절하는 듯이 바람에 이리 넘실 저리 넘실 흔들이고 잇섯건만 옛날 너와 나와 매저진 일이 가슴에 북밧처 올나 오히려 입에 쓴맛이 도는 듯 하엿다. 가슴은 메여지듯이 쓰리고 압헛다.
福男아 이것이 몃 해 전에 고향이라고 차저갓든 설음의 한토막이다. 너도 살어 잇기만하다면 봄마다 봄마다 뒷동산 밋헤 잇는 잔듸밧흘 생각하렷만은-하날도 그 하날, 땅도 그 따, 산천도 그 산천이엿만 사람의게는 웨 이럿케도 변천이 만튼가? 이것이 이 봄이면 옛날 내고향을 애달푸게 그리는 心思의 한토막이다.<259>
