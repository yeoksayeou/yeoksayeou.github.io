女高出身인 인테리 妓生·女優·女給 座談會
						大邱三笠町 徐丙柱
February 17th, from noon to 4 PM, upstairs at the Chongno Grill, Seoul

Participants:
Café Madam Pak Hyesuk (Attended Kyŏngsŏng Ewha Girls' High School (京城梨花女子高普) for three years, went to Yokohama and graduated from a Higher Girls' Technical School, worked in the New Drama movement for ten years as a member of T'owŏrhoe, recently active as a film actress, age twenty-nine)
Actress Sin Ŭnbong (Graduated from P'yŏngyang Girls' Higher Normal School (平壤女子高等普通學校), active in the theater movement for nearly ten years as a member of various drama groups like Ch'ŏngch'un-jwa, also famous as a popular singer, age twenty-seven)
Kisaeng Kim Hansuk (Finished Xmyŏng Girls' Higher Normal School (X明女子高等普通學校), then worked as a girls' school teacher in Suwŏn for many years, holding the teacher's whip. Was involved in various movements during that time, age twenty-five)
Dancer Kim Sŏlpong (Graduated from XX Girls' High School (XX女子高普), went to Taeryŏn and became a dancer at the XX Dance Hall, traveled through Pongch'ŏn, Sanghae, Ch'ŏnjin, etc., age twenty-four)
Waitress Chŏng Sujun (Graduated from XX Girls' School (XX女學校), then graduated from Ch'ŏnjin Girls' School (天津女子學堂) and Peijing Higher Girls' School (北京高等女學堂), currently a waitress at Nagwŏn Café in the city, age twenty-three)
Café Madam Yi Kwangsuk (Graduated from Kyŏngsŏng Government Girls' Higher Normal School (京城官立女子高等普通學校), currently runs the café Ŭnnyŏng (銀鈴) in Susong-dong, Seoul)
Waitress Chŏng Chŏnghwa (Graduated from TaeX Girls' Higher Normal School (大X女子高等普通學校), currently active in her profession as a waitress at Nagwŏn Café in Seoul, age nineteen)
Waitress Cho Ŭnja (Graduated from XX Girls' School (XX女學校), currently working at the Tsubame Café near Pagoda Park (빠고다公園) in Seoul, age nineteen)<163>

How Do You View Men?

Kim Tonghwan (Publisher, Samchŏlli) - Thank you all for coming despite your busy schedules. Generally, you are all intelligent women who received higher education in places like Seoul, Tōkyō, or Sanghae. We are curious why you entered these professions in the line of amorous affairs, which could be called those of "Street Angels" (거리의 天使), and how your views on society or your standards for evaluating men might have changed significantly from your high school days. We men and society are very eager to hear your views on love, marriage, society, and life as "Kisaeng who finished Girls' High School," "Waitresses who graduated from Girls' High School," and "Actresses who graduated from Girls' High School." We want to hear what made you become these "flowers blooming on the streets" and think about it once more. So please, speak very frankly, without hiding anything in any way.
Now, Mr. Yi Sŏgu (pen name Kobŏm) will moderate.

Yi Sŏgu - Ladies, having finished Girls' High School, why did you choose such professions at the very bottom of society? After graduating from girls' high school, the expected path would be to become a girls' school teacher with your higher education, or else marry a scholar or doctor, have sons and daughters, and live comfortably. But you became waitresses, kisaeng, dancers, and actresses. I sense a deep "facet of life" (인생의 一面) here. First, please tell us what you've felt through your years of experience about
"What men are generally like."
Let's try to solve the riddle (수수꺽기) of why you became kisaeng and waitresses right here.

Pak Hyesuk (Café Madam) - Well, whether one has education or not, the way of seeing the opposite sex is probably roughly similar, but in my opinion, men are about "drinking and causing trouble, deceiving women," "pretending to have money they don't have," "strutting around thinking every woman in the world has fallen for them," "hateful and disagreeable (밉살맛고 아니 꼽고)," "arrogant..."

Yi Sŏgu - Wait... wait... Can we really start off this fiercely? Please halt that express train for a moment, Ms. Hyesuk. Surely you didn't hold such a "disagreeable view of men" back when you were carrying your book bag to girls' high school.<164>

Pak Hyesuk - When I attended Ewha Hakdang in Seoul, I recited the poetry of "Goethe and Byron" (꾀-테·빠이론) while dreaming of pure-hearted men like "Duke Nekhlyudov" (네류톱公爵) from "Resurrection." I dreamed of a love like that in Shakespeare's (쒝스피어) "Romeo and Juliet." I thought men were gentle, affectionate, clean, and good adults. I held onto this thought even when studying abroad in Yokohama or during my time as a student in Tōkyō. Back then, life seemed enveloped in a pink mist, like "Pandora's Box" (반도라의 箱子), seeming to hold all sorts of mysteries and unknown happiness deep inside. I imagined roses blooming on every street I would walk... Oh, what's the point of saying it all? As I started being deceived by men, my "youth" (靑春) withered away meaninglessly, and things didn't go my way. So, thinking I'd become a "free bird," I tumbled down the path of being an actress, a kisaeng, a café madam, arriving where I am today. Wrinkles have formed on my forehead, and now that I sit here knowing even the "secrets of the opposite sex's body" (異性의 육체의 비밀), it feels like I understand most of the world.

Yi Sŏgu - Is it the same for everyone?

Kim Hansuk (Kisaeng) - What is?

Yi Sŏgu - The way you see men.

Kim Hansuk - I don't know. Hohoho. But I didn't become a kisaeng out of a desire to curse (呪咀하다) the world or men. I simply jumped into this field, where the income is relatively high, with the thought that women too should achieve economic independence. When I was teaching in Suwŏn, I could only earn a salary of forty or fifty wŏn at most. So I left the teaching platform and became a waitress, but the income wasn't that great either. So I learned a bit more singing and dancing (歌舞) and now I'm a kisaeng. Being a kisaeng is nothing to boast about, nor is it anything to be ashamed of; it's just a profession. Treating it as a profession, every evening when the rickshaw comes, I put on rouge, powder my face, change into a silk skirt, and go to places like Myŏngwŏlgwan, Siktowŏn, Ch'ŏnhyangwŏn, and all sorts of restaurants (료리점)<165> to entertain and then come back.

Sin Ŭnbong (Actress) - But it's not just about money either. Since my high school days in P'yŏngyang, I somehow had this passion to pioneer (개척하여) the fields of drama, music, or film. If I knew how to write poetry or songs, I would have taken up the pen and emerged as a female writer, but lacking the natural talent (天分) for that, I jumped onto the stage. Besides, if people like us, who understand theater a bit, don't step in, would any women jump into artistic fields like theater, where you don't make money and get criticized?

Yi Sŏgu - I understand that, but how do you see men then?

Sin Ŭnbong - Are you asking first and second-year girl students such questions? Men are people. Neither better nor worse than women. (Departs immediately due to approaching performance time at Tongyang Theater)

Yi Sŏgu - "Neither worse" is fine, but "neither better" is a bit disappointing. (All laugh loudly)

Chŏng Sujun (Waitress) - Truly, they aren't particularly outstanding. Looking at the customers who frequently visit our place, there are many fine gentlemen, but also plenty of unimpressive ones. Could I ever have dreamed of such scenes when I attended girls' schools in Ch'ŏnjin and Peijing?

Kim Sŏlpong (Dancer) - Still, men are generally reliable (믿엄즉 하고) and affectionate adults. I haven't been in Seoul long, so I don't know well, but the young men who come to the dance hall where I worked in Taeryŏn are mostly good-looking, well-built, spend money freely, and love women well.

Yi Kwangsuk (Café Madam) - Men can be endlessly affectionate if you think of them as affectionate, and also endlessly harsh if you think of them as harsh. If we harbor thoughts of cursing men, isn't it perhaps because we unfortunately encountered such men? If we had met good adults, our eyes would have remained clean and pure (純).

Cho Ŭnja (Waitress) - That's likely true. Educated women wouldn't necessarily have different thoughts. It's the same as women in general society.

Chŏng Chŏnghwa (Waitress) - That's how it is. Though we are out in these professions due to various circumstances, our hearts always long to meet a sincere man and build a "sweet home" (쓰윗홈). So, in terms of admiration and respect, it's all the same. In fact, perhaps because our circumstances are not favorable, the intensity of feeling might be ten times, a hundred times greater than that of ordinary housewives.<166>

Types of Customers Who Spend Money Well

Yi Sŏgu - I was inwardly wondering if there might be women like "Salome" (사로메), who danced gleefully while sucking the fresh blood from John's neck, or if perhaps, unable to contain their pure feelings like "Nana" (나나) or "Camille" (椿姬 - Tsubaki-hime), they temporarily became flowers blooming on the street, perhaps even attempting to kill men like "Carmen" (칼멘). But hearing your answers, I'll rest assured. Hahaha. Now, let's talk about the 'workplace.' Let's put aside the philosophical, uninteresting talk about views on life or men. I heard there are three types of kisaeng: when parting ways after entertaining together at, say, Myŏngwŏlgwan, some customers are sent off with just a nod from inside the room, some are accompanied to the entrance hall with an extra smile, and still others are followed all the way to their car with elaborate farewells. So, how should one spend money to become a first-class (一等) customer among the first, second, and third-class (一等 二等 三等 客) types?

Kim Hansuk (Kisaeng) - You're just criticizing again (험구시지). Is there a distinction among customers? They are all first-class customers. But still... for customers you know from meeting many times, or even for first-timers who make you feel some charm (챰잉), you naturally feel like following them even in your bare sock-feet to the car door handle to see them off. It's just human nature (人之常情), what can you do?

Yi Sŏgu - Isn't that "human nature" about money? For example, do you see off a rascal (놈팽이) who spends a thousand wŏn in one evening to his car, accompany a fellow (작자) who spends a hundred wŏn to the entrance out of courtesy,<167> and for the lingering cheapskates (이노꼬리패 - inokoripae), is a nod from inside the room sufficient greeting?

Kim Hansuk - It's not about the money. However, it would be best to escort every customer to their car.

Yi Sŏgu - I heard it's not just kisaeng, but waitresses too. Is it also related to tips (틥)?

Chŏng Sujun - Generally, yes.

Yi Sŏgu - How much tip should one give to not be considered neither too unattractive nor a ridiculously spending loafer?

Chŏng Sujun - Two wŏn for the waitress on duty (당번), and fifty chŏn each for the extra waitresses (방가이 - bangai). That's probably about the average.

Yi Sŏgu - A fellow who gives a ten-wŏn tip must have ulterior motives—

Chŏng Sujun - I don't know. Hohoho.

Pak Hyesuk - Once, at our 'Venus' (삐-너스), a customer from Ch'ŏnjin came, had one cup of coffee (고-히), and left a ten-wŏn tip. Seeing him show no sign of ulterior motive, I thought, he's truly a first-class man.

Kim Sŏlpong - In Taeryŏn, at the dance hall, there are people who give fifty or a hundred tickets at once after just one dance.

Pak Hyesuk - I heard that if men dance and leave wiping their mouths clean without giving a ticket, the dancers stretch out their hands like this and follow them to the door.

Kim Sŏlpong - There are some fellows like that among them. Misers (깍쟁이). (Hohoho) But then again, dancing once and giving one ticket, then another ticket, feels too commercial and transactional, making both sides uncomfortable sometimes.

Yi Sŏgu - Generally, what types of customers come to play? Are gold prospectors (금광꾼) still the big spenders at restaurants and cafés?

Chŏng Sujun - They are the main group. Old men come and drink a lot, young men come trying to start 'romances.'

Yi Kwangsuk - People who come late at night and drink silently are usually drinking because they're angry.

Pak Hyesuk - Lately, it seems there are many people from the countryside. Many unfamiliar customers visit my place. They are probably all from the countryside.

Yi Sŏgu - That's natural. People come to sightsee in Seoul, see Kyŏngbok Palace for buildings, and feel satisfied only after meeting Pak Hyesuk for personalities before going back down. (All laugh)

Cho Ŭnja - But nowadays, even country folk are shrewd and don't spend money easily. Even gold prospectors who come to Seoul.

Kim Hansuk - From our perspective, it seems there are many customers from banks and companies.

Pak Hyesuk - Still, it's the gold prospectors for now.

Kim Hansuk - And the most irritating ones are those guys who, just to brag about having money, needlessly pull out ten-wŏn and hundred-wŏn bills<168> from their wallets, putting them in and taking them out.

Pak Hyesuk - Truly, I feel like slapping them across the face.

Chŏng Chŏnghwa - But we are the ones who have to provide service, and they are the customers, so we can't do that. We just have to endure it all.

Kim Sŏlpong (misidentified as Kim Unbong earlier, should be Kim Sŏlpong) - Speaking of spending money well, Koreans in Taeryŏn, those involved in major trade, when they get big money, it's common for them to check into first-class hotels and spend three or four hundred wŏn overnight. When they come to the dance hall, they really have fun, brightly and cheerfully, before leaving. They are quite pleasant, not vulgar (야비하지 안코), and they tip the dancers well. But those who have lived in the area for a long time are truly stingy, many are like misers.

Chŏng Sujun - But one strange thing is, customers spend money freely on kisaeng, but when they come to cafés, they tremble, reluctant to spend. Truly incomprehensible are the minds of men. They are both entertainment venues with professional women.

Chŏng Chŏnghwa - Perhaps it's because they tend to view kisaeng as artists, with singing and dancing—to put it slightly differently—while viewing waitresses as worthless prostitutes (賣笑婦 - maesobō). That perception might be the reason.

Chŏng Sujun - Is that why? There are plenty of men who grimace and refuse to give a tip when asked by an extra waitress (방가이). Anyway, Seoul people spend money well, while country people tremble.

Yi Sŏgu - I heard that men with ulterior motives discreetly fold a ten-wŏn bill, and pretending to shake hands, slip it into the waitress's hand as they leave.

Chŏng Chŏnghwa - However, something like this once happened. A certain stylish man, after drinking, was leaving. I followed him to the doorway, and he gave me a tightly rolled 'napkin' (나후낀) (paper - 조히). Delighted, I returned and secretly unfolded it. Wrapped and wrapped, smaller and smaller, wrapped in five or six layers of paper, I thought there must be two or three ten-wŏn bills inside, but in the end, nothing came out. Where could you find such a miser?<169>

What Kind of Man Suits Your Temperament?

Yi Sŏgu - Was it some request you didn't grant? (All laugh) But putting money aside, what kind of men do you like?

Kim Sŏlpong - I like someone who matches my taste. Someone somehow affectionate.

Chŏng Sujun - Describing physique, I like tall, healthy, not too ugly faces, the sportsman type.

Chŏng Chŏnghwa - Short men are too sharp, while fat men look foolish.

Yi Kwangsuk - Someone robust with healthy beauty.

Pak Hyesuk - I like men like Clark Gable (크락케-불).

Chŏng Chŏnghwa - What's disagreeable is men putting on hair oil, powder; I really hate it (실혀요). Some even use compacts (콤백)!

Pak Hyesuk - Physique and personality aside, men lacking literary cultivation seem vulgar. I like those with an artistic fragrance.

Yi Sŏgu - So, what level of salaryman would make you consider marrying him?

Kim Sŏlpong - If it's just the two of us living, sixty wŏn would be enough. Out of that, five wŏn for cosmetics would suffice. Even after becoming a housewife, it's necessary to wear makeup. And bathe occasionally.

Cho Ŭnja - Money isn't absolutely necessary; I don't like 'modern boys' (모-던 뽀이), I prefer a plain (수수한), middle-aged man. Then, things like lifestyle could be adjusted up or down according to circumstances. Anyway, I dislike men wearing masks.

What is Your Income?

Yi Sŏgu - What is your income? Per month, approximately.

Kim Sŏlpong (Dancer - 舞姬) - My lowest is about three hundred wŏn. Out of that, about one hundred wŏn goes for clothes. One girl I work with at the Taeryŏn dance hall earns an average of seven hundred wŏn per month. A friend of mine in Pongch'ŏn also became a dancer and, in ten months, sent eight thousand wŏn<170> home to buy rice paddies.

Chŏng Sujun (Waitress) - We earn around seventy to eighty wŏn. If things go well, it can be about one hundred wŏn.

Yi Kwangsuk (Café Madam) - Even bar workers earn about fifty to sixty wŏn.

Kim Hansuk (Kisaeng) - In the kisaeng society, the highest income earners can make seven or eight hundred 'hours' (時間 - likely referring to units of paid time), so there are quite a few earning over six or seven hundred wŏn.

Cho Ŭnja (Waitress) - It's usually around one hundred wŏn. So, it's enough to cover the tuition for sending my younger sibling to girls' high school.

Yi Sŏgu - Ms. Pak Hyesuk must earn the most.

Pak Hyesuk - Well, I earn about a thousand wŏn a month, but there are so many expenses here and there, so after setting aside about two hundred wŏn for living costs, the rest is all expenditures.

Yi Sŏgu - Huh, a female Iryŏgun (女 이로군 - reference unclear, possibly a misspelling or colloquialism for someone earning/spending a lot, or a specific person/role). So, when are you most distressed?

Pak Hyesuk - When the creditors (빗장이) come demanding money is the most painful time. (All laugh)

Chŏng Sujun - When customers get angry, it's really difficult. I feel distressed.

Kim Hansuk - When I first started as a kisaeng, when customers told me to play the changgo drum and sing short songs (短歌 - tan'ga), I was truly dumbfounded and sweating. Not anymore, though.

Kim Sŏlpong - I am always cheerful. Perhaps because I resolved to make the customers happy, I just smile and get through it.

Kim Hansuk - If I have one complaint, it's that people seem to regard waitresses with slightly higher respect for their personality, but they tend to treat kisaeng completely as commodities. That's unpleasant. I hope the general public (一般社會人士) recognizes this point.

Yi Sŏgu - By the way, do you all intend not to marry?

Pak Hyesuk - Why not? I plan to live as I please until I'm forty, and then become a housewife in my forty-first year. I definitely want to experience married life.

Kim Hansuk - For a woman, the ultimate goal is, of course, marriage.

Yi Kwangsuk - That's a common notion for any woman.<171>

How Did You Overcome Crises of Chastity?

Kim Hansuk - Let me tell you about one ordeal (受難) I experienced. When I first debuted as a kisaeng, a customer called for me at a certain restaurant. After entertaining him, he said he wanted a second round (二次會) and drove the car towards some house near Changch'ungdan. He was said to be a trader from Ōsaka. When we arrived, it was the famous XX Pavilion (XX亭). As we entered, a maid came out, shut the doors firmly, and my companion who came with me was led upstairs by another man, while I was dragged into some corner room downstairs. Seated in the room, he gave me an envelope. Opening it, I found five one-hundred-wŏn bills inside. Only then did I realize this man was after my chastity. So I told him I had no reason to accept this money and tried to return it, but he forcefully shoved it into my handbag. Then he started forcibly pulling at my skirt. It was truly suffocating. No matter how much I screamed, could I even see the shadow of the hostess or a maid? SOS doesn't work here. So, oh dear. With my skirt pulled off, wearing only my undergarment (단속옷), I blocked the man's arms and thought of a plan. I calmed him down, saying, now that I've started taking off my clothes, I surely have no other thoughts, so relax. Pretending to go to the restroom, I went straight to the 'office' (帳場 - Chōba, Japanese term for front desk/office) and made a phone call to the Kwŏnbŏn (券番 - Kisaeng registry/dispatch office). Asking them to save me— Then I was caught by the man again, waiting for time to pass while listening to all sorts of sweet talk (甘言利說). After about thirty minutes, I heard the sound of a car outside, vroom— Only then could I finally breathe easily. Thus, I was rescued by the office staff from the Kwŏnbŏn and the detective who came with them. It was a close call.

Cho Ŭnja - Since our profession is what it is, we often encounter such situations. Once, some man came and bought me a gold ring. I just thought he was a very grateful gentleman. But then one time, he came and asked me to go on a trip to a hot spring with him. Having received something, how could I refuse? I followed him, and as expected, the man was after my chastity (貞操). I barely managed to make excuses, escaped the crisis, and came back to Seoul alone.

Chŏng Chŏnghwa - Once, while I was at Nagwŏn<172> Café, some man fell for me and kept visiting. He'd come and constantly ask me to go out for a walk somewhere. He spent money freely too. I kept refusing and sending him away. Then one time, while I was sitting in a 'box' (복쓰 - booth), that man came in drunk and started making advances again. Just then, someone who looked like a female student came in. She was his wife. She thanked me profusely. For not seducing her husband and always sending him home safely.

Hobbies and Reading

Yi Sŏgu - That's a praiseworthy beautiful story indeed. Now, let's hear about your reading habits and hobbies.

Pak Hyesuk - I read most Korean magazines and newspapers published in Seoul. From Tōkyō, I read Fujin Kōron (婦人公論) every month. My hobby is being an avid movie fan.

Kim Sŏlpong - I read Kaizō (改造), Fujin Kōron (婦人公論), Shufu no Tomo (主婦之友), Fujin Kurabu (婦人俱樂部), Kingu (キング), the Tonga Ilbo (東亞日報), Samchŏlli (三千里), and Kaizōsha's literature magazine (文藝). I generally like literature.

Yi Kwangsuk - Mine are mostly the same. Fujin Kōron (婦人公論), the Chosŏn Ilbo (朝鮮日報), Samchŏlli (三千里), etc. As for hobbies, I like listening to quiet serenade-like music.

Kim Hansuk - I also read the Chosŏn Ilbo (朝鮮日報), Fujin Kōron (婦人公論), Kingu (キング), several magazines published in Korea. And during my time as a girls' school teacher, I liked the 'guitar' (끼-타), so I still play it often. I prefer the guitar to the kayagŭm or kŏmun'go. Besides that, 'rugby' (럭비-) movies are just okay.

Chŏng Sujun - As for me, I like traveling. Going to scenic places during the flower season or autumn foliage season is really nice. I read the Chungang Ilbo (中央日報) and 'Kingu' (キング).

Cho Ŭnja - Books I read are Shinseinen (新靑年), Ōsaka Mainichi Shinbun (大阪每日新聞). My hobbies are sports and music. Quite ordinary.

Chŏng Chŏnghwa - I like mountain climbing. Going wandering carefree through mountain valleys with fresh air and few houses, I don't know how much I like it. I read the Chungang Ilbo (中央日報) and Fuji (富士).

Kim Tonghwan - Thank you very much for speaking with us for such a long time.<173>

**Summary**

This 1936 roundtable discussion, published in Samchŏlli magazine, features eight educated Korean women (graduates of various girls' high schools) working as kisaeng, actresses, café waitresses, a dancer, and café madams in colonial Seoul. They candidly discuss their motivations for entering these often stigmatized professions (economic independence, artistic ambition, lack of other options), their cynical yet sometimes hopeful views on men, experiences with customers (types, spending habits, harassment), income levels, hobbies, reading habits, and their ultimate desire for marriage and a stable family life despite their current circumstances. The conversation reveals the complex realities faced by educated women navigating economic hardship, societal expectations, and personal aspirations under Japanese colonial rule.

Historians might find this article particularly interesting for its firsthand accounts of the lives of 'modern' working women in colonial Korea who defied traditional paths. It provides insights into the socio-economic pressures driving educated women into entertainment and service industries, their perceptions of Korean men and society, the prevalence of Japanese cultural influence (loanwords, magazines, place names), the nascent consumer culture (cafés, dance halls, tipping), and the stark contrast between their educational backgrounds and chosen professions, highlighting both female agency and vulnerability in that era. The discussion about chastity and harassment offers a raw glimpse into the dangers these women faced.

男性을 엇더케 보나요

金東煥(本社主幹) - 바뿌신데 오서주서서 감사합니다. 대체로 여러분들은 모다 서울이나 東京, 上海 等地에서 고등교육을 받은 인테리 여성들로서 어째서 「거리의 天使」라고나 부를 이러한 艶情方面의 職業線上에 나섰으며 또한 고등여학교를 다닐 때에 생각하여 오던 것과 지금 현재에 있어 우리 사회를 보는 방법이라던지 남성을 평가하는 標準이 많이 달려  젔을 줄을 압니다. 우리들 남성과 사회에서는
「高等女學校 마춘 妓生」
「高等女學校 나온 女給」
「高等女學校 나온 俳優」
들인 여러분의 戀愛觀, 結婚觀, 社會觀, 人生觀을 몹시 듣고 싶어합니다. 듣고서 무엇이 「    당신네들을 시케 이렇게 거리에 피는 꽃」이 되게 하였나 하는 것을 다시 한번 생각해 보    려고 합니다. 그러니까 아주 아주 툭 터러놓고 어느 모으로 던지 숨김없이 말슴하여 주서   요.
이제 司會는 孤帆 李瑞求君이 하기로 하겠읍니다.
李瑞求 - 여러분 高等女學校를 마친 몸으로 어째서 이 사회의 맨 밑바닥인 이러한 직업을택하여 나섰읍니까. 여학교 마첬으면 그 배운 고등교육을 가지고 女學校敎師가 되던지 그렇지 않으면 學士 博士의 안해가 되어서 아들 딸 낳고 옥실각실하게 사러야 순서이겠는데 女給과 妓生과 딴사-와 女俳優가 되다니, 여기 깊은 「인생의 一面」이 있는 줄 느껴집니다. 먼저 여러분이 여러 해 경험으로서 느낀 바
「대체 남자란 어떤 것이더라」
하는 그 말슴부터 들려주서요. 여러분이 妓生, 女給이 된 수수꺽기를 여기서나 풀어봅시다.
卜惠淑(喫茶店 매담) - 그야 교육이 있고 없고 간에 異性을 보는 방법이야 대개 어슥비슥하겠지만 내 생각에는 사내란 「술먹고 주정하고, 계집 속이고」 「없는 돈 있는 체하고」 「천하녀자가 다 저한테 반했거니 웃즐 거리고」 「밉살맛고 아니 꼽고」 「건방지고…」
李瑞求 - 가만… 가만… 이거 대번에 이렇게 맹렬해서야 되겠어요. 그 急行列車 잠간 멈추서요, 惠淑氏이기로니 녀학교에 책보끼고 다닐 때부터 그런 「아니꼬운 男性觀」을 가젔을 리야 없겠지요.<164>
卜惠淑 - 서울서 梨花學堂 다닐 때는 「입으론 꾀-테·빠이론」의 詩를 외우면서 「復活」외 「네류톱公爵」같은 순정적 남성을 그리었지요. 쒝스피어의 「로미오와 쥬-리엣」에 나오는 그런 연애를 그리었지요. 사내란 상냥하고 다정하고, 깨끗하고, 착한 어룬이거니 하였었지요. 이 생각은 橫濱에 留學할 때나 東京留學生時代까지 가지고 있었지요. 그 때는 인생이 열분 분홍안개 속에 잠기어 「반도라」의 箱子 모양으로 온갓 神秘와 온갓 未知의 행복이 그 속 깁히 감처 있는 듯 하였지요, 내가 거러야 할 거리거리에는 薔薇花가 송이송이 피고 에그 다 말해 무엇해요. 그리던 것이 사내들에게 속이우기 시작하여, 「靑春」은 덧없이 시들고 세상일은 내 뜻대로 안되고 보니 「자유로운 새」나 된다고 여배우, 기생, 喫茶店 매담으로 구을고 구을어 오늘에 왔지요. 이마엔 주름살 잡히고, 이제는 「異性의 육체의 비밀」까지 다 알고 나앉으매 세상의 대부분은 다 알어진 듯해요.
李瑞求 - 다 그런가요.
金漢淑(妓生) - 무엇이?
李瑞求 - 사내보는 방법이.
金漢淑 - 몰라요. 호호호. 그러나 저는 무슨 세상이나 남성을 呪咀하고 싶은 생각으로 이렇게 기생 된 것은 아니래요. 다만 여자도 경제적으로 독립하고 보자는 생각에서 비교적 수입이 많은 이 方面에 뛰어 들었지요. 水原 어디서 교원생활할 때야 불과 사오십원 월급 밖게 더 받을 수 있더래요. 그래서 교단에서 뛰여내려 女給이 되었더니 그도 수입이 그리 대단치 않어요. 그래서 歌舞를 조금 더 배워 가지고 지금 기생이 되었지요. 기생이 자랑도 아니고 그렇다고 부끄러운 것도 아니고 그저 직업이지요. 직업이거니 하고 저녁마다 인력거오면 연지찍고 분바르고 비단치마 가라입고, 明月舘이요, 食道園, 天香園이요, 온갓 료리점으로<165> 가서 놀다 오지요.
申銀鳳(女俳優) - 그러나 꼭 돈만 목적하고 이 노릇하는 것도 아니지요. 나는 평양서 여고다닐 때부터 어쩐지 연극이나 음악이나 영화 방면을 개척하여 보았으면 하는 熱이 있었어요. 내가 만일 시나 노래를 지을 줄 알었더면 붓을 들고 여류작가로 나섰을 것이지만 거기 天分이 없는 탔으로, 舞臺에 뛰어 올랐어요. 또 우리같은 좀 연극을 리해한다는 사람들이 뛰어들지 않으면 돈 안생겨, 욕먹어 하는 그 연극같은 예술방면에 뛰어 나오는 여성이 있을 까닭이 있나요.
李瑞求 - 그것은 알겠는데 그러니 남성을 어떻게 보여저요.
申銀鳳 - 一二年級 女學生들이라고 그런 말 무러요. 남성이란 사람이지요. 여성보다 잘나지도 않고, 못나지도 않고 (東洋劇場 出演時間 臨迫으로 곧 退席)
