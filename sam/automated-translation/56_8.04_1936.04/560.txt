詩經
						金岸曙
Regarding the Translation of the *Shijing*

Someone once said that the mouth is the root of misfortune, and now I cannot help but accept this misfortune. There is likely nothing more frightening than not guarding one's words. Now that it has come to this, even if someone were to laugh anew, the matter is already amiss, and responsibility and fact alone will remain forever. What else is this but a reason why words should be guarded? Having already taken responsibility and spoken, I cannot break my word (食言) just because it is difficult; it is truly regrettable.

As is the case with any poetry, when reading the *Shijing*, although the phrasing is difficult, I did not think it would be so hard to translate. This was the beginning of the misfortune. When I mentioned this to P'ain (巴人), he suggested that I should try translating it. I couldn't back out then and there, so I agreed.

However, when I actually took up the brush and faced the manuscript paper, wondering how to transfer it, <338> there was truly a distance as vast as the Pacific Ocean compared to my thoughts. It is probably correct to say that I even thought it was beyond my capabilities. Thus, while cross-referencing with the Japanese translation (日譯) and carefully examining the commentary (注釋) in the original annotated edition of the *Shijing* (詩傳原本), I attempted what I call a translation, but it was exceedingly difficult. Because it is ancient poetry, although its meaning may be simple, it is exceedingly hard to understand.

Therefore, I followed only the broad meaning of the original text (原文) and began a very free transplantation (移植). I do not know if this is right or wrong, but this is the method (方式) I have used for translating poetry until now. I felt a word of personal confession like this was necessary, which is why I am deliberately saying this. Consequently, there will be considerable bold freedom in this poetry translation of mine. And there will likely be mistranslations (誤譯) here and there. For this, I pray for much scolding.

關睢 (Kwanjŏ - Water Birds)

Crying, crying, the water birds circle in pairs,
Singing from the deep river islet,
A beautiful person seeks a mate,
To enjoy life with a beautiful person.
關關睢鳩<339>
在河之洲
窈窕淑女
君子好逑

Uneven, the water plant (*xingcai*) floats on the water,
Wandering endlessly here and there.
The beautiful heart of a beautiful person,
Seeks the beautiful person day and night.
Seeking, seeking, and seeking again,
In sleep, in dreams, before awakened eyes.
Is there no end to the constant seeking heart?
Tossing and turning, unable to fall asleep.
參差荇菜
左右流之
窈窕淑女
寤寐求之
求之不得
寤寐思服
悠哉悠哉
輾轉反側

Uneven, the water plant floats on the water,
Plucking it clump by clump. <340>
The beautiful heart of a beautiful person,
When meeting the beautiful person,
The *kŏmun'go* and zither sound, oh, how harmonious.
Uneven, the water plant floats on the water,
Plucked and prepared, a delicious side dish.
The beautiful heart of a beautiful person,
Simply enjoying life with the beautiful person,
Playing drums and bells together.
參差荇菜
左右釆之
窈窕淑女
琴瑟友之
參差荇菜
左右芼之
窈窕淑女
鍾鼓樂之

葛覃 (Katt'am - Kudzu Vine)

Thickly, thickly the kudzu vine grows beautifully,
Covering the valley abundantly.
Oh, the lovely appearance of the fresh green leaves. <341>
The oriole, *kkwaekkol kkwakkol*, circles the sky,
Quietly landing on the kudzu thicket,
Its song, voice by voice, is so clear.
葛之覃兮
施于中谷
維葉萋萋
黃鳥于飛
集于灌木
其鳴喈喈

Thickly, thickly the kudzu vine grows beautifully,
Covering the valley abundantly,
The lush, dense green leaves.
Cut it, boil it, spin thread, and weave,
Shall I make fine hemp clothes?
Wearing them freely, how joyful.
Court lady (女官?), convey my words to the King,
Tell him I miss my parents at my family home (親庭).
Wash my everyday clothes.
Leave the fine hemp clothes as they were washed.
Which clothes, then, shall I put on <342>
To go back and see my parents?
葛之覃兮
施于中谷
維葉莫莫
是刈是濩
爲絺爲綌
服之無斁
言告師氏
言告言歸
薄污我私
薄澣我衣
害澣害否
歸寧父母 <343>

**Summary**

Kim Ansŏ prefaces his Korean translations of two poems from the Chinese *Classic of Poetry* (*Shijing*) with an apology for the difficulty and potential inaccuracies of his work. He admits to underestimating the task, relying on Japanese translations and commentaries, and opting for a "free transplantation" rather than a literal rendering. He presents his versions of "Kwanjŏ" (seeking a virtuous mate) and "Katt'am" (gathering kudzu, weaving, and longing for home). For historians, this article reveals the challenges and methods of cultural transmission during the colonial period, showing Korean intellectuals engaging with classical East Asian texts, translating them into vernacular Korean, and acknowledging reliance on Japanese scholarship while asserting their own interpretive freedom. The candid discussion of translation difficulties and the choice to translate Chinese classics into Korean reflect efforts to maintain and develop Korean culture under Japanese rule.

詩經譯에 대하야
입은 화단의 근본이라고 누구가 이야기 하엿거니와, 나는 지금 이 화단을 밧지 아니할 수가 업습니다. 말을 삼가지 아니하는 것처럼 무서운 것은 없을 것이외다. 지금 와서 새삼스러이 뉘 웃는다한들 일은 발서 틀녀지고, 언제든지 責任과 事實만이 남게 되엿스니, 이것이 말이란 삼가야 할 것이 아니고 무엇입닛가. 이미 責任을 지고 이야기를 한 이상, 어렵다고 食言할 수도 없는 일이고, 참말 딱하외다.
엇더한 詩든지 그러하지만은 詩經을 읽을 대에 비록 文句의 어려운 것은 잇다하더라도 번역하기에 그러케 어려울상 십지 아니하엿습니다. 이것이 화단의 發端이외다. 이런 이야기를 巴人에게 하엿드니, 그러면 네가 번역을 해보라는 것이외다. 꽁문이를 새삼스러이 뺄 수 없고 그러자고 약속을 하엿든 것이외다.
그러나, 그러나 막상 붓을 들고 원고지를 대하야 무엇이라고 옴겨보랴니까<338> 생각과는 실로 太平洋만하나 거리가 잇섯으니, 나의 힘으로 할 수 업다고까지 생각한 것이 아마 올흘 것이외다. 이리하야 나는 日譯과 對照하야 가면서, 詩傳原本의 注釋을 톱아가면서, 번역이라고 하여 보앗으나 如干만 어럽지 아니하외다. 古詩이기 때문에 비록 그 뜻은 單純하다 하여도 如干만 알기 어려운 것이 아니외다.
그리하야 나는 原文의 큰 뜻만을 따라 가장 자유로운 移植을 시작하엿습니다. 이것이 올흔지, 납분지는 몰으나, 나는 如何間 이러한 方式으로 詩의 번역을 지금까지 하여 왓습니다. 이러한 것이라고 한마듸 내 자신의 告白이 필요할상 십허서 일부러 한마듸 하는 所以거니와 그러기에 나의 이 詩譯에는 상당히 대담한 자유가 잇을 것이외다. 그러고 가다가다 誤譯이 잇을 것이외다. 이에 대하야 만흔 꾸지람이 잇서지어다고 빔니다.
