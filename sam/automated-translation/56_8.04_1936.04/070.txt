**Body Text Translation:**
Tan'gun Rŭng
						Yi Kwangsu

It was already several years ago, one summer day. Mr. Kim Sŏngŏp and I, just the two of us, left P'yŏngyang by car with the intention of visiting and paying respects at the Tomb of Tan'gun (檀君陵) in Kangdong (江東).

The route our car took went via Mansudae (萬壽臺) and Ŭlmiltae (乙密臺), rounded Moranbong (牧丹峯), passed through Hŭngbu, skirted the west side of Haeamsan (海岩山), and headed northeast towards Kangdong on the east side of Taesŏngsan (大城山).

Mansudae is the site of Koguryŏ's old palace, where burned gate foundations were discovered last year, and Ŭlmiltae is the historic site of the Ŭlmil Sŏnin (乙密仙人), who holds a tradition of ascetic practice unique to Chosŏn since the Tan'gun era (檀朝), much like Silla's Kuksŏn (國仙).
The statement that the capital P'yŏngyang itself was the dwelling place of Wang Kŏm (王儉), the mountain sage who came from the east, signifies the flourishing of this Way of the Immortals (仙道 - Sŏndo) since the Tan'gun era. When Ch'oe Koun (崔孤雲), writing on the stele of the Kuksŏn*nang (國仙*郞), spoke of the Way of the Kuksŏn (國仙道) saying, "Our East has the Way (吾東有道)," it surely did not refer only to Silla. It must have been this Way of the Kuksŏn that trained the minds and bodies of Koguryŏ men, enabling them to possess the spirit (水魄 - sup'aek, lit. water-soul?) to establish a great empire spanning north and south for ten thousand *ri*. The Ŭlmil Sŏnin was likely a great immortal among them.<70>

In the pine forest going from Ŭlmiltae to Moranbong, on the side facing the Taedonggang (大洞江), there is a place called Kirin'gul (麒麟窟 - Qilin Cave), said to be a relic related to King Tongmyŏng (東明聖王), but its exact nature is unknown. Yŏngmyŏngsa (永明寺) Temple is said to originally be the site of a Koguryŏ palace. How could the palm-sized space between Ŭlmiltae and Moranbong be so profoundly deep yet brightly open? It is truly a landscape that can be called a wonder of nature. Our ancestors recognized this scenic beauty well, building a palace in front and structures like Pubyŏngnu (浮碧樓) Pavilion, Tŭgwŏllu (得月樓) Pavilion, Chŏn'gŭmmun (轉錦門) Gate, and Hyŏnmumun (玄武門) Gate behind. Although they were half for military use and half for appreciation, we see how they fully savored and supplemented this natural beauty.

That a vulgar building like Yŏngmyŏngsa Temple stands before Moranbong is tolerable, but the sight of a restaurant building called お牧の茶屋 (O-maki no chaya - Maki's Teahouse) occupying the heart of this landscape, and the chaotic planting of acacia trees in this world-class scenic spot, is unbearable to witness. For a knoll like Moranbong, small in size yet outstanding in shape, even tall pine trees should be avoided. Only low-growing shrubs like dwarf pines or junipers and mountain grasses should be left, so as not to disturb its contours. Furthermore, building a pavilion called Ch'oesŭngdae (最勝臺) on the very summit is like cutting off Moranbong's head; if a pavilion were to be built, it should be placed much lower than the peak, allowing the pavilion to rest against and be embraced by the peak's bosom, not suppressing the peak's energy.

The name Moranbong (牧丹峯 - Peony Peak) clearly comes from T'aebaek (太白 - Great White). In ancient Chosŏn, it's clear from present place names <71> that the nation had its national T'aebaek and Sobaek (小白 - Small White), and each district had its own T'aebaek and Sobaek. Paektusan (白頭山) was the nation's T'aebaek, the head of the white mountains, called Pakmŏri (박머리) or Pangmari (박마리). Today's Myohyangsan (妙香山) was also originally T'aebaek. Looking at the topography of the capital P'yŏngyang, there is no doubt that Moranbong is T'aebaek and Ŭlmiltae is Sobaek. Everyone in Kangdong knows that mountains given the Chinese characters for T'aebaek are still called Hambak (함박 - likely a phonetic change from Hanbak, 한박 - large gourd bowl) in our language, and Sobaek is called Ttchokpak (쪽박 - small gourd bowl). It seems natural that the name Hambak, evoking the *hambak* flower (Magnolia sieboldii), changed to Moran (牧丹 - peony). Kangdong's T'aebaek and Sobaek, Kirim's (吉林 - Jilin) Sobaek mountain range (小白山脈), and Seoul's Puaak (負兒岳) and Paegak (白岳) are likely mountain names originating from the same line of thought.

Beyond Moranbong is a place called Hŭngbu (흥부). This place name comes from the temple name Hŭngboksa (興福寺). Hŭngboksa, originally named Ibullan-sa (伊佛蘭寺), along with Hŭngguksa (興國寺), was the first temple in our land and the great western temple of P'yŏngyang during the Koguryŏ period. Even now, traces remain of the canal that drew water from the Taedonggang for both drinking and boat transport.

In this vicinity, the road follows the winding cliffs of the Taedonggang, a hilly area with a truly unique landscape. Red tile fragments (赤瓦片) from the Koguryŏ period are visible everywhere. Near Haeam, in the middle of a millet field, stands a wooden marker (木碑) indicating a Koguryŏ palace site (高句麗宮址). Records and excavated artifacts clearly show that this was a great metropolis stretching 20 to 30 *ri* in all directions, centered on Moranbong, extending to the T'osŏngsan earthen fortress mountain, forward to the Outer Fortress (外城), and east to areas like Munsusan (文殊山), Pan'gyo-ri (盤橋里), and T'osŏng (土城). This is the old site of the capital P'yŏngyang, with 230,000 households and a population of 1,500,000, which was destroyed by the allied forces of Silla and Tang.<72> All that remains today of the Koguryŏ culture from just over a thousand years ago are the architecture and murals of the Five Tombs of Kangso (江西五陵), the cement (?) stone pillars (세멘도石柱) of Tŭgwŏllu, two foundation stones (礎石) of Pubyŏngnu, two foundation stones in front of the P'yŏngyang Museum, and the T'utŏpsŏng (두텁성 - thick wall), which was the Taedonggang embankment work for the Middle and Outer Fortresses.

Tan'gun was but the day before yesterday, King Tongmyŏng was yesterday.
A remarkable five thousand years pass in the blink of an eye.
As life is boundless, it seems like a dream.

Truly, 1,000 years is a moment, and 5,000 years is but a short while. The life of a nation is eternal and boundless, "if only there is spirit (精神) and sincere effort (精誠)." There is no need to look back with agitation at our remarkable 5,000-year history. There is no need to be drunk on the reminiscence of past glory, nor to lament past sorrows. We must simply do our best with present strength and sincerity to build the future. Cause inevitably produces effect, and even heaven cannot take away the cause built by my own hands.

How many bends does the Taedonggang have? A thousand bends? Ten thousand bends?
Flowing, flowing, flowing, it reaches the end of the sky.
Seemingly broken, yet continuously flowing, like that river water.<73>

Taesŏngsan is the guardian mountain (鎭山) of the capital P'yŏngyang. While one might wish it were a bit larger, a bit taller, the grandeur of its lines does not lack the dignity of a sacred mountain (聖山). Legend says there are ninety-nine ponds on this mountain, likely military reservoirs (軍用貯水池).

When the car reached the front of Kangdong, several dozen local dignitaries came out to greet us. It seemed they welcomed me for coming specifically to visit the Tomb of Tan'gun. In today's world, Chosŏn people who deliberately seek out Tan'gun's Tomb are probably extremely rare. In the old days, the old scholars, infected with the malignant disease of崇明 (Sungmyŏng - reverence for Ming China), prevented the people from even hearing Tan'gun's name, making them believe the Three Sovereigns and Five Emperors (三皇五帝) were their ancestors, hence the area around Tan'gun's tomb was desolate; and so it remains today.

The Kangdong dignitaries repeatedly apologized for their failure to properly protect Tan'gun's Tomb as they guided us there. Before Hambak and Ttchokpak, the T'aebaek and Sobaek of Kangdong, I bowed low before the tomb of Tan'gun, our ancestor who first gave us culture and national life. The local people who greeted me also bowed low together. Unworthy descendants prostrating before the glorious X image! Are we not truly unworthy descendants?

To think that even the villagers living right beside the tomb do not know how to appreciate the tomb of this lofty ancestor, allowing chickens and dogs to tread upon it, is an extremely dreadful (悚悚) matter.

There is, admittedly, the question of whether it is truly Tan'gun's Tomb or not. However, even during the Yi Dynasty (李朝), the Kangdong county magistrate (江東縣令) performed rites (致祭) here annually, and folk tradition has passed down by word of mouth that this tomb is Tan'gun's.<74> Isn't it then Tan'gun's Tomb? Let the pretentious intellectuals doubt the existence of Tan'gun and the existence of Tan'gun's Tomb as they please. Even so, Tan'gun, who first gave us national life and taught us the 360 aspects (三百六十事) of cultural life, is an undeniable reality (實在). And if Tan'gun is real, as long as no other tomb of that great figure is discovered elsewhere, is there any place other than the Kangdong Tan'gun Tomb that we can consider Tan'gun's Tomb? Therefore, should we not revere and protect the Kangdong Tan'gun Tomb as the royal tomb (陵寢) of our progenitor Tan'gun (始祖 檀君)?

Chosŏn people are said to worship their ancestors (祖先崇拜). Wherever they go, before the tombs of their ancestors, they spend much wealth to erect stone figures (石物), build memorial halls (祭閣), employ mountain keepers (山直이) for protection, and visit the graves in spring and autumn (春秋省墓). Having done so, how can they leave the tomb of Tan'gun, the ancestor of ancestors, to become overgrown with mugwort, while the tombs of all the great benefactors (恩人) of the nation remain unknown?

While they strive to compile genealogies (族譜) for nameless ancestors who merely ate, drank, and died, there is no one willing to contribute even a penny (分錢) to publish the history of all Chosŏn people, the genealogy of genealogies, and the biographies of great figures. Misguided ancestor worship ultimately ruined Chosŏn.

Even now, is there no wealthy Chosŏn person of special intention (特志家) willing to donate 10,000 *wŏn* or so to repair and protect Tan'gun's Tomb, compile and publish a history of Chosŏn (朝鮮史), publish a dictionary (辭典) and grammar (文典) of the Chosŏn language, find and commemorate the remains of all the national great figures (民族的 偉人) who are Tan'gun's <75> good descendants and our esteemed elder ancestors (兄祖), and compile and publish their biographies? It's not a large sum; 100,000 *wŏn* would suffice. Are there no descendants capable of such a task? Are there no descendants capable of this?

The Kangdong dignitaries collected donations (義捐金) for the repair and preservation of Tan'gun's Tomb, but it has been halted due to certain circumstances (某事情). Let one person step forward! Just one person!

On this day, I undeservedly received the courteous hospitality of the local dignitaries, looked up at Hambak, Ttchokpak, and Adalmae (?) for a day, spent the day filled with boundless emotion (感慨), passed through Tongmyŏnggwan (東明舘) in Sŏngch'ŏn (成川) and the 32 Old Heavens (三十二舊天) of Samdŭng (三登), and came down by water route in the rain to P'yŏngyang, the ancient capital of Tan'gun. P'yŏngyang, the birthplace of Chosŏn culture; P'yŏngyang, possessing the most glorious history of the Chosŏn nation; P'yŏngyang, which evokes infinite attachment in Chosŏn people through its scenery alone.

However, that P'yŏngyang was turned into a field of mugwort by the hands of the Silla-Tang allied forces over 1,200 years ago. 380,000 core elements (精膸分子) of Koguryŏ were taken captive to Tang China. The Han Chinese (漢族) were determined to eradicate Koguryŏ, their great nemesis (怨讎) for generations, leaving them no strength to rise again (再起). Yet, the Silla people acted as their agents (앞잡이). Among the Three Kingdoms, the Silla people possessed the most slavish disposition (奴隸的根性). The culture and bloodline of Koguryŏ, shattered like jade, were severed, and only the bloodline and spirit of Silla, which sought precarious safety, remained—this has been the misfortune of the past 1,000 years.<76>

**Summary**

Yi Kwangsu recounts his journey to the purported tomb of Tan'gun near P'yŏngyang. He reflects on the historical significance of the P'yŏngyang area, recalling the grandeur of Koguryŏ and lamenting the state of neglect of historical sites like Tan'gun's tomb and the general disregard for national history among Koreans. He contrasts the elaborate care given to private ancestral graves with the abandonment of national heritage. Yi passionately calls for Koreans, particularly the wealthy, to take responsibility for preserving their national identity through funding historical research, publications (history, dictionary, grammar), and the commemoration of national figures, starting with Tan'gun's tomb. He concludes by blaming Silla's "slavish" alliance with Tang China for the destruction of Koguryŏ and the subsequent decline in national spirit.
From a colonial history perspective, the article is significant for its strong expression of Korean nationalism centered on Tan'gun, Koguryŏ, and the distinctiveness of Korean culture ("Our East has the Way"). Yi's call for preserving national heritage and history, including language, was a form of cultural resistance against Japanese assimilation policies. His sharp criticism of Silla's past actions and perceived "slavish nature" could be interpreted as a veiled commentary on contemporary collaboration or a nationalist attempt to construct a more favorable historical narrative emphasizing Koguryŏ's strength and tragic demise, thereby fostering national pride and unity in a difficult period.

檀君陵
						李光洙
벌서 여러해 전 어느 여름날이었습니다. 나는 金性業 君과 단 둘이 江東의 檀君陵을 奉審하랴고 자동차 平壤을 떠났습니다.
우리가 탄 차는 萬壽臺와 乙密臺를 거처서 牧丹峯을 돌아 흥부를 거처 海岩山 서쪽을 스처서 大城山 동쪽으로 동북을 향하야서 江東으로 가게 路次가 되었습니다.
萬壽臺는 高句麗의 옛 대궐터로 작년에 불에 탄 門趾가 발견되었고 乙密臺는新羅의 國仙과 같이 檀朝이래의 朝鮮 고유의 修道傳說을 가진 乙密仙人의 遺址.
平壤 서울 자체가 東來 山人王儉의 宅이라 함은 檀君朝이래로 이 仙道의 旺盛하엿음을 표하는 말일 것입니다. 崔孤雲이 國仙*郞의 碑에 國仙道를 말하야 「吾東有道」라 하엿음은 新羅만을 가라친 것이 아니엇슴은 물론이외다. 고구려 男兒의 心身을 단련하야 남북만리의 대제국을 세우는 水魄을 갖호게 한 것이 또한 이 國仙道일 것이니 乙密仙人은 그 중 아마 큰 仙人인가 합니다.<70>
乙密臺에서 牧丹峯으로 가는 松林中, 大洞江으로 면한 쪽에 麒麟窟이라는 것이 있어 東明聖王에 관한 유적이라고 전하거니와 무엇인지 알 수 없고 永明寺는 본래는 고구려의 궁궐터라고 하거니와 乙密臺와 牧丹峯의 사이 손ㅅ바닥만한 지면이 어떠케도 그러케 深邃하고도 陽明하게 생겻을까 실로 자연의 驚異라 할만한 風景이어니와, 우리네 선인들은 이 풍경미를 잘 인식하야 압헤는 궁전을 지엇고 뒤에 浮碧樓, 得月樓. 轉錦門, 玄武門 같은 것을 지어 비록 半軍用, 半翫賞用이라 하더라도 이 자연의 미을 십분 咀嚼하고 또 보충한 것을 봄니다.
