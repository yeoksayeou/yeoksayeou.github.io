Publication Date: 1936년 04월 01일
Article Title: 「솜틀거리」에서 나온 消息 (News from "Cotton-Fluffing Street")
Author: 宋影
Article Type: 소설 (Fiction/Novel)

News from "Cotton-Fluffing Street"
						Song Yŏng

Teacher!
Have you been keeping well during this time?
How did you manage to get through that cold winter, Teacher, especially with your weak constitution?
Moreover, last winter was a cold spell rarely seen in recent years.
My grandfather at home
said, "I've never seen such cold," and would stroke the icicles hanging from his sideburns every morning.
Even we, who stayed warm by the brazier in the warmest part of the room, couldn't stretch out once and spent the time huddled up.
Teacher!
You mentioned there wasn't even a brazier in your room, didn't you?
You said the spiderwebs hanging from the ceiling shook, didn't you?
You said the calendar on the front wall and the embroidery* hanging on the back wall also shook, didn't you? <368>
You said a knife-like wind poured in through the hole pierced in the wall for ventilation, didn't you?
You said the tips of your nose, ears, knees, and toes felt so cold they might fall off, didn't you?
You said it wasn't just stinging and sharp, but painful, didn't you?
Teacher!
But this wretched winter, this heartless winter, this winter with no eyes or nose, has finally gone away.
The winter that tormented and harassed you as it pleased, Teacher!
The winter that afflicted your body in every single part! The winter that made your heart weep, laugh, mocked, and insulted you.
It seems even this darn winter has its time to leave.

The world rejoices that winter has gone.
The blue sky flutters an azalea kerchief, calling the skylarks.
The high mountains, overcome with joy, let the tears frozen during winter flow emotionally down every valley.
The impatient forsythia, still naked, laughs a yellow laugh.
Flies buzz beneath the foundation stones, and puppies jump excitedly at anyone they see.
The world laughs. It sings.
It even sheds tears in the aftermath of chasing away the dreadful winter.
Now, Teacher, you won't be cold anymore, right?
At any rate, it's spring, so you must be enveloped in the spring air.
Health is paramount. It's a victory. Your noble heart, Teacher, which did not yield to winter, must be mingling with the hearts of skylarks, orioles, and all creation singing of spring. Even more surely, you must be rejoicing and possessing a brighter confidence.

Teacher!
But it's already been over a year since you left.
We have clearly known how you've been doing from the letters you sent.
Even more so, we vividly picture it in our minds through our own anticipation.
During that time, we too have changed quite a lot.
We've scattered hither and thither, changing into all sorts of forms. <369>
Our appearance, once of white *chŏgori* blouses and black skirts, has changed into white aprons (하얀에프롱), into green tops and red skirts (녹의홍장 - nokŭihongjang), and into long navy skirt-hems.
The long black hair, braided down in strands, has changed into round chignons, long *t'ŭre* braids, and all sorts of hairstyles.
Some girls now draw their eyebrows like crescent moons, create pink lips, and are slicked down with hair oil.
In our neighborhood, which you always spoke of, Teacher, houses even more like "cotton-fluffing" frames (솜틀), even smaller houses, keep increasing.
Even during this time, we wanted several times to send you detailed news of the events that have occurred within this "Cotton-Fluffing Street" of ours, especially the stories of us night school students whom you taught. However, we hesitated again and again, afraid it might disturb your heart.
But in your last letter, you said,
"Any news is good. Whether sad or happy, as long as it's a true story, it's good.
More than anything here, I want to hear true stories and real news that feel like seeing them with my own eyes.
I miss the mundane world (사파 - sap'a)."
-Because you said this, we intend to send this long piece of news.
However, we still won't write at length. Out of a hundred, a thousand things, we intend to pick out only the major stories, and out of all the twists and turns, only a few of the most poignant passages.

Firstly, after you left, Teacher, our night school just fizzled out and dispersed.
At first, the principal taught us for a few days in your place, Teacher. The principal said,
"The teacher went down to the countryside for a brief matter."
"And he went to do some business, and won't be back easily within a year or two."
"This night school was something that teacher put effort into, but now a different, better teacher will come."
We* cried.
"Why did he leave without even a single word when he left?" <370>
"Why on earth did he abandon us and leave?"
"What kind of business did he leave for so suddenly?"
We felt resentment, curiosity, ** (missing word/illegible section), and blame.
Among us, Yŏngae, even while crying, showed her petulance.
"How could he just leave without a single word of farewell, how could he?"
This phrase, "How could he," went up and down from our lips.
Still, everyone cried. Even Hyesuk, who always sat at the very front, cried, so what more needs to be said? Turi and Chonsuk with their *t'ŭre* braids didn't show any sign in front of us, but it seemed they were upset amongst themselves.
In less than three days, the number of students halved.
The older girls like Ammyŏng, Oksun, Sodam, and Yŏngsuk left completely.
And for the sake of earning more money, they became *omani* (オマニ - maids/servants) in the houses on Chin'gogae where they sleep over.
Sugyŏp and Pogi went to the confectionery factory; Nanyŏng, Poksul, and Mudŏn went to the soap factory!
They all went off to become factory girls earning 15 or 20 *chŏn* a day.
Sunnyŏ and Ch'aesun, who worked at the Monopoly Bureau, also stopped coming.
Gradually, everyone left, and fewer than 10 remained.
So the principal said,
"I'm sorry, but I can't teach you. As you know, the expenses for our night school are barely maintained by small amounts from your monthly fees. With so few of you, it doesn't even cover the electricity bill.
The teacher's salary is out of the question, of course. Since this is a kind of project done for you who have jobs and can't study, the teacher receives no salary.
But even if the teacher* gets no pay, shouldn't it at least cover the electricity bill?"
This was the principal's statement on the last day when the night school was closed.
From beginning to end, it was only about not even covering the "electricity bill."
Teacher!
At that time, we thought of you even more.
The face of you, Teacher, who would only look at our expressions and quietly stamp the seal (도장) on the monthly fee envelopes of poor students <371> flashed vividly before our eyes.
Thus, we all ended up scattering.
For us, this is the very end of our studies.
At home, they say, "It's enough that you girls learned that much," and won't send us anywhere else.
And besides, there are no schools asking us to come and learn.
Even if there were, we couldn't go.
Now that we are girls approaching 20, how could we embarrassingly go to a place gathered with only small children?
Why? Are there so few night schools in Sŏul specifically for older, uneducated girls like us, girls who work in factories, girls who work as *omani*?
For some time now, I've just been staying put at home.
"You're over 20 now too, so stay home and learn needlework."
As they said, I am confined.
However, Suni, Yŏngsuk, and Pongnam, who are older than me, are still working at the rubber factory.

This is a story from last spring.
By then, it had been over half a year since the night school closed.
I was at home, truly learning needlework, and when I got too frustrated, I would review the textbooks I had learned from.
Still, when you were here, Teacher, I could borrow interesting books to read, but where could I find even such books now?
Also, I have no money, but even if I feel like buying one, how would I know where to go and what kind of book would be good to buy and read?
At that time, the two books you lent me, Teacher, which remained after you just left – I just reread those.
Do you know what books they were? Mr. Myuren's (뮤렌) fairy tale book and what was the other one? Oh right, the story of "Chantak'ŭ" (쨘닥크 - Joan of Arc), those two books.
Oh, by the way.
At that time, Nani, Yŏngsuk, Kyŏngnan, and I, the four of us, sometimes wrote letters and visited each other.
At that time, Yŏngsuk was still an *omani* at the Chin'gogae house, Kyŏngnan worked at the rubber factory, and Nani was working at the silk reeling factory.
Right, Nani. When you were here, Teacher, she worked at the soap factory, but by then, Turi had introduced her, and she got dragged into the silk reeling factory as an apprentice worker. <372>
At that time too, a letter came from Yŏngsuk.
"It's been so long, come visit on the holiday. Nani, Kyŏngnan, and I will be waiting at Kyŏngnan's house, so please be sure to come by noon.
The weather is completely warm now.
The azaleas are in full bloom in front of the Kyŏngbokchae spring.
On the green field on the hill behind the school, the grass covers it lushly green.
Let's spend a day reminiscing on the green hill where we used to go up and play together with you, Teacher, when the moon rose, looking down at least at the school where we learned.
In our neighborhood, the houses like 'cotton-fluffing' frames you spoke of, Teacher, have increased even more.
And many more strange stories have arisen."
-A letter with this meaning arrived.
I was so glad that I forced my mother to agree and went to Kyŏngnan's "cotton-fluffing like house," the shabbiest even within the "Cotton-Fluffing Street," before noon on the promised holiday.

As I entered the edge of the neighborhood, thoughts of the school suddenly arose.
The lush green pines and cypresses of the rear Namsan mountain were submerged in a hazy mist.
Green two-story houses, red two-story houses stood tall here and there, even more numerous.
From one house, the sound of a reed organ flowed out.
Previously, only the bank employees' residences were here, but now it seemed many wealthy mainlanders (내지인 - Naichijin/Japanese), like company employees and school teachers, had come to live here.
Instead, the cotton-fluffing houses were all squeezed into the mountain valleys and down into the ditch below the main road.
Teacher!
On the mountaintop of Hyŏnjŏ-dong outside the New Gate (새문밖) and on the hill of Kongdŏng-ni too, houses like cotton-fluffing frames are packed tight, right?
But the cotton-fluffing houses of this neighborhood at the foot of the rear Namsan mountain just creep further down into the ditch, into the valley.
Even within this valley neighborhood, the shabbiest area was this "Archery Range Hole" (활터구녕 - Hwalt'ŏgunyŏng) where Kyŏngnan's house was.
During the monsoon rains last summer, some houses collapsed because so much water poured down. <373>
The cotton-fluffing houses, built precariously with tin roofs and plank scraps on small plots dug out from the sides of the mountain valley, truly look flimsy enough to fly away if the wind just blew.
Because the lower part of the walls crumbled due to the water washing down, the "cotton fluffs" built on top lost a third of their foundation, with only two-thirds barely hanging on, not falling.
To prevent total collapse, long rafters propped up one side of the cotton-fluffing houses lacking foundation ground.
Just looking at it was a precarious sight.
Yet even from these terrifying, seemingly collapsing houses, the sound of children crying flowed out, the sound of laundry bats flowed out. Smoke came from chimneys, and the shrill voice of a scolding mother was also submerged within.
Teacher!
At the corner entering this dreadful neighborhood, a small firewood shop has newly appeared.
This shop belonged to Sunnyŏ's family, who used to work at the Monopoly Bureau.
Why, Sunnyŏ, don't you remember her?
The girl who always had a pale face due to tuberculosis, coughed constantly, and looked frail.
The tall, slender girl with drowsy eyes.
She was already 22 years old then, wasn't she? She's one year older than me, so isn't that right?
Indeed, it seems many women who worked at the Monopoly Bureau had such illnesses.
Why, wasn't Ch'aesun like that too? The woman who got married, came back, re-braided her hair, and went back to work.
Sunnyŏ's illness, contracted by chance, gradually deepened, and by then it had become almost chronic.
When I passed by this shop of Sunnyŏ's, I initially found it very strange.
Sunnyŏ's father did nothing, and furthermore, wasn't the family struggling, barely getting by?
Weren't they just surviving on the money earned by the tuberculosis-ridden Sunnyŏ?
How did this poor Sunnyŏ family suddenly get money to even open a shop? I wondered inwardly.
Perhaps Sunnyŏ's family moved away and someone else moved in, I thought, so as I passed by, I looked inside the shop, and there sat Sunnyŏ's father with his slender face, holding a small pipe. <374>

When I went into Kyŏngnan's house,
the three friends who had promised were already there.
"Hey, it's been such a long time!"
"Welcome, older sister. You've become quite the young lady now."
Everyone was happy, laughing and holding hands.
"So, Yŏngsuk. Are you still at the Kimura house?"
"No, I moved somewhere else. Now I live there permanently. The owner absolutely won't let me budge. I rarely get to come home, maybe once or twice a month at best. The family is small, so the work is relatively light, but being cooped up is the worry."
Yŏngsuk had newly put her hair up in a bun.
Her height hadn't increased, but she had become quite plump, and the nape of her neck was thick.
Looking at her white hands, I guessed that the work she did wasn't comparatively harsh.
And the way she spoke Korean like a Japanese mainlander, laughed, and carried herself somehow seemed steeped in the scent of an *omani*.
"Nani, have your wages gone up a bit lately?"
"Gone up? They've gone up ridiculously. It's barely 25 *chŏn* a day now. It's harder than the soap factory. And seeing the supervisors and managers makes me sick. Damn it all!"
Nani, with her broad face and fiery temper, cursed as if the supervisor was right in front of her.
"But really, Kyŏngnan *ŏnni*, your wages must be quite high now."
Kyŏngnan was two years older than me, yet she looked even more youthful than I did.
Her face was white, and her hands were like candle wax.
"Me? What high wages? Even that, if there's enough work, I get over one *wŏn* a day, but nowadays, where's the work? I'm idle for half the month—"
she said with a slight smile. But she looked very lonely.
For a long while, we talked about what we had been through. We talked about seeing your picture in the newspaper, Teacher, and thought about the hardships you must be facing, feeling quite desolate.
"Are all the other girls doing well?"
I asked.
They heard that most were still working at the mainlander houses (내지인 집)! Factories! <375>
making a living, but among them, the number of girls attending *kwŏnbŏn* (권반 - kisaeng training houses) to learn singing had increased to 4 or 5.
Really, Teacher.
We heard that Suni, who sang solos well, had become a cafe girl (카페껄).
We also heard that Chŏmbun and Punsun had completely finished learning songs and had already started working as *kisaeng* by then.
We talked about the changed stories of all our friends and laughed desolately.
Our chests felt tight, unbearable.
We felt pointlessly resentful and vaguely afraid.

Teacher!
We heard an even more- resentful and frightening story. It was the story that Sunnyŏ had been sold to Manchuria (만주) for 500 *wŏn*.
Teacher!
Sunnyŏ, who was frail with tuberculosis, was sold as a prostitute to Manchuria.
And so, her father started the firewood shop with that money.
Teacher!
How can there be a father who sells his own daughter?
No matter how difficult things are, how can the thought arise of selling the daughter one personally gave birth to, especially a sick girl who might die today or tomorrow?
Everyone in the neighborhood curses him.
"He's not human, he's a dog or a pig!"
"How could he sell his daughter to a pimp (호인놈)?"
"Will a shop started like that ever prosper?"
"He's not selling firewood; it's the same as chopping up his daughter and selling her piece by piece."
Such curses fill this Cotton-Fluffing Street.
Not a single person goes to that shop to buy firewood.
So, he's already eaten through the initial capital, and now there's nothing left but 'ruin'.
Nani, in her grumbling voice, cursed Sunnyŏ's father.
Our long-awaited reunion turned into this kind of tale-telling.
Although the day was a very clear spring day,
our hearts were extremely clouded. <376>

Only when evening approached did we go up to the green hill in front of Kyŏngbokchae.
The rows of Western-style houses glittered in the spring light.
The Cotton-Fluffing Street, crammed into this valley and that, looked like nothing more than a garbage dump.
Teacher!
From within this garbage dump, we stood looking down at our night school building, a slightly larger tin house.
Just then, the sound of middle school students singing drifted up.
Teacher!
Hearing the singing, we felt like tears would fall. Because we thought of you, Teacher.
The melody was a National Language song (국어창가 - kugo ch'angga), the kind heard only in elementary schools.
When you were here, Teacher, you only taught us Korean children's songs, didn't you?
But these days, they only sing National Language songs.
And apparently, the teachers and students only use the National Language too.
"Clean the latrine well. Bring your monthly fees quickly."
-Even the principal, who specialized in making these points his sermon during morning assembly, apparently gives his sermons in the National Language.
Listening to this National Language song, we just stood there blankly for a long time, silent with each other.
After a short while, Kyŏngsuk quietly sang.
"Barefoot friends gathering from the underground village (지하촌)
Gather- gather together and study hard"
-It was the night school song.
We automatically followed along and sang. The sound of laundry bats came from Kyŏngbokchae.
Yŏngsuk said, "Hey kids, look at that!"
pointing to the narrow path below the hill.
We all looked down.
There, Chŏmbun and Punsun, wearing yellow *chŏgori* and navy skirts, were walking side by side.
Their gait already showed the mark of *kisaeng*. On the Yŏnhwabong peak across the way, the red evening sun hung halfway down.
The faces of Yŏngsuk, Kyŏngnan, and Nani were submerged in the red glow of the sunset.

Teacher!
Already, this story has become an old tale from a year ago. <377>
Now it is spring again, but the Cotton-Fluffing Street is still just desolate.
We meet very rarely now.
It's been over two months since Kyŏngnan's letter came.
News of Sunnyŏ has completely vanished. The other girls are similarly making a living.
From inside the school, the sound of singing still flows out.
I, too, am still just shut up inside the house. But really, this is a story that happened a few days ago.
I forgot because it was too long-winded. Poktong's mother, nicknamed the "tough wife" (딱정떼 여편네) in the neighborhood, was taken to the police station and then released.
Why, the tall woman who worked at the Monopoly Bureau with Sunnyŏ.
Apparently, she fought with a supervisor during lunch break, caused trouble for several days, and ended up visiting the police station because of it.
The neighborhood people curse her.
"A young woman fought with a male supervisor and ended up indebted to the police station."
Of course, she was kicked out of the Monopoly Bureau too. Still, Poktong's mother acts even more defiant, turning the neighborhood upside down.
She came by my house once too.
She talked about all sorts of things in that loud voice and left.
There was much in what Poktong's mother, with her hair in a chignon, said that sounded similar to what you used to say, Teacher.
Teacher!
Now, no matter how resentful one feels, it seems one shouldn't make a fuss at the factory.
You just end up like Poktong's mother, visiting the police station and getting your lifeline cut.

Teacher!
Well then, I intend to end this news here.
They say you'll be able to come back in just three more months, Teacher.
If you come back, will you become our Teacher again, and will we become students again? In any case, please stay healthy and well until you come out. Until then, we too will try to greet you with very good faces.
Well then, please stay well.
Kim Paeksun <378>

**Summary**

This fictional letter, written by Kim Paeksun to her former night school teacher imprisoned or otherwise absent, details the hardships faced by young women in a poor neighborhood ("Cotton-Fluffing Street") in colonial Seoul around 1935-1936. After the teacher's departure, the night school closes due to lack of funds, ending the girls' education. Paeksun describes how her former classmates have scattered into low-paying factory work, domestic service (*omani*), or even been forced into prostitution (like Sunnyŏ, sold to Manchuria by her father). The letter paints a bleak picture of poverty, limited opportunities, the changing social landscape under Japanese rule (mentioning Japanese employers, the shift to Japanese language in schools), and the resilience and despair of these young women. For historians, the story offers a poignant glimpse into the lives of marginalized urban working-class women during the colonial period, highlighting economic desperation, the social impact of Japanese assimilation policies (language shift), changing labor patterns (factories, domestic service, *kisaeng* houses), and the moral compromises forced by poverty, such as a father selling his daughter. The mention of *omani* and specific locations like Chin'gogae provides concrete social details.

「솜틀거리」에서 나온 消息
						宋影
선생님!
그 동안에도 역시 몸이나 건강하시엿슴니가?
갓득이나 몸이 약하신 선생님께서 엇떠케나 그 치운 겨울을 지내가시엿슴니가.
더욱히 지난 겨울에는 근년에는 보기듬은 치위엿슴니다.
집의 할아버지께옵서는
『그런 치위는 처음 보왓다고』 하시면서 아츰이면 구레나릇에 달닌 고드름을 쓰다듬고 게시엿담니다.
땃뜻한 아루ㅅ목에 가 화로를 쪼이고 지낸 저이들도 한번도 몸을 펴지를 못하고 옹송거리고 지내엿담니다.
선생님!
선생님 게신 방에는 화로불도 없다고 하시엿지요.
천정에 달닌 거믜줄이 흔들닌다고 하시엿지요.
앞벽에 달닌 『칼렌다』와 뒷벽에 걸닌 수*도 흔들닌다고 하시엿지요.<368>
통풍장치(通風裝置)로 뚜러논 벽에 뚤닌 구녕에서는 칼날같은 바람이 쏘다저 들어온다고 하시엿지요.
코끝과 귀끝과 무릅팍과 발구락들은 띄여나가는 듯키 시리다고 하시엿지요.
아리고 매웁다 못해서 앞으다고 하시엿지요.
선생님!
그렇나 이 몹슬 눔의 겨울 인정업는 겨울 눈도 없고 코도 없는 겨울은 그만 가버리고 말엇슴니다.
마음대로 선생님을 시달니고 호채리질 하든 겨울!
선생님의 몸을 알알삿삿히 괴롭게 굴든 겨울! 선생님의 마음을 울니고 웃기고 조롱하고 모욕하든 겨울.
요놈의 겨울도 가버리는 때가 잇기는 함니다 그려.

겨울이 갓다고 천하는 좋와합니다.
푸른 한울은 아즈랑이 수건을 나붓거리면서 종달새를 불느고 있음니다.
높은 산은 깃븜에 못이겨서 겨울 동안에 얼어 부텃든 눈물을 골작이골작이로 감격하게 흘니고 있음니다.
성미급한 개나리는 벌거버슨 몸으로 노란 웃음을 웃고 있습니다.
파리는 주추돌 아래에서 앵앵거리고 강아지는 아모나 보고도 깡충깡충 뛰여 올늠니다.
천하는 웃읍니다. 노래함니다.
지긋지긋하든 겨울을 쫓아버린 남어지에 눈물까지 냄니다.
인제는 선생님도 치웁지만은 않으실 터이지요.
하여간 봄이라 봄기운에 싸혀만은 게시겠지요.
무엇보다도 건강입니다. 승리입니다. 겨울에게 지시지 않으신 거룩한 선생님의 마음은 종달새와 꾀꼬리와 왼 봄을 노래하는 만물의 마음과 같이 어울녀지시여서 게시겟지요. 그보다도 더더 확실하게 깃버하시고 더 명낭한 자신을 가지고 게시겟지요.



선생님!
그런데 벌서 선생님이 가신 지가 1년이 넘엇슴니다 그려.
그동안 선생님의 지내시는 양은 선생님 주신 편지에서 역역히 알고 지냇슴니다.
그보다도 저이들이 미리 짐작으로 더 똑똑히 눈 앞에 그리고 지내가고 잇담니다.
그동안에 저이들도 퍽 많이 변해버렷담니다.
서로들 뿔뿔히 헤여저서 가진각색의 모양들로 변했담니다. <369>
흰 조고리를 입고 검정치마를 입고 지내엿든 저이들의 모양은 『하얀에프롱』으로도 변하고 녹의홍장으로도 변하고 길다란 남치마짜락으로도 변해버렷담니다.
쭈렁쭈렁 따서 느린 검은 긴머리는 똥그란 쪽지머리, 길죽한 트레머리, 별의별 머리로 다 변해버렷담니다.
눈섭을 의쪽달 같이 그리고 분홍입술을 만들고 머리기름으로 범벅을 하고 있는 아이도 생겻담니다.
선생님이 항상 말슴하시든 저이들 동리는 아조 더 「솜틀」같은 집이 그 보다도 더 적은 집이 작구만 늘어가고 있담니다.
그 동안에라도 벌서부터 몇번이나 이 저의들의 『솜틀거리』 속에는 일어난 이약이를 더욱히 선생님께서 가르켜 주시든 저이들 야학생들의 이약이를 자서히 전해 보내 듸리려고 햇슴니다마는 돌오혀 선생님의 마음을 휘정거려 듸릴가바 겁이 나서 주저를 하고하고 하엿담니다.
그렇나 선생님게서 저번 편지에
『소식이면 아모 것도 좋다. 슲은 것이나 깃븐 것이나 정말 이약이면은 좋다.
이 곧에서는 무엇보다도 눈으로 보는 듯한 정말 이약이와 참된 소식이 더 듯고 싶다.
사파가 그립다』
-하시기 때문에 이 길다란 소식을 전해 듸리려고 하는 것이람니다.
그렇나 역시 길게는 쓰지를 않읍니다. 백가지 천가지 중에서도 그 중의 큰 이약이만을 그리고 별별 곡절 중에서도 그 중 긔막힌 구절만을 몇 가지만을 추려 놓으려고 함니다.



첫재로 선생님이 떠나가신 뒤로는 저이들의 야학이 그대로 흐지부지 헤여저 버리고 말엇담니다.
처음에는 선생님 대신으로 교장 선생님께서 몇일 동안은 가르키여 주시엿담니다. 교장 선생님께서는
『선생님께서 잠간 볼 일이 게시여서 싀골로 내려가섯다고』
『그리고 무슨 장사를 하시러 가시엿는데 한해 두해 안으로 그리 쉽사리 돌아오시지 않으시리라고』
『이 야학은 그 선생이 힘을 쓰든 것인데 인제는 앞으로 다른 더 좋은 선생님이 오시리라고』
저이*은 눈물들이 낫담니다.
『왜, 가실 때 단 한마듸 말슴이나마 않이하시* 가셋슬가?』<370>
『대관절 왜 우리들을 버리시고 가셋슬가』
『무슨 장사를 그렇게 별안간 가시엿슬까』
야속도 하고 궁굼도 하고 **도 하고 원망도 스러웟담니다.
그 중에도 영애는 울면서도 암살을 피엿담니다.
『엇저면 인사 한마듸도 않이하시고 가시니 엇저면 글세
이 『엇저면 글세』 소리가 저이들의 입에서 올으내렷담니다.
그래도 울기들은 모다 햇슴니다. 맨 앞에 앉엇든 헤숙이까지 울엇스니 말할 게 잇서야지요. 트레머리한 『두리』와 『존숙』이도 우리들 앞에서는 아모런 기색을 않이 보이지만 자기들끼리는 언짠어 하든 모양이드군요.
단 사흘이 못되여서 아이들은 반이나 줄엇슴니다.
아명이 옥순이 소담이 영숙이들 같은 커다란 처녀들은 아주 나가버렷슴니다.
그리고 돈, 더 밧는 맛에 밤에 자고 있는 진고개집들 オマニ들이 되엿담니다.
숙엽이 복이들은 과자 공장으로 란영이 복술이 무던이는 비누 공장으로!
모두들 하루 15전 20전짜리 일공녀로 들어들 가버리엿습니다.
전매국 단이는 순녀와 채순이도 안단김니다.
그럭저럭 다 나가고 단 10명도 않남엇지요.
그래서 교장 선생님께서
『미안하지만 너이들은 가르켜 줄 수가 없다. 너이들도 아다싶이 우리 야학에 경비는 너이들의 월사금으로 조금식 유지해 나가는데, 너이들이 이렇게 적으니 전기불 값도 못되지 않니.
선생님 월급은 물논이다. 다만 너이들 직업 가지고 공부 못하는 너이들을 위하야 일종 사업으로 하는 노릇이니까 선생님의 월급은 없다.
그렇나 아모리 선생님* 보수가 없드래도 전기불 값이라도 되여야 하지 않니』
이것이 마즈막 야학을 파하든 날에 하시든 교장 선생님의 말슴이엿슴니다.
처음부터 끝까지가 『전기불 값』도 못된다는 말슴뿐이엿슴니다.
선생님!
저이는 그때에 저이들은 선생님 생각이 더 한층 낫슴니다.
저이들의 눈치만 보시면서 가난한 학생들의 월사금 봉투에다<371> 슬그머니 『도장』을 찍어 주시든 선생님의 얼골이 확확하고 눈 앞에 지첫담니다.
이렇게 저이들은 모두들 헤여저 버리고 말엇지요.
저이들은 이것이 아주 마즈막 공부입니다.
집에서들은 게집애년들이 그만큼 배홧스면 넉넉하지-하시고들 다른 데에는 다시 보내 주시지 않으심니다.

순녀의 소식은 아조 묘연해젓슴니다. 다른 아이들도 맛찬가지로 버리들을 단이고 있음니다.
학교 안에서도 여전히 창가 소리가 흘너 나옴니다.
저도 역시 집안에만 들어 앉엇슴니다. 그런데 참, 이것은 몇칠 전에 일어난 이약이임니다.
넘우 장황해서 니저버렷슴니다그려. 동리 안에서 딱정떼 여편네라고 별명 듯든 복동어머니가 경찰서에 붓들녀 갓다가 나왓담니다.
왜, 순녀와 같이 전매국에 단이는 키 큰 여자말임니다.
점심시간에 감독하고 싸홈을 하고 몇일동안 말성을 일으키드니 그옇고 경찰서까지 단겨나왓다나요.
동리 사람들은 욕들을 한담니다.
『젊은 게집년이 사내 감독하고 싸호다가 경찰서 신세까지 끼첫다고요』

언제인가 저의 집에도 한 번 놀라왓다가 갓지오.
그 큰 목소리로 별별 이약이를 다하고 갓슴니다.
