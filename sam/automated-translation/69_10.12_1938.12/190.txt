**Body Text Translation:**
Film Art Special Feature, The Grand Illusion
Original Story & Screenplay: Jean Renoir, Charles Spaak
Director: Jean Renoir
Cinematography: Christian Matras
Starring: Jean Gabin

This film is the latest work by France's top-tier director Jean Renoir, hailed as the greatest masterpiece since the beginning of cinematic art.
Selected as France's representative work at the Venice International Film Festival held in 1937, it received the highest award for artistic excellence.
The original story is a collaborative original by Jean Renoir and Charles Spaak, but Renoir collected the experiences of former soldiers who participated in the Great War and became prisoners of war to write this work. The location was chosen in the Alsace region, and he reportedly tried his best to represent the atmosphere of that time.

It was during the height of the World War—1916. One day, at the <157> French bombing squadron on the front line, the soldiers were having boisterous fun during their short leisure time. Their fate was uncertain—not knowing what might happen tonight or tomorrow—so when they played, they played hard and laughed hard.
— "Josephine! You really don't seem like a single woman."
— "Hey!"
Just then, Staff Officer Captain de Boëldieu arrived. At that moment, it was necessary to go out to the enemy camp for reconnaissance, so Flight Lieutenant Maréchal took de Boëldieu aboard his plane and departed for the reconnaissance mission.
De Boëldieu was of aristocratic origin, while Maréchal was an engineer, essentially a suddenly commissioned officer. Although Maréchal grew up in a completely different environment from de Boëldieu and received a different kind of schooling, once the war made them identical soldiers, they did not seek class differences or distinctions of noble or commoner.
They both shared the single mind of living and dying for their country. While fearlessly flying the small plane, the engine was hit by enemy bullets, and they had no choice but to make an emergency landing in enemy territory. They were captured by the German air squadron, but the squadron leader here, Major von Rauffenstein, a Count, treated them with knightly goodwill, not as prisoners, but rather as unfortunate heroes, even inviting them to his dinner table. However, it seems they couldn't stay like that forever. Soon, the two were sent to an enemy prisoner-of-war camp. Here, there were not only soldiers but also former actors and former school teachers, and although it was like a *** cave (***굴속), they could find warmth there. Sometimes they danced, and sometimes they were not idle in digging the ground and piercing the walls with the thought of escape. The excavated soil was stealthily discarded during walks. It was just like the Count of Monte Cristo in his prison (嚴窟).
One day, suddenly, an alarm bell rang within their camp. This sound announced that the Verdun fortress, Fort Douaumont (銷鑰 도워몬), had been recaptured by French soldiers. Maréchal clenched his fists, jumped up and down, then danced wildly, leaped onto a high platform, and shouted loudly.
— "The French army has taken Douaumont!" —
Everyone became tense. A moment later, <158> they all raised their voices together and sang a song. It was entirely from a blood-boiling heart for their homeland.

— Arise, children of the Fatherland! The day of glory has arrived. Against us, tyranny's bloody standard is raised (우리들의 피뿌린깃발은 들녀워젔다 - *literal translation: our blood-sprinkled flag is raised*).

A few days later, Maréchal was punished for shouting and singing and had to stay alone in a prison even more gloomy than the camp. The only comfort for Maréchal was the sound of a harmonica secretly brought in by a guard. In the camp, even after Maréchal was gone, they continued digging the hole for escape, but things didn't go as planned, and just as Maréchal returned to the camp, they had to move to another camp, leaving the hole they had dug. They stood in line in a wide square. There stood British prisoners who shared the same fate.
It seemed they were to enter the camp the British were leaving, exchanging places with them.
Maréchal approached one of the British officers and spoke in a low voice.
"Look here. We've dug a tunnel there, just so you know."
However, the British officers, who *knew* (으는 - *likely typo for 아는*) French, didn't understand what was going on and remained silent, but perhaps realizing it was a gesture of goodwill, they patted Maréchal's shoulder, smiled, and seemed very pleased, but then a guard came and they had to part like that.

They would run away, get caught, and once caught, plan to run away again. They continued this several times, but all attempts failed, and eventually, Maréchal and Boëldieu were confined together in a tower within an old castle. One surprising thing here was that the commander of this military prison was the same Major von Rauffenstein from before. The Major had been wounded afterwards, could not go to war, and inevitably ended up working here. Here, they could no longer enjoy ope*** (*illegible*) or dig the ground... *(following 3 lines illegible)*... Above, a pack of fierce dogs guarded them.

The two gained another companion. <159> He was *** *** (*illegible*), and his father *was a major Jewish banker* (*likely meaning of* *태大은** 있읍니다 - *partially illegible*). Various care packages arrived daily from his wealthy home.
They shared these things fairly and got along well.
However, they hadn't completely forgotten their escape plan.

Now, lukewarm methods would absolutely not work. Boëldieu finally decided to sacrifice himself to let his comrades escape. As Maréchal and Boëldieu spent more time together, their minds increasingly clashed, and they didn't get along so well, but Maréchal didn't want to escape leaving only his comrade Boëldieu behind. Boëldieu spoke again.
"I'm not helping you escape for your sake, but for France."

One night, the long-awaited escape preparations were complete. As planned beforehand, they made noise from each cell, banging on walls, shouting, doing all sorts of things to cause a disturbance. The guards were preoccupied with stopping this. Seizing this opportunity, Boëldieu ran up to the rooftop and whistled loudly. Following that, a commotion like a shamanistic ritual (굿) erupted even more fiercely. No matter how much they were told to stop or threatened, they wouldn't listen. The Major, who had shown them special favor, now had no choice but to draw his pistol and aim. Bang! The fired bullet struck Boëldieu squarely in the abdomen. Unable to bear the pain, he collapsed on the spot. The Major rushed over in alarm.
"I was aiming for your leg, but it turned out like this. Please know it was due to my clumsy skill."
"No, I was wrong. But I had to fulfill my duty."
This Great War was, so to speak, an elegy for the aristocracy, where nobles and commoners all became equal.
The prospect of a new century unfolded widely before their eyes, and the worn-out foundation was about to crumble right then. For those two men of noble birth, enemy and enemy, this became endlessly desolate. Finally, Boëldieu fell asleep forever. With trembling hands, the Major plucked a beautiful flower from a carefully tended pot and placed it on the unmoving body that would never awaken.
Taking advantage of this chaotic situation, Maréchal and <160> Rosenthal escaped down a thick rope they had prepared beforehand, fought off the rushing dogs, finally ran away, and reached a nameless wide field.
It was winter. White snow lay piled high here and there in the shaded places.

Wandering the fields for several days, battling hunger and cold. But nothing appeared to save them. Facing death like this, although they used to joke, laugh, and chat before, the mood of the two men grew increasingly gloomy with each passing day.
"I can't go with the likes of you anymore. I'm going ahead."
"Who told you not to go first? I'm sick and tired of even looking at your ** (*illegible*)."
"If we're lucky, let's meet again."
"I'd rather die than meet the likes of you again."
The two split off in opposite directions, taking one step, then two, but what should they do next? Somehow, turning back, holding hands, and walking together seemed better, so the two took hands again and walked on.

Far away, something twinkled like the morning star. It seemed to be a lamp. For those suffering from hunger and cold, how joyful must that light have been, whatever it was? The two summoned their last remaining strength and went towards the twinkling light. When they arrived, it was a tiny farmhouse. In that house lived a young widow, whose husband had died in the war and whose brothers were all away at war, living alone with her young daughter. Seeing them, the woman initially looked quite startled, but she didn't report them to the police; instead, she nursed and treated them sincerely and hid them well. People who live with the soil possess much human feeling even amidst war. Although humble, that house had a fire in the hearth, warm soup, and quiet peace.
During this time, a joyful Christmas came to this poor house. A Christmas tree decorated with candles, bells, pinecones, and cotton, and the face of the child happy to receive presents—here alone, the bitterness of war hid its shadow. Under the dim lamplight, Maréchal and the woman, facing each other, came to feel love for one another without realizing it. However, the soldiers <161>
(Continued on page 161)
could not stay here forever.
"If the war ends and we survive to meet again, I will definitely come back here. Then let's go to France together."
Saddened by this farewell, which might be their last, the woman kept crying. Consoling her, Maréchal shed tears, lifted the child, kissed her, and said,
"Stay well. Okay?"
The woman, forcing back the tears that welled up like a spring, watched their retreating figures for a long time, then waved her hand and cried out emotionally,
"Please come back safely!"
Hiding in dark forests during the bright day and walking at night, the two hurried their steps towards the Swiss border, which the beloved country woman had shown them. The shadows of the two men gradually shrink on the field covered in white snow.
"Fugitives!" A German border guard raised his rifle, but suddenly one of them stopped him.
"Hold fire! They're in Swiss territory."
Ah! — How long will this war continue? Will the two men truly be able to step on the soil of their beloved homeland? <167>

**Summary**

This 1938 article from the magazine *Samch'ŏlli* provides a detailed plot summary of Jean Renoir's 1937 anti-war film, *La Grande Illusion*. It recounts the capture of two French officers, Maréchal (working class) and de Boëldieu (aristocrat), their experiences in various German POW camps alongside other prisoners like Rosenthal (Jewish), their repeated escape attempts, the class dynamics highlighted by their interactions with the German commandant von Rauffenstein (also an aristocrat), Boëldieu's sacrifice enabling Maréchal and Rosenthal's escape, their arduous journey, the temporary refuge found with a German war widow, and their eventual crossing into Switzerland. The article emphasizes the film's critical acclaim, its anti-war message, and the theme of dissolving class boundaries amidst conflict. For historians, this article is interesting as it shows the reception and detailed discussion of a major European art film in colonial Korea during a period of increasing militarism and Japanese control. The focus on themes of camaraderie across class lines, escape, and the human cost of war, even within a European context, might reflect subtle commentary or simply a channel for cosmopolitan cultural consumption available to Korean intellectuals at the time.
