Short Pieces (小品)

Narcissus (水仙)
For the past month, due to company work, various meetings, or because the nights are long, chatting with friends at teahouses, I often came out late.
The baby and my wife were usually asleep. If there was wind, the wind chime would just go "deng-geureong," and when I entered the room, a single narcissus placed on the cabinet would greet me with a bright smile, as if lifting its heavy head.

Narcissus.
"Where is your hometown?"
Lying in bed last night, I suddenly whispered this to it. It seemed to answer me clearly, word by word:
"My hometown is far away. It's not cold like this. It's a place where the sky is like jade, warm sunlight lingers like breath, and the water, looking up at us like a mirror, flows murmuringly. There are butterflies too. It's a place where bees (부얼) also fly in."
Its voice seemed to pitifully, pitifully touch my heart.
"Then do you want to go back to your hometown even now?"
"Yes, yes, I really didn't know I would bloom in a room like this, so stuffy, with no birdsong, and no clear blue sky."
"Do you miss the sky?"
"Yes, the warm sky."
"Do you miss the sound of birds?"
"Yes, and the sound of water, the sound of bees..."
"Then why did you bloom in a room like this?"
"That's my destiny. It's my sad destiny to have to bloom anywhere as long as the water and temperature are right.
That's why I used to cry alone every evening."<256>

I too was sad.
I am someone who has spared no love and devotion for it. I had believed it would be satisfied with my love alone.
Love can be cruel too; lying with the lights off, I realized this.
But what can I do? Buying and growing it in winter is a pleasure I have coveted above all else. That is my indispensable happiness.
It is a sad thing.

Trees (樹木)
Even if the yard is only a few p'yŏng, how grateful it is to be able to stand together with the trees! Starting with the dozen or so cherry trees planted around as a fence, the persimmon tree, pine tree, jujube tree, and one or two peonies and white birches—these are cherished guests revered by all members of our household.
These beings, which give us flowers, give us fruit, and also give us green shade and their clear fragrance, receive nothing from us. Giving them a little water when it's dry, or wrapping the trunks of a few trees with straw when it's cold, is nothing compared to the beauty, the deliciousness, the fragrance and coolness they give us. Truly, it is nothing. Which friend, which rich person, would be so magnanimous (泰然) as to only give to us and receive nothing? Nature, teaching and nurturing us through trees, is great.
The trees still stand silently. Spring still seems thousands of miles away. But whenever I stand near the trees, I feel spring already. If you bend and grasp a branch of any tree, the buds (눈들) formed tightly here and there seem swollen, full, whispering that they will burst open with just one night's fine rain (細雨), one morning's warm sunlight.
Spring, come quickly.
There is likely no one more impatient for spring than a person strolling beneath winter trees. Nor is there likely anyone whose dream of spring is purer. Let me boast of the happiness of this pure dream.<257>

Whenever the cherries ripen, whenever we pick the jujubes and persimmons, our family thinks of the previous owner who sold us the yard, rather than this house. Moreover, since the persimmon tree bore its first fruit the summer after we arrived, they left having only planted the tree without seeing its fruit. There's a certain sense of apology, like harvesting in someone else's field. I often recall a short story by a certain French (佛蘭西) author titled "The Indian Hut" (인도인의 오막사리). It's a story about a scholar who travels the world (遍踏) seeking truth, but ultimately fails and, on his way back, encounters a storm and enters the hut of an Indian. The hut's owner is a Pariah (파이리아), the lowest caste (賤族) in India, whose life is completely cut off (絶綠) from civilization. However, the scholar finds a thread of truth from this Pariah that he couldn't obtain even from high-ranking monks (高層巨僧). Among their conversations, a passage with the following meaning, spoken by the Pariah, is still memorable:
— Wherever I pick and eat fruit, I always bury its seed in the soil. It's not so that I can come back and eat when the seed grows. Anyone can come and eat it. Doing so is merely following the will of Heaven (하늘의 뜻). —
How simple, yet how sacred! Whether the previous owner of our yard buried the fruit seeds in this yard wishing to follow Heaven's will (天意) like that Pariah or not, we don't know, but the fact that they left us various fruit trees—beautiful to look at, delicious, and even fun just to pick—is a kindness our family can never forget.
However, I also sometimes think differently and feel some dissatisfaction. Perhaps it's my excessive desire (過慾), but I am not satisfied that the previous owners planted many small trees. It's because I want to stand under one large tree rather than many small ones, even if I don't get to eat the fruit.
The bigger the tree, the better. And the older, the better. I am not unaware of the occasional elegant charm (雅趣) of looking at flowers blooming or fruit bending the small branches, but rather than a tree I can stroke like that, I long for a tree<258> that covers me, my house, and even the yard completely, making me, like a wide-eyed child, constantly realize my own smallness and respectfully stroll beneath its beauty—a tree soaring high like a mountain peak (뫼뿌리).

I want to live under a large tree, even just one. Rather than hesitating among low fruit trees (果木), smacking my lips, how much more noble a taste (취미) it would be to stroll under a lofty old tree, with an empty heart and empty steps, where only the pure wind (淸風) comes and goes. To live in its deep, blue shade like the sea in summer, and in autumn, let the yard and roof (집웅) be entirely covered in its fallen leaves—what a fulfilling autumn harvest (秋收) that would be! On winter nights, the sound of the wind—how resounding it would be! It would be the vibration of the largest pipe organ (最大 風琴). Even with a thread-like life (목슴), I want to rest under such a large tree, gaze at the starlight in the distant sky (하눌), and contemplate life.
(Late January, Chŏngch'uk Year [丁丑正月下澁])<259>

Travelogue (紀行)
From a Tokyo Travelogue: Gold Dust, Red Mountains, Curves (東京 紀行中에서 砂金·赭山·曲線)
The train *** at Anyang (安養). In autumn, *** *** for *** play is *** *** seen most closely. As the train slowly starts moving, in the pear orchard, the spaces between the trees pass by like spreading sunlight, lined up 쭉쭉. It's an orchard planted geometrically (幾何學的), with rows aligned vertically (縱) and horizontally (橫). Several thousand trees seem identical in height, and if you counted the branches, they'd likely all be the same. They look like students of the same grade (同學年), all standing with feet together and arms outstretched. It seems like humans are making even nature do gymnastics (體操)—no, it looks like they are being punished by being forced to stand still.
How many branches on one tree, how many flower buds on one branch, how many pears per flower bud, what's the average market price (平年時價) per pear, how much profit (實益) if a lot of cultivation cost (裁培費) is invested, ahem, the orchard owner (園主) was commanding this orchard while already 'estimating' (見積) the several thousand Yen (千圓也) autumn would bring.

Approaching Ch'ŏnan (天安), here and there, perfectly good rice paddy fields were dug up. Thinking they were digging for hot springs because it's Onyang (溫陽) territory shows how ignorant I was of worldly affairs (世情); some looked like child's play, others quite large-scale, but all those dug-up places were gold panning (砂金) sites.
"Cutting off the singing springs, digging into beautiful mountains and hills like rubbish heaps (버리둥지), people foolishly search for gold in the earth! Not knowing the gold they should seek is within their own hearts..."
Such sacred words from Tolstoy (토로-?)!
Today, we live too much only in 'indirect value' (間接價値).<260>
A mobile policeman (移動警察) came to me, who had no one to talk to. Back in my student days (學生時代), I used to confront them pointlessly, turning red in the face, which often turned out badly. This time, when asked one question, I answered with two or three preemptive words, so he quickly left as if he had business elsewhere.
The train must have traveled 300 or 400 *ri* from Seoul, but on the timeline of seasons (季節의 時間線上), it felt like more than ten days had passed. Passing Maep'o, a river appeared, completely different from the Han River (한강) in spring. Not a single piece of ice, even thin ice, remained; it was calm like oil, winding around the foot of the mountain. It must ultimately be the upper reaches of the Paengma River (白馬江).
The upper reaches of the Paengma River! Perhaps because it's spring water, the beautiful river seemed fragrant. Once, it was the source (源泉) of a nation, nurturing the Thousand Flowers of Puyŏ Castle (滋扶餘城千種花), but now it only enriches the Mangyŏng Field (萬頃田). The disappearance of Paekche must be a sorrow (恨事) not just for people.
The best time to look out from the train is when crossing a river. While trudging along (타박타박 步*) and reaching the riverbank to wait for a ferry offers its own special charm (一種 *味*), even sitting in the noisy train, the moment the steel railing (쇠欄干) ends and the river suddenly appears—what else could it be but a sacred painting (聖畵)?

Gazing absentmindedly, blue mountains are nice, but red mountains aren't bad either. Like Cézanne's (고-쟌) paintings, the earth's color is passionately red, making one sweat, yet with delicate, sharp lines as if carved by a knife—nervous lines. It's magnificent painting and sculpture that can only be seen in Korean mountains (朝鮮山).
When those red mountains once redden the Naktong River (낙동강), screams arise from human villages as if seeing blood. It's pitiful. For the great earth, great nature, isn't it necessary to have stages like this for its own excitement, rather than dedicating every nook and cranny solely for human profit? Shouldn't people allow nature such leeway and sometimes appreciate its magnanimity (豪宕)?

Sitting on the train, one feels even more strongly that Korea (朝鮮) is a land of curves. Mountains in any country are unlikely to be straight lines, but the roofs (집웅들) of the villages nestled snugly beneath them, the winding corners of walls (담모퉁이들), the paths, rice paddy ridges (논둑), and field boundaries (밭둑) that rise and fall following the terrain,<261> the rainbow-like bridges—all are beautiful curves seen from ****.
However, straight new roads (신작로) are being laid everywhere (處處). Matchbox-like tin houses (함석집) increase in every village. Our traditional culture (재래의 우리문화) was a culture of curves, but modern new culture (현대의 신문화) seems to be one of straight lines. Streamlined shapes (유선형) or tear shapes (淚線型) might be fashionable for a time, but only for fountain pens or cars; modern civilization is primarily a champion of the straight line.

Straits at 3 AM (海峽午前三時)
Stepping out of the cabin, the sea breeze hits me, smelling fishy like fresh oysters yet fragrant.
To someone who has only lived on land, the sea is completely a foreign country (異國). The black castle wall of the ferry (연락선) docked slantwise at the wharf (부두), the dazzling white corridor (廊下) above it—the faces of all the people looking up at it have wide, tense eyes, as if seeing a dragon palace suddenly risen from the water.
Those who board, those who cannot; those who taste ***, those who taste ***—here their circumstances diverge. I've never tried to board and failed, but I've never climbed the gangplank (갑판) with a light heart.

Departure (出帆) at 11 PM. Whenever I hear the sound of the gong being struck as someone walks around before departure, it reminds me of the Tsukiji Little Theater (築地小劇場). It's a much more sea-like sound than the electric bell at a train station.
A sound even better to hear than the gong is the ship's horn (배고동) that blows after the sound of weighing anchor (닷감는 소리) finishes.

The sound that makes even that massive funnel vibrate—it's the bass (빼-스) of a colossal Chaliapin (샤리야핀). It's regrettable that it only blows once before leaving. I wish it would blow repeatedly as we go.

Previously, I often took the steamship (汽船) running from Wŏnsan (元山) to Unggi (雄基). When rounding Mus쇠끝 (Iron Cape?), it was often foggy. The ship would stop moving and only blow its horn. If far from land, behind the horn's sound,<262> there was only silence (寂寞), completely enclosed in the pale fog; if close to land, the mountains on shore would send back an echo (에고-) after the "Woong—". The ship's horn heard then sounded terribly homesick for land. When it sounded and sounded but received no echo from the land, its loneliness; the steamship seemed sad, like an eternal wanderer.
I had faintly drifted off to sleep, but my head felt heavy and dizzy, and I felt slightly nauseous (嘔逆), so I opened my eyes. The sound of the waves is loud. The ship bobs like a fat whale. Trembling "ppureureu," one side rises up, then sinks dizzily like a dying flame as the other side goes down.
Cheolsseok—Swaa—
The whining engine sound (엔징소리) is consistent (행결같다). It's reliable. Quietly looking at the clock showing 3 o'clock and pulling myself together, my ears ring like the inside of a conch shell. Chiyong's (芝溶) poems of the Straits (海峽의 詩篇들) come to mind. They were poems he conceived on this sea, on this ship.
Being a cabin on the deck (갑판), there's no round window 'as if pierced by a cannonball' (砲彈으로 뚫은 듯한), but 'waves flourishing like heads of cabbage sprouting' (甘藍 포기포기 솟아오르듯 茂盛한 물이랑) must be surging over the window, and thinking this must be the 'location where transparent schools of fish parade' (透明한 魚族이 行列하는 位置), the thinness of my bedding feels anew.
I can't sleep, there's no friend to talk to, everyone seems to be sacrifices to fate, lying down in the places designated by the boys (뽀이들). If 'tomorrow morning' didn't exist for them, what a tragedy it would be.<263>

Writings Centered on Character Descriptions (人物描寫 中心의 文章들)

From the Short Story "Moonlit Night" (달밤)
It must have been five or six days after moving to Sŏngbuk-dong (城北洞). That night, I pushed the newspaper I was reading to my bedside, lay down, and thought anew,
"This place really is the countryside!"
It wasn't because I first saw how dark it was outside, or first heard the sound of the stream and the soughing of the pine wind. It was because I met a man named Hwang Sugŏn for the first time that evening.
After exchanging just a few words, it immediately became clear he was a fool (못난이). This fool, more than the mountains, the waters, or the little shortcuts of Sŏngbuk-dong, gave me the feeling that Sŏngbuk-dong was the countryside.
It's not that there wouldn't be fools in Seoul, but in the big city, fools can't come out onto the streets and conduct themselves properly. Perhaps because in the countryside, even the biggest fool can roam around freely, fools seem common only in the countryside and are often easily spotted there. And often, with eyes as foolish yet innocent as someone from primeval times, he gives the guest first entering his village the most rustic sentiment of the countryside.
Anyway, that night, Hwang Sugŏn came looking for our house around ten o'clock.
From the dark yard, he called out in a piercing voice,
"Ah, is this the house from inside the gate (문안서)..."
and entered. Ignoring pleasantries, like someone facing a crisis, he rushed to the door of the opposite room (건넌방) and said,
"Uh, uh, is this the house that came out from inside the gate, from Sŏdaemun street or somewhere?"
Looking at him, he wasn't wearing a happi coat (합비), but carrying a newspaper,<264> he was a newspaper deliveryman (신문 배달부).
"That's right. Is that the newspaper?"
"Ah, for three days, I, I went looking over there on the other side... drat..."
he said, handing the newspaper into the room,
"But look here, why did you buy such a tiny house and come here? Ah, if I had known, there are many big tile-roofed houses down below..."
His words were so absurd that I looked closely at his appearance. The first thing that struck my eye was his closely cropped head, but the skull was larger than average. Moreover, seen from the side, he had a long head (장두대가리).
"Is that so? Anyway, thank you for your trouble finding the house."
At this, his large eyes and large mouth simultaneously grinned,
"Not at all. This is my job, you know,"
he said, not retreating readily but craning his neck to survey the room. Then, unprompted,
"I am, you know, Hwang Sugŏn, who lives in this neighborhood..."
he introduced himself. I politely gave him my name as well. He grinned again and said,
"You don't have a dog at your house, do you?"
"Not yet."
"Don't get a dog, that darn thing."
"Why is that?"
I asked, and he quickly replied,
"Houses that read newspapers, you know, shouldn't keep dogs."
"This is interesting," I thought, and asked again,
"Why is that?"
"Ah, the house in the back neighborhood where someone works at the bank, you know, they have a dog as big as a pony, ah, I can't even deliver the newspaper!"
"Why?"
"It just tries to bite me, jumping at me, you know."
Since his words made no sense, I just laughed, which made him even more enthusiastic.<265>
"That darn dog, just once, I should feed it some Western poison (洋떡)..."
he said, shaking his fist. Looking at it, his hands and wrists were disproportionately small and slender compared to his head.
"Go get some sleep, you must be tired."
Reluctantly, he retreated, saying,
"Sir, truly, Mr. Yi, sleep well. My house isn't far from here, you know."
Then he turned and left.
The next evening, even though he knew the house, he arrived after nine o'clock, shouting,
"Newspaper delivery has arrived!"
as he entered.
"Why are you late today?"
I asked.
"It just happens,"
he said, changing the subject.
He told me he originally worked at the Samsan School (삼산학교) down below but left because he didn't get along with a certain teacher; that he delivers newspapers now, but not as the main deliveryman (원배달), just an assistant (보조배달); that his family consists of seven people: his parents, his older brother and sister-in-law, a nephew, and himself and his wife; what his father's and older brother's names were; that his surname is Hwang, and his given name is Sugŏn (壽建 - meaning 'longevity' and 'build'), so kids tease him calling him 'Norang Sugŏn' (Yellow Towel/Handkerchief), and he proudly related that in Sŏngbuk-dong, every household knows who 'Norang Sugŏn' is. That day too, only when I said,
"Come on, you need to deliver papers to other houses too, don't you?"
did he reluctantly leave.
At home, they asked why I bothered talking to such a half-wit (반편), but I enjoyed chatting (지꺼리기) with him.
I liked how he talked earnestly about trivial things, how talking with him for any length of time wasn't tiring, and how after chatting for a long time, nothing remained but laughter, leaving me feeling refreshed. So, unless I was in the middle of something, I would engage with his talk for quite a while.
Some days, our conversation would stall. Not because we couldn't answer,<266> but because we couldn't think of what to say next. But he was always quicker than me at finding topics. He asked if I ate pheasant meat even in May or June, whether I put on the jacket or trousers first when wearing a Western suit (洋服), and which would win if a cow and a horse fought. Anyway, the scope of topics he drew from was beyond imagination; I simply couldn't match his range. One day, I asked him, "What is your lifelong wish (평생 소원)?" He replied, "Answering something like that is a piece of cake," and said his lifelong wish was to become a main deliveryman himself.

From the Short Story "The Bride/Young Woman" (색시)
Thinking back now, we ended up not knowing the surname or first name of a person who lived with us for a year.
She wasn't just any vagrant, and being still young, calling her "Ŏmŏm" (Mommy - term for married servant woman) or "Singmo" (maid) felt a bit harsh. My wife first called her "Saeksi" (색시 - Bride/Young Woman/Miss), and later even the children, told to call her "Ajumŏni" (auntie), called her "Saeksi, Saeksi." I too, when facing her and saying,
"Give me water—"
or
"Take the table away,"
used no pronoun, but when speaking of her to others, I also said "Saeksi did this or that..."
Thus, as "Saeksi," there seemed to be no inconvenience for either party, and her surname and first name remained unknown until she left.

Saeksi came to our house late last spring. When I came home in the evening, an unfamiliar woman (안악네) was in the yard with a bellows (풍노) placed outside, her face flushed red, blowing on the fire. Seeing the smoke drift over the nasturtium (활련) patch where seedlings had just been planted, I asked my wife, who was just coming out to the sarang (사랑 - outer room/master's quarters),
"Who is that? Who is blowing smoke right onto the flower patch?"<267>
"That is..."
my wife started, but realizing the sharpness in my tone, went back into the inner yard and said,
"Saeksi? Isn't that the flower patch? Don't blow the smoke that way, it would be better to blow it over there..."
Then,
"Ayu! Why fuss so much over mere nasturtiums? How strange..."
she said, standing up. Not only her voice, but her height and build were incredibly imposing for a woman.
However, her response was so fussy (호들갑스러운데다), and she immediately chuckled "kilkilkil," that I felt no need to reprimand her further, and instead got the impression, "She probably doesn't get angry easily."
When hiring help, the most troublesome thing was someone grumpy (왜쭉삐쭉) and easily angered. Someone whose expression changes instantly if asked to rewash a spoon, or from whose kitchen the sound of a poker (부저깔) being thrown down immediately comes if asked to reheat the stew just once—having had our fill of such irritable maids (식모), we hoped this time for someone we could order around without having to watch their mood, even if she wasn't particularly neat. So, my wife, of course, and I too, were pleased with her from the start. But then, when my wife said,
"Isn't she a bit too young? They say she's a widow... at that age..."
hearing this, besides feeling sorry for her misfortune, the thought of having someone with such sadness in her heart as part of the family wasn't very pleasant.
"So where is she from, how did she find our house?"
"Her parents' home is apparently in Munemi (무네미), further on from Tonam-ri (돈암리). You know the old woman who came to do sewing sometime back? She's that old woman's niece."
"Ah-ha. And then?"
"She intends to remarry (재가할 작정) if she finds a decent place, but where is it that easy? Her parents aren't well-off, so partly for a change of air and partly out of frustration (홧김에), she heard about our house from her auntie and came."
"Well, that worked out."
"But it's hilarious."
"Why?"
"Didn't she just giggle 'kwae-ni kilkil' earlier? She laughs quite a lot."<268>
"Fortune comes to a laughing house, so that's good," we said and laughed too.
"Still, becoming a widow in the prime of youth, if you think about it, it must be somewhat heartbreaking? But does her face look like someone with worries? Just giggling 'kwae-ni kilkil'."
"She's probably still immature," I thought, attributing it solely to her lack of maturity.

Anyway, Saeksi loved to laugh. Since she came, the quiet inner quarters often erupted in laughter. Sometimes my wife would run out, unable to contain herself, telling stories like:
"You know, Saeksi was imitating her former mother-in-law..."
Other times,
"She imitated the Chinaman who came selling barbecue grills..."
And other times,
"Imitating a shaman (무당)..."
claiming she was good at imitating voices (입내) and making people laugh. Not only did she make others laugh, but after making them laugh, she herself would burst into laughter until she was breathless. She felt she couldn't live if her insides felt stuffy without laughing like that once or twice a day.
For someone with a fire burning inside, living like that might be good for her, and it wouldn't harm the people living with her either. However, her carelessness in work became increasingly problematic.
She never put anything down gently. I only heard about her frequently breaking dishes in the kitchen, but even with things like washbasins, she rarely bent down to place them. It was common for her to throw them down while standing straight (뻣-뻣이), causing ceramics to chip and metal to dent—I saw this happen several times. When my wife asked why she acted like a clumsy male farmhand (선모슴),
"What can I do if anger wells up?"
was her most common reply, followed by,
"Why make such a fuss over that?"
She often used that phrase "그까짓걸" (that trivial thing) in other situations too. It seemed nothing was precious to her. She disliked cooking rice as instructed. She'd unnecessarily cook enough for one extra person, push it around from this bowl<269> to that, and liked to throw it out if it spoiled. She only knew how to light the firewood (나무) after stuffing the furnace (아궁이) until it seemed ready to burst. Everything flowed through her hands like water, wastefully (헤푸다). Even I noticed that requests from my wife to buy oil because it ran out, or candles because they ran out, were twice as frequent as with previous maids. No matter how much my wife nagged, she apparently never used oil bottles or candle holders with caps or covers.
"Is it that hard to cap it?"
If asked,
"There's nothing more frustrating in the world than finding a cap on something when you want to use it,"
she would say, even getting angry if someone else capped things. Sometimes, when my wife lost her temper and asked,
"How did you manage married life (시집살이) like that?"
She would giggle and say,
"Still, my father-in-law (시아버지) always praised me for handling difficult tasks well."
"That's because it was a household that made the daughter-in-law do such hard work. What if you go to a well-off family now?"
"If I have my own household (내 살림), I want to do well too,"
was her bold reply.
She rarely spoke about her deceased husband; whenever she sat quietly, she was thinking about remarrying. The evening she received her few won monthly salary (월급), she would finish dinner before sunset, go into the city (문안), and wastefully buy various cosmetics like powder (분) and cream (크림). When we bought something like a plate or teacup, she would jump into the middle asking, "How much is this? How much is that?" and inevitably
"I should buy things like that too when I set up my own household... Hwashin Department Store (화신상회), you said?"
and take note (벨르다?). Not only taking note, but the electric iron (전기대리미) was so fascinating that after receiving her second month's salary, she bought one of those first.
"Saeksi, what kind of person do you wish to marry?"
my wife once asked her.
"As long as he has a good character (인품) and enough to eat, that's fine... oh, and someone who's cool and refreshing (시원시원허구)..."
she said, then happened to see a group of middle school students passing by our house playing harmonicas (하모니까),<270>
"Oh! You know, I like men who play the harmonica well,"
she said, giggling and laughing heartily again. What are children? This Old Man Uam's current wife, Kiyong's mother, was his second secondary wife (小室). At first, he had intended to cleanly grow old together (偕老) with his first wife, thinking why covetously desire what fate hadn't granted. But one winter night in the year he turned fifty, his first wife voluntarily brought a young woman and pushed her into the sarang.
That was the first so-called secondary wife he saw, but after living with her for three years, still no daughters or sons were born.<297>■■■■■■■■■■■■So she vaguely disappeared to someone else's■■■■■■■■■■■■■■■From the chest, here■■■■■■■■■■■■■■■as a final word, one■■■■■■■■■gathered.
■■■■■Having obtained a son to succeed him, Old Man Uam felt for a while as if even his dimming eyes had brightened again, and even food tasted new.
"Even if a person dies on their dying day, there should be days like this when one feels truly alive."
The Old Man felt human joy (人間樂) anew in his later years (末年).
However, this one bright fact cast several dark shadows.
The first wife, unlike when she had voluntarily pushed a young woman into the sarang, became intolerant (寬大하지 못하였다) of leaving the old man's side.
"Goodness, in what lifetime will you raise that thing to be called a child and entrust your final affairs (후살) to him..."
she began to disparage Kiyong's existence. Also,
"Be careful, do young... things really care about looking after old people's bones (늙은이 골는걸)?"
she would say, unnecessarily sharp-tongued (앙칼진 소리).
It wasn't just the first wife who changed. Kiyong's mother, who previously couldn't even cough loudly, started clearing her throat (가래를 돋우는 것) loud enough to make the wooden floor (대청) ring, as if stamping her feet, after giving birth to Kiyong.
The Old Man felt a kind of disillusionment (幻滅). Their chameleon-like tempers (豹變하는 성미) made the beauty of wifely virtue (婦德의 美) the Old Man had believed in seem hollow (虛無), disrupted the family customs (家風), and seemed to demean his own dignity (치신) as head of the household (家長).
"...And then, the spring after next, elementary school, elementary school is six years, middle school is five years, and after that, professional school or university..."
Indeed, as the first wife said, it was difficult not to feel despair (絶望) about Kiyong. Especially when he considered his own markedly decreased appetite (食慾이 減退), cold hands and feet (手足이 차지고), and increasingly dim vision (視力이 한층 더 침침해 감) since last spring, he thought he could endure at most long enough to see Kiyong graduate elementary school, if that.
"So, what will that poor thing eat, wear, and study with, inheriting just this one house..."
Furthermore, thinking about his destitute wife and child being pointed out by others after his death—"That's so-and-so's wife, that's so-and-so's son"—and his own name being dragged through the mud dishonorably<298> (불명예스럽게), he suddenly felt regret, "I've bought myself unnecessary worry!"
"Whose child is twenty years old..." (Meaning unclear, perhaps contrasting ideal age with Kiyong's infancy)
Yet, he couldn't just dismiss Kiyong as not being his child.
○
Old Man Uam took off his padded socks again, removed his jacket, and blew out the lamp—still swirling with halos white as snow and broad yellow—as if committing murder (殺生), puffing breathlessly five or six times before it finally went out. He lay back down in bed.
"Even if it's called 'a candle in the wind' (風前燈火), it takes effort to blow out a lamp..."
He muttered this to himself and thought that human life (목슴) must be like that too. There might be lives extinguished like a candle in the wind, but there must also be tenacious lives (떡심 같은 목슴) that cling on endlessly, seeming about to go out but ultimately exhausting themselves.
And he felt afraid that his own end (臨終) might be like that.
Death!
The Old Man found it hard to fall back asleep. He closed his gritty eyes, trying to be as calm as possible, but a tumultuous and terrifying noise, like gongs clanging, began incessantly in his ears. He opened his eyes again and looked at the ceiling (천정). The ceiling was endless. The pitch-black darkness, where nothing was visible, seemed infinitely high and infinitely deep. And realizing that death must be of that color, where nothing is seen, the room suddenly felt unbearably desolate, like being deep in the mountains or the sea. And when he realized that this infinitely deep darkness and desolation would henceforth be his closer companion than his first wife, his second wife, or Kiyong, the Old Man felt the hair on his head stand on end (머리끝이 쭙볏 곤두솟았다), as if confronting a terrifying wild beast (野獸).
"That beast!"
A dark shadow seemed to take a step forward, looming closer.
His previously fluffy forehead became damp with sweat.
"Hoo-..."
Old Man Uam fumbled at his bedside again. He was looking for the matchbox. He wanted to light another cigarette, but more than that, he longed for fire, for light, even just a single spark.<299>

**Summary**

This collection presents Yi T'aejun's evocative prose from 1938, featuring short vignettes ("Narcissus," "Trees"), travelogue excerpts ("From a Tokyo Travelogue," "Straits at 3 AM"), and numerous passages from his short stories and novels focusing alternately on character sketches and scene descriptions. The pieces explore themes of nature's solace and indifference, the anxieties of modern life clashing with tradition (curves vs. straight lines, modernization's impact), poignant encounters with marginalized figures (a simple-minded newspaper boy, a lonely widow, impoverished children, an abandoned old man), the complexities of human relationships (family conflicts, romantic longing, societal expectations), and vivid sensory details of urban and rural landscapes, often imbued with a melancholic or pensive mood. For historians, the text offers rich glimpses into colonial Korean society: descriptions of urban development (Seoul, Tokyo), rural life and poverty, changing social norms (widow remarriage, concubinage), attitudes towards modernization and traditional culture, interactions between Koreans and Japanese ("mobile policeman," "mainland Japanese trash"), everyday objects and customs (oil lamps, clothing, food, transportation), and potentially censored passages (indicated by ■■■) hinting at the political constraints of the era.

水仙.
『너는 고향이 어디냐?』
나는 지난밤 자리에 누으며 문득 그에게 이렇게 속삭였읍니다. 그는 다음과 같이 도련도련 대답해 주는 것 같았읍니다.

樹木
몇평 안되는 마당이나마 나무들과 함께 설 수 있음은 얼마나 감사한 일인가! 울타리 삼아 둘러즌 十數株의 앵도나무를 비롯하여 감나무, 소나무, 대추나무와 牡丹, 白樺의 한두 그루를 이들은 우리집 모든 식구들이 다 떠받들어 옳은 귀한 손님들이다.
우리에게 꽃을 주고, 우리에게 열매를 주고, 또 푸른 그늘과 그 맑은 향기를 주는 이들은, 우리에게서 받음은 아모것도 없는 것이다. 가믈면 물을 좀 주는 것이나, 추우면 몇나무의 및둥을 집으로 싸주는 것쯤은, 그들이 우리에게 주는 그 아름다움과, 그 맛남과, 그 향기롭고 서늘함에 비기여 아모 것도 아닌 것이다. 실로 아모 것도 아닌 것이다. 어느 친구나, 어느 부자인들, 우리에게 이처럼 주기만 하고 받음이 없음에 泰然할 것인가 自然이 나무를 통하여 우리를 기르고 우리를 가리킴은 크다.
나무들은 아직 묵묵히 서있다. 봄은 아직 몇 천리 밖에 있는 듯하다. 그러나 나무아래 가까이 설 때마다 나는 진작부터 봄을 느낀다. 아모 나무나 한가지 휘여 잡아보면 그 도틈도틈 맻어진 눈들, 하롯밤 細雨만 나려주면, 하로 아침 따슨 햇발을 쪼여주면 곧 뜨리라는 소근거림이 부르튼 듯, 가득가득 몰려있는 것이다.
봄아 어서 오라.
겨울 나무아래를 거니는 사람처럼 봄이 급한 사람은 없을 것이다. 또 그 사람처럼 봄의 꿈이 純潔한 사람도 없을 것이다. 나는 이 순결한 꿈의 행복을＜257＞ 자랑하자.

우리 식구들은 앵도가 익을 때마다, 대초와 감을 딸 때마다, 이 집이라기보다 마당을 우리에게 팔고 간 그전 주인을 생각한다. 더구나 감나무는 우리가 와서부터 첫여름이 열린 것이니 그들은 나무만 심고 열매는 보지 못한 채 떠난 것이다. 남의 밭에 드러 추수하는 것같은 미안이 없지 않다. 나는 몇번이나 佛蘭西 어느 작가의 『인도인의 오막사리』란 적은 소설 한편을 생각하군 하였다. 어떤 학자가 세계를 遍踏하며 진리를 찾는 이야기인데 필경은 뜻을 이루지 못 하고 도라가는 길에서 폭풍우를 만나 한 인도인의 오막사리로 드러가게 되였다. 오막사리의 주인은 파이리아라는 인도 최하급의 賤族으로서 그의 생활은 문화와 완전히 絶綠된 것이였다. 그러나 학자는 이 파이리아에게서 어느 高層巨僧에게서도 얻지 못 하였던 진리의 한 끗 실머리를 부뜰게 되였다. 그들의 대화중에 파이리아의 말로 다음과 같은 뜻의 구절이 아직 기억된다.

안영감은 성이 안가는 아니었다. 어느 떡장사 마누라가 한번은 쉰 떡을 그에게 먹이면서 그의 사정이야기를 듣고 안변(安邊)서 왔다고 해서 「안변영감」이라 한 것이 귀하지 못한 사람의 이름이라 되는대로 「안영감」이라 불려진 것이였다＜278＞
안영감은 등전이 두푼만 모이어도 그 장사할멈들에게 들고 와서 몇 번씩 되푸리하는 이야기거니와, 본래 자기는 그리 적빈하지는 않어 글자도 배워서 이름자는 적는터이며 의식도 삼베중이에 조밥이나마 굶고 헐벗지는 않고 살았노라 하였다. 단지 남과 같이 아들자식을 두지못해서 딸하나 있는 것을 데릴사위를 드렸더니 그것도 자기 팔자 소관인지 딸이 사내를 딿으지 않고 다라났다는 것이다. 다라난지 며칠 뒤에 「청진쪽으로 가서 술장사를 해서 돈을 모아가지고 아버님을 모시러 나오겠다」는 편지가 한번 있기는 했으나 그후 삼사년이 지나도록 소식이 없고, 의탁할 곳은 없이 떠날 때에는 걸어서라도 딸을 찾아 청진까지 가보려든것이었노라 한다. 그러나 원산까지 와서는 벌서 두 여름이 되는 동안 그저 떠나보는 날이 없이 혹시 딸이 이배에나 지배에나 돌아오지나 않을가 하고, 망망한 바다에 배 소리만 기다리고 사는 것이라 한다.
사는것이라야 남보기엔 죽지 못해 사는것이었다 그러나 그도 자기 마음엔 그렇지는 않은 듯 누가 자기의 목숨을 멸시하면 그것처럼 분한건없어 하였다. 어떤때 장사 마누라들이 먹을 것을 주어놓고 저이끼린 동정하는 말로

안영감은 다시는 안 볼것처럼 들어내팬다. 그리다도 배가 고파 어찌 할수 없을 때엔 어느 제숫댁를이나 찾아오듯, 다시 그 할멈들의 함지앞으로 어슬렁어슬렁 나타났고 그 할멈들도

『호-』

『날이 꽤, 차졌군……』
그는 등피는 닦으면서 아직 눈에 익지 않은 정원을 둘러보았다. 이끼 않은 둘충게 밑에는 발이 묻히게 낙엽이 쌓여있고 상나무, 전나무 같은 상록수를 베여놓고는 단풍나무까지 이미 반넘어 이울어 어떤 나무는 잎이라고 하나도 없이 설-멍하게 서 있다. 「무장해제를 당한 포로들처럼」하는 생각을 하면서 그런 쓸쓸한 나무들이 이구석 저구석에 묵묵히 섰는 것을 그는 등피를 다 닦고도 다시 한참이나 비라보다가야 자기방으로 정한 밖알채 작은 사랑으로 올라갔다.
여기는 그의 어느 친구의 별장이다. 늘 괴벽한 문체(文體)를 고집하여 독자를 널리 갖지못하는 그는 한달재 이십원 남짓하면 독방을 차지할수 있는 학생층의 하숙생활조차 뜻대로 되지 않었다. 긍여의 일책으로 이렇게 임시로나마 겨우내 그냥 비여 두는 친구네 별장방 하나를 빌린 것이다. 내년 칠원까지는 어느방이든지 마음대로 쓰라고 해서 정자직이가 방마다 문을 열어 보이는대로 구경하였으나 모다 여름에나 좋을 북향들이라 너무 음습하고 너무 넓고 문들이 많어서 결국은 밖알채로 나와, 상노들이나자는 방이라는 작은 사람을 치이게 한 것이다.
상노들이나 자기 방이라 하나 별장전체를 그리 손색있게 하는 방은 아니었다. 동향이어서 여＜280＞름에는 늦잠을 자지 못할것이 흠일가 겨울에는 어느 방보다 밝고 따뜻할수 있고 미닫이와 들창도 다갑창까지 드린데다 벽장문과 두껍다지에는 유명한 화가인지 아닌지는 몰라도 낙관(落款)이 있는 사군자(四君子)며 기명절지(器皿折枝)가 불어 있다. 밖으로도 문 우에는 추성각(秋聲閣)이라 추사(秋史)체의 현판이 걸려있고 양쪽처마끝에는 파-랗게 녹쓸은 풍경이 창연히 달려있다. 또 미닫이를 열면 눈아래 깔리는 경치도 큰 사랑만 못한 것 같지 않으니, 산기슭에 나붓이 섰는 수각(水閣)과 그밑으로 마른 연잎과 단풍이 잠긴 연당이며 그리고 그 연당언덕으로 올라오면서 무룡석으로 석가산을 모으고 잔디밭 새에 길을 돌린 것은 이 방에서 나려다보기가 기중일듯 싶었다. 그런데다 눈을 번뜻 들면 동편 하늘이 바다처럼 티이고 그 한편으로 훨칠한 늙은 전나무 한 채가 절벽같이 가려섰는 것이다. 사슴이 뿔처럼 씩정귀가 된 상가지에는 히끗 히끗 새똥까지 묻히어서 고요히 바라보면 한눈에 태고(太古)가 깃드리는듯한 그윽한 경치이다.
오래간만에 켜보는 남포불이다. 펄럭-하고 성냥불이 심지에 옴기더니 좁은 등피 속은 자옥하게 연기와 김이 서리였다가 차츰 차츰 밝어지는 것이였다. 그렇게 차츰 차츰 밝어지는 남포불에 뗑-둘러앉었던 옛날 집안사람들의 얼굴이 생각나게 그렇게 남포불은 추억 많은 불이다.

아무댈 가로 저런 동네는 없을 것이다. 읍엘 갔다와도 선왕당턱만 나려서면 바람 한점 없이 안윽하고, 빨래하기 좋고 먹어도 좋은 앞 개울물이며, 날이 추우면 뒷산에 올라 솔잎만 긁어도 몇칠씩은 염녀 없이 때더니……이전 모두 남의 동네 이야기로구나!
『어서 갑시다』

새삼스럽게 슬하가 쓸쓸함을 탄식하면서 건는방 미닫이 앞으로 드러섰다. 그리고 숨을 죽이노라고 기운 없는 턱을 혼덩거리면서 유리쪽으로 넌즛이 방안을 들여다보았다.
기용이 모자는 생각했던 것처럼 잠들어 있었다 기용이는 어미의 팔과 벼개를 을러 베고 새발앗게 닳은 입술을 붕어처럼 동그랗게 벌리고 가슴을 높이 들먹거리었다.
『내일은 김주부약을 그만 끓어버려? 향의를 믿어봐……』
노인은 한참 생각하고 섰다가야 기용어미의 자는양도 살펴보았다. 방에 외풍이 없이 하라고 일른 자기 말대로 불을 많이 때인 듯 이불은 나려놓지도 않고 속곳바람으로 사지를 퍼드렸드리고 있다. 윗도리도 반이나 드러났으되, 또 이제 서른넷밖에 난 젊은 小室이로되 老人의 神經은 淡淡한채로 움직임이 없었다. 느낀다면, 弱한 지게꾼이 무거워 지게 처다보듯 一種의 壓力을 느낄뿐이었다.
『거것두 불상한 것……』
하면서 역시 이 방 남포불에서도 시퍼런 무리가 도는 것을 깨닫자 곧 사랑으로 나오고 말었다.

자식이 무엔지 이 愚菴老人은 기용이모가 두 번재의 小室이었다. 처음엔 팔자에 없는걸 구지욕되게 바라랴 하고 깨끗이 두 늙은이끼리 偕老나 할 작정이였으나 쉰이 되든해 거울 어느날 밤인데 큰마누라가 자진하여 웬 젊은 것 하나를 다려다 사랑에 밀어놓었다.
그게 처음 본 소위 小室이였으나 그후 三年을 살아보되 역시 딸도 아들도 소생이 없으므＜297＞■■■■■■■■■■■■니 흐지부지 남의사■■■■■■■■■■■■■■■가슴부터는 예서 제■■■■■■■■■■■■■■말막음으로 하나■■■■■■■■■모였다.
■■■■■로갈아들을 하나 얻은 愚菴老人은 한동인은 침침하던 눈까지 다시 밝아지는 듯 음식에까지 새 맛을 느끼였다.
『사람이 죽는날 죽드라도 이렇게 사는가 싶은 날이 있어야』
老人은 末年에 이르러 人間樂을 새로한번 느끼였다
그러나 이 한가지의 밝은 事實은 려러가지의 어두은 그늘을 가지고 왔다.
큰마누라는 자진하여 젊은 것 하나를 사랑방에다 밀어넣어주던 때와는 딴사람처럼 영감의 자리 옆을 떠나는데 寬大하지 못하였다.
『그걸 글세 어느 세월에 길러가지구 자식이라구 후살 맡겨보누……』
하고 기용의 存在를 헐기 시작하였다. 또
『조심해요 괘-니 젊……은것들이 늙은이 골는걸 돌본답디까?』
하고 공연히 앙칼진 소리도 하였다.
달러진 것은 큰마누뿐도 아니였다. 기침 한번을 크게 못하던 기용이 모도 기용을 낳어놓고부터는 발을 굴르는 듯 대청이 울리게 가래를 돋우는 것이였다.
老人은 一種의 幻滅을 느끼였다. 이들의 豹變하는 성미는 老人이 믿어온 婦德의 美를 虛無스럽게 하였고 家風을 어즈럽히며 家長으로서의 자기의 치신까지 깎이는 것 같었다.
『……인제두 후년봄에나 소학교, 소학교가 육년중학교가 오년, 그리구두 전문학교니 대학이니…』
아닌게 아니라 큰마누라의 말대로 기용에게 絶望이 안 되기도 이려웠다. 더욱 작년봄부터 현저하게 食慾이 減退되고 手足이 차지고 視力이 한층 더 침침해 감을 돌불 때 자기는 앞으로 고작 견디어야 기용의 소학교졸업이나 볼지 말지 였다.
『그러니 그까짓게 겨우 이까짓 집이나 한 채 물려 갖구 무얼 먹고 무얼 입고 무얼루 공부하구……』
뿐만 아니라 자기가 죽은뒤에 헐벗는 처자식이 남들에게「저게 아무게 에펜네요 아무개 아들이오하는」말을 들어 자기 이름이 가장 불명＜298＞예스럽게 굴러다닐 것을 생각하니 문득 「공연한 걱정거리를 삿군!」하는 후회까지도 났다.
『어느 이십안 자식이라니……』
그렇다고 기용이를 자식이 아니거니 돌릴 수도 없는 일이다.

『풍선등화라구 해두 불도 끄려면 힘들어……』
혼자 이렇게 중얼거리고 사람의 목슴도 저런 것이거니도 생각해보았다. 바람에 촛불 꺼지듯하는 목슴도 있겠지만 꺼질 듯 꺼질 듯 하면서도 한없이 질기게 끌어 나종에는 제진에 나가 떨어지는 그런 떡심 같은 었슴도 있으려니 하였다
그리고 행여 자기의 臨終이 그런것일가봐 겁아 나기도 했다.
