Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Roundtable Discussion)

**Roundtable Discussion on Soviet Affairs, Heard from a Red Russian Officer Who Defected to Korea**

**Attendees:**

Defected Red Army Officer Ivan Yakovlevich Nakharov
Defected Red Army Soldier Pyotr Makarovich Ivanov
Kwŏn Chung-il, Pak Tŭk-hyŏn, Kim Han-gyŏng, Pak Yŏng-hŭi,
And Mr. Takai Kunihiko, Consultant to the Korean Army

This roundtable discussion was held centered around two Soviet soldiers who defected from the Soviet Far Eastern Army during the recent Zhanggufeng Incident (張鼓峰事件) and came to OO seeking a new life. The questioners include several individuals who were formerly Communist Party cadres and even had experience entering Russia. The reason we organized this roundtable is not only to gather information as a resource for understanding the Soviet Union but also to learn how these defecting soldiers view their homeland, the Soviet Union.

**Why Did You Defect?**

Reporter: We would like to hear the real story of the Soviet Union from the Soviet soldiers who defected from the USSR during the Zhanggufeng Incident to understand the actual situation there. We are grateful that the two Soviet soldiers could join us today, and also thank Mr. Takai from the Korean Army Command for being here.
Mr. Takai is someone who lived in Russia for 17 years and recently returned. I've also heard that among the attendees are those who once advocated for communism or nationalism, possess considerable knowledge about the Soviets, and even defected to the Soviet Union. Tonight, we aim to frankly exchange opinions or hear about the real situation in the Soviet Union from the Soviet defectors.

Takai: Then, let me first introduce the two Soviet soldiers <92> who defected from the site during the Zhanggufeng Incident. This is Mr. Nakharov, 24 years old. That gentleman is Mr. Ivanov, 22 years old. Both are from European Russia. Mr. Nakharov graduated from an 8-year school and left a non-commissioned officer training school midway through his military service. Mr. Ivanov attended school only very briefly, so it's no exaggeration to say he is uneducated.

Nakharov: It happened shortly before the incident broke out. Six Soviet officers went near **** [censored/unclear]. Although the guards clearly saw them enter the forest, all six officers went missing. I think they too defected into Manchurian territory (滿領). At that time, the entire regiment I belonged to was mobilized, and we searched the entire mountain area near the border where the six officers had entered, but we couldn't find them. As we returned, my comrades envied the escaped officers, saying, "Those officers who ran away did well," and talked about foreign countries. This caused a stir in my heart.

Reporter: How about Mr. Ivanov?

Ivanov: As an individual farmer (個人農), I was constantly oppressed by the Kolkhoz (꼬르포-즈) and continued to live a very difficult life. The more I thought about it, the more I grew to hate Russia.

Pak Yŏng-hŭi: What kind of oppression did individual farmers face? Being pressured by various Kolkhoz operations...

Nakharov: At the time of my defection, I was out on reconnaissance (斥候). So, I took that opportunity, exploited a gap in the fighting, and crossed the border. I walked about 8 kilometers and managed to enter Manchurian territory safely without being detected by either side. By chance, I met two Japanese soldiers passing by and asked them for help.

Pak Tŭk-hyŏn: What was the direct motive for your escape?

Nakharov: I served three years in prison <93> and experienced the bitterness of forced labor. My resentment towards the Soviet regime at that time is something I still cannot forget. Even later, while studying at the NCO school, I felt pressured for various reasons. Considering all these things, I felt that some kind of crisis was approaching me personally. This was the direct cause. Also, frequently hearing about foreign situations and having a strong yearning for foreign countries was another reason.

Reporter: Does yearning for foreign countries mean you were suffering from hardships in life?

Ivanov: Our family's daily life could truly be called a continuous sequence of terror. Even when the wind blows and the window rattles, our nerves are frayed wondering if the Gepe'u (께페우 - GPU/NKVD) is coming. When working out in the fields, if we see someone in military uniform approaching from afar, we tremble, thinking they are coming to arrest us. This is the life of us peasants.

Reporter: We'll talk about peasant life later. Please tell us about your border crossing.

Ivanov: I was in an infantry heavy machine gun unit and received an attack order. I dashed through the marshy area, but at one point, the Japanese army's attack was so fierce that I hid in the bushes. Two of my comrades were with me then, but both were wounded by Japanese artillery shells. Fortunately, I wasn't injured, so I thought this was my chance. I pierced through the darkness and walked as far as possible towards the trenches where I couldn't hear Russian. After walking for a while, I occasionally heard voices again. Then I heard Russian voices again. Thinking, "This won't do," I summoned all my strength and walked again. Finally, I found the Japanese position. From there, I took out a handkerchief, waved it, and broke through the front line.

Pak Yŏng-hŭi: So you two didn't defect together?

Takai: They met here after arriving; they came from completely different directions.

Reporter: But it happened to be on the same day?

Ivanov: After escaping, I didn't eat or drink for two days.

**Soviet Education – How is Japan Taught?**

Reporter: You must have looked quite miserable then. (Laughter) Well then, let's move on to the main questions. Earlier, Mr. Takai mentioned the educational levels of the two soldiers when introducing them, <94> so let's start by hearing about Soviet schools.

Takai: Let me speak first. First, there is a 4-year elementary school, then 7th, 8th, 9th, and 10th grades, which are like middle school. After that, there are labor schools, but generally, the types are mostly similar to Japan. Russian compulsory education is 4 years, which is 2 years shorter than Japan's. However, since they don't need to learn Chinese characters like in Japan, 4 years is considered sufficient.

Pak Tŭk-hyŏn [Seems like a typo, likely Pak Tŭk-hyŏn mentioned earlier]: Isn't it true that there's no such thing as home education in Russia?

Takai: Currently, education that could be called home education is not practiced. It seems they think it poses some hindrance to unifying education.

Kwŏn Chung-il: When I was in Russia, because various ethnicities lived together, education was conducted in each ethnic language...

Takai: This is something I heard from these two individuals before, and I also know it myself. Ethnic education is currently receiving particular attention. Now, ethnic languages are still incorporated, and education is conducted using books written in the Roman alphabet (로-마자). While ethnic languages are used in some schools, generally, ethnic languages are now forbidden, and everyone uses Russian.

Pak Tŭk-hyŏn: I heard that there is discrimination in school education between the children of Communist Party members and non-party members. Is that true? What actual benefits do the children of party members receive?

Nakharov: There used to be, but now it's exactly the same. However, school management is run by the People's Commissariat of Education (敎育人民委員部), so perhaps from middle school onwards, there might be discrimination. School principals, teachers – those who form the core of management – are selected from party members or their children.

Kim Han-gyŏng: In Soviet schools, what do they teach about East Asian affairs, particularly Japan?

Nakharov: In the 4-year compulsory education schools, they don't teach about Japan or other foreign countries. Geography is taught, but only very broadly. In schools above the 4-year level, general political science is taught, covering the political and domestic situations of various countries. Especially regarding Japan, they focus on teaching the negative aspects of Japan as seen by Russia. Because I constantly heard that Japanese peasants are very poor or that there are no goods, I thought so until today. But now <95> I know that's wrong, and I realize that Russian education is not truthful and engages in counter-propaganda. As schools get higher, political education advances, teaching about Russian foreign policy, but this tendency is strong in top-level universities or normal schools. Also, the teaching materials are mainly drawn from newspapers, but those newspapers ultimately have to be Russian newspapers.

Takai: Because foreign newspapers cannot be seen at all.

Kim Han-gyŏng: What is the level of education among peasants compared to urban workers?

Nakharov: I heard that about 25 percent of people in rural areas are uneducated. The rest have received the 4-year compulsory education. I heard that about 15 percent go on to higher schools, like vocational schools. In the cities, there probably isn't anyone you could call uneducated.

Kim Han-gyŏng: If compulsory education is implemented, why are there still 25 percent uneducated people?

Takai: Wouldn't the reason lie in the rural situation? Mr. Nakharov, could you answer?

Nakharov: Yes. Because the land in rural areas is vast, extending educational facilities is very difficult. Recently, with the Kolkhoz system, those who owned horses and cows in the villages were successively imprisoned, so horses and cows vanished from the countryside, replaced by tractors. Previously, people commuted to school by horse or cart, but recently, they can no longer do that, making it impossible to attend school.

Ivanov: Even if it's compulsory education, there's no penalty if you don't attend school. That's why I've never heard of anyone being punished for it. (Laughter)

Kwŏn Chung-il: In that case, isn't it likely that the general populace harbors dissatisfaction and complaints about the current Soviet school education?

Nakharov: Of course. However, rather than general dissatisfaction, there are many demands regarding specific subjects. Those who hold dissatisfaction are mainly people educated before the 1917 revolution and the children influenced by them. They not only know the previous state of education but are also well aware of foreign situations, so they harbor resentment. Before that, people might make requests regarding education, but it seems there weren't many who harbored such dissatisfaction.

**The Question of God in the Soviet Union**

Takai: In the past, religion was taught, and schools had ethics subjects, but now <96> those are completely gone, so the children's character has become very bad. This is surely a matter of concern among intellectuals. Consequently, there's too much propaganda in teaching, which seems to cause weariness.

Reporter: Since the topic of religion came up, I'd like to ask one thing. Do you believe in religion?

Nakharov: That's difficult. (Laughter) I heard various stories about God from my father and mother, so I vaguely know that God exists. I feel that getting this opportunity after escaping the battlefield this time might also be due to God's help. Regarding faith, Mr. Ivanov is a more fervent believer than I am. When we arrived in OO, we once had a debate about religion. Of course, our viewpoints differed, but ultimately, our goal was the same. Neither of us denies faith.

Ivanov: Since I was young, I learned about God from my parents. I still think so and believe. The situation of those who betrayed God and ran to the Kolkhoz is that they are plunged into misery, unable to eat even if they want to. My father, while being oppressed by such people, lived a better life as an individual farmer than those in the Kolkhoz. I always heard from my father that this is solely because he believes in God. So, I believe in God.

Kim [Han-gyŏng]: I heard that the Soviet government's policy towards religion is not as harsh recently as it used to be. How is it?

Nakharov: The government legally recognizes freedom of religion, but it creates atheist and non-religious groups and uses them to indirectly devise extermination measures.

Kwŏn [Chung-il]: What is the current sentiment of the Soviet populace towards religion? We heard that when atheist campaigners go to rural areas to propagandize, peasants gather and beat them, breaking their skulls before they return. Is this true?

Nakharov: It's no exaggeration to say that everyone educated before the revolution believes in God. Even among communists, when doubts arise about Marxism, they turn to faith. Moreover, due to parental influence, such people sometimes pray secretly.

**Peasants Are Extremely Desperate**

Reporter: Let's change the topic here and listen to the actual peasant life, focusing on Soviet agricultural management. <97>

Takai: There's so much to talk about regarding Russian agricultural management that it would take all day to cover everything.

Reporter: Please just tell us the most interesting points.

Nakharov: Russian agricultural management consists of three types: Sovkhoz (쏘-포-즈), Kolkhoz (꼴포-즈), that is, state farms and collective farms, and one more, individual farms.

Takai: Among these, the most distinctive is the Kolkhoz.

Nakharov: That's right. Collective farms are gradually turning into state farms. Why? Because the amount of agricultural produce delivered to the state increases, there's absolutely no surplus production. On the other hand, because they receive distribution of tractors used for cultivation, there's no way to repay the cost of the machinery. As a result, the property of the Kolkhoz is gradually confiscated by the state, and the unique characteristics of the Kolkhoz are completely lost. Thus, what belonged to the Kolkhoz itself is confiscated before one knows it and becomes state-owned. Sovkhozes are mainly managed by people appointed by the state, who employ laborers. And individual farming is like in Japan.

Kwŏn [Chung-il]: I heard that the individual peasants from the old Tsarist era were all liquidated in 1928, and none remain today.

Nakharov: Generally speaking, the process started in 1928, and while not overtly, some still remain. Some manage to survive through connections, like marrying their daughter to a Communist Party member, but even so, they cannot hold important positions in the Kolkhoz and just remain desolately.

Kim [Han-gyŏng]: Are the peasants truly happy?

Nakharov: As individual farmers, their property is confiscated. The Kolkhoz organization pays wages, which was apparently very welcomed by diligent farmers in the past. However, currently, as mentioned before, there is no hope for the future, so Soviet agriculturalists are very disappointed. They say life was better before the revolution.

Pak Tŭk-hyŏn: In that case, what is the difference between the current peasant life and peasant life before the revolution?

Nakharov: In the Tsarist era, there were very large landowners, and peasants were compared to them, so it seemed like there were many unpleasant things, but even then, it was incomparable to the current peasants. <98>

Ivanov: They say it's not even a question.

Nakharov: I cannot provide precise figures here to compare current peasants with Tsarist-era peasants, but based on what I heard from my parents and siblings, during the Tsarist era, before the World War, there was a movement for landowners to distribute land to peasants. So, peasants had the opportunity to acquire land from large landowners and possess their own land and property. Recently, since becoming part of the Kolkhoz, they cannot own their property or land, and due to regulations on regular deliveries, the burden is heavy, making it difficult even to solve the bread problem, let alone clothing.

Takai: Let me add a word here. The Soviet authorities collect barley from peasants at 5 kopecks per kilogram, process it by milling or grinding, and sell it for 1 ruble kopecks to 2 rubles, i.e., 30 to 40 times the price. Even accounting for milling or grinding costs, the price is about 20 times higher. In the case of milk, they collect it at 15 kopecks per liter and sell it as is for 1 ruble 50 kopecks, i.e., 10 times the price, through state stores to the public. The difference accumulates across the entire union, forming half of the Soviet state budget. This shows that the Kolkhoz is nothing more than a means of systematically exploiting peasants. Since peasants are exploited by the government, losing 10 times, or even 30 to 40 times their income, it's obvious that Soviet peasants will fall into poverty.

Pak Yŏng-hŭi: What is the extent of the wealth gap among peasants?

Nakharov: Since the former wealthy peasants (kulaks) were liquidated, there isn't such a striking difference. Only the formerly poor peasants organized the Kolkhozes and were elected as Kolkhoz officials... because only poor peasants were eligible for election. So, those people become Kolkhoz officials and receive some salary, while the rest are merely in a state of starvation.

Reporter: Please tell us truthfully about the food you eat daily. That will give us a general idea.

Ivanov: For breakfast, milk and black bread (껌정팡)... Having milk means you're doing relatively well. Lunch is potatoes. If we can buy meat, we eat it, but now, since we can't raise chickens and cows, we can't eat beef at all. Now, without such meat, <99> we generally mix potatoes and onions* to make cutlets (가*쯔데쯔 - katlety?). With that, soup – also potatoes and cabbage (카*쯔 - kapusta?). Dinner is tea and black bread. Sometimes we eat things like salmon*.
*(TN: The original text has asterisks near '짓*', '가*쯔데쯔', '카*쯔', '연어같은 *을'. These might indicate footnotes, unclear text, or emphasis in the original magazine, possibly referring to specific types of dishes or fish preparations common then.)

Kwŏn [Chung-il]: When I was at the Vladivostok (우라지오) Pacific Labour Secretariat (太平洋 勞働書記局) in 1931-1932, I heard that black bread had disappeared then. Does it still exist?

Ivanov: It's common knowledge for us.

Nakharov: It might have disappeared in the cities for a time, but currently, in the Far East, it's the essential food for Soviet peasants.

Kwŏn [Chung-il]: They were also propagandizing not to eat sugar. Ostensibly, it was a sacrifice for construction, but knowing the situation, it was just a ploy to hide the lack of goods.

Kim [Han-gyŏng]: How much tax do peasants bear?

Takai: Among the taxes peasants pay, the largest is the income tax. Since this is paid according to the income rate*, I don't think I can state it concretely here.
*(TN: Original has 지불*니까, asterisk might denote missing info or footnote).

Nakharov: Besides that, there are over ten types of taxes, like the culture tax.

Takai: There's also the insurance premium.

Nakharov: In addition to taxes, they pay a lot of money for various things. First, they must obligatorily buy bonds. Besides that, there are funds to rescue comrades due to the Spanish War (스페인 전쟁), funds to rescue children in China (支那) suffering due to the incident [Sino-Japanese War], or funds to rescue people in other countries due to revolutions in various nations; there are quite a lot of them.

Pak Tŭk-hyŏn: Then, let me ask one thing. What hope do Soviet youths have?

Nakharov: Firstly, education.

Ivanov: But those who can afford* education** are extremely few.
*(TN: Original has 받을 만한 *, **은 **은 - text seems corrupt or missing).

Kwŏn [Chung-il]: Peasants seem satisfied* with the current government's policies, right?
*(TN: Original has 만족*하는 - asterisk might denote missing info or footnote).

Nakharov: Satisfaction doesn't exist in the Soviet Union. Peasants are by no means satisfied. Before the revolution, even if you say poor people* in the villages had no money, they were only about 15 percent. The other 85 percent lived much better lives than now. But now, 100 percent have become poor. Of course, not a single person is satisfied with the new government's policies. The only ones who could be seen as satisfied are the young Communist Party members in the villages. Those people don't need to participate in the Kolkhoz's physical labor; they just play a role in propaganda or tax collection, <100> so since they avoid physical labor (筋肉勞働), they are satisfied.

**Urban Worker Life**

Kim [Han-gyŏng]: How about urban workers?

Takai: Speaking of urban workers' lives, although the Labor Law limits working hours to 8 hours, one cannot support a family with 8 hours of labor. Even though it's limited, for the sake of their families, they have to extend their work by 3 to 4 hours, working 11 to 12 hours.

Reporter: So they are paid by the amount of work done, not a salary?

Ivanov: Even for a single person, 8 hours of labor is just barely enough to get by.

Kim [Han-gyŏng]: Still, are workers happier in life than peasants?

Ivanov: Of course, workers' lives are better than peasants'. However, it cannot be compared to the labor before the revolution. What seems relatively happy in the Soviets are the office workers (勤務員). Among them, those with education and special skills. Stalin always uses the slogans, "Technology solves everything," and "Cadres decide everything." Therefore, technicians and educated people who fit that description live relatively affluent lives.

Pak Yŏng-hŭi: Given such circumstances, that's what communist society is like.

Reporter: Let me ask frankly. Do you believe that a communist society will be realized?

Nakharov: I don't believe at all that a communist society will be realized. Russia is the best example of this. It's already been 20 years since the revolution in Russia; it seems like a communist society should have been realized by now, but you still can't find a communist society in Russia. In various aspects... failure in the Kolkhozes, failure in planned industry, now it's on the verge of complete collapse.

Ivanov: I don't believe at all that a communist society will be realized.

Kwŏn [Chung-il]: Still, not all Soviet youths think that way, right?

Nakharov: Of course, you can't say everyone thinks that way. But communism is only good for communists. Why? Because by advocating communism, they can obtain good positions and live relatively comfortable lives. So, those people think communism is very good. And the general youth react negatively towards it, <101> thinking that communism is empty. It belongs to the communists, not to us.

**Hearing Examples of Purge Operations**

Reporter: Let's change the subject here and talk a bit about Stalin's purge operations. What is the general atmosphere in Soviet society regarding Stalin's purge operations, such as imprisoning or killing meritorious figures of Soviet construction?

Pak Yŏng-hŭi: Anyway, it seems they are doing things we can't even imagine. Audacious, perhaps?

Nakharov: I will speak only from my impression. Of course, not just me, but anyone would think that it deals a major blow to Russia. However, perhaps Stalin had no other method to take. Because he felt himself to be in danger. And I think it will continue, spreading further and further. And eventually, the Soviets will collapse.

Takai [misspelled as 酒井 Sakai in original, but context implies Takai]: You also served 3 years in prison, right? (Laughter) The fact that Mr. Nakharov was punished seems to have some circumstances that well represent today's Soviets, so let's hear about it.

Nakharov: Just thinking about it makes me disgusted, but I served 3 years of forced labor (强制勞働). Before going to prison, I was working in the dispatch department of a certain store. That store was dominated by three powers: the manager, the Yacheyka (야체-카 - party cell), and the Gepe'u (께페우 - GPU/NKVD). Since I was in dispatch, I would receive an order to send goods somewhere, and just as I was about to send them as ordered, an order would suddenly come from the Gepe'u telling me to send the goods elsewhere. Since it was an order, I did as the Gepe'u ordered. Then, the original intended recipient didn't receive the goods, so it was reported as lost.

Takai: Since the Gepe'u's intervening order was secret, the blame ultimately falls on the dispatch department, right?

Nakharov: Although the goods disappeared, the Gepe'u couldn't be brought to trial, so it became the store's responsibility, and thus the responsibility of the dispatch department that didn't send what should have been sent. Because of that, I was put on trial for causing damage to the state. I was punished at that time for being harmful to the state.

Reporter: Tell us about the trial situation.

Nakharov: The court where I was sentenced was a People's Court (인민재판소). At the trial, <102> I stood before the presiding judge and the court president, and there was also a prosecutor. In addition, there were jurors (배심원) – one of peasant origin, one of worker origin, and besides them, one female clerk. The trial was conducted by this composition. Without much investigation, they just took the written statement, asked a few questions, simply affirmed the statement, and in less than 10 minutes, I received the verdict sentencing me to 3 years in prison and forced labor. And I spent the 3 years of forced labor in Moscow's Mavorga [likely a specific camp/location, transcription might be slightly off].

**Crime, Beggars, Prostitutes**

Reporter: Then, let's change the topic around this point and hear about general social conditions this time.

Pak Yŏng-hŭi: What kind of crime is most common in the Soviets?

Takai: More than anything, it must be the currently fashionable arrests of Trotskyists (트로쯔키스트), right? (Laughter)

Nakharov: It might not qualify as crime, but those who harm the state or whom the state dislikes are all arrested as Soviet criminals under the pretext of being Trotskyists; this is the most common. Next is embezzlement of public funds (公金消費). Other than that, it's pretty much the same. Murder seems common in cities, while theft seems common in rural areas.

Takai: That proves how poor the rural areas are. Let me briefly introduce something here since crime came up. The most common crime in the Soviets now, I think, is the currently fashionable Trotskyist [arrests]. From January 1937 to May 1938, the number of people arrested under that pretext totaled 2,609,000. And according to government data used as the basis for determining the expenses for forced labor camps in this year's budget of the People's Commissariat of Internal Affairs (內務人民委員部), the number of inmates in forced labor camps is 6,112,432, about 10 million people [Note: 6 million is stated, then approx 10 million; possible typo or exaggeration in the original text]. The government likely considers the majority of these as so-called Trotskyists.

Pak Yŏng-hŭi: I heard a lot that Russia eliminated all beggars. Have beggars really disappeared?

Nakharov: Yes, there are no beggars in Russia. Article 35 of the criminal code states that those wandering around without a job are arrested. And people who are unemployed because they have no work are all arrested by the Gepe'u (께배우 - GPU/NKVD) and subjected to about 3 years of forced labor. When that's finished, they are given a job. So, there's no way beggars could exist. (Laughter)

Ivanov: In Russia, you really can't <103> see beggars. But I don't think everyone in Russia lives better than beggars. (Laughter)

Pak Tŭk-hyŏn: I heard rumors that there are quite a few* prostitutes (매음부) in the Soviets!
*(TN: Original has 상당히 *타는 - text seems corrupt or missing).

Ivanov: Russia doesn't have a public prostitution system (公唱制度) like your country.

Takai: There are no licensed quarters (遊廓). So, it's considered that there are no prostitutes at all.

Nakharov: However, as everyone knows, in reality, there are many prostitutes who don't cost money. (Roaring Laughter)

**Divorce is Truly Common in the Soviet Union**

Reporter: By the way, what kind of equality is the gender equality (男女同權) in the Soviet Union? We don't know...

Nakharov: Soviet Russia has literal gender equality. But women don't like gender equality. Why? Because the law recognizes equality, women also have to work. But it's not just about work; the wages are the same too, so since women are weaker, it's quite disadvantageous. In this respect too, women dislike equality.

Takai: Even if women have equal rights, somehow there seems to be a custom of submitting to men, which manifests in the family. Also, women have burdens beyond raising children and managing household affairs, but it seems no benefits or considerations are given to women regarding those aspects under gender equality. They seem to want to return to the past family life rather than such empty gender equality. However, other measures to improve the status of women are seen to be devised in various ways. For example, things like giving special treatment to pregnant women (姙婦), postpartum women (産後婦人), or young children are much advanced.

Pak Yŏng-hŭi: Is marriage entirely love marriage?

Takai: Marriage in Russia is certainly mostly love marriage. However, I think it's somewhat different from what we usually think of as love marriage. I heard an interesting story from Mr. Nakharov. Mr. Nakharov, please share one.

Nakharov: I'm single, so... (Laughter) But there's this story. This might represent love marriage in the Soviets. A son went out alone last night. The next morning, when his mother opened the son's door, there were two people (複數). (Laughter) <104>

Ivanov: I know that legalizing a marriage involves extremely simple procedures, but I haven't done it myself. Love marriage is also something concerning others; I haven't experienced it yet, so I don't know.

Kim [Han-gyŏng]: It seems Soviet women dislike having children. Is it because family life is difficult?

Nakharov: Since divorce between spouses is simple, women might wait for a child to tie down the husband's heart, but on the other hand, perhaps they avoid it due to livelihood issues.

Takai: Divorce was most common around 1929 to 1930.

Nakharov: There were many cases where the husband was forcibly made a Communist Party member, and then the wife initiated divorce due to religious reasons.

Takai: There's a Russian saying, "The relationship between a man and a woman is like a glass of water." It means that men and women can marry and divorce as easily as drinking a glass of water.

Nakharov: Recently, because there are too many divorces, as a measure to prevent this, the procedures have become very cumbersome. First, to get a divorce, previously only one party needed to file, but now agreement is required. And when filing, property registration must be done, and the property must be divided equally between the two. Previously, marriage was also free, but recently, the first time costs 50 rubles, the second time 150 rubles, and so on for the marriage registration fee. So, there are many cases where peasants cannot get married even if they work their whole lives without eating or wearing clothes. This also seems to play a role in preventing divorce.

Pak Yŏng-hŭi: Even if women divorce multiple times, they don't consider it shameful, right?

Ivanov: Of course, they don't think it's shameful.

Takai: In materialist Russia, a woman's reproductive organs are nothing more than a material existence and are not considered mystical as in other countries. From this point of view, no matter how many times one divorces, it's never shameful. That is, the concept of chastity is weak.

Kwŏn [Chung-il]: Isn't it true that Stalin also married multiple times? We once thought Stalin was an absolute existence.

Takai: Let me tell you. Stalin married five times. Among them, his second wife committed suicide, lamenting Stalin's conduct. I heard recently that he's getting divorced again. <105> (Laughter)

Pak Tŭk-hyŏn: In the past, there were supposedly many sexually transmitted diseases (性病) in Russia. How is it now?

Nakharov: Previously, especially because it was a multi-ethnic republic, there were indeed many STDs. However, recently, they have decreased significantly. The criminal code imposes imprisonment of up to six months to three months for infecting others with a disease, so it's gradually decreasing.

**Poor Public Entertainment**

Kim [Han-gyŏng]: What is public entertainment like in the Soviet Union?

Ivanov: There are various things in the city, but in our rural areas, it's mostly just dancing to an accordion (手풍금), and sometimes drinking alcohol on holidays.

Nakharov: Other things may be lacking, but alcohol is supplied well. Therefore, rural entertainment is mostly dancing and drinking.

Kwŏn [Chung-il]: When we were in Russia, most movies were propaganda films about the Five-Year Plan, and the rest were mostly American comedies.

Nakharov: Publicly shown movies previously included German ones, it seems, but now they are mainly American movies.

Pak Yŏng-hŭi: How are the talkie (토-키-) facilities?

Ivanov: In rural areas, most movie theaters lack talkie equipment, and they mainly show propaganda films.

Nakharov: As for anti-Japanese films (排日映畵), recently there seems to be one dealing with Japan's Siberian intervention. Besides that, there are many anti-Japanese films, but they mostly show pictures of Japanese peasant life being harsh or workers' lives being miserable.

Pak Yŏng-hŭi: It's counter-propaganda. Jealousy also plays a role to some extent, leading to reactionary depictions.

Reporter: It seems even the three-star general Lyushkov was surprised by the abundance of goods in Japan. What is the general situation regarding goods in Russia?

Nakharov: Goods in the Soviet Union are generally scarce. Even if things exist, there are many unnecessary items and few necessary ones. There's a lot of vodka, for instance. But things like bread (팡) are scarce, one could say. When I came here, I visited a department store. The goods in that one department store alone are probably more abundant than all the goods in the entire city of Khabarovsk (하바롬스크), and I believe there are more things the public would enjoy.

**Account of the Zhanggufeng Incident**

Reporter: The conversation has become quite long, but let's now move to the Zhanggufeng Incident. I met with foreign correspondents at the site and heard their Q&A, which was quite interesting. The content <106> is mostly known to the world. So, let's address a different issue and discuss why this incident broke out.

Nakharov: After the revolution, Russia intended to occupy Zhanggufeng. However, they couldn't execute this until today. But entering this year, our commanders, under orders from Blyukher (뿌륙헬) and Voroshilov (뽀-로시로프), kept propagandizing that Zhanggufeng was Soviet territory or that the Soviets had already occupied that place. And recently, coordinating with the center, they instigated the incident. Of course, I also think Russia instigated this incident.
At the beginning of the battle, I thought Japan would be completely crushed because they were exhausted from the current China Incident (支那事變). The general public also thought this way. And at the site of Zhanggufeng, there was a strict order from the center: "Do not retreat a single step. If anyone retreats, they will all be shot." Why they retreated, I don't know. Those on the front line thought, "We can't win fighting Japan like this." I believe the commanders also thought there was absolutely no chance of victory.
"The Soviet army does not want to fight not only Japan but any other country. Russia is the most peace-loving country in the world. However, if circumstances force Russia to fight, Russia always has the strength to fight." I learned this kind of thinking while in the army. As written in the first words of the Infantry Drill Manual (步兵操典), the existence of the Russian military is for liberating the world, which simultaneously means the Russian army is ultimately a means to conquer the world. Therefore, it means they have the intention to fight Japan.

Pak Tŭk-hyŏn: Is military service (兵役) in the Soviet Union voluntary or compulsory?

Nakharov: Military service in the Soviet Union is formally voluntary. Even if they conscript, when you go to the conscription examination center (徵兵檢場), as I did, the conscription officer asks, "Will you serve in the military?" And they say, "It's your freedom." But inside that conscription officer's head, the big letters written are "Those who evade military service are traitors to the country (國賊)." Seeing that, everyone volunteers willingly. (Laughter)

Takai: Isn't that also suspicion of being a Trotskyist? (Laughter)

Reporter: Then, the final question. Do both of you have parents or siblings? Or are you single? <107>

Nakharov: I am single. I lost my father, and only my mother is at home. I have one brother and one sister. They all work in the Kolkhoz (꼴-즈).

Ivanov: Both my parents are alive and well. I have two brothers and five sisters.

Takai [Referred to as 古井 (Kojŏng/Furui?) here, likely a typo for 高井 Takai]: It’s already been two months since you defected. Don’t you miss your mother and siblings now?

Nakharov: Lately, I sometimes feel somewhat lonely.

Ivanov: Thinking about it is useless, so I've decided not to think about it.

Reporter: What impressed you the most after coming to Japan? Please answer briefly.

Ivanov: What impressed me most is how extremely peaceful it is despite being wartime, and how what I heard in the Soviet Union – that Japan lacks goods because it's fighting a war, while Russia has abundant goods – was the complete opposite. And one more thing is that Japanese people are very kind.

Reporter: Then, just one last thing. You both were impressed and happy after coming to Japan, finding joy. Isn't it impossible that there was nothing in Russia that impressed you or made you happy? There must have been something.

Nakharov: We might not know much, but perhaps the most impressive thing in the Soviet Union, and maybe happy for some people, is how extremely simple it is to get married*.
*(TN: Original has 결혼*는 것 - asterisk might denote missing info or footnote).

Ivanov: And divorce too, perhaps. (Laughter)

Reporter: An interesting conclusion. Then, let's conclude here. <108>

**Summary**

This 1938 roundtable discussion features two Soviet defectors, officer Nakharov and soldier Ivanov, who fled to Japanese-controlled territory during the Zhanggufeng Incident. They describe harsh conditions in the USSR, citing Kolkhoz oppression, poverty (especially among peasants), pervasive fear of the NKVD (Gepe'u), ineffective and propagandistic education, suppression of religion despite legal guarantees, and widespread dissatisfaction. Nakharov recounts his unjust imprisonment and forced labor experience. Both express disillusionment with the communist system, believing it unrealizable and benefiting only party members. They contrast the scarcity and fear in the USSR with the perceived abundance and peace in Japan (despite the ongoing war), highlighting Soviet propaganda's falsehoods. They attribute the Zhanggufeng Incident's initiation to Soviet leadership.

From a historical perspective, this article is valuable as anti-Soviet propaganda published in colonial Korea under Japanese rule, using defectors' testimonies to portray the USSR negatively. It offers insights into how Japan leveraged such narratives during border conflicts. For historians, the defectors' detailed, albeit potentially biased or exaggerated for the audience, accounts provide glimpses into everyday Soviet life, economic hardships (Kolkhoz system failures, scarcity), social issues (purges, lack of freedoms, divorce rates), and the psychological impact of the regime (fear, disillusionment) in the late 1930s, as well as Soviet perspectives on the Zhanggufeng Incident.

이와노푸. 나는 보병 중기관총대에서 공격명령을 받고 濕世帶를 단숨에 빠져 나왔으나 한때 日本軍의 공격이 어떻게 심한지 스풀 사이에 몸을 숨겼읍니다. 그때 우리 戰友 2명도 있었으나 두사람 다- 日本포탄에 마저서 부상했읍니다. 나만은 幸히 부상하지 않었음으로 이것을 기회로 생각코 어둠을 뚤고 될 수 있는 대로 로시아어가 들니지 않는 塹壕에로 작고 걸어갔읍니다. 한참 가다가도 또 말소리를 간간이 들었읍니다. 그러느라면 또 로시아 말소리가 들님니다. 이건 안되겠다고 또 죽을 힘을 다 내여 걸습니다. 이렇게 해서 드디여 日本陣地를 찻게 되었읍니다. 그로부터 나는 한카치를 꺼내어 흔들면서 제1선을 돌파해 온 것입니다.
