Longing for the Heroines of Beloved Masterpieces

Life's Love and Lotte - The Sorrow of Goethe's Werther
Mo Yunsuk

Werther and Lotte, they are *ones who lost happiness because it could not be*. However, because their desire for happiness was too great and because they knew the logic of undying happiness, they transcended the achievable joy within possible limits.
In the process of transcending this, Werther suffered, wandering through the chains of life's various emotions. Although the editor requested that I write impressions of the heroine, the impression received from the male protagonist was strong from the time I read the work, so I will try to write as Werther's emotional countenance comes to mind. Lotte! She is a beautiful woman. According to reports, this work also originated from Goethe's actual experiences. It is said that Goethe went to visit a friend living nearby, danced the waltz with the friend's wife each time he visited, and in the rhythm of that waltz, the love between the two blossomed<84>.
Whatever its source material, this work cannot but be an eternally immortal, sad life-story, its life extending long and far. Lotte is another's wife and the mother of three children. She is a virtuous woman who, within her quiet home, knows only her husband's heart as her universe and believes in it. More internally than externally, her character was calm and, in a way, asleep. Her life, dormant in her husband's love, could not rest from the moment she sensed an inextinguishable love from the soul of Werther, a man of passion and contemplation. Furthermore, she became aware through Werther of the incompleteness of her own life, which needed to bloom further and flower to be complete. This pairs with the destiny that she must move herself to the closest possible place to Werther. This agony begins to arise from the contradiction with the fundamental principle stemming painfully from the premise that she is another's wife.
Such as the words, "I love Lotte's pure heart. But last night I grieved that Lotte's form was not near me. This thought frequently desecrates my sacred thoughts,"
Or, "Since I love you most in this universe, what contradiction is it that you also have a husband besides me who protects and loves you? What meaning does any existence other than me have for you?" These words too exist, and ultimately, reaching the conclusion that life cannot escape life for Lotte, who thinks and loves only with the spirit, her life, possessing only pure breath, grew weary and collapsed. Lotte, having such an intense object [of affection], felt fear before the passion of life she hadn't fully realized even while acting as another's wife, and wandered, but worried about what Werther's actions might lead to, and in anxiety and impatience, she tried to soothe him like a child and calm his heart.
Lotte believed that with her usual affection and consolation, she could sufficiently calm Werther's sadness and pacify his agony. She endeavored to avoid misunderstanding him and to guide his mind with calm reason, giving hints to bring him peace. I see infinite wisdom and patience in Lotte's method, and a heart intending to purify sorrow and look towards a more distant path. I admire her as a woman of high cultivation. However, one heartbreaking thing is that the consolation and affection shown by Lotte, combining her greatest goodness and beauty, had no effect whatsoever on Werther.
When Werther realized that affection, consolation, or intense kindness had<85> no power to cure his fever, when the sensual power that trampled and **** came ruthlessly swept away and destroyed the substance* of the high ideals he asserted, he was startled himself and took his own life. Within the castle of thought embracing Lotte, he sees not only Lotte's soul moving but also Lotte's [...] existing together as one mass, sometimes **. However, Werther collapses, unable to endure further under the high ethics that prevent him from fully embracing this image that includes even Lotte's flesh.
He preferred to end his life rather than defile the morality of this pure heart. He settled all the confused desires of life with poison. Other things all fall away, and only the single pure flower he sought to elevate rings out victory on eternal ground. Believing this alone would be his eternal satisfaction, he died.
Werther is a sorrowful happy man. He came into the world and departed with a soul heated by extreme love. His counterpart, Lotte, merely feared and defended herself lest Werther's love might harm her*. At the very end, she seemed to understand Werther briefly, but I think Lotte ultimately did not know the agony of Werther's deep, deep love. Wasn't this perhaps the cause that made Werther even sadder?
Werther is eternal. Werther could not love any woman other than Lotte. To be able to love another he considered a great insult. His pain, too, flows for a long, long time in people's hearts.

"Anna," You Are the Heroine of a Love That Cannot Be Lived - Tolstoy's Anna Karenina
Yi Hŏn'gu

I must become "Vronsky" and write a piece longing for "Anna" — "The first time I met 'Anna' was the day the train from 'Petrograd' arrived, dropping my mother off in Moscow. From the moment her small, beautiful hand firmly grasped mine, I adored her."
However, no matter how much I think about it,<86> it seems I am not "Vronsky." The ethics of this land do not allow one to love another man's wife who has a son eight years old (even if such love exists, it cannot be expressed outwardly, nor is there any possibility of it being established). Thinking this way, there is absolutely nothing to write. I lack the courage.
A certain friend of mine sincerely taught me that true love is yearning for another's wife. I deeply felt the profound beauty of life hidden in this view of love.
Not only in the Renaissance era of old, but the Middle Ages regarded this beautiful feeling of love as the highest expression of Beauty and Love. Dante and Petrarch were like this, as were the knights. Through the 18th century and into the 19th, this emotion was highly valued and flowed as the lifeblood of the generation.
And this led us to eagerly read Goethe's "The Sorrows of Young Werther" early on, and eventually to yearn for Madame de Rênal in Stendhal's "The Red and the Black."
Yet our "Anna," colliding with excessively realistic circumstances, had to meet her end on the railway tracks, a victim of the fatalism akin to that "somehow ominous premonition," the miserable scene she witnessed the day she was first captured by affection, while feeling acutely in her heart the anxious psychology of hesitating whether to jump into the water while throwing off her "opera bag" during sea bathing.
Why was "Anna's" life so miserable? Was it because she was a wife and also a mother that she should not have felt love?
When her husband "Karenin" scolds her, saying, "What is it but despicable to gobble up the food your husband provides even after exchanging husband and child for a lover?" or even when faced with a divorce premised on the husband taking custody of the child, we still feel a kind of aversion towards "Anna," who finally becomes a public prisoner of passion.
However, "Anna" loves her child to the end, demonstrating unabated maternal love by seeking out her son while her husband sleeps, and because of this, she doesn't formally marry "Vronsky," tormenting herself tragically. This psychology of "Anna" is perhaps incomprehensible unless one is a woman. And perhaps even more incomprehensible unless one is a mother.
Indeed. It is an extremely subtle emotion unique to women. "If only your love is mine, there is nothing to despise.<87> Rather, I consider my circumstances a source of pride!" Thus Anna demanded "Vronsky's" love more strongly than life itself. Furthermore, while the thought expressed by her husband "Karenin" in his final *ultimatum, calling her a fallen woman devoid of honor, humanity, or religious sense, might be excessive, "Anna's" blind(?) romantic sentiment, seeking only to obey love, is a matter requiring various considerations.
If I were to yearn for a woman like "Anna," thinking realistically, it would surely not be a happy love, and this would have to be, to the very end, a spiritual, sometimes one-sided love unfelt by the object of affection. The love felt by the "demon poet" Baudelaire for Madame Sabatier, the love Balzac felt for Madame Hanska, and the devotion Turgenev offered to Madame Viardot are indeed loves to be eternally praised, yet there may be no love as lonely, unhappy, and empty as such loves. The more devout and sacred the love, the more human emotions and reason can be purified and refined, but this inevitably comes paired with extraordinary pain and agony.
After hearing "Karenin's" weeping plea, "It's fine even if you trample me into the mud," Vronsky returns home alone and thinks, "Perhaps this is when people go mad. Perhaps this is when people commit suicide!"
"I must sleep. I must fall asleep and forget."
After such agony, we can better understand his state of mind as he finally points a pistol at himself.
Ultimately, "Love is tragedy!" To learn through experience (*得하고) and comprehend the truth hidden in this phrase, trampled underfoot on the street, is not an easy task, nor is it something anyone can do. Moreover, at a time when love commonly passes from mouth to mouth like a cheap cup (to use a currently circulating, offensive expression), "Anna" was a woman far too ignorant of the world, and "Vronsky" too could not help but be miserable.
Writing this story, I'm not sure if "Anna" is ultimately a heroine I long for or not, having sympathized with and resented her by turns. However, the final point that must be made is that love must be livable, and that love which cannot be lived is inevitably always a tragedy – this is my own commonplace view of love. And yet, should cowardice and timidity, which prevent one from even extending a hand just because a love is unlivable, be even more contemptible?<88>

Gautier of "The Lady of the Camellias" - The Heroine Appearing in *Tsubaki-hime* (*La Dame aux Camélias*)
Yi Sŏnhŭi

The editor's urging is severe, demanding I write a few words, anything at all, about the heroines appearing in world masterpieces, especially the story of Marguerite Gautier, known as the Lady of the Camellias (*Tsubaki-hime* 椿姬), so I cannot refuse and thus write a few words here.
When I first read "The Lady of the Camellias," I was suddenly captivated, like a child startled by the taste of a delicious sweet, and reread the finished book again and again, here and there. However, in this novel, I actually liked the male protagonist more than the heroine, and even more than that, I marveled at how certain passages of the novel, which the male protagonist writes as if it were his own story, were written so truthfully.
The heroine of this novel is described as a typical Parisian beauty, but for some reason, I cannot feel the same affection for her as I do for the heroines of Russian novels.
She is too refined, too immaculate, like a woman made of glass, and her pure love and her tragedy lack significant darkness.
A tragedy without darkness cannot make one unbearably sad, and the passion pouring forth from it is insufficient. However, when thinking of the Lady of the Camellias, one cannot forget her appearance, beautiful as a masterpiece painting, and her splendid, luxurious figure, riding in a golden carriage after the Opéra-Comique.
Here, Armand, a blond youth still unable to shed his downy feathers, follows her with bloodshot eyes, fixated only on the hem of Gautier's skirt.
Only when this young Armand wanders the streets of Paris, radiating light, and weeps and cries at the feet of the Lady of the Camellias, pleading to become her dog, her slave, does she gain value.
Even if the Lady of the Camellias sells her golden carriage, her cashmere shawl, and her pearl necklace due to debt, and finally dies of consumption in an empty room devoid of people, how can it compare to the sorrow<89> of Armand, who remains to the end, digs up her corpse, and faints?
Afterwards, thinking about how much Armand must have wept in Paris, his heart wrenching in nine bends and turns, now that the Lady of the Camellias was dead and gone, I felt incomparably sorry for the living Armand more than the dead Lady of the Camellias.
In any case, it is a good thing that, thanks to the Lady of the Camellias, the status of the courtesan has been somewhat elevated throughout all ages and places, and the gain is that the city of Paris seems a bit more visible before my eyes because of that beautiful and pitiful heroine.

Cute Miss Hester - Lawrence's "Do You Love?"
Kim Kwangsŏp

When a maiden enters holding flowers, a man often becomes an animal that eats those flowers.
It cannot be helped. If driven by instinct [or curiosity/hunger, 굼굼하면], eating is the animal's nature, and that is passion.
Because of this passion, how many beautifully arranged bouquets are buried in sighs?
From the day a man's arm wraps around a woman's waist, the man generally continues his fall, and the woman whispers, "I didn't know it would be like this!"
Miss Hester, too, as she aged, required a man—or rather, a man sought her out.
Miss Hester trusted and loved the man as much as she did in her childhood. But the man was not like that. And so, in his feelings and method of love, he committed a wrong against Miss Hester. If she had known it would be like this, Miss Hester regretted, she would not have let him closer than a friend.
"Hester, come here and sit on this sofa."
When the man said this, Miss Hester detected baseness in his face, and even the sofa seemed dirty.
The man played the piano, intending to make the room warmer with its sound.
The October night surrounded the room they were in.
Miss Hester, as if slipping out through a crack in the door, ran<90> into this darkness.
She ran with her hair down until she could no longer hear the piano sound, but there was a place where the road was blocked.

"I did not wish to marry anyone. I am unhappy now. I am deformed. Women generally make a business of falling in love. Otherwise, men wouldn't pursue love. Most are ordinary, but I am deformed. I climb trees. I am helpless. Joe (lover) ruins things between us. And yet he wants to marry me. Ah, my heart is stifled. Life is a dirty thing. How much one must hate the unclean! When the sound of the bungalow door closing was heard, tears fell from Miss Hester's eyes. The night air is terribly cold. Miss Hester had no choice but to re-enter the room with the closed door."

After reading D.H. Lawrence's tiny little story "Do You Love?" ["The Lovely Lady" - *likely misremembered or mistranslated title by the author, the plot described closely matches Lawrence's "The Lovely Lady"*], I noted down the following impression:
Love, Body, and Home
If it is common for the man's body to become the woman's grave and the woman's body the man's grave, then dreams revolve outside of that.<91>

**Summary**

This 1938 article features four Korean intellectuals (Mo Yunsuk, Yi Hŏn'gu, Yi Sŏnhŭi, Kim Kwangsŏp) reflecting on iconic female protagonists from Western literature: Lotte from Goethe's *Werther*, Tolstoy's Anna Karenina, Marguerite Gautier from Dumas fils' *La Dame aux Camélias*, and Hester from a D.H. Lawrence story (likely "The Lovely Lady," misidentified as "Do You Love?"). The authors analyze the heroines' complex situations, often focusing on the tragic nature of their love affairs, societal constraints, and the perspectives of the male characters involved. They grapple with themes of passionate versus spiritual love, societal morality (especially regarding married women), female suffering, and the perceived incompatibility of idealized love with real life.

From a colonial history perspective, the article is interesting as it shows Korean intellectuals actively engaging with and interpreting Western literary canon during the Japanese occupation. Their discussions about love, suffering, morality, and the constraints on women, while framed through European characters, subtly reflect the concerns and perhaps the limitations felt within their own colonized society. Yi Hŏn'gu's comment on the impossibility of loving a married woman "in this land" hints at prevailing social norms, while the overall melancholic tone regarding tragic love could resonate with the broader sense of disenfranchisement or constrained possibilities under colonial rule.

그리운 名作의 女主人公들이여
생명의 사랑과 롯테 - 괘-테의 웰텔의 悲哀

여기에 아직도 솜털을 다 못버슨 금발의 청년 알만이 충혈된 눈으로 고-티에의 치마ㅅ자락만 노리고 쪼처 단닌다.
이 알만 청년이 巴里의 거리를 發光해서 도라다니고 春姬에 발 앞에서 개가 되여지이다고 노예가 되여지이다고 울고 부르지즈매 비로소 椿姬는 갑이 있다.
椿姬가 빗에 쪼들여 황금마차를 팔고 가시미야숄-을 팔고 진주 목거리를 팔고 나종엔 사람 하나 없는 빈 방에서 폐병으로 임종을 했대야 끗까지 남어 있어 椿姬에 시체를 파보고 기절하는 알만에 슲음에＜89＞ 비길 배 되랴.
나는 그후 알만이 椿姬가 죽고 없어진 巴里에서 얼마나 九曲肝臟을 썩이며 울었을까를 생각하니 죽은 椿姬보다도 산 알만이 견딀 수 없이 딱했다.
어째든 椿姬로 말미아마 동서고금에 娼婦에 지위가 일단 놉하진 것은 좋은 일이며 그 아름답고 가련한 여주인공 때문에 巴里에 都城이 좀더 눈앞에 보히는 것 같은 것이 수확이다.

貴여운 헤스터孃 로렌-스의 사랑하느냐
金珖燮

한 처녀가 꽃을 들고 들어오면 한 사나이는 각금 그 꽃을 먹는 동물이 되는 일이 만타.
할 수 없는 일이다. 굼굼하면 동물은 먹는 것이 본능이니까 그리고 그것이 정열이니까.
이 정열 때문에 아름답게 짜여진 꽃다발들이 얼마나 탄식에 파뭇치느냐.
사나이의 팔이 여자의 허리에 감긴 날부터 사나이는 대개 타락을 계속하고 여자는 「그럴 줄은 몰랐는데!」 하고 속삭인다.
헤스터孃도 나이들게 되니 사나이를 요구했다는 것보다 사나이가 찾어 왔다.
헤스터孃은 그 사나이를 어릴 적 때만큼 밋고 사랑했다. 그러나 사나이는 그러칠 안었다. 그래서 사랑하는 감정과 방법에 있어서 그는 헤스터孃에 대해서 잘못을 범했다. 그럴 줄을 알았든들 헤스터孃은 그를 동무 이상으로 갓가히 하잔었을 것을 하고 후회하였다.
헤스터 이리와서 이 소파에 안게나.
사나이가 이렇게 말할 때 헤스터孃은 그의 얼골에서 비열함을 차저냈고 심지어 그 소파까지 더러워 보였다.
사나이는 피아노를 처서 그 소리로 방안을 더 후눈하게 하고저 했다.
10월의 밤이 그들이 있는 방을 둘러쌓고 이섯다.
헤스터양은 어느새 문틈으로 새여 나가드시 이 어둠 속에 다름질＜90＞  첬다.
그 피아노 소리가 들리지 안는 곳까지 머리를 풀어 헤치고 달었으나 길이 막히는 데가 있었다.

나는 누구하고도 결혼하고저 하잔었다. 나는 지금 불행하다. 나는 畸形的이다. 여자란 대개는 연애에 빠지는 것을 장사로 한다. 그러치 않으면 남자들은 연애를 하지 안을 거시다. 대개는 보통인데 나는 畸形的이다. 나는 나무에 올은다. 나는 가이없다. 죠오(애인)가 우리 사이를 망처 놋는다. 그리면서 결혼할냐고 한다. 아, 가슴이 매킨다. 인생이란 더러운 것이로구나. 불결한 것을 얼마나 미워해야 하나! 반가로-의 문이 닷치는 소리가 들일 때 헤스터孃의 눈에서 눈물이 떠러젔다. 밤 공기가 몹시 차다. 헤스터孃은 다시 그 문이 닷친 방으로 들어갈 수밖게 없었다.

D.H. 로-렌스의 이 조고만한 소설 「사랑하느냐」를 읽고 난 다음 나는 다음과 같은 感想을 적어 두었다.
