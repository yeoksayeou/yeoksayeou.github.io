Film Special Feature, Border Filming Chronicle, *From the Land of Happiness, Ten Thousand Ri* Musan Letter
						Sim Yŏng

Brother R,

Autumn in Sŏul also knows how to deepen.
I am always concerned about how your health has been lately. I meant to send a letter, but feared I would steal your precious time, so I stopped my brush. The memory of exchanging talk with you about the new drama and cinema of this land at H Restaurant the day before I left Sŏul now... (Following 6 lines illegible.)
There are things to do, there is comfort, perhaps this is what it means to be human. Please send lots of good news.
As for *Pokchi Malli* (福地萬里 - A Land of Happiness, Ten Thousand Ri), we have now barely come 2,000 *ri*, so to cover the full ten thousand *ri*, we still have four-fifths further to go, but surely we will break through the remaining 8,000 *ri* by next spring.

The filming during the first course of one month in Musan was truly a "life-or-death struggle." This phrase, "life-or-death struggle" (죽을 내기), is a slogan coined by Chu In-gyu, meaning "desperate" (必死的). The approximately 2,000 members of the location crew are, quite literally, engaged in a "life-or-death struggle" every day.<170> The activities of each department, from directing to acting, are moving most faithfully under the command of Director Chŏn, like officers and soldiers who have come to a battlefield. I received a new impression, one I hadn't tasted in this field for nearly 10 years, and felt how great the orderly power of the ranks is. Of course, since you have read the *Pokchi Malli* scenario, I trust you know it well, but I think the success or failure of this work lies in the controlled collective strength (全體力) of us all. It's not about one actor making a beautiful gesture or one cameraman showing a good camera angle, but I believe we must realize the relationship between real life and artistic creation. Because of that, as you know, Brother, our group is quite strict about regulating the private lives of all members.

From wake-up and sleep times, outings, and abstaining from alcohol (abstaining from smoking isn't being well practiced, which is troublesome), even down to matters of nutrition, we do not neglect monitoring and reflection.

Brother R,
I am overflowing with great hope. Please rejoice for me. Regardless of whether this *Pokchi Malli* has market value in the future or not, I am joyfully growing stout just being together, living and sleeping, in this kind of spiritual atmosphere. However, in truth, I've recently lost about 500 grams. As it's a life-or-death situation, my scholar's hands have developed large calluses from carrying machinery and equipment weighing several tons every day, and my skin has become dark from the autumn sun. As you may have seen in the newspaper the other day, when the crane (기중기) overturned, cameraman Yi Myŏng-u, holding the camera weighing over 10 *kwan* (approx. 37.5 kg) to his chest, fell from a height of 5 or 6 meters and injured his chest, and Kang Hong-sik and Song Ch'ang-gwan were injured trying to block it with their bodies. They performed the aforementioned "life-or-death struggle." We who were standing nearby could not stop the hot tears that flowed unknowingly.

Brother R,
If only the film equipment in this land were a little more sufficient, wouldn't this kind of danger have been avoided?
That day too was a day when we looked up at the sky, which alternated between cloudy and clear, feeling very anxious, and skipped lunch to save the remaining time. This place is a high mountainous area, so low pressure is always coming from the direction of Paektu-san (白頭山) to the west. Due to the weather, we fell terribly behind schedule, but unlike past filmmaking practices, abandoning the evil practice of [*maniaŭi sae* - likely "making do" or rushing], Director Chŏn Ch'ang-gŭn's dedication to truthfully filming even just one cut a day makes my head bow automatically,<171> and when I clench my fist thinking, "This is how it should be done," my heart just overflows.

Brother R,
Will even you, Brother, understand that the traces of our one month, and the "life-or-death struggle" we will walk until next spring, will emanate like tracks of blood upon the screen of *Pokchi Malli*? I want to share these kinds of stories with you.

I have become completely a free laborer of Musan. Even mingling among the hundreds of local laborers here, my appearance, at first glance at least, is not inferior to theirs.
Some time ago, when I went to the barbershop, the barber asked awkwardly, "Aren't you perhaps just out of that detention center?" and sometimes when I enter a shop to buy something, I receive treatment like, "Are you a worker hired by Hazama-gumi (間組)? I'll sell it cheap, please buy our goods." Feeling triumphant, I looked down again at my attire. I had agonized greatly over portraying my laborer role. Before becoming a laborer in appearance only, I tried to become the person of the role, the inner form (內面的 形象), and went out to the filming site every day, not sparing my strength in carrying the heavy crane. For the first time in my life, I carried a wooden beam (목도) on my shoulder, chanting "Hiyŏng hayŏng, hayŏng," and pushed myself hard. As a result, being originally of a scholar's constitution, I harmed my health and was bedridden for 6 or 7 days. When diagnosed with "Typhoid" (膓疾扶斯), everyone was startled and anxious! My fever fluctuated around 39.7-8 degrees Celsius, and my condition was not minor, causing everyone to whisper amongst themselves! There was even discussion of hospitalizing me at the Nanam Provincial Hospital (羅南道立病院). Director Chŏn came to my bedside to offer comfort. How many times I bit my lip. From the day I fell ill, Song** of the medical department and the new actor ** lost their appetite and sleep, administering injections like "Rosénon" (?) or "Bagnon" (?) that had been prepared, and devoted all their energy to nursing me.
"The sincere love of friends," which I had never received before—I muttered countless times in my mouth that I would be content even if I died. Fortunately, when I recovered after a week and realized it was a misdiagnosis, the joy was incomparable, and the gloomy crew members suddenly became cheerful.

Brother R,
I thought about it. I keenly realized how much sorrow the misfortune of one individual causes to a group of dozens, and how heavy that responsibility is. It is regrettable that my writing talent is too poor to record my state of mind at that time here.<172>(Continued from page 169)
(Continued from page 172)
Finally, I will describe the local customs here that cannot be seen in Sŏul. Especially the lives of the women in this region are something unseen in Sŏul; strolling the streets, I was astonished by the women's tenacious vitality. Husbands work as husbands do, and wives as wives do; their diligent manner is worlds apart (雲泥之差) compared to the so-called idle wives (有閑夫人) of Sŏul, who just sponge off their husbands' salaries, sit around reeking of powder amid luxury and negligence. There isn't a woman here who doesn't carry luggage on her head; seeing them driving oxcarts (소달구지) or sitting in the marketplace selling peppers, garlic, sesame seeds, fruit, etc., seemed enviable and reliable compared to the women of Sŏul. I think all these scenes of life are one of the good gifts I will take back to my wife. This is not boasting (ノロケ - *noroke*). It is a true feeling (眞感).

Please always take care of your frail health.

Early 11th Month, Year of the Tiger [1938]
Leaving Musan<169>

**Summary**

This article is a personal letter from the actor Sim Yŏng to an acquaintance ("Brother R") written in late 1938 while filming the movie *Pokchi Malli* near Musan on the Korean border. Sim describes the physically demanding and dangerous conditions on set, referring to the work as a "life-or-death struggle." He recounts a crane accident injuring crew members, his own serious illness (misdiagnosed as typhoid), and the strong sense of camaraderie and collective effort under Director Chŏn Ch'ang-gŭn. He contrasts the harsh realities of filming with the artistic commitment of the team and reflects on the hardworking lives of local Musan women compared to the perceived idleness of upper-class women in Sŏul. The letter conveys both the hardships and the profound sense of purpose Sim felt participating in this major film project.

For historians, this letter offers a valuable first-hand account of filmmaking conditions in late 1930s colonial Korea, highlighting the physical risks, logistical challenges (primitive equipment, difficult terrain, weather dependence), and the intense, almost militaristic, group discipline ("collective strength," "regulation of private lives") employed on a large-scale production like *Pokchi Malli*. The mention of Japanese companies (Hazama-gumi) and the film's theme itself (implicitly promoting settlement in Manchuria, aligning with colonial policy) provide context to the socio-political environment influencing cultural production. Sim's social commentary comparing Sŏul and Musan women also reflects contemporary perceptions of urban versus rural life and changing gender roles.
