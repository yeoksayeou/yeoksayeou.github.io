Publication Date: 1938년 12월 01일
Article Title: 長篇作家 訪問記(1), 健筆 10年의 張赫宙氏 (Interview with a Novelist (1), Mr. Chang Hyŏk-chu of the Vigorous Pen for 10 Years)
Author: 一記者 (One Reporter)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Discussion)

Interview with a Novelist
						One Reporter
On the 26th, the 20th day of the New Cooperative Theater Troupe's (新協劇團) Chosŏn performance of "Ch'unhyangjŏn" (春香傳), I met the original author, Mr. Chang Hyŏk-chu (張赫宙), who came with them, at the Samjung Inn (三重旅舘). If I were to describe my impression of him in one word here, it would be that he was extremely gentle and soft.
I immediately wanted to ask this gentle and soft Mr. Chang, "Did you show people such a gentle impression even when you were writing 'Path of the Hungry Ghost' (餓鬼道) or 'The Pursued People' (쫓기는 사람들)?" but I changed the subject to avoid awkwardness between us and asked him to speak about the difference between the time he wrote his early works, namely "Path of the Hungry Ghost" or "The Pursued People," and his current work tendencies.

1. Progression of Work Tendency
Chang: Let me see. If we talk about early works, it would be "Path of the Hungry Ghost" and "The Pursued People." The tendency of my works from when I wrote these up until <190> now has completely changed. If "Path of the Hungry Ghost" and "The Pursued People" are my early works, then "A Man Named Kwŏn" (權이라는 男子) and those after it would be my first period works. That is to say, in the early works—"Path of the Hungry Ghost," "The Pursued People"—it was mainly from a desire to tell the story of the lives of Chosŏn people. And another ambition was to let others know that Chosŏn people also have talent, but as I gradually continued writing, that feeling disappeared, and now I have only the desire to create true art.

Reporter: What was the motivation?
Chang: There wasn't any particular motivation; after "Path of the Hungry Ghost" and "The Pursued People" were published, the Tokyo literary critics said things like, "Chang's novels are merely reports on life, lacking artistic beauty," and although I listened to their criticisms, I also realized that true art must not be just depicting life. So, a year after writing "The Pursued People" and "Path of the Hungry Ghost," from the time I started writing "A Man Named Kwŏn," I purely intended to depict human beings, never forgetting that. In other words, shall we say my work tendency reached a philosophical stage from that point on?

Reporter: So, your work tendency changed right after publishing "Path of the Hungry Ghost" and "The Pursued People"?
Chang: Until I published "A Man Named Kwŏn," I didn't write anything at all in between and thought about various things for about a year. I spent a whole year thinking about how to decide on my creative attitude. Because of that, in the Chosŏn literary world, there was a fuss, saying things like, "Chang Hyŏk-chu has reached a dead end (유끼즈마리 - yukizumari)," or "He quit because he lacks talent," but when "A Man Named Kwŏn" was published, the Tokyo literary world went wild. From then on, through works like "Prostitute" (갈보), "The Man Visiting a Grave" (參墓하는 男子), "Night of the 16th" (16日夜), up to recent works like "Alley" (路地) and "Atmosphere" (雰圍氣), I have continuously pursued my philosophy. While other works weren't particularly criticized, "A Man Named Kwŏn" and "Prostitute" received very high praise in the Tokyo literary world. Some critics even said that current writers in the Tokyo literary world couldn't possibly write something like that even if they tried their hardest. It seems they were heavily criticized in the Chosŏn literary world, though. They gossiped, saying I exposed the shameful aspects of Chosŏn people, or something like that, but I merely truthfully narrated the subtle psychological states between people and the customs of human feeling...

Reporter: Regardless of the opinions of critics or the literary world, isn't it enough if the writer themself is satisfied?

2. Writers and Criticism
Chang: Still, is it ever easy for a person to be like that? If others say it's good, you feel happy; if they say it's bad, you feel unpleasant and anxious, <191> don't you? If it's guidance from a proper critic, there's nothing more educational and daunting than that. I take guidance from people like Mr. Kobayashi Hideo (小林秀雄氏), Mr. Moriyama Kei (森山啓), and Mr. Kamagawa Tsurujirō (窯川鶴次郞氏) into consideration to some extent. Recently, Mr. Kobayashi pointed out that my works contain quite a bit of playful writing (戱作), so I plan to cut that out from now on. Among Chosŏn critics, I also acknowledge people like Kim Mun-jip (金文輯君). His eye for evaluating works is sharp, unmatched by anyone. He's a man with 'intuition' (勘 - kan), so to speak. But the one thing I detest is that damn personal feeling (私感). If it weren't for that, and he just went by what he sees, he'd be excellent. I met him just yesterday and told him that directly; he blinked his eyes and asked if that was so, saying he'd be careful from now on, but who knows how it will turn out... Critics should possess higher and deeper sensibility and literary cultivation than writers, but since they generally lack that, they always fail to see the work correctly and pick out something completely different from what the writer intended – and usually, it's insults rather than praise. It cannot but be embarrassing for the critics themselves to make such pronouncements. Whether a work is good or bad, understanding the writer's intention and pointing out the strengths and weaknesses to teach them would be such a good thing. However, most critics in the Chosŏn literary world don't seem to be like that, which is disappointing.

Long Novel "Katō Kiyomasa"
Reporter: Aren't you writing any long novels after "Rainbow" (무지개)?
Chang: I'm planning to serialize it in Mojie [Magazine Name? possibly 'Modean Ilbo' or another publication starting with '모지'] starting from the New Year. I've written about 250 pages already and brought it with me; I just need to write another 400 pages.
Reporter: What kind of content is it?
Chang: I intend to write about the culture of the late Yi Dynasty period. Chosŏn culture reached its peak during that era.
Reporter: What is the title?
Chang: I titled it "Katō Kiyomasa" (加藤淸正).
Reporter: "Katō Kiyomasa"?
Chang: I named it that because I decided to primarily write about the lives of Katō Kiyomasa's soldiers at that time.
Reporter: So it will be about 650 pages long?
Chang: That's right. It won't be that long. Something shorter seems better than something too long.
Reporter: Do you have to send the entire manuscript finished for magazine or newspaper serializations?
Chang: Sometimes I write and send the whole thing, and sometimes I write each installment, but for this "Katō Kiyomasa," I will return and write the remaining 400 pages completely before submitting it.
Reporter: When will you be returning?
Chang: This Chosŏn performance tour covers seven places in total, so if I follow them to each location, <192> I'll probably return around mid-November. After returning, I'll have to write diligently every day to make it in time for the New Year issue...
Reporter: How many pages do you write per day?
Chang: I write ten pages.
Reporter: From what time to what time?
Chang: I wake up at six, take a walk, eat breakfast, and after doing this and that, from ten o'clock until 2 PM, I definitely sit down and only write the manuscript.
Reporter: You write that much in that time?
Chang: The time spent thinking is longer than the time spent writing. To create one piece, I think about it for a year, or two or three years, sometimes even four or five years. So, the preparation time takes much longer than the writing time.

3. The Current Tokyo Literary Scene
Reporter: Who are the most active figures in the current Tokyo literary scene?
Chang: Among critics, there are Kobayashi Hideo (小林秀雄), Moriyama Kei (森山啓), Kamagawa Tsurujirō (窯川鶴次郞). Among writers, there are Hozumi (?) (保出), Asano Akira (淺野晃), Hayashi Fusao (林房雄), and among the rising talents, Araki (荒木), Yuasa (湯淺), Ishikawa (石川), Takami (高見), Akita (秋田). They are all likely very capable individuals.
Reporter: Which female writers are popular?
Chang: Among the rising talents, Ōtani Fujiko (大谷藤子), Usuda Tsuyoko (?) (失田津世子), etc., Hayashi Fumiko (林芙美子), and others are popular.
Reporter: Does Uno Chiyo (宇野千代氏) not write anymore?
Chang: Since she became engrossed in the magazine "Style" (スタイル), she hasn't been writing much. It seems she wrote something for Kaizō (改造) recently. She appears to have considerable talent... but she just doesn't write. Ms. Hayashi Fumiko is truly interesting. She's a good person, and her house is quite nice too. The rent is supposedly 80 yen per month.
Reporter: How much are the manuscript fees for such writers, generally per page?
Chang: People like Ms. Yoshiya Nobiko (吉屋信子氏) receive 80 yen per day's installment for newspapers. Ms. Hayashi doesn't get that much. It's probably the same everywhere, but talented writers aren't necessarily popular. Compared to Ms. Yoshiya, Ms. Hayashi might be much better, but the manuscript fees are higher [for Yoshiya].
Reporter: How much do you receive, Mr. Chang?
Chang: It's very little. It amounts to about 3 or 4 yen per day's installment.
Reporter: Still, can writers in Tokyo make a living from manuscript fees?
Chang: Not really. Some might, but it's generally difficult. It's especially hard for critics. Recently, Mr. Akita Ujaku (?) (秋田羽雀氏) mentioned that he had been wearing the same suit for decades, and it was all worn out. I had a bitter smile about it, but truly, the life of a writer in Tokyo is insignificant. Mr. Akita <193> is nevertheless a veteran of the literary world, isn't he? Yet he still lives in poverty. Really, if you look at that suit, it's all falling apart. He says the manuscript fees barely cover the cost of rice.
Reporter: What about the proletarian writers? What are they doing now?
Chang: People like Shimaki Kensaku (島木健作), Hayashi Fusao (林房雄)—their creative stances have all changed. They've all adapted towards Japanism (日本主義), so to speak.

4. Who Are Your Close Friends?
Reporter: Who are your close friends currently in Tokyo?
Chang: Araki (荒木), Yuasa (湯淺), Hayashi Fusao (林房雄), Murayama (?) (村山), Hozumi (?) (保高 - likely 保出 earlier?), Takami (高見), etc.
Reporter: How do you spend time when you meet your friends?
Chang: Mostly we drink alcohol, talk loudly, and have fun. Sometimes we talk about literature. Drinking is truly enjoyable. When I'm alone, I'm very quiet and melancholic, but when I drink, I become quite loud. That's why people who only see me drinking and having fun might think I'm a frivolous man. However, when I'm walking alone through the bushes, I truly dislike even breathing. I want to sit still for hours and just think.

5. View on Love
Reporter: What do you think about during those times?
Chang: Nothing much, I just sit there. But it's not like I sit there without any thoughts either. I feel like crying for no reason...
Reporter: Still, there must be a reason. Would you feel like crying without cause?
Chang: That's a habit of my nature. It's not because I have unforgettable sad memories like other people.
Reporter: Then perhaps you were thinking about someone you love?
Chang: Not at all. It's probably due to the loneliness of someone who has left their hometown, along with various other melancholic things and an innately possessed habit.
Reporter: Would you mind telling us your view on love?
Chang: My view on love? I believe love should exist in life, but I reject love-supremacism (戀愛至上主義). In other words, love that ruins society and oneself because of it is utterly detestable. If love doesn't make one beautiful and sound, it must be a lie.
Reporter: Are you engaged in such love now?
Chang: No. I've already lost interest in any kind of love. Around the age of 20, I thought love was the one and only thing. Now, I don't think so. It seems there's no partner with whom I could have true love. So, I just give up on it. When I observe modern love closely, it seems purely egoistic. Rather than engaging in such love, a platonic friendship between a man and a woman seems more beautiful.

6. Hobbies and Recreation <194>
Reporter: What do you do for recreation?
Chang: Rather than recreation, I spend a lot of time reading books, and I enjoy watching movies. And I like taking walks alone.

7. Recent Reading Tendencies
Reporter: What kind of books do you read?
Chang: I read American novels.
Reporter: What did you read before?
Chang: Before, I read a lot of French works. Especially Balzac's works, I've read almost all of them. But recently, I've grown quite fond of American novels. Stalin apparently said that he thought Russian novels were the best, but American novels could compare to Russian novels. It's true. Recently, American novels are popular yet not vulgar at all. I read the novel "Gone with the Wind" (바람과 함께 가 버리다) a few days ago and was truly moved.

8. Chosŏn Classical Novels and the Current Literary Scene
Reporter: What do you think of Chosŏn classical novels?
Chang: I don't want to evaluate classical novels highly. They don't have much artistic beauty, do they? "Ch'unhyangjŏn" seems somewhat decent because it has songs, but the novel itself isn't much. Rather, "Lady Sa's Southward Journey" (謝氏南征記) seems a bit better.
Reporter: So you're saying modern novels are much better?
Chang: That's right. Much better.
Reporter: Which writer's works do you like?
Chang: I haven't read much, so I don't know well, but I've read most of Mr. Yi Kwang-su's (李光洙氏) novels. Although contradictions are often found while reading, he is probably the only one who occasionally shows sublime descriptions and great, broad, deep aspects. It's just as pointed out in Kim Tong-in's (金東仁氏) "Study of Ch'unwŏn" (春園硏究). "Study of Ch'unwŏn" is truly a masterpiece.

9. Royalty Fee for "Ch'unhyangjŏn"?
Reporter: How much did you receive as a royalty fee for the performance of "Ch'unhyangjŏn"?
Chang: Not much. The New Cooperative Troupe (新協) is poor, so even the actors' salaries range from 25 yen to 90 yen at most. That's why, until now, they couldn't stage original works at all and only did translated pieces. Often, they performed works written by members of the troupe. For this Chosŏn performance tour covering seven *places, I received a total of 50 yen. In Tokyo, it was also 50 yen; in Osaka and Kyōto, 30 yen. If it gets restaged, another 30 yen, that's all. For a large theater, it could be several hundred yen to several thousand yen. Anyway, I'm glad that "Ch'unhyangjŏn" has been very well received this time. It's said to be sensational not just in Tokyo but also in Chosŏn. I heard it was a full house last night too. The book version also sold well. Yesterday, a place called "Taehaksa" (大學社) offered to publish my novels written in the national language [Japanese], and I agreed. They say they'll give a considerable royalty too.
Reporter: From "Taehaksa" in Seoul?
Chang: Yes, that's right. <195>

**Summary**

This 1938 interview with Korean novelist Chang Hyŏk-chu, conducted during his visit to Korea with a Japanese theater troupe performing his adaptation of "Ch'unhyangjŏn," reveals his literary evolution. He discusses moving from depicting Korean life ("Path of the Hungry Ghost," "The Pursued People") towards a more philosophical, universal exploration of humanity ("A Man Named Kwŏn"), partly influenced by Japanese critics like Kobayashi Hideo. Chang comments on the Tokyo literary scene, naming prominent Japanese writers and critics, discusses his meager writing income despite recognition in Japan, notes the shift of former proletarian writers towards "Japanism," expresses his writing habits, his preference for solitary walks and American novels (mentioning "Gone With the Wind"), his reserved view on love, and his critical stance on classical Korean novels compared to modern ones like those by Yi Kwang-su. Historically, the interview is significant as it portrays a Korean writer navigating the complexities of the colonial period, achieving success within the Japanese literary establishment while facing criticism from some Koreans. His comments on Japanese critics, writers, meager pay, the pressure to adapt ("Japanism"), and the publication of his Japanese-language works by a Seoul publisher ("Taehaksa") offer valuable insights into the cultural and political dynamics affecting Korean intellectuals under Japanese rule.

長篇作家 訪問記
						一記者
新協劇團의 「春香傳」 朝鮮公演 제 20일이든 지난 26일에 나는 그들과 함께 오신 원작자 張赫宙씨를 三重旅舘에서 많났다. 氏의 인상을 여기에 한마디로 적는다면 氏는 몹시 상양하시고 부드러웠다는 것.
나는 이 부드럽고 상양하신 張氏에게 「餓鬼道」나 「쫓기는 사람들」을 쓸 지음에도 그렇게 부드러운 인상을 사람에게 보여 주섰읍니까 하고 당장 뭇고 싶었으나 다시 말을 돌여서 피차에 어색함이 없도록 초기작품 즉 「餓鬼道」나 「쫓기는 사람」들을 쓰든 때와 지금의 작품경향을 말슴해 달나고 이렇게 청을 했다.

1, 作品 傾向의 進展
張. 글세요. 초기의 작품이라면 「餓鬼道」「쫓기는 사람들을 들것인데 이것들을 쓸 때와 지금에＜190＞ 이르기까지」의 작품경향이 전연 달너젔다고 할 것입니다. 「餓鬼道」 「쫏기는 사람들」 이것을 초기에 작품이라고 한면 「權이라는 男子」로부터 그 이 後의 것은 제1기의 것이라고 하겠읍니다. 말하자면 초기의 ― 「餓鬼道」 「쫓기는 사람들」에 있어서는 주로 朝鮮사람의 생활을 이얘기해 보자는 그런 마음에서였읍니다. 그리고 또 한가지 야심은 朝鮮사람도 재조가 있다는 것을 남에게 알여보자는 것이였는데 차츰 글을 써 오는 사이에 그런 마음이 없어지고 지금은 오직 참된 예술을 창조해 보자는 마음뿐으로 변했읍니다.
記者. 어떤 동기에서요?
張. 별 동기랄 것은 없구요, 「餓鬼道」나 「쫓기는 사람들」이 발표된 뒤에 東京文壇 평론가들의 말인 즉 張의 소설은 生活報告 뿐이지 예술미가 없다구 욕들을 하는 것에도 귀를 기우렸겠지만 내 생각에도 참된 예술이란 단지 생활을 그리는 것뿐이 아닐 것이라고 깨달었읍니다. 그래서 「쫓기는 사람들」 「餓鬼道」를 쓴 뒤 1년만에 「權이라는 男子」를 쓸 때부턴 순전히 인간을 그려보겠다는 것 잊지 않었읍니다. 말하자면 그때부터 내 작품경향은 철학적 경지에 이르렀다고 할까요.
記者. 그러니까 「餓鬼道」 「쫓기는 사람들」을 발표한 뒤 작품경향이 곳 달너지섰군요?
張. 「權이라는 男子」를 발표하기까진 그 사이에 도모지 쓰지 않고 한 1년 여러가지로 생각했지요, 내 창작태도를 어떻게 결정할까 하는 것을 1년 동안이나 생각했든 것입니다. 그렇자니까, 朝鮮文壇에선 인제 張赫宙는 「유끼즈마리」를 했‹¤느니 재주가 없어서 그만 쏙 들어갔다느니 하고 야단들이였지요 마는 「權이라는 男子」가 발표되자 東京文壇에선 아주 야단들이였었어요. 그로부터 「갈보」 「參墓하는 男子」 「16日夜」 등에서 최근의 작품 「路地」 「雰圍氣」에 이르기까지 쭉 내 철학을 경주해 온 것입니다. 다른 것도 별로 욕먹은 것은 없지만 「權이라는 男子」와 「갈보」는 東京文壇에 매우 호평이었어서요. 어떤 평론가들은 東京文壇의 현작가들은 꺽구로서도 못 써낼 것이라는 말까지 했으니까요. 朝鮮文壇에선 욕들을 많이 한 모양이드군요. 朝鮮사람의 추태를 들추어 냈다나 어쨋다나, 하고들 – 들어답디다 마는 나는 단지 사람과 사람사이의 미묘한 심리상태와 인정 풍습을 진실하게 이얘기 한 것 뿐인데...
記者. 평론가나 문단의 여론이야 어쨋든 작가 자신의 마음에 불만이 없으면 그만 아니겠읍니다.

2, 作家와 評論學
張. 그래도 어디 사람이란 그렇게 되기 쉬운 건가요. 남이 좋다면 즐겁구요 나쁘다면 불쾌하고 불안한 것이＜191＞ 아님니까, 정당한 평론가의 지도라면 또 그것처럼 공부되고 무서운 것은 없으니까요. 小林秀雄氏 같은 분이나 森山啓 窯川鶴次郞氏 등의 지시는 어느 정도까지 참고하게 됩니다. 요새 와선 내 작품속에 戱作의 성질이 다분히 있다고 小林씨가 指摘해 주어서 인제부터는 그것을 도려내 버릴 작정입니다. 朝鮮評論家 중에도 金文輯군 같은 사람은 보아 주었읍니다. 그의 작품 보는 눈은 누가 따를 수 없도록 예리하지요. 말하자면 「勘」이 있는 남자지요. 그런데 똑 한가지 질색이 그놈의 私感이거든요, 그렇지만 않고 그대로 제보는 그대로만 나간다면 훌융해요. 내 어적게도 맛나서 직접 그런 말을 해줬드니 눈을 끔벅거리며 그런냐고 인제부턴 주의하겠느라고 하두구만 또 어떨지... 평론가란 작가보다 더 높고 깊은 감각과 문학적 교양을 가저야 할 것인데 도모지 그렇지 못 하다보니 항상 작품을 바로 보지 못하고 작가의 이얘기한 것과는 아주 달는 것을 집어내서 ― 그것도 칭찬보다 늘 욕설이지요 ― 이러니 저러니 한다는 것은 評家 자신이 붓그런 일의 아닐 수 없읍니다. 잘됐든 못됐든 작가의 의도를 알고 잘되고 못된 것을 지적해서 가르처 준다면 얼마나 좋은 일일지 모르지요, 그러치만, 대개의 朝鮮文壇 평론가들은 그렇지 못한 것 같애서 섭섭합니다.

記者. 언제 도라가시게 되나요?』
張. 이번 朝鮮公演이 전부 일곱곳인데 그곳마다 다 따라다니느라면＜192＞ 11월 중순에나 도라가게 되겠읍니다. 도라가서부터는 매일 부즈런히 써야 신년호에까지 마니아우 할 것인데...

3, 東京 文壇의 現在
記者. 現在 東京文壇에서 가장 활약을 많이 하는 이들은 대개 어떤 분들입니까?
張. 평론가로서는 小林秀雄, 森山啓, 窯川鶴次郞들이고 작가로는 保出 , 淺野晃, 林房雄 등이고 신진으로선 荒木湯淺, 石川高見, 秋田 등인데 모다 실력있는 분들일걸요.
記者. 여류문인으로선 어떤 분이 인기 있읍니까.
張. 신진 大谷藤子 失田津世子 等 林芙美子, 諸氏가 인기 있읍니다.
記者. 宇野千代씨는 안 씀니까?
張. 「스타일」이란 잡지에 골돌하면서부터는 잘 쓰지 않드니 요새 改造에 뭐 하나 섰는가 부드군요. 실력은 매우 있나 보든데... 통 쓰질 않으니까요. 林芙美子씨 참 재미있어요. 사람이 좋아요, 집두 썩 좋구요. 한달에 세가 80圓씩이라나요.
記者. 그런 분들의 고료는 대개 한장에 얼마식 됨니까.
張. 吉屋信子씨 등은 신문 하로분에 80圓식입니다. 林씨는 그러치 못하고 어디나 다 그렇겠지만 실력 있는 작가가 반드시 인기있는 것도 아니지요, 吉屋씨 등에 비하면 林씨가 무척 낳을 것이지만 고료는 더 많거든요.
記者. 張先生은 얼마식이나 됩니까?
張. 아주 적어요. 하로분에 3, 4圓 가량 되니까요.
記者. 그래도 東京서는 문인들이 고료로 생활해 나갈 수 있지 않어요?
張. 웬걸요. 하는 사람도 있겠지만 대개는 어렵지요. 더구나 평론가들은 어려워요. 요새도 秋田羽雀씨가 양복을 입을 때마다 몇 십년을 두고 양복 한개를 가지고 입어서 다 떠러젔다는 이야길 하길래 苦笑한 일이 아지만 정말 東京서도 문인의 생활이란 보잘 것 없읍니다. 秋田씨＜193＞ 같은 분은 그래도 문단의 원로가 아님니까. 그런데도 아직 가난하게 살고 있으니까요. 참 그 양복을 보면 죄다 떠러젔어요. 원고료는 쌀값이나 겨우 된다는군요.
記者. 푸로레타리아 작가들은 지금 어떻게 무었을 하고 있읍니까.
張. 島木健作, 林房雄씨 등이 다-그 작품태도가 달너젔어요. 모다 日本主義로 개량된 셈이죠.

4, 切親한 동무는 누구,
記者. 선생의 친한 동무는 현재 東京의 어떤 분들입니까?
張. 荒木, 湯淺, 林房雄, 村山 ,保高 ,高見 등입니다.

8, 朝鮮 古代小說과 現 文壇
記者. 朝鮮 古代小說을 어떻게 생각하심니까.
張. 古代小說을 난 높이 평가하고 싶쟎어요, 예술미라군 별로 없쟎어요. 「春香傳」은 노래가 있고 하니까 웬만한 것같지만 소설만은 보잘 것 없지요. 보다도 「謝氏南征記」가 오히려 좀 낳은 것 같두군요.
記者. 그러니까 現代小說이 훨신 낫다는 말슴입니까.
張. 그렇죠. 훨신 낫지요.
記者. 어떤 작가의 것을 좋아하심니까?
