Looking at the overall story depicted in The Good Earth, it shows what urban life in China (支那) is like, and also reveals the unnaturalness and irrationality of Chinese society. Furthermore, one can clearly see that there are starving people. They seem to go hungry year-round. But on the other hand, there is the life of the wealthy... grand mansions and countless wives and concubines... slaves, banquets morning and evening; when they go out on the streets, they ride in carriages. This perpetually dances before the eyes of the poor. Thus, mobs <162> separately arise, and small-scale revolutionary disturbances occur. As reported in newspapers and the like, the society of China's warlords and politicians is entirely different from the society of the Chinese populace. The work the Chinese masses do is labor for the preservation of their own individual lives. And the government seizes their property, and the army forcibly conscripts soldiers. For that reason, this story depicted in The Good Earth shows how much happiness the people would feel if these politicians and soldiers were swept away from China, allowing the people to live peaceful lives. Thus, one can feel what kind of atrocities the thing called the Chinese government inflicts upon the populace. The original author, Ms. Buck (빽女史), skillfully depicted the woman named O-lan (阿蘭), the wife of Wang Lung (王龍), the protagonist in The Good Earth. Although the setting is China, O-lan should not be seen merely as a Chinese woman; it would be fair to say she represents women worldwide. Or, looking more broadly, it would be fair to say she alone represents the burdensome fate of humans carrying the world and living. O-lan's endless misfortune did not cease, and O-lan ended up dying... That woman spoke of life's emptiness and departed. The original author, Ms. Buck, was the daughter of an American missionary and grew up on Chinese soil. Because of this background, the content of the original work was created from things she personally saw and heard. There may be some differences between the novel and the film, but that is commonly seen whenever any literary work is adapted into a film, so it cannot be helped.

The Content of The Good Earth
The protagonist in the Chinese countryside was a poor farmer called Wang Lung (王龍). Rather than being a possessor of the firm human instinct to love the farmland, he could be called just an ordinary Chinese peasant. Wang Lung, being a peasant, thought that the only person who could become the wife of someone as insignificant as himself would be a "slave" or "a woman sold to a great house, that is, a servant," and so he arranged to marry a servant from the great house of Huang (黃). That servant had the name O-lan (阿蘭). <163> O-lan was born in Shantung (山東) and, in a year when farming failed, was sold by her parents and became a servant at the great house of Huang. When Wang Lung took money and went to the great house of Huang, he was severely looked down upon and harassed by the gatekeeper fellow. Braving this, when he came out to the main gate with O-lan, he disliked going out through the same main gate he had entered earlier. When he asked O-lan if there was another gate, she, understanding the situation and knowing the house layout well, took Wang Lung's hand and led him to another small gate.
On the wedding night, Wang Lung gathered the village acquaintances and held a small feast. From the day she arrived as a bride, O-lan prepared food in the kitchen. Late at night, after the guests had all returned, when Wang Lung went out to the kitchen, O-lan was asleep in the cowshed along with the farm cattle. Wang Lung woke O-lan and led her into his room. From then on, the two became husband and wife. O-lan was calm and also a victim who disliked speaking. However, she did not forget to work from morning until night. She went to the mountains to gather firewood and collected horse dung on the streets to make fertilizer. When Wang Lung's fieldwork was busy, O-lan would come out to the fields with a hoe without a word. O-lan became pregnant for the first time. The two lived a happy life, unchanged from always. Their barley grew. One day, Wang Lung was harvesting the barley. At that moment, O-lan suddenly put down her bundle, her complexion changed, and she said,
"It seems I'm about to give birth now. I'll go home first, so please don't come until I call. And when you come, please cut some reeds, peel off the skin, and split them for me. I'll wash the baby with that."
Hearing these words, Wang Lung quickly ran to the pondside. He cut the lush reeds there, peeled the skin, and split them. When Wang Lung ran back home, a warm evening meal was prepared, just like always. Wang Lung passed the reeds he brought through a crack in the door to O-lan and waited outside, anxiously listening for the baby's cry. Their happy life continued as usual. However, the god of fate was mischievous, and a terrifying drought inevitably came to the place where they lived. The crops in the fields withered up. People survived on grass roots, but now even those became unobtainable. People and livestock could not endure and ended up dying. Wang Lung, too, unable to do anything, finally decided, dragging his weak legs, to take his wife and children and seek <164> a way to survive in a certain city in the South. He left his home and all his land behind.
In the outskirts of a certain southern city, Wang Lung built a dwelling out of straw mats and pulled a rickshaw. His wife and children barely survived day by day, essentially begging.
Just then, it was said a war broke out somewhere, and the army was recruiting soldiers (兵丁). The populace did not know who fought where or why, nor did they try to find out. Simply, if caught by the army, one had to become a soldier, even forcibly. That was what they feared so much. One day, when soldiers came for recruitment, Wang Lung hid so as not to be seen. In the city where Wang Lung had sought refuge, such riots broke out, so Wang Lung and O-lan couldn't help but be swept along with the crowd into a certain grand mansion. Heading towards a corner room, Wang Lung discovered a man trembling there, fearing he wouldn't be able to save his own life. The man begged Wang Lung to save his life, saying he would give him a lot of money. Wang Lung shouted at him to hand over the money. The man gave out a lot of money. Thus, Wang Lung received a large sum of money from that man. When the drought and the fighting had disappeared, Wang Lung took that money, returned to his old place, bought the house of the great Huang family where O-lan had formerly been sold as a servant, and Wang Lung himself began to live as a wealthy man. One day, as Wang Lung was about to sleep, he happened to notice something hidden in his wife's bosom. Wang Lung immediately tried to take it and look, but at first, his wife was reluctant to show it, so Wang Lung, with his own hands, broke the string around his wife's neck and finally pulled out the hidden object. It was tightly wrapped in shabby rags. Wang Lung, tense, unfolded it. Inside, many jewels emerged. Wang Lung couldn't help but be surprised by the unexpected discovery. Many jewels glittering in five colors! Wang Lung, speechless and bewildered, asked his wife softly, "Where did you-" O-lan calmly replied that she had stolen them from the grand mansion when she was swept inside with the crowd. At that time, O-lan had entered some room, and since she had experience living in the Huang family house before, she knew well where treasures might be kept. Seeing a gap in a wall tile, she touched it and pulled the jewels out from inside. Wang Lung said that such jewels could not be kept in their house. If anyone found out, they would surely be taken away, so it would be better to sell them <165> and buy land with the money. And so, he wrapped them back up in the shabby rags and put them in his own pocket. O-lan, sitting beside the bed, gazing blankly at this scene, watched her husband's expression with an awkward look, and then asked in a truly low voice,
"Are you selling... all of them?"
"Why? I told you, there's no need to keep jewels like these in our house."
"Could you give just two of them to me?"
"What for?"
"If you just give me two... rather than putting them somewhere, I'd just like to touch them."
Wang Lung, unknowingly, felt a pang in his heart at these words. So, he let O-lan choose freely from the jewels he had put in his pocket. And he felt pity for his wife, who lived like a servant before him, thinking her a pitiful woman. Time flowed by. Afterwards, whenever Wang Lung saw O-lan, he wondered if she still carried those jewels in her bosom. But he didn't ask, and O-lan never took out the jewels to show him. As time flowed, people's hearts also changed. Wang Lung, once a peasant, now a wealthy man, thought about taking a beautiful concubine. One day, while O-lan was washing her husband's clothes by the pond, Wang Lung looked at his wife doing laundry and asked,
"What happened to those jewels?"
His voice was very harsh. Wang Lung also felt the shame of his conscience. Because he felt ashamed, he deliberately made his voice harsher.
O-lan, rubbing clothes on the washing stone, replied,
"I still have the jewels." Wang Lung, without looking at his wife's face, gazed at her thin, water-soaked hands and muttered to himself,
"What's the use of having jewels you don't even wear?" O-lan paused for a moment, then said,
"I was thinking of making earrings in a few days, or maybe giving them to my daughter when she gets married."
Wang Lung, composing himself, said,
"What good is wearing jewels on skin as dark as earth? Jewels are for women with pretty and fine faces."
They fell silent.
"Give them here. I have a use for them."
Wang Lung's voice became harsher. O-lan, without any answer, reached into her waistband with her thin, wet hand and found the jewels. As the jewels were placed in Wang Lung's hand, the gems reflected the sunlight and shone frighteningly. Wang Lung laughed loudly. O-lan picked up her laundry bat and continued washing as before. She did not even try to wipe away the tears that seemed to well up and stream down. Only the sound of the laundry bat echoed in the distant mountains. As sad times flowed by, O-lan happened <166> to fall ill and took to her bed. As her condition worsened, Wang Lung called for a doctor. The doctor was surprised. But fearing what might happen if the patient didn't recover, he said,
"Well— I misspoke. If I am right [about the illness/prognosis, or perhaps if she recovers], you must pay 500 nyang of silver."
This was tantamount to a declaration that the patient could not be cured. Wang Lung gave him 10 nyang of silver and sent him away... O-lan died. Wang Lung ran to the kitchen where his wife O-lan had devoted her life. O-lan was not there. Only the things O-lan used, scattered in the kitchen, seemed to stare accusingly at him. Wang Lung pressed his forehead against the soot-blackened wall and wept. Although a change of heart occurred briefly due to having money, the firm and sound ****, nurtured like the Good Earth, did not leave him. <167>

**Summary**

This 1938 article from the magazine *Samch'ŏlli* reviews the American film "The Good Earth," based on Pearl S. Buck's novel. It summarizes the plot, focusing on the hardships of Chinese peasant Wang Lung and his wife O-lan, including famine, displacement, revolution, sudden wealth, and eventual tragedy. The reviewer highlights the film's depiction of the suffering of the Chinese masses under corrupt government and warlords, contrasting it with the opulent life of the rich, and praises the portrayal of O-lan as a symbol of enduring womanhood and human struggle. From a historical perspective, the article is interesting as it was published during the Japanese colonial period in Korea and amidst the Second Sino-Japanese War. The focus on Chinese suffering, social injustice, and governmental failure could have resonated with Korean audiences experiencing their own colonial subjugation, while also potentially aligning with Japanese wartime propaganda depicting China as chaotic and needing intervention, albeit filtered through an American lens.

大地에 그려있는 바 이 얘기의 大體를 본다면 支那 도시의 생활은 어떠한 것이며 또 支那 社會의 不自然과 불합리하다는 것을 보여 주었다. 또 한편 굼주리는 인민들이 있는 것을 잘 알 수가 있다. 그들은 年中 굼고 지내는 듯 하다. 그러나 그 반면에 있어 부호들의 생활... 그것은 대저택과 수 없는 妻妾... 노비 朝夕의 만찬 그들은 거리에 나스면 마차를 달닌다. 이것이 언제이나 빈민 앞에 춤춘다. 이리하야 그곳에는 따로 暴民이＜162＞ 생겨나고 소규모의 革命騷勤이 이러난다. 신문 등에 발표됨도 있거니와 支那의 軍閥이나 정치가들의 사회와 支那 민중들의 사회와는 전연 다른 것이다. 支那 민중들이 일하고 있는 것은 자기 한 사람의 생활보존을 위한 勞働이다. 그리고 정부는 그들 재산을 빼앗고 군대는 군인을 강제 모집을 한다. 그런 까닭에 大地에 그려있는 이얘기는 支那로부터 이러한 정치가와 군인을 일소하고 인민으로써 평화한 생활을 하게 한다면 그들은 얼마나 행복을 느낄가 하는 것을 알 것이다. 그리하야 支那정부라는 것이 민중에게 어떻한 참혹을 준다는 것을 느낄 수 있다. 원작자 빽女史는 大地 가운데의 주인공 王龍의 처인 阿蘭이라는 여자를 잘 그리였다. 무대는 支那라고 할지라도 阿蘭은 支那 여성이랄 것이 아니라. 전세계 여성을 대표하엿다고 하야도 좋을 것이다. 또는 널니 본다면 인간들이 세상을 질머지고 사라가는 수고러운 운명을 혼자 대표하였다고 해도 좋을 것이다. 阿蘭의 끗없는 비운은 끝나지도 않어서 阿蘭은 죽고 말었다... 그 여자는 인생의 공허를 말해주고 갔다. 원작자 빽女史는 米國 선교사의 딸로서 支那 땅에서 자라났다. 그런 관계로 원작 내용이 자기가 친히 보고 듣고 한 것으로 만든 것이다 . 다소 소설과 영화가 다른 점은 있겠으나 그것은 어떠한 문예 예품이든지 영화화되는 때에 흔이 볼 수 있는 일이니까 할 수 없다.

大地의 內容
支那 농촌 主人公은 王龍이라 부르는 貧農이였다. 농터를 사랑하는 굿은 인간본능에 소유자라는 것부다 한낫 평범한 支那 농민이라 할 것이다. 농민인 王龍은 자기잇같이 보잘 것 없는 자의 안해될 사람은 「노예」거나 「대가집에 팔려간 여자 즉 종 밧게 없겠다」 생각하고 黃이라는 대가의 종을 안해로 마지하기를 약속하였다. 그 종은 阿蘭이란 일홈을 가졌다.＜163＞ 阿蘭은 山東 출생으로 농사가 아니되는 어느 해 부모에게 팔님이 되여 黃大家의 종이 되었다. 王龍은 돈을 만들어 갖이고 黃大家엘 갓슬 때 파수보는 놈에게 몹시 업수히 보히였고 시달였다. 그러함을 무릅쓰고 阿蘭을 데리고 대문까지 나왔을 때 았가 드러왔든 대문으로 나아가기가 싫엿다. 阿蘭에게 다른 문이 없느냐 물었을 때 그 눈치를 알고 집안 내용을 잘 아는 阿蘭은 王龍의 손을 잡고 다른 조고마한 문으로 끌고갓다.
결혼한날 밤 王龍은 洞里 아는 사람을 모여놓고 조고마한 연회를 열었다. 시집이라고 오는날로 阿蘭은 부억에서 요리를 만드렀다. 밤 깊어 손님들이 다 도라간 뒤에 王龍이 부억에 나갓슬 때 阿蘭은 외양깐에서 농우와 한가지로 잠이 드러 있었다. 王龍은 阿蘭을 이으키여 자기 방으로 다리고 드러갓다. 그로부터 두 사람은 부부가 되였다. 阿蘭은 침착하고 또 말하기를 싫어하는 희생자엇다. 그러나 아츰부터 밤까지 일하는 것은 잊이 않었다. 산에 가 나무도 하여오고 거리에 馬糞을 주어서는 비료를 만들고 하였다. 王龍의 들일이 밧쁠 때면 阿蘭은 아모 말도 없이 호미를 들고 들에 나왔다. 阿蘭은 처음으로 아희를 배였다. 두 사람은 언제나 다름없는 행복한 생활이였다. 그들의 보리는 자랐다. 어느날 王龍은 보리를 비고 있었다. 그럴 때 阿蘭은 벌안간 뀀¬을 놓고 안색이 달나지며
「이제 아마 애기를 낳게되나 보아요. 저는 먼저 집으로 가겠으니 부를 때까지 오시면 안돼요. 그리고 오실 때는 했갈대를 꺾어 껍질을 벗기고 쪼개여다 주세요. 그것으로 애기를 씻길 터이니까요.」
이 말을 들은 王龍은 얼는 연못가로 달여갓다. 그곳에 무성한 했갈대를 잘나서 껍질을 벗기고 쪼껐다. 그래 가지고 王龍이가 집으로 달여 갓슬 때는 언제나 다름없이 따뜻한 저녁 밥상이 준비되여 있었다. 王龍이 가지고 드러온 갈대는 문틈으로 阿蘭에게 주고 밖에서 애기의 우름 소리를 王龍은 기다리며 가슴을 조리고 있었다. 행복한 생활은 여전히 게속되였다. 하나 운명의 신은 짓구저서 무서운 旱災는 그들이 사는 곳에도 찾어오고 말었다. 들 곡식은 말나 붙었다. 사람들은 풀뿌리로 연명하였으나 이제는 그것도 얻어보지 못하게 되였다. 사람들과 가축은 참다 못하야 죽고야 말었다. 王龍도 엇지할 수 없어서 생각마 못하야 힘없는 다리를 잇글어 처자를 대리고 南方 어느 도회로＜164＞ 살길을 찿었다. 살든 집과 토지 전부는 그대로 두고 떠났다.
