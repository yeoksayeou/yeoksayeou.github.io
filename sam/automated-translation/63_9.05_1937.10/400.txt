Publication Date: 1937년 10월 01일
Article Title: 어머니와 딸 (Mother and Daughter)
Author: 羅惠錫
Article Type: 소설 (Novel/Fiction)

Mother and Daughter
						Na Hye-sŏk

“I’m not envious of those so-called accomplished women.”
The landlady, who came to Han Un’s room whenever she had a chance, giggling “hee hee hee hee,” was sitting across from Han Un and Ri Kwi-bong again this evening, deliberately raising her voice so Mr. Kim in the room below could hear.

“Why is that?”
Ri Kwi-bong knew the landlady’s temperament well. Wondering what she would say this time, he asked like this to hear her out.

“A woman should do needlework and weaving, manage the household well, and eat the food her husband provides.”
This inn landlady, who one day sat facing Kap, Ŭl, Pyŏng (甲乙丙), the next day faced I, Ro, Ha (イロハ), and sometimes even spoke with A, B, C, had overheard many discussions about “new women” during gatherings. Among those discussions, this particular sentiment had stuck in her head the most.

“Why? Can’t new women do needlework and weaving? Wouldn’t it taste better to eat the food they earn themselves rather than their husband’s?”
Ri Kwi-bong, who had divorced a year ago and was again curious about new women, retorted like this. In response, the landlady lacked the strength to counter with the same forceful tone as before.

“I said it for her to hear.” (Pointing to the room below with her finger)
Han Un poked Ri Kwi-bong’s side and said this.

“Well, but what is she doing alone day and night in the room below?”
Ri Kwi-bong changed the subject again to match the landlady’s mood.

“Writing a novel or something, they say.”
The landlady pouted her lips; whether it was a curse or held some other meaning was impossible to tell.

“What does it matter to the landlady if someone else writes a novel or does whatever? Why should you be so jealous (배가 앞흘 것이)?”
The landlady seemed about to say something but closed her mouth.

“Why is that, really?”
Ri Kwi-bong, more than anything, wanted to hear the landlady’s bold pretense of knowledge.

“It’s no good for a woman to be accomplished.”

“Is it good for a man to be accomplished?”

“It’s no good if a man is too accomplished either.”

“Then one must be moderately accomplished. That sounds rather difficult.”
Ri Kwi-bong smacked his lips. Sitting up straighter again, he asked:
“Landlady, why do you say it’s no good for either women or men to be accomplished? Let’s hear about it.”

“What would I, an ignorant person, know? But if a woman is accomplished, she won’t obey her husband, and if a man is accomplished, he makes his wife suffer.”

“That’s absolutely right (그건 꼭 그렀오). Now I see, landlady, you are a great philosopher and literary figure.”
He flattered her excessively for a moment. He wanted to explain that this was a reality left behind when the counterpart lacked character, somewhat rectified in cities or civilized countries, but still a fact in transitional (괴도긔에 잇는) uncivilized countries or rural areas. However, thinking she wouldn’t understand, he gave up and just flattered her.

“There’s some truth to that statement.”
Han Un said this, blinking his dark eyes and smoothing down his hair.

“Why do you say that? Let’s hear it.”
Ri Kwi-bong eagerly jumped at Han Un’s words.

“Isn’t it a fact that accomplished women get divorced and accomplished men get divorced?”

“That’s not because they are accomplished, but because they are not compatible.”

“Ultimately, isn’t incompatibility because someone is accomplished, or perhaps too accomplished?”

“It must be a fact arising from the human instinct to progress.”
Ri Kwi-bong, having been divorced himself, gave a somewhat weaker reply. To Han Un, who was still unmarried and dreaming of the future, he wanted to deny the concept of divorce as much as possible.

“Can’t one progress without getting divorced?”

“It’s because one tries to find satisfaction where there is dissatisfaction.”

“If that’s the case, one might as well live alone from the start. Unless one can satisfy oneself, seeking satisfaction through another person is unreasonable.”

“If we delve into it that deeply, there’s no end to it, and one can’t simply advocate living alone. It’s a difficult problem.”
Ri Kwi-bong became somber, pondering his current unemployment and wondering what kind of woman might become his wife and satisfy him. Seizing this pause, the landlady spoke again.

“Really, since Mr. Kim arrived, that girl says she won’t marry and just wants to study more. What am I to do?”

“If it’s possible, it’s better to let her study more.”

“What’s the use of studying more? Graduating from girls’ high school is enough.”

“Women need specialized education too. Is there anything more precarious than a woman’s life?”

“That’s why it’s better not to become an accomplished woman.”

“If she encounters misfortune without a specialty to support herself, she’ll trouble her parents, siblings, and friends, so it amounts to the same thing.”

“If she’s not accomplished, she won’t encounter misfortune.”

“Then why did Tolsoe’s mother separate from her husband and end up working as a servant in this inn?”

“It’s all fated, that’s why.”
Having no other answer, the landlady said this.

“If you put it that way, everything is like that.”
Ri Kwi-bong, thinking further talk would be futile, ended the conversation simply.

“Let’s just play hwatu (화토).”
As if tired of listening, Han Un pulled out hwatu cards from the desk drawer.

“Shall we play stop-go (막고내기) hwatu?”

“Stop-go, one point per 200 score.”
The three each picked up their cards and sat down.

2
Early in the morning, the landlady entered Mr. Kim’s room.

“Please come in. Come down here where it’s warm.”
Mr. Kim said, putting aside the manuscript she was writing.

“What are you writing day and night?”

“Oh, just fooling around (작란하고 있지요).”

“Isn’t it lonely being alone day and night?”

“Not really. I’ve graduated. And I’m studying how to overcome loneliness.”

“A person of deep cultivation is different.”

“Not really.”

“How did you manage to study so much (엇저면 그렇게 공부를 많이 하섰서)?”

“What do you mean, studied so much?”

“Truly, you are admirable (훌늉하시지) for a woman.”

“Not at all.”

“If everyone who studied became like Mr. Kim, who wouldn’t study?”

“Why do you say that?” <48>
Mr. Kim, having heard the conversation from the room above the previous night, wondered what new whim this landlady had and asked this.

“Should they do manual labor like us? Sitting comfortably, writing… it’s like the life of an immortal.”

“…”
Mr. Kim wanted to say, “You people are the fortunate ones,” but felt it would lead to a long conversation and remained silent.

“How much do they give you when you write novels like that and send them to magazines?”

“I just write because I’m bored.”
She didn’t mention she was writing for a 150-won prize novel competition.

“Still, I heard you earn a lot of money.”

“That’s a lie.”
Although she had won several hundred won from prize novels in the past, several hundred won from serialized novels in newspapers, and received a considerable amount each month for magazine contributions, she didn’t say so, as it felt like bragging.

“You travel like this because you’ve earned a lot, right?”

“Yes. I have several thousand won in my savings account.”
To this question, asked as if by an interrogating detective (형사가 힐분하드시), she replied nonchalantly, as if wanting to get it over with, feeling relieved inside. Mr. Kim inherently detested talking about money.

“Oh my goodness, listen to that!”

“Don’t worry about me skipping out on the meal bill.”

“Oh, heavens no.”

“By the way, Mr. Kim.”

“Yes.”

“Doesn’t it cost a lot to stay in an inn like this?”

“That’s my business to manage (내가 아러채서 할 일이지요).”

“I’ve arranged for a room over there.”

“Does that mean you want me to leave?”

“If you get (엇어서) a room and cook for yourself, it won’t cost much, will it? In these times of economy (경세시대에), one must economize.”

“Thank you, but as the landlady, you don’t need to worry about such things for your guest.”
A hint of annoyance showed on Mr. Kim’s face. The landlady, feeling awkward (미안이 역이면서), said:
“It’s because I think of you like family.”

“I pay my room and board just like anyone else. There’s no need for you to tell me to leave or stay (나가라 드러가거라).”

“…”

“I won’t move (옴기지 않겠오) elsewhere. I’m the type to stay put once I’ve settled in one place (한곧).”
Mr. Kim, knowing well that if she moved, she wouldn't be able to write her current piece calmly for some time, felt somewhat displeased but spoke thus.

“What exactly is the reason (까닭은 무엇이오) you want me to leave? Let me understand.”

“I knew it would be rude to say such a thing to a guest, but I did.”

“What’s the reason?”

“Well, you see. It’s like ‘near ink, one gets stained black’ (금묵자흑으로). Since the teacher arrived, my daughter Yŏngae refuses to get married and insists on studying more. What use is more studying for a woman?”

“Can you afford her tuition then?”

“We don’t have the money, and even if we did, I wouldn’t let her.”

“Why is that?”

“It’s enough for a woman to eat the food her husband provides.”

“What if she eats her husband’s food, and then becomes unable to eat it?”

“Only accomplished women end up like that.”

“What if an unaccomplished woman ends up like that?”

“We’ll marry her off somewhere where that won’t happen.”

“Who gets married thinking it will turn out like that from the start?”

“What good does it do for a woman to learn more?”

“The more she learns, the better. What is there besides knowing more?”

“What good does knowing much do? Bearing children and managing the household is enough.”

“Well, that’s true, but this isn’t a world where one can live trusting only tyrannical men.”

“Mr. Kim, you keep telling (해들니니까) such things to my Yŏngae, that’s why this is happening.”

“I haven't spoken to her about it, but if I were to speak, that’s what I’d say.”

“Then why does that girl say she won’t marry?”

“How would I know? She must have her own thoughts. It’s not my fault, and there’s no reason for you to ask me to leave.”

“Goodness, Mr. Kim, if we let a promising young man like Han Un slip away, where else can we find someone like him?”

“If you look, you’ll find another.”

“Well, I went to see for myself.”

“Han Un’s house?”

“Yes!”

“How was it?”

“Stacks of rice bales, piles of wood, his father is a scholar, the siblings are harmonious, good gentry status, the young man himself is gentle. What more could one ask for?”

“Did you tell her that?”

“Of course, I did.”

“What did she say?”

“She doesn’t want to.”

“Why doesn’t she want to?”

“Mr. Kim would know better than I.”

“Would she tell me things she can’t tell her mother?”

“What would she say to an ignorant mother (에미)? Mr. Kim, you’re all in the same boat, so she’d talk.”

“Don’t blame me, just try to gently persuade (달래시오) your daughter.”

“Does that girl listen to me? If I mention it again, I’m not human.”

“Is there any need to tell me anything?”

“Mr. Kim ruined (버려놈넨다) my daughter!”
The landlady threw out this final remark and stood up. Mr. Kim grabbed her skirt hem, pulling her back.
“What are you saying? If that’s truly the case, I will go elsewhere.”

“…”

“Instead, try coaxing Yŏngae and ask her if there’s someone she likes.”

“That girl isn’t the type to engage in romance (연에나 하는 년 아니오).”
She slammed the door shut and left.
Mr. Kim sat alone, staring blankly at the ceiling. It was amusing, interesting, and infuriating all at once. Then, her own daughter came to mind. What if, like this mother and daughter, I approved, but she refused? She added this unexpected misfortune to the program of all her past misfortunes.
“Should I stay longer and watch how the incident unfolds?”
Then she thought,
“Ah, forget it. Who knows what other trouble I might get into?”
She decided to leave the inn and looked at her scattered belongings.

3
“Teacher.”
Yŏngae entered. There were traces of tears in her eyes.

“Come in.”

“Teacher.”
Yŏngae collapsed onto Mr. Kim’s lap. Her shoulders shook.

“Don’t cry, tell me everything.”

“…”

“Yŏngae.”

“Yes.”
Yŏngae sat up, wiping the flowing tears with her skirt hem.

“Have you promised yourself to someone?”

“No.”

“Really, it doesn’t seem like it to me either.”

“No.”

“Then why refuse when your mother found a good person and wants you to marry? Huh?”

“I don’t want to.”

“Do you mean you don’t want to get married, or you don’t like Han Un?”

“I don’t want to get married, and I don’t like him either.”

“Then what do you plan to do?”

“I wish I were dead.”

“If dying is truly necessary, one can die.”

“Teacher.”

“Yes?”

“I want to study more.”

“Do you have money?”

“Even if I have to work my way through school.”

“Is it that easy?”

“Oh, I wish I were dead.”

“Dying isn’t something you discuss with others.”

“Oh, Teacher.”
Tears welled up in Yŏngae’s eyes again.

“Is your mother unable to afford the tuition?”

“No!”

“Are there no other relatives who could pay for your tuition?”

“No!”

“It’s a pity, seeing your talent.”

“If someone could just lend me the money, I’d pay it back after graduating.”

“Whether you can pay it back or not, nobody knows, and is there such a kind person?”

“Teacher, couldn’t there be someone like that?”

“If I had money, I would lend it to you, but I don’t have any.”

“Why don’t rich people give me some money?”

“If you study, what will you specialize in?”

“Literature.”

“Literature?”

“That’s good.”

“It’s difficult.”

“Difficult it may be, but if done well, it’s good. Yŏngae, you read a lot, so literature would suit you. While living individually, living socially gives life flavor. Becoming a person in society by publishing good creative works <49> – there’s nothing more joyful than that.”

“Oh, I could die.”

“Don’t have such delusions; take the close (갓갑고) and easy (쉬은) path.”

“What path is that?”

“Since you can’t study due to lack of money, shouldn’t you get married?”

“I don’t want to.”

“You probably don’t like Han Un, right?”

“Yes, I don’t like him.”

“Why? What’s wrong with him?”

“He’s immature (ナツテイナイノテ゜スヨ).”

“But your mother seems to really like him.”

“He might be okay as an individual, but he won’t become a figure in society.”

“Being okay as an individual is enough.”

“Teacher, what did you just say?”

“Being okay as an individual means being a social figure.”

“Then isn’t everyone like that?”

“Not exactly, but…”

“I don’t like that person.”

“Why? He seems fine to me.”

“He’s a spineless man (イクチナイオトコ).”

“He is a bit immature.”

“He hasn’t amounted to anything (モノニナツテイナイ).”

“Then ask your mother to find someone else.”

“I don’t want to.”

“You dislike this, dislike that, dislike everything. What are you going to do?”

“I just want to die.”

“That’s also a fantasy. You need to quickly make a decision, one way or another, to calm down (オチツク). Even the person next to you (겻헤 사람) is getting irritated (イライラ).”

“Oh my! Mother is coming down!”
Yŏngae hurriedly stood up.

“Go quickly. She’ll think we were discussing something.”

“She really dislikes me coming to this room.”

“That’s what I mean.”

“Teacher, I’ll come again.”
Yŏngae quickly exited.

4
“You wench, sleeping (자빠저 자니) at this hour!”
As soon as the landlady entered Yŏngae’s room, where she lay alone sleeping, she snatched off the blanket, grabbed her, beat (뚜듸리고) her, and shouted loudly.
“Sleeping till midday, eating the food brought to you (해다주는 밥 먹고), just staring (듸려다 보면) at books day and night – do clothes or food appear? I hate the sight of you, wench! Get out of here!”

“Oww, oww, Mother, I was wrong!”

“What could an accomplished wench like you have done wrong?”

“…”

“I hate the sight of an accomplished wench like you. Get out immediately!”

“Where should I go?”

“Go anywhere. Go to that lover boy you’re dating (연애하는 서방)!”

“Mother, you’re being ridiculous.”

“You must like someone, that’s why you refuse to marry!”

“I don’t!”

“You wench, I educated you through girls’ high school hoping you’d become a proper person, but now I regret it immensely.”

“…”

“What use is a child who doesn’t listen to her mother (에미)? Didn’t Shim Ch'ŏng sell herself to open her father’s eyes (눈을 띄우지 아니했니)? You and I have no relation. Get out right now (오날 지금이라도), this instant!”
She beat her again.

“Ouch, ouch!”

“Either die or get out, wench! I can’t stand the sight of you!”

“Ouch, I won’t do it again.”

“What do you mean you won’t do it again when I’m telling you to get out?”
At this point, unable to listen anymore, Mr. Kim opened her door.
“Excuse me. Excuse me. Please come here for a moment.”

5
One evening after dinner. Han Un came into Mr. Kim’s room.
“I was bored, so I came to visit (놀너 왔음니다).”

“Welcome. Please sit down.”

“During the day, I’m busy (밧부게) at the office, but at night I get bored.”

“What kind of office work (사무) do you do?”

“It’s related to agriculture and forestry (농림).”

“Ah, you graduated from an agriculture and forestry school, right?”

“Yes.”

“You work at the Provincial Office, don’t you?”

“Yes.”

“Are you busy?”

“Yes. Quite busy (상당이 밧뿜니다).”

“You should get married soon and start a family.”

“If I had my way, I’d prefer to live life as a bachelor, but my parents and siblings won’t leave me alone.”

“Why is that? The joy of marriage is the greatest in life.”

“Is that so? It seems more troublesome than being single.”

“There’s fun amidst the trouble.”

“I don’t know why the Creator made men and women.”

“It’s because men and women exist that this fascinating world came about.”

“Living alone seems most comfortable.”

“Still, men and women must unite for life to be unified and personality to be unified, what can you do (었재요)?”

“Is that so?”

“Yes. Single people lack composure, what can you do (었저구)?”

“That seems plausible. It does get lonely.”

“Get married soon.”

“Is it that easy?”

“How are things progressing with Yŏngae?”

“I don’t know.”

“If it doesn’t work out with Yŏngae, you should propose elsewhere.”
Mr. Kim asked this way to gauge his feelings.

“If I intended to propose elsewhere, I would have done so already.”

“Then you definitely want to marry Yŏngae?”

“…”

“With utmost sincerity, even the heavens are moved (지성즉감천). Try showing perseverance a hundred times. Is there anything that cannot be achieved if one sets out to do it?”

“She says she wants to study.”

“She needs tuition money.”

“Couldn’t I contribute some, and her mother contribute some?”

“Really? Have you mentioned that to the landlady?”

“She absolutely refuses to let her study.”

“She must really like you, Han Un-ssi.”

“What does it matter if her mother likes me? It’s a matter between the individuals involved.”
Han Un, still completely unaware, spoke like this.

“What if Yŏngae refuses to marry you, Han-gong?”
She said this, hoping he would catch on at least a little.

“…”

“Let’s drop that subject and play a record.”
Mr. Kim, wary of gossip about others’ affairs, changed the subject.

“Shall we listen to some Western music (양극)?”
Han Un, also not wanting to talk further, put on the gramophone (축음긔).
“How about that one?”
As the powerful sounds of Carmen, Faust, Hamlet, Marseille concluded one after another, Yŏngae’s faint laughter drifted in from Ri Kwi-bong’s room. Han Un listened intently, but his expression showed no reaction. Mr. Kim, feeling needlessly anxious sitting opposite him (마조 안젓는), thought:
“Ah, what an innocent young man.” <50>

**Summary**

This story by Na Hye-sŏk depicts the conflict between a traditional innkeeper mother and her daughter, Yŏngae, who desires further education in literature rather than an arranged marriage. The mother, influenced by traditional views reinforced by guests like Ri Kwi-bong, sees marriage to the promising Han Un as the only path for her daughter. Yŏngae, however, inspired implicitly by the presence of the female writer Kim Sŏnsaeng (a guest at the inn), rejects the marriage and the suitor, finding him immature and unaspiring. The story highlights the tension between societal expectations for women (marriage, domesticity) and the burgeoning desires of "new women" for education, independence, and self-fulfillment, even resorting to considering desperate measures when thwarted.

From a historical perspective, the story is interesting for its portrayal of the "new woman" ideal clashing with entrenched patriarchal values in 1930s colonial Korea. Kim Sŏnsaeng represents this modern, educated, financially independent woman, while Yŏngae embodies the aspiration towards it. The mother's pragmatic, traditional views, Ri Kwi-bong's cynical commentary on divorce and accomplishment, and Han Un's naive traditional masculinity provide contrasting perspectives. The casual use of Japanese phrases (イロハ, ナツテイナイノテ゜スヨ etc.) reflects the linguistic reality of the colonial period. Historians might find the depiction of female aspirations for higher education (specifically literature), the economic barriers faced, and the societal pressures surrounding marriage particularly revealing about women's changing roles and consciousness during this era.

어머니와 딸
						羅惠錫
『나는 그 잘낮다는 녀자들 부럽지 않아』
틈만 나면 한운의 방에와서 『히히히히』하는 주인 마누라는 오날 저녁에도 또 한운과 리긔봉과 마조안저 아랫방에 잇는 김선생 귀에 들니라고 일부러 목소리를 크게 하여 말했다.
『왜요』
리긔봉은 주인 마누라의 심사를 잘 아는 터이다. 또 무슨 말인가 하고 드러보기 위하야 이 이렇게 물었다.
『녀자란 걷은 침선방적을 하야 살림을 잘하고 남편의 밥을 먹어야 하는 거시야』
오날은 갑을병(甲乙丙)과 마조 안고 내일은 이토하(イロハ)와 마조 안게 되고 때로는 ABC와도 말하게 되는 이 여관집 마누라는 여러 번 좌석에서 신여자 논란이 나는 것을 만히 주서 드렀다. 그리하야 그 중에 이런 말이 제일 머리에 백혔든 것이었다.
『왜요. 신녀성은 침선방적을 못하나요. 남편의 밥보다 자긔 밥을 먹으면 더 맞있지』
일년 전에 리혼을 하고 다시 신녀성에게 호긔심을 두고 잇는 리긔봉은 이렇게 반항하였다. 이에 대하야 다시 주인 마누라는 처음과 같이 강한 어조로 반항할 힘이 없었다.
『드르라고 그랬지』(손구락으로 아랫방을 가라치며)
한운은 리긔봉의 업흘 꾹찌르며 이렇게 말한다.
『아니 그런대 아랫방에서는 혼자 밤낮 무엇을 하고 잇는 모양이야』
주인 마누라의 성미를 맛추어 이렇게 다시 화제를 리긔봉은 이었다.
『소설을 쓴다나 무엇을 한다나』
입을 빗죽하는 주인 마누라는 무엇을 저주함인지 무슨 의미인지 대체 알 길이 없었다.
『남이 소설을 쓰거니 무엇을 하거니 주인이 그렇게 배가 앞흘 것이 무엇 잇소』
주인 마누라는 무슨 말을 할듯할듯하다가 입을 다문다.
『왜 그래요 글새』
리긔봉은 무엇보다 그 주인 마누라의 대담히 아는 체 하는 것이 더 듯고 싶었다.
『녀자가 잘나면 못써』
『남자는 잘나면 쓰구요』
『남자도 넘어 잘나면 못쓰지』
『그럼 알마치 잘나야겟군. 좀 어려운걸』
리긔봉은 입맞을 쩍쩍 다신다. 다시 밧삭대 앉으며
『주인 대체 녀자나 남자나 잘나면 못쓴다니 왜 그럿소. 말 좀 드러 봅시다』
『내야 무식하니 무얼 알겟소 마는 녀자가 잘나면 남편에게 순종치 아니하고 남자가 잘나면 게집 고생식켜』
『그건 꼭 그렀오. 인제 아니까 주인이 큰 철학가요 문학가거든』
한참 비행긔를 태었다. 그렇고 그것은 상대자의 인격이 부족한 때 남기는 현실이오 도회지나 문명국에는 다소 정돈이 되었으나 괴도긔에 잇는 미문명국이나 지방에서는 아직도 사실로 잇다는 설명을 하고 싶었으나 알아들을 것 같지 아니하야 고만 두고 비행긔만 태운 것이었다.
『그 말도 일리가 잇는 말이야』
한운은 이렇게 말하며 검은 눈을 끔먹 끔먹하고 내러오는 머리를 한번 씨다듬었다.
『왜 그럿소. 어데 드러봅시다』
리긔봉은 한운의 말에 반색을 하야 대들었다.
『잘난 녀자도 이혼하고 잘난 남자도 이혼하는 것은 사실 아니오』
『그건 잘나서 그런 것이 아니라 맛지가 않아서 그런 것이지』
『결국 맛지 안는다는 것이 누가 잘낟든지 잘나서 그런 것 아니오』
『다 진보하랴는 사람의 본능에서 생기는 사실이겠지』
자긔가 리혼을 한 사실이 있는 리긔봉은 대답이 좀 약해젓다. 아직 미성혼 중으로 장래를 꿈꾸고 있는 한운에게는 어대까지 리혼이라는 것을 부정하고 싶었다.
『리혼 안하면 진보할 수 없나』
『불만족한데서 만족을 차지려니까 그렇지』
『그렇면 당초붙어 혼자 살지 자긔가 자긔를 만족한다면 모르거니와 타인을 상대하야 만족을 구한다는 것은 될 말이 아니야』
『그렇게까지 어렵게 드러가자면 한이 없고 혼자 살잔 말도 못되고 어려운 문제야』
리긔봉은 음울해지면서 자긔가 지금 무직으로 놀고 있난 것 엇던 녀성이 자긔 안해가 되여 자긔를 만족히 하야줄가 하는 것을 묵상하고 있다. 이 틈을 타서 주인을 다시 말을 끄집었다.
『글세 그년이 김선생이 온 뒤로붙어 시집을 안 갈냐고 하고 공부만 더해지라니 엇저겠소』
『할 수만 있스면 공부를 더 식히는 것이 좋치요』
『공부는 더 해 무엇하겠오. 고등녀학교 했으면 족하지』
『녀자도 전문교육을 받어야 해요. 녀자의 일생처럼 위태한 것이 어데 있나요』
『그렇기에 잘난 녀자가 되지 안는 것이 좋와』
『제 한 몸을 추수를 할 만한 전문이 없이 불행에 이른다면 부모형데 친구를 괴롭게 하니까 결국 마찬가지야』
『잘나지 아느면 불행에 이르지 않치』
『아니 그렇면 돌쇠 어머니는 어째서 남편과 생리별을 하고 이 여관집 법어멈 노릇을 하구 있소』
『다 팔자소관이니까 그렇치』
주인은 대답할 말이 없어 이렇게 말하였다.
『그렇게 말하면 다 그렇치요』
리긔봉은 더 말해야 아라들을 것 같지 아니하야 이렇게 간단이 말해 버렸다.
『우리 화토나 합세다』
다 듯기 실타는 듯이 한운은 책상 설압에서 화토를 끄냈다.
『막고내기 화토나 할가』
『이백끗에 막고한각식』
세 사람은 다 각기 들고 안젔다.
