**Body Text Translation**
Today, I went out into Shanghai city center and saw female police officers from the Shanghai Municipal Police Bureau, wearing pistols on their slender waists, conducting household surveys. Having recently seen female customs officials at the Shanghai customs office, and now seeing even female police officers, I have been surprised many times realizing how actively women are advancing in Chinese society.

Are there female police officers or female customs officials yet in Chosŏn? Are there even any in Tokyo? If a spirited woman like Pok Hyesuk were dressed in military uniform, carrying a gun, and made to walk back and forth on Chongno Intersection or in front of T'aptong Park, wouldn't the streets of Sŏul be quite surprised?

But here in Shanghai, they are quite numerous. On the wharf, in the city center, wherever you go, women in this field stride about freely. They are truly plentiful.

I heard that in Sŏul, the Hwasin Department Store has also hired several dozen female clerks, but here too, at the major department stores like Wing On Company (永安公司), Sincere Company (先施公司), Sinch'ul Company (新出公司), and others, female clerks are appearing and steadily pushing out the male clerks.

In Shanghai's countless large and small dance halls ("dance halls"), there are numerous dancers ("tansŏ") with slender waists wrapped in Chinese clothes fashioned into evening dresses ("evening dress"). There are also guide company women (*導社女人) who accompany guests upon request to restaurants, dance halls, or anywhere else; restaurant staff ("ttoresŭt'aran"), Eres (에레스), typists ("t'aip'isŭt'ŭ") – wherever one goes now, flower-like women are taking charge of work in all sectors.

Looking at the intellectual sphere, because universities opened their doors to female students as long as 16 or 17 years ago, the number of new women who have graduated from higher education has significantly increased. Thus, librarians at the Shanghai Municipal Library, editors, translators, teachers, and artists for newspapers and magazines are numerous, and female writers like Ding Ling (丁玲), Ling Shuhua (凌叔), and Bing Xin (泳心) are all quite prominent.

In any case, in the minds of intellectual women in major cities like Shanghai and Nanking, Confucian ethical codes like "Men and women should not sit together after the age of seven" (男女七歲不同席) have already completely disappeared – and shouldn't they? After all, it was already 20 years ago that Mr. Chen Duxiu's (陳獨秀氏) famous "Theory of Women's Liberation" (婦人解放論) and "Anti-Confucianism Theory" (非孔論) appeared. <41>

**Summary**

This 1937 article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine describes the remarkable presence and diverse roles of "New Women" in Shanghai and Nanking, China. The author expresses astonishment at seeing female police officers, customs officials, department store clerks displacing men, taxi dancers, escorts, restaurant workers, and typists. The piece also notes the significant number of highly educated women working as librarians, journalists, translators, teachers, artists, and writers (mentioning Ding Ling, Ling Shuhua, and Bing Xin), attributing this progress partly to universities admitting women for over 15 years and the influence of earlier feminist and anti-Confucian ideas like those of Chen Duxiu. The author contrasts this vibrant scene with the situation in Korea (Chosŏn) and Japan (Tokyo), using the hypothetical image of a famous Korean actress as an armed guard in Seoul to illustrate how shocking such female visibility would be there.

For historians of Korean colonial history, this article is interesting as it reflects a Korean perspective on modernity and social change through the lens of contemporary China. It reveals Korean intellectuals' awareness of and fascination with the rapid advancement of women in Chinese urban centers, implicitly highlighting the perceived lag in similar developments within colonial Korea and even Japan. The comparison serves as a commentary on different trajectories of modernization and changing gender roles in East Asia during this period, potentially fueling aspirations or anxieties about social transformation in Korea itself.

上海南京의 新女性, 우오른大學 敎授에서 女巡査에 일으기까지
오늘 上海 市中으로 나가 보았더니 上海市 경찰국의 女巡査가 가는 허리에 「피스돌」을 차고 호구조사을 다닙데다. 요 일전에 상해 세관에서 女子稅關吏를 본 눈으로 다시 이제 女巡査까지 보고 나니 중국 사회에 여성들이 얼마나 활발하게 진출하고 있는가 함을 알고 놀나기를 여러 번 했습니다.
女巡査나 女稅關이 아직 朝鮮에 있읍니까 東京엔들 있읍니까. 卜惠淑같은 활발한 여성을 군복 입혀서 총 메고 종로 네거리나 塔洞公園 앞거리를 왔다 갔다 하게 한다면 서울 거리가 좀 놀나겠읍니까.
그런데 여기 上海에는 아조 수두룩합니다. 埠頭에도 시중에도 어듸든 橫行 濶步하는 이 方面의 여성이. 참으로 많습이다.

서울에도 和信서 여점원을 여러 십 명 채용하고 있다는 말을 들었거니와 이 곳 大백화점인 永安公司, 先施公司, 新出公司 등 여러 곳에서도 모다 여점원이 나서서 남점원을 작고 구축하고 있읍니다.
上海에 수십 곳으로 헤일 수 잇는 大小 舞踊場 「단스·홀」 「이스닝·도레스」化한 중국 옷에 가는 허리 감은 수많은 단사들이 있고 손님이 청하는 대로 음식점에도 단스·홀에도 어데에든지 안내 동반하여 주는 *導社女人 또레스타란, 에레스 -다설, 타이피스트 등, 어데로가든지 지금은 꽃 같은 여자가 모든 부문의 일을 맡어 보고 있읍니다.

지식층 방면을 보면 대학이 여학생에게 그 문호를 개방하여 놓은 지 벌서 16, 7년이나 되는 까닭에 최고학부를 나온 신여성들이 훨신 많어저서 上海市 圖書舘이나, 신문 잡지의 편집, 번역 교사, 미술가들이 수두룩하며 여류작가로서도 丁玲, 凌叔, 泳心 등 다들 상당합니다.
