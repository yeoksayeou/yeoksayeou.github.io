An Account of Mixed Bathing, One Night in a Hakone Bathtub
						By Yi Sŏ-gu

It was three years ago, in late summer. Longing for Tōkyō, I went rushing off even without any particular business. This is because Tōkyō is a grateful city that provides most of the various things I long for.

Whenever I go to Tōkyō, there are friends I invariably meet and hang out with – Ma Hae-song and Im Nam-san. That day too, the three of us met in Ginza, had dinner together, and wandered into the back alleys of Ginza. After wandering through several places and houses, we ended up settling down at a bar called "Villa" (별장) on the lower floor of the Kyōshukusha building. The time was 1:00 AM. The owner of this place was the young master of a wealthy family with a large farm in Korea. His "madam" (매담) was an "ultra-modern girl" who had spent time playing in America. While exchanging glasses of Western liquor, someone brought up the idea of taking a drive to Hakone (箱根). The Hakone area is a scenic spot famous for its many hot springs and beautiful landscapes. However, the roads were rough, making it unsuitable for a drunken excursion, but just then, a road exclusively for automobiles had been completed, allowing us to reach the mountain pass while seated. The decision was unanimous.

"Call a car!" Loading sandwiches, White Horse whisky, and French soda, we departed Ginza at 2:00 AM. The car sped through the night, flying through the deep mountains of Hakone. We watched the sunrise over the Pacific coast near Odawara, sang verses of "Arirang" amidst the forests shrouded in morning mist, and even taught the madam from "Villa" the "Sŏul Taryŏng" (Song of Seoul). Eventually, we arrived at the Hakone Hotel. That day, we slept late! In the afternoon, we descended the mountain again and headed towards Yugawara (湯ケ原). In the Yugawara hot springs, there was a luxurious ryokan called "Hakonekan" (하고네관), a regular haunt of Mr. Ma Hae-song for many years. In front of the station, we met the novelist Mr. Kitamura Shōshō (北村小松), famous as the owner of a Datsun car, who had come to meet his wife. Our party settled into the south-facing rooms of the Hakonekan.

"Well then, let's take a bath!"

Being impatient, I went down to the bathhouse first. The private bath water was too hot, and feeling that the public bath would better capture the hot spring mood, I entered the public bath. Perhaps it was early, as I was the only bather!

The feeling of unwinding the fatigue from being dragged around all night in the hot spring was endlessly refreshing.

*The green pines on Namsan, the azaleas on Pukak Mountain,*
*At the ferry crossing of the flowing Han River,*
*Pairs of boats, white cranes play beneath Samgak Mountain.*
*Ah, it's wonderful, it's Sŏul!*

As I soaked in the water reciting the "Sŏul Taryŏng," I heard the sound of a door opening!
*Someone must be coming in.* Then the sound I heard next was—
A lovely voice—clearly the voice of a young lady.
Startled, I looked back. I thought perhaps a maid from the ryokan was coming in to check on the bath.
But—what is this? A beautiful woman of marriageable age, revealing her entire jade-white body without reservation!
Judging by her hair, she seemed to be a gisaeng. Covering her front with just a small towel, she smiled sweetly and approached, saying,
"Gomen nasai." (Excuse me.)

My vision blurred. I wasn't usually faint-hearted when it came to women, but being unexpectedly confronted by the assault of a naked beauty, I felt as though struck by lightning in my sleep.
"Ah—"
I could only manage a nod. I had heard that mixed bathing (男女混浴) was common practice in hot springs, but experiencing it for the first time, it was utterly awkward.

The woman calmly sat down, washed herself, and leisurely entered the bath. I was so embarrassed that I found it difficult to lift my head straight. (It's true.) The rosy pink flesh shimmering in the water like a rose! I had only read about such a color in writing; this was the first time seeing it just a few feet away. I had bathed together with my wife, but the feeling then and the feeling now stemmed from entirely different consciousnesses. Even now, if I close my eyes, I remember it. A charming nose, wonderfully large eyes! Hands long and beautiful like those of Irie Takako (入江たかこ)! The pose she struck, unfolding the towel with those hands and rubbing her plump shoulders that emerged above the water, was truly exquisite.

Not wanting to look was suffocatingly frustrating.
Not looking made me curious. If only there had been at least one other person, it might have been better, but with just the two of us sitting there, it was simply too awkward— It felt as though excessive good fortune, when overwhelming, brings suffering.

What on earth were Ma and Im doing? It felt like a waste to behold such a masterpiece alone, and most of all, I was dying of boredom.

Soon, Ma's voice followed, and Im's bulky figure entered around the same time.
*Wink.*
Im responded to my meaningful wink with a gentle smile. Presently, the woman calmly stood up, stepped out of the water, lathered her towel with soap, and composedly washed her body. Her skin, ripened in the water, seemed to exude a tempting mist.
"Ah! This is enough. Please get out quickly,"
I almost blurted out. Only Ma, who frequently traveled to hot springs for health reasons, could watch such a scene calmly; for Im and me, it was a rare spectacle. I had imagined that encountering the naked body of a beautiful young woman so casually would be exciting and might even stir impure thoughts, but experiencing it firsthand—especially with the woman acting so composedly and indifferently—it merely felt stifling, the situation uncomfortable, and I became careful lest any strange expression on my part be noticed by her, ending up like a student standing before the teacher's desk. After the bath, while pouring beer,
"Ma! We saw something truly fine today."
I offered a piece of my impression.
"You're still such a country bumpkin,"
He snorted with laughter. Had I truly revealed my country bumpkin nature? <32>

**Summary**

The author recounts a trip to Hakone, Japan, with two friends, Ma Hae-song and Im Nam-san, three years prior. After a night of drinking in Tōkyō, they impulsively drive to Hakone. While bathing alone in a public hot spring bath at a ryokan in Yugawara, the author is joined by a beautiful young woman, likely a gisaeng, who bathes completely naked with nonchalance. Despite having heard of mixed bathing (konyoku), the author is flustered and embarrassed by the unexpected and intimate encounter, feeling awkward rather than aroused. His friends later join, and Ma Hae-song teases him for his discomfort, calling him a "country bumpkin." The article offers a glimpse into the leisure activities and travel experiences of affluent Korean intellectuals during the colonial period, including visits to Japan. It highlights the cultural shock and differing social norms regarding nudity and bathing between Korea and Japan at the time, revealing the author's conflicted feelings of fascination, embarrassment, and perceived naivete when confronted with Japanese customs.
