"Chosŏn Conscription Problem" and Diet Discussion

Regarding the people of Chosŏn, there are three pressing major issues: the "Implementation of the Conscription System" problem, the "Suffrage" problem, and the "Implementation of Compulsory Education." Among these, spurred by the Sino-Japanese Incident and facing the current national emergency, the Chosŏn conscription problem has reappeared, drawing the attention of the world once again. On August 4th, on the floor of the 71st Diet, which established the quasi-wartime system, there was a heated debate between members of the House of Representatives and government committee members. We now publish the full proceedings of that day.

Mr. Pak Ch'un-gŭm (朴春琴君)
This petition is from concerned individuals of Chosŏn residing in Tokyo, requesting that the duty of military service be granted to Chosŏn. As you know, this is the first such petition since the annexation, so I seek the sympathy of the chairman and all committee members. As this is a grave matter for the state, I deeply apologize, but I ask for a little of your time. As you know, it has been 28 years this year since the annexation of Japan and Korea (內鮮). At the time of the annexation, as you all likely know, His Majesty the Meiji Emperor and the Korean Emperor concluded the annexation, and His Majesty the Meiji Emperor spoke of *isshi dōjin* (一視同仁 - impartial benevolence) towards the 20 million people of Korea. Therefore, we believe we have unquestionably been subjects of the Great Japanese Empire for 28 years. Successive bureaucratic cabinets, specifically the four cabinets since the Saitō cabinet, have often spoken of "national unity" (擧國一致). They have often spoken of "sweeping administrative reforms" (庶政一新). So, are the bureaucratic cabinets truly practicing politics of national unity or not? Are they practicing politics of sweeping administrative reforms or not? If the current cabinet is one of sweeping administrative reforms and national unity, we believe the same governance must be applied to the same people. Furthermore, it goes without saying that the Great Japanese Empire is currently in a state of emergency. Facing this emergency, Japan and Korea must become one body (內鮮一體) and punish the utterly brutal China (暴戾 至極한 支那). In this situation, what we find deeply regrettable is that Japanese born in Chosŏn cannot stand on the front lines as soldiers of the Empire and be active. There are various aspects to our nation's emergency, but I believe the future path for Japan lies in resolving everything through its Continental Policy. If so, regarding the Continental Policy, the development of Chosŏn, which has become part of our country, must be carried out smoothly. Japan and Korea must become completely one body, and the Japanese spirit possessed by the 70 million people of the mainland must be quickly instilled (付植) in the 20 million people of Chosŏn, the new Japanese subjects, so that 90 million become one body to overcome this emergency. Furthermore, instilling this is the path for the senior gentlemen, the old Japanese subjects, to take, and at the same time, I believe the government must strive towards that end. As you know, and as the Parliamentary Vice-Minister for Colonial Affairs (拓務政務次官) is surely well aware, today, gold production in Chosŏn has surpassed 100 million won. It goes without saying that materials for military industries, such as molybdenum (모리부덴), tungsten (당구스텐), coal, and iron (鐵), are abundantly produced in Chosŏn. Despite Chosŏn being part of Japan like this, general Japanese politics seems to forget the heat once it passes the throat, as the saying goes. Although Japan went through much hardship at the time of the Japan-Korea annexation, as peace and order were gradually maintained in Chosŏn, it seems the 20 million people living there have been somewhat forgotten. If Japan annexed it, it is the same country. <3> Some people forget the development of this part of their own country and leap over it to vigorously discuss the Manchurian Problem. I believe this stems from not recognizing it as the same country. Furthermore, the Chinese (支那人) are destroying the Japan-Manchuria economic bloc (日滿經濟『뿔럭』). What kind of propaganda (宣傳) do those Chinese spread to the Manchurians? They say, "Hey Manchurians, didn't Chosŏn annex with Japan and receive such XX treatment? Now they're going to do Japan-Manchuria XX, so you must be careful." This is like their catchphrase. It goes without saying that they are destroying the Japan-Manchuria economic bloc from its roots in this way. Even looking at it from this perspective, regarding the Japanese born in Chosŏn, while things that cannot be done certainly cannot be done, for things that can be done, I believe it is the duty of the Great Japanese Empire to grant the feeling of having become Japanese to those who voluntarily take pride in having become Japanese. Some ten-odd years ago, as you know, there were pro-Japanese (親日) and anti-Japanese (反日) factions, and the anti-Japanese party ran rampant (跋扈) in Chosŏn, frequently assassinating members of the pro-Japanese party. I myself was one of those targeted. It is a fact that Min Wŏn-sik (閔元植), who was in Tokyo, was assassinated by those malcontents (不逞分子) at the "Station Hotel" (스레이슌 호텔). How is it today? There isn't a single one of these malcontents left. They too realize that they must become one body with Japan and maintain peace in East Asia. Therefore, the sentiment is arising among Japanese born in Chosŏn to voluntarily rely on the mainland, wishing for suffrage and military service duty. I believe the Parliamentary Vice-Minister for Colonial Affairs is well aware of this. Furthermore, in the recent North China Incident (北支事變), it goes without saying that a person born in Chosŏn, Unit Commander Kim (金部隊長), is commanding the Imperial Army (皇軍) and playing a major role. Also, a person like Son, who ran the marathon (마라손), while being the world's number one marathon runner on the world stage, flew the Japanese flag (日章旗) at the World Olympics. I believe this too is a good thing for Japan-Korea unity (內鮮一體), and I am very pleased. Thus, I believe this is not the time to talk about being Japanese or Korean; we both have a responsibility to become one body and overcome this difficult situation. However, regarding this military service duty, asking for the exact same duty as mainlanders (內地人) is unreasonable. Why unreasonable? How can you recruit someone as an Imperial soldier (帝國 軍人) who doesn't know the language, who doesn't understand commands like "Right face," "Left face," "Forward march"? However, those who attend school and know the national language well, being born on the continent, are stronger than those born on the islands. If these individuals are tested and recruited as volunteer soldiers—that is, those who themselves wish to become Imperial soldiers—then in times of national emergency, war will not be within the country but will inevitably pass through Chosŏn to Manchuria. At that time, considering geography and climate, Japanese born in Chosŏn must be active on the front lines for the sake of the Great Japanese Empire. I believe politicians must enable them to be active starting now. (Applause) Therefore, even regarding this petition, military service duty is not for everyone. I have submitted petitions to the Petitions Committee several times. Even things like suffrage cannot be granted identically to the mainland. If suffrage is granted with limitations in each province, perhaps to taxpayers paying 10 won or more in national tax per province, allowing one person from each province to come out, it would serve as a reference for those mainlanders lacking awareness of Chosŏn or reflecting Chosŏn's situation in political circles, and at the same time, wouldn't it be very fitting for the nation? Thus, if at least 10 or 20 Japanese born in Chosŏn advance here among the 466 members, I think how wonderful that would be for Japan. I am lonely, having gained a seat here solely as one person born in Chosŏn, thanks to your kindness. But even as just one person, I am one person who has a great impact on the external territories such as Chosŏn, Taiwan (대만), Karafuto (樺太), and the South Seas (南洋). What are they like? They think, "If we also have the Japanese spirit like Pak Ch'un-gŭm and are active in Japan, we too can become Diet members (代議士) and ministers in the future." Just giving them this great expectation and hope, I see, has a great effect. Therefore, truly, since it is already late, we must instill in the Japanese born in Chosŏn a genuine feeling of admiration towards those born in Japan. As the Parliamentary Vice-Minister for Colonial Affairs might know, there are people in this Diet who discuss Manchuria without knowing the situation there. As I mentioned yesterday, when discussing the Manchurian Problem in Japan, I think we must consider Japan as the head, Chosŏn as the stomach, and Manchuria as the feet. No matter how smart the head is, if the stomach is rotten, Manchurian development <4> will not happen. Only when the head and stomach are firmly connected will the development of Manchurian resources not be difficult. From this point, being in Chosŏn, we understand the feelings of the Korean people well. What are they like? Internally, they harbor some discontent, but they see that today's Japan has become a first-rate nation in the world, feared by no country, and that Chosŏn is peacefully governed because of this strength. You must think carefully about this. And regarding Manchuria, even though Japan is undertaking such a large burden to achieve the independence of Manchukuo (滿洲國), isn't it a fact that the world views Japan through colored glasses? However, if you think about whether the 20 million people of Manchuria themselves are happy about Manchurian independence, that's a separate issue. They too harbor discontent internally, so Japan must gird its loins considerably when dealing with Manchuria in the future. The government must remember that the Chinese (支那人) are adept at repaying kindness (恩) with enmity (仇). Therefore, regarding this point, we must grant expectations and hope to the Japanese born in Chosŏn. I request kind responses from the Parliamentary Vice-Minister for Colonial Affairs (拓務政務次官) and the Army Counsellor (陸軍參與官), and at the same time, I hope for its adoption through your sympathy.

Government Committee Member Hisa (比佐政府委員)
I will state the Army's opinion on this proposal. The purport that mainlanders and Koreans (內鮮人) should face the Empire's national defense together without distinction is greatly welcomed. The policy of our Chosŏn administration and the principle of impartial benevolence (一視同仁), establishing no discrimination, are indeed the principles held. However, in terms of popular enlightenment (民度), public sentiment (民情), and other various circumstances, Chosŏn is not identical to the mainland. Therefore, attempting to immediately implement the mainland's conscription system is not only untimely, but also, from the standpoint of personnel recruitment, its necessity is not recognized for the time being. Furthermore, regarding the proposal to implement a volunteer soldier system as a prerequisite for implementing the conscription system, this too is recognized as still premature.

Committee Member Imanari (今成委員)
I would like to ask the proposer: Regarding Mr. Pak's patriotic proposal and opinion, we are quite sympathetic and impressed, but have you investigated, Mr. Pak, how many people from Chosŏn currently serve as commissioned officers on the mainland after graduating from the military academy?

Mr. Pak Ch'un-gŭm (朴春琴君)
That number is extremely small; only 2 or 3 people have graduated from the military academy and become soldiers. Why? Because traditionally, only those related to the Yi Royal Family (李王家) have been accepted until today. The one currently in North China is Unit Commander Kim (金部隊長).

Government Committee Member Hakkaku (八角政府委員)
As Mr. Pak has repeatedly stated, based on the great principle of impartial benevolence (一視同仁) as mentioned in the Imperial Rescript on the Annexation of Korea (日韓合倂の御詔書), advancing Chosŏn's culture, promoting the people's welfare, and realizing the harmonious friendship between Japan and Korea (內鮮の渾然な友誼) are points on which there is no disagreement whatsoever; rather, this is the policy consistently held by the government. Furthermore, in light of the current situation, achieving the substance of national unity (擧國一致) is fundamentally the ideal, and there is no disagreement. However, regarding the implementation of this system, since Chosŏn still differs from the mainland in terms of popular enlightenment (民度) and other various circumstances, attempting to immediately implement the conscription system, or the volunteer soldier system as its prerequisite, requires the most careful study, and we believe the time has not yet come. Furthermore, regarding the volunteer soldier system, please consider carefully that Japan's volunteer soldier system is recognized only on the foundation of the conscription system, so please do not think of this simplistically.

Committee Member Kitahara (北原委員)
I was deeply moved listening to Mr. Pak's words just now. As you all mentioned, the Meiji Emperor established impartial benevolence (一視同仁) as the great spirit of the Chosŏn annexation from the beginning. As the Parliamentary Vice-Minister just said, this should not be called premature; the government must consider how to proceed. Generally, dealing with newly attached peoples (新附の民) is something we mainland Japanese must handle with great consideration. We keep saying it's too early, it's too early, but if it's too early, shouldn't there be a proposal for what to do then? Currently, Mr. Pak has come, become a member of the House of Representatives, and explained things just now – isn't he just the same as us? I think this requires much consideration. You say the conscription and volunteer systems are not possible, but then, does the government have any thoughts on what it will do? I would like to ask this one thing.

Government Committee Member Hakkaku (八角 政府 委員)
The request to implement the conscription system, or to adopt the volunteer soldier system as its prerequisite
(Continued on page 11) <5> (Continuation of the Diet report from page 5) has a history dating back quite some time. Regarding this, we are studying it carefully in accordance with the situation in Chosŏn and have by no means abandoned it. As a result of careful study, the authorities have reached the conclusion that the time has not yet arrived. We have certainly not abandoned it. I hope you will understand this point.

Mr. Pak Ch'un-gŭm (朴春琴氏)
One more word for reference... Currently, along the banks of the Yalu River (鴨綠江 沿岸), Korean policemen are driving out bandits (馬賊) from Manchuria, as they all cross over from the opposite bank into Chosŏn. Among those fighting for the country like this, there are quite many Japanese born in Chosŏn. And as someone who has lived on the mainland for over 30 years, when I hear the usual discontent of Koreans and what they hope for, some speak recklessly, but many say very good things. Also, because I carry the title of Diet member, they think anything can be achieved through me, not knowing the actual situation in the Diet. When I go over to Chosŏn, they speak to me with great expectations. Looking at what they say, their thinking has changed considerably compared to when I went the year before last and when I went this year. The year before last, there was much talk of useless things like national self-determination or Chosŏn XX, but today, they have already become Japanese and agree that they must punish the Chinese in Manchuria. So, when we usually interact with mainlanders, in that situation, if you would love them, then let's be obedient while receiving love. This is like the relationship between an older brother and a younger brother: the older brother loves the younger brother, and the younger brother follows the older brother. To the world as well, I hope you will quickly stop treating those Japanese born in Chosŏn as children and make them into fine Japanese. I believe this is the duty of the older brother. In that sense, we must love them and make them submit to Japan with a true heart. I believe there are many points that require great consideration. Rather than saying the time is early or whatnot—the Parliamentary Vice-Minister for Colonial Affairs probably knows well—what is happening with compulsory education in Chosŏn? How much discontent do Koreans have about that? For example, if 1000 applicants wish to enter elementary school, there is only space to accommodate about 10 of them. Because of this, they hold entrance exams for elementary school students—in what country does such a thing exist? I hope Parliamentary Vice-Minister Hakkaku will also worry about this at some opportunity. As much as possible, on occasions like this, I hope the government will remember to give Koreans the great expectation of becoming Japanese, that is, that the light of Japan illuminates the world (日本の光は世界を 빛초인다는), and put it into practice.

Committee Member Yamazaki (山崎委員)
The opinions have generally been exhausted, and the hearts of us committee members are united, but what is regrettable is the authorities' response. Isn't there something cold in the authorities' response? There may be circumstances, I suppose. Even if there are circumstances, I wish there had been slightly kinder, more sincere words towards the Koreans. I will not particularly say this or that to the authorities, but Mr. Pak is questioning the authorities with an extremely cautious attitude. In response, the authorities' words are indescribably cold, and their attitude also seems mocking. I find this truly regrettable. I hope they will pay special attention to such points and approach such situations accordingly. I hope you will carefully consider the ripples caused by Finance Minister Kaya's (賀屋藏相) attitude in this Diet the other day.
(Someone shouts, "Adopt! Adopt!")

Chairman Aoki (靑木委員長)
Is there any objection to adoption?
(Someone shouts, "No objection!")

Chairman Aoki (靑木委員長)
Recognizing no objection, it is decided to adopt the petition. <11>

**Summary**

This 1937 article from "Samch'ŏlli" magazine documents a debate in the Japanese Diet concerning a petition by Koreans in Tokyo requesting the implementation of military conscription for Koreans in Chosŏn. Pro-Japanese Korean Diet member Pak Ch'un-gŭm passionately argues for at least a volunteer system, framing it as a necessary step towards achieving true Japan-Korea unity (內鮮一體), enabling Koreans to prove their loyalty, contribute to the war effort against China, and counter anti-Japanese sentiment in Manchuria. Government officials (Hisa and Hakkaku) cautiously respond that while the goal of unity is shared, implementing conscription or even a volunteer system is "premature" due to differences in "popular enlightenment" (民度) and other conditions in Chosŏn. Other committee members express sympathy for Pak's plea and criticize the government's cold response. The petition is ultimately adopted by the committee.

The article offers a fascinating glimpse into the complexities of colonial assimilation policy and Korean collaboration/agency during the wartime period. Pak Ch'un-gŭm's rhetoric, emphasizing loyalty, shared destiny against China, and the desire for equal duties (like military service) alongside rights (like suffrage and education), highlights the arguments used by some Koreans seeking integration into the Japanese Empire. The Japanese officials' responses, citing unpreparedness and the need for gradualism, reveal the limits and perhaps the insincerity of the official *isshi dōjin* (impartial benevolence) policy. The mention of discrimination in military academy admissions and the severe lack of educational opportunities further underscores the inequalities faced by Koreans, issues Pak uses to argue *for* greater integration and responsibility. Historians would find the explicit articulation of the collaborator's logic, the official Japanese resistance couched in paternalistic terms ("premature," "popular enlightenment"), and the raw political dynamics of the Diet discussion highly valuable.

「朝鮮 徵兵 問題」와 議會 論議
조선 민중에 대하여는 「徵兵 制度 實施」문제와 「參政權」문제와 「義務敎育 實施」의 세 가지 당면한 중대 문제가 잇다. 그 중에도 日中事變을 계기로 現下 國家非常之秋를 당함애 朝鮮人 徵兵 問題는 다시 一世의 주목을 끄을면서 재등장하엿스니 지난 8월 4일 準戰時體制를 확립하든 第71 議會 壇上에서도 衆議院 議員과 政府 委員 사이에 白熱的의 논의가 잇섯섯다. 이제 당일의 議事 全文을 게재하건대.

朴春琴君

또 한마듸 참고적으로... 그런데 금일 鴨綠江 沿岸에서 朝鮮人 순사가 그 馬賊을 滿洲로부터 逐出하고 있음으로, 전부 朝鮮에 對岸으로부터 건너옴니다. 여기에 대하여 이러틋 나라를 위하야 應戰하고 있는 자는 자못 朝鮮 출생인 日本人이 많은 것입니다. 그리하여 나는 종래 內地에 30여 년이나 居하는 한사람으로서 朝鮮人의 평소의 불평을 가지고 있는 것, 그리고 彼等의 희망하고 있는 것을 내가 드를 때 그 중에는 함부로 말하는 자도 있으나 매우 좋은 말을 하고 있는 자가 만슴니다. 그리고 내가 議員이라는 간판을 가지고 있음으로 의회 중의 如斯한 實情을 모름으로 나를 통하면 무었이나 될 줄 알고 朝鮮에 건너가면 그들은 큰 기대를 가지고 나의게 말합니다. 그 말하는 것을 보아 재작년 건너갔을 때와 금년에 건너갔슬 때와는 대단히 사상이 변하여 젔읍니다. 재작년만 하드래도 쓸데없는 민족자결이라든지 朝鮮XX이라는 말이 많었으나 금일은 벌서 彼等은 日本人이 되어 滿洲에서 支那人을 혼내지 안으면 안된다는데에 일치하고 있읍니다. 그래서 우리들은 평소에 內地人들과 접촉해보면, 그 경우에 역시 여러분이 사랑해 주었으면, 그리고 사랑을 받으며 順從하자. 그런 것은 형과 아우의 관계로 형은 동생을 사랑하고 弟는 兄을 따른다는 것으로, 세계에 대하여도 하로밧비 저 朝鮮 出生 日本人을 어리다 어리다 하지만 말고 아름다운 日本人을 만드러 주었으면 합니다. 이는 형으로서의 의무라고 생각합니다. 그런 점에서 彼等을 사랑하여 참마음으로 일본에 屈服하게 안으면 안될 것입니다. 이는 크게 고려치 안으면 안되리라는 점이 만히 있다고 생각하는 바임으로 시기가 일느다 어떻다 하느니 보다-拓務政務次官은 잘 아시늊� 바이겠지요만 朝鮮의 의무교육이란 것이 어찌되여있읍니까. 거긔 대하여 朝鮮人이 얼마나한 불평을 가지고 있읍니까. 예컨대 1000人 입학할 희망자가 있다면 수용할 장소는 그 중에 겨우 10인 정도입니다. 그것 때문에 소학생에게 대하여 시험보혀 입학시키지마는 이런 것이 어느 나라에 있읍니까. 이것도 어느 기회에 八角 政務次官께서 걱정해 주시기를 바라는 바입니다. 될 수 있는 대로 如斯한 기회에 朝鮮人을 日本人이 되게 하여 준다는 큰 기대를 줄 것 즉 日本의 光은 세계를 빛초인다는 것을 잊지 말고 정부가 실행해 주시기를 바라는 바입니다.
