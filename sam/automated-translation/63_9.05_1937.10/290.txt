A Record of a Penniless Trip Around the World, Sentenced to Death While Crossing the Soviet-Manchurian Border
						By Yu Sang-gŭn
The truly astonishing tale of Mr. Yu, a 23-year-old traveler who set out on a journey around the world without a penny in his pocket, experienced various hardships unimaginable for ordinary people, saw strange lands, and returned.

Seeking a New World – Interest in Columbus's Discovery of America Since Elementary School
From a young age, I enjoyed traveling, and I found particular interest in traveling on foot daily. My long-cherished desire was to explore unknown places before others did.
Even during my elementary school days, when my teacher engagingly recounted tales of Columbus's discovery of America and Robinson Crusoe's shipwreck, the ambition to tread every corner of the six continents with my own small feet unknowingly swelled within my chest as days and years passed. By the time I reached middle school, the beautiful and mysterious mountains, rivers, customs, and myriad phenomena blooming within geography and history intensely fanned the flames of my longing, leading me to trace travel routes on the world map with a red pencil countless times. Then, in Taishō 13 (1924), after graduating from the 3rd year of the Private Chungdong School in Kyŏngsŏng and briefly attending to family matters, I resolved to fulfill my lifelong wish and departed from my beloved hometown on June 1, Shōwa 5 (1930). At that moment, a self-awareness, almost a reflection, flashed through my mind like lightning. It was: I am a human born of Chosŏn. Therefore, I must first tour my peninsula. And the meager travel funds I possessed would be completely spent after barely a week... Wouldn't the heavy burden of acquiring bread then impede my steps? However, I once again regretted that thought. Wouldn't it be a hundred, a thousand times more valuable a journey for me to walk, shaking hands equally with all humanity, regardless of the high or low status of races scattered across the globe, regardless of the superiority or inferiority of peoples, through my own true effort, rather than wealthy individuals easily traveling by constructing bridges of gold across the entire world without shedding a drop of sweat? At this moment, the vigorously blue sky smiled perfectly above my head, and my footsteps on the ten-thousand-li journey felt light as if flying. Time flowed. One year and one month – during that time, I toured various places in Chosŏn, and the following July, I crossed the Hyŏnhae T'an (Genkai Sea) again and completely traversed the Japanese mainland's 3 prefectures and 43 districts, as well as Hokkaidō and Karafuto. Having thus completed all these travels by January of Shōwa 8 (1933), I planned to head towards Europe through the Soviet continent. I returned home and recovered my fatigued mind and body somewhat with about a week's rest, then set out walking towards the Soviet border from North Chosŏn, Sŏsura, and the opposite bank.
The fierce wind of severe winter shook every branch of the old trees standing by the roadside, and the road's earth was frozen solid like steel; small dust clouds swirled and vanished in the ruts of oxcarts. The ash-colored sky, spitting snow driven by the blizzard, hung low as if to embrace everything, and the chill penetrating the body felt truly as if tearing flesh.
To understand the tense border situation in more detail, I visited the Japanese police station responsible for border security. I showed them my travel records, explained my travel course, received brief advice, and then, waving my hat, briskly crossed the border over the ice of the Tuman River.

At the Soviet-Manchurian Border – Accused of Espionage by the GPU and Arrested
The winding path was buried in white snow, and the forest, standing dense like bamboo stalks, screamed, unable to withstand the blizzard. Suddenly appearing before my eyes were two GPU agents, seated on large horses, peering intently with eyes sunk deep by perhaps a foot, aiming their guns, and shouting. They silently gestured with their hands for me to walk ahead. For about an hour, I walked silently as they directed. Thus, I was led to a guard post as messy as an outhouse, where my belongings and pockets were first searched, and then I was interrogated. Among them, the older-looking GPU agent asked, "Did the Japanese police chief order you to enter the country when you crossed the border?" I replied in my poor Russian, "My world travel course was planned to pass through this continent to Europe, so I entered without permission." Hearing this, he retorted, "You brat! Stop the nonsense! If your purpose is world travel, there would be a more definite route. You undoubtedly entered with the objective of spying on our country's secrets, a spy for the XX military!" Saying this, I was immediately placed under their cold surveillance. I tried my best to explain, but it had no effect. Unable to sleep a wink in the unbearably cold detention cell, worrying about an uncertain future, I passed the long night. The next morning, after eating a piece of bread and drinking a cup of hot water they brought, my waist was tightly bound with a sturdy rope. Driven by two mounted GPU agents, I walked, treading through snow and battling the wind. Even then, I repeated the purpose of my journey several times. Their whips landed mercilessly on my head, so feeling there was no point in speaking further to them, I walked silently. If I didn't walk fast enough, they would push me with the horse's head and beat me severely. As evening fell that day, we reached a small town called "P'oset'ŭ" (Posyet), a distance of 135 *ri*. My limbs had completely lost sensation, and my stomach was so empty it now ached. There too, I ate one piece of bread and drank a cup of hot water. And I chuckled bitterly, wondering if this simple meal might be my last. Inside the detention cell, fresh straw was spread on the dirt floor, resembling a pigsty. However, it was rather warmer than a wooden floor without heating in the dead of winter. The next day, during the interrogation,
the interrogator was a Korean. Hearing Korean spoken for the first time in days, and seeing that yellow complexion, how affectionate it felt! I explained in one breath the significance of my journey, the reasons for entering this country, and how my records clearly explained everything. The Korean <36> interrogator asked again, "Where and who provides the huge travel expenses for your world tour?" I replied, "I visit schools or public organizations, talk about my experiences, receive some donations, and barely manage to travel with the sympathy of various gentlemen of influence." Upon hearing this, he replied coldly, "You brat, stop lying! You are an international spy sent by the Japanese government with supplied funds to spy on the secrets within our Soviet country. Therefore, according to Soviet military law, you will be shot. If you confess honestly, I will sympathize and try to save your life." And so, I finally received the ultimate sentence. When I received the death sentence, even from the Korean whom I had been so glad to see and had placed such hope in, I felt an unparalleled hatred towards that Korean man. With nowhere in the world to appeal, only the sound of the gunshots that would take my life awaited me. Thinking of myself dying a worthless death in the harsh northern country, my ambitions unfulfilled, hot tears flowed ceaselessly. Having already spent five days in P'oset'ŭ, awaiting the final moment, wondering if it would be now or later, yet clinging to the extremely faint hope that my proven innocence might save my life, I was led away by them again at dawn to the second courthouse in "Sŭrabayakk'i" (Slavyanka?). There, I was imprisoned for 40 days.
During that time, Russian prisoners could receive bread whether they worked or not, but Chinese and Koreans often could not get bread even if they worked. Also, although they claimed no difference in living standards between upper and lower classes, I saw generals eating white bread while lower-ranking soldiers ate black bread, making me think there were distinctions here too.
At that time, the people imprisoned in the detention center there were 23 Koreans, 3 Chinese, and 6 Russians. One day, a young Korean man named Yi among them pointed at me and explained to the numerous detainees: "This young man came from mainland Korea and is a Japanese military spy. Therefore, he is a person absolutely intolerable to us Soviet citizens." Hearing this, all the detainees clenched their fists, hurled all sorts of abuse, and began to close in on me as I sat silently, utterly dumbfounded. Then, an older-looking Russian among them commanded as if giving an order: "Beat him to death with your fists, throw him into the latrine pit, and dispose of him at night." Facing this imminent crisis, as a last and only resort, I quickly stood up and, with a tearful voice, recounted my past experiences, striving to convey my innocent intentions to them. I forcefully stated that the records I possessed were the most certain proof that clearly spoke of my past. To what extent my words calmed their agitation, I don't know, but they lowered their raised arms and sat back down in their places. Heavy silence once again filled the dark detention cell. Cold sweat ran down my back, and my bloodshot eyes couldn't see. I felt fortunate to have extended my life, even if only for an hour.
Furthermore, the rule for distributing food in that country was: workers received 1,000 grams of bread per day, farmers 700 grams per day, office workers 600 grams, and prisoners 400 grams. To receive this distribution, one had to go to a designated workplace office with both a certificate of employment and money to purchase the allotted amount of bread. For prisoners especially, the government provided 400 grams of bread, but instead of dividing it into three meals a day, they gave it all at once and told us to divide it ourselves for three meals. For someone as hungry as me, dividing such a small amount of bread into three meals was truly agonizing. Also, in the morning and evening, they specially distributed one raw sardine each. For a while, I couldn't eat the raw sardine. It smelled fishy and seemed dirty, so I couldn't eat my bread with it beside me. Later, forget about it being dirty, I ate every bit, even the bones, and still wished there was more. After about a week passed,

Alas, Awaiting Heaven's Decree – From Death Sentence to Expulsion
One day, the GPU chief called for me. Like a mouse trembling before a cat, I went to the office. Beneath an unadorned table where charcoal embers glowed, 4 or 5 Russian GPU agents and a Korean interpreter sat in a circle. Their ash-colored eyes, emitting needle-like gazes, focused entirely on me. I bowed to them in respect, and they gestured for me to sit. The interrogation began. The GPU chief, who appeared to be about 6 feet 5 inches tall, asked questions in a slow tone, which the Korean interpreter translated into Korean. "You say you are on a world tour. How do you prove you are a world traveler?" "Wouldn't you know by looking at the signatures I received in Chosŏn and mainland Japan?" I replied thus. Hearing my response, the interrogator gave a sneering laugh and said, "But we don't know if this name is truly yours. How can we prove you didn't steal someone else's?" I answered again. "The fact that I am making a world trip has been written about extensively in newspapers in various regions of the mainland. Not only were there articles, but my photograph was also published. If you compare that photograph with my current face, you will clearly know." Only then did the interrogator say – "Pravda" (this means 'correct' in Russian) – and told me to go back to the detention cell. Afterwards, I spent three anxious days in uncertainty and anticipation. Would my fate truly meet its end here, or would I be rehabilitated? My life, weak as a flickering candle flame in the wind, could not rest. Three days later, a low-ranking GPU soldier came to the front of the cell, pointed at me, said – "Posly" – (Russian word for 'depart') – and ordered me to prepare. I was certain they were taking me to be shot. The weather was relatively warm. The snow on the road had melted slightly, and water sloshed under my shoes. However, after being imprisoned for such a long time, my body was weak. Walking 300 *ri* day and night, I felt like just collapsing and begging them to kill me, <37> but still wondering where this road led and hoping against hope for a different outcome, I gritted my teeth and walked with all my might. We finally arrived at "Nŭbulmi" at 5 o'clock the next morning. Seeing the sky redden over the distant mountaintop directly ahead of us, I realized that direction was east, and that walking 300 *ri* east meant this place was the tri-border area of Japan, Manchuria, and the Soviet Union. I thought I would be released without charge and sent either to Chosŏn or Manchuria. However, the attitude of the GPU agent escorting me was so stern that it didn't seem like a simple release. As a last resort, I considered escaping and looked for grassy fields, but there was no grassland beside the road, only thick white snow. After walking for several more hours, we reached a place, the name of which I didn't know, but it was definitely the border. They led me into a border guard post. Inside, three GPU agents were dozing. Then, one of the GPU agents who had brought me said, "Since there is proof that you are a world traveler, we will send you back to Chosŏn from here." After saying this, he gave me three pieces of bread and a bottle of milk, telling me to eat this and wait here until night. However, I couldn't trust their words. And thinking that these three pieces of bread and bottle of milk might be my last meal, I couldn't bring myself to hold it and eat. Also, I suspected the place they called "Chosŏn" might be where they executed spies like XX. I stayed in the guard post's detention cell during the day, and around eight in the evening, two GPU agents came again with rifles and told me to walk. Oh – thinking now, truly, I would be forever erased from the roster of all humanity, I decided to die quietly and obediently if I had to die, and walked silently as ordered. After walking south for about three hours, the mirror-like ice of the Sea of Japan shone white and blue. There was nowhere further to walk. This shore must be my execution ground; tears blurred my vision, and I couldn't see anything. If I were sacrificing myself for my ambitions, that would be gratifying, but dying under false accusations was even more infuriating. "Go around this headland (도래미) and proceed along the coast to Chosŏn." They pointed towards the headland and told me this. I walked along the coast towards the headland. Feeling as if bullets might fly from behind at any moment, I quickened my pace frantically. When I rounded the headland and looked back, fortunately, those two terrifying figures were gone. They must have seen me cross into Chosŏn territory and turned back. Thus, by the grace of Heaven, truly escaping death by a hair's breadth, I once again set foot on my beloved native soil.

**Summary**

This travelogue excerpt by Yu Sang-gŭn recounts his youthful ambition to travel the world pennilessly, beginning with tours of Korea and Japan. In 1933, attempting to enter the Soviet Union via the Korean border to reach Europe, he was arrested by the Soviet GPU. Despite showing his travel records, he was accused of being a Japanese spy, interrogated harshly (including by a Korean Soviet official who condemned him), and sentenced to death. Imprisoned for over 40 days under dire conditions and facing hostility even from fellow Korean inmates, he narrowly avoided execution after further interrogation seemed to verify his identity as a traveler through newspaper photos. Ultimately, he was escorted to the border and expelled back into Korea, narrowly escaping death.

From a historical perspective, the article vividly illustrates the extreme dangers and suspicions prevalent at the volatile Soviet-Manchurian-Korean border in the 1930s. It highlights the precarious situation of Koreans under Japanese colonial rule, who were often viewed with suspicion by neighboring powers like the Soviet Union, especially if originating from Japan-controlled territory. The account of being accused by a fellow Korean official working for the Soviets, and later by Korean inmates, points to the complex political divisions and mistrust within the Korean community itself during this period. Historians might find the detailed description of the arrest, interrogation process (including the specific accusations and the author's defense), prison conditions (differential treatment based on nationality), and the eventual expulsion valuable for understanding Soviet border control practices, international espionage fears, and the lived experiences of Koreans navigating the geopolitical tensions of the era.

새 世界를 차저-소학생 때 콜넘버스의 米州 발견에 흥미
나는 어려서부터 여행을 즐겨하였고 여행하게 되면 日常 도보로서 하는 것이 흥미 있었다. 세인들보담 남 먼저 미지의 그 곳을 탐방할여는 것이 나의 숙망이였다.
보통학교 시절에 가끔 선생이 콜넘버스의 亞米利加 발견담과 로빈손의 표류담 등을 慈味스럽게 이야기하는 것을 들을 때부터도 언제나 나의 적은 발노 6대주의 방방곡곡을 삿삿치 밟어 보리란 포부는 날이 가고, 해가 갈수록 남모르게 나의 가슴속에서 부푸러 올넜다. 어느듯 중학 시대가 되매 지리와 역사 속에서 방글방글 피여나는 千態萬像으로 아름답고 신비스러운 山河風習은 기어코 나의 동경의 정열에다 강열한 부채질을 한 남어지 세계지도에다 붉은 연필노 여행의 코-스를 몇 번이라 없이 그리였다. 그러다가 大正 13년에 京城 私立 中東學校 3년을  畢業하고 잠간 家事에 일보다가 평생에 소원을 달성할 결심으로 昭和 5년 6월 1일에 정든 고향 산천을 등지고 출발하였다. 그 순간 나의 머리에 반성에 가까운, 자각이 번개처럼 번쩍이였다. 그것은 나는 朝鮮이 낳은 인간이다. 그래서 나는 위선 나의 반도를 歷訪하여 보아야 하리라. 그리고 내가 소지한 빈약한 여비는 불과 1주일 후이면 남김없이 소비되고 말 것을...그러면 나의 등에는 빵의 습득이란 무거운 重荷가 나의 발작욱을 阻止시킬 것이 아닌가. 그러나 나는 또 한번 그것을 뉘웃첬다. 裕足한 인간들이 세계의 全幅에다 황금으로 교량을 가설하고 땀 한 방울 흘님 없이 용이히 여행하는 것보담 내 자신이 참다운 노력으로 지구 표면에 널녀 있는 종족의 귀천과 민족의 우열을 가릴 것 없이 一律히 전 인류와 악수하며 걸어가는 것이 백 배나 천 배나 가치 있는 여행이 아닐까. 이 때 힘것 푸른 대공은 나의 머리 우에서 完美롭게 미소하였고 萬里旅*의 나의 발자욱은 나는 듯이 가벼웟다. 세월은 흘넜다. 1년 1개월을- 그 동안에 나도 朝鮮의 곳곳을 歷訪하고 이듬해 7월 다시 玄海 灘을 건너 일본 內地의 3부 43현과 北海道 믿 樺太까지 전혀 답파하였다. 그래서 이 모든 길의 여행을 昭和 8년 정월까지 맡이고 인제야 쏘베-트 대륙을 통과하여 歐洲 방면으로 향할 예정으로 귀향하여 疲勞된 심신을 약 일주 간의 안식으로서 다소간 회복하여 가지고 北鮮, 西水羅, 對岸으로서 쏘베-트 국경을 향하여 것께 되였다.
엄동의 사나운 바람이 路邊에 서 있는 고목의 가지가지를 뒤흔들어 놓고 도로의 흙은 얼어  붙어 강철처럼 땅땅한 牛車 다니는 호국에 조-그만 먼지가 맴돌다 사라젓다. 눈보라맞어 뿜어내는 재빛깔 하늘은 어루맍일 듯이 났고 몸에 숨어드는 치움은 그야말노 살을 뜯어내는 듯 하였다.
나는 험연한 국경의 정세를 좀더 세세히 알기 위하야 국경 경비의 일본 경찰서를 방문하였다. 그리고 나의 경과하여 온 기록을 보이며 내 여행의 코-쓰를 말하고 간단한 주의를 받은 후에 두만강 빙상으로 모자를 흔들면서 활발하게 越境하였다.
