Classical Jokes

Dried Persimmons Too
A certain gentleman (yangban), though of distinguished name, found it difficult to make a living and, after much thought, decided to become a persimmon seller. He set out for the village carrying a load of dried persimmons on his A-frame carrier. However, the cry "Dried persimmons for sale!" (곳감 사시오) simply wouldn't come out of his throat due to his aristocratic pride. But if he were to say "Buy dried persimmons!" (곳감 사게), he would likely get beaten by the villagers for being impolite. Just then, a salt seller passed by, crying "Salt for sale!" (소곰*오). The gentleman quickly followed behind him, calling out "Dried persimmons too—" (곳감도-)

The Deaf Family
All five members of a household were deaf.<40> One day, a friend from the neighboring house came looking for the master and said, "Sir scholar (샌님), could you please lend me a hoe (鍬)?"
The master replied, "My traditional hat (冠) cost five *ton*."
"No, please give me the hoe."
"I'm telling you, that hat of mine cost five *ton*."
The villager went back with nothing accomplished. The master then told his wife, "So-and-so from next door asked about my hat, so I told him it cost five *ton*."
The wife, observing her husband's lips moving, called for her daughter-in-law and said, "Bring the fruit I told you to prepare nicely for guests. Your father-in-law is asking for it."
The daughter-in-law, a widow who had lost her husband, pouted and said, "Widow, widow! No matter how much of a widow I am, who likes it when you call me a widow day and night?"
Hearing this, the *haengnang* woman (female servant living in the servants' quarters) said, "Goodness, what's in the storehouse (광) that you keep fussing day and night about whether the storehouse door is closed or not?"
The *haengnam* man (male servant living in the servants' quarters) then said, "Why are you telling me to go to Kwach'ŏn (果川) again? I just went there recently. What business is there that I should go again?"

The Scariest Thing
The scariest thing in this world is a tiger (범).
No, it's not. It's a robber (强盜).
No, it's not. It's a *yangban* (aristocrat).<41>
No, it's not. It's a *yangban* robber riding a tiger!

The Quick-Witted Daughter-in-Law
A mother-in-law, angered that her daughter-in-law only slept day and night, scolded her, "The sun is short (짤는데 - i.e., the day is short), yet you don't do needlework and only take naps?"
The daughter-in-law retorted, "Far from being short (짤느기), the sun is just round (둥글기만)."
"Your way with words (말솜씨) is quite good."
"Even if you say the village (마을) is good, there isn't a single tile-roofed house in this village."
"Perhaps you'll carry one *mal* (말 - measure unit)."
"Carrying one *mal* is light, carrying two *mal* is heavy."
The mother-in-law, utterly flabbergasted, said, "You must have missed sleep (잠을 밋젓구나 - i.e., stayed up) during the long, long nights of the winter solstice month planning such retorts."
"Long? It's the nights nowadays that are long (길어요). Today, father went to the market and bought a *mal* (말) of chestnuts (밤), but far from being long (길기는), they were just round and round (동글동글)."

The Governor and the Headman
The Governor (府使) of Andong (安東), wanting to test the wisdom of the local Headman (座首), asked:
Governor: "That wall is a stone wall (돌담, 石垣), but why isn't it turning (돌지)?" (Pun: 돌 means both stone and to turn/spin)
Headman: "Because it is an *entire* (맨) stone wall, it cannot turn." (Pun: 맨 can mean 'entire' or imply 'tied/bound' [縳])<42>

The Chancellor and His Subordinate
Once upon a time, a Chancellor (宰相) and one of his subordinates sat facing each other, engaging in wordplay.
Chancellor: What is a swallow (제비) sitting and crying on a peach (복송아) tree?
Subordinate: It is T'ao Yüan-ming (陶淵明) (Pun: 桃 peach, 燕 swallow, 鳴 cry).
Chancellor: Impressive. Now, you try one.
Subordinate: What is a cicada (매암) falling from a plum (왜-지나무) tree?
Chancellor: That's Li Chê-hsien (李謫仙) (Pun: 李 plum, 滴 fall/drip, 蟬 cicada).

Just Like You
A lady who had already given birth to seven daughters became pregnant again and went into labor. The Chancellor (宰相) waited in the room across, separated by a window. After a short while, the baby cried out, "Waaah!"
"How did it go? Is it a boy this time? Or a girl?"
The wife, too ashamed to say it was yet another girl, replied, "Well, it's just like you."
The Chancellor sighed despondently, "Then I suppose the bottom half is just like you."

Majority
A certain country scholar (村夫子) had no sons, only twelve daughters. When a friend asked about the number of children under his roof (슬하), he replied, "I only have five daughters (딸), five girls (게집얘), and two female offspring (女息)."<43>

**Summary**

This article presents a collection of short, humorous anecdotes and jokes titled "Classical Jokes." The jokes rely heavily on wordplay (puns), misunderstandings arising from deafness, social satire involving the aristocracy (*yangban*), clever retorts, and observations on family life, such as the preference for sons. The humor often stems from literal interpretations of figurative language or exploiting the multiple meanings of Korean words. From a historical perspective, the collection offers a glimpse into popular humor and social commentary during the Japanese colonial period in Korea (1930). The persistence of traditional social structures (like the *yangban* class, even if impoverished), family dynamics, and the sophisticated use of linguistic humor mixing Hanja and Hangul are notable for historians studying culture and society of that era.

곳감도
명색이 대단히 휼융한 양반 한 분이 살아가기 곤란하야 생각다 못하야 감장수로 나섯것다. 곳감 한 지게 질머지고 동리로 나섯스나
「곳감 사시오」
하는 선소리가 양반 체면에 참아 목구멍에서 나오지 안는다. 그러타고
「곳감사게」 한다면 동리놈들에게 버르장이 업다고 매나 어더 마즐 것이다. 그럴 지음에 마츰 소곰장이가
「소곰*오」
하고 지나간다. 양반이 얼는 뒷 따라
「곳감도-」

귀먹어리
집안 식솔 다섯 식구가 모다 귀먹어리다.＜40＞ 하로는 이웃집 친구가 차저 와서 주인더러
「샌님 괭이(鍬) 좀 빌너주시오.」
하고 말하니 주인은
「내 관(冠) 닷돈 주엇네.」
「아니 괭이 좀 주서요.」
「그놈 내 관 닷돈 주엇다니까.」
동리사람이 할 일 업시 도라갓다. 주인은 제 안해더러
「이웃 집 아모개가 관을 뭇기에 내 관 닷돈 주엇다 햇지.」
안해는 남편의 입이 돌아가는 것을 살피고 머느리를 불너
접대 잘 두라고 부탁한 과일 가저오너라. 아버님께서 찻는구나.」
며누리는 남편을 일흔 과부인지라 입을 빗죽거리며
「과부, 과부하니, 내가 아모리 과부지만 밤낫 과부라고만 하면 누가 조타나뇨.」
그 말을 듯는 행낭어멈이
「아이공, 광에 무엇이 잇다고 밤낫 광문 다첫소 마럿소 하오?」
행남아범이
「과천(果川)은 웨 또 가라고. 접대 갓다 왓는데 무슨 일이 잇서 또 가라나요.」

뎨일 무서운 것
이 세상에서 뎨일 무서운 것이 범이지.
아니야. 强盜야.
아니야. 양반이야.＜41＞
아니야. 양반의 강도가 범을 타고 오는 것이야!

입심 조흔 며느리
며느리가 밤낫 잠만 자는데 화가 나서 시어미가
「해는 짤는데 바느질은 아니하고 낫잠만 자느냐.」
그러니 며느리가
「해가 짤느기 커녕 둥글기만 합레다.」
「네 말솜씨가 좃코나.」
「말(洞里)이 조타 하여도 이 마을에는 기와집 하나 업습데다.」
「행혀 한 말이나 질나.」
「한말 지면 가비엽고 두말 지면 무겁지요.」
시어머니 너무도 긔가 막혀
「너는 동지섯달 긴긴밤에 저런 궁리하느라고 잠을 밋젓구나.」
「길기는 요새 밤이 길어요. 오늘 노마아비가 장에 가서 밤 한 말 사왓는데 길기는 커녕 동글동글합데다.」

府使와 座首
安東府使가 座首의 지혜를 시험할 次로
府使 「저 담은 돌담(石垣=돌은 돈다는 뜻)이지만 엇재서 돌지 안코 잇서?」
座首 「맨(모든...縳의 意) 돌담이니 돌지 못함니다.」＜42＞

宰相과 部下
녯날 재상과 그 부하 한 명이 마조 안저 입작난한다.
재상=복송아 나무에 제비 안저 우는 것이 무엇일가.
부하=陶淵明(桃燕鳴)이올시다.
재상=용하군 그래. 자네 하나 하여 보게.
부하=왜-지나무에서 매암이 떠러지는 것이 무엇이오니까.
재상=李嫡仙(李滴蟬)이지.

우만 당신과 갓해
딸만 일곱을 나은 부인이 또 아히를 배어 해산하게 되엇다. 宰相은 창문을 隔하여 저쪽 방에서 기다릴 때에 조곰 잇다가 「으아」하고 아해가 낫다.
「엇더케 되엇고 이번에는 사내요? 게집애요?」
안해는 또 다시 게집애가 낫다고 할 염치가 업서서
「우만 당신과 꼭 갓틈니다.」
재상이 撫然히 탄식하며
「그러면 아래는 자네와 갓단 말이구나.」

多數
엇던 村夫子가 사내자식은 하나도 업고 게집애만 열둘을 두고 잇섯다. 엇든 친구가 슬하의 자녀수를 무르니
「딸 다섯에 게집얘 다섯에 女息 둘이 잇슬 뿐이요.」＜43＞
