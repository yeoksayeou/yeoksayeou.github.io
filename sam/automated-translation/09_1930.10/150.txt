**Confessions of Love Letters**

**During My Tokyo Study Abroad Days**
**Ch'oe Rin**

When I was studying abroad in Tokyo during my youth, I happened to fall passionately in love with a Japanese woman named M, and I have memories of exchanging many "love letters." Since those were the youthful days full of regrets, that woman also loved me deeply, and I couldn't bear it if I didn't see her, longing for her. When we met, we laughed and cried together. I still remember it now. That woman had already finished higher girls' school then and had received much ideological baptism, so her talent and insight in writing were far superior to mine. Her appearance was also on the beautiful side.

We exchanged love for several years, and accordingly, we exchanged many love letters. But back then, unlike now, we inevitably ended up parting ways due to the differences in our countries and ethnicities. Apart from this one woman, I haven't had the chance to write anything called a love letter since. As for the future, who knows???

**One Thousand Two Hundred Letters in Eight Years**
**Hŏ Yŏng-suk**

I have only ever written love letters to one person, Ch'unwŏn (Spring Garden, pen name of Yi Kwang-su), and I have only ever received them from him. However, just once, I received a letter from some man containing sentiments similar to romantic feelings, and I sternly replied with my intention that it was unacceptable.

However, the letters with Ch'unwŏn were certainly not love letters from the very beginning. When I was enrolled at Tokyo Women's Medical College (東京女子醫專), he came to our school hospital because he had signs of pulmonary tuberculosis to have his bloody sputum (血啖) tested. Then, starting with his first letter asking whether there were indeed bacilli in the bloody sputum or not, questions about medical matters, such as what kind of medicine would be good to use, came frequently. So, at first, I only sent replies about medicine. Then, the name he used to call me, "Teacher" (선생), subtly changed from my given name Yŏng-suk (英肅) by just taking the character "Yŏng" (英) to become "Yŏng-mae" (英妹, Younger Sister Yŏng), and later, letters came calling me "Yŏng" (英), "Yŏng" (英) <62>, and the content also...

At first, I also called him "Teacher" (선생), but later I wrote "Du" (뚜, German: Du) in the letters. "Du" is a word used only within a family in German. For example, it's used when a father calls his son, a sister calls her older brother, or a wife calls her husband.

However, times were different then compared to now, and even though we lived in the same city of Tokyo, there were no opportunities to meet. In an era where just the rumor that a young woman wrote a letter to another man would lead to condemnation, how could I dare to visit his boarding house? Could he also come to my dormitory? So, we could only exchange letters. That was when I was eighteen, so recalling it now from the age of thirty-five, it's already a distant past of 18 years ago.

During the approximately 6 years I was in Tokyo, I must have received about 1,000 letters from him. After he went to Shanghai, about 200 more came. However, during the house search at the time of the March 1st Movement (3·1運動), most of those carefully collected 1,000-plus letters were confiscated by the police. So afterwards, I went to the police station and managed to coax them into returning about 50 letters. The ones remaining now are kept in a small separate box. I'll publish them after Ch'unwŏn dies and I die too. Or perhaps, when we are both much older and hear ourselves called grandma and grandpa. Wouldn't that be right?

**Always Wrote at Dawn**
**Im Wŏn-gŭn**

When I was in Shanghai, I wrote quite a lot to H. Quite a lot flew in from H as well. I usually wrote letters to my lover by deliberately waking up around three or four in the dawn.

"While all sounds are utterly silent (萬籟俱寂), my beloved, you must be wandering through the tranquil land of dreams at this hour. But this body, unable to attain dreams or sleep, has risen alone in the dark night and taken up this lonely brush to confide in you."

In that style, using many passionate phrases that seem amusing now, I wrote long letters of five or ten pages. After writing, I would enclose a petal from a flower in the vase in my room or a couple of leaves of "forget-me-not" (닛지 말난 풀) and send it sealed.

After sending it, I would eagerly await the reply, as if on fire. Considering that the letter had to cross the distant Yellow Sea (黃海), reach the home country, and be delivered to her by a Seoul postman (遞傳夫), quite a lot of time was required for one exchange of letters. Therefore, the feeling of waiting was extraordinary.

Besides H, I have memories of writing to two other women. But I will omit this. And in the letters, when addressing the other person, I would first put 5 dots, for example, if it was 'My dove-like H!', I would put 7 dots. If it was 'My beloved H!', I would just put four dots and write 'Miss H!'. Then, at the very end, I would write 'Your R is' or 'Your person!' and use the capital letter of my name. I think there is no writing as revealing of human nature and as beautiful as a love letter, but while wandering through Moscow (莫斯科) and Shanghai, I lost some of those letters and burned others.

**Planning to Write When I Turn Sixty**
**Kim Pyŏng-no**

"Love letters" should be a symbol of youth, but unfortunately, I spent my young days wandering around Tokyo and the Samnam (三南) regions in worn-out clothes and tattered shoes (弊袍破履), so I never had the chance to turn my emotions towards such matters.

Indeed, sitting here today, having passed the peak of 40, I don't even have the memory of writing a letter to someone of the opposite sex. I haven't even exchanged correspondence with the person who is my wife in the 30-odd years since our marriage. So clean, should I say, or so desolate.

I heard that even the famous General Aguinaldo (애기날드) of the Philippines (比律賓) wrote a love letter to some young girl recently, when he was over 60, and even got married. It's not that I'm imitating him, but perhaps when my body reaches the age of 60 (回甲), I really want to try writing just one "love letter." It would be too sad to die without ever having written a charming affectionate note (情札) in one's entire life (人生 一生中)... Haha. <63>

**About Fifty Letters**
**Chu Yo-han**

I remember writing about 50 love letters, and almost all of them were written while I was attending school in Nanking (南京) to someone in Chosŏn (朝鮮). When I sent a letter written with all my heart from here, a reply expressing a certainly pure heart would surely come floating across the Yellow Sea from there. So, over here, fired up with passion, I couldn't help but write again and again. Thinking back now, it seems to have been quite fierce. As a result of sending those "love letters," I gained the person with whom I now live together, having sons and daughters, so the effect could be called 101 percent, I suppose.

I initiated the letters first. Women rarely initiate, you know. That's probably the fault of propriety and shame. And when addressing the other person, I just wrote her name followed by the character 'ssi' (氏). For example, My Miss Ch'oe XX...

I believe there's no writing as pure and passionate as a love letter. Thinking those letters from her were very precious in this sense, I kept them all carefully stored in a desk drawer, but when leaving Nanking, I ended up burning them all. It would have been nice if I still had them now.

**During Girls' School Days, in the Restroom**
**U Pong-un**

After throwing myself into social movements, I haven't written anything called a love letter, but during my girls' school days at Chŏngsin Girls' School (貞信女學校) when I was under twenty, I wrote quite a lot. Not only that, but my love letters continued by my hand even during the three years I was a teacher at Kyesŏng Girls' School (啓星女學校) in Taegu (大邱) after finishing that school. The place I wrote letters to was a young man named Ki T'ae-jin (奇泰鎭); handsome face, fine physique, good knowledge and ideas – oh dear, I've written all sorts of amusing things. Anyway, that young man who was my lover, it was only because his family had no money, otherwise he was quite a promising young man, truly one with a bright future (前途有望). I got to know him because I attended Chŏngsin Girls' School, which was renowned back then as the most modern-style school in Chosŏn (at that time, 20 years ago, Chŏngsin Girls' School was number one in terms of substantial content and the number of students from well-off families who dressed well, even compared to Ewha Hakdang (梨花學堂) or whatever else there is now). As I occasionally met various figures in the Christian society, that young man, who was a believer (信者), somehow found out about me and wrote me a letter. Since a letter came, I wrote a reply. Since I wrote a reply, another letter came. I wrote again. Another came... This continued constantly for 5 years. So, I probably wrote several hundred letters myself, and the number of letters I received from him must also have been several hundred.

Despite this, I hadn't seen his face. I had only heard from others that he had a fine physique, but I had no way of knowing how his nose was shaped or how his eyes were set. After carrying on this unseen romance for three or four years like that, one day, I received a letter from him with his photograph enclosed in a separate envelope. I felt my heart pounding and realized my cheeks were blushing involuntarily. That was right during my time as a female teacher, so afraid that the students might see the photo, I hid it inside the pages of a textbook. I couldn't bear to look at it in the dormitory room (奇宿含), so I carried the book into the school classroom. Then, while teaching, I couldn't stand it anymore, and with trembling fingers, I tore open the envelope (封皮) and quickly pulled out the photo. As soon as his fine forehead came into view, I was so startled that I couldn't look fully and just put it back, starting to teach again nonchalantly, saying something like "Jesus Christ is..." But I still really wanted to see the photo, so after a while, pretending to flip through the textbook pages secretly from the students, I started to take it out again. Then, tightly suppressing my pounding heart and the blood rushing through my whole body, just like before, I barely managed to pull out the edge of the photo with trembling fingers. This time, past the forehead, his bright, cool eyes came into view. Startled as if by something, my mind <64> felt a chill, and I quickly put it back again, pretending to teach the lesson irrelevantly, saying, "In Jerusalem, to the twelve disciples..."

A young maiden's heart is truly subtle and delicate. On the one hand, the desire to see it flared up like fire, unable to calm down for even a moment, leading me to bring the photo even into the classroom during teaching hours. But after trying 5 or 6 times, I ultimately failed. If today's female students saw my blushing face, they would probably giggle, thinking, "Oh, that teacher is acting like a cat in heat."

Later, even when I went into the office, I was afraid the teacher next to me would see, so I couldn't bring myself to open the photo. Using the break time, I quickly took it into the restroom. Then, locking the restroom door tightly, pressing down on my pounding heart, I carefully reread the letter that came bundled with it once more, and took out the sealed photo. Even after taking it out, I was too scared to look at it right away and quickly turned it face down. Then, hesitating whether to turn it over or not, I summoned all the courage I'd had since birth, quickly turned it over, and finally saw the whole face. Sweat flowed down my body inside my clothes. Thinking I had indeed done something frightening... However, my heart leaped as he was as handsome a man (好男子) with refined features (眉目) as I had imagined. From then on, my beloved's photo never left my side, whether sleeping, going to school, or eating.

After long letters, we got married. Then, he became involved in a certain major incident before the annexation, where 38 people including Yi Kap (李甲), An Ch'ang-ho (安昌浩), and An Chung-gŭn's (安重根) younger brother An Myŏng-gŭn (安明根) were arrested together, and he fled overseas.
That became the end of our marital relationship. He is now said to have entered Kŭmgangsan (金剛山), abandoned the secular world, and become a monk practicing asceticism. Anyway, whenever I think about that photo incident, I still can't help but laugh.

**Three Letters or More is Inertia**
**Kim An-sŏ**

Since I married young due to my father's command (父命), I didn't have the romantic story like others who exchanged "love letters" with their current wife. However, some years ago, I had an occasion where I wrote many love letters.

There were probably several tens of them. But when writing love letters, after about three letters, one tends to have said most of what one wants to say. Therefore, when writing more than three letters, it's merely repeating what has already been said out of inertia (墮力). My experience was like that.

Regarding the effect of the love letters, well, since the objective was achieved anyway, what percentage should I say?

**That Person Wrote and Left It First**
**Yi Kwang-su**

I have never written love letters to anyone other than the person who is my wife. How could I remember the number of letters, but looking at the collected letters from that time placed in a box (函子), I believe there must have been around a thousand. As for who wrote the first letter between the two of us? Well, when she was in Tokyo, she came to my boarding house, brought medicine, and wrote down instructions on how to take it, leaving the note behind. So, although the nature wasn't meant as "love," in any case, she was the one who wrote first. Also, the letters I received from the opposite sex were almost entirely from the person who is my wife. However, when I was in Tokyo, a certain Korean female student gave me letters several times and also came to see me. But after getting married, there hasn't been a single one. Who would want to write a letter to a man who already has a wife?

**Only to My Wife**
**Sin Hŭng-u**

I think it would be good to first decide the meaning of a love letter (戀愛書翰). If it refers to letters conveying one's loving feelings to the person one loves for the sake of what the world calls "love," then I am quite removed from that. If, however, writing a letter to someone of the opposite sex due to some incident is also called a love letter, then I suppose I have written and received a fair amount. Moreover, <65> since I am always traveling around Europe and America, I often write many letters to my wife at home due to various matters. But calling these love letters would be somewhat bland, like flat beer.

I married decades ago, in ancient times when the term 'love marriage' wasn't even known, so even during the engagement period, I couldn't write a letter to the person who is now my wife.
In short, my past is quite distant from such matters.

**Just Once**
**Yi Chŏng-sŏp**

Until now, I have never received a written message from the opposite sex that could be called a love letter. This is because I had an old-style marriage when I was young, and another reason is that I was busy traveling domestically and internationally for studies, so there was absolutely no time for such things.

However, although I haven't received any letters, I did write one, just once, to a certain woman. The result? Well, please don't ask...

**It Came from the Other Side First**
**Yi Ŭn-sang**

In my life, I have written love letters to only one person. For about 3 years, I probably sent an average of ten letters per month, so that's 120 letters a year, 360 for three years... But somehow, it felt like a few more than that.

Just as the love letters I sent were to that one single person, the love letters I received also came from just one person. That person is my current wife. So, we, like a mouse chasing its tail and that mouse chasing its tail again, wrote letters, received letters, wrote again, received again, and thus repeated the banquet of youth, excited and infinitely happy and satisfied, for three years, which resulted in, namely, marriage.

When addressing the other person, I wrote the name followed by 'ssiyŏ!' (씨여!). Sometimes I even put three or four exclamation marks after the character 'ssi' (氏)! Others supposedly call their beloved 'My nightingale!' or 'My chick!', but I was dignified. Simple.

The very first initiation of the love letters came from the other side. It definitely came from the other side first. I was always passive.

**One Old Sijo Poem**
**Ch'oe Hak-song**

In my past half-life, consistently marked by poverty (貧困) and wandering (放浪), could there be even a fragment of fragrant memory? Moreover, I have never exchanged letters carrying the meaning of love with the opposite sex. Having gone through several marriages without putting romance first, I never had the chance to write a "love letter" even to someone who was a maiden.

However, one unforgettable thing is that some years ago, I meticulously wrote out a chapter of an old sijo poem (古時調) and sent it to a certain kisaeng (妓女). Since it was an old sijo, I didn't compose it myself, but I remember that my state of mind at that time held the passion and sincerity of someone writing a genuine love letter. However, even then, I was somewhat drunk.

And of course, I have never received even a single letter from the other side.

**Wrote Erotic Letters**
**Chŏng Sŏk-t'ae**

I am someone who had an old-style marriage when young. Therefore, I did not have the fortune (分福) to engage in romance with other women during my youth. However, there was this one incident. When I was at Tokyo Medical University (東京醫科大學), a Japanese nurse there took a liking to me and occasionally sent letters, which I received. But at that time, my mind was full of thoughts of going abroad to study, so fearing that it might break a man's resolve (丈夫의 뜻), I cautiously avoided her. That ended just like that.

When I was in Berlin (伯林), if I went to a cafe (캅페), the waitresses (웨트레스) there, or women who could be seen as gentlewomen of leisure (隱君子), would send letters via a boy (뽀이); I received several tens of those. However, these are like unsolicited letters coming from a kisaeng, completely lacking deep flavor and purity. In other words, it would be correct to call it a kind of love game (戀愛遊戱). Such letters cannot be called "love letters"; rather, viewed from an "erotic" perspective, they should be called "ero-letters" (에로렛타-). So, I can say I received quite a few "ero-letters." <66>

**Summary**

This article compiles recollections from various prominent Korean figures in 1930 about their experiences writing and receiving love letters ("러부렛타-"). The accounts range from passionate youthful exchanges (Ch'oe Rin, Im Wŏn-gŭn, U Pong-un, Chu Yo-han, Yi Ŭn-sang, Kim An-sŏ) and long-term correspondences leading to marriage (Hŏ Yŏng-suk/Yi Kwang-su) to a complete lack of experience due to arranged marriages or busy lives (Kim Pyŏng-no, Sin Hŭng-u, Ch'oe Hak-song, Chŏng Sŏk-t'ae), or very limited encounters (Yi Chŏng-sŏp). Several mention the constraints of the time, including societal judgment, gender roles (women rarely initiating), national differences preventing relationships (Ch'oe Rin), and political events like police confiscation of letters during the March 1st Movement (Hŏ Yŏng-suk).

For historians, this article offers valuable insights into the personal lives and romantic sensibilities of Korean intellectuals and activists during the Japanese colonial period. It reveals the evolving concept of romantic love ("러부렛타-" itself being a loanword), the tension between modern ideals and traditional customs (arranged marriage vs. love marriage), the impact of studying abroad (especially in Japan) on relationships, and how personal lives intersected with political realities, such as police surveillance and nationalist sentiments influencing personal choices.

그리면서도 나는 그분의 얼골을 못보앗다. 남들이 전하는 말로 풍채 조흔 이란 말만 들엇지 코가 엇더케 백이고 눈이 엇더케 박엿는지 전혀 알 까닭이 업섯다. 그러케 삼사년을 보지도 못한 연애를 하여오다가 하로는 그분으로부터 편지 속에 딴 봉투로 봉한 제 사진 한 장을 너허 보냇다. 나는 가슴이 울넝거리고 두 볼이 저절로 빨개지는 것을 깨달엇다. 그때가 바로 여교사시대라 학생들이 그 사진을 볼가봐서 교과서 책장 속에 감추고 그 책을 끄내어 奇宿含 안에서는 참아 보지 못하고 학교 교실까지 끄을고 드러갓다. 그래서 교수하다도 참아 못견듸어 떨니는 손꼬락으로 그 封皮를 뜻고 얼는 사진을 끄내다가 번듯한 이마가 쑥 나타나자 그만 놀라서 채 보지 못하고 그냥 집어너코서 「예수 그리스도는」 운운하며 또 딴청으로 교수를 시작하엿지. 그러나 여전히 그 사진이 정말 보고 십허서 또 한참 가다가 학생 몰래 책장을 뒤적어리는 체하고 또 끄내어 보기 시작하엿다. 그래서 앗가가치 울넝거리는 가슴과 전신의 피가 용소슴치는 것을 꽉참고 간신히 떨니는 손끄락으로 사진 끗흘 집어 내기 시작할 때 이번에는 또 이마를 지나 영채잇는 시언한 두 눈이 보엿다. 나는 무엇에 놀난드시 마음이＜64＞ 선듯하며 또다시 얼는 집어노코 「예루살렴에서 열두제자에게...」하고 뚱단지로 학과교수하는 체 하엿다.

그러다가 사무실에 드러와서도 겻헤 선생이 볼가봐서 끗끗내 그 사진을 펴보지 못하고 휴식시간을 이용하야 변소로 얼는 들고 드러갓다. 그러고 변소문을 꼭 닷고 울넝거리는 가슴을 내려누르며 정성스럽게 함께 싸보낸 편지를 다시 한번 쭉 내려보고 봉하엿든 사진을 끄내엇다. 끄내어 노코도 참아 보기 무서워서 얼는 뒤로 업헛다. 그러고는 번지어 볼가 말가 망서리다가 생후 처음의 용긔를 다 내어 얼는 번지어 얼골 전체를 보고 말엇다. 一身에서 땀이 웃속 흐른다. 과연 두러운 일을 하엿구나 하면서...그러나 내가 환상하든 바와 가치 그가 眉目이 수려한 호남자임에 나의 마음은 뛰엇다. 그제부터는 사랑하는 그분의 사진이 잘 때나 학교갈 때나 밥먹을 때나 나의 겻흘 떠난 때가 업섯다.

나는 어릴 때에 父命으로 장가들엇기에 남들과 가치 현재의 안해와 「러부렛타」교환하엿다는 그런 로만틱한 로-만쓰를 가지지 못하엿다. 그러다가 몃해 전에 엇든 기회로 연애편지를 만히 쓴 일이 잇다.
아마 數十通은 되리라. 그런데 연애편지는 세 번까지 쓰면 하고십흔 말을 대개 다하여 버리게 된다. 그럼으로 세통이상을 쓸 때는 그때는 그저 墮力으로 한 말을 도로 곱씹고 또 그리함에 불과한 것이라 내 경험이 그러하엿다.
러부렛타-의 효과에 대하여는 좌우간 목적을 달하엿스니까 몃파-센트라고할는지요.

그 사람이 몬저 써 두고가
李光洙
나는 내 안해되는 사람에게 밧게 연애서한을 써 본 일이 업슴니다. 그 장수를 엇지 기억하릿가만은 函子 속에 너흔 그 때의 모아둔 편지를 보면 아마 천장 내외는 되엇슬 줄 암니다. 맨 처음에 두 사람 사에이 누가 몬저 편지를 썻는가고? 그야 그분이 東京잇슬 때 내 하숙에 차저와 약을 갓다주면서 그 먹는 법 등을 기록한 편지를 써 두고 갓스니깐 성질이야 「러-브」를 의미한 것이 아니지만 좌우간 그분이 몬저 쓴 셈이지요. 또 이성에게서 편지밧기는 안해되는 사람에게서 밧은 것이 거의 전부엇고 다만 東京잇슬 때 엇든 조선여학생이 나에게 여러번 편지를 주엇고 또 차저와 주는 것도 맛낫슴니다. 그러나 결혼한 뒤는 한 장도 업섯슴니다. 이미 안해가 잇는 사내에게 누가 편지를 쓰려함니까.

안해에게 밧게
申興雨
戀愛書翰의 의미부터 몬저 결정하는 것이 조흘 줄 아는데 그야말로 세상에서 가르치는 「사랑」을 위하야 제가 사랑하는 뜻을 사랑하는 사람에게 전하는 편지를 가르친다면 나는 거기에는 대단히 인연이 멈니다. 그러치안코 무슨 사건으로 말미아마 엇든 이성에게 편지쓴 것도 러브렛타-라 한다면 그야 나도 상당히 쓰고 밧은 줄암니다. 더구나＜65＞ 나는 항상 구미각지로 도라단니는 터이니까 여러 가지 일 때문에 곳장 집에 잇는 안해에게 만흔 편지를 함니다. 그러나 이것을 러부렛타-라 하기에는 긔운이 나간 맥주가치 좀 싱거웁겟지요.
저는 수십년전 연애결혼이란 일홈도 모르든 태고시절에 장가들엇기 때문에 약혼시대에도 지금 안해되어 잇는 사람에게 편지라고 써 보지 못하엿슴니다.
말하자면 나의 과거는 그런 방면에 대단히 사이가 먼 셈이지요.

저쪽에서 몬저 왓다
李殷相
나는 생전에 꼭 한분에게 러부렛타를 써 보앗는데 아마 약 3년동안 두고 한달에 평균처서 열통씩은 써 부첫스니 1년치고 120통 3년치고 360통...그러나 엇전지 그보다도 몃장쯤 더 되는 듯 하엿슴니다.
내가 보낸 러브렛타도 그 엇든 단 한분이엇든 모양으로 내가 바든 러부렛타도 꼭 한분에게서 왓섯지요. 그이는 현재의 나의 안해지요. 그래서 우리들은 그 쥐 그 꼬리물고 또 그 쥐 그 꼬리무는 모양으로 편지를 하고 편지를 밧고 또 하고 또 밧고 래서 흥분이 된 그러고도 무한히 행복스럽고 만족한 청춘의 잔채를 3년동안 거듭한 결과 曰 결혼에까지 미친 것이지요.

맨처음 러부렛타의 개시는 저쪽에서 몬저 하엿슴넨다. 확실히 저쪽에서 몬저하엿슴넨다. 나는 늘 수동적이엇지요.

古時調 한 장을
崔鶴松
빈곤과 방랑으로 一貫한 나의 과거 반생에 무슨 香그러운 기억이 한쪼각인들 잇스랴만은 더욱 이성에게 사랑의 의미를 띈 편지를 주고 밧은 일은 업다. 연애를 압세우지 못하고 數次의 결혼을 한 몸이매 처녀되엇든 사람에게조차 「러부렛타」를 쓸 기회가 업섯다.
다만 이처지지 안는 일은 몃해 전에 엇든 기녀에게 古時調 한 章을 정성스럽게 써서 보낸 일이 잇다. 古時調이매 내가 지은 것이 아니나 그때의 나의 심정은 眞純한 러부렛타를 쓰는 그 사람의 정열과 진심을 가젓든 것을 기억한다. 그러나 그때도 다소 술에는 취하엿섯다.
그러고 물론 저편으로부터 바더 본 것은 한 장도 업다.

에로, 렛타는 썻다
鄭錫泰
나는 어릴 때에 구식 결혼을 한 사람이외다. 그러기에 청년시대에 다른 여성과 연애할 分福이라고 가지지 못하엿슴니다. 그러나 이러한 일은 잇섯슴니다. 내가 東京醫科大學에 잇슬 때 그곳 日人 간호부가 나를 따르며 각금 편지하여 주는 것을 밧엇슴니다. 그러나 그때는 외국유학을 갈 생각에 마음이 가득하든 때임으로 幸혀 丈夫의 뜻을 꺽길나!하여 슬슬 피하엿섯슴니다. 그는 그러다가 말엇지요.
伯林잇슬 때 「캅페」에 가면 그곳 「웨트레스」들이 또는 隱君子라고 볼 여성들이 뽀이를 식혀 편지하여 주는 것은 여러 十張을 밧앗지요. 그러나 이것은 기생에게서 請치도 안는 편지가 오는 모양으로 깁흔 맛과 순결한 맛이 조곰도 업는 것이지요. 즉 一種 戀愛遊戱라 함이 오를 것이외다. 이러한 편지는 「러부렛타-라」고 할 수 업고 말하자면 「에로틱」한 점으로 브아 「에로렛타-」라 할 것이요. 그래서 「에로렛타-」는 나도 상당히 밧엇다 할 것이외다.＜66＞
