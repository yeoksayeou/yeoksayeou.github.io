기사형태 문예기타 (Literary Miscellaneous)

Autumn in the Old Capital
Memories of Chinling (金陵)
Chu Yohan (朱耀翰)

When Chinling was first established,
Could one have known it would become an empty site?
Where the palace was torn down,
Only roof tiles lie scattered.
The wind, tangled in the grass,
Seemed not to know where to go.

I have visited Nanjing (南京) five or six times. When I was attending school in Shanghai (上海), I went briefly twice with the football team, once with the music troupe, and once for a debate competition. After graduating, I lived there for about half a year. I also visited as a newspaper reporter after Nanjing became the new capital.

Among these visits, the most deeply imprinted memory is, of course, the half-year I spent living in Nanjing. Moreover, at that time, I was working as an English teacher at a small school run by our people (Koreans), mingling within the small society formed by Koreans gathered together. Although materially, it was an extremely destitute (苟借莫甚) life, spiritually it was a pleasant one, so the memories of that time well up with deep emotion.

Thoughts of boating on the Qinhuai (秦淮) and viewing the moon, thoughts of going out to the Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum (明孝陵) with the elders and circling Zijin Shan (紫金山), a day at Xuanwu Lake (玄武湖), a day at Yanziji (燕子磯), memories of strolling to Jiming Temple (鷄鳴寺) and Wutai Shan (五臺山) – these are all the recollections from my solitary half-year.

The Qinhuai, famous from the song "Mooring at night on the Qinhuai near the taverns" (夜泊秦淮近酒家), is a stream in the southeast part of the city. At night, the poetic phrase "Mist veils the cold water, moonlight veils the sand" (煙籠寒水月籠沙) truly seems apt, but in the daytime, it is merely a dark sewage canal. Taverns line both sides, their reflections cast upside down in the water, and the sound of the *huqin* (胡琴胡歌 - Chinese fiddle and song) drifts mixed with the moonlight. I seem to recall it takes about half an hour to go upstream by painted barge (畵舫) from the Confucius Temple (夫子廟) to Tongji Bridge (通濟橋). But who, floating a boat on the Qinhuai, would hurry the time? Rowing near Xiushan Park (秀山公園, now Zhongshan Park 中山公園), on the right bank, there is a field of sorghum. The presence of a sorghum field in the middle of Nanjing city also deepens the sense of nostalgia (懷舊之念), but the moon shimmering on the sorghum leaves, dancing in the wind, evokes an unbearable homesickness and yearning for the past (感古心). Since Nanjing became the capital (奠都), courtesans were abolished, and consequently, the painted barges of the Qinhuai became draped only in cobwebs. Even if the winds of amusement (遊興) are forbidden, who can stop those seeking the poetic charm (詩趣) of a moonlit night? From now on, those who visit the Qinhuai will likely appreciate the moon and wind as substitutes for beautiful courtesans, songs, and dances.

Rowing by moonlight on the Qinhuai,
Upstream towards Tongji Bridge,
Reflected in the water,
Is it lamplight or the moon?
From somewhere, the sound of a *huqin*,
Tugs at a non-existent sorrow.

Zijin Shan is lovely in spring, but even better to see in autumn. The red leaves dotted (点綴) among the evergreen trees near the summit are autumnal, and the purple hue (紫金色) of the distant rocks seems most beautiful. The Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum is on the mid-slope (中腹) of Zijin Shan; the vermilion-plastered walls (朱土) of the shrine catch the eye even from afar, and stone statues of civil and military officials, elephants, horses, and camels stand guard along the entrance path (門路). Behind the mausoleum (陵後), acorns have formed (매처), and women are busy gathering them. Standing atop the mausoleum, the inside of the city walls spreads out clearly (宛然히). More than half of the area within the walls is overgrown fields (草土). The site of the Ming Old Palace (明古宮趾), occupying the northeast, is entirely ruins (廢墟), with only a stone gate (石門) remaining. Traveling to the Ming Mausoleum by rickshaw, carriage, or donkey (nowadays, it would be by car), one passes through the old palace site on the way there and back. Aside from the jarring building of the Historical Site Preservation Society (古蹟保存會), the sites of the duck pond (鴨池), stone bridge (石橋), and hot springs <57> remain as they were (如昔), with clear water flowing. Here and there are farmhouses built with old bricks (故磚) and stonework (石築), the paths are lined with walls made of broken bricks, and the famous yellow tiles (黃瓦) are scarcely seen, perhaps managed by the Preservation Society or perhaps taken one by one by passersby until none were left. Carts (小車떼) constantly carry away broken bricks, but since there were originally so many, it seems to make no difference. Now, Zhongshan Road (中山道路) cuts through the middle, Sun Yat-sen's (孫中山) mausoleum with its blue-tiled roof stands even higher than the Ming Mausoleum, and in an age where Beijige Pavilion (北極閣) has been torn down (헐니고) and a broadcasting tower (放送臺) built, the day when the National Government's (國民政府) "White House" (白아舘) stands on the old palace site cannot be far off.

Shall I float a boat among the reeds of Mochou Lake (莫愁湖)?
Shall I ride a donkey to Ming Xiaoling on Zijin Shan?
Shall I go against the current of the Yangtze River (楊子江)?
Shall I climb the Twelve Heavenly Caves (十二洞天)?
Autumn has already deepened,
I yearn for my hometown.

The place called Yanziji is about 20 *li* (approx. 10 km) by boat from Xiaguan (下關), northwest of the city. Midway, there is a place called the Twelve Heavenly Caves; it's named for having 12 cave-like places (岩窟). In high spots, wooden cloud bridges (雲橋) have been built to allow ascent, and climbing to the highest cave makes one's feet tingle slightly. I saw evidence that Koreans had passed through, with "Korea [Hanguk], So-and-so" (韓國某某) written in ink on a wooden board (板張). Yanziji has become the name of a village with a few shops. Behind the village, if you climb up the rocks, a cluster of rocks juts out by the water's edge facing the Yangtze River. The lower parts are good for fishing, the higher parts good for suicide. Looking down, the muddy waves (濁浪) roar as they bite at the rocks and flow downstream. Pomegranates (石榴) are sold in the village; these are pomegranates grown right in this vicinity, and their taste is first-rate. Mischievous boys secretly enter the pomegranate orchards and break off branches, because pomegranate wood (石榴材) is excellent for making short walking sticks (短杖). Reed flowers (갈꼿) fly about profusely, floating and playing on the water.

Reed flowers float.
Reed flowers float on the river water.
The reed flowers floating on the river
Follow the waves so well.
The wind has caught the sail,
Shall I follow it?

There are old and new historic sites like Bailuzhou (白鷺洲) and Lianghua Terrace (兩花臺), but historical sites, when actually visited, are often unremarkable. More than that, though perhaps mundane, the moonlight on Wutai Shan or the cawing of crows at Jiming Temple evoke the sweet sorrow (甘悲) of autumn even more. The crows in Nanjing are truly numerous. Morning and evening, they fly in flocks with fixed regularity, going somewhere and coming from somewhere. Seeing the black masses cover the sky, one wants to say there are thousands, tens of thousands.

The sun sets on the canal water,
The white sail has turned red.
Evening crows
It's time for them to cross the fields.
Clouds creep up,
It looks like night rain will fall.

Jiming Temple is a temple within the city walls. I don't know its current state, but when I was there, the temple buildings (寺屋) were relatively well-preserved (完存), and notably, the calligraphy on the hanging boards (현판) inside the temple was not vulgar. Crows built nests in the trees in the front courtyard (庭前), and crickets chirped from the cracks between the flagstones (石徑). I failed to learn about its history.

Wutai Shan is a small hill inside Hansimun Gate (漢西門). It was only a few hundred steps from the house where I lodged (留宿). The hill has not a single tree, only grass covering its entire surface (一面). However, this wide-open scenery is most fitting for moon viewing on an autumn night. The sky over Jiangnan, where drought had continued for over three months, was boundlessly clear, and the cold moonlight shone on the grassy hill, unobstructed as if it were day. Lying flat under that moonlight, I would meditate (冥想), sing songs, and also talk with friends if they were there. On sleepless nights, wandering until even the cold became indistinct, my mind would become clear as a mirror, and I felt as if I understood the true meaning of human life, like a tiny speck within the boundless universe (無邊無涯).

The moon is bright on Wutai Shan,
Crickets chirp at Jiming Temple.
The singing voices from the Qinhuai,
Though not the "Rear Courtyard Flower" (後庭花 - a song associated with the fall of a dynasty), <58>
Seem now to evoke
The sorrow of a lost nation.

Autumn deepens. Every scene of the old capital evokes homesickness. Oh, the longed-for moonlit nights of Wutai Shan! When I visited the new capital, Nanjing, I ultimately did not get the chance to see that moon again.

To forget my sorrows,
I went out to see the moon.
The wind blowing over the mountain ridge
Is cold upon my collar.
Let it be, perhaps it will carry
News from home.
(All quoted song verses above are old works written in Nanjing)

Autumn in Hanyang (漢陽)
Mun Ilp'yŏng (文一平)

The brilliantly clear autumn sky, the transparently clear stream water, the sound of the wind whispering through the leaves, the moonlight shining on the dew – these symbolize the clear feeling of autumn.
Autumn is not a dream but reality. Its clear and high firmament (宇) makes one think of the character of a philosopher who has attained great enlightenment (大悟徹底).
However, autumn is somewhat solitary (孤寂). The autumn of an old capital is even more so. Isn't it true that just imagining the autumn of Nakhwaam Rock (洛花岩) or Sŏnjukkyo Bridge (善竹橋) evokes feelings of desolation?
Without needing to go far to Puyŏ (扶餘) or Kaesŏng (開城), when we appreciate the autumn of Hanyang, full of sentiment, how much emotion it stirs in us! Hanyang, being a city (市府) developing in a modern way, is different from Puyŏ and Kaesŏng, but historically, it already belongs to the category of old capitals. Crumbling city walls, empty palaces, and the like await preservation as historical relics (古蹟), and the autumn of Kyŏngbok Palace (景福宮) evokes not just solitude but a mood of sorrow (哀愁).
Though gone now, just ten-odd years ago, entering through Kwanghwamun Gate (光化門), passing Kŭnjŏngjŏn Hall (勤政殿), and going around Kangnyŏngjŏn Hall (康寧殿) and Kyot'aejŏn Hall (交泰殿), one reached Kŏnch'ŏng Palace (乾淸宮). Among its buildings, Okho Pavilion (玉壺樓) was where Empress Myŏngsŏng (明成皇后) was tragically murdered (배참하게 구끼신 곳). Though one might expect to find traces of the profusely spilled blue blood (碧血) on the golden headboard (黃金床頭), only cosmos flowers ('코쓰모쓰'), blooming crimson and colorful as if boasting of the late light, embroider the dark historic site (遺址).
Pugak Mountain (北岳) still wears its green, but Kyŏngbok Palace has changed its former appearance. Whenever I climb Pugak Mountain and look down (바러 볼) at Kyŏngbok Palace, I picture in my mind the ambitious plans (覇圖) of the Taewŏn'gun's (大院王) prime, his sharp green spirit. His ten-year regency certainly showed extraordinary spirit (氣魄) in both domestic affairs (內治) and foreign relations (外交), but merely lacking the insight (眼光) to discern the great trends of the world (天下大勢), he ultimately brought about failure. If he had possessed modern knowledge and his measures (措施) had been appropriate (得宜), would today exist?
The conflict between the mother-in-law (Queen Dowager Cho) and daughter-in-law (Empress Myŏngsŏng), along with the factional strife (黨爭) among court officials, further accelerated the declining fate of the nation (國命). Among the dramatic events staged at Kyŏngbok Palace in the history of the Hanyang dynasty (漢陽朝史), none were as intensely dramatic (극적 색채가 찌튼) as the numerous recent political changes (近世政燮). Among these, the tragedy at Okho Pavilion that occurred (닐어난) on August 20th of the Ulmi year (乙未 8월 20일 - 1895) was the most representative.
If the Kapsin Coup (甲申政變 - 1884) arose from the conflict between Japanese and Qing (日淸) influences, the Ulmi Incident (乙未政變) arose from the clash between Japanese and Russian (日露) powers. That such international sacrifices were made explains how intense the international competition was in Chosŏn (朝鮮) at that time.
Time flows heedlessly, and the Ulmi Incident has already become a trace of the past (陳跡) from 35 years ago. Today too is autumn. The bright moon shines down, piercing the ruins of Okho Pavilion, and the cool wind blows gently through the remains of Kŏnch'ŏng Palace. The autumn of Kyŏngbok Palace, symbolizing the autumn of Hanyang, is always a source of poignant reflection (興傷的 원천) for those who seek the autumn of the old capital. <59>

Autumn Moon Night at Manwŏldae (滿月臺)
Kim Tonghwan (金東煥)

I stand now upon Manwŏldae. Standing, I tightly close my eyes. If I don't close them, I cannot avoid seeing the ruins of the Koryŏ (高麗) royal palace, scattered like skulls in the moonlight. And I quietly block my ears too. If I don't block them, I surely cannot avoid hearing the sound of Koryŏ's insects crying beneath the foundation stones (주츳돌).
However, before my closed eyes, I see throngs of court officials prostrating (附伏) on the ground amidst rising white incense smoke (香불 연긔), reporting state affairs (政事). On the vast plain below Songak Mountain (松岳山), I see tens of thousands of Royalist Guards (勤王黨) holding spears (槍), receiving the King's inspection (閱兵) on his white horse. I also see the Royal Astronomer (天文博士) descending from Ch'ŏmsŏngdae Observatory (瞻星臺) after observing the sun and moon (日月), his scholar's robe (도포자락) fluttering in the wind. Is that all? I see, for the last monarch who passed away (昇遐) in misfortune after shouldering (드러업고서) the 475-year dynasty following debauchery (淫佚) and misrule (失政), the high priests from the 480 temples of the royal capital gathered together, chanting sutras (誦經). In one corner, smoke rises to the sky from burning the gold and silver ornaments (金銀佩物) the King used. Moreover, I even see the funeral procession (大擧) moving out, with pallbearers (喪擧꾼) marching in step to cries of "Wŏ-i! Wŏ-i!", while royal relatives, former officials (宗戚舊臣), and court ladies hold onto it (붓잡는). Perhaps that weeping willow tree near the stone pillar, with its broken branch, was snapped (불너진) by the hearse (목마) following behind the procession back then? Thinking this, it's said that descendants (後腐) like us, in our special circumstances, should not look upon old palace sites (故宮址) at all (아예). Especially not in youth, especially not on a moonlit night. This is because one feels (늣기는) an indescribable sorrow (悲哀) and solitude (孤寂), like a jilted lover visiting a cemetery.

Humans live 60 years, trees 100 years
After humans and trees are long gone
Only the palace site's stones have lingered 500 years
We thought the nation would linger longer than stone

Moon, oh moon, moon of Manwŏldae
The moon remains, but Koryŏ is gone
Wouldn't it be right if Koryŏ remained and the moon was gone?
Who would look at the moon without a nation?

Oh, flute-playing shepherd boy (樵童)
If someone asks later
Tell them a certain youth sought the moon at Manwŏldae
Saw a distorted moon, wept, and departed.

The autumn wind rustling through the pine forest (솔밧) of Songak Mountain makes one prick up one's ears, as if someone stood watch within. In such a dense green (蒼鬱) pine forest, might not a band of righteous outlaws (義賊) like Robin Hood (로빙, 훗-드) be hiding? Might not discontented men (不平客), disillusioned with state affairs and pursued by the powerful (强權者), habitually gather in such a pine forest, practicing archery (활도 쏘고) and spear-throwing (창쓰는 법), awaiting a change in fortune (風雲)?
Might not the 72 Sages (七十二賢) who, to uphold their high principles (高節), demonstrated their refusal to serve the new king and lived in seclusion (蟄居) in Tumun-dong (杜門洞) outside the city walls, enduring the transient rain and dew (雨露), perhaps only feigned seclusion outwardly while gathering on Songak Mountain behind the royal palace, reading classics (經도 닑고) and preparing (작만하고) military strength? And then, like a fierce wind (질풍가치), appeared in the counties and districts (郡邑), beheading corrupt officials (탐관오리), seizing the wealth (財貨) of powerful local bullies and treacherous merchants (土豪奸商), and giving it to the pitiful commoners (細民)? Truly, it should have been so (올흘 것이언만), but the scholars (선비) of the East lacked even the courage for that, simply decaying with the grass and trees (草木同腐). In this respect, I earnestly wished that we too had possessed a group like that of the strongman (力士) Robin Hood, who played a major role in the Western Middle Ages. If so, it would surely add some vitality (生彩) to the history of trampled people's rights (民權). And why should Robin Hood have existed only long ago in the Middle Ages? Perhaps even now?

Pine, oh pine, green pine
The Koryŏ people who grew together with you
Where have they been cast off?
Only you alone are green (獨也靑靑).

Coming down from Manwŏldae towards the city, I imagined such a dream. <60>
"Hey there, Squad Leader Kim (金番首)!"
Drawing out his voice, Squad Leader Pak (朴番首), who had rowed his small boat down from the ferry landing (渡船場) near Kyŏngdŏk Palace (敬德王宮 - likely a Koryŏ-era palace area), reached the latticed window (沙窓門) of a certain house, tied his boat to a post (말똑), and tapped the window several times with the end of his pole (놋대끗), calling his friend.
On the 16th night of the 9th autumn month, the white moonlight shimmered upon his red silk robe (紅羅). The landscape-patterned fur cap (山水 털병거지) slung loosely behind his neck bobbed up and down (웃줄웃줄) with the boat as the current stirred.
It must be nearing the time for the morning bell (파루), as the sound of patrolmen's (巡羅꾼) footsteps echoed loudly near Namdaemun Gate (南大門). Yet, on this river flowing through the center of the Ten Thousand Households of Chang'an (萬戶長安 - metaphor for the capital), picturesque small boats floated silently here and there in the bends. They must be sentimental young scholars (多情多限한 王都의 젊은 선비) of the royal capital, cherishing the moon and idly spending this night on the river. Squad Leader Pak gazed quietly, as if drunk, at the flickering lights of the thousands of houses lining both banks (려렴집), then thumped the boat's prow (선창)! and called once more:
"Squad Leader Kim, are you dead? Squad Leader Kim, I've come!"
Only then did the square, double-latticed window on the stone wall open squarely, and a white face poked out.
"Ai, Brother, what's all the fuss?"
"What were you doing, not answering when called?"
"Playing the kŏmun'go (거문고 - Korean zither), wasn't I?"
"Bah, stop plucking that green thing. All the gentlemen of Chang'an are out enjoying themselves. By the way, where did Squad Leader Kim go?"
"He came out drunk from the palace earlier. I'll wake him for you."
And then, the smiling face of the young kisaeng (기녀) disappeared, and shortly after, Squad Leader Kim's face popped out instead.
"Why are you being so annoying, I can't even sleep?"
"Hey, I brought something good!"
"Wine?"
"The moon!"
"Crazy fellow, does eating the moon fill your belly?"
"If so, let's eat the moonlight as a side dish (안주)."
Saying this, Pak rummaged in the boat's storage (배창), pulled out a watermelon-sized wine bottle (술병) from the stern (북덱이쪽), first gulped it down himself (월칵월칵), then gave it to Squad Leader Kim. Kim received it (바더들고) through the window, rubbed his eyes, drank it all, and then tossed the bottle splash (철석) onto the river. Startled awake by the noise, a pair of mandarin ducks cried out and swam towards the opposite bank (江岸).
"Hey, those ducks, they say 'ori' (duck/5 *li*) if they go 5 *li*, 'ori' if they go 10 *li* (십리). Did you know the reason before drinking?"
"Well then, the wagtail (할미새 - lit. grandmother bird), today I'm a wagtail, yesterday I was a wagtail too. Did the wine tell you the meaning of that?"
Drunkenly exchanging witticisms (才談), the two burst into hearty laughter (껄껄걸). Then, promising to meet again, Squad Leader Pak untied his boat and headed downstream, while Squad Leader Kim closed the window and listened to his beloved courtesan's (愛妓) kŏmun'go, staying up through the short night (짜른 밤 길게 새이더라). <61>

**Summary**

This collection of three essays under the title "Autumn in the Old Capital" presents nostalgic reflections on former capitals—Nanjing, Seoul (Hanyang), and Kaesong—during the autumn season of 1930. Chu Yohan reminisces about his time in Nanjing, blending poetic descriptions of sites like the Qinhuai River and Zijin Shan with reflections on change and loss. Mun Ilp'yŏng contemplates Seoul's Kyŏngbok Palace, focusing on the tragic history of Empress Myŏngsŏng and the late Chosŏn dynasty's decline amidst foreign pressures, framing Seoul as a historic site imbued with sorrow. Kim Tonghwan evokes the melancholy of Kaesong's Manwŏldae, the Koryŏ palace ruins, imagining past splendors and lamenting the lost dynasty, even conjuring a vivid scene of Koryŏ nightlife. The essays collectively convey a deep sense of *pathos* tied to historical memory, the passage of time, and the beauty and solitude of autumn, particularly poignant when viewed through the lens of Korea's colonial status. Historians might find the palpable sense of loss, the invocation of past national glories and tragedies (especially the pointed reference to Empress Myŏngsŏng's assassination and the critique of past leadership), and the yearning for a lost past significant indicators of Korean intellectual sentiment and veiled nationalist expression during the Japanese colonial period. The comparison with Nanjing and the specific historical references provide insight into how contemporary Koreans situated their own history and present condition.

南京은 대여섯번 가본 일이 잇다. 上海서 학교에 다닐 시대에 蹴球隊로 두 번, 音樂隊로 한번, 辯論競爭으로 한번 잠간식 다녀왓고 졸업한 후에는 약 반년동안 가서 살앗다. 도 南京이 新首都가 된 뒤에 신문기자로 가보앗다.
그 중에 가장 인상깁게 나마잇는 것은 물론 반년간에 南京생활이다. 더구나 그때에 우리사람들이 경영하든 조고만 학교에서 영어선생 노릇을 하면서 조선 사람끼리 모여 이룬 적은 사회에 석겨 잇서 비록 물질적으로는 苟借莫甚한 살림이엇스나 정신적으로는 유쾌한 생활이엇슴으로 그때 추억이 매우 감회 기피 어러난다.
秦淮예서 배타고 달구경하든 생각, 老人들과 가티 明孝陵으로 나가 紫金山을 일주하든 생각, 玄武湖의 一日, 燕子磯의 一日, 鷄鳴寺와 五臺山에 산책하든 기억 등이 다 나의 孤寂한 반년의 생각나는 추억뿐이다.
夜泊秦淮近酒家의 노래로 유명하든 秦淮는 城內東南에 잇는 개천이다. 밤에 보면 煙籠寒水月籠沙라는 詩句는 실로 그럴 뜻 하지마는 나제 보면 식검언 하수도에 불과하다. 좌우에 酒家가 물에 그름자를 걱구로 하고 列立하고 胡琴胡歌의 소리가 달비체 석겨 홀러온다. 夫子廟에서 通濟橋까지 畵舫을 타고 약 반시간이면 올라가는 듯이 기억된다. 그러나 秦淮에 배띄운 사람이 시간을 재촉하랴. 秀山公園(지금은 中山公園) 가까히 저어 오르면 右邊으로 수수밧이 잇다. 金陵城 한가운데 수수밧이 잇는 것도 懷舊의 念을 지트게 하는 것이지마는 수숫닙에 달이 어리어 바람에 춤추는 것은 견딀 수 업시 향수와 感古心을 자아내는 것이다. 南京 奠都 以後에 기녀를 廢함에 따라 秦淮의 畵舫에도 거미줄만 쓸게 되엇다. 遊興의 風은 禁할망정 月夜의 詩趣를 찾는 者야 누가 막으랴. 이제부터 秦淮를 찻는 사람은 달과 바람을 美妓歌舞로 알고 갈 것이다.

紫金山은 봄에 보아도 조흐나 가을에 보기가 더 조타. 山頂 가까히 잇는 상록수 틈에 紅葉이 点綴한 것도 가을답거니와 멀리서 紫金色으로 보이는 바위의 비치 가장 조흔 듯하다. 明孝陵은 紫金山 中腹에 잇서 朱土를 바른 祠堂의 바람벽이 遠景으로도 눈에 띄우고 門路에는 文武官과 象馬駱駄形의 석상들이 옹립해 잇다. 陵後에는 도토리가 매처 여인들이 줍기에 바쁘고 陵 우에 올나서면 城內가 宛然히 드리다 보인다. 城內는 반이상이 초토가 되어 잇다. 東北部를 占한 明古宮趾는 石門 한가지만 남고 전부 廢墟가 되어 잇다. 明陵을 차저 인력거나 마차나 나귀를 타고(요새가트면 자동차로)나오느라면 往復에 고궁터를 지나가거니와 古蹟保存會의 건물이 눈에 거슬니는 것 외에 鴨池와 石橋, 온천 등의 자리가＜57＞ 如昔하고 맑은 물이 흐르고 잇다. 여기저기 故磚로 石築으로 한 農家가 잇고 길 좌우에도 깨어진 벽돌로 담을 싸엇스며 유명한 黃瓦는 保存會에서 처치를 햇는지 行客이 하나 둘식 주어가서 업서젓는지 별로 볼 수 업다. 小車떼가 깨진 벽돌을 작고 실어 가되 본래 만흔 벽돌이라 자리도 아니 나는 것 갓다. 지금은 中山道路가 그 중간을 뚤르고 나갓고 明陵보다 한층 놉히 푸른 기와 더픈 孫中山 陵墓가 서 잇스며 北極閣이 헐니고 放送臺가 생긴 세상이니 故宮터에 國民政府 白아舘이 설 날도 머지 안을 것이다.

鷄鳴寺는 城內에 잇는 절깐이다. 지금은 어떠케 되엇는지 모르거니와 내가 잇슬 때까지도 寺屋이 비교적 完存되엇고 특히 寺內의 현판 등의 글시가 상스럽지 안핫섯다. 庭前의 나무에는 까마귀 집을 지엇고 石徑 틈 구이에서 귀뜨람이가 우지짓는다. 유래갓튼 것을 알아보지를 못햇다.
五臺山은 漢西門 안에 잇는 조고만 언덕이다. 내가 留宿하든 집에서 수백보면 갈 수가 잇다. 언덕이라야 나무 한 개도 업고 오직 잔듸가 一面을 더펏슬 뿐이다. 그러나 이 훨닥 버서진 풍경이야 말로 가을 밤의 달구경에는 가장 마즌 것이다. 3개월 이상 가믈이 계속된 江南의 하늘은 맑기가 한량업는데 찬 달빗이 가리울 것업는 잔디의 언덕을 낫가틔 비최고 잇다. 그 달빗미테 번듯 나가 누어서 冥想도 하고 노래도 부르고, 또 친구가 잇스면 이애기도 한다. 잠이 안오는 밤에 축으마저 흐들해지도록 거닐느라면 마음은 거울가치 맑아지고 無邊無涯한 우주의 속에 개암이가튼 인생의 참 의의를 깨닷는 듯 하다.

漢陽의 가을
文一平

팔하게 쾌청한 하늘, 맑아케 투명한 냇물 나무닙 사이로 속삭이는 바람소리, 이슬 우에 빗나는 달빗, 이들은 가을의 맑은 기분을 상징하는 것이다.
가을은는 꿈이 아니오 현실이다. 맑고 놉흔 그의 宇는 大悟徹底한 哲人의 풍격을, 방불히 생각게 한다.
그러나 가을은 얼마큼 孤寂한 이다. 古都의 가을은, 더욱 그리하다. 洛花岩의 가을과, 善竹橋의 가을은 상상만 하야도 처량한 생각이, 소사나는 것이 아니냐.
구태여 멀니 扶餘나 開城에 갈 것 업시 정취만흔 한양의 가을을 감상할 때에, 우리는 얼마나 감회를 자아내게 되는가. 한양은 현대적으로 발전되는 市府이니만치, 扶餘와 開城과는 다르지만, 역사적으론 발서 古都의 부류의 속한다. 문허진 성벽, 븨인 궁전과 가튼 것은 古蹟으로서의 보존을 기다리게 되엇거니와 景福宮의 가을은, 孤寂하다는 것보다도 애수적 기분이 떠도는 것이다.
지금은 업서젓스나, 한 십수년전만 하야도 光化門을 들어가, 勤政殿을 지내서 康寧殿 交泰殿을 돌아가면 乾淸宮이 잇섯스며 그 중 玉壺樓는 明成皇后의 배참하게 구끼신 곳으로 黃金床頭에 뿌런 淋漓한 碧血이 차저보면 응당 그 자취가 남아 잇스련만 부질 업시 느즌 빗을 자랑하는 듯이 울긋불긋 피인 『코쓰모쓰』, 껌껌한 遺址를 繡노앗슬 뿐이다.
北岳은 의연히 프른 것을 띄엿스되 景福宮은 전의 면목을 고치엿다. 北岳에 올라 景福宮을 바러 볼 때마다 大院王의 한 當年 프른 서슬의 覇圖를 머리 속에 그려 보앗다. 10년동안 그의 執政은 內治와 外交의 양방면을 통하야 확실히 氣魄의 비범함을 나타내엿스나 다만 천하 대세를 洞祭하는 眼光이 부족하거 때문에 마츰내 실패를 招致하고 말엇지만 만일 그로하야금 근대적 지식을 具有하야 措施가 得宜하얏든들 엇지 금일이 잇스랴.
舅婦사이에 알력은 朝臣사이의 당쟁과 아울러 끼울려저 가는 國命을 한층 더 촉진한 것으로서 극적 색채가 띄튼 漢陽朝史에 景福宮을 무대로 연출된 幾多의 近世政燮처럼 극적 색채가 찌튼 것은 업슬 것이며, 그 중에도 乙未 8월 20일에 닐어난, 玉壺樓畔의 비극은 가장 대표적이엇다.
甲申政燮이 日淸 두 勢의 알력에서 생겨난 것이라 하면, 乙未정변은 日露 두 勢力의 충돌에서 생겨난 것이라 하겟다. 이와갓튼 국제적 희생을 내인 것은, 당시 조선에 잇서 국제적 경쟁이 얼마나 격열하얏던 것을 설명하는 것이다.
光陰은 덧업시 흘러, 乙未政變도 발서 35년 전의 陳跡이 되어 버렷다. 오늘 날도 가을이오. 밝은 달은 玉壺樓의 폐허를 찌듯이 비최주며, 선을한 바람은 乾淸宮의 遺址를 산들산들 불어준다. 漢陽의 가을을 상징하는 경복궁의 가을은 언제든지 古都의 가을을 찬는 이의 興傷적 원천이 되어 잇다.＜59＞

나는 지금 滿月臺 우에 섯다. 서서 눈을 꼭 감엇다. 감지 안으면 저 달비체 해골가치 어지렵게 노힌 高麗王宮의 잔해을 보지 안을 수 업고 또 두 귀도 가만히 막엇다. 막지 안으면 주츳돌 밋헤서 우는 고려의 벌네소리를 아니드를 수 업스리.
그러나 감은 내 눈압헤는 힌 香불 연긔 피어오르는 속에서 땅에 附伏하여 政事를 엿자오는 군신의 떼가 보이고 松岳山 아래 저 너른 평원에는 槍을 쥔 勤王黨 수만이 백마 탄 王의 閱兵을 밧고 잇는 것이 보이고 또 도포자락을 바람에 폴폴 날니면서 瞻星臺 우에 올라 日月을 살피다가 내려오는 天文博士가 보인다. 그뿐인가. 淫佚과 失政 끗헤 475년의 왕업을 드러업고서 불우 중에 昇遐한 최후의 군주를 위하야 480절깐의 王都의 고승들이 한데 모여서 誦經하며 한쪽에선 王이 쓰든 金銀佩物을 태우는 연기가 하늘로 오르는 것이 보인다. 더구나 「워-이」「워-이」하며 大擧를 메고 宗戚舊臣과 시녀들이 붓잡는 속에 喪擧꾼들이 발마처 나가는 것까지 보인다. 돌기둥 엽헤 가지 꺽긴 저 수양버들은 그때에 大擧 뒤에 따라가든 목마에 지치워 불너진 것이 아닐가 이러캐 생각함애 우리가튼 특수한 처지에 노힌 後腐들은 아예 故宮址를 보지말나 하엿다. 더구나 청춘에 더구나 달밤엔 볼 것이 아니라 하엿다. 그는 마치 실연한 사람이 묘지에 온 것 가튼 말할 수 업는 비애와 孤寂을 늣기는 까닭이다.
