Couple's Round Table Discussion: The Most Harmonious Couple in Chosŏn, Mr. and Mrs. Yun Ch'i-ho
						Samch'ŏlli Company Woman Reporter Kang Sun-ok

This time, we decided to move the "Couple's Round Table Discussion" to the home of Mr. Yun Ch'i-ho. The photo shows the couple enjoying a boat ride at Hae Kŭmgang.
Regarding Mr. Yun, anyone would know him well without me needing to say more here, so I will refrain from speaking about him. With Mr. Yun being so renowned in our society for such a long time, I thought I would try to find out how he spends his days at home and how harmoniously the couple lives together. So, on a late year-end morning that was rather warm for a winter day, I visited their home early.

I had already tried to visit Mr. Yun's home a couple of times the day before, calling by phone and going directly, but was told he leaves the house by nine in the morning, busily going around to the Chongno Central Christian Young Men's Association (YMCA) and various other places, so he wasn't there, and I returned empty-handed.
Today, I had informed them in advance, and also had a general idea of the time Mr. Yun usually goes out, so I stepped inside the gate of this house thinking he would surely be there.

It was early morning, nearing nine o'clock. Passing through the darkish gate of the old Chosŏn-style building, winding this way and that, I entered an inner room resembling a reception room only after passing through the fourth gate.
In the spacious yard, various trees stood tall,<81> and underneath them, nameless grasses lay withered here and there. On one side, a poplar tree, aged several decades, stood alone, soaring high into the pale winter sky, shedding its yellowed autumn leaves one by one, standing desolately.
Somehow, just glancing at the front yard of this house revealed clear traces of a renowned, prestigious family of the past, and in the quiet garden bathed in the early morning sun, only a somber and lonely atmosphere seemed to linger.

I hesitated a couple of times, then asked for Mr. Yun. Perhaps because of the prior arrangement, Mr. Yun emerged promptly. I quickly bowed in greeting.
"So that's Mr. Yun..."
As this thought occurred, I felt his appearance was indeed fitting. His presence, with his long, white-haired beard flowing, was impressive, and the warm human kindness he showed towards us young people seemed to pierce my heart instantly. His voice, though seeming slightly trembling, was cheerful and resonant, appearing to still retain the vigor of the eloquent speaker, the orator Mr. Yun of former days. Though he is an old man past seventy, whenever he attends any gathering and begins to speak, his voice flows on for 30 minutes or an hour; hearing it up close today, how soft and clear it was!

I followed the teacher respectfully into a large room that seemed to serve as the reception room.
The winter morning sun was already shining brightly into the spacious room, illuminating it, and arranged all around the room were potted plants (盆栽) with four deep pink flowers resembling hwalnyŏnkkot (likely a type of lily or hibiscus) and white chrysanthemums in full bloom, filling the room with a fragrance wafting from the flowers that seeped into my heart, making me feel as if I were in a botanical garden.
In the upper part of this room, wardrobes and chests reaching almost to the ceiling were placed elegantly, and on the back wall, two large mirrors (체경) were hung imposingly.

After a short while, the rustling sound of footsteps was heard from the inner room, suggesting the wife was coming out. The glass door slid gently<82> open, and Mr. Yun's wife, looking no older than her 40s or 50s, entered.
The wife quietly walked over to Mr. Yun's side and sat right down next to him.
Look, she wore a silk durumagi (overcoat) that was both greyish and pinkish, and her skirt was exceptionally long, seeming to wrap around her feet. Her roundish face looked generous and kind, like a single stalk of white chrysanthemum, seemingly born with the qualifications to be the eldest daughter-in-law of a wealthy family.

After briefly exchanging greetings guided by Mr. Yun's words, I had the couple seated together and began to inquire about the inner workings of their family life and the various aspects of their cozy home's daily activities.

Me - Did the two of you have a love marriage?
I directed this first question to the wife, who looked significantly younger than Mr. Yun.
Wife - What is a love marriage? We just somehow ended up getting married.
Me - That may be so, but it wasn't entirely an old-style marriage, was it?
Yun - If you put it that way, it might seem so. We came to live together based on some mutual understanding. It wasn't like the old days, solely according to the parents' wishes. It wasn't new-style, nor old-style; you could call it a compromise-style marriage, perhaps. Hahaha.
Me - Regarding marital love, what must one do to live peacefully?
Wife - Well...
Yun - Well, that's not just limited to marital love. For people to have affection for each other and live in harmony, one shouldn't specifically pick out others' faults, but rather focus on their strengths to grow closer. Since everyone, without exception, has both flaws and strengths, it's better not to deliberately expose the flaws. Especially between a husband and wife who are trying to build a family! So, the reason our family always maintains peace is because we mutually, the wife towards me, and I towards the wife, completely forget each other's flaws as if wiping them away, and only praise the strengths. Thus, we naturally spend our days amidst laughter<83> and harmony. This way of thinking is especially necessary between spouses, but I believe it's always needed in social interactions with others as well.
Me - How many children do you have in total?
Wife - We have twelve children in all.
Me - What method do you use for education at home?
Wife - We don't really have a specific policy. We just leave it up to their freedom. When they come home from school, the child who likes music does music, and the one who likes sports does sports. When it's time for them to study, they usually study until ten o'clock and go to bed without anyone having to tell them to.
At this moment, through the door connecting to the room below, the daughter of the house, prettily dressed in a navy skirt and yellow jeogori (upper garment), entered gracefully carrying tea utensils on a tray. The appearance of the daughter, raised gently in a large household, was very modest. She poured the tea and then sat softly beside her mother's knee.
After a while, the daughter gathered the tea things and left.

Me - When did you get married?
Wife - She merely lowers her head.
Yun - I first married when I was fifteen. However, that wife passed away soon after due to illness, and later I married this wife.
Me - Are there times when the two of you disagree and argue?
Wife - Now that we're old like this... what's there to...
Yun - I listen to whatever my wife says, whatever it may be... so there's absolutely nothing to argue about...
Me - How do you handle the children's daily allowance?
Wife - We give them a little bit when they ask occasionally. We don't interfere much with things like the children's purchases.
Me - How much pocket money do you spend on household expenses per month?
Wife - With many children, school expenses amount to quite a lot each month. The small allowances we give them also add up to a considerable sum. And household expenses, one way or another, seem to well exceed<84> 100 Wŏn per month.
Me - What are your thoughts on the children's marriage issues?
Wife - As parents, we try not to interfere much, but how can we completely disregard it?
Yun - It's the affair of the individuals involved, so we leave it to them, but parents need to look after things well from behind the scenes.
Me - Are all your household dining manners purely traditional Chosŏn style?
Wife - It's neither Chosŏn style nor Western style, but rather a 'bibimbap style'. Sometimes we eat Western-style meals, but how can we not eat kimchi, kkaktugi (까뚝이), or gochujang (고초장)? So we don't stick to just one way.
Yun - Speaking of meals, when I was in America in the past, I naturally ate only Western food according to the customs there. I couldn't eat kimchi or kkaktugi, and oh how stuffy and uncomfortable it was; my wife even cried back then.
Me - Teacher, are you very fond of kimchi and kkaktugi?
Yun - Is there any Chosŏn person who doesn't like them? Also, just a few years ago when I was the principal of Songdo Higher Common School (松都高普), we tried to eat with individual rice bowls (공기ㅅ밥), in a somewhat improved style (개량식). However, it still seemed inconvenient for us. Nowadays, housing styles are gradually changing from before, with terms like 'cultural houses' (文化住宅), being built differently, and many people seem to be using individual rice bowls. But for all Chosŏn people to eat with individual rice bowls, it will likely take another sixty years...
Me - What are the happiest moments in your family life?
Wife - For old people like us, there aren't really any particularly happy moments regarding marital affection to speak of... Just sitting together with the children at meals and watching them chatter happily feels most reassuring. The master of the house still goes to bed at one or two in the morning, wakes up before seven, takes a morning walk, and then, as soon as he finishes his morning meal, he's always busy going out. When do we ever sit down together leisurely like this and talk?<85>

The chime of the clock from the side room strikes eleven times—
The morning sun has already spread brightly into the gloomy, rough yard of this house and is flowing through it.
Mr. Yun takes a watch out of his pocket and looks at it.
"Ah— I have an appointment..."
He says, rising from his seat.
"Well— please continue chatting with my wife and rest leisurely before you go..."
Feeling I had spent too much time here, I quickly stood up with him and came straight out.
Released from a somewhat solemn and heavy atmosphere, I moved with light steps towards the Chongno intersection.<86>

**Summary**

This 1936 Samch'ŏlli magazine article features an interview by reporter Kang Sun-ok with Yun Ch'i-ho and his wife, lauded as Chosŏn's most harmonious couple. Conducted in their traditional home, the interview explores their views on marriage, family life, and daily routines. Yun Ch'i-ho attributes their harmony to focusing on each other's strengths and overlooking flaws. They describe their marriage as a "compromise style," blending old and new customs, and discuss raising their twelve children with relative freedom. Their household reflects a mix of Korean and Western elements, particularly in meals, which Yun humorously calls "bibimbap style." The wife notes Yun's busy schedule limits leisurely family time but finds joy in shared meals with their chattering children.

For historians, the article offers insights into the lives of the Korean elite during the colonial period. Yun Ch'i-ho's perspective on navigating tradition (arranged marriage, Korean food) and modernity (love marriage concepts, Western influences, "cultural houses") is significant. His comments on the slow adoption of practices like individual rice bowls (공기밥) and his description of their "compromise" lifestyle reflect the broader societal and cultural transitions occurring in Korea under Japanese rule. The glimpse into their child-rearing philosophy and household budget also provides valuable social history details.

夫婦座談會, 朝鮮第一로 和睦하신 尹致昊 夫妻
						三千里社 婦人記者 康順玉
이번에는 尹致昊 선생님의 댁으로 「夫婦座談會」를 옴기기로 작정했읍니다. 사진은 海金剛에 배띄고 노시는 先生夫妻
이 尹선생님이라고 하시면 어느 누구라도 내가 이 자리에서 다시금 말슴을 드리지 않더라도 잘 아실 줄로 알기에 선생에 대한 말슴은 않기로 하겠읍니다. 이렇듯이 너무나 우리사회에서 오래 전부터 그 이름이 높으신 선생은 그 가정에서는 어떻게 그날 그날을 지내시는지, 또 두 夫婦사이에는 얼마나 화목하시게 지내시는지를 알어보려는 생각으로 나는 겨울날 하고는 좀 따스-한 歲暮의 어느날 아침 일즉이 그 가정을 찾어 들어갔읍니다.
벌서 그 전날부터 이 尹선생님댁을 찾을려고 한 두번 전화로도 말슴디렸고, 즉접 그 댁으로도 찾어갔었으나, 아침 아홉시만 되면 벌서 댁을 나오서서, 鍾路 中央基督敎靑年會로나, 그밖에도 여러 곳으로 자주 밧뿌시게 돌아다니신다고 하며 尹先生님이 안계시기에 그냥 빈손으로 돌아왔었던 것입니다.
이 날은 미리 말슴도 여쭈워 두었던 것이며, 또 선생이 나다니시는 시간도 대개 짐작하고 있으므로 미리부터 선생이 계시려니 생각하며 이 집 대문 안으로 발을 옴겨놓았읍니다.
때는 이른 아침 아홉시 가까운 때이다, 그 옛날 조선식 건물의 거머죽죽한 대문을 이리돌고 저리돌아, 네번째의 관문을 지나서야 이집의 응접실 비슷한 안쪽 방으로 들어섰읍니다.
그 넓다란 뜰에는 여러가지의 나무들이 우뚝우뚝<81> 솟아있고 그 밑으로는 역시 이름모를 풀들이 여기 저기에 시들어저 있다. 한편에는 몇십년을 묵은 포푸라 나무가 한 대 우뚜럼이, 뽀-얀 겨울 하날 높이 솟아있어, 단풍든 누런 잎사귀를 한잎 두잎 떨어트리며 쓸쓸하게 서서 있읍니다.
어쩐지, 이 댁은 그 뜰 앞만 한번 훌터 보아도 옛날의 한다하는 名門의 大家인 흔적이 확연히 엿보이고, 아침해 빈죽이 드러쪼이는 고요한 정원에는 침울하고 적료한 분위기만이 떠도는 듯 싶읍니다.
나는 두어번 머뭇머뭇 하다가 尹선생님을 찾었다. 벌서 미리 약속해 두었던 연고인지, 尹선생님이 쑥 나오신다. 나는 얼른 허리를 굽혀 인사를 드리며
「저분이 바로 尹선생이구나...」
하는 생각이 들매, 과연 그 생긴 품조차 그럴 듯하게 역여젔읍니다. 그 백수가 히날리는 기-다란 수염의 풍채도 좋으려니와 어린 우리에게 주는 따스-한 인정미는 나의 마음을 단번 쿡찌르는 듯 합니다. 약간 떨리는 듯 하면서도 그 명랑하고 쟁쟁한 음성은 전날의 말잘하시던 尹선생, 웅변가이던 尹선생의 호기를 아직 그냥 그대로 간직해 가지고 계신 듯 하었읍니다. 아직도 七旬을 넘겨보는 노인이었만 어떠한 회합에 가시던지, 한번 말슴을 할나치면 30分이고 한 時間이고 좍좍 내려갈기는 그 말소리, 내 오날 정작 가까이 마주 앉어 들어 보매, 어찌 그리 부드럽고 깨끗한지!
나는 웅접실로 된 듯한 넓다란 방안으로 선생의 뒤를 따라 공손히 발자죽을 옴겨놓았읍니다.
그 넓다란 방안에는 벌서 겨울의 아침 해가 디리 쪼여서, 환-하게 방안을 어리게 하고, 방안 사면으로 죽-ㄱ 돌아가면서는 활년꽃 비슷한 진부홍꽃 네 송이와, 그리고, 하-얀 국화꽃들이 활작 피어 있는 분재(盆栽)들을 나란히 늘어놓아, 방안에는 꽃에서 풍겨저 나오는 향훈만이 나의 가슴속으로 슴여드는 듯, 마치 식물원이 아닌가 하는 늣낌을 가지게 합니다.
이 방 웃목에는 천정에 다을 듯하는 의거리, 장농이 모양좋게 놓여저 있고, 뒷벽으로 두 개의 커다란 체경이 으리으리하게 걸려저 있읍니다.
얼마후에 곧 안방에서 부인되는 이가 나오시는지 자작 자작 발자욱 소리가 들리더니 유리 문이 슬며시<82> 열리며, 아직도 4, 50歲밖에 안되어 보이시는 尹선생의 부인이 들어왔읍니다.
부인은 슬며-시 尹선생 옆으로 거러오더니  바로 그 옆에 주저 앉는다.
보라 회색빛도 돌고 분홍색도 도는 비단 두루마기를 입고, 치마는 특벌이 길게 입으서서, 발 끝에 휘휘 감기이는 듯하다. 그 둥그스럼한 얼굴은 보기에도 너그럽고, 어글어글하여 마치 한 포기의 하-얀 국화송이과도 같고, 부잣집 맛며누리될 자격을 타고난 듯하였읍니다.
나는 尹선생님의 말슴으로 간단하게 인사를 엿주운 다음, 이 두 夫婦를 한자리에 계시게 하고 이제부터 그 가정의 생활내면과 폭은한 가정의 그날 그날 움즉여저 나가는 여러 가지 말슴을 듣기로 하였습니다.

나- 두분께서는 연애 결혼을 하섰읍니까?
나는 尹선생보다는 훨신 나이 젊어보이는 그 부인에게 먼저 이렇게 질문을 건니웠다.
부인- 연애 결혼이 다-무엇이겠어요, 그저 어찌어찌 돼서 서로 결혼하었지요.
나- 그렇지요만 아주 구식결혼은 아니였었겠읍지요.
윤- 그렇다면 그럴 법도 하지요, 서로 다소간의 리해 밑에서 살게 되었으니, 옛날처럼 그런, 부모 마음대로는 안한, 신식도 아니고 구식도 아닌 말하자면 절층식 결혼이었다고나 하겠지요. 하하하.
나- 부부애에 있어서는 어떻게 해야만 평화롭게 지낼 수 있겠읍니까?
부인- 글쎄요...
