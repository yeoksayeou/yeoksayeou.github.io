My Ten-Year Dance Memoir
						Ch'oe Sŭng-hŭi

On the afternoon of March 19th, Shōwa 1 (1926), though it wasn't even lichun, as the early spring daylight faded and a chilly wind seeped into my collar, I walked listlessly up the Ch'ebudong alley.
"Why was I born so young? If only I were one year older—" I muttered to myself on the way home. In all my sixteen years, I had never had a day as upsetting and infuriating as today. Yet, even just this morning, I had been thinking,
"Yes, even with my meager strength, I can certainly save my family. Isn't a woman a person? Next year or the year after, I'll become a female teacher and earn over 40 won a month. Then I will rescue my pitiful mother—my poor older sister (my sister was in unfortunate circumstances at the time)—and strive along with my older brothers<106> to save the household."
I constantly chanted this. And truly, whenever such thoughts came to me, I felt like the 'Jeanne d'Arc' (짠닥크) I learned about in history, filled with boundless courage and the possessor of brilliant hope. But today, that beautiful ideal had turned into a mere fragment of a dream. I don't know how much I cried in the yard of the Normal School (now the site of the Vocational School) beyond Ahyŏn Pass. Riding the streetcar and walking along the street, tears welled up, and I wished I could just flop down and have a good cry, but I couldn't even do that. Forcing back the rising tears, I hurried home. "Did Oppa manage to sell some rice? Mother was worried this morning because we had no rice," I thought as I entered the gate. Mother, who was in the room, and my sister, who was in the yard, all welcomed me gladly, asking,
"How did it go?"
"You must be a little hungry? Did you pass?"
The tears I had held back until now burst forth. Though I had resolved never to cry in front of Mother no matter what, upon seeing her, I felt so inexplicably sad that I buried my head in her lap and sobbed. The room was silent for a long while, then,
"So you failed. That's also fate; what can be done?"
Mother said, crying along with me. My sister seemed to be crying too. I wailed loudly. The sound of the three of us crying must have carried outside the door, because
"What's wrong? Why is everyone crying?"
The person entering was Oppa.
"Hey Sŭng-hŭi, why are you crying? You failed the exam. Is that any reason to cry? Tsk, you foolish thing. Get up now."
It was Oppa's cheerful voice, something rare in recent days. We wiped away our tears.
"Did you fail?"
Oppa looked into my face again, smiled faintly, and asked. In fact, at that moment, seeing Oppa's face—his face overflowing with joy, as if he had some happy 'news'—I still haven't forgotten that face.<107>
"I didn't fail."
I answered thus. Actually, out of 860 applicants for 100 spots, I had placed seventh. Considering that among the graduates of Sookmyung Girls' High School that year, there were nine honor students and I graduated eighth with honors, my Normal School exam results were comparatively better.
Everyone looked startled.
"Then why are you crying?"
Oppa asked.
"They said I'm too young, so I should take a year off and come back next year."
"Hahahaha."
"Hahahaha."
The room instantly burst into laughter.
"That's right. Even if you entered this year and were appointed as a Type B teacher two years later, you'd be eighteen. How can an eighteen-year-old girl teach other people's children? Just forget about it. Sŭng-hŭi. Now, listen to what I have to say."
"Oh, Oppa, did you sell the rice?"
"Yes."
"Mixed grain again?" (양쌀)
"Well, what fate allows us to eat white rice?" (이-ㅂ쌀)
Indeed, for the three or four years since our family's fortunes declined, we had only eaten mixed-grain rice.
"So, what was the story Oppa wanted to tell?"
"It's nothing much, but today I went to the library and saw the Kyŏngsŏng Ilbo. It said that a dancer from mainland Japan, Ishii Baku (石정<0x9F20>), has arrived and will be holding performances at the Public Hall for two days starting today. At the end, it mentioned that this is his first time in Chosŏn, and for some reason, he feels that many artists will emerge from Chosŏn. He also said he would like to take a couple of Korean girls who wish to learn his dance art back with him."
"Oppa, what is dance?"
I asked my brother. Truly, although I had graduated from high school, I didn't know what dance was, and of course, I had never seen a dance performance. To be perfectly honest, I hadn't even been to a theater to watch a motion picture even once. I had merely thought, 'Dance is just dancing.'
"Dance is dancing. And it's art. It's the art with the longest history among human arts."
But I couldn't understand Oppa's answer. Since I respected Oppa as an artist, I just learned it as <108> 'I suppose that's what it is.'
After Mother and Sister went out to prepare dinner,
"Hey Sŭng-hŭi, don't you want to go with him?" Oppa asked me, looking over his shoulder.
"With whom?"
"Ishii Baku—"
I just stared at Oppa's face for a long time without answering.
"Anyway, you can't answer without knowing what dance is. But if you were to learn it, considering your physique, your understanding of music, and your intelligence, I believe you could become a great creative dancer for Chosŏn in the future. Anyway, do you want to go see the performance with me tonight?"
"Yes, let's go."

The night of Ishii Baku's dance performance—this night was the most memorable night of my life. As the sound of the gong (銅鑼) rang out, the lights dimmed. Through the gelatin filters (제라진), beams of cobalt (코발도) and green (그린) light intersected, and amidst a solemn piano melody (메로듸) of some tune, Ishii Baku's solo dance, 'Prisoner' (囚人), began. Entangled in chains, taking heavy steps across the stage, one step, two steps—ah, at that moment, I thought, this isn't just dancing, it's expressing something. Until then, I had believed dancing was only done during times of joy and pleasure. But now, he was expressing heaviness and suffering. Yet, in the next moment, he broke those thick chains, raised his head to the heavens, lifted both arms to express ecstasy, and then collapsed onto the stage. Again the gong sounded, the spotlight (스폿트) went out, and the house lights came on. The next dance—'Mountain Climbing' (登山)—a young man and a young woman look up at a high mountain and climb and climb. But it seems endless. They lean on each other's shoulders to rest, then climb again, and again, perhaps reaching the summit. They smile faintly. And sit down. Leaning my shoulder against Oppa's,
"I really want to learn,"
I said forcefully.
"Alright, then let's go,"
replied Oppa, taking my hand and leading me into a room adjacent to the stage. There, another new <109> world unfolded before my eyes. Piles of costumes here and there—a mirror on a table—powder, rouge, a water kettle—and naked young women. And Mr. Ishii himself, having just finished dancing, entered—he seemed out of breath, panting as he spoke to someone. Oppa greeted him and introduced me. He looked me over for a moment, then said,
"Mr. Terada (寺田), dreams are truly strange things. Last night in my dream, I met a young woman called a Chosŏn girl. She kept asking to follow me. So I told her she could, and then I woke up. It's truly a strange occurrence. Please talk to her. And since you reside in Chosŏn, if you vouch for her, I will take her and teach her well. Looking at her, she seems well-qualified."
Almost everyone in the room turned their gaze towards me. I just stood there with my head bowed. Just then, the sound of the gong rang out again from outside, followed by piano and violin music. Immediately after, someone called out, "Sensei—"
and Mr. Ishii rushed out onto the stage. Thus, we ended up sitting face-to-face with this Mr. Terada in the basement dining hall.
"I'm from the Kyŏngsŏng Ilbo (京城日報). You say you want to follow Mr. Ishii and learn dance; what is your motivation? When a person decides to do something, there must first be a motive, and second, a firm basis, shouldn't there?"
Oppa answered all these questions. He explained that he had known Mr. Ishii's art since his time in Tokyo, and that he knew well the spirit with which Mr. Ishii was leading the new dance movement in mainland Japan after returning from Germany. Consequently, Oppa admired Mr. Ishii, and upon seeing the Kyŏngsŏng Ilbo article today, he had told me to learn from him. He also described how he viewed my potential, and mentioned that the new dance movement in Chosŏn was completely virgin territory. He asked Mr. Terada (寺田氏) to make arrangements for me to go and learn, if possible. We then agreed to meet the next morning at the Pujihwa Ryokan (不知火 旅舘) in Honmachi (本町), and returned home.
That night, I tossed and turned, unable to sleep.<110>
'If I follow him alone—if I learn dance, can I become a great dancer? And the most difficult problem, will Father and Mother permit it? Run away? No.'
'But earlier, when Oppa and I walked home from the Public Hall, weren't those steps filled with happiness and hope?'
Smiling faintly to myself, I turned over and fell asleep.
The next morning, Oppa and I headed towards Honmachi without telling Mother where we were going or mentioning last night's events.
"Yes, I heard everything from Mr. Terada last night. It's a very commendable thing. But your father might be a bit surprised to hear that you want to learn dance after graduating from high school. Anyway, since you're so passionate, and especially since you like music, then I'll teach you dance at home and also send you to music school—tuition-free, of course—"
Ah, could there be any words more welcome and grateful than these? Overcome with joy, I barely managed to hold back tears.
"However, you'll need your parents' permission. When I take someone else's daughter, I must receive her parents' consent."
But upon hearing this, I felt confident. He had said he would even send me to music school.

"Mother."
"Yes?"
"Shall I go to mainland Japan?"
"What are you talking about! How?"
Oppa explained the details to Mother on my behalf.
"I don't know. It's for you two to decide. But that young child, going all the way to mainland Japan? And how do we know what Father will think?"
"Father will surely approve too. Especially since they said they'll even send her to music school."
"But who can we trust?"
Mother, teary-eyed, began to cry again. Sure enough, when Father returned in the evening and heard the news,
"What on earth is this dancing? Dancing is—well, something only kisaeng do."
On this point, Father and Oppa debated for quite <111> a while. Father argued it was right to let me wait another year and then send me to the Normal School, while Oppa insisted that since many others could serve as female teachers in Chosŏn, it was right for me to take a pioneering stance for the sake of Chosŏn's art world. Eventually,
"Well then, meet with Teacher Sŏng (Sŏng Ŭi-gyŏng) and Teacher Kim (Kim Yŏng-hwan), who used to teach Sŭng-hŭi, discuss it with them, and do as they advise," was the conclusion reached. Thus, Oppa and I went to Sookmyung School to find Teacher Sŏng and Teacher Kim. Teacher Kim said,
"Since dance is also art, I suppose it doesn't matter."
And Teacher Sŏng remarked,
"Actually, since she is talented and has potential, I was planning to send her to a Normal School or Music School in Tokyo next year as a scholarship student, but if her guardian wishes otherwise, how could I object—."
This was their response. Frankly, I hesitated. Because my teachers were not absolutely supportive. When the teachers who had taught and nurtured me for 10 years weren't showing signs of approval, it's true that my courage faltered to some extent.
But on the way home after leaving the school gate, Oppa said,
"Don't hesitate, just go. Once you've decided, don't look sideways, just go forward."
He encouraged me like this. 'That's right, I must go. Aren't I currently thinking that whatever Oppa says is right, and whatever Oppa does is good? I must go.' Chanting this inwardly, I kicked a stone on the roadside with my foot shod in rubber shoes (부용화 신).
"Father, Teacher Sŏng and Teacher Kim both approve."
I said this, not even knowing if my words were lies or truth.
"Alright then. Go ahead."
"But child, what clothes will you wear when you leave?"
Mother looked at me with tear-filled eyes, worrying about my clothes.
"They said Korean clothes are fine too. They'll get me Western clothes there."
I answered thus.
"When are you leaving?"
"Tomorrow morning."
"What?"
Father too showed a sad expression—<112> Mother looked almost startled.
"But tonight, Father, please meet Mr. Ishii and talk to him. Mr. Ishii said he needs Father's permission."
"Yes, of course."
And so, that night, Father and Mr. Ishii, along with Mr. Terada and Oppa—and me—we sat together as Father made his request to Mr. Ishii, and Mr. Ishii spoke to Father—with Oppa interpreting back and forth.

Today is the day of departure.
The morning of March 21st, Shōwa 1 (1926). Although it was an early spring morning, it was foggy and overcast, a dreary morning. The atmosphere in our house was melancholic, even sadder, from dawn. "Because we have no money, we can't educate our child further and have to entrust her to others," lamented Father, sitting and just smoking tobacco! 'How will the young child manage to study well so far away? Will she stay healthy? I hear they don't even heat the rooms in mainland Japan; won't she be cold?' worried Mother, just crying! But Oppa alone tried his best to lighten the mood, that atmosphere, making jokes, calling out "Father—Mother—" unnecessarily, and bustling in and out.
"We find it too painful to watch you leave, so we won't go to the station."
These were Father's words.
Kyŏngsŏng Station platform (푸라트홈)—the train stood waiting. Mr. Ishii's party was busy checking luggage, and I was inside by the window—Oppa outside the window.
"Write often when you get there. And study hard—understand!"
I just nodded silently. But I was sad. Why, I didn't know myself. Was it sorrow at leaving my father's embrace? No—then was it fear of going alone? No—then why? I didn't understand the emotion. I just felt sad. The bell hanging on the platform pillar rings. The conductor raises his hand. And Oppa stands right in front of me, holding his hat he has taken off. But what is this? Isn't Mother running down from the stairs over there? And aren't Teacher Son and Teacher Im running behind her? But the train starts to move. And Mr. Ishii places his hand on my shoulder and looks out.

She rented a house within the grounds of the city's Yŏnhŭi College (延禧專門學校) and is living there while researching ancient Korean art and learning the Korean language that accompanies it.

**Summary**

This excerpt from Ch'oe Sŭng-hŭi's memoir recounts the pivotal few days in March 1926 when, at age 16, her path dramatically shifted from aspiring teacher to pioneering modern dancer. After narrowly missing the age requirement for normal school despite passing the exam, her supportive older brother encourages her to attend a performance by the visiting Japanese dancer Ishii Baku. Captivated by the expressive power of modern dance, she resolves to study under him. The narrative details her family's mixed reactions—her brother's enthusiastic support, her mother's tearful worries, her father's initial disapproval comparing dance to kisaeng entertainment—and the eventual, somewhat hesitant, consent obtained after consulting her former teachers and meeting Ishii Baku. The piece ends poignantly on the day of her departure for Japan, filled with sadness and uncertainty despite her determination.

From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into colonial Korean society. It highlights the limited career options for educated women (teaching being a primary goal), the economic hardships faced by formerly affluent families, and the complex family dynamics involving patriarchal authority and sibling influence. It vividly portrays the introduction and reception of Japanese modern art forms (specifically, modern dance) in Korea, representing a significant cultural encounter. The initial resistance equating dance with lower-class entertainment (kisaeng) versus the brother's view of it as high art reflects societal tensions and changing values. Furthermore, the reliance on Japanese figures like Ishii Baku and intermediaries like Mr. Terada for artistic training abroad underscores the colonial power structure and Japan's cultural influence during this period. The emotional departure scene encapsulates the personal sacrifices and anxieties involved in pursuing unconventional paths, especially for a young woman traveling alone to the colonizer's homeland.

이치지도 안는 昭和 元年 3월 19일 오후, 이른 봄 한낫이 기울고 쌀쌀한 바람이 옷깃으로 슴여들 때 나는 기운업시 체부동골목을 올나간다.
『내가 웨 나희가 적엇든가 한살만 더-먹엇드면』하고 혼자 속으로 중얼거리면서 집을 차자 드러가는 길이다. 나는 나히 열여섯살이 먹도록 오늘과 가티 속상하고 화가 난 날은 업섯다. 그래두 나는 오늘 오전까지도
『오냐 나의 적은 힘으로도 능히 집안을 구할 수가 잇다. 녀자는 사람이 아닌가? 나는 래년이나 후년이면 녀교원이 되여서 한달에 40원 이상 수입이 잇다. 그러면 나는 불상하신 우리 어머님-가여운 언니(언니는 그때 불행한 경우에 잇섯다)를 구하여 가면서 옵바들과 가티<106> 집안을 구하기에 힘쓰겟다』
이러케 늘 부르지젓다. 그러고 참말이지 그런 생각이 나는 때이면 나는 마티 력사에서 배온 『짠닥크』나 되는 것과 가티도 한업는 용기가 용소슴하고 찬란한 회망의 소유자이엿다. 그러나 오늘와서는 그 아름다운 리상이 한토막의 꿈으로 도라가고마니 나는 아현고개 넘어 사범학교마당 (지금 직업학교자리)에서 얼마나 울엇는지 몰낫섯다. 전차를 타고올 때에도 길에 걸어올 때에도 울음이 복바처서 털석 주저안저 한바탕 울엇스면 조흐련마는 그러지도 못하고 나오는 울음을 억지로 참고 집으로 달니여갓다. 『참 옵바가 쌀이나 팔어왓나? 아츰에 어머님이 쌀이 업다고 걱정을 하섯는데』 이러한 생각을 하면서 집대문을 드러섯다. 방에 게신 어머님이시며 마당에 잇든 언니는 모도 다-반가워 하면서
『엇더케 됏니?』
『조-ㅁ 배가 곱흐겟니? 그래 부텃니?』
나는 여태것 참엇든 울음이 복바처 올나서 어머님 앞에서는 엇더한 일이 잇든지 울지를 아니하랴고 결심을 하엿것마는 어머님을 뵈오니 웃지나 설어운지 고만 어머니의 무릅에 고개를 파뭇고 늣껴 울엇다. 방안이 한참이나 고요하드니
『락제를 한게로구나. 그것두 다-운수지 하는 수 잇니?』
하시면서 어머님이 따라 우신다. 언니도 우는 모양이다. 나는 엉엉 울엇다. 세식구의 우는 소리가 방문 박까지 들니엿든지
『웨 그래? 웨들 그래?』
하면서 드러오는 사람은 옵바이엿다.
『얘 승희야 웨 우니? 너-낙제하엿구나. 그랫기로 울긴 웨 울어. 예끼 못생긴 것. 어서 이러나.』

이리하야 그날밤 아버님과 석정씨 그러고 사전씨와 옵바-나-우리는 한자리에 안저서 아버님이 석정씨의게 부탁하시는 말슴 또 석정씨가 아버님께 하시는 말슴-옵바의 통역으로 오고가고 하엿다.

오늘은 떠나는 날이다.
昭和 6년 3월 21일의 아츰 이날은 비록 이론 봄 아츰이것마는 안개가 끼이고 날이 음산하야 쓸쓸한 아츰이엿다. 우리 집안의 공기는 새벽부터 우울한 그 보다도 슲은 날이엿다. 『돈이 업서서 자식을 더-공부식히지 못하고 남의게 맛기여 보내다니』하시면서 한탄을 하시면서 담배만 태우시고 안저게신 아버님! 어린 것이 웃더케 그-먼데 가서 공부나 잘 할른지? 몸이나 성할난지 일본내지에는 방에 불도 아니 때인다는데 치웁지나 아니할른지?』 하시면서 우시기만 하시는 어머니! 그러나 옵바 혼자만이 그 기분-그 분위기를 될 수 잇는대로 명랑하게 하여보랴고 우수운 소리도 하고 아버님-어머님-하면서 일업시 부르기도 하고 안박으로 왓다갓다한다.
『우리는 가는 것이 보기 실혀서 정거장에도 아니 나가겟다.』
아버님의 말슴이시다.
경성역에 『푸라트홈』-기차는 가로 노혀잇다. 석정씨의 일행은 짐바다놋키에 분주하여 잇고 나는 창문 안-옵바는 창문 박
『가거든 편지나 자조 여라. 그러고 렬심이-알엇지!』
나는 아모 말업시 고개만 끄덕어렷다. 그러나 나는 슲엇다. 웨 그것은 나도 모른다. 아버님의 품 안을 떠나기가 설어워서? 아니-그러면 혼자 가기가 무서워서? 아니-그러면 웨? 나는 그 감정은 모른다. 다만 슲으기만 하다. 『푸라트홈』 기둥에 달니인 종이 운다. 차장이 손을 든다. 그러고 옵바는 바로 내 앞에 서서 모자를 버서든다. 그러나 저게 왼일이냐 저편 충게 우에서 어머님이 달려내려 오시지를 아니하느냐? 그러고 그 뒤에는 손선생하고 임선생이 달려오시지를 아니하느냐? 그러나 기차는 움직기기 시작한다. 그러고 석정씨는 나의 억개에 손을 언고 내여다 본다.

子와 結婚을 하야 一女를 나헛는데 現今 그 夫人되는 이는 朝鮮을 硏究하기 爲하야 令孃을 다리고 昨年 8월頃에 京城에로, 滿一個年間 豫定을 하고 나와서, 市內延禧專門學校 構內에다 借家를 하여가지고, 生活을 하여가면서, 朝鮮古代藝術을 硏究하고 잇스며 그에 딸은 朝鮮語를 배우고 잇다.
李元淳氏 이 분도 호노루루에 잇서 名望이 가장 잇는 분이다. 아직 50年 前後한 年齡으로서 米州本土의 某大學을 卒業하고 나온 이다. 現在 호노루루淃에 와서 活動하는 移民局과 稅關에 囑託으로 잇서 米國政府의 信用이 잇슬 뿐만아니라, 조선인 會社에서도 만흔 活動을 하고 잇는 이다. 여긔까지 적어오고 나니, 퍽 未備한 데가 만흔 듯하나 그만 붓을 놋키로 하겟다. (끝) <65>
