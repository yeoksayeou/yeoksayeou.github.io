**Body Text Translation:**
Account of Meeting British and American Women's Suffrage Activists
						Na Hye-sŏk (羅蕙錫)

An interview regarding women's issues with an elderly unmarried woman who was a member of Mrs. Pankhurst's suffrage movement in Britain, as she happened to be my English teacher.

R: Who first started the suffrage movement?
S: Twenty years ago, when we were holding demonstrations, there was an old woman who was too old to come out, but she would open her window, sit and watch, then close the window and meditate. She is the founder of the women's movement in Britain, Mrs. Fawcett (she is deceased). The second generation is Mrs. Pankhurst (팡크하스트 夫人), and she was the first to start street demonstrations. Forty years ago, "There were 10,000 women all marching in the street to the Albert Hall." I was young then, and my mother participated.
R: What was written on the banners?
S: They read, "Fight for woman's independence" and "Fight for woman's Rights."
R: Of course, many must have been arrested?
S: Arrested? Absolutely. They were all arrested and taken in, went on hunger strikes, and caused quite a commotion.
R: Do members have a badge or symbol?
S: Yes, they do. They wear "Votes for women" written on their hats, wear badges, and sashes. This is the sash they wore back then.
(The lady showed a faded navy blue sash with gold lettering.)
R: Please give this to me.
S: What will you do with it? <91>
R: Who knows if I might become the founder of the women's rights movement in Chosŏn (朝鮮)?
S: That's right. Please take it as a memento.
(Who was member of mrs Pankharstis Womens Social and Politecal union afeetwasdos Womesn Freedom Le gne) [Note: Original English text seems garbled here, likely meant "Who was a member of Mrs. Pankhurst's Women's Social and Political Union, afterwards Women's Freedom League"]
R: What was the cause of the suffrage movement?
S: Ultimately, it's because men didn't reflect on themselves and thought only they were competent. Men always say (言必稱), "What does a woman know?" However, no matter how great a hero (英雄豪傑) he may be, there isn't one who doesn't fall for a woman's charms. Wisdom and learning are the exterior of life; the interior of life (內面) is bound by human feeling (人情). At that time, women thought, "How can we, who are so clever and intelligent, be satisfied with laws (法律) made by those foolish men?" That's how the women's rights movement (女權 運動) began (始初).
R: What were Mrs. Pankhurst's main arguments (主論)?
S: The labor issue (勞動問題), chastity issue (貞操問題), divorce issue (離婚問題), and voting issue (投票問題).
R: Did you also give speeches during the demonstrations?
S: Of course. When I stood on a chair on the roadside giving a speech, one woman asked the reason (理由). I said, "This is for you and your daughter. God created you and men equally. Why shouldn't you be able to do what men do?" The tens of thousands in the crowd (數萬群衆) gathered to watch applauded, shouting, "Hear, hear!" (올소 올소). (Photo shows Na Hye-sŏk in the center, surrounded by Pankhurst supporters - 中央 羅女史)
R: Were there many women's groups during the demonstrations?
S: There were many organizations (團體), and they demonstrated in separate groups according to their specialized professions (專門 職業).
R: Isn't it true (事實) that men are superior (優勝) to women?
S: Why do you say that? Men boast that they are strong and rich (富), but in reality <92>, they are mostly foolish. Women remain silent, appearing weak (弱), but if they have something they want to do and put their minds to it, there's nothing they can't achieve. Here's an example (例). When a husband asks his wife to go somewhere, suppose the wife just says she doesn't want to for no reason. The husband will likely insist unreasonably. But suppose the wife holds her head and says she has a severe headache (頭痛) and can't go. The husband will likely show a sympathetic (同情) face and suggest they don't go. Doesn't this show how foolish and weak men are, and how clever and strong women are?
R: What about the relationship (關係) with children (子息)?
S: Everyone, when young, thinks of their mother as greater than the whole world (世上). But look what happens when they grow up and get married. For them, their husband becomes their mother, or their wife becomes their mother. There's a story like this: A mother, who had given all her affection and love, passed away. At this time, the husband fell ill and was bedridden. The wife said, "I should naturally go in your place, but since you are sick, I won't go." However, a few days (몃칠) later, the wife's mother passed away. At that time, despite being ill (압흔몸으로), the husband and wife went together (同行) to the grave site (山所). Morally (印度上), this is wrong, but it shows the power of a woman. That's why we have a proverb (俗語) in our country: "A woman laughs up her sleeve." (Photo shows Ms. Na Hye-sŏk - 寫眞은 羅惠錫氏)
R: Is gender discrimination (男女差別) severe in Britain (英國) too?
S: Yes, it was. Previously, daughters were made (식혀) to wash their brothers' clothes (빨나), but not anymore. Previously, the wife did everything for the husband (하엿스나), but now, everyone does things for themselves as much as possible.
R: Regarding children, who among the parents (兩親) bears greater responsibility (責任)?
S: If a child has faults, the mother is not blamed; the father is. If the father dies first and the mother remains, the mother raises (敎養) the children as best she can. But if the mother dies first and the father remains, <93> another woman comes in to manage the household, and concurrently, the children's education is neglected. Therefore, for the children's sake, it's better for the mother to be alive and the father to die first. So, when there is no father and only a mother, the responsibility falls on the mother, but when both parents are present, the responsibility falls on the father. If, after the husband dies, she goes to another husband, the responsibility is placed on the man. When an unmarried woman has a child, the responsibility is placed on the woman. If there are children and a divorce suit occurs, the judge (裁判長) looks at both parents and assigns responsibility for the children to the party deemed more suitable (有利).
R: In what circumstances is divorce most common?
S: Mostly, divorces arise from economic problems (經濟問題). And many occur due to misconduct (不品行) between men and women.
R: What are your thoughts on the labor issue?
S: When I was a teacher (敎師), I had 60 students (學生), and the male teacher (男先生) in the classroom (敎室) next door also had 60 students. Although I had additional sewing classes (裁縫時間), my salary (月給) was lower. So, I complained (不平). But now, for the most part, the difference (差異) has disappeared (업서 젓습니다).
R: What is the voting age (年齡)?
S: Until last year, it was 21 for men and 30 for women, but starting this year (금년붓혀), it became the same age (同年) for women too.
R: What are your feelings (所感) about living a single life (獨身生活)?
S: Just as married (己婚) women have both pleasures (快樂) and loneliness (孤寂), single women likely have pleasures and loneliness too.
R: Then which side is better? (Photo shows Ms. Na on the Indian Ocean - 寫眞은 印度洋上의 羅女士) When one is young, perhaps single life is better (나홀거시오). (Remainder omitted - 下畧-원문) <94>

**Summary**

This article is an interview conducted by Na Hye-sŏk, a prominent Korean feminist writer and artist, with her unnamed elderly English teacher, who was an active participant in the British women's suffrage movement led by Mrs. Pankhurst. The interview covers the origins and history of the British suffrage movement, mentioning key figures like Mrs. Fawcett and Mrs. Pankhurst, tactics such as street demonstrations and hunger strikes, and core issues including voting rights, labor equality, divorce, and chastity. The interviewee shares personal anecdotes from her activism and discusses gender dynamics, parental responsibilities, reasons for divorce, and compares the lives of married and single women. From a Korean colonial history perspective, this article is significant as it shows a leading Korean intellectual engaging directly with Western feminist movements and activists in the 1930s. It highlights the transnational exchange of feminist ideas and Na Hye-sŏk's interest in applying lessons from the British suffrage movement to the situation of women in colonized Korea (Chosŏn), even humorously suggesting she might become the "founder" of the Korean women's rights movement.
