December 8th, 5 PM to 11 PM, at Myŏngwŏlgwan (明月舘), Seoul <129>

**吹込할 때의 苦心談** (Tales of Hardship During Recording)

Kim Tonghwan - (This Magazine's Chief Editor) Good evening. I am grateful for the opportunity to gather you all tonight and hear your thoughts on popular songs, which hold tremendous influence right now. It's probably a rare occasion recently for singers from the capital's six major companies and all the literary department heads to gather in one place like this. I believe this setting will allow for a full discussion of all issues.
According to my investigation at the Government-General authorities, the number of records sold annually in Korea now is about 1.5 million discs, of which one-third, or 400,000 to 500,000, are said to be Korean records. Records targeting an audience of 400,000 to 500,000 people are indeed casting a significant influence on our society and culture.
As such, record fans scattered throughout the thirteen provinces will surely want to hear from you singers and literary department heads. Please don't hold anything back; tell us many good stories, funny stories, and surprising stories. First, to the singers, do you eat eggs or drink cold water when you record?

Wang Supok - (Polydor Female Singer) - Well. I heard Katsutarō (勝太郞 - of Shima no Musume [島の娘]) say once that eating eggs makes the voice come out well—but that's not the case for me. If I have a recording that day, I eat just a little breakfast, moisten my throat with cold water, and then stand before the microphone. Women like Ichimaru (市丸) say that having a couple of glasses of sake lifts their spirits (浩然) and makes their voice come out well, but that's not the case for me either.

Kim - Even in your daily life, don't you worry about producing a good voice?

Wang - Well, it's my profession. Even when walking down the street, if I unconsciously see a patent medicine (賣藥) advertised on a utility pole, something like "Beautiful Voice Medicine" <130> that claims to make your voice good, I rush off to the pharmacy, buy it, and keep taking it.

Kim - Do recordings usually happen in one take? After practicing, when you stand before the microphone, do you never have to do it again?

Wang - Why wouldn't I? If I'm not satisfied with it, or if the literary department head tells me to do it again, I'll do it two, three, five, even six times. When I was in Tokyo, I saw Katsutarō do five or six takes when recording.

Sŏnu Ilsŏn - (Polydor Female Singer) - It's not just Katsutarō. I saw Shōji Tarō (東海林太郞) recording during the summer then, and he redid it four or five times. (Photo is of Ms. Wang Supok recording)

Kim - Ms. Ilsŏn, what do you do before recording?

Sŏnu Ilsŏn - Me? If I have a recording tomorrow, I don't eat dinner tonight. That way, when I stand before the microphone hungry, my mind is focused, and my voice comes out clear and bright.

Wang P'yŏng - (Polydor Literary Dept. Head) - Let me confess how these two record. Ms. Ilsŏn starts singing with proper posture, but by the end, her back gradually bends, and eventually, she's practically touching her nose to the microphone. <131> (Everyone laughs) And Ms. Supok starts composed, but as one or two verses pass, she gradually leans to one side, almost to the point of falling over. It seems they both lose their composure at that moment.

Wang Supok - And then, after pouring my heart out singing like that, if the teacher who was in the next room comes over looking displeased, the thought "Oh no, this recording must be ruined again" hits me, and I just want to hug the microphone and cry.

Kim - Before recording one song, about how many times do you practice?

Sŏnu Ilsŏn - After the composition is done and comes into my hands, I find myself humming it absently while walking down the street, or humming it while sitting at home talking with guests—my mind is always on it, so over ten days, I probably practice humming it about a hundred times.

Wang P'yŏng - Humming day and night, hoping it sells well, ho ho ho.

Kim - A hundred times! That's certainly a lot of effort!

Kang Namhyang - (Okeh Female Singer) If you assume ten times a day for ten days, that amounts to a hundred times of humming.

Kim Nŭngin - (Okeh Literary Dept. Head) Usually, the lyrics are learned separately, and one needs to know the melody first.

Kim - Ms. Kang Namhyang, what do you eat when recording?

Kang Namhyang - I don't eat anything special, but I try hard to sleep well and get into a cheerful mood.

Ko Poksu - (Okeh Male Singer) I don't take any special medicine either, but about an hour after eating, I take a walk in the park or somewhere to aid digestion, and then go into the recording session in a cheerful mood. Sometimes I drink milk.

Kim Sŏnch'o - (Siren Female Singer) Me too. If I eat too much before recording, my voice changes and doesn't sound like my own.

Kim Yonghwan - (Polydor Male Singer) - I think an empty stomach is better. Like they say, a hungry dog catches the pheasant, ha ha ha.

Yi Ŭnp'a - (Okeh Female Singer) - Drinking a cup of black tea (紅茶) is also good.

Kim Haesong - (Okeh Male Singer) - More than food or medicine, it generally depends on one's mood. I suppose that's true for any artist.

Yi Hayun - Generally, the lack of good recording studios in Korea is a major problem. For instance, people whose condition <132> was good and sang well when practicing in Seoul often catch colds after being worn out from many days of travel by boat and train to Tokyo. Not being able to eat kimchi or kkakdugi and having to eat that salty food makes it easy for the vocal cords (聲帶) to change. You really don't know how much pain the lack of recording studios causes. (Photo is a collection of signatures from those gathered that night)

Pak Yŏngho - That's right. That's a big problem. If there were recording studios in Korea, we could practice freely and use orchestras as we please.

Min Hyosik - What's more painful is that because they travel all the way from Seoul to Tokyo, if the female singers happen to have days related to their physiological condition (生理上關係) only women have, the recording might even be postponed for several days.

Kim Nŭngin - Is that all? Because we go to Tokyo specifically, having to record seven or eight sides a day makes it even harder.

**演奏旅行하면 어느 곳이 나은가** (Which Places Are Better for Performance Tours?) <133>

Kim Tonghwan - As street nightingales, you must have thoroughly traveled the length and breadth of Korea's eight provinces for several years. Among the various regions, where did they particularly understand popular songs well, and where were the box office results good?

Wang P'yŏng - Except for Ullŭng Island and Cheju Island, there isn't a place in the three thousand ri, north or south, that we haven't visited. Based on our Polydor company's experience, the Hamgyŏng Province region (咸鏡道地方) seems good. Next might be Kyŏngsang Province (慶尙道).

Kim Yonghwan - When you go on stage and sing, if you don't get two or three encores, it doesn't feel like the audience is enthusiastic. But when we went to Hamhŭng (咸興), maybe because the Hamgyŏng Province people are passionate like Spanish people, they cheered genuinely and sincerely, creating a real rapport (意氣投合) between the audience and the performer.

Yu Yŏngguk - (Victor Company Sales Manager) - P'yŏngyang is good in that respect too. If they like it, they welcome you so enthusiastically, as if they want to embrace you.

Kim Nŭngin - That's right. Looking at the results of our Okeh tours, P'yŏngyang is good, and then Sŏnch'ŏn is also good. Even though it's a town where Christians live, they understand music quite well.

Ko Poksu - From what I've seen, Ch'ŏngjin and Sŏngjin in Hamgyŏng Province are also good. Maybe it's because fewer troupes perform in those distant places.

Kim - I heard you went to Cheju Island (濟州島). How was it there?

Kim Nŭngin - The Okeh company didn't go as a group; I went alone to collect folk songs. If only we could go to southern towns like that, which possess so much local folk art, it would be nice, but the cost is just enormous.

Kim - Has anyone been to Kando (間島) or Manchuria (滿州)?

Wang P'yŏng - We went. We first performed a play at the "Sŏngsoe Stage" in Kando. There was a scene in the play involving firing a gun, so we fired a blank, and the audience got scared by the gunshot sound and ran away. (Everyone laughs)

Pak Yŏngho - Just as their nerves are that sensitive, on the other hand, if the performance is good, they keep you on stage <134> and praise you endlessly. Their personalities are very straightforward. It used to be difficult because of public security (治安), but now it's become much easier to perform there.

Kim Yonghwan - Moreover, in the past, there were so many groups demanding free admission – this group, that group – that having forty or fifty people push their way in was truly exasperating. It's probably not like that now.

Kim - Yes, I understand. So, where are the box office results (興行成績) better?

Min Hyosik - Generally, the first-tier locations (一等地).

Kim - First-tier locations means?

Wang P'yŏng - Seoul, P'yŏngyang, Taegu.

Pak Yŏngho - That's right. Then the second-tier locations would be Chŏnju, Kwangju, Mokp'o, Wŏnsan, etc.

Kim Yonghwan - And besides those, we travel around the smaller towns.

Kim - In a first-tier location like Seoul, how much income do you make in one night?

Kim Yonghwan - Well, there are cases where we earn 700 or 800 wŏn. Usually, in the second-tier locations mentioned earlier, we make about 300 to 400 wŏn per evening.

Wang P'yŏng - In some places, we might only make 50 or 60 wŏn, but those are all small villages (小村落).

Kim Nŭngin - Once, when Okeh went to P'yŏngyang, we made 800 wŏn in one night. We hadn't imagined it would be that good either.

Wang P'yŏng - If you hit it right, making four or five hundred wŏn a night isn't difficult. In P'yŏngyang, I once led a troupe and earned 550 wŏn on the first day.

Kim - How about Taihei?

Min Hyosik - We haven't actually done performance tours.

Yu Yŏngguk - Ask the Victor literary department head about that. I don't know much about those aspects.

Yi Hayun - For Columbia too, I haven't been with the company for many days yet, so I don't know well, but I suppose it was generally like that.

Kim - Generally, if a record sells well, how many copies does it sell? Please disclose the details for each record company.

Yi Hayun - Well, it differs by company, but for our Columbia, Kang Hongsik's "Ch'ŏnyŏ Ch'onggak" (處女總角 - Maiden and Bachelor) <135> sold over thirty thousand copies. And anything sung by Ch'ae Kyuyŏp never sells less than eight thousand copies.

Kim Nŭngin - The standard for Okeh is similar.

Wang P'yŏng - It's roughly comparable.

Kim - If it sells, most of them probably go in the first month?

Pak Yŏngho - About sixty or seventy percent sell in the first month. That's why the first month is important. It's a kind of barometer.

Kim - What are the most recent best-sellers for each company?

Wang P'yŏng - With various singers involved, it's awkward for us sitting here to say whose record it is.

**연출 중 로맨스와 러부레타-** (Romance and Love Letters During Performances)

Kim - Tell us about any romances you encountered while touring the country for performances. Please also disclose things like love letters. They say actresses like Dietrich or Greta Garbo receive two thousand letters a day.

Wang Supok - When I return from local performances, I invariably receive several letters from people I don't know. Some send encouraging letters saying, "Ms. Supok, please go study music in Milan, Italy, and become a world-class vocalist," but from some others, well, strange and bizarre... I don't know.

Kim - Were they letters asking for your love?

Wang - That's right. Saying they'll live or die for me. And then there was this incident. Sometime ago, when an article about my visit appeared in "Samch'ŏlli," it mentioned I lived next to a certain lithograph printing shop at P'yŏngyang Ch'aegwan-ri. After that, letters started arriving addressed simply to "So-and-so, next to the lithograph printing shop," without a specific house number. The postman always teased me. Another funny thing is, several unfamiliar men would come to that lithograph printing shop every day asking, "They say Wang Supok lives in this alley, where is it?" The owner of the lithograph shop would say, "Thanks to you, our print shop gets good advertising, but honestly, <136> it's too bothersome, I can't stand it." Ho ho ho.

Kim - When you receive such letters of affection (求愛), do you reply?

Wang - How can I reply to every single one? Among them, I do reply to the kind elders who genuinely care for me and critique my songs.

Sŏnu Ilsŏn - It's similar for me as for Supok ŏnni. About five or six arrive each day. Among them are nice letters, unpleasant ones, and some asking me to send an autographed photo.

Kim - Do you send one every time?

Ilsŏn - I send them for very earnest letters, and for letters from innocent peers like female students, I don't mind writing replies.

Kim Nŭngin - Besides those sent directly to the singers, many are sent to the company. Hundreds come in a month, and popular singers routinely receive five or six letters daily. Seeing those makes you feel the warm affection of the record fans.

Ko Poksu - Letters criticizing are one thing, but I once had this experience. I went to Yongjŏng (龍井) in Kando for a music performance, and at the theater, I sang "T'ahyangsari" (타향사리 - Life in a Foreign Land), a song I'm most confident in. The whole audience welcomed it warmly, so I felt grateful. When I returned and sat down at the inn, a young, beautiful woman I didn't know came looking for me. Surprised, I asked what was wrong, and she just said "Teacher" and collapsed onto the floor, bursting into tears, sobbing profusely. I was stunned.
So, I managed to calm her down and wipe her tears, and heard her story. She was a woman from somewhere in Kyŏngsang Province who had followed some man and drifted all the way to Yongjŏng in North Kando. That day, she happened to go to the theater and heard the sad song "T'ahyangsari," which resonated so deeply with her own feelings of drifting like rootless duckweed, that she came looking for me without thinking.

Kim Yonghwan - Found a beauty in a foreign land— (Everyone laughs)

Ko Poksu - No, it's not something to laugh off like that. So I painstakingly reasoned with her and finally sent her away. She stood in the yard for a while, looking up at the half-moon in the sky. <137> She seemed like a woman with considerable education—

Kim Yonghwan - Things like that happen sometimes. I won't mention the exact place, but after performing in a certain city, I returned to the inn that night and received a visit from a woman. She said she came because she was moved by the song. Ha ha ha.

Wang P'yŏng - I had a terrifying experience once. I went to Yŏngil (延吉) in Kando with Wang Supok, Sŏnu Ilsŏn, Kim Yonghwan, and others for a performance tour. That night, there were rumors that bandits (馬賊) were attacking, and I was truly scared. The crowds that had filled the theater got scared by the gunshots and fled. I barely managed to find my way back to the inn, only to find the others missing. I searched the terrifying streets, sweating, and thankfully found them. But that night, the bandit gang ended up attacking the Japanese inn where we were staying. We also grabbed guns, fought them off (接戰), and made them retreat (退却).

Kim Yonghwan - Hey— wasn't there also that life-or-death (死生決斷) drama when we were leaving Kukcha and coming out of Yongjŏng?

Wang P'yŏng - There are so many stories like that to tell. When we were leaving then, traveling by car at night over the Changt'ae pass, suddenly on a deserted, lonely road, two strong men holding clubs jumped out and stopped the car. They were members of the XX Party. I was about to faint (氣絶), but seeing Ms. Ilsŏn and Ms. Wang Supok hiding under blankets, holding their breath, was truly heart-wrenching. Their faces were ashen. Everyone thought we were surely dead now. They raised their revolvers, fired terrifying shots, and demanded money. It was truly desperate.

Kim - And then?

Wang - Just then, luckily, another car with its headlights on came speeding up from behind. Seeing our car stopped, they must have thought something was strange, as they started shouting "Whoa, whoa!" from the rear car. So, the XX Party members who had boarded our car quickly hid again in the pitch-dark grassy field. We were truly lucky to survive. My bones were rattling then.

Kim Nŭngin - That was almost a huge disaster. I don't have <138> such a thrilling episode, but when I went to Cheju Island to collect folk songs, the scene of the Cheju haenyŏ (海女 - female divers) singing and gathering mussels by the sea with camellia flowers floating on the water was truly beautiful. I wanted to capture it on a movie camera.
And when our boat was leaving the harbor, unfamiliar island girls waved their red headscarves until the boat was out of sight, bidding us farewell, which brought tears to my eyes. Because such local sentiment (鄕土情調) flows there, that island has produced so many beautiful songs.

Kim - Instead of just circling around Korea for local performances, don't you want to visit fellow Koreans in places like Hawai'i or America?

Wang - I want to go. Because it's such a distant place, the cost is high, but if a Korean organization there invited us, I think the expenses could somehow be covered. I want to sing looking up at the palm trees (椰子樹) on an island in that southern tropical region (熱帶地方). Like Dietrich in the movie "Morocco," I want to walk barefoot chasing across the desert.

Pak Yŏngho - Me too. Such a romantic impulse always flows in a corner of my heart.

Min Hyosik - If there's an opportunity to do so, it's something I'd like to try. Aren't there many places within Korea too that I'd like to visit? Singing under the bright moonlight (月明夜).

**노래잘하고 作曲잘하는 숨은 天才를 차즐길 업슬가** (Isn't There a Way to Find Hidden Geniuses Who Sing and Compose Well?)

Kim Tonghwan - Isn't there some way to find hidden artists who sing well and compose well?

Wang P'yŏng - Many people from the countryside come directly to the company saying, "I sing really well, please hire me." But when we listen, they're all unsuitable. Then again, those who just graduated from Tokyo Music School (東京音樂學校) might be good at Western music, but they are completely wrong for Korean music.

Yi Hayun - It's a big problem. No matter how good a singer is, you can only utilize one characteristic feature from that singer. We need new singers with new characteristics to emerge, <139> but the self-proclaimed talents (自請客) who jump in saying they are good are mostly unremarkable. So, naturally, our eyes turn towards the actors (俳優) performing in theater troupes.

Min Hyosik - There really aren't any suitable new people. So, instead, we sometimes ask friends in the countryside to send any famous singers from their area to Seoul, and our company has had some success that way.

Kim Tonghwan - What is the typical income for a singer?

Kim Nŭngin - The highest is 300 wŏn, and there's no lower limit.

Kim Tonghwan - Is it usually around 50 or 60 wŏn?

Pak Yŏngho - It's probably around that level. If they are reasonably competent, their livelihood is guaranteed.

**流行歌를 向上식힐 길** (Ways to Improve Popular Songs)

Kim Tonghwan - There's a tendency in some households to ban records from coming in, citing that the popular songs flowing through the streets today are too vulgar (although this might be just a portion), and criticism is sometimes heard from general society as well. Isn't there a way to improve them?

Pak Yŏngho - I don't really understand what society means by "high-class, high-class." When we tried releasing sheet music (譜版) with compositions by, say, Mr. An Kiyŏng (安基永), calling it high-class, the sales results were poor. So, inevitably, we have to choose songs that will sell in the general market.

Kim Tonghwan - How about reviving traditional folk songs (民謠)?

Yi Hayun - Efforts in that direction could pioneer a new path. However, if we talk about "Tambaekku T'aryŏng" (담바꾸타령 - Tobacco Ballad), it shouldn't be the old, transmitted "Tambaekku T'aryŏng" as is, but rather new works incorporating contemporary consciousness would be needed, I believe.

Kim Tonghwan - How about utilizing the Yi Royal Music Institute Orchestra (李王職雅樂隊)? <140>

Yu Yŏngguk - Our Victor company recorded them, but more British and American people buy them than Koreans. Just the other day, an Englishman staying at the Chosŏn Hotel saw a sample, so I brought it over. He said it was really good and bought five or six discs. But if we target Koreans, using the Royal Music Institute Orchestra would likely be a commercial failure.

Wang P'yŏng - Ultimately, the public's knowledge level needs to rise.

Min Hyosik - I agree.

Kim Tonghwan - Thank you for talking with us for such a long time. (End) <141>

**Summary**

This roundtable discussion from 1936 features prominent Korean singers and record company executives discussing the popular music scene. They cover the hardships of recording (lack of studios in Korea, travel to Tokyo, performance anxiety, multiple takes), the varying reception during performance tours across Korea and Manchuria (regional preferences, enthusiastic vs. reserved audiences, dangers like bandits), fan interactions (love letters, emotional responses to songs), the search for new talent (difficulty finding suitable singers/composers), singer income, record sales figures (best-sellers reaching 30,000 copies), and the ongoing debate about improving the quality of popular songs versus market demands. From a colonial history perspective, the article vividly illustrates the dependence on Japanese infrastructure (Tokyo recording studios), the cultural dynamics within the empire (mention of Japanese artists, tours in Japanese-controlled Manchuria), the specific challenges faced by Korean artists (travel difficulties, safety concerns in border regions), and the burgeoning mass culture centered around records and popular music in 1930s Korea.

吹込할 때의 苦心談

金東煥-(本社主幹) 오늘저녁 여러분을 모시고 지금 굉장한 세력을 가지고 잇는 류행가에 대하여 말슴드를 긔회를 어덧스니 감사함니다. 아마 이와 가치 장안 륙대회사의 가수 제씨와, 문예부 간부 전부가 한자리에 모여보게 된 것은 근래에 업는 일로, 이 자리면 모든 문제를 충분히 이약이 할 수 잇슬 줄 암니다.
내가 총독부 당국에 가서 조사한 바에 의하면 지금 조선서 일년동안에 팔려가는 레코-드 장수는 약 일백오십만장으로 그 중에 삼분에 일인 사오십만매가 조선소리판이라 함니다. 사오십만명의 대중을 상대로하는 레코-드는 실로 우리 사회문화 우에 크다란 영향을 던지고 잇는 줄 암니다.
그만치 십삼도 방방곡곡에 흐터저 잇는 레코-드팬들은 가수 여러분과 문예부장 여러분의 말슴을 듯고저 원할 터이니, 조곰도 숨기지 말고 조흔 말슴, 우수운 말슴, 놀라운 말슴을 만히 들녀주세요. 몬저 가수 여러분께서 취입할 때에 계란이나 냉수를 잡스심니까.
王壽福-(포리돌 여가수)-글세요. 갓쯔다로-(勝太郞-島の娘의)가 언제 이약이 하는 것을 드르니 계란을 먹으면 목소리가 잘 나온다고 하든데요-그러치만 저는 그러치안어요. 그날 낫에 취입이 잇다면 아츰밥을 조곰치 먹고 냉수로 목을 축이고 그러고 마이크로폰 압헤 서지요. 잇지마루(市丸) 가튼 녀자는 술 두어잔 마시면 긔분이 도연(浩然)하여저서 소리가 잘 나온다고 하지만은 그러치만 저는 그러지는 안어요,
金-평상시에도 조흔 목소리내고 십허 고심하지 안어요?
王-그야 직업이 직업이니깐요. 길가다도 무의식적으로 전봇대 가튼데 「美聲藥」이라 하는 목소리<130> 잘 내게 하는 매약(賣藥)이 잇다 하면 불이나케 약국으로 쪼처가 사다간 먹고먹고 하지요.
金-취입은 대개 대번에 되어요? 연습하고 난 뒤 마이크로폰 압헤 서면 다시 두 번 하지안어요?
王-웨 안할까요. 제 마음에 아니마즈면 또 문예부장이 다시 하라면 두 번 세 번 다섯 번 여섯 번도 하지요. 동경서 보니까 갓즈다료-도 취입할 때에 오륙차 하드군요.
鮮于一扇-(포리돌 여가수)-갓즈다료-뿐인가요. 쇼-지다로(東海林太郞)도 그때 여름철에 취입하는 구경을 하엿는데 네 번 다섯 번 곤처하드군요.(사진은 왕수복씨 취입광경)
金-일선씨는 취입하기 전 엇더케 하세요.
鮮于一扇-저는요- 래일가치 취입이 잇스면 오늘 저녁부터 밥을 안먹어요. 그래서 배가 곱하진대로 마이크로폰 압헤 서면 정신이 한 곳에 집중되고 또 목소리가 청청하게 나와요.
王平-(포리돌 문예부장)-두 분이 취입하는 모양을 내가 고백하지요. 一扇氏는 처음 노래를 시작할 때는 몸을 단정히 가지고 잇지만 마즈막쯤 가면 점점점 허리가 꾸부러 내종엔 마이크로폰 압헤 코를 대일지경으로 밧작 달나붓지요.<131>(一同笑) 또 壽福씨는 처음은 단정이젓다가 한 곡조 두 곡조 흐르는 사이에 차츰차츰 한쪽에 기우려 거이 너머갈 지경이지요. 모도다 그때는 제정신이 업는 모양이드군요.
王壽福-그러다가두 그러케 고심하여 불어너흔 것을 겻방에 잇든 선생님이 화색이 업시 달러오면 「앗불사 이 취입이 또 틀닌게로구나」하는 생각이 나며 그양 마이크로폰을 안고 울고십허요.
金-한 가지 노래를 취입하기 전에 대개 몃 씩 연습하서요.
鮮于一扇-작곡이 되어 내 손에 온뒤면 길가다도 무심이 외오게 되고 집에 안저 손님허고 이약이하다가도 외이게 되고-늘 정신이 거기가니까 열흘동안 대개 백차쯤은 외워보는걸요.
王平-밤낫 융융거리지요. 잘 팔녀달나고 호호호.
金-백타! 당상히 고심하는군요!
江南香-(오-케 여가수) 하로 열 번 잡고 열흘에 백 번은 웨이는 셈이 되지요.
金陵人-(오-케 문예부장) 대개 가사는 따로 오이고 曲부터 몬저 알어야 하게 되지요.
金- 江南香씨는 취입할 때 무얼 자세요.
江南香-벌로 먹는 것은 업스나 잠을 잘자고 유쾌한 긔분을 어드려고 애써요.
高福壽-(오-케 남가수) 나도 먹는 약은 벌로 업스나 밥 먹은 후 약 한 시간쯤은 공원으로 산보하든지하여 잘 소화를 한 후 유쾌한 긔분으로 취입에 나가지요. 혹 우유를 먹기도 하지요.
金仙草-(시에론 여가수) 저두 그래요. 취입하기 전 너무 음식을 먹으면 목소리가 자긔 목소리갓지 안케 변해버려저요.
金龍煥-(포리돌 남가수)-나는 공복이 조터구만. 시장한 개가 꽁을 잡는다고 하하하.
李銀波-(오-케 여가수)-홍자(紅茶) 한 잔쯤 먹는 것도 조와요.
金海松-(오-케 남가수)-음식이니 약이니 하기보담 대체로 긔분에 달렷스니까요. 어느 예술가든지 다 그러켓지만도.
異河潤-대체로 조선에 조흔 취입소가 업는 것이 큰 탈임니다. 가령 서울서 연습할 때는 컨듸슌이<132> 조와서 노래가 잘 나오든 사람들이 동경만 가면 여러날 선차(船車)에 치이고 난 뒤인지라 감긔들지요. 김치 깍둑이를 못먹고 그 짠음식을 먹게되니까 성대(聲帶)가 변하여지기 쉽지요. 정말 취입소 업는 것이 엇더케 고통인지 모르지요(사진은 그날밤 모엇든 이의 서명집)
朴英鎬-그러치요. 그것이 큰 문제지요. 조선에 취입소 잇게 되면 맘대로 련습도 하고 악대(樂隊)도 마음대로 쓸 수 잇스련만은.
閔孝植-더구나 고통은 서울서 동경까지 가는 사이에 녀가수 제씨들은 녀자만이 잇는 생리상관게(生理上關係) 잇는 날이나 당하면 취입을 몃츨 연긔하게까지 되니까요.
金陵人-그뿐인가요. 별느다가 동경 가는 관게로 하로에 칠팔매씩 너케되니까 더 어럽지요.

演奏旅行하면 어느 곳이 나은가<133>

金東煥-여러분은 거리의 꾀꼬리로 몃 해를 두고 조선팔도 방방곡곡을 삿삿치 도라다녓슬 터이니 여러지방 중 어디가 특별히 유행가를 잘 리해하고 또 흥행성적이 조왓서요.
王平-울능도, 제주도, 두 곳만 빼어노코 남북 삼천리를 가보지 못한 곳이 업는데 우리 포리돌 회사의 경험으로 보면 함경도지방(咸鏡道地方)이 조터군요. 그 다음이 경상도(慶尙道)라 할가요.
金龍煥-스테지에 올나가서 노래부르면 앙콜 두 세 번 쯤 밧지안코는 청중이 열광하는 것 갓지 안는데 함흥(咸興) 갓슬 때 보니 원악 함경도 사람들이 서반아(西班牙) 사람 모양으로 격정적(激情的)이 되어 그런지요. 정말 진정으로 환호(歡呼)해 주어서 군중과 연출자가 그만 의긔투합(意氣投合)이 되어요.
柳榮國-(빅타-會社販賣部長)-그런 점에서 평양도 조와요. 마음에 들기만 하면 그양 끌어안을드시 열열히 환영해 주니까요.
金陵人-그러치요. 우리 오-케 순회연주의 *적으로 보아 평양이 조코 그리고는 또 宣川이 조와요. 크리스찬이 사는 읍이라 하지만 음악은 퍽 만히 리해하여 주어요.
高福壽-내가 보기에는 함경도 淸津, 城津도 조와요. 멀니서 흥행하는 패들이 적어서 그런 관게인 지 물나도.
金-濟州島 섬으로 가보섯다더니 거기는 엇대요.
金陵人-오-케-회사로서 단체로 간 것이 아니고 나 혼자 민요채집하려 간 것인데 鄕土藝術을 만히 가진 이러한 남쪽 고을로 갈 수만 잇다면 갓스면 조흐련만 넘우 비용이 엄청나게 드니까요.
