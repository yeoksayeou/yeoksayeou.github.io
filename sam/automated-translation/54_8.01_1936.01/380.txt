Article Title: 契約 (Contract)
Author: 張赫宙
Article Type: 소설 (Novel/Fiction)

契約
						張赫宙
Author Sketch

Since debuting in the Tokyo literary world in Showa 6 (1931) with the piece "餓飢道" (Path of Starvation), he has continuously published several short stories such as "갈보" (Prostitute), "權이란 作者" (The Author Named Kwon), and "十六夜月" (Sixteen-Day-Old Moon), receiving very favorable reviews. Afterwards, he published two full-length novels, "무지개" (Rainbow) and "三曲線" (Three Curves), in the Tonga Ilbo, and several short stories including "南風" (South Wind) in the Chosŏn Ilbo. His solid realistic style has always been a focus of attention in the literary world. Regarding his education, he was an elementary school teacher for many years in a village in his hometown of Taegu. He is thirty-four years old. His stand-alone books include "餓飢道" and "權と云ふ男" (The Man Called Kwon).

Whenever I think of him, my insides twist and I feel disgusted. His pinched, shriveled face had deep wrinkles, and perhaps because he was old and shrunken, he didn't look that small from afar, but when he came near and stood beside me, his frame was smaller than that of a ten-year-old child. I cursed him, calling him an evil-demon-like fellow, but truly, his impression seems most fittingly described as a small demon (小鬼).

I have forgotten his name, so I cannot call him by name as I tell this story now. No, never mind his name, I hate even thinking about him, who seemed filled to the brim with cunning schemes and fault-finding, so much that it makes me shudder. I wish somehow that his existence would completely disappear from my mind. Especially when I had repeated dealings with him regarding the incident I will relate <254> next, just thinking of him made me lose my appetite.

To be fair, I was also at fault. Therefore, I was even prepared to pay a considerable fine. However, when I realized he was trying to profit excessively by taking advantage of my mistake, I was truly dumbfounded, mouth agape, just staring at his face.

It was late summer this very year. I was looking around various places for a suitable plot of land, intending to build a new house in a slightly quieter location. The house I had lived in until now was right by the roadside, so during the day, there were the sounds of people and carts. Not only that, but occasionally, cow or horse carts would crash noisily into the fence, causing a commotion. Every time this happened, I was startled and inevitably had to argue with the drivers or carters, which frayed my nerves immensely. On top of that, at night, drunkards and brawlers would invariably choose the spot under my fence to fight noisily, making sleep impossible.

Therefore, for someone like me who constantly uses their head, I simply could not bear living in that house any longer. I resolved to move elsewhere by this autumn without fail, and since I was looking for a new place anyway, I decided to build a new house according to my own preferred design. I arranged for a carpenter and selected a site. Someone promised to help with the slightly insufficient funds, so I was determined to finally realize my long-held hope.

After struggling for a good month looking for a site, I realized that building a house was no small matter either. After negotiating in various places, I finally decided to settle quickly on any suitable spot, and the one I chose happened to be that blasted piece of land. Because I contracted to buy this site, I cannot tell you how much pointless mental energy and expense I incurred.

Let me omit the other stories here. I intend to speak only of the man I am currently discussing, so the stories of the former county magistrate (前郡守) named Yi Mo (李某), who threatened me, saying he would block the passage or obstruct me to prevent me from building a house there, and the Kim family members who acted slyly under him, I will tell on another occasion. In the end, I decided to return that land and choose a different site. If it had ended there, it wouldn't have been much of a worry, but amidst the many disputes over the land, the carpenter, without asking me, transported the lumber for the house and, intending to set the foundation stones, pulled out the eggplants (茄子) planted on that land. The site was a field belonging to some Westerner, and it was <255> being divided and sold as residential lots. When I was about to build there, Mr. K, who worked as an instructor at the normal school, had just completed his new house and moved in; the remaining land still had eggplants growing on it. Between that field and the road, there was a narrow strip of rice paddy left like a belt, and unless one bought that paddy, there would ultimately be no access road. Realizing this beforehand, the former county magistrate, Yi Mo, a very imposing figure, bought the paddy cheaply from the owner and was scheming every trick to sell it to us expensively, so I had to go there frequently myself to negotiate.

I didn't consider the eggplants planted in the field much of an issue. The normal school instructor who had just built his house also said,
"What? The eggplants? Just pull them out and build. These eggplants? The person living right over there is tenant farming it. Just pay him something for the value of the eggplants, and that's it. It's already close to the first frost, isn't it almost time to pick them anyway?"
He encouraged me to hurry up and build so we could live as neighbors. And regarding the access road issue, he said that if Yi Mo refused, we would take it to court and secure the road no matter what.

However, wanting to handle things smoothly before complicating the matter, I negotiated with Yi Mo a couple of times. But it didn't seem likely to work out well, and just when I found a good plot of land elsewhere and went to inform the carpenter, I was told that the carpenter had transported the lumber a couple of hours earlier and pulled out the eggplants in the field. Wondering what had happened, I rushed over there, and indeed, the carpenter and the man farming the field were in the middle of a heated argument. The carpenter was saying, "What? Let's just hurry up and build the house. Surely they won't refuse to give us access?" I told them I had decided on another site and stopped the carpenters from pulling out more eggplants.

At that moment, the shortish man who had been arguing furiously with the carpenter, his face pale with anger, walked towards me, smiling obsequiously.
Since I had exchanged greetings with him a couple of times before, I said courteously, taking off my hat,
"Ah, this is very regrettable. We pulled out the eggplants like this without getting your permission, the owner of the eggplants."
"What does it matter? I'm sure you, sir, thought it through before ordering it."
He continued smiling obsequiously,
"However, the carpenter insisted that since you, sir, bought this field, <256> why should the eggplants be mine? So I was just arguing that if you bought the field, you bought the land, did you buy the eggplants too?"
He said this, putting his hand in and out of the pocket of his shabby, worn-out overcoat.
"Of course, that can't be right. The eggplants are surely yours, so I will compensate for the damages, don't worry. However, I apologize for pulling out someone else's property arbitrarily."
I did not say that I wouldn't be building the house here. I had a fleeting thought: if I mentioned that, might this man demand an excessive amount? Or if he retorted, "Why did you pull out someone else's eggplants if you're not even building a house?", wouldn't the carpenter have no answer? I wasn't unaware that this thought was a sly calculation (간사한 꾀) on my part, but although he responded to me with a smile, seemingly good-natured, I instantly sensed that the man was no ordinary troublemaker.

However, I thought, since about 20 or 30 *p'yŏng* of the eggplant patch was pulled out, it would amount to perhaps just over a hundred stalks in total. Since the eggplants would have been picked and sold anyway, calculating only for the slightly less mature ones, paying maybe 2 or 3 *chon* per stalk should suffice.

I sent the carpenter away, telling him to stay behind, and said to the farmer,
"Could you please count the number of eggplant stalks? What? Whether I supervise or not, I trust you'll do it properly. I completely trust you."
And I apologized to him courteously again.

The next day, thinking I should pay him quickly, I first told the carpenters that I had decided on a different site, so they should bring back the lumber. However, the workers returned empty-handed, saying that when they went to get the lumber, the eggplant owner said they couldn't take it until the eggplant compensation was paid.

Hearing this, I felt a bit offended, but I also thought it was somewhat understandable. So, I decided to go myself, pay the compensation, and retrieve the lumber. A little after noon, I went looking for his house. His house was what you might call a shack (토막), built on a small leftover piece of land, barely two *p'yŏng*, right at the foot of a mountain. The roof was covered with gathered scraps of sheet metal, and the frame was roughly tied together with rafters. It was a two-room hut, one room and one kitchen, yet it even had a small yard and the semblance of a narrow wooden veranda.

I felt a sense of pity. He too must be destitute; how difficult his life must be. Thinking this, I considered that even if I paid an extra 1 or 2 *chon* per stalk, it wouldn't amount to a huge sum. Someone might criticize such thoughts as mere humanitarianism or pity, but anyway, that's what I thought as I stepped into the yard <257> and called for the owner, but no one was there. I went out onto the path and stood looking down at the water flowing in the ditch between the rice paddies. Someone greeted me. Looking up, it was a woman washing something by the ditch. She was the eggplant owner's wife. I greeted her gladly, "Ah! I just went to your house, but no one was there. Where did the master of the house go?" Compared to the man, who was shabby and decrepitly old, the woman was much younger and had a pretty face.

The woman said he had just gone out to work and she would send him to my house in the evening. She apologized for making me take the trouble to come. I mentioned the workers who had come for the lumber and expressed my apologies again, asking her to convey my regards to her husband when he returned. Then I went back home.

That night, as I was waiting, the man came. When I invited him up, his expression turned unpleasant, and he said it was fine here, sitting down on the edge of the veranda. I thought, "Ah, this negotiation is going to be difficult." I urged him to come straight into the room, offering him a cushion. He hesitated as if entering a forbidden place but eventually came into the room. However, I frowned as I saw clumps of dirt falling from his bare feet onto the floor and noticed him carelessly spilling tobacco shreds and ash nearby while continuously filling his pipe.

"Let's not dwell on apologies... So, how many stalks were there?"
I spoke coldly, slightly annoyed that he was holding the lumber hostage for the eggplant payment.
"Well, how many stalks would there be? Please calculate it as you see fit, sir."
He also sat with a stiff, pinched expression. Sensing his unyielding attitude, an uneasy feeling crept up within me about how much he would demand.
"How can I know? Shouldn't you know? If you say it's a thousand stalks, I'll take your word for it, and if you say it's a hundred, I'll proceed accordingly..."
"Well, we can find out by counting, can't we?"
"So, how many stalks was it?"
"You can come and count them tomorrow if you like."
"Look, didn't I ask you to count them? How many stalks were there? It's not something to make so difficult."
"..."
Even when I asked somewhat impatiently, he sat calmly, without batting an eyelid, <258> seemingly assessing the situation. Growing increasingly frustrated, I pressed,
"Hey, I asked how many stalks, why are you just sitting there? Shall we go and count them now?"
"Well, we did count them. But..."
"If you counted, tell me how many stalks."
"Altogether..."
He paused again for a moment before speaking.
"Altogether there are 422 stalks..."
"400?"
I was shocked.
"Yes. Never mind the value of the twenty-odd stalks, just pay for 400 stalks."
"Hmph."
I folded my arms. Four hundred stalks, four hundred stalks, I muttered under my breath. I wanted to take him straight to the field and count the stalks, but I had seen him carefully tying up the pulled stalks that day, so I didn't think he was likely lying. He seemed like a simple laborer, so how could I suspect him? Taking him to the field or meticulously counting each stalk felt crass, like distrusting him, and it was also bothersome.
"Alright, let's make it 400 stalks. If it's 400 stalks, how much should I compensate per stalk?"
I yielded. To be honest, losing even 10 or 20 *won* for nothing wasn't pleasant for someone like me who wasn't wealthy, but I wanted to pay whatever amount quickly and forget the whole incident.
"Why aren't you answering quickly? I'm also suffering an unnecessary loss here. For you, it means you would have picked and sold them anyway, so just sell what you can, and claim damages only for the ones that hadn't fully matured, perhaps a few per stalk. If you make excessive demands beyond that or try to satisfy your greed, it will be unpleasant for both of us. And through this connection, perhaps later, or rather, soon, since I will be building a house, and you are also a laborer, you could come and work for me."
I said this, disliking his frightfully frowning face and trying to appease him, wondering how much he would ask for.

However, he remained utterly unmoved by my persuasive talk. I had expected him to readily agree, saying something like, "Yes, sir. Please just give me about one *chon* per stalk. And I'll come work at your place, so please just pay me fair wages." <259>

Instead, he said,
"Sir. Truly, raising those eggplants, I went through hellish hardships. This summer, did it even rain properly?"
"Ah, the weather was dry, wasn't it."
I agreed, trying to match his sentiment and humor him.
"On top of that, buying the seeds, that cost a total of 20 *won*."
"For the entire field, you mean?"
I asked, surprised, wondering if he meant that was the amount I should pay.
"Yes. And, probably, to raise one stalk, I must have carried water more than twenty times. Is that all? When the sun was hot, I had to make little hats to cover them, right? If bugs appeared, I had to catch them, right?"
He recounted in detail the hardships of growing the eggplants. Listening to him, my soft heart began to think I should probably pay him as much as 10 *won* per stalk.
"Is there any need to say all that? Just tell me, how much should I pay per stalk?"
I asked, picking up the abacus, wanting to quickly calculate and settle the matter.
"Hold on. I'm just saying this because we're talking about it. I'm not trying to make you pay for all that, sir."
He finally cracked a slight smile, wrinkles forming on his face, and glanced at me sharply with his white eyes.
"However, if we just waited one more month, I could get over 1 *chon* for each eggplant."
"1 *chon* per eggplant!"
"Yes. I sell them to Japanese people from the mainland (일본 내지사람들) for pickling. And there are dozens more still forming on each plant. Honestly, if we had just waited one month, we wouldn't have had to have this unpleasant conversation. What a shame."
He said this, again packing tobacco into his pipe, spilling shreds carelessly.

I was dumbfounded. "This fellow is way beyond me. I tried to deceive him, but he's turning the tables on me," I thought.
"Alright, anyway, how much do you want me to pay? Just name your price."
"Heh. Will you give me everything I ask for? It has to be a fair amount." <260>
"Alright, just name a price."
"Oh, it's hard to say. To someone as gentle as you, sir..."
"..."
"Sir. Let's not mince words. Right now, there's an average of ten eggplants per stalk, so just pay 10 *chon* per stalk. I've already demanded 15 *chon* from the normal school teacher."
"10 *chon*? Heh!"
I was truly astonished. I was speechless for a moment. Ten *chon* per stalk without even giving me a single eggplant. I thought, "This guy must think he's hit the jackpot, exploiting the weakness that we pulled out the eggplants without the owner's consent, and he's coming at me aggressively." That old, shriveled man looked utterly disgusting and hateful, like an evil demon. How could anyone look so wicked? Was it his poverty that made him this way?

"Look."
However, I opened my mouth.
"Let's assume what you say is true for argument's sake. But I doubt there were even five eggplants per stalk, let alone ten. And you can sell the large ones, can't you? So, let's settle for 3 *chon* per stalk. Three times four hundred is twelve. I'll give you 12 *won*..."
"3 *chon*? Heh! Do as you please. If you don't give me a single penny, how can I accept it? Give it if you will, or do whatever you want."
He sneered like this for a while, then abruptly left.

I was completely exasperated. I thought resolving this matter would be no easy task. The next day, I asked several people. They all replied that paying around 5 *won* should be sufficient. When I mentioned I had offered 12 *won*, everyone said I was being too soft-hearted. My family also scolded me, "Giving away 12 *won* for nothing!" But I couldn't stand letting it be. I wanted to resolve it quickly. I even considered going to find him and suggesting we count the eggplants together, but I hated the idea.

I met him once more. I told him that public opinion suggested 5 *won* was enough, so wasn't 12 *won* sufficient? "You should take a bit of a loss too, and I'm losing 12 *won* for nothing, so please consider that," I pleaded, almost begging.
But he replied, "Well, sir. Am I asking for an unreasonable amount? Raising these eggplants..." and again launched into a long story about his efforts, adding, "These eggplants have already shriveled like this, so I can't even sell them," showing me a few withered <261> ones. He insisted he would only accept 10 *chon* per stalk and wouldn't take a penny less.

At that, I finally lost my temper. "If that's how you want it, then I won't pay a penny more than 12 *won* either! If you have a way to collect it, go ahead and try!" I yelled.
Therefore, I ended up unable to retrieve my nearly one hundred *won* worth of tools, planes, and dozens of pieces of wood from him. I was furious and resentful that he was holding onto hundreds of *won* worth of my property over a mere 10-odd *won*. I thought about how I could knock him down with one strong punch, smashing his skull – I entertained all sorts of malicious thoughts in my head.

But the face and eyes of that emaciated man, who looked like he could disappear any moment, appeared before my eyes like a great evil demon. His small frame, his pinched, shriveled face, and his sharp, envious eyes reminded me of a small demon (小鬼) spreading plagues and disasters from house to house.

Enduring criticism from my family, I compromised and offered 15 *won*. But he still refused to listen.
"That rascal must be trying to make a fortune for life by seizing this one chance," my mother cursed.
However, changing his mind was no easy task. I, too, was unwilling to pay any more than that.

About two weeks passed like this. The lumber became unusable and had to be returned to the lumberyard. The lumberyard sent a bill for damages. I grew angrier and angrier. At that time, I also fell ill and was bedridden for about a week.

News reached me from somewhere that the eggplant fellow was going around threatening to have me arrested by the police, beaten severely to extract the money, or set fire to my house. Hearing this while my mind was weakened by illness, my anger flared up intensely. Therefore, I sent my servant to tell him, "Go ahead and have me locked up by the police if you can! Now I won't give you 15 *chon*, let alone 15 *won*. And because you held onto the lumber, I have to pay damages to the lumberyard, so I won't pay you even if I have to go to court!" However, my mind was never at ease.

This time, it seemed to have an effect, as he came to see me immediately.
"Oh my, sir, if I had known you were unwell, I would have come to visit sooner."
He said, smiling obsequiously.
I found that obsequious smile terrifying. But <262> I also spoke kindly, asking if he would accept just 12 *won*.
He again showed anger, sat hunched over, and demanded more. At his words, I abruptly sat up in my sickbed and challenged him, "Lock me up in the police station? Did you?" I confronted him point-by-point about the rumors he had been spreading. He vehemently denied ever saying such things, claiming, "It was that carpenter rascal who pulled out the eggplants, did you pull them out, sir? That's why I cursed the carpenter like that." Then, instantly smiling obsequiously, he said,
"Sir, please don't be like that. Just give me 15 *won*. Instead, think of it as saving my life, and please write me a contract stating I received 10 *chon* per stalk."
he said.

Unexpectedly pleased that he was compromising, I replied,
"That's not difficult. Alright, let's write the contract."
And I immediately took out the money and gave it to him. He counted the bills, and as I was about to write the receipt, he said, looking at my brush,
"Actually, please write 40 *won* on the receipt too."
I thought that wasn't particularly difficult either and wrote 40 *won*. In reality, I paid 15 *won* but wrote a receipt for 40 *won*, so there was no harm to me.
After writing the receipt, I asked if I should also write the certificate as promised, stating "Payment of 10 *chon* per stalk," but he tilted his head and said, "What? It's enough if you just keep this, sir. If anyone comes asking, just show them this." He then left, seemingly satisfied.

The next day, I found out that the access road issue for the remaining part of the field had been resolved, and five or six houses were going to be built there.
I finally understood the reason why he wanted me to write the contract. He had done it out of greed, intending to demand 3 or 4 *won* per person for the eggplant compensation from the others.
I found his cunning scheme endlessly hateful.

A few weeks later, I heard that many of the people intending to build houses in that field had either paid compensation for the eggplants like me or had postponed their construction due to the eggplant issue.
Whenever I think of that man's tedious, clinging persistence, I still shudder. <263>

**Summary**

The narrator, seeking to build a house in a quieter location, has his carpenter mistakenly destroy eggplants belonging to a poor tenant farmer. A protracted and acrimonious negotiation over compensation follows. The farmer, described negatively by the narrator, demands what the narrator considers an exorbitant sum (40 *won* based on 10 *chon* per stalk), leveraging the narrator's mistake and holding his building materials hostage. Eventually, the narrator pays 15 *won* but agrees to sign a fraudulent receipt for 40 *won* and a certificate validating the 10 *chon*/stalk price, which the farmer intends to use to extort similar amounts from other future builders on the adjacent plots. From a historical perspective, the story vividly portrays everyday economic conflict, negotiation tactics (including extortion and manipulation), and social tensions possibly related to class differences in colonial Korea. The detailed account of the dispute, the specific monetary values debated, and the final deceptive "contract" offer historians insight into the micro-level realities and moral ambiguities faced by individuals navigating property and compensation issues during that period. The mention of specific crops (eggplants) and their market (pickling by Japanese residents) adds to the texture of the time.

						張赫宙
作家點描

昭和 6年에 「餓飢道」의 一編을 들고 東京文壇에 「데뷰-」한 以來 계속하야 「갈보」, 「權이란 作者」, 「十六夜月」 등의 여러 短篇을 發表하야 非常한 好評을 밧앗고 그 뒤 東亞日報에 「무지개」, 「三曲線」의 두 長篇과 朝鮮日報에 「南風」 등 數三의 短篇을 發表한 바 그 典實한 寫實主義的 作風은 文壇의 恒常 注目의 標가 되여왓다. 學歷은 故鄕 大邱 어느 村落에서 多年小學校敎員으로 잇섯스며 春秋는 三十四 單行本으로 「餓飢道」, 「權と云ふ男」가 잇다.

나는 그를 생각할 때면 그만 창수가 비트러지고 비위가 상해진다. 그 깍짝깍짝하니 생긴 얼골에는 굵은 주름이 잡히고 늙어 쪼그라저서 그런지 먼 대서 볼 때는 그러케 적게는 보이지 안으나, 곁에 와서 같이 서면 여남어살 먹은 아이보다도 몸피가 적엇다. 나는 그를 악귀(惡鬼)같이 생긴 작자라고 해서 욕을 햇지만 정말 그의 인상은 소귀(小鬼)라고 해야 가장 알마진 것 같다.
나는 그의 이름을 잊은지라 지금 여기서 그의 이름을 불으며 이야기하지는 못한다. 아니 나는 그의 이름은 커녕 그 머리끝까지 간악한 꾀와 트집으로 꽉 차진듯한 그를 생각하기조차 진저리가 처지듯이 싫다. 나는 어떻게 해서든지 그라는 존재가 영영 내 머리에서 사러지고 말기를 소원하고 잇다. 더욱이 내가 그와 다음에 <254> 이야기하는 사건으로 루차 교섭을 할 때에는 나는 그를 생각만 해도 입맛이 끊어질 지경이었다.
하긴 내 쪽에도 잘못이 잇섯다. 그러므로 나는 거기에 상당한 벌금을 내랴고까지 하엿든 것이다. 그러나 그가 내 실수를 기화로 분에 넘친 리익을 보려고 덤비는 것을 꺠달을 때는 정말이지 어한이 막히어 입을 벙벙하니 열은 채 그의 얼골만 바라볼 뿐이었다.
바로 금년 늦여름이었다. 나는 조금 종용한 곳에다가 주택을 하나 신축해 보려고 여러 곳 부지를 구하려 단이었다. 지금까지 살고 있는 집은 바로 길까에 있는 탓으로 낮에는 사람소리 구루마 소리, 그뿐인가 잘못하면 소나 말구루마가 와닥탕하고 판장에 부디치고 요란하게 소동을 일으킬 때마다 나는 놀라고 그런 일로 해서 마부나 구루마꾼들과 싸우지 아니하면 아니돼었으니 나의 신경은 말할 수 없이 불쾌해지는 것이었다. 거기다 밤이 되면 술쥐정하고 싸움싸우는 작자들은 하필 남의 담장 밑에 와서 와당탕 하고 싸우니 잠을 잘 수 없다.
그러므로 일상 머리를 많이 쓰는 나로는 도저히 이 집에서는 견디지 못하게 되었다. 나는 금년 가을까지는 단연코 다른 곳으로 이사를 하기로 작정하고 기왕 다른 집을 구할테면 내 마음에 맞는 설계로 새집을 지으리라는 결심으로 대목까지 정하고 부지를 골랏다. 조금 부족한 비용은 누가 보아주기로 약속이 되어 나는 년래의 희망을 기어히 실현키로 하였다.
부지를 골르느라고 한 달 장간이나 곤난을 격고 나니 집 짓는 것도 여간한 일이 아니라고 생각되었다. 여러 곳을 교섭하다가 나종에는 아모대라도 좋으니 속히 정해바리자고 정한다는 것이 하필 그놈엣 땅이었다. 나는 이 부지를 사기로 게약한 때문에 얼마나 헛된 머리를 색이고 헛 비용을 디렷는지 몰은다.
나는 여기 다른 이야기는 다 빼기로 하자. 나는 지금 내가 이야기하려는 사나이만을 골라 이야기 하려고 하는 것이니 이 부지 때문에 관련된 사람 가온대 나를 거기다 집을 짓지 못하도록 통로를 막느니 헤방을 하느니 하고 얼르든 전 군수(前郡守)란 리모(李某)나 그자 밑에서 간사히 돌든 김가들의 이야기는 다른 기회에 이야기 하겟스나, 나는 결국 그 땅을 도루 물리고 다른 대로 부지를 정하기로 하엿다. 그것만이면 별로 걱정이 없었슬 터인데, 땅 때문에 분쟁이 많든 가온대 대목이 내게 말을 뭇지도 않은 채 집나무를 운반하고 주치를 놓는다고 그 땅에 심었든 가자(茄子)를 뽑아바리었다. 그 부지는 어느 서양 사람의 밭이었고 그것을 <255>분활해서 주택지로 팔기로 한 것이나 내가 거기다 집을 지으려고 할 때는 사범학교 교유로 근무하는 K씨가 막 신축락성을 해서 이사를 하였슬 따름이고 나머지 땅에는 가자가 그냥 심어저 있었다. 그 밭과 도로 사이에 허리끈 같이 좁게 남은 논이 있어 그 논을 싸지 안으면 결국 통로가 없어지는 것이었다. 그것을 미리 알어차린 전군수라고 아주 호기가 당당한 리모가 논임자에게 헐하게 싸서 우리에게 빗싸게 넘기려고 가진 흉게를 피우므로 나는 자조 그리로 가서 교섭을 하지 안으면 아니 되었다.
나는 밭에 심어 둔 가지쯤이야 문제도 아니삼었다. 신축한 사범학교 교유도
『머? 가지야 뽑고 짓지오. 이 가지요? 바로 저기 잇는 저 집 사람이 소작 한다는데요. 얼마간 가지 값이나 물어주면 고만이지오. 벌서 서리 올 때가 가까우니 지금 딸 때도 거진 다 돼잔허요?』
하며 나더러 얼른 집을 지어서 같이 이웃해서 살자고 권햇다. 그러고는 통로 문제는 만약 리모가 불응할 때는 재판을 해서라도 길은 내고야 말테니, 하는 것이었다.
그러나 나는 사건을 어렵게 만들기 전에 순조로 일을 하려고 리모와 두어번 교섭을 하였다. 그러나 잘 할 것 같지 않고 마침 다른 곳에 좋은 땅이 있어 대목에게 알리려 가니 한 두어 시간 전에 대목이 집나무를 운반하고 밭에 가자를 뽑었다고 하였다. 나는 어찌된 세음인가 하고 그리로 달려갓드니 안일까? 대목과 밭부치는 사나이가 한창 싸우는 중이었다. 대목은 『머? 집이나 얼른 지어두지요. 통로쯤야 설마 아니내어 줄라고』하는 것이었다. 나는 다른 곳에 부지를 정햇단 말을 하고 대목들이 가자 뽑는 것을 중지식혓다.
그 때 성이 시퍼러케 나서 대목과 싸우든 낮이막한 사내가 살랑살랑 웃으며 내 곁으로 걸어왓다.
나는 전에도 두어 번 인사를 하든 김이라,
『아- 이거 아주 미안하게 됏습니다. 가지 임자인 당신헌테 승약도 얻지 않고 저러케 가지를 뽑아 버리었으니』
하고 좋은 낯으로 모자를 벗고 인사를 햇다.
『머 상관 잇습니까 선생님이 어련이 생각하시고 식히었겟습니까』
그는 연에 살랑살랑 웃으며,
『그러치만은 목수 양반이 이 밭을 우리 선생이 삿스니 가지가 어째서 네 것이냐고 하기에 내가, 밭을 삿스면<256> 땅을 산게지 가지까지 산느냐고 지금 그 승강입니다.』
하고 골이 빠진 낡은 외투 포켙 속에 손을 넣엇다가 내ㅅ다가 하며 말햇다.
『거야 그럴 리가 잇소. 가지는 당신 것일테니 손해비용은 물어들일테니 염려 마시오. 허지만 남의 것을 임이로 뽑아서 미안합니다.』
나는 여기다 집을 짓지 않겟노라고는 하지 않었다. 만약 거까지 이야기를 해 놓으면 혹시 이 사내가 과다한 금액이나 요구하지나 않을까? 또는 집도 짓지 안으면서 남의 가자는 왜 뽑엇느냐고 대어든다면 대목이 대답할 말이 없잔흘까고 나는 번듯 생각햇기 때문이었다. 이런 생각이 나의 간사한 꾀임을 나도 짐작하지 안음이 아니나, 살랑살랑 사람 좋은 듯이 웃으며 좋은 낯으로 나와 응대를 하기는 하나 그 사내가 여간한 트집쟁이가 안이믈 나는 직각햇기 때문이었다.

그러므로 나는 그에게서 근 일백원어치나 되는 보짱이며 쟁이며 수십개 나무를 찾지 못하고 지낫다. 나는 그가 불과 10여원에 남의 수백원어치 물건을 잡고 놓지 아니하는 것이 미웁고 분하엿다. 억센 주먹으로 때려 눕히려면 단 한주먹에 박이 날것이, 하고 나는 가진 악설을 머릿속으로 생각하엿다.
그러나 그 금방에 없어지게 생긴 뼈만 남은 사내의 얼골과 눈이 무슨 큰 악귀나 한 것처럼 내 눈에 떠올럿다. 악병이 재앙(災秧)을 가저다 집집이 넣고 단이는 소귀(小鬼)와 같이 그 적은 몸피와 깍짝하게 생긴 얼골과 힐끔하니 시기스런 눈깔이 보이는 것이엇다.
나는 가족에게 비난을 들으면서 15원까지 주마고 양보햇다. 그러나 그는 고지 듯지 아니하엿다.
「그 놈이 한 목 잡고는 한 평생 팔자를 고치랴는갑다.」 하고 나의 어머니는 악설을 퍼부엇다.
그러나 그의 마음을 돌리기는 여간 아니엇다. 나도 그 이상의 돈을 물기는 싫엇다.
그러자 보름이나 지낫다. 나무는 쓰지 못하고 재목 점에 돌려보낼 수밖에 없이 되엇다. 재목점에서는 손해비를 청구해왓다. 나는 이럭저럭 화가 낫다. 그떄 또 나는 병이 나서 일주일이나 누엇섯다.
어대서인지 그 가자쟁이가 도라단이면서 나를 경찰서에다가 잡어다 넣고 꽁꽁 두들겨가며 돈을 받느니 그 놈엣집에 불을 놓느니 하고 별른다는 소문이 들리어 왓다. 나는 병약한 머리에 그 소리를 들으니 화가 무럭무럭 치밀어왓다. 그러므로 나느 사환을 식혀, 경찰서에 가두거던 가두어 보라. 인저는 15원은 커녕 15전도 안 줄테니. 그러고 나무를 잡고 잇섯기 때문에 재목점에 손해비를 물어주어야 되니 재판을 해서라도 돈은 못 주겟노라고 말을 식히엇다. 그러나 나의 심사는 늘 편치 못햇다.
그랫더니 이번에는 효과가 잇서서 당장에 그가 찾어왓다.
「아이고 선생님이 그러케 편치못한 줄 알엇스면 곧 문병이라도 왓슬턴데.」
하며 살랑살랑 웃엇다.
나는 그 살랑살랑 웃는 꼴이 보기가 무서웟다. 그러나 <262> 나도 좋은 말로 12원만 받으려느냐고 하여 보앗다.
그는 또 노기를 띠고 쪼그리고 앉어서 더 내라고 하엿다. 그 말에 나는 벌떡 병석에서 일어나 앉이며 경찰서에다 가두어? 하고 그가 하고 단인 소문을 낯낯이 캐니, 그는 절대로 그런 말은 한 일이 없고 대목놈이 가자를 뽑앗지 어디 선생님이 뽑앗습니까 해서 내가 대목을 그러케 욕을 햇지오. 하고 금방에 살랑살랑 웃으며,
「선생님 그러시지 말고 15원만 주십소. 그대신 나를 살려주는 세음잡고, 게약서를 하나 한 피거리에 10전식 받은 것 같이 써주십쇼.」
하고 말햇다.
나는 이외에 그가 양보를 하는 것이 반가워서
「그거는 어렵잔소. 그럼 게약서를 쓰지오.」
하고 나는 곧 돈을 꺼내주엇다. 그는 지화를 세고는 내가 영수증을 쓰랴니까,
「참 그 영수증에도 40원이라고 쓰시지오.」
하고 내 붓을 디려다 보앗다. 나는 그것도 별로히 어려운 것이 없다고 생각하고 40원이라 썻다. 실상은 15원을 주고 40원 영수증을 썻스니 내게 해로울 것은 없엇다.
영수증을 쓰고 또 약속대로 한 피거리에 일금 10전식 지불이라.는 증명서를 써주랴나? 고 물으니 그는 고개를 갸웃하더니 머? 이건만 선생님이 가지시면 되지오. 혹 누가 와서 뭇거던 그건만 보여주소. 하고 만족한 듯이 나가버리엇다.
그 이튼날 알어보니 그 나머지 밭에도 통로문제가 해결되어서 대여섯채 집을 짓게 되엇다 하엿다.
나는 그가 내가 게약서를 써 달라는 리유를 비로소 꺠달엇다. 그는 다른 이헌테 3,4원식이나 가자값을 받을 욕심으로 그러케 한 것이엇다.
나는 그의 간악한 꾀가 미웁기 한량없엇다.
몇 주일 지난 뒤에 들으니 그 밭에 집을 지으려든 사람들이 역시 가자 문제로 해서 나 모양으로 돈을 문 사람도 잇고 집짓기를 연기한 사람이 많다 하엿다.
나는 그 사내의 지긋지긋하고 달라붙든 일을 생각하면 지금도 몸서리가 처진다. <263>
