Article Title: 失職 (Unemployment)
Author: 廉想涉
Article Type: 소설 (Novel/Fiction)

失職 (Unemployment)
						廉想涉 (Yŏm Sangsŏp)
Writer Sketch

In the literary world of the Peninsula (半島文壇), the one counted foremost among long-form writers might be said to be this gentleman (氏). Indeed, he has his hand in many long works, and those already published number over seven or eight, including “Love and Sin” (사랑과 罪), “Sunflower” (해바라기), “Before Manse” (萬歲前), “Three Generations” (三代), and “When the Peonies Bloom” (모란꼿필때). Besides these, stand-alone books like “*Cow Flower” (*牛花) and “Gold Ring” (금반지) also exist. His writing style is consistently grounded in realism. Originally born in Sajik-tong, Kyŏngsŏng (京城社稷洞), he dropped out of Posŏng Middle School (普成中學), graduated from Kyōto Second Middle School (京都第二中學), went to Tōkyō (東京) and attended Keiō University's (慶應大學) literature department before dropping out mid-way. Afterward, he served as the Arts Editor for the Tong-a Ilbo (동아일보) and the Chosŏn Ilbo (조선일보). Currently, while working as the political editor for a certain newspaper (某新聞), he steadily wields his creative brush. He is forty years old (春秋).

"Why are you so late today?"
The landlady, who had been rinsing rice alone in the kitchen, stopped her hands and glanced blankly at Tŏkch'ang as he entered, reluctantly speaking.

"Yes! Just..." Tŏkch'ang, looking puzzled yet flustered, scanned the *maru* (마루 - wooden floor hall) front and the room across, but seeing no sign of anyone inside, and noticing only his wife's ghostly rubber shoes at the edge of the *maru*, but neither dress shoes nor the girl's shoes, asked with a look of disappointment,
"When did she leave?"

"For some reason, she stayed late today and just left a little while ago...?"
The landlady looked up as if asking back why he was in such a hurry. <230>

"Huh? What's going on......" Tŏkch'ang muttered to himself, then said,
"I just called, and she wasn't there either. Setting that aside, she suddenly said she quit that place. I said some unpleasant things yesterday, but she endured it without a word until just a while ago, and now without any discussion..." Tŏkch'ang stood there, looking blankly across at the landlady, trying to hear if she had said anything before leaving.

"Eh? Said she quit? She left for good?"

"Well, that's what they said on the phone, so it's not certain... Anyway, did she say anything when she left?"

"I didn't hear. Maybe she found a better place and moved."
The landlady replied casually like this and, finishing the last rinse, poured the rice into the pot and started washing the basin (*inambak* 이남박).

"Anyway, since things are looking up starting tomorrow, I was thinking of having her quit and stay home, but even so..."

"Eh? What worked out? Did the thing that was supposed to work out, work out?"
Because he had been saying for a year or two that it would work out, only making empty claims, the news that it had actually happened was as comical yet surprising as hearing a widow had given birth. She put down the basin she was holding, and though her eyes held a hint of derision mixed with a smile, she showed the courtesy of looking pleased.

"Yes- I finally got the position (탁방). I start tomorrow. So I called to quickly share the good news, and this happened."

"Well, if she really quit, she should be back soon. Anyway, that's truly fortunate. More fortunate than tomorrow. So, how much is the salary?"
The landlady suddenly started making a fuss.

"40 *wŏn* (원)!"
Tŏkch'ang replied energetically. He wanted to brag and say 50 *wŏn*, but he only added ten *wŏn*.

"Oh my, with that much, the three of you can live comfortably now. Kinsang (긴상 - informal address for Tŏkch'ang) suffered quite a bit being unemployed for so long, but he's fundamentally talented!" she added praise.

"But where did that girl-brat (계집애년) go?"

"Pong'i took her out to play."

"Ah, Pong'i took her... Now, gradually, we'll be able to repay Pong'i's kindness, and your kindness too, ma'am (아주머님)."
Tŏkch'ang's spirits lifted, forgetting the strange anxiety that had arisen from his wife suddenly leaving the café she worked at without a word. <231>

"Not at all, what kindness are you talking about? Anyway, now, the overdue rent (세전), though it might be difficult all at once, pay it off in two or three installments, and from next time, pay promptly."

"Of course, needless to say..."
Tŏkch'ang, his wife, and their four-year-old daughter had been renting the room across the courtyard in this house for nearly two years now. Not long after moving here, the job as a life insurance solicitor (외교원) proved fruitless, and after being let go having earned little money, he had been living by depending entirely on his wife (안해의 턱살만 치어다 보며), enduring unspeakable hardship and secret shame as a young man, unable even to complain.

While working as an insurance solicitor for about two years, he met his current wife, Puni, also at a café. After she became pregnant, he set her up in a home, dragging her around rented rooms. By chance, they ended up in this house, where an old woman lived with her only daughter, making a living as a *sumo* (수모 - female attendant for ceremonies). Though not a grand, respectable *sumo*, she still had steady work averaging once a month, and with the rent from the tenants' room, she managed a household without worrying about two meals a day. In any case, since it was a house frequented only by *sumo* and matchmakers—meaning Tŏkch'ang was the only male voice—he felt slightly less ashamed sitting idly at home. Moreover, after his wife Puni started working at the café again, the landlady's daughter, Pong'i, looking after his barely weaned daughter for about a year, became a significant help. For these reasons, he had lived in fear of being asked to leave for nearly two years. Yet, what always unsettled Tŏkch'ang was the constant traffic of matchmakers and *sumo* in the house, especially since his wife was earning money working in a place like a café (카-페).

Whenever she came home unreasonably late, say two or three in the morning, reeking of alcohol with glazed eyes, arguments would inevitably erupt. Every time, words like "You get out!" or "I'll get out!" were exchanged every two or three days. And each time, Tŏkch'ang felt as if he would surely lose this woman, never having a moment's peace. Furthermore, seeing her become buddy-buddy with the landlady and some young *sumo*, calling each other older sister, younger sister, auntie, and whatnot, felt like they were doing something behind his back, excluding him. He desperately wanted her to quit and to escape this house as soon as things improved slightly, but he endured.

However, regarding the rent, though some months were skipped entirely, <232> he had never managed to save up and pay more than 2 or 3 *wŏn* a month, usually just trickling by with 1 *wŏn*, 2 *wŏn*, or even just tens of *chŏn* (전 - 1/100th of a *wŏn*). Naturally, it piled up, and though neither party kept exact track now, it was likely a good forty or fifty *wŏn* was overdue. Even if he had just found a job, escaping this debt completely in a month or two was impossible. Setting that aside, the fact that the landlady patiently endured receiving inconsistent rent while letting her own daughter act like a nanny for the tenant's child, without showing any displeasure, became a great source of suspicion for Tŏkch'ang the more he thought about it. She seemed to be doing reasonably well lending money in small sums of 5 or 10 *wŏn* at monthly interest (월수), and yet this fickle, old *sumo* landlady, barely getting rent money, wasn't even sending her precious daughter to school but having her babysit the tenant's child. It seemed bizarre even upon waking thought, making him suspect she must see some benefit in it. - Anyway, now it was time to have her quit, and then, even if they had to live on gruel for half a year, he would pay off the rent and try to move somewhere else...

Thinking this, Tŏkch'ang took off his shabby Western suit inside his room, hung it up, changed into Korean clothes, and came out. Though evening rice was likely cooked along with the morning meal, like a widower's household (호래비 살림), come evening, Tŏkch'ang had to potter about at the stove set under the ledge of the raised wooden floor (*t'oenmaru* 퇴ㅅ마루) to light the fire and feed the two of them.

Tŏkch'ang dumped the cold leftover rice into the pot, lit the fire, and then, intending to set the table, reached to lift the top one of two stacked small dining tables (*soban* 소반). He almost cried out, "Eh?", startled, dropped the table he held in one hand, and picked up an envelope lying on the bottom table. Tŏkch'ang instantly realized that his long-held suspicion, the fantasy he had entertained even until now, had manifested clearly before him as reality. Fearful, shocked, resentful, wronged, hateful... tossed by a complex mix of emotions impossible to articulate clearly, he frantically tore open the letter, knowing its contents even before reading.

After reading it through in one breath, Tŏkch'ang felt his throat go dry and swallowed hard once, gulping empty air.
"Now, having tired you out enough and deceived you enough, I bid farewell to you. Not just farewell to you, but farewell to this world as well. Don't waste your effort searching. If you find the body, how could you arrange the funeral (감장)? Rather, you would be angry. Only Wŏnhŭi (원이) weighs on my mind, but having raised her to four years old, <233> surely you can manage somehow? If it's truly difficult, please consult with the landlady (주인 마님) and ensure she is raised well......"
It was mostly talk like this. And enclosed was a separate letter addressed to the landlady, expressing gratitude for her long kindness and requesting her to find someone to raise the daughter if possible.

Though she often threatened to leave or die, his assumption had always been that if some change occurred, she would surely grab some other man's hand and disappear. But contrary to expectations, she had run off leaving behind something like a suicide note from someone going to die—not just like, but perhaps truly a note from someone heading to the Han River railway bridge. Should he believe this, or was it just an empty document fabricated by a runaway woman who had nothing else to say? Tŏkch'ang had no time to ponder the context or sort things out.

"What's that?"
The landlady (*mananim* 마나님), having somehow lit the fire under the main room's rice pot, had gone into her room and was smoking, peering out through the glass pane and speaking.

"Ah, look at this. Did she really go to die, or did she run away......"
Tŏkch'ang's voice trembled like his fingertips holding out the letters outside the sliding door, choked and halting.

"Eh? What are you talking about?" The mistress (*mananim*) jumped out onto the *maru*.

"Surely, it can't be true. You argued last night too, so she's probably just trying to teach you a lesson."
The landlady glanced carelessly at the two notes in her hand, then stood gazing blankly at the distant mountains as if pondering something.

"Where are you going?"
As Tŏkch'ang rushed into his room, hastily put on his Western suit, and came out tying his necktie, the mistress turned and asked, almost chidingly.

"First, I need to go report it to the police station," Tŏkch'ang said, stepping agitatedly (*pudŏng pudŏng* 부덩부덩) down into the yard.

"Why are you in such a rush!"
The landlady, seemingly having some thought, scolded him again.

"Ah, rushing? I need to quickly request a search and go check the Han River railway bridge, or look in likely places. Who knows what can happen to a person."

"But even if she went to the Han River bridge, she'd probably go after dark. Surely not before sunset. Maybe she'd hang herself from a tree branch— <234> if she were that desperate to die, how could she have lived this long?"
The landlady said this with a snort.

"Well, that's true too!" Tŏkch'ang suddenly felt a sense of relief, standing blankly, looking up at the landlady as if waiting for more comforting words to emerge from her mouth.
"No, but ma'am (아주머니), did you hear anything?" he asked.

"Absolutely not! If she had told me anything, do you think she would have left me a letter like this too? Think about it," she retorted sharply, again giving him a hard time.

"No, I just thought maybe you noticed something."
Tŏkch'ang sounded sheepish, his tone now almost placating the landlady.

"Noticed? Wouldn't the couple sleeping in the same room notice things better?"
As the landlady delivered another rebuke, Tŏkch'ang was rooted to the spot, able only to stare blankly at the distant mountains from the stone embankment.

From the pot under the *t'oenmaru* of the rented room, the reheating rice boiled over, the lid rattling (*pugŭl pugŭl* 부글부글) as foam and rice water trickled out, but Tŏkch'ang saw it and ignored it. Even if the pot boiled dry and cracked, he was in no state to attend to such things. However, the landlady, her mind apparently elsewhere, had completely forgotten about lighting the fire under her own rice pot until she saw the rice water overflowing from the tenant's pot. Only then did she rush down and into the kitchen. The smell of burning rice from the main room's pot wafted strongly, reaching Tŏkch'ang's nose.

"I smell rice burning."

"Never mind that worry, just open the lid of your own pot. Or push the fire back."
A sharp voice came from the kitchen, sounding irritated about something. In truth, Tŏkch'ang had spoken absentmindedly, and also partly out of a desire to lean on the landlady, his only source of support and advice in this situation. But realistically, he should have worried about his own pot boiling over before worrying about someone else's rice burning.

"That's why people like him lose their wives to other men. He smells others' rice burning but not his own."
Following that, the landlady muttered this as if to herself, scooping the fire from the stove into a brazier.

"Ma'am (아주머니), what are you saying? Lose my wife to another man? <235> Are you talking about me?"
Tŏkch'ang's ears perked up. Still ignoring his own pot, he approached the kitchen door.

"What's this nonsense now? I'm just speaking hypothetically, who's talking about you, nephew (족하님)? Just go out and search as you see fit. Stop suspecting innocent people and picking fights..."
The landlady, for some reason, softened her expression again, using the term "nephew" (족하님) soothingly. This landlady had different ways of addressing him: "Kinsang" (긴상), "you folks" (*imjane* 임자네 / *chagyane* 자갸네), and "nephew" (족하님), each used at different times.

In any case, Tŏkch'ang was puzzled. He had long suspected this old woman (*rogu* 로구) might pull some trick, and her behavior now—seeming worried about something, then suddenly flaring up in anger before calming down—was suspicious. But anyway, finding Puni was urgent, so he held his tongue and turned to leave.

"However, don't rush recklessly to the police station. If you wait a bit, she might just come back on her own... Surely she wouldn't die so easily."
From behind, the old woman (*ropa* 로파) muttered this emphatically.

Somehow, from the landlady's tone, Tŏkch'ang gathered that Puni probably hadn't died, and felt a sense of relief wash over him. Yet, where should he even begin to look if he left now? It was uncertain. What pained him most was that if she had just endured for a few more hours, he could have told her about the job she had so eagerly awaited, and seen her happy and joyful. But by some twist of fate, just as he received the official appointment letter, she had vanished without a trace. That was the most heart-wrenching part.

Just as Tŏkch'ang turned the corner of the rented room, he ran into Pong'i leading Wŏnhŭi by the hand.
"Daddy—" Wŏnhŭi rushed towards him. Seeing her, Tŏkch'ang didn't pick her up (*ŏnji anŏtta* 언지 않었다). This child, like a widower's child, didn't know her mother's affection but only clung to her father. From the time she was weaned, her mother slept until late morning, then hastily dressed up and left, returning around one or two in the morning while she was asleep, only showing her awake face briefly the next day. So it was natural.

"Daddy, give me money—"
The child clung to his legs.

"Where would I get money? You have to wait for Mommy."
He placated her with the phrase he always used.

"Mommy left money. There's money over there."

"Huh? Where?" <236>

"Go look over there. She said to give it to Daddy when he comes..."
The little girl scampered into the room, opened the drawer of the dressing table (*kyŏngdae* 경대), and came out holding a coin purse.

It was Puni's worn-out coin purse (*talgappajin* 달가빠진).
-When did she buy a new purse? Leaving behind the one she carried until yesterday... - Thinking this, he took the purse and opened it. Inside were three 1-*wŏn* bills and about 40 *chŏn* in change. Seeing this, Tŏkch'ang felt his hair stand on end (*morikkŭt'i tchutpyŏt* 머리 끝이 쭛볏하며) and his heart dropped (*kasŭmi tŏllŏng* 가슴이 덜렁). It was impossible that she suddenly acquired a large sum of money. Seeing that she had emptied her purse and left the money she carried, it became instantly clear that she had truly gone off to die.

Thinking back to the previous dawn, when she had remained silent as a mouse despite his harsh words—unlike other times when she would have fiercely retorted (*p'ongp'ong massoŭlt'ende* 퐁퐁 맛쏘을텐데)—and hadn't fallen asleep even after Tŏkch'ang himself grew tired and dozed off first, it seemed she had indeed made some firm decision. Whether that decision was to die, or whether it was made after preparing to go off with another man, now regret washed over him, and realizing his casual dismissal (*yesarohi yŏgyŏttŏn kŏt* 례사로히 역였던 것) had been foolish felt absurd. Anyway, thanks to the 3 *wŏn* and some change Puni had left at home, Tŏkch'ang could take the streetcar instead of walking half the day, arriving swiftly at M Cafe (M 카페-). Reaching the entrance, Tŏkch'ang felt intimidated (*chŏgŭi ka toeŏsŏ* 저긔가 되어서) by his own shabby appearance without an overcoat and hesitated, but mustered his courage and went straight in. Though he only knew it by word of mouth, it was less a proper café and more a teahouse (*kkiktajŏm* 끽다점), considered second or third-rate even in Seoul. It was winter, just getting dark, and there were only a couple of customers sitting under the dim lights, each with a girl seated opposite them, mouths shut tight and eyes downcast as if lost in deep contemplation.

As Tŏkch'ang entered, he quickly scanned the women's faces. But Puni, of course, wasn't there. The girls glanced at each other and giggled pointlessly, it seemed, but perhaps eager to deliver the order, brought the requested bottle of liquor in less than five minutes.

"Isn't there a woman here named Midori (미도리)?"
Tŏkch'ang glanced (*kŏndŭt* 건듯) at the face of the woman pouring the liquor and asked politely, pretending to be a country bumpkin (*sigolttugi* 시골뚝이). Although it was run by Koreans, frequented mostly by Koreans, and all the women working there were Korean, they all used names like "Midori" or "Sayo-chan" (사요쟝).

"Why do you ask?"
She retorted as if expecting an argument. Her tone implied that given Tŏkch'ang's shabby <237> appearance, he was asking things he shouldn't.

"Well, I need to know why..."

"She left."
As soon as the words left her mouth, the woman went over to the other side, sat down with her back to the stove in front of another customer.

Tŏkch'ang just stared silently, the words "She left" echoing in his ears. He hadn't come here expecting her to be present, but considering this cold reception, it seemed plausible that Puni leaving him was a natural consequence. He also thought that woman too might run away abandoning her husband, wondering if her husband was a man like himself.

Thinking this, he felt just as the old landlady had said, like he'd lost his wife to another man. Even though Puni left her wallet behind, it seemed unlikely she was dead. He concluded that trying to get information from this place was the best course of action.

Drinking alone as if punishing himself, though it wasn't much liquor in one bottle, he quickly finished it and asked more details when the second bottle was brought.
"When did she quit?"

"Why do you ask? What's your relationship?"
Puni had only been at this place for four or five months, and because they were mutually embarrassed, Tŏkch'ang had never visited, nor had Puni talked about her personal affairs to her colleagues. So no one would guess Tŏkch'ang was her husband.

"Well, I just arrived from the countryside today. To be honest, it's embarrassing, but she's my younger sister. I went looking for her at the house where she was staying, and they said she was here. So I came straight here, only to hear she's gone somewhere else again. I'm just dumbfounded and want to find out any news before I go..."
Having the knack (*tchikkŏkchira* 찍걱지라) from his days as an insurance solicitor, he spun this plausible tale.

"Really? Are you her older brother (오라버리)? You came up from the countryside?"

"Hey, I said I'm her real older brother, does it sound fake?" he suddenly snapped, his tone changing (*tallajyŏtta* 달러젔다) unintentionally.
Tŏkch'ang, hungry all day, felt the alcohol quickly spread, and the skills from his old days courting Puni while frequenting cafés as an insurance solicitor began to surface.

The woman stared intently at Tŏkch'ang's face, as if trying to see a resemblance to Puni, then smirked slightly without a word and scurried inside. A moment later, a lanky man, about thirty, plump (*ttingtting'i tchŏssŭmyŏnsŏdo* 띙띙이 쩟으면서도) yet shapelessly dull, appeared and stood squarely in front of Tŏkch'ang. <238>

Tŏkch'ang, utterly surprised—and with his nerves already frayed and agitated—flinched in alarm.

"You're 'Midori's' older brother? Perfect timing."
The man, despite his unimpressive build, stood firmly with both hands planted on the hips of his jacket (*tchakkessŭ* 짜켓스), forcing his small eyes to look fierce.

"Eh? Yes!" Tŏkch'ang, overwhelmed by his awkward situation, hesitated (*ŏrŭmkŏrimyŏ* 어름거리며) and shifted his weight nervously for no reason.

"That girl... look here, she came to my place for 300 *wŏn*, but after only two months, having paid off just 20 *wŏn*, she's skipped out somewhere (*tollannŭnji* 돌라앉는지), maybe run off with some guy. Anyway, she said she'd bring 100 *wŏn* as an advance payment for leaving, but she hasn't shown her face since yesterday. Do you know the story behind this (*kŭnyŏk* 그녁)?"
Aside from the aggressive way he spoke, Tŏkch'ang knew perfectly well Puni had worked here for four months, yet this owner claimed it was two. Tŏkch'ang inwardly scoffed—*you shady bastard*—as he looked back at him.

"Huh— Is that so? Actually, I just came up from the countryside myself and am looking for her..."
Even as he said this, Tŏkch'ang felt relieved that Puni was, at least, alive. At the same time, the thought flashed through his mind: Did she deceive even me to move somewhere else? - Please let it be just that... But anxiety also gnawed at him: If this guy really takes me for a country bumpkin and presses me, how will I handle this trouble?

"So— what are you going to do? Even if you had the 180 *wŏn*, would you pay if I demanded it? Or, since Midori isn't here, would I accept it even if you offered? So, somehow, you need to find Midori yourself, settle the accounts, and then leave. It's unfortunate for our first meeting, but I've also been waiting anxiously (*chobibidŭt* 조비비듯) yesterday and today, wondering when she'd show up. You too have come all this way looking for your sister. So, find her and deal with the aftermath (*twikalmang* 뒤갈망). Otherwise, I'll have to report it to the police!"
Tŏkch'ang, thoroughly drunk (*palkkak chwihasŏ* 술이 발깍 취해서), just sat there with heavy, drowsy (*sŭmŏl sŭmŏlhan* 스멀스멀한) eyes lowered.
Thinking about his daughter at home, who would be fussing (*ogu* 오구) more as the night wore on, was heartbreaking enough. Putting Puni aside for now, even if he felt resentment and anger, how could he handle this man's demands? And even if he luckily escaped this, how would he manage the consequences later (*najong kalmang* 나종 갈망)? It felt like trying to remove a lump only to add another (*hokŭl tteryŏda puch'indaegiro* 혹을 떼려다 붙인대기로). (Continued) <239>

**Summary**

Tŏkch'ang, long unemployed and supported by his wife Puni who works at a café, finally secures a job. Returning home eager to share the news, he finds Puni gone, having left abruptly without explanation. The landlady is evasive. Tŏkch'ang discovers a note suggesting Puni might have left him or even committed suicide, along with her empty wallet containing only a small amount of cash, intensifying his fear. He rushes to the café where Puni worked, only to be told she quit. Trying to inquire further by pretending to be her brother, he is confronted by the café owner demanding repayment of a large advance Puni allegedly took before disappearing, leaving Tŏkch'ang trapped and bewildered.

This story offers insight into the economic hardships faced by educated Koreans like Tŏkch'ang under Japanese colonial rule, forcing men into unemployment and women into potentially exploitative environments like cafés (*k'ap'e* 카페) for survival. Historically, the story reveals the precarious urban life in 1930s Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul), the function of cafés as places of female employment often involving debt bondage (as hinted by the owner's demand for 300 *wŏn*), and the complex gender dynamics where male unemployment emasculated men and pushed women into morally ambiguous social spaces. The use of Japanese names by Korean café workers further underscores the colonial context.

半島文壇에 잇서 長篇作家로써 第一指에 곱히는 이가 氏라 하리만치 氏는 長編에 손대인 것이 만하서 이미 發表한 것만 헤여도 「사랑과 罪」 「해바라기」「萬歲前」「三代」「모란꼿필때」 제 7, 8편을 넘는다. 또 단행본으로 나온 것도 이밧게 「*牛花」「금반지」等이 잇다, 그의 作風은 어디까지든지 사실주의에 일관되어 잇다. 원래 京城社稷洞出生으로 普成中學을 中途退學, 京都第二中學을 졸업, 東京에 이르러 慶應大學文科에 재학하다가 중도퇴학, 그 뒤 동아일보, 조선일보 學藝部長을 역임, 현재는 某신문 정치부장으로 재직하는 한편 창작의 붓을 꾸준히 날니고 잇다. 春秋는 四十,

「오늘은 웨 이리 늦었오?」
주인 마누라는 혼자 부억에서 조리질을 하든 손을 멈추고 들어오는 덕창이를 신푸녕스런 눈치로 멀금히 건너다 보다가 마지못해 말을 붙인다.
「네! 좀...」하며 덕창이는 의아해 하면서도 당황한 기색으로 마루앞과 건넌방을 훌터보다가 방안에 인기척도 없고 마루끝에 안해의 귀신같은 고무신짝 밖에는 구두도, 계집애 년의 신짝도 눈에 안 띠우자 실망한 눈치로
「언제 나갓세요?」하고 묻는다.
「오늘은 웬일인지 늦도록 있다가 조금 전에 나갓는데...?」
주부는 왜 허둥허둥하느냐고 도리어 뭇는 눈치로 치어다 <230> 본다.
「응? 그 웬 일인가......」하고 덕창이는 혼자ㅅ소리를 하다가
「지금 전화를 걸어 보니까 저기도 없구, 그건 고사하구 별안간 저기를 그만 둔다구 했다니, 어제 좀 듣기 싫은 소리는 했지만 아까까지도 암말없이 참다케 있던 사람이 한마디 의론도 없이...」하며 덕창이는 주부에게나 무어라 하고 나갓는지 드러보려고 물금히 마주 건너다 보고 섰다.
「에? 그만둔다구? 아주 나왔대?」
「글세, 전화로 하는 말이니까 자세치는 않지만......그래 나갈제 아무말도 없세요?」
「못드렀어. 아마 더 좋은 데가 있어 옴겨간게지.」
하며 주부는 지나는 말로 이렇게 대꾸를 하고는 마즈막 조리질을 쳐서 솟속에 쏟고 이남박을 들어 일기 시작한다.
「어차피, 인젠 내일이 좀 피우게 됐으니까 그만두게 하고 들어앉히랴는 생각이지만, 그러기로...」
「에? 무에 됐오? 된다던 게 됐오?」
하두 일년 이태를 두고 된다된다 하며 헛소리만 하던 것이 됐다는 바람에 과부가 아이 낳다는 말이나 들은 듯이 웃으깡스러우면서도 신기해서 손에 든 이남박을 내려놓고, 멸시하는 듯한 눈초리에 웃음까지 먹으머 보이며 그래도 반색을 하는 인사성을 보인다.
「네- 겨우 탁방이 낫세요. 내일부터 가게 됐어요. 그래서 어서 반가운 소식을 들려주려고 전화를 걸었더니 그 모양이죠.」
「그야, 정말 그만 두었다면, 좀 있으면 들어오겠지, 그런데 참 고맙소. 내일보다 더 고맙소. 그래 월급은 얼마란 말요?」
주부는 별안간 호들감을 떨기 시작한다.
「40원요!」
덕창이는 활수잇게 대답을 한다. 이왕이면 50원이라고 풍을 치고 싶었으나, 겨우 십원을 더 불렀다.
「어구, 그만하면 세 식구 인젠 떵떵거리고 살겠오. 긴상이 오래 노느라고 고생두 퍽 했지만, 원체 재주는 좋거던!」하며 칭찬이 느러젔다.
「헌데 게집애년은 어디 갔에요?」
「봉이가 데리고 놀러 나갔지.」
「아, 봉이가 데리고...... 인젠 차차 봉이 신세도 갚게 되고, 아주머님 신세도 갚어 드리게 되겠죠.」
덕창이는 안해가 말 한마디 없이 별안간 다니던 「카페-」에서 나왔다는 데에 이상한 불안이 떠올라 오던 것도 이저 버리고 신기가 좋아젔다. <231>
「천만에 신세는 무슨 신세란 말요. 아무턴지 인제는 밀린 세전이나 한거번에는 어렵겠지만, 두서너 번에 질러 끄구, 이 다음엘랑 또박또박 내구려.」
「그야 말슴않기로...」
덕창이는 내외에 네 살 먹은 딸년을 데리고 세 식구가 이 집 건넌방에 세를 든 것이 벌서 근 이태나 되는 것이다. 이리 떠나온 지 몇 달 못 돼서 생명보험의 외교원이라는 것도 불관한 일에, 실은 돈도 몇 푼 못 먹고 떨려난 뒤로는 담배 한 개까지라도 안해의 턱살만 치어다 보며 사라오느라니, 젊은 놈이 말 못할 고생, 남모를 창피도 당할 만큼 당하면서 입도 못 버리고 참아왔던 것이다.
보험회사 외교원을 한 이태 다니든 사*에 지금의 안해 분이를 역시 「카페-」에서 만낫던 것이요, 아이가 들자 들어 앉혀가지고 세ㅅ방 구석으로 끌고 다니던 것이지만, 우연히 걸려든 이 집은, 늙은 마누라가 단 딸 하나 데리고, 수모 노릇을 해 가며 사는 터이다. 제법 어엿한 큰 수모는 아니지만, 그래도 한 달에 한번 평균으로는 꾸준히 일거리가 있어서, 건넌방 세 전 받고 하면 하로 두끼니에는 걱정이 없는 살림이다. 어쨋던 수모, 중매 따위만 드나드는, 말하자면 사네소리하는 덕창이 하나밖에 없는 집이니만치, 덕창이는 쭉치고 드러앉었어도 조금은 덜 창피하고 더구나 안해 분이가 다시 「카페-」로 나가게 된 뒤로는 안방집 딸 봉이가 있어서 겨우 젖을 떠러젔던 딸년을 일년싹시나 보아주는 것이 행결 도음이 되었던 것이다. 이래저래 이 집에서 나가랄까 보아 무서워서 하며 근 이태나 사라왔지만 그래도 늘 덕창이의 마음이 놓이지 안는 것은, 갓득이나 「카-페」 같은데 내놓아서 버러드러는 것을 얻어먹는 판에, 중매니 수모니 하는 따위가 드나드는 것이 마음에 께름한 것이다.
하도 눈꼴 틀리거나, 제시간에 대어 들어오지를 않고, 새로 두시 세시나 되어서 입으론 술냄새를 풍고 두 눈이 개개 풀려 들어오는 것을 보고는 아무래도 말다틈이 되고, 그럴 때마다 네가 나가라거니 내가 나가겠다거니 하는 소리가 나는 것도 이튼 사흘 돌리요, 또 그럴 때마다 덕창이는 이 계집을 꼭 빼끼고 마는 건만 같애서, 한때도 마음을 놓고 지낸 날이 없지마는, 게다가 이 집 주인 마누라나 몇몇 젊은 수모들 하고 한통속이 되어서 형님, 아우님, 아주머니, 어쩌고 지내는 것을 보면 자기만 돌려 세 놓고 무슨 짓들을 하는 것 같애서 조곰 웬만만 하면 드려 앉히고 이 집도 어서 면해버리고 싶은 생각이 골뚝같은 것을 참고 지내오는 터이었다.
그러나 세전만 하더라도 순연히 걸르는 달도 있었지만, <232> 한 달에 2원 3원씩 밖에 그남아 모아서 내 본 일은 없고 1원, 2원, 심지어 몇십전...... 요렇게 흘려 넘어가는 형편이니, 자연히 싸이고 싸여서 지금은 피차에 셈도 분명치 않으나 아마 사오십원은 넉넉히 밀려 있는 터이니, 지금 겨우 취직이 되었다손 처도 한두달 동안에 그것을 깡그리고 빠저나가긴 어려운 노릇이다. 그건 그렇다 하고 대관절 세전도 변변히 못 받고 게다가 자기 딸년은 건넌방의 아이보기처럼 내맡겨 두면서 조금도 싫여하는 내색이 없시 구준히 지내는 것이 가다가다 생각할수록 덕창이에게는 큰 의심거리이기도 한 것이다. 잔돈냥이나 모이면 5원, 10원씩 월수를 놓아 빗노리도 쑬쑬히 하는 모양이요 게다가 변덕이 습진을 하는 이묵이 다-된 이 수모 마누라가, 셋돈도 변변히 못 내는 자국에 귀한 딸자식은 학교도 안 보내고 건넌방 아이나 보아주게 하니, 자다 생각해도 히한한 일이요 무엇이던지 보는 자국이 있어서 그러는 것만 같이 의심이 나는 것이다. -하여간 인제는 들어 앉힐 일이요, 그 다음엔 반년 동안만 죽을 쑤어 연명을 하야 가면서라도 세전을 끄고 좀 다른데로 떠보아야 하겠다. ......

덕창이는 그것을 생각하면 아까 주인 로파 말맛다나 제 계집 남에게 빼낀건만 같고, 돈 지갑은 그대로 두고 나갔다기로 죽었을리는 없을 것 같어서, 아무래도 이 집에서 수소문을 해내는 것이 상책이라고 생각하였다.
혼자 벌주를 켜듯이 얼마 안되는 한 병 술이지만 훅닥 마시고 둘재병을 가저왔을 제 좀 자세히 물었다.
「언제부터 그만 두었나요?」
「왜 그러세요? 어떻게 되세요?」
분이가 이 집에 온지도 너덧달 밖에 아니되거니와, 피차에 창피해서 덕창이가 이 집에를 와본 일도 없고 분이가 동모들에게 제 속사정을 이야기 한 일도 없으니 덕창이를 분이의 남편으로 것짐작할 사람도 없다.
「아니, 난 시골서 오늘 올라왔는데, 실상은 창피한 말이지만, 내 누이동생이란 말이죠. 지금 저 있던 집으로 찾어 가보니까, 여기 있다기에 허히단심 찾어온 길인데 또 어디로 갔다니 하두 기가 막혀서 소식이나 좀 알구 가려구......」
보험회사 외교원을 다니던 찍걱지라, 이렇게 그럴 듯이 꾸며대었다.
「정말 오라버리세요? 시골서 올라 오섰세요?」
「이 사람아 정말 오라버니라니, 가짠 듯 싶다는 말인가?」하며, 벌안간 무심중에 말씨가 달러젔다.
덕창이는 종일 굶은 속에 술이 드러가서 금시로 퍼지기도 하였지만, 예전 보험회사 외교원 시대에 분이에게 반해서 카페-도 드나드든 시절 솜씨가 차차 나오기 시작하였다.
여자는 덕창이의 얼굴을 분이와 한 모습인지를 보려는 듯이 가만히 바라보더니, 아무말 없이 입귀로 비웃는 듯한 웃음을 살짝 비치곤 안으로 쪼르르 드러가 버린다. 조금 있다가 키대가 후리후리하고, 살이 띙띙이 쩟으면서도 모인 구석이 없어서 투미하디 투미한 삼십가량 된 남자가 덕창이 앞에 나와서 가로 딱 막아선다. <238>
덕창이는 너무나 의외에- 그리고 한참 신경이 날카롭게 흥분된 끝에 깜짝 놀라며 질겁을 첬다.
「당신이 「미도리」의 오라버니라구? 마츰 잘 되었군요.」
되지 않은 체격에 두 손을 짜켓스 입은 좌우 허리에 딱 집고 버티고 서서 조그만 눈을 애를 써 부리부리해 보인다.
「에에? 네!」하고 덕창이는 군색한 자기 처지에 눌려서, 어름거리며 공연히 황겁히 엉덩이를 들먹하였다.
「그 애가, ... 이거 봐요, 그 애가 내 집에 300원에 왔는데 두 달밖에 안있는 동안 겨우 20원만 까고 어디로 돌라앉는지, 어 놈을 마저가는지 하여간 가겠다고 선금으로 100원만 치르고 남어지는 그적게 가저온다더니, 어제부터는 그림자도 안 보이니, 대관절 그녁은 그 내막을 알겠구려?」
그녁이라고 덤비는 수작도 수작이려니와, 번연한 분이가 이집에 넉달 다닌 것은 덕창이가 더 잘 아는 터인데, 주인이란 이 자는 두달이라고 하는 말에, 덕창이는 속으로 이 컴컴한 놈아- 하고 마조 건너다 보았다.
「허- 그래요? 실상은 지금 나도 시골서 올라와서 찾어 다니는 길 데요......」
그러면서도 덕창이는 분이가 어잿던 살았고나 하고 안심이 되었다. 동시에 어떤 달은데로 옴겨 가느라고 자기까지 속였는가? - 제발 그렇기나 해주었으면...... 하는 생각이 번개같이 머리속에 떠올라왔다. 그러나 이 놈이 정말 자기를 시골뚝이로 알고 달고치면 이 성화를 어떻게 받나? 하는 불안도 없지 않었다.
「자-, 그러니 어떡하시겠오? 지금 당신이 180원이란 돈이 있기로 내가 내라면 내겠오-, 또는 미도리가 이 자리에 없는 다음에야 받으라기로 내가 받을 리가 있겠오. 하니 어잿던 당신 손으로 미도리를 찾어놓고 청장을 해 주고서 가야 될 일이 아니요. 우리가 초면에 안되기는 했으나, 나두 어제 오늘 이때나 오나 저때나 오나 하고 조비비듯 기다리던 판이요. 당신도 애를 써 누이동생을 찾으러 왔다니, 어잿던 찾어가지고 뒤갈망을 해주고 가슈. 그렇지 않으면 경찰에 수속을 하는 수밖에!」
덕창이는 술이 발깍 취해서 스멀스멀한 두 눈을 내리깔고 가 이 앉었을 뿐이다.
집*에서 딸년이 밤이 들어갈수록 오구를 며*바지를 찾을 생각을 해도 기가 막히려니와, 분이는 그쯤하고 갔다니 염려보다도 인제는 분하고 괘씸하다하더라도 이 놈의 성화를 어떻게 받고, 요행 빠저나간다기로 나종 갈망을 어떻게 할*인가. 혹을 떼려다 붙인대기로 이런 일도 있을까 싶었다. (계속) <239>
