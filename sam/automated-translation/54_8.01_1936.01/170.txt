A Letter Left by Ubo
						Ch'oe Ŭn-hŭi
Older Sister!

It seems it has been over 10 years since we last met. When you were living in Wŏnsan (元山), there was a Newspaper Branch Managers' Association meeting, and you came up to Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul) with your husband; when was that exactly? It truly was a meeting and parting as swift as lightning; we only briefly exchanged handshakes on Ponch'ŏng Tong (本町通) street, unable to even properly share our feelings of longing before parting ways. Since then, having lost contact with each other, already 10 years have passed. Unexpectedly, your husband's visit this time brought me great joy and became a solace during my tedious sickbed days. I developed a fearsome abscess called *puhujong* (腐後腫, also known as *palchi*), and from November 1st, I began receiving treatment from Dr. O Wŏn-sŏk (吳元錫). A boil appeared on the back of my head, seemingly insignificantly, but the discharge spread, making my head sore like one often sees with countryside children. I underwent home treatment for about two weeks, and it was almost healed, but ultimately, trouble arose. The doctor and I tried light therapy, ice packs, and injections to prevent it from festering, but we saw no effect, and eventually, pus (膿) formed, leading finally to surgery on the 7th of last month. Older Sister! Though I may seem spirited to others, in reality, I am a person who cannot even touch a single live creature or insect. Even when my children ask me to catch things like cicadas, dragonflies, or grasshoppers, I cannot grant their request. Though I adore baby birds or puppies in my heart, I cannot bring myself to touch them. I am the possessor of a frail and weak (殘弱) character, unable to dig out and remove a thorn from someone else's hand with a needle<118> for them. So, when I saw my own head, after being cut open more than two *ch'i* (inches) wide and deep with a sharp knife, covered in blood, and bandaged with gauze (까-제가パクツ로 - *likely gauze bandage*), goosebumps rose all over my body, and the doctor, smiling brightly, seemed terribly cruel and heartless (沒情). Although my recovery after the surgery seems relatively good, for 4 or 5 days, I absolutely could not move my head from the pillow, having to lie face down, sucking milk or thin rice gruel through a straw, and even needing assistance with urination and defecation, reaching such a critical state. Only recently have I become able to barely get on the streetcar (電車) by myself to go to the hospital. My body is extremely weakened, my mind is weary, and steeped in loneliness, I am passing the time in endless reverie. Older Sister! Although I have a good teacher on this earth whom I respect and who serves as a consultant for my affairs, and also two good friends who love each other, keep faith, and understand each other's intentions, because my house is inconveniently located on the slope of Inwangsan (仁王山), they cannot be of help to me when I miss them and feel wanting. If there is any small comfort, it is the momentary joy of seeing my young son, just past his first birthday, push heavy objects with both hands while lying down when asked to bring them because they are too much for his strength, or the quirky wisdom he displays when, if there's food he shouldn't eat, he hides it in his fist and pretends to wipe his nose with that fist while slipping it into his mouth. It truly is a desolate sickroom. Yet, hearing that you, Older Sister, have borne sons and daughters and live a blessed life in a peaceful home, I cannot help but feel admiration. Older Sister! Not only you, but all the people for whom I served as a bridesmaid (들노리) are now living happily. Ms. Mun Suk-yŏng (文淑英) of Anju (安州) came to Kyŏngsŏng as her husband became a doctor for the Chiyoda Life Insurance Company (千代田生命保險社), and they are living a honey-sweet life in Waryongdong (臥龍洞). Ms. Hwang Sin-dŏk (黃信德) joined the *Sindonga* magazine, commuting daily to the *Tong-a Ilbo* newspaper office with her husband, Im Pong-jun (任鳳淳), like a pair of mandarin ducks. And Ms. Yu Kyŏng-jun (劉慶俊), the younger sister of Yu Yŏng-jun (劉英俊), whom I accompanied all the way to Changyŏn (長連) for her wedding, is said to be living a life of luxury as the wife of the foremost wealthy man in that area. But why is it that only I, who stood as bridesmaid for these fortunate ladies, am unable to partake in such blessings? I too bore a son and a daughter, and after quitting the newspaper company, I obtained a permit to sell postage stamps and revenue stamps (郵便切手及收入印紙賣捌許可), stabilizing my livelihood with the income. However, the hardships and joys (風霜苦樂) I have experienced during that time could not be half told even if the long spring days of March and April were doubled.

Older Sister! It seems that one's destined fate in this world cannot be controlled by human effort. During my studies in Tokyo (東京), there was a young man to whom I was engaged, waiting for me to graduate so we could perform the first sacrament of lighting the candles (i.e., get married), but he could not achieve that wish and became a departed guest who would never return. And the so-called love affair that occurred while I was working at the *Chosŏn Ilbo*, which became the protagonist of my fate today, unfolded over eight years of spring winds and autumn rains (春風秋雨八個星霜),<119> becoming an affair too full of incidents and too highly talked about. To dredge up that complicated old history anew is distressing, but whether good or bad, it has now forever become mere material for memories within a stream of tears.

* * *

Now it seems autumn has come. When a lonely feeling drifts about the treetops, when a lonely feeling rushes into my chest, if it is like that here, then over there, the autumn mood must be even stronger. How lonely must you be? How do you sleep, and how are your meals? Is Sŏnghae hyŏng (星海兄 - older brother Sŏnghae, likely Ubo's given name/pen name) well and being a somewhat affectionate companion? I am curious about everything. It is fortunate enough that there is nothing amiss here. Only, when I sit idly and meditate, there is just the feeling as if seeing my lonely soul, like one standing alone in a desert, pitifully wandering, wishing I could go to an uninhabited place (無人地境) and weep to my heart's content. But Ŭn-hŭi (恩喜)! For Ŭn-hŭi's distant future happiness, I, who will be your faithful companion (伴侶), am here, so cast away all worries. And rest assured. Though our bodies are apart, thinking that I, whose heart never leaves your side for even a moment, am here, can you not laugh a laugh of solitary contentment even on a deserted mountain (無人空山)? Consider that a person can manage somehow, ultimately, just by their state of mind (맘세). Can you not also open your mouth wide and laugh joyfully in a fleeting, momentary instant? Please let me hear your laughter. Hastily,
August 30th
Having finished the second page editing, listening to the cicadas on the back hill, Ubo Saeng (牛步生 - Student Ubo)

* * *

You've caught a cold, and your lodging is uncomfortable; won't recuperation just be hardship? The weather in mountainous regions changes drastically between midday and morning/evening, so please be careful about catching cold. By the way, what short-sightedness is this matter concerning XX (XX行云云)? I swear before heaven and before you, Ŭn-hŭi, so please trust (*) me and rest assured. Although my body may be weak, my mind troubled by many things, and my pockets empty, a man's spirit will not be frustrated and fail due to such obstacles. Let us discard (*) useless thoughts and strive to realize our plans. Let us strive only to live bravely and immersed in love. When a person advances towards their desired goal in life, just like a long stream flowing through a valley bends around rocks it encounters, disperses in sandy fields, forms ponds in deep places, and becomes waterfalls over layered cliffs and precipices, adapting to circumstances (隨機應酬) as it changes into myriad forms – isn't this the reason why life has change and joy (樂)? Let us embrace optimism as we pass through life.
My cute baby! Be a good girl, listen to what I say! And<120> be quiet/well-behaved! Okay! (お可愛いまい ぺ-ぴ! 宜子ながら おの云ふ事を聞け! そしておとなしくせよ! ね!)
September 3rd, Ubo Saeng (牛步生)

* * *

Older Sister! During the time I was recuperating at Sŏk'wangsa Temple (釋王寺) due to various troubles arising just when a loving partner was about to be considered, I received these letters from Ubo (牛步). How trustworthy, dependable, and sincere his gentle heart seemed as a protector! It was beyond comparison to the sort of young pups who chase after you saying they'll die for you or live for you, but more than that, I had always respected his character, and I trusted that his learning and position were sufficient for him to maintain his own dignity and possess a sense of responsibility. Thus, I sacrificed everything and, just as he said, decided to cling to his arm and spend my life together with him. However, I will not record the details here of the repeated tribulations that arose from that, which overturned and shook that gentle and soft human heart, ultimately causing our plans to crumble and our path to diverge. After he took another woman as his wife, unable to bear the agony, he wandered through Sŏsan (瑞山), T'aean (泰安), and Manchuria (滿洲), until June 20th of last year, when, at the age of 41, leaving behind this world full of regrets, he met a miserable end in a corner of a crumbling thatched house in Kungjŏngdong (宮井洞). Older Sister! My daughter Kyŏngnae (卿來), born as the fruit of this tragic history, turned six this year and entered Chukt'ae Public Common School (竹添公立普通學校). She is tall, has a soft voice, and resembles her father greatly. I have agonized endlessly to prevent the misfortune of her parents from extending into the innocent child's heart. Regarding the consciousness of a 'father,' I have strived entirely for normalcy. Even now, I still speak of other people's good mothers but forbid talk of other people's good fathers, teaching her that there is no one better than a mother in the world, no greater love than a mother's love, and that if only mother is present, all matters can be resolved. But my heart is often sorrowful. When she comes in holding her test paper, saying, "Mother, I got 100 points in arithmetic today!" my heart feels like it's breaking. When she comes rushing in from the middle gate, shouting, "Mother, look at my report card! I got honors! The teacher said I studied well and showed my report card to the other children!" tears pour from my eyes. Older Sister! How can I not be sad when I have no one with whom to share this joy, this delight?

Older Sister! When I think about it, life is truly futile, isn't it? In our bubble-like (泡影) existence, how much honor can we see, and how much humiliation can we endure, that we should grapple so tenaciously? That even a beautiful woman is but a skull (髑髏) beneath one layer of skin is a warning phrase from Tokyo's (東京) great monk, Ikkyū Zenji (一休禪師).<121> To rejoice when praised by others and frown when criticized; to put on silk clothes over a leather-skinned skull and be full of self-satisfaction; to envy the luxury of others; and from waking until falling asleep again, to spend one's time busily fussing over trivial matters (營營屑屑) – thinking about this, there seems to be no animal as shallow-hearted as a human. Therefore, a greatly enlightened person is transcendent even over life and death, unmoved by honor or disgrace, poverty or wealth, and even sheds the worldly emotions of joy, anger, sorrow, and pleasure. Thinking like this, isn't a person ultimately nothing more than a fleeting, deluded dream (一場의 迷夢)?

Older Sister! Did you read the article in the September issue of *Samch'ŏlli* under the title "New Women Widowed in Their Youth" (청춘에 과부된 신여성기), subtitled "Ms. Ch'oe Ŭn-hŭi Visits a Grave in the Autumn Wind" (秋風에 墓叅하는 崔恩喜女史)? Even if I had harbored resentment towards Ubo during his lifetime, once he became deceased, as a final act of sincerity to console his sorrowful spirit, I offered a wreath at his grave (露前) to express my wish for a final farewell. This fact, along with some misinformation – perhaps a rumor that I frequently visit the grave, and other stories about my children – was written there. Then one day, a threatening letter arrived, filled with all sorts of dreadful words: "While the deceased was ill for over a year, you, as a woman with a husband, committed adultery and bore an illegitimate child. *Samch'ŏlli* misreported this. However, I will remain silent about it, and in return, you must pay 300 won (金三百圓). If you refuse, not only will I bury you socially, but I will also directly and indirectly torment you and your beloved child, and bring misfortune upon the persons of you and your child." etc., etc. First, it demanded delivery at 9 PM on a certain date at the central stone altar in Sajik Park (사직공원). The second time, it demanded the money be left inside the memorial pavilion at Pagoda Park (빠고다공원) at 8 PM. The third time, it demanded a face-to-face handover at Ch'ŏngnyangsa Temple (淸凉寺) at 3 PM. The fourth time, it asked to meet at 7 PM on the main approach road (表叅道路) to the Chosŏn Shrine (朝鮮神宮). Furthermore, it instructed me not to speak of this to anyone else and to bring the money alone. I investigated through three different channels, but it was completely obscure (五里霧中), and I couldn't determine the culprit's identity, so I had no choice but to rely on the police authorities. When the criminal was finally arrested, I couldn't help but be astonished that it was a young man from the direct lineage of the X family (0氏家 - name likely anonymized), someone I had previously treated with goodwill and with whom mutual obligations should have been upheld. As a result of various special negotiations (特別交涉), the matter was resolved peacefully without a major incident erupting. However, I feel and must consider quite a lot about my own future path, having been subjected to unwarranted threats and seen as someone easy to push around. Even now, I recall pleading tearfully with Ubo the night before he brought Ms. Kim home, arguing that, thinking of a parent's responsibility towards a child, it would be proper to handle the procedures for me and my daughter – submitting the marriage registration (婚姻屆出), Kyŏngnae's birth registration (卿來의 出生屆), and divorce procedures simultaneously – before bringing the new person into the house.<122> Whether during his lifetime or after his death, if I were to marry another person or bear children, I doubt anyone could see this as any sort of crime, legally or morally. Older Sister, from the day Mr. X (氏 - referring to Ubo) took a new wife, out of spite from my wounded pride, I employed matchmakers (媒婆), asked senior colleagues and acquaintances, and even placed a personal ad seeking marriage (求婚廣告) in the *Tong-a Ilbo*. But perhaps that too was fated, as nothing came of it. Today, with my tense heart relaxed and the objects of my search having vanished, candidates abound instead – virtuous people, healthy people, learned people, widowers, divorcees. But now, my heart has decayed and decayed again; if it can become sufficient fertilizer to nurture and raise the new life that is my child, then no matter what hardships my body endures, my hopes will be fulfilled, and my life will be content. I end my tedious writing here. Older Sister, I pray and pray (祝手 또 祝手) that health and happiness may never leave you together.

Ŭlhae Year (1935), December 10th, Your younger sister Ŭn-hŭi respectfully writes.
This letter is addressed to Elder Brother K (K형) in Kando (間島).<123>

**Summary**

This poignant letter, written by Ch'oe Ŭn-hŭi to an unnamed older sister ("Hyŏngnim," likely a close female friend or relative addressed with a traditionally male term of respect) details her recent struggles, including a severe illness requiring surgery, her deep sense of loneliness despite having friends and a young son, and reflections on her past misfortunes. She recounts her complicated and ultimately tragic relationship with a man identified as "Ubo," including excerpts from his affectionate yet perhaps controlling letters, his eventual marriage to another woman, and his untimely death. Ch'oe also reveals the existence of their daughter, Kyŏngnae, and the challenges of raising her alone, shielding her from the painful past. A recent, deeply unsettling incident involves a blackmail attempt related to her past and a misinterpreted magazine article, which was fortunately resolved but left her feeling vulnerable. Despite past heartbreaks and current difficulties, she concludes with a sense of resignation and finds purpose in dedicating her life to her child.

From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into the personal lives and social pressures faced by educated "New Women" in colonial Korea. It touches upon changing relationship dynamics (engagement, love affairs outside marriage, divorce), the precariousness of women's social standing, the challenges of single motherhood, economic struggles (even for those with some means like a stamp selling permit), experiences with modern medicine, and the psychological toll of societal judgment and personal tragedy. The mention of specific locations (Wŏnsan, Seoul, Tokyo, Kando), institutions (*Chosŏn Ilbo*, *Tong-a Ilbo*, Chiyoda Life Insurance, Chosŏn Shrine), and social practices (bridesmaids, matchmaking, personal ads) provides concrete details about life in the 1930s. The blackmail incident highlights the vulnerability of women whose lives deviated from traditional norms.
