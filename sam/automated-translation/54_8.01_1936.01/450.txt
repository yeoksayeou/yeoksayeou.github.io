By the Bronze Brazier
						No Ch'ŏnmyŏng

Beneath the lo-ng wall, homeless children
huddle together, whimpering like puppies
each time the wind creeps in chillingly,
relying on each other's body heat (體溫).

On a night that freezes the bat's wings,
by the bronze brazier, the tale of two, mother and daughter (母女),
gathering and scattering the cold ashes, knows no sleep.

The night the son, his firmly set lips trembling,
swallowed his tears and left—the scene of that night
is painfully carved into the mother's heart.

On the night the year changes <294> the old mother
murmurs her son's name and sheds tears.
Since the young man left, this is the third such night—

Somewhere in a strange land, under the same indigo roof,
there must be a hurting heart, resenting the old home, yet unable to hate it,
torn by the sword of affection (情).<294-295>

**Summary**

This melancholic poem by No Ch'ŏnmyŏng depicts a mother and daughter spending a cold New Year's Eve by a bronze brazier, unable to sleep as the mother grieves for her absent son who left three years prior under painful circumstances. The scene contrasts the indoor sorrow with the external suffering of homeless children huddled against the cold. The final stanza suggests the son is also suffering in a "strange land," torn between resentment and longing for home. From a colonial history perspective, the poem offers a poignant glimpse into the hardships faced by Koreans, hinting at poverty (homeless children), family separation possibly due to economic migration or forced labor mobilization, and the emotional toll of displacement common during the Japanese colonial period.

청동화로가엔

기-ㄴ담 미테는 집 업는 아희들이
옹송그리고 누어 바람이 소스라치게 기여들 때마다

박쥐의 날개를 얼리는 밤
청동 화로ㅅ가엔 두 母女의 이야기가
찬 재를 모으며 흐트며 잠들 줄 모른다.

아들의 굿게 담운 입살이 떨리며
눈물을 삼키고 떠나든 밤- 그 밤의 광경이
어머니의 가슴엔 압흐게 색여젓다.

해가 밧귀는 밤 늙은 어머니는<294>

젊운이가 떠나고나서 이런 밤이 세 번째--

가튼 남빗집웅 밋 낫썬 땅 한 구석에서는
녯집을 원망하나 그러나 미워하지 못하는
