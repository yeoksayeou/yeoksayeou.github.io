**Body Text Translation:**
The Go-Stop Bus Girls' School, An Army of 104 Women Active on the Streets, Seeking the Street Girls' School 3

On the afternoon of the 10th, at 3 o'clock, I set out to find the bus girls' school, where they cheerfully shout "Go-, Stop-, Alright!"
The cold of this winter day, attacking mercilessly, seemed to seep even into the cellular tissue of a person's body.
One could easily guess the severity of the cold<164> just by seeing how it unattractively ruined the nicely made-up faces of the young ladies passing on the street.
Probably anyone who has sat on a streetcar or bus would have heard them. Dressed in grey uniforms and caps, with armbands reading "Guidance" (案內), a small bag tied around their waists, a bright red necktie around their necks, standing by the door and calling out:
"Tsugi wa Angoku-tō de gozaimasu. Oriru kata wa mae kara orite kudasai."
(Next is Anguk-tong. Those getting off, please exit from the front.)
"Naka ga suite imasukara sukoshi mae no hō e tsumete kudasai."
(There's space inside, please move a little further forward!)
"Hanahada sumimasen ga kippu o kiranai kata wa kippu o kitte kudasai."
(Terribly sorry, but those who haven't punched their tickets, please punch them!)
So, under what rules do they work, how much work do they do, and how much pay do they receive? Also, what kind of feelings do they experience day to day? I will now describe their situation.

I caught a streetcar in front of Hwasin Department Store and headed to the bus girls' school located on the wide grounds of the training center east of Seoul. This truly was a street girls' school. Just a few carelessly made benches were scattered here and there in a haphazardly built structure. It gave me a very bleak feeling. Most were out on the streets, with only one or two bus girls slumped over as if unable to bear the fatigue. I turned back and knocked on the door of the Tram Division office inside Tongdaemun Gate. It was just quitting time, and everyone was coming out, putting on their overcoats. I handed my business card to the receptionist and requested an interview. The office boy said, "The general affairs office is on the second floor. Let's go together," and guided me there. As I reached the doorway, there was no sign of anyone, only the slender voice of a woman answering the phone striking my auditory nerves.

104 Young Women in Uniform Caps and Uniforms

Opening the door, I found Ms. M from Mudŭng<165> (or possibly: a pile of papers). She was hastily tidying something up. When the office boy passed her my card, she brought out a chair and asked me to sit. After exchanging greetings, I lit a cigarette and ventured a question, which she readily answered.
"Me": Sorry to bother you when you're busy, but how many female conductors are there in total right now?
"Her": There are 104 in total.
"Me": Mostly, where are they from?
"Her": They are all from Kyŏnggi Province.
"Me": What are their ages?
"Her": Most are around 17 years old.
"Me": Are there any married women among them?
"Her": Not a single one. We don't hire married women in the first place.
"Me": Are the uniforms and caps provided by the company?
"Her": Yes, that's right.
"Me": What happens if they get injured on the streetcar?
"Her": They are immediately treated. The treatment costs are, of course, paid by the company. And even if they cannot come to work for several days due to injury, their salary is paid as usual.
"Me": Are there any facilities specifically established for the female conductors?
"Her": There isn't anything specifically established yet. After taking over from the Prefecture (Keijō-fu), we just set up a temporary lodging (임시喆所) at the training center, but it will be relocated to a newly constructed place shortly. Maybe things will change then, who knows...
"Me": Aren't there things like moral training, strengthening sessions, or recreational movie gatherings for the female conductors?
"Her": There are no recreational movie gatherings, but there are monthly mental and moral training sessions. Also, from time to time, there's a large athletic meet for all employees.
"Me": What happens if, perhaps, a love relationship develops between male and female conductors?
"Her": There haven't been any such cases yet. And there's no definite internal regulation on how to handle it. I suppose we'd deal with it appropriately based on the circumstances at the time.

Highest Monthly Pay 40 Wŏn, Lowest 18 Wŏn

"Me": What are the working hours?
"Her": There are morning and afternoon shifts. The morning shift starts at 7 AM and works until 2:30 PM, and the afternoon shift starts at 2 PM and works until 9 PM.
"Me": What about the monthly salary?
"Her": The highest, including housing allowance, is 40 wŏn, and the lowest, including housing allowance, is 18 wŏn.
"Me": What about bonuses (賞與金)?
"Her": We give 40割 (400%), that is, 4 times the monthly salary. However, since they originally come from poor families, they seem to be greatly struggling for money.
"Me": What kind of punishment is given if it's discovered that they are unkind to passengers on the streetcar or bus, or let someone ride for free?<166>
"M": That depends on the severity, but if it's recognized as excessively serious, even considering the person's situation, meaning they might become unable to make a living if they quit, we declare their dismissal.
"Me": Well, thank you for your time. That's all for today.
"M": Goodbye.
Exchanging these farewells, I left the Tram Division office; the sun had already set in the west.

I caught a streetcar heading for Sŏdaemun, went around Chongno, and arrived at the bus girls' station (詰所) in a corner of Pukch'on. Six or seven of them were gathered around the stove, whispering about something unknown. I wanted to probe their feelings and start a conversation, but since it was a 'street girls' school,' I didn't dare speak up in a place where so many people awkwardly came and went. However, having come all this way, I couldn't leave without asking at least a few words. Taking advantage of a quiet moment, I obtained the supervisor's consent and quickly began asking a few questions.

A Question-and-Answer Session with the Complex and Multifaceted Female Conductors

"Me": Isn't this daily life distressing?
"Woman": It's truly distressing. Especially being hassled by hundreds of people every day, could it be more distressing?
"Me": Don't you ever have unpleasant experiences with male passengers on the streetcar or bus?
"Woman": How could we not? We experience it several times a day. There are troublesome passengers who are dead drunk, don't punch their tickets, and just tease (hiya-kashi 冷やかし) us relentlessly. Or people who speak rudely using informal language (panmal). Is that all? Middle school students, for instance, often make life difficult for us with needless comments.
"Me": When that happens, why don't you just snap back at them?
"Woman": If they take it the wrong way, we often just end up getting cursed out thoroughly. We just pretend not to hear. Among them, the worst are the middle school students. They say things like 'she's ugly,' 'her butt is big,' or 'that red necktie is stylish' – it's dumbfounding sometimes.
"Me": When do you feel most troubled on the bus or streetcar?
"Woman": There are many troubling situations. It's difficult when passengers, like those just arrived from the countryside, repeatedly ask how to get somewhere. It's also difficult when disabled (病身) or elderly people linger for a long time without getting off quickly.
"Me": Since you encounter countless people every day, you must see all sorts of individuals.
"Woman": That's right. When it comes to people-watching, we're second to none.<167>
"Me": Could you tell me about any particularly memorable impressions you've had of people?
"Woman": There are so many, but generally speaking, when I see people dressed in expensive clothes (갑가는 의복) and showing off, I feel envious, but at the same time, I can't help feeling all sorts of discontent rising in a corner of my heart. And when I see passengers who look poorly clothed and marked by poverty, I can't suppress an uncontrollable sense of sorrow.
"Me": What are your plans for marriage in the future?
"Woman": That's something one has to wait and see, so I can't say for sure. But even though this is an era where people are blinded by grand houses (高大廣室) and luxurious living (錦衣玉食), willingly even becoming someone's concubine, I intend to choose a partner with whom I can join hands (손맛 붓잡고) and seek our bread (빵) together on the front lines of life (생활전선).
"Me": Have you ever received things like love letters?
"Woman": Occasionally. It's relatively rare from gentlemen passengers, but sometimes students follow us home late in the evening, find out our address, and send letters simply addressed to 'Miss Bus Girl (빠스껄殿) at such-and-such address.'
And one funny thing is, there are many embarrassing and funny incidents on the streetcar or bus. People slyly step on your foot (발둥을 발는다든가), jostle (떼민다든가) you, or lean their body against yours while feigning innocence (싯침이 뚝따고) and pretending to look somewhere far off. I don't know why they do that.
"Me": Perhaps that's human instinct that cannot be deceived? - I provoked.
"Woman": Well... - was her only reply, with no further explanation. I said my farewells, expressing apologies for the imposition, and turned my steps towards Anguk-tong. It was already 7 PM. I hurried my steps and returned to my boarding house.

I have now sought out the 'street girls' school' three times. Each time I see them, I am greatly moved. My feeling after meeting these bus girls is somehow unnatural.

Is it because they are burdened by the exhausting work of engaging in vehicle (구르마) operation, which is unsuitable for women? An expression of being unable to bear the fatigue truly flows from them. Indeed, jostling (업치락뒤치락) among countless people, they must have sighed secret sighs several times a day.

How must their suffering have been during times when the cold pierced to the bone, or when it was so hot that sweat flowed freely? Or what about the displeasure (불캐함) when receiving the ignorant teasing (히야가시) of foolish men who think of women only as playthings (롱낙물)? Oh, Lord of Great Mercy and Compassion (大慈大悲)! Please grant happiness in the future as sweetness after bitterness (苦盡甘來) to those female conductors (女車掌 저씨) experiencing hardship (골난) in this world, on this land! - Thus I prayed.<168>

**Summary**

This 1936 article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine reports on the lives of female bus and streetcar conductors in Seoul (Keijō). The reporter visits their rudimentary training facility ("bus girls' school") and interviews a supervisor at the Tram Division office and several conductors themselves. The article details their working conditions: 104 young women, mostly unmarried 17-year-olds from Kyŏnggi Province, working morning or afternoon shifts for a monthly salary between 18 and 40 wŏn (plus housing allowance and a large annual bonus). They face hardships like harsh weather, long hours, dealing with difficult passengers (especially drunk men and teasing students), and the physical/emotional toll of the job. The conductors express feelings of distress, envy towards the wealthy, pity for the poor, and hopes for a future marriage based on partnership rather than wealth, alongside recounting incidents of harassment. The reporter concludes with a sympathetic prayer for their well-being.

From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into female labor in colonial Korea, particularly in modern, urban occupations. It highlights the socio-economic background of these women (often poor), the specific rules governing their employment (e.g., prohibition on marriage), their wages relative to the cost of living (implied by their struggles despite bonuses), and the daily social interactions and challenges they faced, including sexual harassment and class consciousness. The mix of Korean and Japanese language (including direct quotes of announcements in Japanese) reflects the linguistic reality of colonial Seoul.
