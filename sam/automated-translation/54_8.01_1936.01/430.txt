學士
						白信愛
Writer Sketch

The lady writer has long been renowned as a short story author. Her hometown is Panyawŏl (半夜月) in North Kyŏngsang Province (慶北). After finishing Taegu Girls' High School (大邱女高), she studied abroad in Tōkyō (東京). Currently, with her husband (夫君), she operates an orchard (果樹園) in the aforementioned Panyawŏl countryside (田園), while diligently pursuing the path of creative writing (創作). Age 25 (春秋).

Yi Pyŏnghwan (李炳煥) is an economics graduate (經濟學士) from W University.
In his late father's time, they used to harvest two hundred sacks of rice, but all he and his brother inherited was one large house and some worn-out furniture.
Therefore, from his university undergraduate years onward, he had to work his way through school (苦學). He received assistance from well-off friends, did manual labor (勞動), and occasionally his older brother sent some money, but his university life was miserable; it was truly a struggle just to remain a student.
When only a year remained before graduation, his older brother, burdened with their elderly mother (母親) and young children (子女), was in such dire straits that he couldn't even afford consistent meals. He would occasionally write letters to Pyŏnghwan lamenting his difficult circumstances and his powerlessness (無力) to provide even a small amount for tuition. Whenever Pyŏnghwan received such letters,<280> he felt indescribable anxiety and frustration.
He believed that once he graduated from university, all his family's misfortunes and suffering would instantly disappear. It wasn't that he had a definite job lined up after graduation, nor did he have any specific prospects for employment, but
"Surely, once I graduate..."
It was a vague thought, perhaps absurdly groundless, but for Pyŏnghwan, it was a hope many times sweeter than having a confirmed post-graduation plan.
"Once I graduate..."
Thinking this filled him with soaring courage (勇氣). He felt there was no one in the world to envy, and that once he graduated, no one could overcome him in any endeavor.
When he thought like this, all his ideals (理想) seemed set to be realized from graduation day, making him want to shout for time to rush forward and bring that day quickly.
However, time seemed to crawl as if cursing Pyŏnghwan, and the letters from his brother, filled with poverty, suffering, and tears, only became more frequent. He endlessly blamed time, the cause of all his family's misfortune, for not bringing the graduation day that would deliver happiness to them all.
When misfortune strikes, people tend to identify a cause for their unhappiness and find self-consolation, satisfaction, and maintain face by lamenting this cause before others; few try to overcome the lamentable source of their misfortune. Pyŏnghwan, too, whenever he read his brother's letters, felt his heart break and would pour out his resentment towards time in his replies.
Receiving these letters, Pyŏnghwan's brother would sigh,
"Hmph, you are fortunate to still have something to resent. How will you endure the misfortune later, when you wish to resent but have nothing to blame!"

As the long-awaited graduation day approached, Pyŏnghwan promptly had business cards printed: Yi Pyŏnghwan, Bachelor of Economics. His brother, through some means—who knows how he managed it—sent forty won. With that money, he had a spring suit (洋服) tailored, carefully packed his graduation photo (寫眞) in a box, and hurried back to his hometown, A. Though no one came to meet him, he stepped lively off the train at the hometown station.<281>
Entering his house, he frowned involuntarily. His elderly mother, his brother dressed in indescribably shabby clothes, his nieces and nephews swarming like a pack of beggars, and especially his sister-in-law's (兄嫂) grime-streaked face—all of it struck him less with pity and more with a sense of displeasure.
To think that these were the people welcoming him today, upon his triumphant return (今之還鄕) after five long years of arduous study abroad (客窓 螢雪之功), made him sigh despite himself.
Sitting down to the evening meal (夕食床), his nieces and nephews, glancing furtively at their mother, stared at Pyŏnghwan's food. The older child swallowed hard, while the younger one whined, "I want white rice too!" Pyŏnghwan couldn't swallow his food.
With a heavy heart, he went out into the street, but the faces of the people who looked upon him, returned after his arduous studies, were all expressionless and cold; they didn't recognize him as a university graduate. No one even blinked at his smart, newly tailored suit.
"Hey—isn't that Hyŏngsik?"
He suddenly spotted an old friend and greeted him energetically.
"Ah? Is that Pyŏnghwan? When did you get back?"
The friend grasped Pyŏnghwan's hand warmly.
"I just got back today."
"Oh? When are you leaving again?"
"I just graduated, so..."
"Oh—is that so? Congratulations! By the way, have you found a job somewhere?"
"..."
Pyŏnghwan felt as if he'd been shot, momentarily stunned and unable to reply quickly.
"Competition is fierce, so you should find a job somewhere quickly."
His friend said this.
"Well, I suppose finding a job shouldn't be too hard..."
He spoke nonchalantly, feeling a degree of superiority over his friend.
"Since you've graduated from university, finding a job itself won't be difficult, but you have quite a burden, you know."
"What burden do I have?"
"If you say you don't, that's fine, but your older brother doesn't have any particular skills..."
At his friend's words, Pyŏnghwan's pride felt utterly crushed. The image of his family's wretched state <282> flashed before his eyes.
"So everyone knows how our family has fallen,"
The thought struck him, making further conversation with his friend unpleasant.
"Let's meet again soon. I have somewhere to be right now!"
He parted ways with his friend.
He then headed to the house of his maternal cousin sister. This cousin, a graduate of a girls' high school, was married to a bank employee and living a comfortable, cultured life.
"Aigo, Oppa, welcome! Congratulations! You're a scholar (學士님) now..."
Only a year apart in age and having grown up together, they were as close as friends. His cousin greeted him warmly, mixing in jokes.
"So, have you been well? Where's your husband (主人)?"
He entered the room, brightly lit by an electric lamp (電燈).
"Oppa, since you'll be living here now, will you stay at your older brother's place?"
This question from his cousin pleased Pyŏnghwan. He interpreted it as asking how someone like him, who had been in a glamorous place like Tōkyō and, moreover, graduated from the highest level of education, a dignified young gentleman, could possibly live in such a squalid house.
"Yes, really, why has the household ended up in such a state...?"
He said, as if blaming his brother's lack of resourcefulness (變通).
"That's what I mean. Big Oppa is a bit too mild-mannered, it's hopeless. How are you going to bear that burden all by yourself in the future, Oppa?"
His cousin seemed to understand him perfectly, and everything she said resonated with him.
"It's hopeless. Somehow, I have to take responsibility (責任)! But don't worry about such things!"
The courage he had mustered, thinking "Once I graduate...", was still intact, so he answered lightly.
"Aigo, really, if you want to live on a salary (月給生活), there's so much unpleasantness. Oppa, how will you endure it in the future?"
"Is there really a need to become a salaried worker?"
He laughed brightly. It seemed childish to him that his cousin assumed he would just become a salaried worker. His dream was, at the very least, to be a young entrepreneur (靑年實業家).
"If there's a good job other than being a salaried worker, how wonderful would that be. Oppa, you don't plan on working as a salaried employee?"<283>
"Even among salaried workers, there are ranks (階級), so it's not that I absolutely refuse,"
He thought that his cousin's husband, being only a commercial school (商業學校) graduate, might face unpleasantness, but someone like himself, a university graduate, wouldn't likely work under others, so there would be no cause for unpleasantness.
However, his sensitive (敏感) cousin seemed slightly displeased (不快) by Pyŏnghwan's remark.
"Of course, there are ranks among salaried workers, but do they give you such a good position right from the start?"
She retorted (應酬).
"Oh, Oppa, have you thought about getting married (結婚)?"
Worried that her retort might have been too harsh, she quickly changed the subject and prepared to pour black tea.
"Marriage? Well..."
Pyŏnghwan was also glad the topic changed and replied readily, smiling.
"There are many pretty girls, but would they suit Oppa's taste!"
"Well, I really don't know what kind of girl is good."
It struck Pyŏnghwan as strange that he had never once concretely considered this important issue until now; it felt like one of the indispensable, urgent, and critical matters for him at present.
"Shall I arrange a match for you?"
His cousin smiled sweetly, placing a teacup before Pyŏnghwan.
"First, I need to know Oppa's ideals regarding marriage."
She opened a box of sweets.
"Well... I haven't had the leisure to think about such things yet."
"Then shall I guess? First, she must be an intellectual woman (인테리-女性). Second, she must have a gentle face. Third..."
His cousin laughed and stopped.
She had intended to say "daughter of a rich family," but stopped, laughing, to protect Pyŏnghwan's pride (自尊心). Pyŏnghwan didn't catch the implied meaning of the third condition.
"I don't know. Anyway, if she's about as good as you in all respects, that would be sufficient (十分)."
He lifted his teacup.
"Aigo, heavens no. Girls like me are a dime a dozen."
"If there are so many, try arranging one for me."<284>
"But—Oppa, if you're getting married, what's the one condition that cannot be omitted, the most important one?"
"The primary condition... Well, I don't know."
"If you like the person, does it matter at all if her social status is low or if she's poor?"
The clever cousin had already guessed that this would be the biggest condition for Pyŏnghwan's marriage, but she asked subtly, careful not to offend his ears.
"What does low status matter? Moreover, how could poverty be an issue? I never wanted a woman from a rich family in the first place."
"..."
The cousin found Pyŏnghwan's answer both childish and pitiful.
"Women from rich families are arrogant..."
Pyŏnghwan failed to perceive his cousin's inner thoughts.
"You mean you dislike women from rich families because they are arrogant?"
"Not only arrogant, but according to my friend, they try to control their husbands completely. Besides, I can't stand arrogant women."
"Aigo, Oppa, does that make sense? If a woman is arrogant and tries to dominate her husband, can that really happen? It all depends on the husband. Where would you find a fool who gets controlled by a woman? And no matter how arrogant she is, can any woman be more so than a man?"
"That might be true in theory (理論), but if she were truly just arrogant, you couldn't even kill her, it would be maddening. Anyway, a woman should be womanly, gentle, and modest!"
"Ah-ni, Oppa, your thinking is completely outdated!"
"Ah-ni, are you also seeking gender equality (男女同等)?"
"Heavens no, not equality, but..."
His cousin thought,
"Well, let's see. If he doesn't find a job soon and suffers a bit in that household, he'll learn. Eventually, he'll be scrambling to marry into a rich family."
She suppressed the words that nearly escaped her lips (立*) and, deciding this topic wasn't worth pursuing further, didn't continue.

"Wouldn't you like to work at the county office (郡廳)?" A few days later, Pyŏnghwan's brother asked with a troubled face.
"At the county office?"
"Yes."<285>
"The county office..."
He repeated "county office" as if disgusted, then looked at his brother.
"Is there a good position?"
"It probably won't meet your expectations, but what can we do? Given the family situation, you need to get a job first."
The brother's tone was almost pleading.
"Roughly how much is the salary?"
Pyŏnghwan asked eagerly. He was curious (好奇心) to know how much he, an economics graduate, would be valued (評價) if he entered salaried life.
"It should be about forty won. This is special because you're a university graduate."
His brother hesitated before stating the amount plainly.
"What? Forty won... Hahaha!"
He laughed heartily, as if finding it absurd.
He had always looked down on those fellows who, having barely graduated from ordinary school (普通學校), worked at the county office for decades and then went around acting important as county clerks (郡主事), considering them pitiful creatures (未物). The thought that he, today, was being offered a job for forty won, forcing him to breathe the same air and become a colleague (同僚) with them, felt like a truly senseless joke (精神없는 익살) from his brother. He laughed again, several times in succession.
"But if you turn down this position, it will be really difficult."
His brother, feeling frustrated by his seemingly naive younger sibling, tried hard to get his consent.
However, Pyŏnghwan simply said,
"I really can't bring myself to do it."
He politely brushed aside (一蹴) his brother's suggestion.
After that, Pyŏnghwan wrote letters to friends requesting help finding a job, and his brother desperately made efforts. As a result, three or four salaried positions became available, but none were any better than the initial county clerk (高員) position.
"Forty won..."
This seemed to be Pyŏnghwan's fixed price (定價), and he couldn't bear the insult (侮辱).
His brother, understanding his younger sibling's feelings well, found his naivety pitiful, frustrating, and even somewhat laughable. Yet, he didn't rest, throwing himself into finding one position after another. During his student days, Pyŏnghwan had pitied his brother for being unable to support even one family, deeming him incompetent (無變通) and pathetic. But now, seeing his brother working so furiously (猛烈) on his behalf <286> to find him employment, he couldn't help but be newly surprised (驚).
"Honestly, in present-day Korea (朝鮮), even if you graduated from the very pinnacle of university ancestors, there's no place that will just hand you a position like company director, county magistrate (郡守), or police chief (署長), saying 'We've been waiting for you.' You need to make up your mind to take a job."
He tried every possible argument, hoping for consent.
"But it's just too unfair and demeaning! How can I!" Pyŏnghwan remained consistently stubborn.
"I know, but firstly, the family situation is dire, so you should take whatever you can find for now and wait for opportunities and better luck later."
"That's not right. No matter how temporary it is or how casually one takes a job, once employed, that person's value is already assessed. And compared to being unemployed, the chances of finding a better position while already working decrease. Primarily, if someone needs a person and wants to recruit me, wouldn't it be more advantageous if I were unemployed rather than employed? Also, can our household survive on forty won? Even if it's a bit difficult, we need to start with a good position."
Pyŏnghwan's reasons for not wanting to take a forty-won job gradually changed. Now, the forty-won salary no longer made him laugh in disbelief, nor did he talk much about the indignity of being colleagues with ordinary school graduates. It took a long time for him to become somewhat more calculating (打算的) and pragmatic.
That spring, summer, and autumn passed, and as winter approached, the life of Pyŏnghwan's family became desperately difficult. With no income, they survived on money his brother obtained from old friends and by shamelessly scrounging like a vagrant. Skipping meals occasionally became commonplace. The job offers presented endlessly before Pyŏnghwan weren't inexhaustible either.
"This is frustrating... maybe even for forty won..."
But when he thought this, even scraping barren fields wouldn't easily yield a position.
Through spring, summer, and autumn, he somehow managed to get by (出入) with the spring suit he had tailored upon graduation. But when winter suddenly arrived, Pyŏnghwan had no choice but to stay confined indoors. His student uniform worn in Tōkyō (冬*) had been generously given away to a friend working his way through school upon his return home,<287> and his only suit was for spring. So he lay huddled (드러백혓다가) in the cold room all day, only managing to glimpse the outside world (門* 구경) by borrowing his brother's overcoat (두루맥이) when he came home.
"How can things be like this?"
He wandered around visiting friends, a bitter, cold smile (冷笑) on his cheeks, without even a scarf.
Though he hadn't held any money in his hands for nearly a year since graduation, his consumption of alcohol (酒) and tobacco (煙草) had increased dramatically.
"That damned brat, does he think graduating university makes him the best? Why does he hang around other people's guest rooms day and night doing nothing, dragging perfectly fine sons off to restaurants (料理집), driving them crazy?"
The wives and mothers of his friends all came to detest and curse Pyŏnghwan, but he himself remained blissfully unaware of their animosity. He believed that although he was in dire straits, his company must surely be, if not an honor, at least not unpleasant or unwelcome to his friends who had money but hadn't even finished middle school. Thus, despite his shabby appearance, he even developed a habit of taking the seat of honor (上座) when going to restaurants with friends. Even when tagging along uninvited (不請客) with friends heading to restaurants, he gradually lost his sense of propriety.
The pretense (體面) of saying, "I feel bad always getting free drinks. If I had money, I'd treat you too," gradually faded. His self-respect (自尊心), sense of superiority (優越感), and self-control (抑制心) vanished somewhere, replaced by an increasing tolerance for alcohol fueled by unwelcome free drinks. As meals became scarcer at home, his foremost thought, overriding job hunting or anything else, became:
"Isn't someone treating somewhere?"
He would scout around friends' houses, sometimes coaxing, sometimes predicting, sometimes forcibly arranging a treat, which became a daily routine. However, occasionally, after forcing an excessive amount of free drinks, he would return home and think:
"Huh? Am I really any better than a beggar (乞食)?"
His chest would tighten, but finding himself unable to change his circumstances, he could only sigh in frustration (鬱憤) before giving up.
Within a few months of Yi Pyŏnghwan, the dignified future young entrepreneur and university graduate, descending into the life of an upper-class vagrant (高等浮浪者) or loafer (룬팬), all his so-called friends began to shudder at the thought of encountering him.
When he learned that his friends dreaded meeting him and held gatherings (會合) or went to restaurants secretly, <288> he didn't reflect or reproach himself (反省, 自責), thinking, "Have I really become such a pathetic person?" Instead, he would curse them:
"Damn them! Just you wait! I'll get this treat out of you one way or another. No matter how arrogant you act, if you stood naked, neither the world's elite (大慰) nor even the kisaengs (妓生) would prefer you bastards. It's only because you have money, and use that money to adorn yourselves well (裝飾), that your bodies are considered the best. That's all it is."
He muttered this inwardly. By now, Pyŏnghwan lacked conscience or self-respect, and correspondingly, his life grew more desperate day by day.
It wasn't just his friends and their wives and parents (父母) who despised and hated Pyŏnghwan; even his maternal cousin sister's attitude (大尉) towards him was now poles apart (天壤之差) from the day he first returned from Tōkyō. Nowadays, her greeting was essentially a sneer.
"Oppa, how can you keep playing around like this? You should have some shame (廉恥). Isn't it embarrassing for your older brother's family?"
She would nag him.
"Hey, I don't want to hear it. Do you think I choose to live like this?"
"Aigoo, what kind of world (世界) do you think this is?"
"I probably know a bit more about it than you, so don't worry. How about arranging a match for me?"
"Aha—Oppa, when I urged you before, you flatly refused..."
His cousin pursed her lips without hiding her feelings.
"Hey, I'm only saying this because it's you, without embarrassment, but isn't there a suitable girl (시악시) somewhere?"
Though Pyŏnghwan had become thick-skinned (鐵面皮), recalling how he had once vehemently insisted to his cousin that he wouldn't marry a rich woman, he couldn't bring himself (섭적) to ask directly for a match with a wealthy girl.
"A 'suitable' girl? You mean a woman who meets Oppa's ideals?"
"Rather than ideals, perhaps someone with a bit..."
Knowing his cousin's use of "suitable" implied "rich," he still felt awkward (간지러워) answering her deliberate probe and couldn't help but hesitate.
"Well—Oppa, you wouldn't want a rich girl even if you died..."
His cousin was relentless in her sarcasm.<289>
"Even a rich girl would be fine."
He said this with a serious expression (正色).
"Hahaha, Oppa, it's too late now. Right after you graduated (直後), there were many girls eager to marry you, lining up. But now, everyone knows you're just a bum (부랑자), a complete good-for-nothing loafer (건달건달이 상건달), and nobody wants to marry you."
His cousin delivered this piercingly sharp criticism (皮肉).
"Hahaha, is that what you think of me? Never mind that, how about an only daughter from a rich family..."
Though his cousin's stinging words pricked his heart somewhat, he couldn't just stand there feeling awkward (無聊), so he tried to pass it off as a joke (冗談).
"Are you serious, Oppa? Only daughters from rich families supposedly slap their husbands' cheeks!"
As if to show that no matter how his cousin tried to dissuade him, his determination (决心) to marry a rich girl was firm and unchanging (變), Pyŏnghwan smiled broadly.
"That's unlikely. If she hits me, I can hit back. Is that really an issue?"
"Now you seem to be becoming human, Oppa—. By the way, Oppa, will you listen to me for a moment?"
His cousin's demeanor changed completely (一變), and she began speaking seriously (正色).
"Oppa, believe it or not, I've been worrying every day about what to do with you. But, frankly speaking, the world now recognizes (認定) you as a vagrant. So, I'll suggest something good. Oppa, you might think you're the best, but what good is it if the world doesn't acknowledge you?"
What she proposed was for Pyŏnghwan to become a laborer (勞動者). Her husband was tied down with work and had no free time (餘暇), but she, being unemployed, had plenty. She had savings (貯蓄), land rented out to tenants (小作), and moreover, had leased about 15,000 p'yŏng of state-owned land (國有地) the previous year. They could plant fruit trees (果樹) there, grow other crops (農作物), and find plenty to do by trying various things. She asked if he was interested in working the soil with her.
"That sounds good too."
"Look here. If we plant fruit trees, there will be no income until they bear fruit, so we should also keep bees (蜜*) and raise silkworms (養蠶). Let's not hire other workers; let's do the labor (勞動) ourselves as much as possible."
She took out materials on fruit cultivation methods (果樹栽培法) she had researched, as if pulling them from storage.<290> Pyŏnghwan initially heard it as a joke, but as his cousin's words became increasingly earnest (眞儉), he couldn't help but think it over.
"Okay, that sounds good too. Let's do it."
Even when his cousin added emphatically,
"You have to become a thorough laborer! Like someone doing chores at another's house (남의 집 담사리)!"
He readily agreed,
"Okay, don't worry. I'll definitely try."
But inwardly, he thought,
"Am I some kind of agricultural school (農業學校) graduate?"
He found his cousin's earnestness (熱中), oblivious to practicality, amusing and naive (幼稚, 天眞). Moreover, he desperately wanted to nag her to arrange a marriage into a rich family, but her demeanor held a certain inviolable dignity that seemed to overwhelm him. The words wouldn't come out, and instead, he listened distractedly (귀 밖으로) to the detailed budget (子細한 豫算) for the farm plan (農場計劃), merely responding enthusiastically.
"But it's not like money will be earned in just a year or two. Won't it be too tedious?"
As the conversation neared its end, Pyŏnghwan couldn't hold back anymore and blurted this out. His cousin looked at him, startled, her expression gradually hardening.
"Ah-ni, Oppa, I thought you were listening to me, but were you just pretending to agree and mocking me all along?"
Her voice was thin yet forceful.
"No..."
Pyŏnghwan was taken aback by her expression and tone, which seemed tinged with pity, and waved his hand dismissively (取消).
"Oppa, you still need to suffer more."
She said no more. Feeling slightly awkward (無聊), Pyŏnghwan stood up and left.
"People naturally come to their senses when they suffer, but Oppa, the more he suffers, the less he overcomes it. He just collapses on the spot and degenerates without even realizing it."
His cousin lamented, wondering how she could make Pyŏnghwan realize his step-by-step descent into degradation (妥落). "The End"

**Summary**

Yi Pyŏnghwan, a proud economics graduate, returns home expecting opportunities but finds only poverty and disillusionment. His inflated sense of self-worth prevents him from accepting low-paying jobs, leading him down a path of unemployment, dependency on friends for food and drink, and social alienation. His initial dreams of success curdle into resentment and denial. Finally, his cousin proposes a joint farming venture requiring hard labor, but Pyŏnghwan, still clinging to unrealistic expectations and unwilling to truly humble himself, balks at the long-term commitment, revealing he hasn't learned from his hardship.

From a historical perspective, the story offers a poignant glimpse into the limited socioeconomic mobility for educated Koreans under Japanese colonial rule. Despite achieving higher education (a significant feat), Pyŏnghwan finds the promised rewards elusive, reflecting the systemic barriers and discrimination faced by Koreans. His psychological decline highlights the demoralizing effects of thwarted ambition and lack of opportunity in the colonial context, portraying the educated 'surplus man' phenomenon. The stark contrast between his pre-graduation hopes and post-graduation reality underscores the harsh socio-economic conditions of 1930s Korea.

기사형태	소설

學士
						白信愛
作家點描

女士는 短篇作家로써 일즉부터 令聞이 놉다. 故鄕은 慶北 半夜月이라 大邱女高를 마치고 東京에 留學. 現在 夫君과 함께 前記 半夜月이란 田園에서 果樹園을 經營하는 한편 創作의 길에 精進하고 잇다. 春秋 25.

리병환(李炳煥)은 W대학을 졸업한 경제학사(經濟學士)이다.
그의 선친 때는 이백석 추수는 하든 것인대 그들의 형제가 상속 받은 것은 크다란 집 한 채와 때묻은 가구뿐이엿다.
그럼으로 대학 본과부터는 고학(苦學)을 햇든 것이다 돈 잇는 친구의 보조도 밧고 또 로동도 햇고 잇따금 그 형이 얼마식 보내주기도 햇으나 그의 대학 생활은 처참하야 실로 억지의 학생생활을 햇든 것이다.
졸업을 앞으로 일년 밖에 남기지 안엇을 때는 그 형은 늙은 모친과 어린 자녀를 거나리고 끼도 이여 나가지 못할 형편이엿음으로 잇따금 병환의게 곤란한 자긔 형편과 얼마만이라도 학비를 보조해 주지 몯하는 무력함을 한탄하는 편지를 하는 것이엿다. 병환은 이러한 편지를<280> 받을 때마다 말할 수 업는 초조와 안타가움을 늣겻다.
대학을 졸업만 하고 나면 자긔 일가의 모-든 불행과 괴로움은 금시에 해소되고 말 것이라고 그는 믿엇다. 졸업 후에 할 일이 확정되여 잇는 것도 안이요, 또 취직이라도 할 무엇이 잇는 것도 아니엿으나
『설마 졸업만 하고나먼야』
하는 막연하다면 긔막히게 엉터리 업는 막연한 생각이엿으나 병환의게는 발서 졸업 후에 할 일이 확정되여 잇는 것보다 몃갑절 더 달콤한 희망이엿음으로
『졸업만 하면』
하고 생각하면 용긔가 충천하는 것 같엇다. 세상에 부러운 사람이 없고 엇더한 일이라도 졸업만 하고 나면 자긔를 이겨낼 사람이 없을 것 갓게도 생각되엿다.
이러한 생각을 하면 모-든 리상은 졸업하는 날부터 실현되는 것이니 세월이 어서 달음질하야 졸업날을 가저오라고 고함을 치고 십흔 것이엿다.
그러나 세월은 병환을 저주나 하듯이 더듸고 그 형의게서 오는 가난하고 괴로운 눈물의 편지만 도수가 자저젓다. 그는 자긔 일가족의게 모-든 행복을 가저오는 졸업할 날을 어서 가저오지 안는 세월이 자긔 일가족의 모-든 불행의 원인이라고 끝없이 한끝 세월만 원망하엿다.
불행하면 누구든지 자긔를 불행하게 한 원인이 잇고 이 원인을 사람들 앞에서 원망해 보임으로서 자위와 만족을 늣기며 체면유지를 하려는 것이라 그 원망스런 불행의 원인을 극복 식히려는 사람은 드물다. 병환이도 자긔 형의 편지를 볼 때마다 가삼이 미여지는 것 같어 세월만 가득 원망하야 편지답을 써보내는 것이엿다.
이 편지를 바더보는 병환의 형은
『흥, 너는 아즉 원망할 대상이 잇으니 행복하구나, 나종에 원망하고저하나 할 대상이 없는 날의 그 불행을 엇더케 익여 나가려노!』
하고 한탄하는 것이엿다.
병환은 기달이고 바라든 졸업날이 닥처오자 곳 경제학사 리병환이란 명함을 박엇다. 그 형이 무슨 노릇을 하야 엇더케 구변해 낸 돈인지 사십원을 보내 주엇음으로 그것으로 봄양복 한 벌을 지어 입고 졸업사진을 상자에 곱게 간수해 가지고 불이야 불이야 고향인 A로 돌아왓다. 아무도 마중나와 주지도 안은 고향 정거장에 그는 활긔잇게 내려섯다.<281>
그는 자긔집에 들어서자 부지중에 눈살이 찝흐려젓다. 늙은 어머니, 말 못하게 초라한 옷을 입은 그 형, 거러지떼 같이 욱덕이는 족하 아희들, 더구나 그 형수의 고러부튼 얼골, 모다가 가엽다느니 보다 불쾌한 감이 앞을 서는 것이엿다.
길고 긴 오년 동안을 이 객창에서 영설의 공을 닥거 금의환향한 오날의 자긔를 마저주는 사람들이란 것이 모마 이 모양들이라고 생각함에 부지중에 한숨이 나오지 안을 수 없엇다.
저녁상을 받고 앉으니 족하 아이들이 자긔 어머니 눈치를 엿보아 가면서 병환의 상우를 바라보며 큰 아희는 침을 삼키고 적은 아희는 나도 이밥 달나고 칭칭대엿다. 병환은 그 밥이 넘어가지 안엇다.
답답한 가삼으로 거리로 나가 보앗으나 영설의 공을 닥고 돌아온 자긔를 바라보는 사람들의 얼골은 모다가 무표정하고 쌀쌀하야 대학출신인 자긔를 몰나보앗다. 스마-트한 그의 신조양복을 보고는 눈하나 크게 뜨는 사람이 없엇다.
『이요-형식군 아닌가』
그는 문득 눈앞에 낱아난 옛친구 한 사람의게 활긔잇는 인사를 건냇다.
『아? 병환군인가, 언재 귀향햇나』
그 친구는 반갑게 병황의 손을 잡엇다.
『오날 돌아왓내』
『응, 언재 또 가나?』
『인재 졸업햇으니까』
『오-그런가, 축하하내, 그런대 어대 취직구나 정햇나』
『...』
병환은 총쌀이나 맞은 것 같이 뜨끔하여저 얼넌 대답이 나오지 안엇다.
『경쟁이 심하니까 어서 어대 취직부터 해얄 것인대』
그 친구는 이럿케 말햇다.
『글새, 설마 취직 쯤이야』
그는 얼마만치 그 친구의게 우월감을 가지며 이럿케 걸님없이 말햇다.
『대학 졸업을 햇으니까 취직 쯤이야 어러울 것 업지만 자네도 짐이 만으니까 말일새』
『내야 무슨 짐이 잇나?』
『업다면 그만이겟지만 자네 형님이 별 긔술이 없으니까』
친구의 이 말에 병환의 자존심은 여지없이 내려 백히는 것 같엇다. 그의 눈앞에 자긔집 식구의 지지한 꼴이<282> 떠오르며
『우리 집안이 이러케 된 줄 몰으는 사람이 업구나』
하는 생각이 번적하야 그 친구와 더 말하고 섯기가 불쾌햇음으로
『또 천천이 만나세, 지금 좀 가볼 떼가 잇엇어!』
하고 그 친구와 갈너젓다.

하든 체면도 차차 살어지고 자존심도 우월감도 억제심도 어대로 달아낫는지 턱업고, 미움밧는 공술에 공연히 주량만 느리게 되엿다. 그의 집에서 끼를 자조 굼게 됨을 따라 그는 취직보다, 무엇보다 제일 압서는 문제는
『어데서 누가 한턱하지 안나』
하는 생각이엿음으로 이 친구 저 친구 집을 엿보다가 혹은 권하고 혹은 예언하듯, 혹은 억지로라도 한턱을 식혀 우려먹기도 일수가 되엿다.  그러나 그도 잇따금, 너머 억지의 술을 어더먹고 돌아오면
『허? 이거야 참 거러지에 질 배가 잇나』
하고 가삼이 앞을 때도 잇으나, 그러타고 엇더케 할 수도 업는 사정이라, 울분하야 한숨만 짓다마는 것이엿다.
대학출신인 당당한 장래 청년실업가가 될 리병환이가 고등부랑자 룬팬으로 진출하게 된 지 몃 달이 못되여 그의 친구라는 친구는 모조리 서로 마조칠가 몸서리를 내게까지 되엿다.
친구들이 그를 만날가 울겁을 대며 요리집엘 가든지<288> 무슨 회합을 하든지 하는 것을 알게되면,
『내가 이럿케까지 못난 놈이 되엿든가』
하고 반성이나, 자책은 할 생각이 업고 도로혀
『죽일 놈들, 어대보자, 긔어히 이 턱을 빼앗어 먹고는 말이라 네놈들이 아모리 건방거려도 빨가벗고 느려서서 보면 세상의 대위도 또는 긔생들까지라도 너의 놈들을 좃타고 하지는 안을 것이다. 오직 돈이 잇으니, 그 돈으로 몸을 잘 장식하고 잇는 까닭에 너의 몸을 제일로 녁이는 대 불과하다』
고 그는 가삼 속으로 중얼거리는 것이엿다. 지금의 병환의게는 량심이나 자존심을 가지지 몯한만큼 날날이 그 생활은 핍박하여 갓든 것이다.
병환을 멸시하고 미워하는 것은 오-직 그의 친구며 친구들의 안해, 부모들 뿐만 아니라 고종사촌 누의까지도 동경서 첨 나오든 날과는 대위가 첫재 천양지차로 달너젓다. 요즈음은 그를 대하면 비웃는 것이 인사가 되엿다.
『옵빠는 늘 그리고 놀어서 엇더케요, 좀 념치가 잇어야지. 첫재 큰 옵빠댁 보기 창피하지 안어요』
하고 복거대는 것이엿다.
『예, 듯기 실타, 낸들 이러고 잇기를 자원하는 줄 아늬』
『에이그, 지금 세상이 엇더한 세상이라구』
『너보다는 좀더 알고 잇을 터니 념려마라 어대 중매나 좀 하렴』
『아하- 옵빠두 내가 그럿케 권할 때는 바-루 안하겟다드니...』
누의는 감촘없이 입을 비숙거렷다.
『예 너니까 붓그럼없이 하는 말이다 마는 어대 그럴 듯한 시악시 업늬?』
임이 철면피가 된 병환이엿으나 자긔가 이 누의에게 돈잇는 녀자의게는 장가들지 안을여고 하늘갗이 벗퇴여 보엿든 때가 잇엇느니만큼 섭적 돈잇는 색시의게 중매하라는 말이 나오지 안엇다.
『그럴뜻-한 색시라니, 옵빠의 리상에 맛는 녀자 말슴이요?』
『리상보다, 좀』
그는 누의가 그럴 뜻-이란 말의 의미가 돈잇는-하는 말을 암시하는 것인 줄 알며서도 일부러 파고 물음에는 대답하기가 간지러워 머뭇거리지 안을 수 없엇다.
『저- 옵빠야 돈잇는 집 색시는 죽어도 원치 안을 터이고...』
누의는 어대까지든지 비꼬앗다.<289>
『돈잇는 집 색시라도 괜찬타』
그는 이럿케 정색을 하며 말을 하는 것이엿다.
『하하하, 옵빠두, 인저는 글넛어요, 졸업하고 나온 즉후엿드면 나도 너도 하고 시집오려든 색시가 많엇지만 이제는 고 부랑자요 건달건달이 상건달이라고 아모도 시집 안오려는대요』
누의는 침침끗같이 날카랍게 피육을 하엿다.
『허허허, 나를 그럿케 생각하나? 그리말고 돈잇는 집 외딸이나...』
그는 누의의 찌르는듯한 말이 가삼에 조곰도 자극되지 안는 바는 안이나 그럿타고 무류히 않었을 수도 없어 롱담같이 말을 붓치는 것이엿다.
『아리크나, 옵빠, 부자집 외딸은 남편의 빰을 막 친대요』
병환은 누의가 아모리 다잡드라도 자긔가 부자집 색시와 결혼할 결심은 이러케도 굿고 변하지 안는다는 듯이 싱글싱글 웃으며
『그럴리야 잇나, 치면 두들겨 맛기도 하지, 그까짓것 문제가 되나』
『인제는 옵빠도 사람이 되나보-, 그런대 옵빠 내말 좀 듯겟어요』
누의는 태도를 일변하야 정색하며 말을 끄냈다.
『옵빠, 나는 이래뵈도 날마다 옵빠를 엇더케 하나 하고 념려해왓서요, 그런대요, 옵빠는 지금 바른 말을 하면 부랑자로 세상이 인증하고 잇어요, 그러니까, 좋은 일을 하나 가르켜 들이겟어요, 옵빠는 옵빠가 제일인 것 갓지만 세상이 알어주지 안는대야 엇더케요』
하고 이약이하는 것은 병환으로 하여곰 로동자가 되라는 것이엿다. 자긔 남편은 매인 몸이라 여가가 업지만 자긔는 안해도 없으니 여가가 많이 잇는 까닭에 지금까지 저축한 돈도 잇고 소작으로 준 전지도 잇스며, 더구나, 지난 해에 국유지(國有地)를 일만오천여평 대부해 놓은 것이 잇으니 여긔에 과수(果樹)도 심그고 다른 롱작물도 지으며 일방 여러 가지로 애를 쓰면 할 닐이 많으니까 자긔와 갖이 흙 속에서 일할 생각이 업느냐 라는 것이엿다.
『그것도 좃치』
『이것보세요. 과수나무를 심으면 과실이 열 때까지는 아모 수입이 없을 터니 꿀벌(蜜*)도 먹이고, 양잠(養蠶)도 해야 되요. 다른 일꾼을 쓰지 말고 될 수 잇는 대로 두 사람이 로동합시다』
하며 과수 재배법을 연구한 적에서 잠대내놓등양 끄<290> 앗다. 병환은 처음은 롱담으로만 들엇든 것이 차차 진검(眞儉)해지는 누의의 말을 듯자 다소 생각하지 안을 수 없엇다.
『그래 그것도 좋다. 해보자』
하고 누의가
『아주 철저한 로동자가 되여야 해요. 남의 집 담사리처럼!』
하고 다짐을 하여도
『그래, 념려업다. 꼭 해보겟다』
하고 쾌히 응락하엿다, 그러나 속으로는
『내가 어대 롱업학교 출신인가』
하고 누의의 턱 모르고 열중하는 태도가 우습고 천진스러웟다. 그뿐 아니라 어서 돈잇는 집에 장가나 들게 중매하라고 졸느고 십흔 맘만 가득 하엿으나 그 누의의 태도에 어대인지 범할 수 업는 위엄이 자긔를 압도하는 것 같어 그 말은 입에서 나오지 안코 롱장게획에 대한 자자한 예산을 귀 밖으로 들으며 대답마는 열심히 하고 잇엇다.
『그럿치마는 일년이년에 돈이 벌어지는 것도 아닌대, 지루해서 하겟늬』
병환은 이약이가 거의 끗날 때 쯤하야 참다몯해 한마대 내놋코 말엇다, 누의는 놀난듯이 병환을 바라보며 그 표정이 점점 굳어지며,
『아-니 옵빠는 내 말을 들어주는 줄 알엇드니, 찬성하는 척하고 나를 놀닌 셈이서요?』
하고 말소리가 가늘며서도 힘있엇다.
『아니야...』
병환은 누의가 자긔를 가엽게 보는듯한 그 표정과 말에 일번 놀나며 취소하듯 손을 헌들엇다.
『아즉 옵빠는 더 고생을 해야겠어요』
하고 더 입을 열지 안엇다. 병환은 조곰 무류하야 앉엇다가 이러서 나왓다.
『사람이란 고생을 하면 자연 정신을 치리게 되는대 옵빠는 고생을 하면 할수록 그 고생에 익여나지 못하고 그 자리에 업더저 자긔도 몰으는 사이에 타락되고 마는 사람이야』
하고 누의는 생각함에 엇더케 해야 병환이가 한거럼한거름 타락해감을 뉘우치게 할 수 잇스랴, 하고 한탄하엿다. 『끝』
