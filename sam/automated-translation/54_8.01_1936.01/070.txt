Chronicle of Things Heard
Yŏ Unhyŏng Officiates Weddings for Fifty Couples

The "Wedding March."
Wherever the sound of this reed organ is heard, it is said that Mr. Yŏ Unhyŏng's refreshing face is almost always seen. Mr. Yŏ is famous for officiating many weddings. Exactly how many times a year does this gentleman help tie the knot for talented men and beautiful women, fulfilling their mandarin duck dreams?
Last year: 53 couples
This year: 57 couples
(As of November 24th)
Indeed, he officiates weddings roughly twice a month. This year alone, it's fifty-seven couples up to the twenty-ninth day of the eleventh lunar month, and he has already received reservations for about ten more places entering the twelfth lunar month, so it seems likely to exceed 70 couples this year. If it keeps increasing like this, next year will probably exceed 100 couples. How prosperous! Is he a wedding officiant incarnate?

Ch'unwŏn's "Way of the Husband"

Mr. Yi Kwangsu hurriedly gathered something over 10,000 wŏn. For a man of the pen, over 10,000 wŏn is a large sum. To raise this money, he sold land he had in Kahoe-dong, sold the copyrights to several novels, collected manuscript fees in advance, and even did radio broadcasts at the Broadcasting Station, pooling all the money together to make this huge sum. After securing the money, he selected and bought land in a place with clean air in Hyoja-dong, Seoul. The purpose is said to be to build a "Sanwon" (産院), a hospital<66> like birthing center, for his beloved wife, Madam Hŏ Yŏngsuk. Having thus bought the land and made all the arrangements to build the house, Ch'unwŏn reflected, saying, "As someone who merely wields a brush, I cannot reliably earn a steady living. Therefore, I put forth my sincere effort (誠力) to build a maternity clinic for my wife. This would fulfill the wish of my wife who studied medicine, and also stabilize our family's livelihood."

"Kim Kyŏngjae's Geography Study"

Mr. Kim Kyŏngjae, who was said to have headed to Manchukuo to search again for several hundred *ilgeng* (일경) of wasteland near the Russo-Manchurian border that he had bought from a Pole long ago with Yu Tongyŏl, first passed through Tumen and Yongjŏng, reached Xinjing (新京), then went via Jilin (吉林) and was heading north. In every large city, things had changed so much compared to that time. Though it was land he had wandered so much in the past, nowadays the familiar places were nowhere to be found, and he was reportedly just looking at maps, studying geography.

Ch'oe Sŏnik by Ch'oksŏngnu Pavilion

Mr. Ch'oe Sŏnik, who invested a huge sum of several hundred thousand wŏn前后 in the newspaper business and has now withdrawn even from the strenuous duties (劇務) at the Chungang Ilbo, quietly observing the general situation, took a trip in mid-December. Accompanied by several friends including Pak Tonsŏ, Ch'oe Mun'u, and Yi Yongsik, he started with Kwanghallu Pavilion in Namwŏn, visited Ch'oksŏngnu Pavilion in Poju (普州 - Jinju), tracing the footsteps of Ch'unhyang and Nongae. After leisurely strolling (逍遙) for a while, he returned to Seoul. Failing to find kindred spirits in the present time, seeking them far away in antiquity has meaning indeed.

Tosan Likely to Come to a Sanatorium

Tosan (島山), who is currently recuperating from a physical illness (身病) on the outskirts of P'yŏngyang, seems likely to come to Seoul next spring and enter the sanatorium (療養院) currently under construction outside Tongdaemun for a planned period of about three months. It is whispered among people that during this stay in the hospital, he will likely further study and grasp the "study of real Chosŏn" (現實朝鮮學). This is just relaying the whispers<67> among people.<66-67>

**Summary**

This article compiles short news items about prominent Korean figures in late 1935/early 1936. Yŏ Unhyŏng is noted for officiating a rapidly increasing number of weddings. Yi Kwangsu (Ch'unwŏn) has raised significant funds, including selling assets and advance fees, to build a maternity clinic for his wife, Hŏ Yŏngsuk, aiming to fulfill her wish and secure family income. Kim Kyŏngjae is searching in Manchukuo for previously acquired land near the Russian border, finding the region greatly changed. Ch'oe Sŏnik, having left the Chungang Ilbo, seeks solace by visiting historical sites in southern Korea. An Ch'angho (Tosan) is expected to move from P'yŏngyang to a new sanatorium near Seoul's Tongdaemun, where it's rumored he will study contemporary Korean affairs.

From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into the lives and concerns of Korean elites under Japanese rule. Yi Kwangsu's financial maneuvering highlights economic pressures even for famous writers. Kim Kyŏngjae's journey reflects Korean connections to Manchuria and the passage of time impacting land claims possibly related to earlier independence movement activities. Ch'oe Sŏnik's withdrawal and historical tourism could suggest disillusionment or the stifling atmosphere for Korean journalism. Most significantly, the mention of An Ch'angho's potential study of "real Chosŏn" (現實朝鮮學), even couched as rumor, hints at continued nationalist thought and engagement with contemporary issues despite illness and surveillance.
