Impressions of the President Giving His Inaugural Address
						O Yŏngsŏp, Philippine University

Dear Brother X,
On November 15th last, at 7:30 AM, I accompanied several friends to the plaza in front of the Philippine National Assembly Building to witness the Philippine Commonwealth Establishment Ceremony, an unprecedented grand event in Philippine history that stirred Manila, the city of eternal summer and host of the Far East Olympics.
By then, there were already 200,000 attendees. I quickly noted the schedule as follows:
November 15th
7:15 AM Doors Open
7:45 AM General Guests Seated in Special Seats in front of the Assembly Building
8:15 AM Governor-General, U.S. Secretary of War, President, and Vice President Seated
8:20 AM Ceremony Begins
8:40 AM President and Vice President Take Oath
8:45 AM Presidential Inaugural Address
9:00 AM Recess
9:30 AM Parade Begins
(New President moves to Malacañan Palace after the parade) <87>
4:00 PM National Assembly Convenes, Members and New Speaker Sworn In
9:30 PM Grand Congratulatory Ball held at Wallace Field Auditorium
This was the schedule that caught my eye. The joy of the Philippines on this day overflowed at the ceremony site. It felt as if the entire Philippine archipelago, consisting of 7,083 islands, was astir. The faces of Manila's 450,000 citizens were filled with vitality.
At the ceremonial stage where the new President Quezon and Vice President Osmeña, the two giants, would triumphantly ascend, the Philippine flag was hoisted on the right, and the American flag flew on the left. This main stand, mostly white with little other color, was packed. Even before the appointed time, it had literally turned into a sea of people. From the main road outside the venue to the golf course, people thronged with no more standing room. The scene was magnificent, with people clinging like grapes even onto the walls of the old fortress.
The ceremony began at 8:15 AM with the solemn entrance of Secretary of War Dern, President Quezon, and others.
After the invocation by Archbishop Gabriel M. Reyes of Cebu, dressed in purple vestments, at 8:28 AM, High Commissioner Frank Murphy ascended the platform amidst applause and cheers that seemed to shake heaven and earth. He introduced Secretary Dern, the special envoy of U.S. President Roosevelt. Secretary Dern delivered a powerful, lion's roar of a speech lasting about 20 minutes.
Following this, from 8:42 AM, Mr. Murphy read President Roosevelt's proclamation of Philippine independence (Commonwealth status). Quezon, Osmeña, and the members of the National Assembly were introduced. From 8:47 AM, the deeply significant oath-taking of President Quezon took place. Presided over by Chief Justice Ramón Avanceña of the Philippine Supreme Court, Mr. Quezon raised his right hand and took the oath. Everyone stood, unfolding a scene of solemn gravity.
At that very moment, the reverberating sound of a celebratory cannon salute drifted from the Fort Santiago (후오-도산자고) battery of the Army Headquarters, stirring the heartstrings. The solemn atmosphere surged like waves.
From 8:52 AM, Vice President Osmeña's oath-taking was held, similarly presided over by Chief Justice Avanceña. Secretary Dern rose again and, on behalf of President Roosevelt, declared that the old Philippine government hereby joyfully came to an end and the Commonwealth Government was formally established. Mr. Quezon, with a smiling face, ascended the rostrum and delivered a speech brimming with emotion. This speech, whether viewed from Quezon's own perspective or from that of Philippine history, was an unprecedented historical event, creating extraordinary tension in the venue. When he finished his approximately 25-minute speech and descended the platform, U.S. Vice President <88> Garner was the very first to stand and offer congratulations.
Thus, the inauguration of the President and Vice President concluded without any hindrance, leading to the emergence of a new Commonwealth in a corner of the East.

Dear Brother X,
After this ceremony ended, the congratulatory parade began. First came the procession of Philippine soldiers. Then followed the procession of tens of thousands of uniformed groups from various provinces, whose uniforms and colors were not identical. During this time, I observed various military flags and heard various military band tunes.
Truly, witnessing such a grand spectacle for the first time gave me, a young man, much stimulation (剌戟). Next was the procession of students from various schools, followed by groups from rural plains, mountains, and valleys across the Philippines. In this procession too, I glimpsed the unique clothing and cosmetic arts of different regions. Everything I saw was new. Following them was the procession of people from various countries residing in Manila. I saw people from about ten countries and observed all their behaviors and physical characteristics. Particularly, the women's attire, reminiscent of the feathered robes (羽老) of celestial maidens with its pure white men's-style outfit, was elegant clothing, so light and beautiful, scarcely found anywhere else in the world. This uniformly gorgeous attire is a custom from the Spanish colonial era, roughly the 16th-17th centuries, preserved in the Philippines, a land always warm, and developed to the progress seen today. The women of Manila, sashaying along in beautifully tailored skirts made of abaca fiber (麻紗), rustling their hems, wearing peculiar shoes – truly! truly! – evoked cries of admiration.
The scene on this day, with steps taken one after another to the tune of the clear and resonant (嚠喨한) military music that seemed to plunge Manila into a dream of peace and make it surge in a vibrant sea of activity, indeed created a feeling as if it were a paradise on earth. And interspersed throughout were automobile processions. This automobile parade was also quite a spectacle. They were decorated in various ways; one car had a large Filipino house built on it, and another carried a large steamship model. Besides these, there were countless cars decorated with indescribably interesting and amusing things. Particularly drawing everyone's attention were the beautifully adorned beauties from various nations residing in Manila riding in the cars – truly, they were like dazzling flowers in a garden (灼灼園中花). Following that, finally, was the procession of Japanese people from the mainland (內地人). This group specifically shouted three rousing cheers of "Banzai!" (만세삼창) right in front of where the President was seated. With this, the meaningful Philippine Commonwealth Establishment Ceremony concluded, leaving a deep impression on the 200,000 attendees.

Dear Brother X,
As a reflection after witnessing this splendid ceremony, I would like to briefly talk <89> about the old history of the Philippines.
The history of the Philippines is said to have been recorded starting from the arrival of the Spanish. Events before that are based on legends, the current distribution of races, archaeological research, and other sources, such as scant records concerning the Philippines found in Indian and Chinese history; thus, its history came to be. The aboriginal inhabitants were said to be small, black-skinned Negrito (네구리-도) people with tightly curled hair. This race is said to have left its traces not only in the Philippines but throughout the Malay Archipelago (馬來群島). The Igorot (이고로데族) and Ifugao (이후가오族) tribes currently in the northern mountains of Luzon Island (呂宋島) are said to be of Indonesian race (인도네사 人種). Thereafter, Malay people (馬來人種) are said to have migrated from various regions, bringing a different culture from that of the Majapahit era (마자-란도?). Particularly, the fact that people residing in the central islands of the Philippines are called Visayans (비사야人) proves that the Srivijaya (스리비사야族) people from the kingdom of Sumatra (스마도라), which flourished most actively from the 7th to the 13th century AD, migrated as far as the Philippines. Also, when the Spaniard Magellan discovered the Philippines, there were tribal names in various places. The origin (語源) of the word "barangay" (바란가이), which was used even later during the Spanish colonial period as the name for the smallest administrative division of a region, means "boat," indicating that people migrated by boat (船), settled in a specific area, formed a village (部落), and eventually shaped the Philippines of today.
Now, looking at the clothing and food of the Filipino people, men's attire consists mostly of pure white suits (세비토 - 背広?), with few wearing stand-up collars (쯔메 에리 - 詰襟). Those employed in labor such as tram/car crews, technicians, and factory workers use khaki-colored (가-기-色) clothing. Besides that, an upper garment similar to a collarless shirt (와이사쓰), called Barong Tagalog (바론다가로구), thought to have been popular during the Spanish era, is quite widely used. This is made of thin fabric woven from fine threads like abaca (麻) or pineapple fiber (빠이낫뿌루의類), and those with excellent colors or exquisite needlework (針裁) look so delicate (연연함니다) when worn that one might mistake the wearer for a woman. Women's clothing is mostly Western-style (洋裝), like in Europe and America.
Next, the staple foods are rice or corn, which they call "mais" (마이스), eaten as ground meal. This mais is a staple food in the Visayan Islands (비사얀 諸島) and the provinces of Cagayan (가기얀州).
Finally, one more thing I'll talk about is marriage. It's not arranged by parents or matchmakers; rather, a man and woman freely associate for a long time, and then the woman's consent is obtained. Once there's an understanding with the woman, a proposal (出願) is made to her mother. If she refuses (不應), the two prepare their belongings (道具) together and just disappear. Also, if a woman experiences heartbreak (失戀), she often doesn't marry for life. Instead, she goes to a Catholic convent (가도릭 修道院) and becomes a nun (僧女). Also, regarding presents (푸레센트) during courtship (戀愛關係 中) <90>
(From page nineteen) such as flowers (花), the colors peach white (?) (色挑白) or white signify a wish to spend time happily together, while brown or red signify prohibition (禁斷). The wedding ceremony is usually held in a church (敎會), it is said. On the day before the ceremony, the couple goes to the church (寺院 - likely meaning church here) individually to confess the circumstances regarding their marriage and ask for God's permission. For the ceremony, the two wear pure white veils (베-루) provided by the church, stand in the center, and respond while the priest (僧侶 - likely meaning priest here) reads the vows (誓文), then place rings on each other's fingers. After the ceremony, they eat food and also dance (딴쓰), it is said.

Dear Brother X,
I intend to stop here for now. I hope to provide more details later when another opportunity arises. Please understand? This is all for now. <90>

**Summary**

The article is a personal letter from a Korean student studying at the University of the Philippines, O Yŏngsŏp, describing his experience attending the inauguration ceremony of the Philippine Commonwealth President Manuel Quezon in Manila on November 15, 1935. He details the schedule, the vast crowds, the dignitaries (including US officials), the oaths, speeches, cannon salutes, and the subsequent grand parade featuring soldiers, students, regional groups in traditional attire, and decorated floats. O also shares observations on Filipino history, culture, clothing (especially the Barong Tagalog and women's elegant attire), food staples (rice and corn), and marriage customs (courtship, proposal, elopement, role of the church). For historians of Korean colonial history, this article is significant as it provides a rare glimpse into the perspective of a Korean colonial subject observing another Asian nation achieving a degree of self-governance from a Western power (the US). O's detailed descriptions and tone of admiration, particularly for the nationalistic fervor and cultural expressions, implicitly contrast with Korea's situation under Japanese rule, making it a valuable document reflecting colonial-era aspirations and comparative observations.

就任演說하는 大統領의 印象
						在比律賓大學 吳永燮
親愛하는 X兄
지난 11月 15日 오전 30분 常夏의 도시요, 極東올린픽 대회가 열엿든 마닐라를 뒤끌케 하는 比律賓史上, 空前의 盛儀인 新政府 樹立式을 구경하기 위하야 몃몃 동무들과 同伴하야 比國國會議事堂前 廣場에 다다럿슴니다.
그 때는 벌서 二十萬이라는 參禮者가 잇서슴니다. 나는 얼는 아래와 갓흔 式順을 보앗슴니다.
11월 15일
오전 7시 15분 開門
仝 7시 45분 議事堂 前 特別席에 一般 賓客 着席
仝 8시 15분 總督 米 陸軍長 正副 大統領 着席
仝 8시 20分 開式
仝 40분 正副 大統領 宣誓
仝 45분 大統領 就任演說
仝 9시    休憩
仝 9시 30분 行列開始
(新大統領은 行列 後 말카니얀 宮殿에 移)<87>
오후 4시 國民議會 開會, 各議員 新議長 宣誓
仝 9시 半 와-레스, 후일도의 아우지도리움에서 祝賀 大舞蹈會 開催
이러한 式順이 눈에 띄윗든 것임니다. 比律賓의 이날의 歡喜는 이 式場에 넘처 흐르고 잇섯슴니다. 7,083島嶼로 된 比島가 움직이는 듯 하엿슴니다. 45萬이나 되는 마닐라 市民의 얼골에는 活氣가 彌滿하엿든 것임니다.
케손 新大統領과 오쓰미니아 副大統領 兩巨頭가 의기양양하게 登壇할 式場에는 右側에 比島旗를 揭揚하엿고, 左側에는 米國旗를 날이고 잇는 이 메인, 스탄드는 모-닌 그는 적고 힌세비로가 만엇슴니다. 定刻 前부터 그만 문자 그대로 人山人海로 化하여 버렷슴니다. 式場 밧갓 大道로부터 골푸場까지 사람은 몰여서 더 설 데가 업섯슴니다. 舊城의 壁上에까지 방울이 달이듯이 막 우올여 싸이는 盛况을 일우윗슴니다.
