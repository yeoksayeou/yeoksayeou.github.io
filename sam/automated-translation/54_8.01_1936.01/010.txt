2.7 Billion Yen Invested in Chosŏn After Annexation

After the Japan-Korea Annexation, the total public and private capital invested in Chosŏn from mainland Japan amounts to approximately 2.7 billion yen. The breakdown is as follows:
National Expenditure (Supplementary funds, Public bonds)            950 million yen
Colonial Bank Debentures                     500 million yen
Oriental Development Company Debentures                     460 million yen (80% in Chosŏn)
Various Company Bonds and Loans             100 million yen
Investment in Companies Headquartered in Chosŏn  230 million yen
Branch and Individual Investments               500 million yen
Additionally, miscellaneous income such as pensions and insurance premiums is estimated at about 100 million yen. Based on this, the amount remitted back to mainland Japan through profits and recovery of invested capital reached approximately 1.3 billion yen. This is based on relatively accurate investigations according to past statistics and other data. Considering the portion that cannot be investigated, the actual investment is considered to be close to 3 billion yen.

Tonga Ilbo Has Savings of 100,000 Yen

Due to the flood of advertisements from Osaka and Tokyo, rumors are rampant in the streets that the three private newspapers are not only breaking even but some are even showing a regular monthly profit. Whether this is true is unknown to outsiders, but according to the report presented by the Tonga Ilbo Company at its shareholders' meeting, there are deposits in Haedong Bank and others amounting to 114,151 yen and 67 sen.
Considering the company plans to expand its building in the New Year, perhaps this deposit will be used as part of the building expansion fund.
Furthermore, looking at the business advertisements for the recent year, the net profit is said to be 4,500 yen.

Record Industry Selling 400,000 Discs Annually

According to investigations by the Government-General, the number of records sold annually in Chosŏn currently numbers an astonishing 1.2 million discs.
Of these, about one-third, approximately 400,000 discs, are Korean sound records, which clearly shows the current prosperity of the record industry. If the average market price of these records is estimated at 1 yen, it amounts to sales of 400,000 yen.

Ms. Ch'oe Sŭng-hŭi Invited by South Manchuria Railway

Ms. Ch'oe Sŭng-hŭi, the 자랑할 (pride-worthy) dancer born on the peninsula, has been invited by the South Manchuria Railway <20> this spring and is scheduled to tour major cities in Manchuria such as Dalian, Fengtian (Mukden), and Lüshun (Port Arthur) on a dance tour. At that time, she is also expected to perform newly developed dances in her homeland. The planned performance locations in Chosŏn are
Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul), Pusan, Taegu, P'yŏngyang, and Sinŭiju, five locations in total, and the dates will likely be around the 20th day of the first lunar month.
After the performances in Chosŏn and Manchuria are finished, Ms. Ch'oe is expected to follow her planned program and embark on a dance tour to Europe, meaning it will likely be difficult to see her form for several years.

Hwasin's Year-End Sales of 500,000 Yen

The Hwasin Chain Store's year-end grand sale, recently featuring eye-catching prizes like ten yellow bulls lined up in newspaper photos, along with rice sacks, wardrobes, etc.—quite tempting for a single store's prizes—how much merchandise is actually being sold is still unclear as of today (December 18th). However, according to the plan of the Chain Store Division headquarters, they expect to sell at least 500,000 yen worth across the 370-plus chain stores throughout Chosŏn. They prepared 5.5 million prize tickets for 1-yen purchases and distributed them to each chain store. If, indeed, 500,000 yen worth is sold during the twenty days from December 10th to the end of the month, a certain business source observes that even at a 10% margin, a profit of 50,000 yen would fall into their hands.

The movie "Ch'unp'ung," based on An Sŏk-yŏng's original work and directed by Mr. Pak Ki-ch'ae, is currently being released in various regions and receiving favorable reviews. Survey of Modern Salaryman Incomes

◇ Singer
If you become an exclusive singer, besides the monthly salary of 60 yen, adding income from royalties and special appearances totals around 100 yen. However, buying clothes and socializing often leaves one indebted to the company.
◇ Bus Girl
Daily income is 75 sen, and working hours are 10 hours. Supporting my mother, younger sibling, and myself, three people, it's usually insufficient.
◇ Newspaper Reporter
Monthly salary 70 yen.
Daily working hours from 9 AM to 4 PM. Living expenses: Boarding 20 yen, cigarettes 6 yen, socks 1 yen, drinks 10 yen, Western clothes installment 15 yen, other miscellaneous 15 yen... If things go well, about 10 yen is left over.
◇ Female Factory Worker
Daily income 45 sen. Working hours from <22> 7 AM to 5 PM. After paying 9 yen for dormitory fees per month, I use the rest to buy clothes.
◇ Magazine Reporter
Monthly income 50 yen.
Daily working hours from 9 AM to 5 PM. If I want to go to cafés a bit, it's always in the red.
◇ Pastor
Nowadays, the church budget has shrunk, so the monthly salary is probably around 50 or 60 yen. There's no fixed work, but preparing sermons for Sundays and visiting believers means always being on the move. The income is small, but since there's no waste, we manage somehow.
◇ Doctor
A salaried doctor usually makes 100 yen, and if開業 (opening one's own practice), cash income is over 300 yen.
Working hours are from 9 AM to 4 PM -- but you have to go even if a patient calls in the middle of the night!
Living expenses are quite high.
◇ Café Waitress
Where is the income ever fixed? This place is quite slow. Still, there are days I make 3 or 4 yen, and days I don't even make 1 yen. There isn't much 'labor,' but I work from 1 PM to 2 AM.
Clothing costs are high, so I end up in debt.
◇ Rickshaw Puller
At best, I earn 50 sen a day. If it rains, I might make a whole yen.
When I earn a lot, I spend a lot; when I don't earn, there's nothing but starvation.
◇ Female Shop Clerk
Monthly salary 25 yen.
Working hours are over 10 hours.
Trying not to look shabby in clothes and other things uses up all 25 yen. Fortunately, I get meals from home, so I don't go into debt.
◇ Tofu Seller
At best, I make 30 or 40 sen a day.
Shouting and walking around, I need to have a bowl of 막걸리 (makgeolli - rice wine).
◇ Tram Conductor
I get paid 13 sen per hour. I work 10 hours a day. Income and working hours differ depending on the conductor.
Except for the 1 yen the company saves for me, I spend everything else.<23>

**Summary**

This 1936 article from "Samch'ŏlli" magazine provides a snapshot of Korean society under Japanese rule. It begins by listing prominent Korean figures by age bracket, including nationalists abroad, religious leaders, educators, businessmen, writers, and artists, indicating their spheres of influence. It then covers economic news, such as Japanese investment in Korea reaching 2.7 billion yen since annexation, the Tonga Ilbo newspaper's financial status, and the booming record industry. Cultural updates include dancer Ch'oe Sŭng-hŭi's upcoming tour, literary publications, and film news. The article concludes with a revealing section comparing the monthly incomes and working conditions of various occupations, from professionals like doctors and reporters to low-wage workers like factory girls, bus girls, and rickshaw pullers, highlighting significant income disparity.

For historians, the article is valuable for several reasons. The extensive list of "famous figures" provides a who's who of the Korean elite and diaspora in 1936, mapping networks across politics, religion, culture, and business, both within Korea and abroad (China, Siberia, USA, Japan). The economic data, though presented from a colonial perspective (Japanese investment), offers figures on capital flow and the rise of new industries like recorded music and film. The final section on salaries offers a rare glimpse into the socio-economic stratification of colonial Korean society, detailing the precarious livelihoods of the working class alongside the relative comfort of professionals and the wealth accumulation by certain businesses like Hwasin or newspapers like Tonga Ilbo. The mention of KAPF arrests and the activities of figures in exile also subtly points to the underlying political tensions of the era.
