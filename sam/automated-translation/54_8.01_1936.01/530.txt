Holding Flowers (꼿을 잡고)

Sŏnu Iljŏn (鮮于一扇)

A. When flowers bloom
In the gently blowing spring breeze
That unforgettable past of bygone days

B. Though I try to forget the past months and years
As if they were clouds
My sorrowful heart cannot forget

C. Playing while picking flowers
Seems like only yesterday
But that person has gone, and I am alone

How can I not weep as memories make my heart sorrowful?
Unable to hold you, I only pick the flowers.

Lyrics by Kim Ansŏ (金岸曙)
Composed by Yi Myŏnsang (李冕相)
Pando Polydor Nanhwa Orchestra (半島 포러도-루蘭和樂團)

In a Foreign Land (他鄕)

Ko Poksu (高福壽)

1. Counting the years lived in this foreign land
Ten years since leaving home, only youth has aged

2. My life like a floating weed, alone and dumbfounded
Opening the window and gazing, the sky is over there

3. The willow tree in front of my hometown must be green again this spring
But the time I broke off a branch to make a whistle is long past

4. They say a foreign land becomes home if you grow fond of it
But whether I go or come, it's always a foreign land

Lyrics by Kim Nŭngin (金陵人)
Composed by Mun Howŏl (文湖月)
Okeh-1677<2>

Maiden and Bachelor (처녀총각)

Kang Hongsik (姜弘植)

Spring has come, spring has come
Even to the heart of a young maiden
Saying she's going to gather herbs
She toddles off to the field
A gentle breeze blows
The Arirang tune comes naturally
Heung...

The spring lass sighs deeply
Throws down her flower basket
Breaks off a willow branch
Half-reclining in a sunny spot
Her hand holding a small knife, snip snip
She makes a flute and plays it
Heung...

In the heart of that bachelor
Tilling the field with a hoe
Spring has also found its way
Blood boils, heart pounds
His humming tune is also plaintive
It sounds quite stylish
Heung...

The spring breeze, carrying the song,
Blows softly
The old bachelor, dumbfounded,
Throws down his hoe handle
Matching the flute's sound
He laments his fate
Heung...

Lyrics by Pŏm O (凡吾)
Composed by Kim Chunyŏng (金駿泳)

Song of Sorrow (哀傷曲)

Kim Pokhŭi (金福姬)

That faint star by the sea at dusk
I'll forget all painful dreams and follow it
O beloved port where we sang through the night
Leaving my loved one, I depart alone
Following the stars I go, my love
Farewell, I go, leaving my beloved behind

Don't hold me back as I leave, I will return someday
Pushing away your tear-soaked sleeve, my heart just aches
Don't ask when I'll return if I leave now
Though my body goes, I leave my love behind
Following the stars I go, my love
Farewell, I go, leaving my beloved behind

Poem by Yi Hayun (異河潤)

Tumen River Boatman (豆滿江 뱃사공)

Kim Yonghwan (金龍煥)

1. Yesterday, carrying these people across
Today, delivering sorrowful news
The old boatman crossing the Tumen River (豆滿江)
Listlessly dips the oar into the blue water

2. When departing travelers weep sadly
He comforts them, saying 'Return successful'
For one year, two years, five, ten, forty years
He has grown old on the waves of the Tumen River

3. Though yesterday he carried a sorrowful letter
Hoping perhaps those people who promised
To return might cross the river
Today again, he rows the boat and departs

Willow of Regret (情恨의 버들)

Ch'oe Namyong (崔南鏞)

1. Holding hands on a fading spring day
'Farewell,' 'Go well,' the weeping lover parted
The willow planted at departure now overhangs the road
But the indifferent departed lover shows no sign of returning

2. The plea of love, soaked in blood and frozen
Blooms anew whenever the spring breeze blows
The scattering willow leaves are my heart's state
Fluttering in the blue void, calling the departed lover

3. Writing my heart on the tender willow leaf
I'll float it on the flowing spring water
If I trim a willow branch and play it like a flute
Perhaps my lover of old might return

The pure heart (純情) within must be redder still

We deeply vowed to live nobly

3. Thousands of layers of sorrowful regret (情恨) in the heart
And this devoted heart (一片丹心) praying for glory (榮華) here
Even if the world changes
How could it ever fade?

Camellia Flower (冬栢꼿)

Carries well on the coming and going breeze

2. Because the castor oil plants and camellias grew thick

3. That girl over there, her crimson skirt fluttering (낫기린)
Weeps terribly when rowing the oars (로저을), when weighing anchor (닷감을)
Whenever she applies camellia oil in the brothel
How much will she weep, yearning for her old hometown?

**Summary**

This article from the January 1, 1936 issue of Samch'ŏlli magazine is a supplement containing the lyrics of 16 popular Korean songs (流行歌) of the time. Each entry includes the song title, performer, lyricist, composer, and often the arranger, accompanying orchestra, record label, and record number. The songs cover themes typical of the era, such as longing for lost love, sorrow of separation, nostalgia for hometown while living elsewhere (타향살이), appreciation of nature intertwined with emotion, and reflections on history or social conditions, like the tragic fate of women sold into prostitution ("동백꽃"). For historians, this collection offers a valuable snapshot of popular culture and sentiment in mid-1930s colonial Korea. The prevalence of themes like displacement ("타향"), longing ("그리운 님이시여"), sorrow ("애상곡"), and historical lament ("낙화삼천"), alongside mentions of Japanese record companies and arrangers, reflects the complex cultural landscape under Japanese rule, showcasing both enduring Korean emotions and the pervasive influence of Japanese cultural industries.
