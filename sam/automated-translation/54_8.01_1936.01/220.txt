Oh, Tearful Yŏngwŏl! A Narrative Song, "The Tragic History of Tanjong Continued"
						Hyŏn Ch'ŏl

As King Munjong's health declined daily, knowing he could not rise again, in May of that year—King Munjong passed away on May 14th—nine evenings before his passing, he had a banquet prepared for the scholars of the Hall of Worthies (Chiphyŏnjŏn 學士). This was no ordinary banquet. It was King Munjong's intention to once again entrust the future affairs of the young Crown Prince, that is, King Tanjong, after his own thousand autumns (death). Of course, at the time of his passing, there would be an official testament (顧命) to the three high councilors, six ministers, and senior court officials, but rather than such formalities, he wished to privately call just the Hall of Worthies scholars and entrust the Crown Prince to them. This Hall of Worthies was a special institution established by King Sejong the Great, gathering young scholars of combined talent and learning from across the country to make them loyal subjects for the nation's future. Thus, even between ruler and subject, there was a closeness like that of father and son, or brothers, more private than public. Considering the favor received both publicly and privately during the reigns of Kings Sejong and Munjong, <151> it was largely for the sake of entrusting the future affairs of King Tanjong.

For some reason, from King Sejong the Great to Munjong, and even to his mother, Queen Hyŏndŏk, whenever they were near death, they always seemed greatly concerned about the future affairs of this King Tanjong, which cannot be seen as mere coincidence.

On this day, Tano festival (端午) was appointed for this final calling of the scholars. At that time, the senior attendees were Chŏng In-ji, Sin Suk-chu, Kim Suk-cha, Yi Maeng-gyun, Sŏ Kŏ-jŏng, Yang Sŏng-ji, Ŏ Hyo-dam, Kim Chil, Kang Hŭi-an, Yi Sŏk-hyŏng, Kim Su-on, Yi Kye-jŏn, Yi Ye, Sŏng Kan, and others. The eternally loyal subjects Sŏng Sam-mun, Pak P'aeng-nyŏn, Ha Wi-ji, Yi Kae, Yu Sŏng-wŏn, along with Yi Po-hŭm, Kwŏn Chŏl, Wŏn Ho, Ch'oe Tŏk-chi, Ki Ch'u, Yi Maeng-jŏn, Cho Sang-chi, and other civil officials attended. Among the military officials, Yu Ŭng-bu, one of the Six Martyred Ministers (死六臣), was also present.

It was not uncommon for the king
to bestow food upon his subjects.
But tonight's gathering, this royal banquet,
Was a meaningful assembly entrusting
the future of the young Crown Prince.
Tears welled in the poured wine cups,
Sorrow permeated the clear, elegant songs.
The king raised his hand, looked left and right,
Munjong: "Tonight, this banquet might be the last time in this world for Us and you, lords. Speak your minds freely, without any reservation, and let us try to relieve our pent-up feelings."
Then King Munjong first sang:
"The king is gravely ill,"
"The Crown Prince is young."
"After my thousand autumns,"
"Will great winds perhaps arise?"
"Where can I find brave warriors"
"To guard the four directions?"

Pak P'aeng-nyŏn rose,
His throat choked with emotion, singing:
"They say gold comes from the river Yŏsu (金生廲水),"
"Does gold come from every water?"
"They say jade comes from Mount K'un'gang (玉出崑崗),"
"Does jade come from every peak?" <152>
"No matter how much they say a wife must follow her husband (女必從夫),"
"Should one follow just any lord?"
His voice filled with tears.

Sŏng Sam-mun followed suit:
"When this body dies and goes,"
"What shall it become?"
"On Pongnae Mountain's highest peak,"
"It will become a solitary, tall pine."
"When white clouds fill heaven and earth (白雲이 滿乾坤할 際에),"
"It alone shall remain green (獨也靑靑)."
As his voice trailed off,

Yi Kae rose:
"Does the crow, struck by snow and rain,"
"Become white though it is black?"
"Does the night-shining pearl (夜光 明月珠)"
"Become dark even at night?"
"This single-minded loyalty towards the king (一片丹心),"
"Can it ever change?"
The boiling heart of utmost loyalty and integrity
Ended with boundless tears.
The night deepened, suddenly it was the fifth watch (五更).
From somewhere, the tolling sound
Of the bell of impermanence (無常).
Only the sorrowful grief
Of fleeting human life remained.

As the ruler and subjects thus gathered, unaware of the night passing during this meaningful banquet, the east was already beginning to lighten. Everyone was intoxicated and weary, so Chŏng In-ji rose, prostrated himself, and said:
Chŏng In-ji: "Attending Your Majesty through such a meaningful night, the royal grace is boundless. Even if our bones are ground to powder (粉骨碎身), how could we, as subjects, possibly forget the events of this night? We humbly wish Your Majesty to trust us and retire to the bedchamber."
Munjong: "Oh, Lord, you are the head of the scholars and the Crown Prince's teacher (傳師). The former king especially cherished you and entrusted the Crown Prince to you. This is not a light matter, but I too <153> once again entrust him to you. Do not forget these words, whether awake or asleep (寤寐間)."
Leaving these tearful words, as he retired to his bedchamber, he personally covered the scholars who had fallen asleep drunk with the silk bedding (衾寢) he had been using. King Munjong, who so loved his subjects and worried for the Crown Prince—whether it was due to the nation's misfortune—passed away (昇遐) on May 14th of that year, after giving his final command (顧命) to the three high councilors, six ministers, and senior officials. King Tanjong ascended the throne (極登) at the age of 12. The court was filled with virtuous officials who, without any gaps, assisted the young king, striving for good governance. However, at that time, King Tanjong became swayed by his uncle, the firstborn Prince Suyang (首陽大君). Prince Suyang had different intentions and, conspiring with Han Myŏng-hoe and Kwŏn Nam, first bribed Chŏng In-ji, Sin Suk-chu, and others. In one night, they slaughtered over 200 loyal officials of the previous reign and forced King Tanjong to abdicate (讓位). Prince Suyang became king; this revered person is King Sejo, and the year was Ŭlhae (乙亥), the 6th month, the 4th year of Tanjong's reign, when he was barely 15 years old. In this turmoil, not only did many officials of the previous reign die, but among relatives—brothers, uncles, nephews—anyone not close to him was killed. The first sacrifice among the grand princes was his younger brother, Prince Anp'yŏng (安平大君), and his son. On the 11th day of the 6th month of Ŭlhae, although formally termed abdication, the throne was forcibly taken. King Tanjong was given the honorary title of King Emeritus (上王). Then, in the 6th month of Pyŏngja (丙子), when the restoration movement of the Six Martyred Ministers occurred, upon the insistence of Chŏng In-ji, Sin Suk-chu, and others, he was demoted to Prince Nosan (魯山君). A royal secretary (承旨) was sent to the King Emeritus.
Secretary: "Receive the royal command (傳敎)."
Hearing this, the King Emeritus became furious (震怒).
King Emeritus: "You cur! Royal command? Who in this heaven and earth could issue a royal command to me? Despicable cur! Quickly withdraw!"
The Secretary heard this and, bewildered, hesitated. The King Emeritus said:
King Emeritus: "Won't you leave, cur? You look like someone who deserves immediate beheading! Get out quickly!"
The Secretary, unable to do anything, returned to the palace and reported this to the government of the time. The court was astonished. As the young King Emeritus grew increasingly sharp-witted (英敏), the people who worried most were Chief State Councilor (領議政) Chŏng In-ji and Second State Councilor (左贊成) Sin Suk-chu. Why were these two most worried? Because they had received the most favor from King Sejong and King Munjong, and had also been most entrusted with the future affairs of the young lord, King Tanjong. Since King Tanjong was becoming increasingly sharp-witted, that was a great concern. Sin Suk-chu said to Chŏng In-ji: <154>
Sin: "Your Excellency (大監)! As Nosan grows older, he becomes increasingly sharp-witted. If we leave him alive, who knows what calamity might befall us? Shouldn't we devise a way to eliminate him?"
Chŏng: "Well, indeed. As much favor as we received from the two previous kings, we two are the most hated concerning Nosan. To completely remove this root of misfortune (禍根), dealing with Nosan is paramount. However, even when the three high councilors and six ministers submitted memorials (啓本) on this matter, they were not approved (不允); even when the Office of the Inspector General (司憲府) submitted memorials, they were not approved; even when memorials were submitted through royal relatives (宗親), they were not approved. The form taken makes it very difficult to discern His Majesty's true intention (尊意)."
Sin Suk-chu: "Your Excellency, can't you guess? His Majesty might have thunderous intentions himself, but privately, he is his own nephew, the direct grandson (元孫) of virtuous sage kings like his elder brother Munjong or his father King Sejong. To depose a monarch of innate sharp wit (天資가 英敏한 君主) without reason, and to dispose of him without sufficient crime, must weigh on his conscience (於心). Although his true intention might not be so, it's clear he wishes to create the appearance of being compelled by those around him. Your Excellency and I should mobilize all officials and royal relatives, while also fabricating some pretext-worthy incident. If it doesn't work the first time, try a second, third, fourth, even tens or hundreds of times. Let's relentlessly submit memorials until this matter is finished."

Not long after this discussion between Chŏng In-ji and Sin Suk-chu, on the 19th day of the 6th month, a Secretary, accompanied by Military Supply Officer (軍資正) Kim Cha-haeng and Palace Attendant (內侍府事) Hong Tŭk-kyŏng, arrived. Vice-Minister (僉知) Ŏ Tŭk-hae, leading 50 soldiers, along with State Tribunal Officer (義禁府都事) Wang Pang-yŏn, brought an unsuitable covered palanquin (屋轎).
Secretary: "Recently, the weather has been very hot, and His Majesty (上監) has not seen the King Emeritus for a long time. He wishes to enjoy a refreshing day out in the eastern suburbs (東郊). He has moved the royal carriage (御駕) to Kyŏngbok Palace and desires you to proceed together to the eastern suburbs. Thus, I report this."
King Emeritus: "Alright..."
The King Emeritus thought he could choose not to go, but just a few days earlier, his 70-year-old maternal grandmother, the Lady of Hwasan (花山府夫人), had been beheaded under the charge of treason (逆謀). <155> Even if she were guilty, she was a woman, 70 years old, and furthermore, a respected elder of the royal family (王室의 尊視). If she had to be killed, administering poison (死藥) would have been appropriate, but this was excessively harsh. Also, there was the incident where Kim Chil threw the ancestral tablet (位牌) of Queen Hyŏndŏk, King Tanjong's mother, onto the courtyard of the royal shrine (宗廟) right before the King Emeritus's eyes. Thinking he should say something about these matters, he boarded the covered palanquin without suspicion. As soon as they reached the main road, as prearranged, a herald (宣傳官) rode up quickly on horseback.
Herald: "Reporting! His Majesty anticipates that the weather ahead is extremely hot and the procession will be very taxing. If you proceed directly through the East Great Gate (東大門), His Majesty will move via the East Small Gate (東小門) from within the palace, and you will meet on the road."
The King Emeritus agreed. After traveling about 10 li (roughly 4km) through the East Great Gate, there was still no sign of movement. Feeling somewhat suspicious, he reached Hwayang Pavilion (華陽亭). Seeing tents and screens set up, he thought this must be the meeting place with the king. Upon arriving, unexpectedly, An No was there alone. He prostrated himself and reported:
An: "Your Majesty (殿下), the consensus of the court is that if the King Emeritus stays in Seoul, treacherous elements (姦慝한 무리) will easily plot disturbances. This is not good for the Royal Ancestral Shrine (宗社) and is also not beneficial for the King Emeritus. Therefore, if you would reside for some time in Yŏngwŏl Prefecture (寧越府), where the mountains and waters are superb (絶勝) and the scenery is beautiful (明媚), the public sentiment (人心) will stabilize. After the treacherous elements withdraw, you will be brought back to Seoul. Please understand this clearly and proceed peacefully."
Realizing then that the matter had been deceitfully arranged through scheming, shouting orders on the spot would be useless. While he remained silent (黙黙히),
An No simply turned back and left.
The stern soldiers, like wildfire,
Escorted the royal carriage eastward, eastward.
He who grew up in the nine-layered palace (九重宮闕) knew not the road beyond ten li,
How could he have dreamt
Of Yŏngwŏl, deep in the mountain valleys of Kangwŏn Province?
Under the scorching sun of the three hottest periods (三伏),
After six days, the place reached was <156>
A nook in the mountains of Ch'ŏngnyŏngp'o, west of Yŏngwŏl Prefecture.
Inside a humble shack like a clamshell (괴딱지 같은 접시집),
With leaning pillars and broken window frames.
The front mountain pressed close like a forehead,
The rear mountain seemed to descend and strike the back of the neck.
How could human impermanence be so severe?
Embracing his oppressed heart,
He lamented his autumn sentiments in a verse:
"Once the resentful bird"
"Left the king's house,"
"A lonely body, a companion shadow,"
"Shone amidst the green mountains."
"Trying to sleep falsely every night,"
"Even false sleep cannot be achieved."
"Trying to dispel the deep恨 (han, resentment) each year,"
"That 恨 does not disappear."
"The sound ceases at the dawn mountain peak,"
"Only a withered shadow remains."
"Blood flows in the spring valley,"
"Only the falling flowers are red."
"Heaven is deaf,"
"And cannot hear this sad plea."
"Why does it allow only this sorrowful person"
"To hear it alone?"
Thus, even days of extreme sorrow and pain
Passed, one day, two days.
The last day of the year Pyŏngja came, and the New Year of Chŏngch'uk arrived.
The ten thousand tangled sorrows
Felt especially fresh today.
Wearing the dragon robe (龍袍), he sat absently.
King Emeritus: "Oh! Tonight is the last departing day of the year Pyŏngja. Time passes so quickly." (Continued in the next issue) <157>

**Summary**

This installment of "The Tragic History of Tanjong Continued" recounts King Munjong's final days, his deep concern for his young son Tanjong, and his entrustment of the prince to the Hall of Worthies scholars, particularly Chŏng In-ji and Sin Suk-chu. It then describes how Prince Suyang, aided by figures like Han Myŏng-hoe and Kwŏn Nam (and crucially, the turncoats Chŏng In-ji and Sin Suk-chu), usurped the throne, becoming King Sejo. Tanjong, initially made King Emeritus, is later demoted to Prince Nosan following the failed restoration attempt by the Six Martyred Ministers. The article details the deceitful plot orchestrated by Chŏng In-ji and Sin Suk-chu to remove Tanjong from Seoul, culminating in his forced exile to the remote Yŏngwŏl, where he laments his fate in verse.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article is interesting as it retells a well-known tragic episode from the Joseon Dynasty. Publishing such historical narratives, especially those emphasizing betrayal, usurpation, and the suffering of a legitimate young king, could have served as a veiled commentary on Korea's own loss of sovereignty and the injustices faced under colonial rule. The focus on loyalty (Sŏng Sam-mun, etc.) versus betrayal (Chŏng In-ji, Sin Suk-chu) might have resonated deeply with contemporary readers grappling with issues of collaboration and resistance. Historians might find the specific narrative choices, the dramatic "narrative song" (歌談) format, and the portrayal of historical figures reflective of popular sentiment and cultural expression during the 1930s in Korea.

눈물의 寧越이여, 歌談 「端宗哀史續」
						玄哲
文宗게옵서 寶體의 靡寧이 날로 沈重하시매 일지 못하실 줄 아시고 그 해 5월 端年 즉 文宗大王게서 5월 14日에 昇遐하섯으니까 도라가시기 아흐래 전날 저녁에 集賢殿 學士들을 모다 잔치를 排設하게 하섯으니 이 잔치는 달은 잔치가 안이다. 文宗게옵서 당신의 千秋後에 나어린 世子 즉 端宗大王의 後事를 다시 한 번 더 付托하시고자 하심이엿음니다. 勿論 昇遐하실 當時에 公式으로 3公6卿과 朝庭 重臣에게 顧命이 잇을 것이나 그러한 公式 보담도 私式으로 마지막 集賢殿 學士들이나 불너서 世子를 付托하시고자 하심은 이 集賢殿 學士라고 하는 것은 世宗大王게서 一國에 才學이 兼備한 靑年 學者들을 모와 後日 國家에 忠良을 만들고자 特別이 設置한 機關이니 君臣間이라도 公的보담 私的으로 父子, 兄弟와 같은 親密이 잇고 世宗, 文宗 兩朝에 잇서 公私를 勿論하고 받은 恩寵으로 <151> 말하면 그 太半이 端宗大王의 後事를 付托하고자 하심이엿음니다.
엇젼 일인지 世宗大王으로 붙어 文宗, 심지어 그 어머님 되시는 顯德王后까지 시라도 臨終 하실 때에 늘 이 端宗大王의 後事를 퍽으나 念慮하시던 일로 보와서라도 결코 偶然치 안타고 볼 수 밖에 없음니다.
이 날 端午를 期約하여 最後로 學士들을 불으시니 其當時 首班으로 鄭麟趾, 申叔舟, 金淑滋, 李孟均, 徐居正, 梁誠之, 魚孝膽, 金礩, 姜希顔, 李石亨, 金守溫, 李季甸, 李芮, 成侃 等과 萬古忠臣인 成三問, 朴彭年, 河緯地, 李塏, 柳誠源, 外, 李甫欽, 權節, 元昊, 崔德之, 奇處, 李孟專, 曹尙治 등 文官이 入侍하엿고 武官으로써는 死六臣의 한 분이신 兪應芓가 叅席하게 되엿음니다.

이렇게 君臣이 모여 하로 밤 뜻잇는 잔치로 밤이 새는 줄을 몰을 동안에 어느듯 東方이 임의 히여질야 함에 모도가 술이 취하고 疲困하므로 鄭麟趾가 이러서 俯伏하며
麟趾 『殿下를 뫼시고 오늘같이 뜻깊은 하로밤을 지내오니 聖恩이 罔極하온지라, 粉骨碎身이 되온들 어찌 臣子로써 오늘 이 밤에 이 일을 잊을 리가 잇겟음니까. 伏願殿下는 臣 등을 믿삽고 寢殿에 듭시게 하심이 좋을가 하옵내다.』 文宗 『오호, 卿은 學士의 首班이요, 世子의 傳師라. 先大王이 특히 卿을 寵愛하시와 世子를 卿에게 付托하신 것이니 泛然할 바 안이나 寡人도 <153> 또한 卿에게 다시 한 번 더 付托하는 것이니 寤寐間이라도 이 말을 잊이마오.』
하시는 눈물겨운 말슴을 남기시고 寢殿으로 듭실 적에 술이 취해서 잠든 學士들에게는 친이 덥흐시던 비단 衾寢을 덥허주섯음니다. 이렇게 臣下를 사랑하시고 世子를 念慮하시던 文宗大王게오서 國運이 不幸해서 그러한 지 그 해 5月 14日에 3公6卿과 重臣들에게 顧命하시고 昇遐하섯으니 端宗大王이 12歲로써 極登하시고 朝庭에 가득찬 어진 臣下들이 물샐 틈 없이 어리신 人君을 도와 善政에 努力하는대 그때에 端宗大王게는 叔王이요. 맛지 되시는 首陽大君에게 휘둘이게 되섯으니 首陽大君이 뜻을 달이 먹고 韓明澮와 權擥 등으로 더부러 첫재로 鄭麟趾, 申叔舟 등을 매수해 가지고 하로밤에 前朝의 忠良之臣 200餘 名을 斫殺하고 端宗大王을 讓位하시도록 하고 首陽大君이 國王이 되시니 이 어른이 世祖大王이시고 해는 乙亥 6月이요. 端宗 4年이며 寶齡이 겨우 15살이올시다. 이 통에 前朝 臣下가 많이 죽엇을 뿐 안이라 兄弟 叔侄間 宗親에 누구를 勿論하고 自己에게 가깝지 못하면 모조리 죽엿으니 大君 중에 第1次로 犧牲된 이가 그 다음 아우되는 安平大君父子분이엿음니다. 乙亥 6月 11日에 形式으로는 讓位라고 햇지만은 其實은 억지로 寶位를 내놓게 하고 端宗大王을 上王이라는 尊號를 드리엿더니 丙子 6月에 死六臣의 復僻運動이 이러나자 鄭麟趾, 申叔舟 등의 主張으로 魯山君으로 降封하고 承旨를 上王게 보내여.
