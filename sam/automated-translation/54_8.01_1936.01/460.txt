기사형태	소설 (Novel/Fiction)

한 敎訓
						張德祚
作家點描 (Author Sketch)

The lady writer holds an unshakeable position in our literary world as a short story writer. Already published works number over ten, including "Wife" (안해) and "Mother and Daughter" (어미와 딸), and each new work has sent astonishment through the literary circles. Her hometown is Chain, near Taegu; she is 22 years old. She dropped out of Taegu Girls' High School midway, graduated from Kyŏngsŏng Paehwa Girls' High School, attended the English Department at Ewha Women's College but dropped out midway, and later worked as a reporter for Kaebyŏk Sa (開闢社).

To the left, a mountain ridge as if sharply cut;
To the right, a stream frozen over;
Between them, on a narrow path, Ch'ŏlsu walked distractedly. He didn't know how far he had come; his two hands tucked into his overcoat pockets grew warm, even damp with sweat, and the slope steepened into a difficult mountain valley, yet he simply trudged on, step by step, like a man who had lost his mind.
Days of anguish.
Nights of sleeplessness.
Anyone could see from his bloodshot eyes that he was steeped in deep sorrow.
"Hah, if only I had endured just a little longer, none of this would have happened!"
His mouth, shut tight like a stone statue, seemed to quiver, and again this lament, uttered more than a hundred times already, tumbled out.
Even today, the image of the wife he had parted with<296> would not leave his muddled mind.
No, coming to Kŭmgangsan like this, walking this mountain path, only renewed his longing for his wife.
"A child at twenty, a fortune at thirty," they say, but he was already past thirty, yet had no children, certainly no accumulated wealth, and now even the wife he had managed to marry a few years ago had become a complete stranger. The thought of drifting again from one boarding house corner to another attic room, alone once more, naturally stirred his heart.
And the separation from his wife – it wasn't as if there was some special reason, or that they had grown tired of each other. Although their three years of married life hadn't held any surprisingly great joys, they had passed warmly without any major fights or complaints. That they had reached this point after a small verbal clash was now beyond regret, no matter how much he repented.
The cause of the fight was a child.
They happened to be out on the street together when they saw a colleague, married just last year, carrying a chubby child while shopping. For no reason, he became angry, and his wife, also provoked, returned home. They bickered, and then their mutual irritation exploded.
Of course, given Ch'ŏlsu's mature age and relatively comfortable family situation, it wasn't unreasonable to long for a child. But both he and his wife were healthy, and if they waited a few more years, joyful news would surely come. It certainly wasn't because of this that he separated from his wife and now endured this hollow longing. So, why did these two, who had been on good terms, part ways without any particular reason and now suffer yearning for each other? It was solely due to their mutual "stubbornness."
Even then, at the height of their anger, when his wife ran off to her parents' home, crying, if he, as the husband, had immediately followed and soothed her, or if she had quickly calmed down and come back to apologize, they could have laughed it off without any issue. But the failure to do so, prolonging the tragedy to the end, was solely because of their mutual obstinacy.
Indeed, one might call it a man's pride (意志), the other a woman's pique. Though in their hearts they regretted and felt remorse, they couldn't readily step forward and express their true feelings calmly. Instead, they just watched each other's reactions, eventually leading to this regrettable situation.
Actually, the attitude of the father-in-law and mother-in-law, who intervened, was also a mistake. <297>
Parents are blamed for their children's faults, so they should have quickly offered an apology on the wife's behalf, or else, even if not, turned it into a joke, saying, "A couple's quarrel is like cutting water with a knife." Then he, who was already deeply regretting it, could have pretended to give in and let it pass. But they rushed over frantically, demanding, "What happened?" "What did she do wrong?" starting to interrogate him formally. This made it impossible for him to simply laugh and say, "Everything up till now was just a joke, please send my daughter back." Naturally, he had no choice but to recount all the wrongs, real or imagined. The old couple flew into a rage, berating their daughter, which provoked him in turn. This fanned the flames further, leading to a fresh protest from the wife's side:
"I can't live with such a liar."
"Then it's a final separation."
And so it went: packing belongings amidst the autumn rain, giving up the rented house, a great commotion, and finally, the day of the breakup arrived. But today, after the seals were stamped and the family register formally divided, finding no way to calm his increasingly melancholic heart, he took a few days' leave from the company he worked for and set out to trace the fond path of the past, heading towards Onjŏng-ri, the destination of their honeymoon trip. Leaving the mountain ridge, the path became relatively flat.
However, Ch'ŏlsu's illusions did not easily dissipate; he was picturing their honeymoon then. That day, too, the wind had been chilly.
Though there might have been a difference between the early March wind anticipating spring and the early winter wind, the feel of it was just the same as today.
Hadn't they entrusted their luggage first to the rickshaw puller they'd ridden with and deliberately wandered up this very path to walk the secluded mountain trail?
Ch'ŏlsu glanced sideways, feeling as if his wife were walking right beside him, just like then.
But there was only a bare rock where leaves had fallen; the wind blowing from behind felt cold on the tip of his nose.
He looked down at the ground again and forced himself to continue the thread of his fantasy.
"Are you tired?" he had asked, offering his strong hand. The bride's white fingers, hesitating shyly to take it; on them, the ruby wedding ring he had placed there just that afternoon glowed red, symbolizing a maiden's passion.<298>
In the three years since, not a short time, her hands, busy in the kitchen and doing all the strenuous laundry herself, had roughened considerably. But the wife's hands Ch'ŏlsu saw now were still soft and beautiful.
The thoughts continued.
When he returned home near midnight after a late company banquet, his wife would still be awake, waiting for him blankly. Sometimes, clinging to him like a child, she would blink her dark eyes and act playfully spoiled.
That mouth, that nose, that ear.
There was a prim and sensitive side to her, but thinking back now, that too was an undeniable charm.
"But she is no longer mine."
When Ch'ŏlsu's fantasy reached this point, he sighed "Hoo" and stopped walking.
Suddenly, a wide main road opened up before him, and the dark roofs of Onjŏng-ri began to appear.

"Do you have a room?"
Entering the K Lodge (K장), he saw the same faded triangular flag playing with the north wind above the roof as back then.
"Yes, we have many vacant rooms."
The owner, who was warming himself by the fire in the office, had changed, but Ch'ŏlsu requested the same room from three years ago.
"The easternmost room, you mean? We're using that as a storage room now..."
The owner looked at this guest, who insisted on vacating that specific room, with suspicion.
The route was exactly the same, the lodge the same; though there were many vacant rooms in the early winter spa town, he insisted on having that particular room cleared.
Night, especially a winter night at a hot spring resort, is quiet. Though K Lodge now had bath facilities (湯泉) it lacked in the old days, Ch'ŏlsu deliberately went out to the public hot spring, just like three years ago. Walking slowly back alone along the quiet street, dangling his wet handkerchief, new memories surged in his heart.
"Let me carry that for you."
His wife's whispered words seemed to echo in his ear, and he inadvertently turned around, then stopped dead in his tracks and stepped back. Astonishing shock and complex emotions pounded in his chest. He saw two<299> women approaching him under a dim streetlamp about three or four *kan* away.
"She resembles her!"
A gisaeng-like woman leading a child entertainer (童技); until their shadows turned the corner into an alley, Ch'ŏlsu remained rooted to the spot.
As they passed him, the slight smile revealing an overlapping tooth, the dark double eyelids, the shape of her hair bun from behind – to Ch'ŏlsu's eyes, she looked exactly like his wife.
No, perhaps to a third party's eyes, the resemblance (相似) might not even have been considered striking.
But even after he finally managed to move and return to the inn, the shock still reverberated in his chest.
The night deepened, but Ch'ŏlsu could not fall asleep.
Getting up, lying down, just as he had suffered for the past few nights, he projected various fantasies onto the ceiling. As he thought of his wife, her face suddenly transformed into the gisaeng he had seen earlier.
The gisaeng's face became his wife's, then his wife's face became the gisaeng's. His already tangled mind grew increasingly disordered, and he kicked off the bedding and sat up.
A small village spa town, and he had even noted the alley she entered – perhaps someone might know her? He finally called the attendant.
"Yes, she has a slight snaggletooth, and double eyelids?" The attendant repeated the description Ch'ŏlsu provided, then said,
"Ah, that must be the gisaeng named Paek Yŏnghwa." Then, glancing furtively, he asked,
"Shall I call her for you?" and added,
"She's a popular gisaeng. But where would she find customers these days? She's probably free," chattering on excitedly by himself, he dashed out without waiting for an answer.
Before long, the attendant returned with Yŏnghwa. Her appearance, freshly made up with her hair redone, seemed incomparably beautiful compared to his wife, who had spent so long only bustling about the kitchen, yet Ch'ŏlsu still thought, "Somehow, she resembles her."
The drinks table was brought in, and as the night deepened, Ch'ŏlsu became quite drunk.
Without a single joke (롱), without any amusement, silently accepting and drinking whatever was offered, Yŏnghwa watched this customer quietly for a while, then said,
"If you get too drunk like this, the wife you left behind will worry,"
and took his cup away.<300>
"You came from Seoul, right? Is your home (댁) in Seoul?"
"I have no wife left behind, nor do I have a home,"
Ch'ŏlsu snapped curtly, then immediately felt sorry for his sharp reply to Yŏnghwa's kind words.
"Oh my, what a lie!"
The gisaeng laughed as if dumbfounded, then gulped down a full cup herself.
"Hey, if you keep that up, you'll get scolded by your own lover," Ch'ŏlsu said, moving closer as if to soften his earlier excessive bluntness.
"Someone like me is destined for this life anyway, but for someone like you, there must be many who love you, many gentlemen who cherish and adore you."
He casually took the gisaeng's cup, just as Yŏnghwa had done earlier.
"Hmph, if I had someone who cherished me like that, would I be doing this?"
Her eyes reddening slightly, Yŏnghwa turned away primly and said,
"Why are you teasing me?"
"Teasing..." Flustered, Ch'ŏlsu started to make an awkward excuse.
"Please forgive me. I have something weighing on my mind, so I sometimes get sensitive like this."
She drank two or three more cups in quick succession (련겁혀).
"Please listen to my story. It's rude to do this before a gentleman I've just met, but..."
She wiped her mouth with a handkerchief.
"Though I drift around now as a gisaeng in this spa town, I actually come from a decent family and even attended a private middle school. Is that all? I was even married to a suitable man, but we separated, and now I'm in this state."
Ch'ŏlsu wondered if this woman was mimicking someone else, but seeing the sincere expression clearly etched on her face, he just nodded silently.
"And it wasn't because of some deep mistake or reason, you know. It was over something trivial, a quarrel that escalated... truly a breakup stemming from a lover's quarrel."
Ch'ŏlsu genuinely felt it sounded like a lie.
Could this woman have read his mind? He suddenly felt embarrassed and deliberately asked,
"So, a joke truly became serious?"
"That's right. Men are just unnecessarily stubborn. How was a nineteen-year-old girl<301> supposed to understand such subtle psychology? When he angrily told me to leave, I retorted 'Fine, I'll leave!' and turned away primly, expecting him to come and appease me, but he never showed up. I wanted desperately to go and apologize, but I felt awkward and embarrassed... If only my mother were alive, I could have confided in her, but there was no one like that. By the time I regretted it a hundred, a thousand times, admitting I was wrong, it was already too late. What could I do?"
Yŏnghwa quietly wiped away tears.
"I was so angry I thought of dying several times, but I gathered my resolve and deliberately fell into this pit, passing the time by catering to the whims of men who are not my husband."
Sighing deeply, she again wiped her nose and eyes with the handkerchief.
"There are similar situations in the world,"
Ch'ŏlsu finally spoke, his heavy lips parting as if waking from deep thought.
"Why, is there someone else like me? Honestly, could there be anyone else this unfortunate?"
"Everything you say is right. Truly, in a marriage, the most important thing is yielding, and then enduring, enduring, and enduring again. There's nothing else,"
he said forcefully, his voice welling up from genuine feeling.
"It's true. If I ever get the chance to be someone's wife again, this time I'll definitely endure as much as possible. I think I've learned enough about the psychology of gentlemen now—" the gisaeng laughed.
Ch'ŏlsu felt this was likely not a lie at all.
"An open-minded woman," he murmured silently, gazing again at Yŏnghwa's beautiful brow.
"That's right. If you marry again now, you'll truly cherish your wife. First, endure, and don't be so stubborn..." But the gisaeng remained seated with her head bowed.
"But finding a suitable person isn't easy."
Though Ch'ŏlsu's words held another meaning, Yŏnghwa sat silently, whether she understood or not.
The rooster crowed loudly for the second time.
Yŏnghwa stood up as if startled, then sat down again and said seriously,
"Please go back to your wife."
"To my wife?"
Ch'ŏlsu too was startled.
"I understand your kind intentions. But, but—"
A choked sound escaped her for a moment.<302>
"How can I inflict the suffering I endured onto someone else?"
"Don't be too disheartened."
Ch'ŏlsu also felt strangely sorrowful.
In the quiet night of the mountain lodge, looking at the beautiful gisaeng weeping before him, he strangely felt no base desire arise.
"Since you regret it so much, act like a man and step forward decisively. Imagine how much your wife must be regretting it, just like me."
Her words were again soaked in tears.
"The sorrow when my husband remarried and started a new family—thinking of it still breaks my heart. How could I possibly inflict such wickedness on someone else?"
Yŏnghwa suddenly became eloquent.
"I am already a fallen woman trapped in this pit. I will be content with the memory of advising you. Please, follow your heart and apologize readily. Not only your wife, but your parents-in-law too, will be overjoyed."

The next day, Ch'ŏlsu's steps leaving the village were incredibly light.
Prompted by Yŏnghwa's persuasion and advice throughout the night, he had made a great resolution and was now returning to his wife. Looking back at the slope, Yŏnghwa stood there.
Having insisted on seeing him off despite his firm refusal, she looked as beautiful as a fairy descended from heaven.
Sunlight (해ㅅ발), unusual for a winter day, gently fell upon Yŏnghwa's head as she waved her handkerchief indefinitely.
-(End)-<303>

**Summary**

The story "A Lesson" by Chang Tŏkcho follows Ch'ŏlsu, a man in his early thirties, who impulsively divorces his wife after a minor argument stemming from their childlessness. Filled with regret, he travels to Onjŏng-ri, their honeymoon destination, hoping to revisit memories. There, he encounters a gisaeng named Yŏnghwa who strikingly resembles his wife. Yŏnghwa shares her own story of a failed marriage due to stubbornness and regret, mirroring Ch'ŏlsu's situation. Moved by her empathy and counsel urging him to reconcile, Ch'ŏlsu resolves to return and apologize to his wife. The story highlights the destructive nature of pride ("gojip") in relationships and the importance of forgiveness and reconciliation.

From a historical perspective, the story offers insights into marital dynamics, divorce, and social expectations in 1930s Korea. The casual mention of divorce initiated over a seemingly minor issue (compounded by stubbornness and parental interference), the protagonist's subsequent regret and travel to a known resort area (Kŭmgangsan/Onjŏng-ri), and the encounter with a gisaeng (who herself is educated and articulate about her past) reflect aspects of modernizing Korean society under colonial rule, including changing attitudes towards marriage, personal freedom, and the presence of leisure travel and entertainment venues. The author's biographical sketch also points to the emergence of female writers and their engagement with social issues.

作家點描

女士는 短篇作家로서 우리 文壇에 不拔의 地位를 가지고 잇다, 이미 발표된 것만 해도 「안해」 「어미와 딸」 等 10여편을 헤이는 데 一作이 나올 때마다 문단에 驚異를 던젓다. 고향은 대구근교의 慈仁, 芳紀 22, 일즉 大邱女子高普를 中途退學, 京城培花女高를 졸업, 梨花女子專門學校 英文科에 다니다가 中途退學, 그 뒤 開闢社 記者로 잇슨 일도 잇다.

왼편은 깍근 듯한 산마루
바른편은 어름 언 개울
그 사이 좁은 길을 철수는 정신업시 걸엇다. 얼마를 왓는지 외투주머니에 너흔 두 손이 훈훈하게 땀까지 배여오며 비탈은 점점 어려운 산골로 접어들엇건만 그는 넉 일흔 사람같이 그저 뚜벅뚜벅 발만 옴겨 노앗다.
몃 날의 번뇌.
몃 밤의 불민.
그의 벌거케 충혈된 눈은 누가 보아도 깊은 서름에 젖어잇는 것을 알 수 잇슬 만하다.
「허 그참 족음만 참앗드면 아무일도 업섯슬껄!」
석상같이 담을고 잇는 그의 입이 벌눔거리는 듯 하드니 또다시 백번도 더 토햇슬 이 넉두리가 굴너 나왓다.

「그래 하도 화가 나서 몃 번이나 죽어버릴까 하는 생각까지 낫지만 마음을 도슬녀 먹고 일부러 이 구렁이에까지 빠저서 남편 아닌 남자들의 비위 마추어 들이는 것으로 소일을 한답니다.
후우 한숨을 쉬이며 그는 다시 손수건으로 코와 눈을 씻는다.
「세상에 같은 사정도 잇소」
철수도 비로소 깊은 생각에서 깬 듯이 무거운 입을 열엇다.
「왜 누가 또 저 같은 사람이 잇세요? 글세 이런 원통한 이가 또 어데 잇겟세요」
「모다 바른 말이오. 정말 부부사이란 제일 중요한 것이 양보하는 것 그리고는 참고 참고 또 참는 것 박게 아무 것도 업슬 것이오」
그는 진정에서 울어나는 목소리로 힘잇게 말하엿다.
「참말이에요. 저도 혹 이 담에라도 남의 안해가 될 수 잇다면 이번에야말노 얼마든지 참지요. 이만하면 남자 어룬들의 심리들도 모다 알게 쯤 되고-」하며 기생은 우섯다.
철수는 그것이 조곰도 거짓말이 아닐 상 십헛다.
「속 티인 여자」그는 이러케 가만히 부르지즈며 다시금 영화의 아름다운 아미를 바라보앗다.
「그럿소 이제 다시 혼인하게 된다면 참 안해를 위할 것 같구려. 첫재 참고 그까짓 고집 좀 안부리지...」그러나 그 기생은 여전히 고개를 숙이고 앉앗다.
「그러치만 맛당한 사람이 어데 쉬워야 하지」
철수의 말에는 다른 의미가 포함되엿건만 영화는 아는지 모르는지 가만히 잇다.
닭이 두 번째 회를 높이 울엇다.
영화는 놀난 듯이 이러서랴다 말고 다시 앉으며
「부인께로 돌아가십시요」하며 정색을 한다.
「부인께로?」
수도 놀랏다.
「저두 고마운 뜻은 알아요. 그러치만 그러치만-」
한참 느끼는 소리가 나드니<302>
「내가 당하든 이 고통을 어떠케 다른 사람에게 또 당케해요」
「넘우 상심마시오」
철수도 공연히 비감해젓다.
고요한 산장의 밤, 무릎 앞에 엎어저 우는 아름다운 기생을 보면서도 그는 이상하게도 아무 더러운 정욕이 일어나지 안앗다.
「그처럼 후회하시고 게시거든 사내 어룬답게 선듯 눙치고 나서십시요. 부인께서도 저처럼 얼마나 후회하고 게실까」
그의 말은 또 눈물에 젓엇다.
「내 남편이 재혼하고 새 살님을 일우웟슬 때의 슯흠-그것을 생각하면 지금도 가삼이 미여저요. 글세 그런 적악을 어떠케 남에게 또 하겟습니까」
영화는 갑자기 웅변이 되엿다.」
「저는 임의 구렁이에 빠진 타락한 몸 당신에게 권고한 이 기억으로 만족할 터이니 부대 마음 잡수신 대로 선뜻 사과하고 나서세요. 부인께서는 물논 장인장모께서도 얼마나 깁버하실나구」

다음날 이 마을을 떠나는 철수의 발길은 한업시 가벼웟다.
어저께 밤을 새워가며 권하고 타일느든 영화의 말에 께끼어 큰 결심을 하고 지금 안해에게로 돌아가는 것이다. 돌아보는 비탈 위 거기에는 영화가 서 잇섯다.
구지 사양하는 것도 듯지안코 이러케 배웅을 나온 그의 모양이 하늘에서 나려선 선녀같이 곱게 보인다.
겨을날답지안은 해ㅅ발이 언제까지나 수건을 흔들고 잇는 영화의 머리 우에 나긋이 나리고 잇섯다.
