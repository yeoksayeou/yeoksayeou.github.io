Shanghai Hsin Shen Pao Reaches 70,000 Copies
The "Hsin Shen Pao" (新申報), the organ newspaper of the new government in Shanghai, which had only about 10,000 readers until last summer, is now said to be publishing over 70,000 copies.

Distribution of "Peace Participation Badges"
Previously, our Japanese military used to drop leaflets called "Surrender Tickets" (投降票) over enemy lines by airplane to encourage surrender. Now, it is said that instead of surrender, they are dropping items called "Peace Movement Participation Badges" (和平運動叅加章) to encourage participation in the peace movement.

Kungmin Sinbo Renamed
Due to circumstances, the "Kungmin Sinbo" (國民新報), which has been published by the Maeil Sinbo Company in the city, will soon be renamed "Ch'ŏngnyŏn Sinbo" (靑年新報) and published as a quarto-sized newspaper.<11>

Mitsubishi Tax Bill: 38 Million Yen
Due to Capital Increase to 240 Million Yen
As various subsidiary companies under the financial kingdom Mitsubishi have all been expanding their businesses under the recent wartime situation, it was decided to procure the necessary paid-in capital. Thus, a capital increase was decided upon, doubling the current 120 million yen to 240 million yen. At the same time, it was decided to widely offer shares previously held exclusively by the Iwasaki family (岩崎家一門). Due to this, the taxes paid to the state amount to a huge sum of 38 million yen. Furthermore, listing the major companies currently in the Mitsubishi group, starting with the 8 direct affiliates Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, Mitsubishi Mining & Trading, Mitsubishi Electric, Mitsubishi Warehouse, Mitsubishi Estate, Mitsubishi Bank, and Mitsubishi Trust, it is said that the number of related companies in munitions and heavy industries, including Asahi Glass, is twice as large.<15>

**Summary**

This article from July 1940 primarily lists Korean individuals holding high-ranking government positions within the Japanese puppet state of Manchoukuo, including recent appointees. It details their names, ranks (Special, *Kan'im*, *Ch'ŏnim*), and specific roles across various ministries, provinces, and agencies like the Concordia Association. Shorter news items report on the increased circulation of a pro-Japanese newspaper in Shanghai, a shift in Japanese propaganda leaflets from demanding surrender to encouraging "peace participation," the renaming of a Korean newspaper, and a massive capital increase and associated tax burden for the Mitsubishi conglomerate, highlighting its expansion driven by the wartime situation.

From a historical perspective, the extensive list of Korean officials serving Manchoukuo is highly significant. It provides concrete evidence of the degree of Korean collaboration and integration into the Japanese imperial structure during this period. This challenges simplistic narratives and offers historians specific names and positions for studying the complexities of colonial administration, elite participation, and the functioning of Japanese imperialism in Manchuria. The mention of the Concordia Association (協和會) also points to the mechanisms used for political control and mobilization. The other news snippets provide context on wartime media, propaganda strategies, and the economic mobilization supporting the Japanese war effort.
