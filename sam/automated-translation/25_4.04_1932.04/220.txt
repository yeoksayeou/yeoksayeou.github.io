Article Title: 巴里畵家生活, 巴里의 모델과 畵家生活 (Paris Painter Life, Paris Models and Painter Life)
Author: 在東京 羅蕙錫
Article Type: 논설 (Editorial/Essay)

Methods of Study
—(Continued from previous issue)—First, from an East Asian perspective, if one has the skill level to be selected for perhaps the second section of the Japan Imperial Art Exhibition (日本帝展), they rent an atelier, employ a model, paint them in arbitrary poses, and meanwhile visit the Louvre (루불), Luxembourg (룩삼불) museums or art dealer exhibitions, taking note of good points for reference. In this way, they prepare works for submission to the Salon or for special selection in the Japan Imperial Art Exhibition.

Second, regardless of one's original skill, if one wants to paint something satisfactory to others' eyes and one's own during a short stay in Paris, one first chooses their most preferred painting style and artist. Then, they go to the studio guided by that teacher. In this case, since they imitate the brushwork according to the teacher's guidance, it is common for their paintings to become very similar to the teacher's.

Third, to synthesize the critiques of masters, that is, teachers at various private studios, one attends different studios here and there for one month, two months, or even a year. By doing so, one takes only the strengths and creates their own style.

In any case, upon first arriving in Paris and seeing the paintings in museums or art galleries, one feels overwhelmed, and the sense of self becomes so insignificant that discouragement sets in for a time. It feels as though if there were a clump of pollack roe, the masters' paintings are the whole clump, while oneself is merely a single egg within it. Thus, grasping the state and knack of the art world and proceeding with a method of study requires considerable wandering and distress. During this time, a year or two passes quickly, and just as one is about to settle down somewhat, circumstances often require them to return. In short, anyone coming to Paris must first break down their own painting style and then rebuild it, so to settle down reasonably well, one needs to stay for at least four or five years.

The Painter's Life
It is said there are 50,000 painters in the city of Paris. Most are international students or government-sponsored students from abroad, but there are also those who study by selling their paintings. Just as there are people who do not hesitate to spend tens of thousands of gold on famous paintings, if one produces a masterpiece,<78> tens of thousands of gold can be earned, allowing one to live lavishly at once. At the same time, if one lacks renown, the hardship is indescribable, even becoming so dire as to catch and roast rats to eat.

They are indifferent to social issues or the various problems appearing in newspapers; their life is extremely peaceful, and simultaneously, they focus on creation, always hoping for tranquility and fulfillment. As they use their minds intensely, nervousness is common, and they like to do, see, and hear anything unusual or strange. The painter's morning sleep is famous; they sleep late in the morning, wake up, have a simple breakfast of bread and tea (often skipped when unavailable), put on their smock (畵服, painting clothes), light a cigarette (the painter's cigarette is famous), go before the easel, tilt their head, narrow their eyes, and look at the painting from the previous day when the model enters. The model poses nude or clothed, and the painter begins to paint. Sometimes they take a knife and tear the canvas; sometimes they cheerfully sing songs loudly.

They paint until noon and send the model away. They make a simple lunch themselves and eat. In the afternoon, to rest their minds, they visit old and new ateliers, search magazine offices for reference materials, or visit art dealers and exhibitions to resolve points that troubled them. Those engrossed in study might spend time drinking coffee (코피) or doing croquis (구로기, sketches). Studying like this is, of course, done with the hope and need to become a master in the future, but for the immediate goal, it is to prepare works for submission to the Salon, or if they are foreigners, for exhibitions upon returning to their home country. Thus, as the Salon opening date approaches, they shut themselves in their studios to create paintings or go sketching in the suburbs, remembering the submission date and submitting their work. On the day the selections are announced, some wake even from their morning slumber to grab the newspaper and become furious, while for others, the newspaper is brought by the hands of parents or spouses. If extremely agitated about a work, many become ill or mentally deranged. If their study progresses smoothly up to selection in the Salon, it can generally be considered a success. However, there is nothing as intense as the metabolism of art; one must continue studying ceaselessly and exert considerable effort. Effort, rather than genius, is seen as enabling success.

Painters and Recreation
The urban facilities, park arrangements, everything in Paris is artistic, of course, and theater, motion pictures—none are not works of art. What especially helps painters find new inspiration are the dance halls. In Montparnasse (몬발나스), being a painters' district, there are many cheap and simple dance halls. Inside barracks (바락크), bands of indigenous people (土人種) play clarinets and beat gongs (깽가리) as they please, shrugging their shoulders and dancing, and men and women embrace each other, shuffling along to the music, then the lights go out for about 10 minutes at a time. Painters spend the night like this, drinking, laughing heartily, and dancing, and the next day (翌日), they approach the canvas with a refreshed, new feeling. Going to the theater, opera, or motion pictures, one finds that none are not excavators (採掘者) of beauty, and all become references. Paris is necessary for painters, and Paris calls painters. Not only painters, but whether poor or rich, anyone can play joyfully, and whether old or not, who wouldn't yearn for Paris, where people play like children?

Painters and Models
In Paris, a model is respected like a work of art, and even women from respectable families may become models if they have a good physique, though most are from the proletariat (무산계급). As for their conduct (품행) and chastity (貞操), it is such that there is little need to speak of it. These women all have abundant knowledge (상식) of painting and possess sympathy (동정) and understanding (이해) for painters. If there is a painter they like and who shows future promise (前途), they not only help the painter with all their might, but since models naturally know many people and hold sway in social circles, they introduce and sell the paintings of their favored artists here and there. The reason the Japanese Mr. Fujita (藤田) achieved such success was due to Madame Fujita, who became his wife after being his model. The author also met Madame Fujita once, and her charm that day held much allure. -one cannot help but respect (尊敬) the power of women. The same must be true for female painters (女流畵家) in relation to men (男性間). Ah! Ah! After seeing the countless paintings in the Louvre and Luxembourg museums in Paris, it is necessary to remember (記憶) why painters who have painted until now throw away their paintings, and at the same time, those who haven't painted start anew.

Truly, nothing should be as indulged as the mind of an artist (藝術家). If one receives even a pinprick's (바눌만콘) worth of stimulus (刺戟) from anything, it becomes a hint (힌트) and manifests greatly in their work. This happens not only in times of happiness (幸福) but is also obtained from misfortune (不幸) and loneliness (孤寂). Therefore, they drink to get drunk (醉해도), play with the opposite sex (異性을 고), and also sequester themselves (蟄居) in remote mountains and poor villages. Thus, when they find this hint and are in a state of artistic excitement (興趣), they work through the night (徹夜) producing (製作); conversely, when they yawn (합픔), there are many times when they find it repulsive (슬혀지난) to even lift the brush once. However, there is something we must not forget (아니될것이잇다). The modern era is an age of science (科學時代), so painting also has scientific tendencies (科學的 傾向), and as the struggle for existence (生存競爭) is severe, works of substance (實力的作品) are demanded more than works based on mood (氣分的作品). That is, while paintings were once created in times of happiness, now things have become rational (理智的), where one paints to become happy, requiring ceaseless effort (不絶한 努力) and awaiting profound genius (深刻한 天才).

**Summary**

This article by Na Hye-sŏk describes the life of painters, particularly foreign students, in Paris during the early 1930s. It details three common approaches to studying art: independent study combined with museum visits, apprenticing under a specific master, or sampling various studios. Na emphasizes the initial discouragement faced by newcomers overwhelmed by the Parisian art scene and the necessity of staying several years to find one's footing. The piece portrays the painters' bohemian lifestyle—late mornings, simple meals, intense periods of creation often fueled by hardship or inspiration found in Parisian culture like dance halls, and the anxiety surrounding Salon submissions. It also highlights the significant role of models, often working-class women, who not only posed but actively supported and promoted painters they believed in, citing the example of Tsuguharu Foujita's wife. For historians, this article offers a rare contemporary perspective from a Korean female artist living abroad during the colonial period. It reveals insights into the aspirations of East Asian artists seeking recognition in the Western art world, the perceived hierarchy (referencing the Japan Imperial Art Exhibition as a benchmark), the challenging yet alluring environment of Paris, and the complex relationship between artists and models, including commentary on the influence of women like Madame Fujita.
