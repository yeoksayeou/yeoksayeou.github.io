A Medical Student's Experience Reveals the Latest Treatment for Amenorrhea
						From Ōsaka, memoir by Takahashi Ayako

My body is still frail. With the graduation exams looming at the end of February pushed aside, the topic among us female students turned to happy rumors and delightful stories about life after graduation, which is like happiness itself. But what should I alone do? For some reason, my head felt heavy, and my heart pounded strangely, making it difficult to sleep well at night. It wasn't just fatigue from studying for the graduation exams. The cause?

It was amenorrhea (月經閉止), something embarrassing and frightening to speak of. Until this spring, when I turned 19, I had spent each day innocently and cheerfully like a child. Even my monthly visitor was so regular I barely noticed. Health itself.

But what happened? The menstruation that occurred every month suddenly stopped completely. Since then, both my health and even my personality have completely changed. My physical condition gradually worsened: I lost my appetite, my heart pounded causing stuffiness, I felt a tearing sensation in my lower abdomen, and suffered from headaches, flushing (上氣), and other unpleasant symptoms that kept appearing. Everything I saw and heard became a seed of anguish and sorrow. The cheerful dreams of student life became so gloomy I didn't even want to look back. Even the pleasant stories of my companions, talking about burning future hopes and aspirations (憧憬), left me alone, lost in gloomy thoughts. Time passed, graduation arrived, and April came with flowers blooming profusely in the fields and mountains. The cherry blossoms scattered beautifully, and the world clamored, celebrating the happiness of spring. But I was still in a sad state, unable to celebrate that happiness. Already three months. What should I do? Would I never see my second menstruation?

It was a time when I couldn't talk about it even to my own siblings, and though I had close friends, they were of no use.

Having been born into a family not entirely unconnected to medicine (醫藥), I couldn't help but be swayed by the countless newspaper and magazine advertisements appearing daily, trying one after another. How much was I deceived by old-fashioned pill remedies (丸藥) that seemed to have no proper name? I was so foolish. Wandering amidst worthless medicines that preyed on people through advertisements, my rewards were only abdominal pain, diarrhea (下痢), and the waste of much money. The anguish (煩悶) I had felt until now turned into anxiety (焦慮), plunging me into distress where I could neither stand nor sit. How could I escape this suffering with my weak strength? Ah – but it was then! What saved me from the agony of death was...!

Should I call this entirely God's blessing? The name of that miracle drug (靈藥), "Sanggŏ," invented by the famous birth control advocate, Mrs. Sanger (상거―夫人) – seeing this made my heart pound strangely. For me, who had suffered headaches from old-fashioned emmenagogues (月やく下し藥), it was a sudden glimmer of hope that this illness of mine could surely be cured by Western medicine, the essence of the latest science. Having taken various menstruation-inducing drugs repeatedly until now without effect, I didn't quite believe that even "Sanggŏ," the latest tablet (錠劑) for internal use (內服劑), would be effective so quickly. But what happened? With the astonishingly small dosage of 2 tablets once, 3 times a day, on the morning of the third day, the menstruation I had waited and waited for came with smooth relief (快通)! The joy and amazement at that time were beyond words. A dream, a dream, a dream! To me now, the painful thoughts of those three agonizing months seem like nothing more than a night's dream. Thanks to "Sanggŏ," I have been completely saved. Without regard for embarrassment, I confess my experience here to the women of the world suffering like me, and I intend to specially distribute (頒配) the miraculous menstruation-inducing drug "Sanggŏ" myself to save the world (救世). Please write down the number of months your menstruation has stopped and send a letter right away to Takahashi Ayako, c/o Takahashi Clinic, Tenmabashi Minamizume, Ōsaka City (大阪市天滿橋南詰高橋療院內 高橋あや子宛). I will reply in detail. Also, for those in a hurry, please enclose 30 sen in stamps (切手) with your application (申込), and I will send the appropriate medicine immediately (至急付送). For those wishing to visit, take the city tram (市電) to Tenmabashi Minamizume (天滿橋南詰下車), and you will find it right away at the Shimamachi corner (島町角).＜59＞

**Summary**

This article, presented as a personal testimonial by a Japanese medical student named Takahashi Ayako living in Ōsaka, describes her suffering from amenorrhea (missed periods) and her eventual cure using a "miracle drug" called "Sanggŏ." She recounts the failure of traditional remedies and the physical and emotional distress caused by her condition before finding relief with "Sanggŏ," which she attributes to the famous birth control advocate Margaret Sanger. The article concludes with an offer by Takahashi to distribute the medicine herself to other women suffering from the same condition, providing contact details for her clinic in Ōsaka. From a historical perspective, this piece is interesting as it appears in a Korean magazine ("Samch'ŏlli") during the colonial period but is written by a Japanese individual residing in Japan, advertising a Western-style medication attributed (possibly dubiously) to Margaret Sanger. It highlights the transnational flow of medical information (and advertising), the anxieties surrounding women's health, the blend of traditional and modern medical approaches, and the use of personal testimonials in marketing new pharmaceutical products in early 20th-century East Asia. Historians might find the Japanese author targeting a Korean audience, the specific mention of Sanger, and the advertising strategy noteworthy.
