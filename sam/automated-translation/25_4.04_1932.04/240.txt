My Romantic Times
A Stroll by the Huangpu Riverbank in Shanghai
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn

You want me to write about my "romantic" days? Yes, I'll do as you ask. <84>
My first period, well, there's the story of my first love written in *Samch'ŏlli* last month, wasn't there? Ah yes, that tale about the platonic first love with the housemaid (下婢) named Okdan (玉丹?), or someone like that. That was right after seeing Cho Ilchae's (趙一齋) *Changhŏnmong* (長恨夢) via the *Maeil Sinbo* (每日申報). It seems it was probably a little before seeing Ch'unwŏn's (春園) *Mujŏng* (無情). It also seems to have been around the time I was rereading, for the second or third time, a translated excerpt of Tokutomi Roka's (德富蘆花) *Hototogisu* (不如歸), which had a simple drawing of a pink bindweed (石竹花) on the cover. And my second period was probably when I went all the way to Shanghai (上海) with grand ambitions, supposedly to study abroad. The school fees, which had been arriving barely enough to keep me going, finally stopped. Half a year later, I lost the last 500 won of funds that arrived to a pickpocket. Ah, I thought about just throwing myself into the Huangpu River (黃浦江) and dying, but I couldn't even die. After spending the entire night standing by the Hongkou Bridge (虹口橋畔), a "romanticism" born as a reaction (反動) from the edge of despair seemed to dominate me then. The only time I've ever written poetry was also then. Submitting poems under the pen name Pyŏkp'a (碧波) to the *Tonga Ilbo* (東亞일보), seeing my simple No. 4 font poems published at a rate of once every four or five submissions, I felt self-consoled as if I'd achieved great success. Furthermore, when an English short poem titled "Rain" (비) that I submitted to "Yŏngŏ Yŏn'gu" (영어연구), which I subscribed to then, was published as an honorable mention (選外佳作), I couldn't sleep one night; that must have been then too. And unable to study and starving in a foreign land (異域), I passed the exam for one proofreader (校正기자) position advertised by the Japanese-language paper *Shanghai Nichinichi Shimbun* (上海 日日신문) on Wuzhou Road (梧州路) and joined the company. As fate would have it, I authored a chapter for the editorial staff's collaborative novel "Aoba no Sora" (靑葉の空). Then, due to the Great Kantō Earthquake (日本 大震災), the serialization of a novel by someone like Nagata Mikihiko (長田幹彦?) was interrupted, so I serialized a sweet, "romantic" novel titled "Yurin" (蹂躪) under the name Tokkyŏn (獨鵑). Receiving letters from Japanese schoolgirls (日女학생) and such riffraff telling me it was interesting (滋味잇다) and feeling a ticklish pleasure—that period must have been quite "romantic" too.

Even more than that, the most romantic period must have been when I chose the pen name Tokkyŏn (獨鵑) without discussing it with anyone. When I lay down at my home in Sinch'ŏn (信川), opening the north window (北窓), the sound of the wind mixed with the sighing of pines (松籟) would drift in—swish—and above that wind, the sound of the cuckoo (뻐국이) could be dimly heard; that must have been the time. It was when I was planning to write a novel far more tear-jerking and interesting than *Hototogisu* or *Mujŏng*. What I conceived then, after undergoing several revisions in my mind, became *Kwaebang Pigok* (儈房悲曲). Therefore, in terms of gestational age, *Kwaebang Pigok* is like the older sister to *Yurin*, which was published in Japanese.

There's no need to speak at length. I confess that I am still in a romantic period even now. A period that doesn't yield to any other—I think about how I need to quickly shed this darned period to become a bit of an adult myself, but it's so hard to take off this old rag (헌 누덕이). What should I do? "Romanticism For Free Transfer - Buy This Old Rag." (로맨틕시즘 無料讓渡-헌누덕이드렁사우)

Wandering to Siberia
Kim Tongjin

The time I left P'yŏngyang (平壤) and crossed over to the foreign land (異域) of Vladivostok (海參威) was still my boyhood, at age 14. If people yearn for their cradle days until death, how could I not miss my beautiful homeland P'yŏngyang, a land of rivers and mountains (錦繡江山), when the ice formed on Kimgak Bay (金角灣) and fierce snowstorms raged!

Yearning for my homeland, I wandered Siberia (西伯利亞). Rowing a boat (뽀트) in Kimgak Bay brought associations of the Taedong River (大同江); bathing in the Amur (아무르) made me long for the sandbank (모래밧) of Tongdaewŏn (東大院); whenever the cold snow swirled, I thought of the shade of the weeping willows (垂楊) on Nŭngna Islet (綾羅島). In adolescence (사춘기), the most sensitive period, the atmosphere of life changed abruptly; <85> how could the waves in a young man's heart remain calm? A young man's beautiful dream (꿈)! Whether others called it illusion or fantasy, this boy drunk in dreams didn't care. Gazing at the southern sky (南天), constantly looking at the endless, vast southern sky, I dreamed infinite beautiful dreams. Dreams tangled in a thousand, ten thousand ways, dreamed again and again—during this time, I approached the age of 20.

During that time, I studied in S Town (S市邑). It was a mountain village (山村) by the western water fortress (西水郭). The water flowed abundantly, and the mountains were steep (險峻하다). Perhaps it was where the Jurchen tribe (女眞族) formerly dwelt (藩居하든 곳); traces of an earthen fortress (土城) were distinct, and old roof tiles (古瓦) were discovered underground. Here, we sang military songs (軍歌) loudly and learned military drills (兵式체조). Every night, lecture meetings and debate societies were held, and there were lectures on tactics and speeches on the current situation by militia leaders (義兵대장) or our officers who had fought (出戰하얏든) in the Great European War (歐洲大戰).

We also put on amateur plays (素人劇) almost weekly. We went into trenches (塹壕) to prevent raids by bandits (馬賊), climbed onto makeshift artillery platforms (假設砲臺) built like lookout shacks (원두막), and even practiced responding to attacks by poking gun barrels through loopholes (銃孔). What was there to fear!

The spirit of youth reaching for the heavens (意氣衝天할 志慨)! In this school, there was a teacher named P (P선생). He is now deceased (故人). Since this teacher was an exile (亡命客) who hadn't received a Russian education, he was like oil on water among the teachers who provided Russian education. Most students were also born there and naturalized Russians (露國歸化), while newcomers (新來種) like myself numbered only 5 or 6.

I, too, along with Teacher P, received the treatment of heretics (異端人). Therefore, we newcomers admired Teacher P, who was also a newcomer. Teacher P was specially invited from Kando (間島) to teach Korean (朝鮮글) to the students. Teacher P was renowned for his knowledge of Chinese classics (漢學). However, he wasn't a scholar who only memorized. He also recited sijo (時調) and knew history well. In his spare time, he would explain sijo to us and teach us from history readers mimeographed (謄寫板) by hand. Through this guidance (薰陶), we came to know Korean studies (朝鮮學).

One day, with about three classmates (學우), I carried a gun (銃) up to the old Jurchen fortress site (女眞古城址), threw out my chest, loudly shouted "Manse!" (만세), and returned to write my impressions (감상문). Teacher P added corrections (加筆하야) and distributed it to the entire student body. From this time, I developed a fondness for writing. Strolling through the birch forest (白樺林) mountain village by the water fortress, carrying a gun on my shoulder, reminiscing (懷憶) about the distant past, how could there not be poetic sentiment (詩趣)? If I had fortunately possessed a poet's disposition, perhaps I might have become a famous poet.

If luck had been with me then, perhaps I could have cultivated my sentiments (情操) a bit more in S Village (S촌), which could be called a separate world (別有天地) within Siberia, but unfortunately, I had no choice but to leave the place. This was because my relationship (새이) with Teacher L (L선생) was not good. One doesn't necessarily need to leave school just because of a conflict with one teacher, but anyway, the thought of wanting to leave arose, so I left that place. The teacher taught mathematics (수학). Still young, he had a very frivolous (輕嫖한) nature. In the house where he lodged, there lived a girl named O (O처녀) who attended a Russian girls' middle school (露女中학교). Her looks were above average, and she had an unusual interest in things Korean, borrowing rare Korean novels (소설)—novels probably meant things like Ch'unwŏn's *Mujŏng*—and literary books (문학서적)—probably things like classical prose and poetry readers (時文讀本)—from me. "She seemed quite melancholic (哀傷的)," I thought. The teacher probably fancied the girl and disliked my association (교제) with her, treating me unpleasantly in all matters. Around that time, some political interference reached the school, and the school's fortunes (校運) declined. Teacher P, whom we admired most, was also preparing to leave, and the Russian army captain (露國군대 대위씨), who had been welcomed for teaching military drills, was recalled and left. I firmly resolved and, together with Teacher P, bade farewell (작별하얏다) to S Village, to the girl who tried to be a sympathizer (同情者) to a lonely traveler, to the old tiles and stone clusters (古瓦石簇), and to the nature that inspired poetry (詩*).

At a friend's recommendation, I went to the large city H (H市) on the bank of the Amur River (아무르 江岸). The muddy great river (黃濁한 大江) flowed majestically, and on the embankment (岸頭), the bronze statue (銅像) of Mr. Muravyov-Amursky (무라비용·아무르스키), the pioneer credited with managing the Far East, stood imposing, holding a rolled-up map in one hand and glaring (노려보고) southward. I too will become a soldier (軍人). I will become a soldier. Who can declare that my bronze statue won't stand in that position in the future? Who can say there won't be a day when my bronze statue stands like that atop Ch'oesŭngdae (最勝臺) on Moran Hill (모란峰) in my hometown! <86> Every day, every evening, I climbed up to Muravyov-Amursky Park (무라비용·아무르스키 공원), gazed upon the vast plains (曠野) of Manchuria (滿洲), and began to dream of being a future hero (영웅).

Just then, the Allied forces (列國군대) occupied H City. The land of the Maritime Province (沿海州) could not escape the ravages of war (兵禍) due to the conflict between the Red Guards (赤衛軍) and the Allied Forces. The cities were not severely affected, but the villages were terribly devastated (蹂躪), reaching a state where habitation (居接할 수가 업는) was impossible. I heard that D Middle School (D중학) in S Town had also been closed. One evening in late autumn, when it was already time to prepare winter clothes (방한복), while taking my daily (日課) stroll (逍遙) in the park, unexpectedly, quite unexpectedly, I encountered (상봉하얏다) the girl from S Village (S촌 처녀). What a coincidence (奇遇)! Her family had abandoned the unstable S Village and moved (移徙하야) to H City. The unexpected arrival of the S Village girl was like pouring a cup of the water of life (生命水) into my withering young heart. With Teacher L gone and no jealous (猜氣만흔) classmates around, the association of the two young people in this place was completely free. Night after night, listening to the mournful sound of the snowstorm beating against the stovepipe (뻬치까 煙筒), we unfolded literary books and lost track of the late hour. I transmitted (傳授하고) the Korean ancient sijo (朝鮮古詩調) I learned from Teacher P to her, and she explained (해설하얏다) Pushkin's (푸스킨) prose poems (散文詩) and Turgenev's (뚜루게넵) lyric poems (敍情詩) to me. Truly, it was from this time that I began to gain some interest in Russian literature (露문학). Until then, my Russian studies had consisted solely of history and language.

I stopped going to the park often. Even when I went to the park, I didn't seek out Count "Mu"'s (무라비용·아무르스키) bronze statue. My sole pleasure was walking on the white snow-covered streets, wearing boots (장화) and making crunching (빠작빠작) sounds. Of course, with the girl.

The XX passion (XX적 열정) that bloomed in my heart gradually cooled. Only the desire to create (창작욕), the desire to publish (발표욕), and affection (戀慕) for the girl remained. Just then, there was a suggestion from friends in Vladivostok (海參威) to publish a coterie magazine (同人雜誌), so I left H City and returned to Vladivostok. Upon arrival, I found that while the funds were considerable, the printing press (兵火에 燒却되얏다.) was gone (burned in the war). So, we arranged for Mr. Yi (李君), who had just arrived from Seoul after finishing some higher common school (高普), to handle the writing (筆當케 하야), and began publishing a lithographed (석판인쇄) magazine called "Munhwa" (문화), issued every ten days (旬刊). While editing the third issue, the Japanese army (日本軍) and the Red Guard (赤衛軍), which had once reached a compromise, clashed again, turning the streets into deadly ground (죽업의 거리). That evening, a telegram (전보) reported that H City was almost completely burned down (全燒되얏다). Then what about the girl? No matter how I tried, there was no way to find out.

About a month after the military uprising (兵變), I visited H City. It was sad. Everything had turned to scorched earth (焦土). Only the lower half of the stove chimney (뻬치까의 굴독) remained as a trace of the place where she and I used to sit facing each other. The entire street was terrifyingly deserted (두려 빠젓슴으로); I couldn't even find her teacher. How many times did I wipe away tears amidst the desolate ruins (荒凉한 폐허)!

Dragging my listless steps, I returned to Vladivostok. I forgot Pushkin's prose poems, which I used to recite fondly. The beautiful dreams were broken. I gave up (단념햇다) auditing classes at D University (D대학). I handed over "Munhwa" entirely to Mr. R (R군). My pastime became wandering (방황하는) the birch forests (白樺林) in the resort area (別莊地) of Okeanskaya (오케안스카) and losing myself in baseball (야구) and soccer (축구) in the sports club of the YMCA (Y·M·C·A).

Seven years after parting, I heard through a letter (傳便) that the girl had escaped safely (其後無事히), gone to a large city in North Manchuria (北滿), married, and is living there now. Her mother, who had shown me kindness, died from a stray bullet (流彈), and her father is said to still be alive.

Under the Paulownia Tree—A Tiny Truckload of Memory—
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi

Up until now, I haven't had a time I could call a romantic period. Nevertheless, I've taken up my pen under the title "My Romantic Times," so perhaps my courage (용긔만은) <87> is decent, but there's no way a writing topic will pop out of an empty—hair-thin—head (터-O뷔인 머리).
So, should I just blame the unmoving pen? There's nothing for it; since I can't write under the given topic, I'll try writing about a tiny truckload (한끗트렉의 사실) of fact that I still can't forget (?).
The time was five years ago from now.
Spring was nice too, but I liked the autumn season (씨―슨).
Perhaps it was a kind of abnormal psychology (변태심리), but while I disliked it, I also enjoyed seeing the pitiful state (가여운 정상을) of yellowed autumn leaves (입사귀) scattering here and there in the chilly wind, more than the sprouting green grass (금잔듸).
It was when the lights-out (소등 消燈) time in the dormitory (긔숙사) had already passed, and the inside of the dorm (사내 舍內), which had been buzzing like boiling water, was extremely quiet. I had gone to bed to observe the dormitory rules, but somehow sleep wouldn't come; my eyes just grew clearer, and my head only ached.
My other roommates (동모들은) seemed to be snoring away, weaving sweet dreams (고흔 꿈을 맷는 듯 십헛슴니다).
The pleading sound (애원성) of the falling leaves and the shadow of the paulownia tree (오동나무) flickering on the front window (압창) tempted me outside. Besides the large paulownia tree in the dormitory yard (뜰), there were no other miscellaneous trees (잡목), so for me, who enjoyed solitude (고독), that rather desolate scene (장면) appealed to me.
I avoided the brightly lit places and sought out a dim spot—under the paulownia tree (오동나무 밋흘).
The crescent moon (초생달) seemed to be dozing above Namsan (남산) in front, as if cursing (저주하는 듯 하엿슴니다) the electric light—that product of civilization (문화의 산물).
Not knowing what to do with my melancholy (우울하고) and anxious (안타가운) heart (가삼), I was walking stealthily towards the Hyangsŏllyo (香雪寮) building to lure out my most understanding friend S (S동모), trying to muffle my footsteps (발자최 소리), when the flow of a mysterious melody (멜노듸) reached my ears from somewhere (어듸서인가). I lost both the thought and the courage (용긔도) to fetch friend S, and my feet began to walk (걸ㅅ게 되엿나이다) towards the faintly audible melody.
The melody, growing steadily closer (갓갓히), was not the sound of a piano, nor the tune of a violin (바요링).
It was definitely the plaintive sound (애닯은 소리) of guitar (키타-) strings being plucked. For some reason I don't know, that sound felt even more melancholy and heartrending (안타가웁게 늣겨젓섯스니) to me at that time.
If anyone had discovered me then, standing stiffly (깟까) like a village guardian post (장승), they would surely have called me either mindless or a wooden doll (인조인간).
Unconsciously, my feet moved (옴겨젓스니) towards the fence (울타리밋흐로) of the house opposite (건너ㅅ편집); this was because I guessed (짐작햇둀 까닭이엿슴니다) that the guitar that had captivated me, and the protagonist playing the guitar, must be sitting (안저 잇서스리라고) beyond it.
The unceasing melody gently lifted my body and transported (옴겨주엇슴니다) me to a distant southern country (남국)—under the green shade of palm trees (야자수), imagining the free life of the native people (토인) there, with songs and dances and flute playing.
The night must have deepened (깁헛든가바요). The sound of the night watchman's clapper (딱딱이) echoed loudly, disturbing the air (공긔) of the quiet night city.
But the guitar was still weeping (울고 잇섯슴니다). More than hearing the guitar's sound (소리를 듯기보담), I wanted to see (보고 십헛나이다) the protagonist who made the guitar weep. I searched for a hole (구명) in the plank fence (널판장-울타리) and peered through it, scrutinizing the entire yard opposite, looking for the protagonist. After scanning around once (한박귀 휘-돌아보아야), I couldn't see anyone, so I suspected it might be coming from a record (레코-드) indoors and felt quite disappointed (한갓 실망을 늣기게 되엿담니다). But when I looked around once more—I discovered a young man (졂은 사나희) sitting beneath sparsely growing trees. Whether there was a reason or not, I was infinitely glad (무한히 깃버햇섯담니다) that he was a young man (나졂은이엿슴을). His face (얼골) wasn't clearly visible, but in my eyes then, he appeared (보혓담니다) to be a handsome man (미남자).
I felt a keen desire to jump over even the fence (울타리래도). But it remained just a thought; I couldn't carry it out, perhaps because I lacked courage (용긔가 적엇든 까닭이엿나보이다). Thinking about it now, although it seems like a small, amusing fact, I confess that until a few years ago, it was a tiny piece (한끄트럭의) of fact that severely troubled (삼을 몹시 졸이고 압흐게 하든) and pained me. <88>

**Summary**

These three recollections under the theme "My Romantic Times" offer varied perspectives from the early 1930s. Ch'oe Tokkyŏn links his romanticism to literary influences (*Changhŏnmong*, *Mujŏng*, *Hototogisu*), personal hardship (losing funds in Shanghai), and early literary endeavors (poetry, novels like *Yurin* and *Kwaebang Pigok*), culminating in the choice of his pen name. Kim Tongjin paints a picture of adolescent romantic yearning intertwined with nationalist fervor in exile; he describes wandering Siberia, longing for P'yŏngyang, participating in quasi-military training and Korean cultural education within an exile community near Vladivostok, and a poignant, interrupted romance with a Russian-Korean girl amidst the turmoil of the Russian Revolution and Allied intervention. Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi offers a more introspective, fleeting romantic moment: drawn out of her dormitory by the sound of a guitar on an autumn night, she becomes captivated by the unseen musician, a young man, whose presence leaves a lasting, bittersweet impression.

From a historical perspective, these accounts are valuable for their depiction of Korean lives during the colonial era beyond the peninsula. Kim Tongjin's narrative provides a particularly rich look at the Korean diaspora in the Russian Far East, detailing their organized nationalist activities (education, military drills masked as school activities), the precarious political environment (interactions with Red Guards, Allied forces, bandits), and the personal impact of regional conflicts. Ch'oe Tokkyŏn's Shanghai experience highlights the challenges and opportunities for educated Koreans abroad, including navigating Japanese cultural spaces (*Shanghai Nichinichi Shimbun*) and the ripple effects of major events like the Great Kantō Earthquake. Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi's piece hints at the emotional lives and constraints faced by young Korean women within institutional settings like dormitories.

나의 로맨틱 時代
上海黃浦江畔의 散策
崔獨鵑

나의 「로맨틕」하든 시절을 적어보라구요. 녜 분부대로 하지요. ＜84＞
제1기는요 먼젓달이엇든가 삼천리에 쓰인 나의 첫사랑 이야기가 잇지요. 앗다 그 玉丹인가라는 下婢와 「뿌랫토닉」한 첫사랑을 하엿다는 그것 말이얘요. 그것이 趙一齋의 長恨夢을 每日申報를 통하야 본 바로뒤요. 春園의 無情을 보기 좀 前인가 보구먼요. 표지에 石竹花 한가지를 雅淡하게 그린 德富蘆花의 不如歸譯抄本을 再讀三讀하든 때도 그 전후인 듯 함니다. 그리고 제2기는 아마 내가 큰뜻을 품고 上海까지 나라가서 유학이랍시고 할 때인데 간신간신히 애가 키이게 오든 학비가 그나마 떠러진지 반년만에 최후로 온 학자 5백원을 소매치기에게 일허버리고, 에이 그만 黃浦江에 빠저 죽어나버릴까 하다가 죽지도 못하고 虹口橋畔에서 밤을 서서 새운 후로 절망 끗헤 反動으로 이러나는 「로맨틔시즘」 그것에 지배되는 그때이엿나 보이다. 내가 지금까지에 詩를 써본 것도 그때이엇슴니다. 碧波타는 匿名으로 東亞일보에 投稿하야 4,5次에 한번 比例로 4호 일반의 詩가 나는 것을 보고는 큰 출세나 한드시 스스로 위로를 바덧스며 더욱이 그때에 購讀하든 「영어연구」에 비(雨)라는 제목으로 영문短詩를 써보낸 것이 選外佳作으로 실닌 것을 보고 하로밤 잠못자든 것도 그때이엇나 보이다. 그리고 공부도 못하게 되고 異域에서 밥굶을 지경이어서 上海梧州路에 잇는 日文紙 上海 日日신문에서 모집하는 校正기자 1명 채용시험에 합격하야 입사하여 가지고 편집동인 합작소설「靑葉の空」의 한 대문을 집필한 것이 因綠으로 日本 大震災통에 長田幹彦이든가 누구가 집필하든 연재소설이 중단되게 되여 「蹂躪」이라는 달듸단 「로맨틕」한 소설을 獨鵑이라는 일홈으로 연재하야 그것이 滋味잇다고 日女학생 나 부랭이들에게 편지를 바다보고 근지러운 쾌감을 늣기든 그 시절도 어지간히 「로맨틕」하엿나 보이다.

그러니 움즉여지지 안는 펜ㅅ대를 원망이나 하면 조흘넌지요 하는 수 업시 제한된 제목아래서는 쓸 수 업슴으로 내가 아직껏 잇지 못하는(?)한끗트렉의 사실이나 적어볼가 함니다.
때는 지금으로부터 오년전임니다.
봄도 조앗지만 나는 가을의 씨―슨이 조왓슴니다.
일종의 변태심리엿는지는 몰으나 돗아나는 금잔듸보담 누-럿케 말나서 싸늘한 바람에 휘날녀 이리저리 흣터지는 가을 입사귀의 가여운 정상을 보는 것이 실흐면서도 조왓슴니다.
긔속사에 소등(消燈)시간도 임이 지나서 와글와글 끌은 듯 하든 사내(舍內)도 몹시 조용할제임니다. 나도 긔숙사에 규측을 직키기 위해서 자리에 눕기는 햇스나 엇쩐지 잠은 오지를 안코 점점 눈이 맑어지며 머리가 압을 뿐이엿섯슴니다.
다른 동모들은 코를 골골 골면서 고흔 꿈을 맷는 듯 십헛슴니다.
떠러지는 입사귀의 애원성과 압창에 어리는 오동나무 그림자는 나로 하여곰 밧그로 꾀여 내고야 말엇담니다. 긔숙사 뜰에는 크-다란 오동나무가 잇는 외에 다른 잡목은 하나도 업섯슴으로 고독을 즐기는 나로써는 오히려 쓸쓸한 그 장면이 마음에 들엇슴니다.
전등이 밝게 비최는 곳을 피해서 으스름한 곳―오동나무 밋흘 차저 가섯지요.
초생달은 압 남산우에서 졸니는 양으로 문화의 산물인 전등―빗츨 저주하는 듯 하엿슴니다.
우울하고 안타가운 가삼을 엇지할 줄 몰나서 나를 가장 리해하여주는 S동모를 꾀일양으로 향설료(香雪寮)쪽으로 발자최 소리를 죽여가면서 것느라니 어듸서인가 신비스러운 멜노듸의 흐름이 내귀에 들여젓슴니다. 나는 S동모를 꾀일 생각도 용긔도 업서지고 내 발은 가늘게 들여오는 멜노듸를 차저서 걸ㅅ게 되엿나이다.
점점 갓갓히 들니는 멜노듸는 피아노소리도 안이엿고 바요링의 음률도 안이엿슴니다.
그것은 학실이 키타-의 줄뜻는 애닯은 소리엿슴니다. 그때의 나에게는 그 소리가 더 한층 우울하고 안타가웁게 늣겨젓섯스니 웬 까닭이엿든지 몰으겟슴니다.
깟까 세워노흔 장승모양으로 뻣뻣하게 서잇는 그때의 나를 발견한 사람이 잇섯다면 어김업시 정신업는 사람이라고 하지 안으면 나무로 맨든 인조인간이라고 햇슬 것이겟지요.
의식업시 내발이 건너ㅅ편집 울타리밋흐로 옴겨젓스니 그넘에는 나를 취하게 만드러 노은 키타-와 그리고 키타-를 뜻은 주인공이 안저 잇서스리라고 짐작햇둀  까닭이엿슴니다.
끈침업시 들니는 멜노듸는 나의 몸을 살작이 들어서 저-먼나라 남국―야자수 푸른 그늘 아래서 노래와 춤 그리고 피리부는 토인들의 자유로운 생활이 상상되는 남쪽나라에 옴겨주엇슴니다.
밤도 깁헛든가바요 「딱딱」이 치는 소리가 조용한 밤도시에 공긔를 흔들어 노흐면서 요란하게 들녀젓슴니다.
그러나 키타-는 여전히 울고 잇섯슴니다. 나는 키타-의 소리를 듯기보담 키타-를 울게하는 주인공이 보고 십헛나이다. 널판장-울타리에 뚤어진 구명을 차저서 그리로 건너ㅅ편집 뜰안을 모조리 살피면서 주인공을 차젓담니다. 한박귀 휘-돌아보아야 사람은 보이지 안엇슴으로 실내의 레코-드에서 들니는 것인가 십허 한갓 실망을 늣기게 되엿담니다. 그러나 또 한번 휘돌아보게 되는 때 ―나는 엉성한 나무들이 만히 서잇는 아래에 안즌 졂은 사나희를 발견햇슴니다. 까닭이 잇섯든지 업섯든지는 몰으나 나는 그 사나희가 나졂은이엿슴을 무한히 깃버햇섯담니다. 그의 얼골이 잘 보이지 안엇스나 그때의 나의 눈에는 그가 잘생긴 미남자로 보혓담니다.
울타리래도 뛰여 넘어가고 십흔 생각이 간절햇슴니다만은 생각 뿐이고 실행치 못햇스니 용긔가 적엇든 까닭이엿나보이다. 지금 생각하면 이것이 비록 조곰한 우수운 사실갓지만 몃년전까지도 내가 삼을 몹시 졸이고 압흐게 하든 한끄트럭의 사실이엿슴을 고백하나이다.＜88＞
