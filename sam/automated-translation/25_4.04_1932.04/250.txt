特急列車
						Chu Yo-han
Runs away, runs away, runs away!
Pebbles run away!
The iron bridge shouts!

Pine trees dance, hills dance
Millet ear field, bean vines
Thatched roofs, pigsties, reddish jeogori

Pine trees, larches, utility poles, utility poles, utility poles
Power lines rising and falling in rhythm
They run away, run away
Cliffs run away
Precipices run away
Passes and villages and signal posts and station workers and station masters and ticket gates and oxcarts and Ford cars and groves and paddy water and streams and apple orchards and billboards and policemen and military police...
The whole world is racing
Field furrows stagger
Mountains race

Mountains race
Small mountains are swift
Large, distant mountains are slow

Puff puff puff
The rising smoke casts black shadows on the railway land
Calves leap startled by the sharp whistle
Runs, runs, the train pierces the night air, pierces the heart of the fatherland and
the fertile ground
Piercing its heart from south to north

The train runs like a madman
Its engine making a pounding, groaning sound
The sound of rails and wheels battling loudly
At a speed of seventy miles per hour
It runs, tearing through the pages of history here and there
The train passes by palaces and pavilions and monuments and inscriptions of old
and tombs
Passing by luxurious villas
Passing by noisy and suffocating cities
The train runs, runs
Past the back alleys of the city, filth and the slums of unsanitary conditions and emitting factories and
the air of factories that make human wrecks and trash cans and poverty and ignorance
and disease and exploitation and oppression and fighting and self-interest<90>
Or past huge grain sheds and grain stacks and pots where porridge isn't boiling and malnutrition and moving bundles and bundles and sick travelers and policemen and police substations and bailiffs and moneylenders and lewdness and landlords and courts and reservoirs and all laziness and fatalism and self-abandonment and bars and brothels and gambling dens
Facing the sun, the train we ride marches on
As the dawn of a new day breaks over there
Sounding its whistle grandly, our train marches on.
A world of green and gold welcomes us.
There, the brilliance of passion shines green, there the fertile land shines gold
The place where new forms of health and cheerfulness and helping and love are drawn!
Land of the Sun!
The earth dances, the mountains crawl
When the old world ends, a new world will come
Like spring comes after winter

Runs
The train runs
Piercing the darkness, parting the clouds
Through the middle of the night where wind and rain rage madly
Its whole body glowing red hot
Our familiar friend
The express train advances.<91>
<90-91>

**Summary**

The poem vividly depicts the experience of riding an express train, emphasizing its speed and power as it rushes through the Korean landscape. It contrasts images of nature and traditional life with symbols of modernity, colonial oppression, and social decay (poverty, exploitation, disease, slums, police presence). Despite passing through these bleak scenes, the poem ends on a powerful, optimistic note, portraying the train as an agent of progress, racing towards a bright, revitalized future symbolized by the dawning sun and a "new world" of health, love, and prosperity, piercing through the darkness of the present. For historians, the poem offers a striking perspective on the consciousness of modernity in colonial Korea. It captures the disorienting speed of change, critiques the social ills exacerbated under colonial rule (poverty, exploitation, police state elements like 헌병 and 주재소), yet simultaneously embraces the train—a symbol of modern technology often associated with the colonizer—as a vehicle for national aspiration and future hope, suggesting a complex negotiation between colonial modernity and nationalist dreams.
