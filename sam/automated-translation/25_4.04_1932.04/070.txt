**Body Text Translation:**
Roundtable Discussion with Three Female Scholars Who Graduated from Foreign Universities
Attendees:
Ms. Pak Intŏk (朴仁德), Bachelor of Arts from Wesleyan University, USA
Miss Ch'oe Yŏngsuk (崔英淑), Bachelor of Economics from a Swedish University
Ms. Hwang Esit'ŏ (黃愛施德), Bachelor of Philosophy from Columbia University, USA
From Our Company:
Kim Tonghwan (金東煥), Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi (崔貞熙)

We are delighted by the sincerity of the three teachers who have agreed to speak despite their busy schedules.
The main issue we wish to discuss now concerns the general situation of women in various European countries. As the three teachers are well-versed in the overall women's issues across European nations, we would like to ask a few questions about this going forward.

(1) Facilities and Institutions for Women and Children in Various Foreign Countries
Reporter: "Aren't there many institutions established for women or children in other countries that cannot be seen in Chosŏn? We often hear that daycare centers (托兒所) or nurseries are numerous. What is the general state of social facilities?"
Pak Intŏk: "That's right. Daycare centers are common. Speaking of what I saw in America, there are homes for the aged. There, only unfortunate women with absolutely no one to rely on are accommodated, allowing them to peacefully live out their lives." <32>
Hwang Esit'ŏ: "In America, institutions for men and women are established equally, so there aren't many institutions specifically created just for women. However, among those I saw, the 'YMCA Dormitory' (YMCA·또메트리) exists in both cities and villages, providing considerable convenience to women. The managers are women, and all the guests staying there are women too. When I first arrived, I also stayed at a YMCA Dormitory. The convenience of the internal facilities and the kindness of the managers were indescribable.
They have movie theaters (映畵館), pools (푸-ㄹ, 水泳場), parks, auditoriums, dining halls, and sufficient recreational facilities, so besides the guests staying there, many individuals bring their friends (동모) to play. Additionally, there is something called the World Women's Industrial Alliance (世界女子實業同盟), but since it's run by proletariat women (무산부인), its existence is still quite weak in capitalist countries, so it hasn't spread widely to the general public. Also, I heard there is an organization that trains boy scouts similar to the Boy Scouts (소년군) here, but I haven't seen it."
Reporter: "What are the internal facilities like in daycare centers or homes for the aged?"
Ch'oe Yŏngsuk: "Are there special facilities? They are equipped appropriately according to the children's age (년영). For newborns just two weeks old, they line up 'baby beds' (애기침상) in a large room (室內) and lay one baby in each. For children old enough to play, they provide many toys (오모기/노름깜) in a separate room and entrust them to a caregiver (보모) who can look after them. Also, some daycare centers have kindergartens attached. I also spent two weeks at a daycare center, and it was quite interesting."
Reporter: "What is the age range for the children, and are there time limits for dropping them off and picking them up?"
Ch'oe Yŏngsuk: "Of course, there are. It's limited to children from two weeks old up to kindergarten age. Drop-off time is usually between six and seven in the morning, and pick-up is generally between six and seven in the evening."
Hwang Esit'ŏ: "That's right. Regardless of the country, working women (직업부인) entrust their children, <33> dropping them off when they go to work (일터) and picking them up when they return (도라오다), so the times are likely similar."
Pak Intŏk: "However, something I observed in Russia is that many women there do not pick up their children even on their way back from the workplace (職場). The reason is that they are tired from a whole day's labor (로동), so there's no need to lose sleep or be inconvenienced by the child at night. Just as a father comes home in the evening after leaving in the morning and doesn't look after the children, isn't it true that there's no principle (원측) saying a woman must look after the children? If there isn't a separate job for men and a separate job for women, then women too – those who want to do social work (사회사업), those who want to do housekeeping (살님), those who want to raise children (길느다) – should all work according to their aptitude as they wish. There is no principle that because one is a woman, she must raise children. Therefore, since they've entrusted the child to someone who wants to raise children, won't that person raise the child better than they, who do not wish to? Based on this reasoning (리론), there were many women who left their children at daycare centers. I asked someone working at a daycare center why they were there. Their reply was that they found the greatest hobby in raising children. To others' eyes too, her raising of children truly seemed like she wanted to do it, not like she was doing it as a job."
Ch'oe Yŏngsuk: "Setting aside any country in the world, there is probably no place where women love their children as blindly as Chosŏn women do!"
Reporter: "Is there no fee paid to the daycare center?"
Pak Intŏk: "In Russia, fees are usually (으레히) paid, but in America, since women from the propertied class (유산게급) do it as a form of charity work (자선사업) for proletariat women, no fees are paid. That's why daycare centers are numerous in factory districts."
Reporter: "Uh, I heard a story that foreign women who give birth to illegitimate children (사생아) take them to daycare centers, but how do they secretly drop them off?"
Ch'oe Yŏngsuk: "Yes, I saw it when I stopped by India on my way back this time. There, it's run by nuns of the Roman Catholic church – should I call it an orphanage rather than a daycare center? Women unable to raise a child – in other words, <34> holding an illegitimate child – go there. There's something like a box (괴짝갓흔 것) placed outside. They lay the baby in it, ring a bell, and leave. Then a nun comes out and brings the baby inside."
Hwang Esit'ŏ: "I heard such things happen frequently in other countries, not just India."

(2) Activities of Foreign Women During Wartime
Reporter: "It is said that foreign women were active in place of men during the Great European War (WWI). Since you teachers must have heard stories from those who witnessed it directly at that time, please tell us."
Pak Intŏk: "That's right. Although I didn't witness it myself at the time, I'll speak based on what I've heard or read in books. I recently heard from an English person who witnessed the Great European War. British women reportedly did everything men did – tram conductors, subway train drivers, post office clerks, mail carriers, bank tellers, even operating machinery in factories. One amusing story is that trying to do everything with clumsy skills and dexterity made things incredibly slow. Train operation, especially subway trains, which run on a strict schedule, often had collisions due to delays, and machinery breakdowns caused chaos. Also, German women reportedly took full charge (맛허햇다) of farming."

(3) Professions of Foreign Women?
Reporter: "What kinds of professions do foreign women commonly hold?"
Ch'oe Yŏngsuk: "In countries like Sweden (서전), it seems unnecessary to distinguish between women's and men's professions. Women can become police officers, detectives, or even representatives in the national assembly, just like men, and can hold any kind of job."
Hwang Esit'ŏ: "That's right. Since there's no distinction between men and women in foreign countries, there's no particular distinction in professions either."
Pak Intŏk: "Generally, they find jobs according to their aptitude. American women often work as teachers." <35>

(4) Phenomenon of Birth Control Among Foreign Women?
Reporter: "Please tell us about birth control (産兒제한)."
Hwang Esit'ŏ: "I don't know the situation in other countries well since I haven't witnessed it directly, but in America, it is strictly prohibited by law, so it seems the educated class (유식게급) secretly practices contraception methods (피임법) individually. Therefore, families above the middle class usually have two or three children, but proletariat families (무산가정) have truly many children. Some scholars express great concern, saying that since the children of people with high intellectual power (지력) are decreasing while the children of laborers are increasing, the intellectual capacity of future generations will become inferior (렬등하겟다)."
Ch'oe Yŏngsuk: "In Sweden (서전) too, individuals might practice it, but there are no institutions encouraging (장녀하는) it, nor is there severe punishment (엄벌)."
Pak Intŏk: "In America too, it was strictly prohibited until a few years ago, but recently, although there is opposition, there is a liberal faction that has established clinics (진찰소) to teach women contraception methods and implement them. And in France (불란서), it's strictly forbidden now; rather, the government supposedly gives awards specifically to those who have many children." Also, countries like Russia go without saying; there, when people go for marriage registration (혼인등긔), the first thing they ask is about their intention regarding childbirth (자녀생산에 대한 여부). If they don't want to have children, they are taught contraception methods well, and if they still become pregnant, they are even told which hospital and which doctor to go to for an operation (수술). The government takes responsibility and teaches contraception methods to any extent."

(5) How Do They Raise Their Children?
Reporter: "Please tell us about points observed from a Chosŏn woman's perspective regarding how foreign women raise their children."
Ch'oe Yŏngsuk: "As I mentioned earlier, Chosŏn women only know how to cherish children, but there are many contradictory points in their methods of raising them."
Hwang Esit'ŏ: "That's right. Foreign women raise their children truly rationally (합리적으로). They absolutely do not ignore the child's opinion, so children enjoy expressing their opinions (의사발표) and do not lose courage (용긔)." <36>
Pak Intŏk: "Indeed. They give children absolute freedom. If a child touches a hot stove (날노), they initially try to stop them, but if the child doesn't listen well, they just leave them alone (버러두다). Then the child learns it's hot and stops by themselves (스사로), thus learning that the stove is hot. In this way, they encourage (장려) the child's freedom and also cultivate habits (慣習). They strive to cultivate habits from an early age and create many experiences. They believe people learn by reflecting on their experiences (경험에 빗처 보아서), so they try to create experiences whenever possible (맨들어 주더이다)."
Reporter: "Until what age do parents bear responsibility for their children?"
Ch'oe Yŏngsuk: "Parents feel a sense of responsibility towards their children up to the age of seventeen."
Reporter: "Is it like that in other countries too?"
Hwang Esit'ŏ: "In America too, parents hold responsibility until the completion of high school level education."

(6) Regarding Marriage and Divorce Among Foreign Women
Reporter: "Please tell us about things like the conditions for marriage among foreign women."
Pak Intŏk: "Amidst the current debate that free marriage produces a high divorce rate (리혼률), it's said that even with free marriage, three conditions must be met.
First, the ideals (리상) of both parties must match, and they must be socially compatible.
Second, they must have the ability to be economically independent (독입할 능럭). Third, they must be physiologically (생리적으로) compatible. Generally, marriage is considered established (성입된다고) only when these conditions are met."
Reporter: "Is there no age limit (년령 제한) for marriage?"
Ch'oe Yŏngsuk: "There is. In Sweden (서전), it's over 25 for men and around 21 or 22 for women."
Reporter: "Is companionate marriage (友愛結婚) becoming very popular recently?"
Hwang Esit'ŏ: "There are many who practice it, and many proponents too." <37>
Reporter: "Now, regarding divorce, could you tell us about differences compared to Chosŏn?"
Ch'oe Yŏngsuk: "In Chosŏn, getting a divorce is quite difficult, but in Sweden (서전), if both parties intend to divorce, they proceed with the paperwork without hesitation. The divorce becomes effective that very day."
Reporter: "Are there instances of requesting alimony (위자료)?"
Hwang Esit'ŏ: "If the man dislikes the woman and initiates the divorce, the woman requests alimony, but if it's based on mutual agreement, there is no alimony."
Reporter: "Can a divorce proceed even if the woman requests it?"
Ch'oe Yŏngsuk: "Of course. It's entirely possible."
Reporter: "How are matters handled when there are children?"
Hwang Esit'ŏ: "In America, they supposedly ask the children whether they want to follow their father or their mother, and the decision is made according to the children's wishes."
Reporter: "If all the children want to follow their mother, what happens with the child support expenses?"
Ch'oe Yŏngsuk: "When the mother takes the children, the father must provide child support."
Reporter: "We have listened well. You must be tired from speaking for such a long time. We are too embarrassed to thank you further." <38>
<32-38>

**Summary**

This 1932 roundtable discussion features three Korean women educated in the US and Sweden (Pak Intŏk, Hwang Esit'ŏ, Ch'oe Yŏngsuk) discussing Western women's lives compared to Chosŏn. Topics include social facilities like daycare (托兒所) and homes for the aged, women's roles in WWI, diverse professional opportunities, differing approaches to birth control (from US legal restrictions and eugenic concerns to French pro-natalism and Soviet state-supported contraception), rational child-rearing emphasizing freedom and experience, and marriage/divorce practices, including conditions for marriage, age limits, companionate marriage (友愛結婚), and relatively easier divorce procedures (especially in Sweden) compared to Korea, including child custody and support. The conversation highlights the speakers' exposure to modern ideas about women's social roles, autonomy, and family structures, implicitly contrasting them with perceived traditional constraints in colonial Korea. For historians, the article offers insights into the perspectives of elite, Western-educated Korean women in the colonial period, their awareness of global discourses on gender, social welfare, eugenics (via Hwang's comment on birth rates), and political systems (capitalism vs. Soviet communism), and how they framed these issues in relation to Korean society. The frank discussion on birth control, divorce, and child-rearing methods reveals the intellectual currents influencing modern Korean women's thought at the time.

(1) 여성及 아동을 위한 諸외국의 시설기관
