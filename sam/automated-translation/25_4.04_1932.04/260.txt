The Sea
						Kim Ansŏ
The Sea

1. Longing for the sea, I came to the sea
Coming here, I see only water shimmering blue in the sea
The heart that longed finds no fulfillment
Gazing vacantly only at the distant edge of the sky.

2. With no place to put this complaining heart
Alone on the sandy beach, I write characters
Swish, swish, the incoming tide swallows them and goes,
Oh water, where are you flowing to?

3. The sea freely leaps and plays
The sea resoundingly sings
The sea broadly grins and laughs
I wish to live my whole life like this too.

4. Looking out, in the distant open sea
A white sail, flickering, loses its way and turns,<91>
Where is it going, where is that boat heading?
At the sea's end, too, the sky touches.

5. The way the white water circles and plays on the sand
So precious, I absently take a sip
What is this? It's salty, rinsing my mouth.
Bitter things are not just life in this world.
—Night of March 11, 1932—

At the Riverside

Like a shadow by the riverside, I stand alone.
The river water flows, the river water flows, it just flows.
Time goes by, time goes by, endlessly running away.
Life is short, sighs great hope, complains.

Water goes, time goes, so the day darkens, the day brightens.
People are born, people die, love too goes, love too goes.
Everything withers, everything dies. Water laughs and time sings.
At the riverside where nothing can return, I stand alone.
—March 12, 1932—

Spring and Mind

Unknowingly, a restless mind
Endlessly circles the empty air
Wrapped in the blue spring breeze
Crossing mountains and seas.
Though no one calls.<92>
<91-92>

**Summary**

This collection features three poems by Kim Ansŏ exploring themes of longing, the relentless passage of time, and the contrast between human limitations and the vastness or freedom of nature. "The Sea" expresses a yearning for fulfillment that nature itself cannot satisfy, observing the sea's indifference and freedom while reflecting on life's bitterness. "At the Riverside" contemplates the irreversible flow of time and life, highlighting the transience of human existence against the backdrop of the ever-flowing river. "Spring and Mind" depicts a restless spirit stirred by spring, wandering aimlessly without destination. From a colonial history perspective, the poems are interesting for their expression of individual yearning, melancholy, and a sense of confinement or displacement ("Alone on the sandy beach," "I stand alone," "restless mind... though no one calls"). While not overtly political, the pervasive sense of longing and observation of nature's freedom could subtly reflect the constraints and emotional landscape of life under colonial rule, offering a space for personal expression amid broader societal limitations.

바다
						金岸曙
바다
1. 바다가 그립길래 바다를 왓소
와서보니 바다엔 물만 펄핫소
그려하든 心思는 채일길 업시
저멀리 하늘끗만 無心이 보오.

2. 하소연한 이 心思 둘 곳 업길래
호을로 모래밧에 글字를 쓰니
사륵사륵 밀물이 삼키고 가네,
이물아, 너 어듸로 흘너가느냐.

3. 바다는 自由로이 뛰며 놉니다
바다는 우렁차게 노래합니다
바다는 히룩버룩 웃음칩니다
내 한生도 이가치 살고 십쇠다.

4. 바라보니 난바다 머나먼 곳에
감실감실 힌돗이 길일코 도네,＜91＞
어듸로소 어듸를 저배 가는고
바다에도 난끗엔 하늘이 닷고.

5. 모래밧을 하얀물 돌며 노는양
하도 貴해 無心이 한입 마시니
어인일가 짜구나, 입안을 흘네.
쓸인 것은 이世相 生만 안이리.
―1932, 3, 11日夜―

江까에서
江까에 그림자가치 외로이 나는 섯노라.
江물은 흘은다, 江물은 흘은다, 그저 흘은다.
歲月은 간다 歲月은 간다 끗업시 달아난다.
生命이 짤다고 큰希望은 탄식한다, 원망한다.

물가고 때가 가니 날은 저문다, 날은 밝는다.
사람은 나고 사람은 죽는다, 사랑도 간다, 사랑도 간다.
모도다 여윈다, 모도다 죽는다. 물은 웃고 歲月은 노래한다.
하나도 돌오지 못하는 江까에 나는 외로이 섯노라.
―1932, 3, 12―

봄과 맘
나도 몰으게 들뜨는 心思
虛空을 끗도 업시 도는고야
파알한 봄바람에 싸혀
山을 넘고 바다를 건너.
