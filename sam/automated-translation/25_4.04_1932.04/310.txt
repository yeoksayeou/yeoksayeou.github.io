Spring Rain on the Naktong River
						Kwŏn Ku-hyŏn

Spring Rain on the Naktong River

Along the fifteen-*ri* long embankment lined with river willows,
The fine, soft spring rain falls.
Is it the poignant sentiment of beautiful youth,
Smiling faintly even while weeping for a beloved?

The smoke drifting long and low
Through the quiet dusk enveloping the riverside village,
Seems to melt into each branch of the silver willows;
Like a lost heart in the deepening rain of early spring.

The edge of the relaxed sky is distant beyond the clouds,
Two or three crows fly by.
Pitifully, where will they rest this night?
Weeping, weeping as they go, even their sound is sorrowful.

As dusk falls on the fifteen-*ri* of river willows,
Where have the sounds of calves seeking their mothers gone?
On the sloped riverbank, a young maiden fetching water;
Only her coming and going footsteps are deeply renewed.

Oh, young bride of the riverside village, will you welcome me?
On this rain-darkened day, you too are in your youth.
Let me be a guest in this village for a night's conversation,
It is the season when moisture rises in willow branches and buds sprout.
—From the poetry collection "Where is the Homeland" (鄕土는 어데로)—

A Small Sorrow

Ah, my beautiful beloved,
Isn't your name even more beautiful?
It is the season when you greet your seventeenth spring.

Upon that, you
Carry gracefully on your shoulders
All the beauty that embroidered my heart.

But time is already late,<95>
The cold wind brushes past my ear,
And you are quietly picking balsam flowers.

Ah, what shall I do with this aching heart?
Gazing up at the sky, I sing a song:
"Oh, fleeting spring light of a transient life, fading away—"
—From the same collection—

Your Path to Take

Your path to take,
Ask it of your heart.
More sincerely than anyone else,
It will also show you the right place to go.

If you ask and there is no answer,
Try holding a three-ch'ŏk dagger reversed.
Your heart, with a single laugh,
Will illuminate the path before you.
—From the same collection, Recent Work—<96>

**Summary**

This article presents three poems by Kwŏn Ku-hyŏn from 1932. "Spring Rain on the Naktong River" evokes a lyrical, somewhat melancholic scene of spring along the Naktong River, blending natural imagery with feelings of longing and transience. "A Small Sorrow" expresses the pain of unrequited love and the passage of youth. "Your Path to Take" offers existential advice, urging introspection and decisive action to find one's way in life. From a historical perspective, the poems reflect the lyrical sensibilities prevalent in Korean literature of the period, often tinged with a subtle melancholy or yearning that could be interpreted as reflecting the constraints and uncertainties of life under colonial rule, even when not explicitly political. The final poem's call for self-reliance and finding one's own path might resonate with the intellectual climate of the time, seeking direction amidst national suppression.
