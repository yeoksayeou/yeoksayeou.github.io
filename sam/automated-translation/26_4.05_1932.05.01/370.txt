자라가는 싹
						崔義順

Panting up the rough Ch'ŏngnyangni road, the Kyŏngin bus line rolled along, nearly tumbling over several times before shuddering to a stop at the Ttŏkjŏn'gŏri terminus.

The passengers packed tightly inside poured out refreshingly. However, following them, other passengers began to fill the seats again, one by one.

(Aigo, why is my head spinning so—)
The conductor, Chŏng-sim, slumped into the empty driver's seat, crying out inwardly like this.

Chŏng-sim's weary arms trembled powerlessly. Darkness swam before her eyes. The sound of chatter directly above her seemed to come from a deep cave. And her mouth, filled with dust, only gathered saliva.

(It must be evening already. How could this be! Just missing two meals, breakfast and lunch, and I end up like this!)
(It’s because exhaustion has piled up—)
Chŏng-sim questioned and answered herself like this.

(How upset Mother must be, unable to even pack a lunch box (변도)!)
A sharp pang suddenly reared its head again in Chŏng-sim’s stomach, which had seemed somewhat settled.

And inside her belly, as if demanding something quickly, it writhed and surged.

As if trying to stretch her shrinking stomach from the outside, Chŏng-sim gathered her thin hands, stroked over her skirt, and was just about to carefully stand up and get off the bus.
Just then, something glittering caught her eye, twinkling beneath her foot which she hadn't yet lowered. Chŏng-sim’s right hand, quick as a machine, snatched up the tiny, sparkling thing.

(A 5-chŏn coin—money dropped inside the bus—who could have dropped it—)
Chŏng-sim couldn't think any further.

Her body flew as if propelled towards the flimsy wooden stall across the way where sweet potatoes were laid out. It was a place Chŏng-sim had only gazed at all day long, coming and going.
Chŏng-sim bought 5 chŏn worth of boiled sweet potatoes and didn't know what to do.

Her gaze quickly shifted to driver Ch'ae, who, finding the young spring grass with its faint green tint lovely, would go down to the roadside bank whenever they took a short break and sit reading a book. Chŏng-sim walked over there without hesitation.

"Please have some of these sweet potatoes with me."
"..."
Ch'ae silently accepted the sweet potato Chŏng-sim offered, observing her every move with a sincere heart.

Chŏng-sim, without a word, crouched down, turned away, and no sooner had she peeled the skin off the remaining sweet potato than she was stuffing it into her mouth.

(She must have been terribly hungry again! But where did she get the money? To be starving even after enduring such harsh labor (로동) at the tender age of seventeen—does it make sense! But several family members depend on Chŏng-sim alone for survival—)
Ch'ae shuddered.

(Has even Chŏng-sim, the kindest-hearted, youngest, and most honest among the ten or so bus girls (뻐스껄), finally learned the knack of earning "dirty money" (군돈)?)
Thoughts of the other bus girls selling their bodies for a few coins flashed through Ch'ae's mind. Ch'ae felt his teeth clench.

"Eat this one too."
Saying this, Ch'ae placed the sweet potato he had been clutching tightly in his fist back in front of Chŏng-sim and abruptly stood up.

As evening deepened, a biting wind carrying flurries of snow crept into the carriage.

"All right!" "Stop—" "All right—"
Chŏng-sim chirped busily, like a skylark greeting the season. The bus was soon about to cross the Han'gang railway bridge. Only one passenger remained, an old gentleman thoroughly drunk.

Only then did Chŏng-sim finally let out a burp from the sweet potato. The lump in her chest seemed to go down a bit, and some vitality seemed to return. However, one thing weighing on her mind was Ch'ae's silent, gloomy demeanor since earlier.

Chŏng-sim felt unusually bored and forlorn just standing quietly by the driver's seat. She pulled out the ticket pouch from her bag in front. She intended to count the money from the completed route.

As it was just getting dark, the newly switched-on room light (룸·라이트) seemed to flicker intensely.
Chŏng-sim counted the money and remaining tickets once, then counted again twice, three times. The more she counted, the paler her face became, flushing red.

No matter how many times she counted, there was clearly a shortage of one 5-chŏn coin.

"What could this be?"
A pathetic sigh escaped Chŏng-sim's lips uncontrollably.

"You mean you lost it?"
Ch'ae, who had been watching intently, asked this, loosening his grip on the steering wheel (핸들) and looking intently up at Chŏng-sim.

Only the tears welling up in Chŏng-sim's clear eyes twinkled in the light— Chŏng-sim gave no answer.

"I'll make it up for you."
"Do you have money?"
Her voice trembled.

"I have 5 chŏn for the streetcar fare to get home later."
"Then you'll have to walk home with me!"
Ch'ae couldn't help but be startled once more by these words.

(Oh—so you intended to walk that far distance later. Then what money was it that bought the sweet potatoes earlier?)
Ch'ae wanted to press her right then, but seeing Chŏng-sim's face, filled with a gentle, innocent smile as if all worries had vanished and peace and calm had returned, no other words came out. All words seemed superfluous.

"Well, another thing has happened that I both understand and don't understand. You see, when we reached Ttŏkjŏn'gŏri earlier, I was deathly hungry. Right at that moment, I picked up a 5-chŏn coin from the bus step (차-스텝) and was incredibly happy, but it turned out to be the very coin I had dropped.
I had silently prayed that the owner of the coin wasn't someone who would fret over losing 5 chŏn—what would I have done! I almost got in trouble for being short 5 chŏn—no, for not having 5 chŏn—."
Chŏng-sim voluntarily whispered this confession into Ch'ae's ear. At that moment, the crying voice of Pok-nye, who had been caught short of money and kicked out right then and there, seemed to echo in Chŏng-sim's ears.

Ch'ae cheerfully picked up speed (스피드).

**Summary**

The story follows Chŏng-sim, a young, impoverished bus conductor ('bus girl') in colonial Seoul, likely in the early 1930s. Struggling with hunger after missing meals due to poverty, she finds a 5-chŏn coin on the bus, which she uses to buy sweet potatoes. She shares them with the driver, Ch'ae, who worries she might be resorting to earning "dirty money" like other conductors. Later, Chŏng-sim discovers she is short exactly 5 chŏn in her fare collection. Ch'ae offers his streetcar fare. Chŏng-sim then realizes the coin she found was her own dropped money, expressing relief that she didn't inadvertently cause trouble for herself by spending lost money that she needed to balance her accounts, narrowly avoiding the fate of another conductor, Pok-nye, who was fired for a shortfall.

From a historical perspective, the story vividly portrays the harsh economic realities faced by young working-class women in colonial Korea. The mention of "bus girls" (뻐스껄), their intense labor, poverty leading to skipped meals, the low wages (highlighted by the significance of 5 chŏn), and the driver's suspicion about earning "dirty money" (군돈), likely hinting at prostitution as a means of survival for some, offer a glimpse into the social conditions and struggles of the urban poor. The use of Japanese loanwords like "bento" (변도) and English loanwords like "all right," "stop," "room light," "handle," and "speed" also reflects the linguistic landscape of the time.
