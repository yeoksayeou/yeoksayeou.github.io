Pitiful Orphans
"If I get a gun, I'll avenge my father."

The Late Kim Chwa-jin's Daughter, Ŭn-ae

Wishing to know the condition of the late Kim Chwa-jin's (金佐鎭) daughter and son, who were suffering badly from measles when I visited some time ago, I once again sought out number 21, Uni-dong on a very windy day.
Perhaps because the two young children’s illnesses had fully recovered, the room that had been so dim was now bright, and it seemed that even the faces of the people in the room were filled with cheer.
The daughter – Ŭn-ae (7 years old this year) – had gone out to play, and the son, Ch'ŏl-han (3 years old this year), was sitting and eating porridge. A woman who had been sitting on one side doing *tadimi* ironing, but stopped upon the reporter's intrusion, was said to be the younger sister of Mrs. Kim's widow, who had returned from Changch'un a few days prior.
"You must be happy that the young children have recovered."
"Oh my, these days it feels like we're really living, whether we eat or starve. When you came last time, I was so distraught I don't even know what I said or how," said Mrs. Yi Hae-guk, her face beaming.
The lattice window opened slightly.
"Mommy, give me food—" The child who energetically ran in was Ŭn-ae, who had cried and pleaded for a pear when she was sick the other day.
Is there any child who eats four or five times a day? Whether she heard her mother asking this or not, she kept insisting she wanted to eat. I wondered if there was indeed enough food for four or five meals. Perhaps sensing that her mother wouldn't give her any even if she pleaded, she tried to pick up a rice bowl placed in a corner with her own hands, but the bowl contained only about a spoonful of yellow millet rice.
What her younger brother Ch'ŏl-han was eating was rice porridge. Perhaps feeling sorry for her older sister who sat wanting to eat the yellow millet rice, which was barely a spoonful and had no side dishes, he said,
"Eat this—okay—" placing the porridge bowl before his sister. Ŭn-ae knew that white porridge was better than millet rice, so she eagerly joined in as her brother Ch'ŏl-han suggested.
"You eat a spoonful, then I'll eat a spoonful."
"Okay, yes!"
Still acting the part of an older sister, she puts a spoonful into her brother's mouth before taking one herself. The beef side dish in a small bowl, she only puts into her brother's mouth; Ŭn-ae never eats it herself, only chewing on a very tough sinew. Despite this, Ch'ŏl-han must have had a change of heart, because he cried that his sister was eating the meat, so Ŭn-ae threw down her spoon and sat down. To the reporter, their actions seemed both very charming and, on the other hand, pitiful.
Ŭn-ae sat quietly for a moment, then, as if struck by a thought, said,
"Mommy—put Father's picture up over there—"
"Do you even know your father to say that?" Her mother's words must have sounded sad.
She rolled on the floor crying.
"I'll put it in a frame for you, so don't cry." Her mother's comforting words stopped her crying.
The reporter couldn't help but be surprised by the earnestness of seven-year-old Ŭn-ae's thoughts—her longing for her father—and at the same time felt a sense of pity.
"Ŭn-ae—do you miss your father?" the reporter asked.
"Yes, I miss him very much."
"Where did your father go?"
"My father passed away."
"How did your father pass away?"
"..."
Ŭn-ae had no answer, so her mother answered for her, "She knows everything. That's why she pesters me day and night to buy her a gun."
"What would she do with a gun?"
"She always says she'll shoot the person who killed her father, saying she'll kill the dog (犬) that killed her father."
And it seems she thinks of her father a lot in her own way. Whenever she goes to someone's house and sees the children there sitting on their father's lap, she cries and makes a fuss. At her father's funeral in Sansi (山市), although she was only four, she apparently followed the funeral procession for five *ri*.
"Didn't she say anything after coming back?"
"She probably asked why they were burying Father in the dirt, right?" Listening intently to her mother talking with the reporter like this, she perhaps felt embarrassed and started nagging her mother not to talk.
The reporter asked Ŭn-ae again,
"What will you do when you grow up big and become an adult?"
"I'll take a gun and kill the 'dog' that killed my father," she said, blushing. Judging by her speech and playful manner, she seemed more masculine than feminine in character. According to her mother,
Even now, when she sees children on the street with guns, she constantly begs for one. And she makes something like a gun out of a piece of wood (나무 각) and pretends to fire it, going "Bang, bang, bang." It's quite remarkable.
"Don't you want to go to school?" the reporter asked, stroking her hair.
"I want to go," she replied, also in a very masculine way.
Mrs. Yi Hae-guk continued speaking again.
"She's making a fuss about going to school right now. Every morning, she goes to the yard of the school nearby and looks, then pleads to be let into the school."
Ch'ŏl-han, who had cried because his sister ate the meat side dish, had fallen asleep nursing at his mother's breast, so the reporter asked Ŭn-ae just a few more words and then left the place. (March 26th)

Become a Soldier and Fight

The Late Mr. Pang Chŏng-hwan's Son

Anyone would feel pity and sadness for a fatherless child. How many such unfortunate, lonely children are there in the world?
Compared to children who grow up cherished on the laps of loving parents, unaware of misfortune, these pitiful orphans must have certain different thoughts beyond those held by the fortunate ones. Therefore, one day around lunchtime, the reporter sought out the house of the late Mr. Pang Chŏng-hwan (方定煥) in Sŏgyŏk-tong.
Stepping into the gateway, I saw a boy who looked to be 6 or 7 years old and a girl about 4 or 5 years old sitting with their heads together, talking and doing something. My<31> feet turned towards where they were sitting instead of towards the main room, guessing that they were the children of Mr. Pang Chŏng-hwan, who had passed away some time ago.
Reporter: "What are you doing?" I went over to where they were sitting and sat down without permission, feeling a bit awkward.
The older brother had put some water in paper and was struggling to wash grass in it, while the younger sister said, "Let me do it," and pulled it towards herself.
Reporter: "What are you doing with this?" I asked, but they both lowered their heads shyly and sat quietly.
Reporter: "Hurry up and do it. Are you making kimchi? Shall I make it well for you then? Look—this (grass), wash it cleanly in this big container here, put it in this small container, and add salt, red pepper powder, and garlic, then it becomes kimchi." As I explained, they watched the kimchi-making process quietly, seeming interested.
After a moment, the younger sister said,
"There's no garlic, what do we do?" and chuckled.
Reporter: "Let's not add garlic then. It's delicious even without it." She showed a look of considerable relief at this answer.
Their make-believe play (손곱노리) preparations were interesting, but since my purpose in getting close to them wasn't to play make-believe together, I brought up a completely different topic.
Reporter: "Is your father in the room now?"
Perhaps because asking if their father, who passed away long ago, was in the room was so absurd, the older brother stood up first, a smile on his face, and ran towards the main room. The younger sister followed behind, saying, "Mommy—give me food." Since they both went in, the reporter had no choice but to follow them inside. Now, inside the room, I had to figure out how to befriend them again.
I decided to try a tactic like one used in kindergarten.
"Let's see who does better."
Reporter: "I'm going to ask something now, let's see if the older brother answers better or the younger sister." As soon as I finished speaking, the older brother told his younger sister,
"You answer, I don't want to." Seeing the situation, the younger sister didn't seem likely to talk, so I had no choice but to coax the older brother.
"Younger sisters usually can't answer well. Older brothers do better. Because they're taller and older—right? Your sister looks like she can't do it well," I praised him, and he smiled faintly.
"What's your name?"
"It's Ha-yong (夏容)."
"You answer really well—how old are you?"
"Eight years old."
"What about next? Do you go to school?"
"I go to Sabŏm Pusok (Normal School Attached Elementary)."
"What grade?"
"I became second grade."
"When did you enter school?"
"I entered last year."
"Who did you go with when you first entered school?"
"I went with Father. Father bought me a new hat, new shoes, and new books then."
"Oh my, is that so? Goodness—did Father go with you this time when you became second grade too?"
"No. Father wasn't there."
"Where did he go?"
"No. He passed away. So he was buried in the ground."
"When?"
"A while—ago—it's been a long time."
"When is he coming back?"
"He's not coming back anymore."
"Then what do you do when you miss your father?"
"..." He just lowered his head without any answer.
"You don't miss him?"
"I miss him." This answer sounded weaker than his previous ones; the tone of his reply was extremely sad.
"What do you do when you miss your father?"
"I look at his picture."
Young Ha-yong's answers grew increasingly faint.

What Will He Become in the Future?

"After graduating from the school you attend now, which school will you go to next?"
"I'll go to the school my older sister attends."
"Which school does your older sister attend?"
"She attends Cheil Kobo (First Higher Common School)."
"After finishing the school your sister attends, where will you go?"
"Then I'll go to the training center (훌연원) and learn to be a soldier."
"Learn to be a soldier? What will you do after learning to be a soldier?"
"I'll fight."
"What happens if you fight?"
"I win. It's good if I win," he answered enthusiastically, raising his voice.
"After all—the fighting, what will you do?"
"After that, I'll learn to be a doctor."
"What do you do if you learn to be a doctor?"
"I fix people who are dying."
"Then you could bring Father back to life too, right? Right?"
"No. You can't do that for someone buried in the ground."
For some reason, whenever the word "Father" came up with the young boy, the cheerfulness disappeared from his face. He quietly moved away from the reporter and went to his baby sister, born just a few days ago, gazing at her sleeping face as if she were precious.
His maternal grandmother, sitting nearby watching his actions, murmured,
"Unlucky girl. A girl who couldn't even see her father."
The situation of the posthumously born daughter, who never saw her father's face—and never will—is endlessly pitiful, but Ha-yong's tragic reply that he looks at a picture when he misses his father deeply pained the reporter's heart. Future soldier, future doctor. Stay strong—leaving this blessing (추복), I stepped out into the alley of Sŏgyŏk-tong.<32>

**Summary**

This 1932 article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine reports on the lives of the young orphaned children of two recently deceased prominent Korean figures: independence activist Kim Chwa-jin and children's writer/activist Pang Chŏng-hwan. The reporter visits the families, describing the children's impoverished circumstances, their interactions, and their poignant memories or lack thereof of their fathers. Kim Chwa-jin's seven-year-old daughter, Ŭn-ae, expresses a fierce desire to avenge her father's assassination, wanting a gun to kill the "dog" responsible. Pang Chŏng-hwan's eight-year-old son, Ha-yong, copes by looking at photos and aspires to be a soldier to fight and win, and then a doctor, though acknowledging he cannot revive his buried father. The article highlights the harsh realities faced by the families of deceased national figures under colonial rule, showcasing both childhood innocence and the heavy burden of their fathers' legacies, including anti-colonial sentiment passed down even to young children. Historians might find the direct expression of vengeance by Kim's daughter, the specific future aspirations of Pang's son (soldier first, then doctor), and the descriptions of poverty and reliance on extended family valuable for understanding the social conditions and nationalist undercurrents affecting families of prominent anti-Japanese figures during this period.

총사주면 원수갑허

故 金佐鎭 따님 恩愛

얼마전에 방문햇을 때 홍역으로 몹시 알튼 金佐鎭의 따님과 아드님의 병세여하를 알고십허서 몹시 바람 부는 어느 날 운이동 21번지를 또다시 차젓다.
그럿케 어둠컴컴하든 방도 밝어젓스며 방안에 잇는 사람들의 얼골까지도 화긔가 떠도는 듯 십헛슴으로 두 어린애기 병세가 완괘한 까닭이 안이라고 못할 것이겟지.
따님- 은애(금년 7세)는 밧게 놀너 나가고 아드님 철한(금년 3세)이가 안저서 죽을 먹는다. 한편 쪽에 안저서 다듸미질을 하다가 기자의 침입으로 해서 끈치고 안젓는 분이 몃츨전에 장춘서 도라온 김씨 미망인의 동생이라고 한다.
「어린 애기들의 병이 나어서 깃브시겟슴니다.」
「아이구 요새는 먹든지 굼든지 사는 것 갓슴니다. 저번에 오섯을 때는 너무도 기가 막허서 무슨 이약이를 엇더케 햇든지도 몰으겟더니만」하고 헤국녀사는 희색이 만면해서 말삼하신다.
밀창이 열니드니
「엄마 밥조-」하면서 원긔잇게 뛰여들어 오는 애기는 일전에 압흘 때 배 사달다고 울며 졸으든 은애엿다.
하로에 네번 다섯 번식 밥먹는 애가 어듸 잇는냐고 어머님께서 말삼하시는 것도 듯는지 마는지 그래도 작구만 먹겟다고 졸으니 정말 네 다섯번식 먹을 만한 밥이 잇섯든지 나는 의문을 가젓다. 정- 졸나도 주지 안을 눈치를 알엇든지 한쪽 구석에 노혀 잇는 밥그릇을 제 손으로 들어다가 먹으려고 한다만 밥그릇에 밥은 한술까락 밧게 되지 안은 노- 란 조밥이엿다.
동생 철한이가 먹는 것은 쌀죽이다. 반찬도 업시 한술밧게 안되는 노- 란 조밥을 먹겟다고 안젓는 누이가 가엽게 생각되엿든지
「이것 먹어- 응- 」하면서 누이압에 죽 그릇을 내여놋는다. 은애도 조밥보담 힌죽이 더 조흔 줄은 알엇슴으로 동생 철한이의 말대로 함께 먹으려고 달여들엇다.
「네가 한술깔 먹고 또 내가 한술깔 먹을테야.」
「응 그래!」
그래도 누이 노릇 하느라고 동생 입에 한술까락 떠너어주고 제가 떠먹는다. 공긔에 담은 소고기 반찬도 동생 입에만 너어주고 은애는 번번히 먹지 안코 몹시 찔긴 힘쭐만 뜨더서 먹는다. 그런데도 불고하고 철한이는 맘이 살작 도라섯든지 누이가 고기를 먹는다고 울엇슴으로 은애는 먹든 숫가락을 던지고 나안젓다. 긔자는 그들의 하는 태도가 몹시도 자미스럽게도 보혓스며 또 한쪽으로는 가엽시 보혓다.
은애는 가만 안젓다가 무엇을 생각햇든지.
「엄마-―아버지 사진을 저기다가 붓처조-」
「네가 아버지를 알기나 알면서 그러니?」하는 어머님의 말슴이 섭섭하게 들엿든지.
방 바닥에 딩굴면서 울다가
「사진틀에 끼여서 붓처 줄테니 울지 말어.」하고 달내는 어머님의 말삼에 울음을 끗친다.
긔자는 겨우 일곱살 되는 은애의 생각―아버지를 생각하는 맘이 간절함에 놀나지 안을 수 업는 동시에 측은한 늣김도 잇섯다.
「은애―아버지 보고 십흐니?」하고 긔자는 물엇다.
「참 보고 십허요.」
「아버지가 어듸 가섯지?」
「우리 아버지는 도라 가섯서요.」
「아버지가 엇더케 도라 가섯지?」
「...」
은애는 대답이 업스니 어머니가 대신으로 「다 알어요. 그러기 때문에 밤낫 총을 사달나고 졸는담니다.」하고 대답하신다.
「총을 해서는 무엇 할나구요?」
「저의 아버지 죽인 사람을 쏴 죽인다고 늘 말하기를 아버지 죽인 개(犬)를 죽인다지요.」
그리고 제딴는 아번지를 퍽 생각하는 모양임니다. 누구집에 가서도 그 집 애들이 저의 아버지 무릅에 안저 잇는 것을 보고는 울고 야단이람니다. 산시(山市)에서 저의 아버지 장례식 때도 겨우 네살이엿는데 오리나 되는 장례식장으로 따러 갓담니다.
「갓다와서 무엇이라고 하지 안어요?」
「아버지를 웨 흙에다 파 뭇는냐고 하겟지요?」이럿케 긔자와 이약이하는 어머님의 말슴을 한참 듯고 잇더니 붓그러운 생각이 드러갓든지 어머님보고 이약이 하지 말나고 떼쓴다.
긔자는 또 다시 은애에게
「인제 크게 자라서 어룬 되면 무엇 할테냐?」
「총을 가지고 우리 아버지 죽인 「개」를 죽여요.」하고는 얼골을 붉힌다. 말하는 것이나 노는 태도로 보아서 녀성적 성격을 가젓다기 보담 어듸를 보든지 남성적이엿다. 자긔 어머님의 말슴을 듯건대
요새도 거리에서 애들이 총을 가진 것을 보고는 작고 사달나고 졸은다구. 그리고는 나무 각으로 총갓치 만들어서는 그것을 가지고 총이라고 「땅땅땅」 하면서 총을 놋는 체 한다니 긔이한 일이다.
「학교에 단이고 십지 안어?」하면서 머리를 쓰담으며 뭇는 긔자에게
「단이고 십허요.」하고 대답하는 양도 퍽 남성적이엿다.
헤국녀사는 또 다시 말삼을 게속 하신다.
「지금 학교에 간다고 야단이람니다. 이 압에 잇는 학교 마당에 아츰마다 가서 보고는 학교에 듸레 달나고 떼를 씀니다.」
누이가 고기 반찬을 먹엇다고 울든 철한이는 어머님의 젓꼭지를 물고 잠들엇슴으로 은애에게만 몃마듸 말을 물어보고 그 자리를 떠낫다.(3월 26일)

아버지업는 어린애라면 누구나 가린하고 불상한 생각을 가지게 됨니다. 세상에는 이러한 불상한 외로운 어린애고 얼마나 만슴니까?
사랑하는 부모님 무릅우에서 불행을 몰으고 곱게 자라나는 어린이들보다 이들의 불상한 고아들은 한갓 행운아들이 가지고 잇는 생각 이외에 엇더한 다른 생각을 가지고 잇슬 것임니다. 그럼으로 긔자는 어는날 점심때 쯤 해서 소격동에 잇는 고 방정환(方定煥)씨 택을 찻게 되엿슴니다.
대문간에 발을 듸례 노흐니 6, 7세 되여 보이는 사나 아희와 4, 5세 쯤 되는 녀아가 머리를 한데 모으고 안저서 이약이를 해가며 무엇을 하고 잇섯슴니다. 긔자에<31> 발이 안방 으로 옴겨지지 안코 그들의 안저 잇는 쪽으로 향하게 되엿슴은 그들이 얼마 전에 세상을 떠나신 방정환씨의 자제인 줄 짐작하엿든 까닭임니다.
기자 「무엇해요.」하고 그들이 안즌 틈에 가서 허락도 업시 안고 보니 좀 미안한 생각이 들엇슴니다.
