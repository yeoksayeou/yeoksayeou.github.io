Article Title: 百結先生 (Paekkyŏl Sŏnsaeng / Teacher Hundred Patches)

Article Type: 시 (Poem)

Paekkyŏl Sŏnsaeng
						Ch'oe Mun-jin

Pink jeogori, neatly worn,
Silk ribbon dangling,
Giggling, sniffling,
Children making New Year's bows,
Though I grow one year older,
I eat rice cake, so I am happy.

The sound of pounding rice cake from the house in front,
Tears for the have-nots;
The sound of pounding rice cake from the house behind,
Laughter for the haves.
Same people, same new year,
Why these tears and laughter?

Patches upon patches sewn together,
Tied up tightly, here and there,
Through the tattered sleeve-ends
Fluttering in the wind,
The profound sound of the kŏmun'go –
Is it not celestial music from Heaven?

Even a body weary from hunger
Is revived by this sound;
A mind clouded from reading
Becomes clear with this sound.
The worries and cares of the world,
Let them be forgotten with the kŏmun'go.

Though poorly dressed his whole life,
Never an unpleasant look seen;
Though starved the whole day long,
His wife, who never complained,
At the sound of the neighbors' rice cake pounding,
Tears fall drop by drop.

Having starved even through breakfast,
How could there be anything for dinner?
Though ragged and starved,
He only strums the kŏmun'go.
On this day, at this time, she frowned.

The sound of pounding rice cakes for the New Year
Makes the whole village bustle,
But this old man, reading day and night
And playing the kŏmun'go –
Does reading create rice?
Does playing the kŏmun'go create money?

"Oh dear, oh dear, my wife,
What is this, what is this?
This insolence never seen before,
What is this, what is this?
These tears never seen before,
Why this unseemly behavior?"

"If you ask for rice, I'll give you rice,
If you ask for rice cake, I'll give you rice cake.
*K'ungdaeng k'ungdaeng k'ungdaeng k'ungdaeng* –
Shall I pound rice cakes with the sound of the kŏmun'go?"
His crying wife, dumbfounded,
Suddenly burst into laughter.

This lifelong desire
Was never for wealth and rank.
The high abode of kings, dukes, generals, and ministers,
Their authoritative commands,
Compared to one tune from this kŏmun'go,
They are not worthy of comparison.

Paekkyŏl Sŏnsaeng was a person from the time of King Chabi of Silla. There are no detailed records about him in the *Sagi* (Historical Records). However, it is true that he was a renowned scholar-recluse (處士) of his time.
His household was extremely poor, but he always played the kŏmun'go and finished his life with integrity. He always wore worn-out patched clothes. When they tore, he would sew them up again; thus, his clothes were sewn from hundreds of torn pieces. Therefore, people called him Paekkyŏl Sŏnsaeng (Teacher Hundred Patches). It was the last night of the year, New Year's Eve. The whole village was bustling, pounding rice cakes, but in Paekkyŏl Sŏnsaeng's house, there was nothing even for the next morning's meal. However, Paekkyŏl Sŏnsaeng still sat in his room, only playing the kŏmun'go. His wife, who until now had never uttered a single unpleasant word to her husband, felt she could not bear it this evening and finally spoke displeased words. The teacher changed the tune he was playing and imitated the sound of the neighbors pounding rice cakes with his kŏmun'go. His wife, who had only been crying until then, finally laughed at this sound, according to the anecdote.<45>

**Summary**

This article presents a poem and a short biographical note about Paekkyŏl Sŏnsaeng, a poor but virtuous scholar from the Silla Dynasty famed for his integrity and skill with the kŏmun'go (a traditional Korean zither). Despite extreme poverty, symbolized by his clothes made of a hundred patches, he finds solace and meaning in music and scholarship, detached from material wealth. The anecdote highlights a moment on New Year's Eve when his wife despairs over their lack of food while neighbors prepare celebratory rice cakes, but Paekkyŏl Sŏnsaeng cheers her up by imitating the sound of rice cake pounding on his kŏmun'go. From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this piece, published in 1932, might be interesting to historians as it emphasizes traditional Korean values like scholarly integrity, artistic spirit, and resilience in poverty. Recalling such historical figures could serve as a subtle assertion of Korean cultural identity and history during a period of Japanese colonial rule and modernization pressures, potentially offering readers a figure embodying timeless virtues distinct from contemporary hardships or colonial influence. The use of older Korean orthography is also notable.

분홍저고리 고이고이
갑사 댕시 듸리우고
히히해해 흐늑이며
세배단이는 아해들아
나 한 살 먹어가도
떡을 먹으니 나는 조와

압집의 떡 방아소리
업는 사람의 눈물이며
뒷집의 떡치는 소리
잇는 사람의 우슴이라
갓흔 사람 갓흔 새해
눈물 우숨이 어인 일고

쪼백쪼백 이슨 누덕이
따닥따닥 얽어매고
찌저진 소매자락
휘날리는 바람결에
현묘한 거문고 소리
天上仙樂이 안닌가

배고라 나룬한 몸도
이 소리에 소생되고
긁 읽던 흐린 정신
이 소리로 맑어갈 제
세상에 근심 걱정
거문고로 이즐러라

한 평생 헐버서도
언자는 빗 볼 수 업고
왼 종일 굶어서도
군말 업던 마누라도
이웃집 떡 방아소리
방울방울 눈물이라

앗츰조ㅅ차 굴믄 몸이
저녁거리 이슬손야
헐벗고 굶어서도
거문고만 더듬을세
이날 잇때 웃찡그럿다

새해 맛는 떡 치는 소리
왼 동내가 들석해도
밤낫으로 글 읽기와
거문고 타는 이 영감아
글 읽으면 밥 생기고
거문고 타면 돈 생기노

여보 여보 마누라님
이게 이게 웬 일이오
전에 업던 이 망발이
이게 이게 웬 일이오
전에 업는 이 눈물이
방정맛게 웬 일이오

밥 달라면 밥을 주고
떡 달라면 떡을 주지

거문고 소리로 떡을 칠가

비할 배 안니러라

백결선생은 신라 慈悲王 때 사람이다. 史記에는 그에 대한 자세한 기록이 업섯다. 그러나 그가 당시 고명한 처사인 것만은 사실이다.
그의 집은 극빈하나 항상 거문고를 타시면서 일평생을 고결하게 맛추시엇다. 그는 늘 헌 누덕이를 몸에 감고 잇섯다. 찌저지면 또 뀌어 매고 이러케 그의 옷은 수백 번 쯔저진 것을 이슨 것이다. 그럼으로 세상 사람들이 그를 백결선생이라고 불럿다. 그 해도 마지막 가는 섯달 그믐날 밤이다. 왼 동내가 들석하게 떡을 치는데 이 백결선생의 집에는 내일 앗츰거리도 업섯다. 그러나 백결선생은 여전히 방에 들어 안저 거문고만 타시엇다. 지금것 자기 남편에게 한 마듸 불쾌한 언사를 하여보지 못한 그 마누라도 이날 저녁만은 참을 수 업다는 듯이 비로소 언자는 말을 하엿다. 선생은 타시던 곡조를 곳처 거문고 소리로 이웃집 떡치는 소리를 하엿다. 지금것 울기만 하던 그 마누라도 그만 이 소리에 웃섯다는 일화가 잇섯다.<45>
