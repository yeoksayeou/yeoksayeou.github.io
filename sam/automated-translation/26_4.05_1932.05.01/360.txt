『A Man Starved for a Wife』(Also known as Bankruptcy) One-Act Play
						Yun Paengnam (尹白南)
Characters

Ko Yubong (高裕豐) XX Bank Managing Director, age 40
Pak Yŏngsun (朴英舜) His fourth wife, age 28
Mun Ponghŭi (文奉禧) Female reporter for a women's magazine, age 24
Hwasun (和順) Maidservant
Ch'adori (且乭) Manservant
Doctor Han (韓醫師) (Person who does not appear on stage)

Late Spring, a certain year

Pak Yŏngsun's drawing room
A Western-style house – a room lavishly decorated with high-class furnishings. Downstage left, a door (뚜어-) leads to the hallway. Upstage right, on a wall bent at a right angle, another door leads to a different room. Through two glass windows on the front wall, beyond the narrow wooden porch (퇴마루), the garden flowers are visible through gaps in the curtains (카-텐).
On the slanted wall downstage left, a piano. On it, several books of sheet music, and above it on the wall hangs an oil painting (油繪) in a frame (額). In the center of the room, a round table (테-불), upon which sit a desk lamp, tea service, and plates with peeled fruit remains, all placed disorderly. In the upstage right corner, a splendid Western wardrobe (衣欌), and next to it, a treadle Singer sewing machine (씽거-재봉긔), an electric motor gramophone (축음기), two sofas (쏘파-), and four ordinary chairs. All the furnishings are expensive and smart.
The husband, Ko Yubong, is sunk into the sofa near the table, reading a newspaper held so high it almost covers his face. The wife, Yŏngsun, sits apart near the piano, her hands busy knitting with woolen yarn (毛糸). Both are dressed in Western clothes.

Ko (Lowering the newspaper from his face) So, you mean you absolutely insist on asking for a divorce?

Yŏng: I am not asking for a divorce.

Ko: Then?

Yŏng: I am saying that I *will* get a divorce.

Ko: Isn't that the same thing?

Yŏng: How is it the same? It's different. When a divorce is obviously necessary, what is there to ask for? It's enough if I just leave.

Ko: Like they do in some country or other, you mean.

Yŏng: It's the truth.
(During this exchange, Yŏngsun pauses and resumes her knitting.)

Ko (Placing the newspaper he was holding onto the table) Wife, have you gone mad? After I've explained my feelings to such an extent, shouldn't you show some consideration for my standing (體毛)? Really.

Yŏng: Yes. I have shown quite enough consideration for your standing. Yes, think about it. Isn't it sufficient that I've endured for over a year since I realized I was deceived by you? Don't you think that's ample? Still, trying to satisfy more greed, trampling on another's feelings—isn't there a limit? Think about it.

Ko: What deception did I commit against you? Huh? Is my love for you less than others'? Do you mean I have another legitimate wife? Do I not spend money? What are you lacking to be like this? (Ko, agitated, stands up from the sofa and paces back and forth) When you came to me, was this piano here? Was this Victrola (삑트로라) here? And for whom was this modern house built? Huh? Everything was built for you! Huh? Wasn't all the money spent to cater to your wishes? So, how else am I supposed to love you?

Yŏng: Oh, I know that much.

Ko: Then? That's what I mean. I cannot understand why you've been bringing up the divorce issue constantly for some time now. A person who would have finished her life as a mere kindergarten teacher has now become the wife of a bank's managing director, hasn't she? The flower of the social circles! And your private Packard car (빠카-드) awaits your command at any time. What are you lacking? What?

Yŏng: So, you're the philanthropist who elevated a pitiful woman destined to spend her life as a kindergarten teacher, are you? Hahaha. Why do you say such childish things? Did I beg you to elevate me because I was a kindergarten teacher? You liked me and brought me here, didn't you? I don't know if I'm beautiful or not, but didn't you bring me here to boast that you acquired a beautiful wife? You used every trick in the book to bring me here when I wasn't even dreaming of it. How can you so brazenly say such things, conveniently covering up the fact that it was to satisfy your own desires? So, you built a modern house and bought a piano because you pitied my situation as a teacher? Why do you twist things done to satisfy your own greed into favors bestowed upon me? Huh? I don't want such favors. When you met me, what did you say? Didn't you say with that very mouth that if you could call a beauty like me 'wife' for just one day, you could die without regrets? Didn't you? If you didn't, say you didn't. In fact, I have been doing you a great charity.

Ko (Approaching his wife closely, bending his waist) Let's say that's true. Let's say it is. I misspoke. Now—let's assume I said what you just claimed I said. No, I certainly did say it. If so, isn't that further proof that I loved you? And truthfully, I cannot live without you. Also, it involves my standing in society.

Yŏng: I've considered your standing for long enough.

Ko: You mean I haven't sacrificed anything?

Yŏng: Money?

Ko: Anything.

Yŏng: You spent a lot, yes. But even buying a painted signboard costs money; doesn't it cost that much money to buy a living person as a signboard? Didn't you acquire me as something to boast about having a pretty wife and to hide your own deficiency (不具)?

Ko: Are you saying you cannot acknowledge even slightly how much I loved you, both spiritually and materially—no, how much I *am* loving you?

Yŏng: If viewed through the meaning of 'love' you are now speaking of, you certainly have loved me a lot. But I detest that kind of love. It's a frightening love that gives me more suffocating pain than prison.

Yŏng (Ko stops his pacing abruptly and glares at his wife. His face aghast...)

Yŏng: The love I demand isn't that kind of love shown through material things, nor the kind of love like a father loving a child or an older brother loving a younger one. What kind of pain have you inflicted upon me until today? Have you ever given me satisfaction? Yes? Have you ever fulfilled the duties of a satisfactory husband? If I simply had no husband, I could perhaps resign myself to that reality. But (The wife throws down her knitting, stands up, and comes before her husband) having a seemingly respectable husband like you... (She paces back and forth, looking angry) The closer you get to me, the more unbearable pain you inflict upon me. A fine-sounding fence, this is! (During this, the husband sinks powerlessly onto the sofa, clutches his head in his hands, and listens as if prostrate) How can I resolve this unsatisfactory, suffocating<40> pain of mine? If my whole life is to be like this, I'll drink lye and die! I will! I'll just die! Honey, the old-fashioned ladies who spent their entire lives just bearing the name of 'so-and-so's wife' without being able to say a word were pitiful ignoramuses! Victims of flawed doctrines (道念)! Slaves to feudalistic thought! But who can prove that their tears didn't soak their pillows? However, they at least had a glimmer of hope that their husbands might return to them. But... but what about you? Aren't you sexually deficient (性의 불구자)? Even if you live your whole life, you will never be able to satisfy a woman, will you? Isn't that right? Isn't that right? Try and bravely answer that it isn't so!

Ko (Powerlessly) Right. I know your words are right. But it's not my fault.

Yŏng: Of course, I sympathize. It's not that I don't feel sorry for you. Considering how you deceived me (소위), I am furious, but when I think about the reasons why you had to deceive me, it's not that I don't think of you with pity.

Ko: So, how about deepening that sympathy a bit more? Huh? What's stopping you? Isn't this also within the scope of your fate (八字)? Are there only one or two people in the world who live their entire lives as widows? Consider yourself a widow and endure it. In return, I will provide anything...

Yŏng: No, that's unacceptable. I can't do it. Do you know how many women avoid hysteria while living like widows? Do you think there are only one or two women weeping beneath memorial gates for virtuous women? I detest that kind of hypocrisy. Even if I have to live in a mud hut (土幕), if there's someone who will hold me tightly... (She gives a hysterical scoff) How many times have you been married? You probably blamed every wife who left you, accusing them of immoral conduct. If their conduct was indeed immoral, whose fault was it? I'm saying I'll leave beforehand because I fear receiving such a reputation.

Ko: That's why I've forgiven you everything from the beginning, haven't I? Haven't I refrained entirely from interfering with your freedom, no matter who you go with or where?

Yŏng: I hate that. I don't want such servile and fawning non-interference. I want a strong husband capable of interfering, scolding, and being jealous, a husband who doesn't inflict pitiful deficiencies upon his wife.
(Having said this, Yŏngsun goes to the piano and begins to fix her makeup.)
Husband Ko's eyes increasingly show the light of anger.

Ko (His trembling hand reaches for the teacup on the table; he picks it up inadvertently and takes a couple of steps forward) Then you absolutely intend to get a divorce? Huh? You intend to leave?
(His voice rises.)

Yŏng (Maintaining her pose looking in the mirror) Haven't I been saying this for some time? Why are you trying so hard? There are plenty of excuses, aren't there? Just say you kicked me out for immoral conduct.

Ko: What?
(As he takes another step forward, the upstage door opens, and the maidservant Hwasun enters.)

Hwa: Mistress, there's a phone call for you.

Yŏng: From where?

Hwa: They said they're calling from Suhach'ŏng (水下町).
(Ko pours tea into the cup in his hand with his own hand and drinks it, not because he wants to, but to cover his awkwardness.)

Yŏng: Alright. Tell them Master's voice sounds weary (목성이 시듸 - idiomatically 'voice sounds hoarse/tired').

Hwa: Yes, ma'am.
(Yŏngsun hastily finishes her makeup and exits upstage. Maidservant Hwasun gathers the tea things piece by piece, glancing furtively at her master who is pacing back and forth like a bear in a zoo cage, and disappears inside. Meanwhile, from inside, the sound of a phone conversation:
"Yes, yes, I'll go right away. Haven't they arrived yet? Then you must be alone. Yes, yes. That's right. I'll go right away. Yes, yes. I said I'll go right away."
(The voice is heard. Then she comes out briefly. And again towards the inside:)

Yŏng: Oh, right, Hwasun!

Hwa (Voice only) Yes?

Yŏng: Tell them to prepare the car.

Hwa: Yes, ma'am.

Yŏng (To her husband) I forgot there's a music concert preparation—no, a rehearsal meeting tonight. I'm going to Doctor Han's house in Suhach'ŏng now. Think it over thoroughly while I'm gone. (She glances in the mirror once more.)

Ko (Rushes over, grabs his wife's shoulders, and turns her to face him) Honey!

Yŏng: Why are you doing this?

Ko: With Doctor Han...

Yŏng: ...?...

Ko: You wouldn't deceive my eyes (내 눈을 기으진 안치 - i.e., cheat on me behind my back)?

Yŏng: Hohohoho. Don't be jealous like flat soda—so pointlessly. Honey. Do you really think of me that way? If I wanted to do *that* secretly, couldn't I do it anytime? It's because I don't want to do that, that I'm openly seeking a divorce.
(At this moment, Hwasun enters again.)

Hwa: The car is ready.

Yŏng: Okay.
(Yŏngsun follows Hwasun and disappears through the downstage door.
Ko furiously tears the newspaper lying on the table, throws it onto the floor, paces angrily a few times, then rings the bell. Hwasun's voice answers "Yes—" from inside.)

Hwa: Did you call, sir?

Ko: Bring the whiskey.

Hwa: The square bottle (角甁)?

Ko: Yes.
(Hwasun goes inside and quickly returns with a whiskey bottle and a cup on a tray. Ko snatches them almost, takes the bottle, and downs two glasses as if drinking water. At this moment, the doorbell rings outside. Hwasun disappears downstage, then returns just as Ko is about to drink his third glass.)

Hwa: Master.

Ko: ...?...

Hwa: That lady reporter from the women's magazine is here. She says she came because she has something to ask the Mistress.

Ko: Why didn't you just say she wasn't here?

Hwa: Yes, sir (Starts to leave).

Ko: Hey, isn't she that young lady who used to come sometimes?

Hwa: Yes. You mean the pretty one, sir?

Ko (Pauses, thinking about something) Hmm—hey.

Hwa: Yes?

Ko: Tell her to come in here. Say the master has something to discuss briefly.

Hwa: Yes, sir.

Ko: Hurry up and go. What are you standing there for?

Hwa: Later... should I not mention this conversation to the Mistress?

Ko: I don't want to hear it. Just go, I said!

Hwa: Yes, sir— (She exits, shaking her head slightly.)
(Meanwhile, Ko carries the whiskey bottle here and there, under the table, onto the piano, eventually placing it on the gramophone, then returns to stand before the table. A knocking sound—)

Ko: Come in.

Female Reporter (Entering the room) Forgive me—Have you been well?

Ko: It's been a while. You must be very busy.

Female Reporter: Just the usual.

Ko: Please sit here (Offers a chair).

Female Reporter: Yes. By the way, the Madam seems to have stepped out?

Ko: Yes, she went out somewhere.

Female Reporter: You mentioned you had something to discuss?

Ko: Yes.

Female Reporter: What might it be?

Ko: Yes.

Female Reporter: I would appreciate it if you could tell me.

Ko (Stuck for words) Yes—yes. It's nothing much, really. My profession is, so to speak, banking, and while others know me for engaging in reputable profit-making (取利), in reality, I have a great interest in cultural enterprises.<41> Like that women's magazine, for instance. I have this thought that I'd like to try making one splendidly.

Female Reporter (Her face brightening, she pulls her chair closer) That's truly delightful news to hear. Korean women are much more enlightened now. I've often thought it would be wonderful if a capable woman could manage this well, but more than anything, it's about money.

Ko: That's right. Of course, it's a matter of funds. But funds, well, if I were to back it, it wouldn't be a difficult problem. How much would it take?

Female Reporter: Roughly 1,500 or 1,600 won would be needed to have enough capital to publish maybe 4 or 5 issues, don't you think?

Ko: What can you do with such paltry money? You need at least 10,000 won. You need to rent an office in a building, get a telephone, and hire 3 or 4 reporters on salary. And you need tremendous advertising.

Female Reporter (Getting progressively excited) If it could be done like that, it could become the king of the magazine world in Korea! But...

Ko: Why do you hesitate? Do you think I couldn't provide such funds?

Female Reporter: Not at all! How could a wealthy man like you not have that much money? It's just a question of whether you truly have that much enthusiasm.

Ko: I do, I do! As long as you are undertaking this work, I will provide any amount of money.

Female Reporter: Yes?

Ko: If you become the president and work sincerely, I'll provide not just 10,000 won, but even more.

Female Reporter (Barely suppressing her ecstatic joy) If you would really do that... But why would you trust me, whom you haven't known for long, and provide such a large sum of money?

Ko: You are teasing me too much. Do I look like someone who would lie?

Female Reporter: Not at all. That wasn't my meaning; I'm just saying it because your words are so gracious.

Ko: No, no. You're saying you still don't quite trust me. Shall I say one thing that will make you trust me completely?

Female Reporter: Yes.

Ko (Moving his chair slightly closer to the reporter) Miss Ponghŭi.

Female Reporter: ...?...

Ko: Don't rebuke me—please marry me. Yes. Please become my wife.

Female Reporter: ?

Ko: It's embarrassing to say, but I've actually been burning with passion for Miss Ponghŭi ever since before you were a reporter, back when you were working as a bill collector (集金員) for Yamaguchi Bank (山口銀行). Before love, there are no heroes, status is useless, and one cannot maintain dignity (體毛). Indeed, for the sake of my own dignity, I have suffered until today like holding a painful secret in my heart (벙어리 냉ㅅ가슴 알듯 - like a mute suffering from a cold heart/chest infection). They say passion cools as the days go by, but for me, the passion grows stronger day by day, and now I've truly reached a point where I cannot bear it anymore. But where was the opportunity? And even if there had been an opportunity, I lacked the courage to confess such agony.

Female Reporter (Blushing) It's so unexpected, I don't know what to say.

Ko: I understand. Of course, you would feel that way. But please grant my wish, as if you were saving a person's life. Yes.

Female Reporter: How can I possibly grant your wish? Firstly, you clearly have a wife. And...

Ko: No, no. That person is probably sitting with her lover right now, drafting the divorce papers to send me.

Female Reporter: You're getting divorced?

Ko: That's right. This issue has been ongoing for some time and has now reached its conclusion. Can I keep a wife with such improper conduct in my house?

Female Reporter: Even so, you must give me some time to think about it.

Ko: I will, of course I will! But please make up your mind. If you just consent, starting tomorrow, Miss Ponghŭi will be the wife of the bank's managing director, the flower of society. And the president of the women's magazine company. If you dislike that, you can quit, that's fine too. And if you don't like this house, we can build a new villa-cum-house wherever you like. And if you don't care for the Packard car, a Hudson (하드손) is fine, a Chevrolet (시보레-) is fine too... (During this, the reporter's eyes seem to chase some vision, and her face, flushed, grows hotter and hotter)
Whatever you want, I will provide. I feel like I cannot live without you. (The reporter's head gradually bows down)
Yes? Yes?
(Ko takes the reporter's hand. Ponghŭi does not try to pull it away. At this moment, Hwasun suddenly opens the door and enters. Ko, startled, shrinks back.)

Hwa: Did you call?

Ko: I didn't call!
(Hwasun disappears again)

Ko (Moves closer to the reporter again, placing a hand on her shoulder)
If you just give your consent right here, I will start the preparations. Yes.
(As he is about to embrace her, Hwasun pokes her head into the room again)

Hwa: Master, did you call?

Ko (Angrily) Ugh—that wench! I didn't call!

Hwa: If you don't have any errands for me right now, I'd like to go to the night market (夜市) for a bit.

Ko (Pleased) Oh—yes, do that. Go ahead.

Hwa: But, um, uh...

Ko (Catching her meaning) Come here. (Takes a 50-jeon silver coin from his pocket and gives it to her) Enjoy yourself thoroughly and come back.

Hwa: Yes, sir— (Exits cheerfully)
(Ko, now with a look of relief, comes to the reporter's side and embraces her. The reporter buries her face on the table)

Ko: Starting tomorrow, I must quickly arrange things so I can call you "Wife". What a joyous day this is for me.
(At this moment, the downstage door suddenly opens, and Yŏngsun appears. Ko, flustered, steps back a few paces, and the reporter, embarrassed, lowers her head again. Yŏngsun stares at the two with harsh eyes for a moment, then her tense face suddenly relaxes, and she bursts into convulsive laughter)

Yŏng: Hohohoho. Honey, you really are something. How starved for a wife can you be? In the short time I was gone, you've already picked out a candidate. Ho... Miss Ponghŭi! I apologize for interrupting your pleasant time together. It's true that I am leaving this house. Rest assured. However, as a gift out of our past friendly acquaintance, let me offer one piece of advice. It's nothing much, but I worry for your sake that before a year is out, you might find yourself publishing a confessional article titled "Why I Left My Husband" in the magazine you manage... I apologize for being a donkey tied in a flower garden (꼿밧테 나귀 맨 격 - i.e., being an unwelcome presence). Enjoy yourselves.
(Saying this, she gathers the sheet music books lying on the piano.)

Yŏng: I just came back for my sheet music and ended up spoiling someone else's fun.
(She disappears through the downstage door. As soon as the mistress vanishes, the reporter lifts her head.)

Female Reporter (In a half-crying voice) Sir! I've been insulted! How can I endure such humiliation? Yes? Now, since I've already suffered this unavoidable stigma (루명), please marry me as soon as possible. Yes? Please grant my wish.
(Ko remains silent, pacing back and forth with his hand supporting his chin.)

Female Reporter: Why aren't you saying anything? Yes?

Ko (Quietly comes before the table, bows deeply) Please return to your work. I have woken from my dream just now. Indeed, I was a man starved for a wife. I inflicted pain on many women to hide the fact that I am sexually deficient—disabled (病身). Please go back quickly and arrange your manuscript. I am a person unqualified to marry.

Female Reporter (Stands up abruptly in utter shock, stares at Ko for a moment. Then:) What about my reputation? Yes? What becomes of my situation?

Ko: Just now, I was insulted by Yŏngsun as "a man starved for a wife," but it's certain that in the future, I will be cursed by your lips as "a man deserving death." Please go now. Yes.
(The reporter stares at Ko for a moment, then, as if anger is surging up, she grabs her handbag (핸드빡), stomps towards the downstage door, glares this way again briefly, then violently throws the door open and rushes outside. Ko rings the bell on the table. Twice—three times—.)

Ch'a (Poking only his head into the room) Did you call, sir?

Ko: Come in.

Ch'a: Yes, sir. (Enters the room and assumes an attentive posture.)

Ko: Bring down all the furniture from the upper floor into this room. And go to Hwanggŭmch'ŏng (黃金町) and fetch a junk dealer.

Ch'a (Eyes wide) Why call a junk dealer in the middle of the night?

Ko: Just fetch him if I tell you to!

Ch'a: Yes, sir.

Ko: This house is bankrupt (破産) today.
(Ko drinks straight from the bottle of liquor placed on the gramophone, and as Ch'adori stands there with his mouth agape, eyes wide, the curtain slowly falls.)
—(If performing this play, please obtain the author's permission.)—
Written April 13, 1932<42>

**Summary**

This one-act play, "A Man Starved for a Wife," depicts the confrontation between Ko Yubong, a wealthy but impotent bank executive, and his fourth wife, Pak Yŏngsun. Yŏngsun demands a divorce, explicitly citing his inability to provide marital satisfaction and rejecting his attempts to placate her with material luxury. She exposes his hidden "deficiency" and leaves. Immediately, Yubong tries to woo Mun Ponghŭi, a young female reporter, promising her wealth, status, and funding for her magazine if she marries him. Yŏngsun returns unexpectedly, mocks Yubong's desperation, and warns Ponghŭi of the future awaiting her. Shamed and confronted with his pattern, Yubong rejects Ponghŭi, admits his inadequacy, declares himself "bankrupt," and orders his servant to sell the household furniture.

From a historical perspective, the play is interesting for its portrayal of a "modern" Korean woman in 1932 demanding divorce based on personal and sexual fulfillment, a stark contrast to traditional Confucian expectations. It critiques the emptiness of materialism offered by the (impotent) patriarch as a substitute for genuine intimacy and exposes male vulnerability hidden behind wealth and status. The setting in a Westernized home with modern amenities like a piano, gramophone, and car highlights the intersection of modernization and persistent social/personal conflicts during the colonial period.
