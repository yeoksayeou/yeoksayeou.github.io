Publication Date	1936년 02월 01일
Article Title	失職(承前) (Loss of Job (Continued))
Author	廉想涉 (Yŏm Sangsŏp)
Article Type	소설 (Novel)

失職(承前)
						廉想涉 (Yŏm Sangsŏp)
Writer Sketch

It can be said that Mr. Yŏm is counted among the foremost long-form writers in the Peninsular literary scene (半島文壇), having undertaken many long works. Those already published number over seven or eight, including "Love and Sin" (사랑과 罪), "Sunflower" (해바라기), "Before the Manse Demonstrations" (萬歲前), "Three Generations" (三代), "Two Hearts" (二心), and "When the Peonies Bloom" (모란꼿필 때). Furthermore, stand-alone books include "Morning Glory" (牽牛花) and "The Ring" (반지). His writing style is consistently grounded in Realism (寫實主義). Originally from Sajik-tong (社稷洞) in Kyŏngsŏng (京城), he dropped out of Posŏng Middle School (普成中學), graduated from Kyōto Second Middle School (京都 第二中學) in Kyōto, went to Tōkyō (東京) where he attended Keiō University's (慶應大學) Literature Department (文科) but dropped out midway. Afterward, he served as the head of the arts and literature departments at the Tonga Ilbo (東亞日報) and Chosun Ilbo (朝鮮日報). Currently, while serving as the political editor for a certain newspaper (某新聞), he steadily wields his creative brush. He is 40 years old (春秋).

Perhaps because Tŏkch'ang spoke meekly, or perhaps because the owner felt it inappropriate to make a racket when customers were crowding in, the M Cafe (카페-) owner's attitude suddenly changed, and he suggested they go upstairs to talk quietly, pulling Tŏkch'ang up to the second floor. Upstairs, despite the cramped space, there were three rooms arranged in a U-shape from the entrance. In front of the door to the room in the far corner, a pair of men's shoes was placed.
"So, where are you staying now?"
The owner, practically shoving Tŏkch'ang into the room directly facing the stairs, stood blocking the door and asked. Tŏkch'ang pictured his daughter waiting at home once more in his mind, but having already spoken, he hesitated before blurting out,
"I'm planning to go find a friend's house now."
"Anyway, have some more drinks and wait just a moment."
Leaving behind the remark that implied 'drink as much as you want if you have money in your pocket,' the owner shut the door abruptly before Tŏkch'ang could say anything and quickly went downstairs.<260>
The alcohol Tŏkch'ang was trying to get drunk on instantly cleared, and he stood blankly in the chilly, gloomy tatami room dimly lit by a 10-candela bulb. In the center sat a table, stained like the tatami, with an ashtray piled high with ashes. In the far corner, next to a dwarf-like mirror stand, two or three small tin basins used by women going to bathe were scattered about, and stained skirts and blouses were hanging here and there. It seemed that if the central table were pushed aside, this room would become the bedroom for the girls who had no home of their own.
Regardless of the situation, he pulled over one of the two or three cushions stacked in this corner and squatted down in front of the table. However, neither the owner who told him to wait a moment, nor anyone else, brought even a brazier to this cold room. From the room where the man's shoes were placed, the sound of women's shoes had been heard earlier, clattering as food was carried in, but now it was dead silent, as if the depths within were profound.
Tŏkch'ang certainly had neither the courage nor the mood to drink more.
To go to work at the newly hired company starting tomorrow, he needed, even if bought from a second-hand shop, at least an overcoat since he was an outside salesman (外交員). But the only place he had relied on for that was now in this state. Setting that aside, could he even cook breakfast himself and go to work? After leaving, unlike before, how was he supposed to leave Wŏnhŭi (원희년) alone?
Now, rather than thoughts of his runaway wife, he sat blankly contemplating the urgent situation starting tomorrow, when suddenly the hopeful thought occurred to him: perhaps Puni (분이) had returned home in the meantime? Could it be that she wrote such a letter in a fit of pique to teach me a lesson, but then things didn't go as planned, and unable to turn her back, she came back? She has a child, after all! He entertained such fantasies.
(Anyway, since things didn't work out well, she couldn't completely sever ties here either— But perhaps she might come here even now.)
Thinking this, he forgot all thoughts of resentment and whatnot, and it seemed as if he could hear Puni's voice coming from downstairs.
Tŏkch'ang sat there blankly, whether for thirty minutes or an hour, just squatting motionless, and downstairs, they simply left him alone. However, weakened and made timid by squatting idly for the past year or two, he couldn't muster the courage to shout for the owner or chase downstairs to demand a quick settlement and leave.
(Are they trying to imprison me? What part of me looks like I could yield even a pittance (피천샐립)!?) Tŏkch'ang snorted to himself. <261>
(How bad must things be to lose even the woman?...) He sighed again, as if mocking himself.
Finally, sounds of activity came from the other room, and a man and woman emerged, laughing loudly and clattering. Tŏkch'ang briefly considered calling out to the woman now going downstairs to ask her to fetch the owner, but his brow furrowed instinctively, and his voice failed him. He himself had engaged in such philandering (오입), but thinking that Puni had been in this very house, and that Puni too might have been with a man like that woman just now, he couldn't help but scowl.
(But now that Puni has ended up like this, what's the use of thinking such things...)
He tried to correct his thoughts.
Tŏkch'ang now found the idea of sitting alone shivering on this empty second floor more absurd than tiring of waiting, so he abruptly stood up.
"Where are you going?"
Just as he was heading inside to find the owner, the fat man, who had been sitting comfortably by the brazier at the desk, spoke in a disgruntled tone. Perhaps thinking Tŏkch'ang was truly just up from the countryside, he interrogated him like a criminal, which even the destitute Tŏkch'ang found unbearable.
"What good will holding me here do? I'll come tomorrow."
Tŏkch'ang's tone, as if his hidden true nature had suddenly emerged, became abruptly defiant (뾰룽뾰룽하얏다). The owner, seemingly dumbfounded by the other's sudden change in attitude, stared intently up at him for a moment, then said,
"No, what would you do coming tomorrow? Are you trying to sound someone out? If you two planned to run away together, you should have run away properly from the start. What do you take this place for, barging in so carelessly (설불리)? Don't say another word, just give me the 180 wŏn. If you can't, then come with me to Midori (미도리) right now..."
Tŏkch'ang instantly regretted the defiant words spoken in anger. But it was difficult to immediately retract the steps already taken.
"Well, aren't we both looking for her?"
"What do you mean, both? Who signed this seal? If you have eyes, look closely!" the owner shouted, slamming his palm down on a piece of paper folded into quarters that had been laid out on the messy desk. Tŏkch'ang's face turned pale. It was something he hadn't dreamed of. It was the loan agreement (차용증서) Puni had signed four months ago when she first came to this house, borrowing 100 wŏn to adorn herself. It was, so to speak, her body price (몸갑). Tŏkch'ang had acted as a guarantor (보) and affixed his seal, but in this chaotic situation, he hadn't remembered its existence even in his dreams. After that, borrowing ten or twenty wŏn <262> here and there hadn't been easy, and when winter began, she borrowed another hundred wŏn to prepare an overcoat and shoes, thanks to which he was now wearing this shabby suit. Anyway, things added up, leading to a debt of 300 wŏn, but how this fellow knew that the seal belonged to him and was confronting him like this left Tŏkch'ang bewildered.
"I've seen guys mortgage their houses, but this is the first time I've seen someone mortgage his woman!"
Since all the customers downstairs had left and it was quiet, the owner's voice grew louder without restraint. Three girls, drawn by the commotion in the quiet atmosphere, guessed he was Midori's husband and, feeling even more embarrassed, scrutinized Tŏkch'ang's face from every angle, exchanging meaningless glances among themselves. These girls, who weren't on good terms with Midori (though they weren't necessarily on good terms with each other either), watched Midori's husband's face with vague animosity, thinking 'serves her right'. Yet, on the other hand, knowing they too were indebted to this owner and could face a similar fate anytime, they felt a sense of sympathy, as if it wasn't just someone else's problem.
"What are you talking about?"
Tŏkch'ang, while sighing (탄하면서도), spoke gently again.
"Are you still calling her your sister-in-law? Anyway, did this fellow stamp this seal or not? Tell me!"
The owner charged forward again furiously (분연히). But Tŏkch'ang lacked the courage to flatly deny that the seal was his. His conscience (량심), which should either be entirely absent or thoroughly present, peeked out from a corner, making Tŏkch'ang the weakest of the weak.
"Whether you take her away, hide her away, get her arrested, or sell her off, what business is it of mine what a husband does with his own wife? Just pay the money and go! You use that money to go drinking, but why do you intend to brazenly swindle someone else's money (멀정하게 때먹을)? Did you think the world was this naive?"
"What money did I swindle? Who do you think I am to talk like this?"
"Who? You're Kim Tŏkch'ang (김덕창), aren't you? I may not have seen your face (코ㅅ백이), only the seal, but do you think I only recognize trousers and jacket (바지저고리만)?"
The women giggled. It wasn't because Tŏkch'ang's predicament touched their hearts, but because the owner's sharp retorts (절발해내는 말) were satisfying.
Tŏkch'ang felt even more ashamed and indignant (분연하얏스나) at the women's laughter, but his nose felt stuffy (코가 맥맥하얏다). Struck by some thought, he abruptly turned his body from facing the seated owner, plopped down (털석 걸터 안즈면서), <263> shouted, "Bring a bottle of liquor!" and scanned the three women.
"You should settle the bill (간죠-) first."
One of the girls who was most envious of Midori said this, as if talking to herself.
"Bill (간죠-)?"
Tŏkch'ang glared, hesitated (훔첨훔첨), then took out Puni's wallet, which he had brought from home just as it was, pulled out a one-wŏn banknote (지페), and threw it down. The banknote fluttered to the floor. Seeing it, Tŏkch'ang felt his tightly constricted chest suddenly open up, feeling refreshed. But seeing that no one moved to pick up the piece of paper, Tŏkch'ang's heart grew dark and heavy again.
"Hey, hurry up and bring it. Do you think this gentleman doesn't have money? He has at least five hundred wŏn. With a face like that, wouldn't he fetch five hundred? In a place like Sinmach'i (신마찌), it could even be a thousand."
The girls disliked hearing the owner's words. However, as soon as the owner finished speaking, the woman whose eyes were slightly red around the edges from drink and whose eyes were glazed over quickly snatched the one-wŏn note from the floor and placed it on the desk. This was the woman who had come down from the second floor earlier.
A lukewarm bottle of liquor arrived promptly. Tŏkch'ang didn't even bother to pick up a cup; he asked for a large bowl (곱보), poured the whole bottle into it at once, and drank it down in one gulp (단숨에 켜고 말앗다). The owner and the women, merely watching his actions, unconsciously frowned. However, the reasons for their frowning differed.
The owner frowned, thinking, 'This guy's drinking out of anger... which means he has no money...' The women, instinctively feeling a momentary sympathy for a member of the opposite sex in distress, frowned.
"You must be tired from your first trip from the countryside. Shouldn't you settle the bill quickly and go find your friend before it gets too late?"
The owner smoothly (늠을늠을하게) provoked him like this. But Tŏkch'ang, suddenly flushed with drink (주긔가 확 올은), silently lowered his head and sat, puffing hard on a cigarette instead of eating side dishes. Then, lifting his head abruptly, the bravado (호긔) from when he threw down the one-wŏn note and demanded liquor vanished as if it never existed. Deflated once again, he began to speak slowly.
"Let me tell you the truth. Indeed, I stamped that seal. Midori is my wife. Until this morning, she was my wife. Do you understand? Until this morning!..." He emphasized "this morning" and then, even more deflated, said,
"But by now, you probably know better than I do in whose arms she lies, laughing at me." <264> He threw his cigarette onto the dimly lit floor with a thud.
"Huh? You say I know better?..."
The owner, who had been listening with pursed lips, feigning indifference, suddenly flared up at the last remark, becoming utterly arrogant (긔고만장이 되엇다).
"...Hey, look at this! Now you're turning it around, implying I lured her away and hid her with some guy? Hey, turns out you're quite something. If you're going to talk like that, I can't stand it anymore. Let's go—to the police station. Let's find out who took whom, let's sort out the black and white."
As if glad to have seized upon the end of the sentence, rather than genuinely angry, he jumped up frantically, grabbed his overcoat, put on his hat, and stomped downstairs, stamping his feet loudly.
Tŏkch'ang turned pale once again. Although he hadn't been entirely without the thought of testing the owner, the words had slipped out carelessly, yet the consequences became serious.
"No, that's not it. It's just that my situation is so desperate, and then you made such accusations, so I spoke without thinking..."
The owner refused to even listen to Tŏkch'ang's explanation.
"No more talk, let's go. Desperation is your problem. Originally, if anyone has reason to be desperate, it's me... Now! Stop talking nonsense and let's get going!"
The owner seemed genuinely determined to drag him to the police station. Tŏkch'ang, reluctant to argue further, tried to remain seated silently, but the owner, whose arm seemed like a beam compared to Tŏkch'ang's, grabbed Tŏkch'ang's upper arm and jerked him forward, pulling him out halfway across the room.
Tŏkch'ang barely managed to stifle a cry of "Ayaya!" and, putting all his strength into the tips of his toes, stood firm, saying,
"I'll go, so let go. It's not that I don't want to go because I'm guilty of something, but shouldn't the person who needs to come out come out without all this racket?" He tried to resist one last time.
"Stop talking nonsense. You need a taste of the detention cell (류치장) to confess where you hid her. I don't even want the money anymore. Just find Midori!"
Since Midori was the popular waitress of this establishment, he had tried countless times to persuade her not to leave, but now he didn't even care about the money, demanding the person herself, as if demanding the return of his own wife who had been lured away.
Tŏkch'ang had no choice but to be dragged out. Guilty or not, he hated the idea of getting involved there, fearing it would inevitably take time and cause trouble. If things went wrong and he had to spend the night there, he worried about Wŏnhŭi left alone at home, and fretted about his first day at the company tomorrow. <265>
Although it wasn't that late at night, the inside of the police station was empty, perhaps due to some incident, with only one or two officers on duty sitting around a brazier. The owner seemed to know the officers, likely due to his business dealings, which put Tŏkch'ang at an immediate disadvantage.
The owner, preventing Tŏkch'ang from uttering a word, puffed up with self-importance and poured out his side of the story. The officer, initially seeming to listen half-heartedly as if it wasn't his jurisdiction, gradually became interested in the details of the case, perhaps having seen Midori before, and started responding.
"So where did you hide her? If you just tell the truth, this can be settled right away. If you hid her, hurry up and pay off the debt."
After hearing the owner's entire story, the officer turned to Tŏkch'ang and, raising his voice slightly, addressed him reproachfully.
"Who hid anyone? I came home from somewhere today and found she had left this letter and disappeared. So I just went there to find out the detailed circumstances," Tŏkch'ang said, pulling out Puni's farewell letter (헷유서) and presenting it to the officer.
"Then why did you lie and say you were her older brother visiting from the countryside?"
The officer gave this rebuke (핀둥이) and was about to unfold the paper he received when the owner, without giving Tŏkch'ang a chance to explain, jumped in.
"I don't know what that letter is, but that's all part of their scheme. Midori got a copy of her family register (호적등본) a few days ago, one of her friends saw it. When she gets a family register copy or extract (초본), it's obvious she's being sold off (파라넘긴 게) to some guy for good. Do you think something like that could happen without the consent (승낙) of her own husband, especially when she has a child?"
"Hmm," the officer nodded as if acknowledging the plausibility, unfolded Puni's letter, and after reading it, remarked sarcastically,
"Looking at this, it seems like she might have gone to the Han River Railway Bridge (한강철교)." Then he asked Tŏkch'ang,
"So why did she get the family register copy?"
"How should I know? This is the first I'm hearing about it here too."
"Does that make sense? A husband not knowing what his wife is doing between them? Who would believe that?" the officer glared.
"So, it means she either conspired (부동이 됏든지) with some guy or was tricked by some guy, abandoning her husband and child to run away." <266>
The officer wasn't entirely unconvinced (긔연가 미연가 하는 생각이 업지 안핫스나), but retorted,
"Where did you get such absurd talk?" and gave another reprimand. The owner followed up immediately,
"If we fall for such obvious tricks, there'd be no one caught for fraud and embezzlement (사긔횡령)," muttering to himself and clearing his throat with a 'hem'.
The patrolman (순사), not being from the judicial section (사법게), seemed hesitant (망서리다가) to make an arbitrary decision (임의로 처단키). He tentatively suggested to the owner, as if coaxing him, that he take Tŏkch'ang away for now and bring him back tomorrow.
"Well, I don't know. If I let him go like this, won't I lose both the head and the basket (괴두 구럭두)?"
The owner's words subtly implied he wished Tŏkch'ang would be put in the detention cell (류치장).
"But since he has a child, surely he wouldn't run away?"
"Officer, you're talking nonsense (망녕의 말씀). He's probably already dealt with the child, but even if the child exists, can't he take the house with him? Can't he take a mountain with him?" the owner chuckled boisterously.
The patrolman, realizing his careless remark sounded foolish for an officer, sat silently. In any case, it wasn't his jurisdiction, he was tired of conducting an investigation through the night, and putting someone into the already full detention cell seemed troublesome (귀치 안핫다). So, he instructed the owner to leave together, not lose sight of Tŏkch'ang, and bring him back tomorrow.
Dragged out of the police station after ten o'clock, Tŏkch'ang was pulled back to the cafe again. The owner insisted he had to spend the night together and go to the police station tomorrow to settle things before letting him go. Tŏkch'ang argued that unlawfully detaining someone was illegal, pleaded for the sake of the orphaned child left alone, but the owner remained adamant (막무가내하다).
He hoped the owner might let him go when closing the doors, but that too was a vain hope (헛다방이엇다). As a last resort, he even suggested they go together to his place and sleep there for the child's sake, but the owner refused that too, shaking his head. It seemed the owner feared that if he witnessed the reality of Puni having abandoned even her young child, he would lose his pretext for holding Tŏkch'ang accountable.
Trapped with no way out, he spent the night sleeping fitfully at the feet of that brute of an owner. More than anything, his biggest worry was the company where he was supposed to start work at nine today. Now that things had come to this, he didn't want to deal (니ㅅ삿을 어우리기도) with that fellow anymore. Like a rat trapped in a jar, he decided to just let things happen and see how they unfolded. If he couldn't go to the company, he couldn't go; let things be as they may, he quietly resigned himself (슬몃이 자포자긔가 되는 것이엇다). He thought about calling to make up an excuse for not coming, but making such a call, especially on the first day, <267> would seem even more suspicious (연문의 ㅅ짓이다).
Tŏkch'ang got up early in the morning and sat shivering. Even as the winter sun spread its light, not even a young puppy (어리친개 색기) stirred upstairs or downstairs. The owner kept his face covered with the blanket, snoring so loudly it seemed the ceiling might collapse (물러안즐만치), until nearly ten o'clock when the sounds of clattering finally came from the kitchen, and the owner himself groggily opened his eyes. This fellow acted as if Tŏkch'ang didn't exist, keeping a straight face.
Breakfast, such as it was, was eaten around noon. After going through all sorts of transactions, the order from the fat man to "Get up and let's go" came near one o'clock. Sitting outside the police investigation room (취조실) took three hours... Only around the time the electric lights (전둥불) came on in the short daylight (짜른해) was he finally released (?) and allowed into the investigation room. Standing before the chief (부장) with two stars on his insignia, Tŏkch'ang felt slightly relieved; it seemed like a straightforward matter if only they asked, giving him hope of being released immediately. But the chief appeared busy. After listening to the owner's entire explanation, he asked only for Tŏkch'ang's address, name, and occupation, then pointed to a long wooden bench, telling him to go sit there, and told the cafe owner to leave. The owner, who also seemed acquainted with this chief, bowed repeatedly and left after making earnest requests. Being separated from that man felt like escaping a tiger's den, which was a relief, but the prospect of being released from here seemed remote. Perhaps because they were dealing with backlogged cases and not touching new ones, Tŏkch'ang was ignored. After a commotion involving questioning people called in from outside and those brought out from the detention cell, dinnertime arrived. Clearing everything away, the chief entrusted Tŏkch'ang and two others to the night duty officer and left, presumably for dinner. Tŏkch'ang also bought dinner inside and continued sitting on the bench that felt glued to his buttocks. The chief returned after eight o'clock. Seeing the chief's face brought Tŏkch'ang some relief. However, after an hour of interrogation filled with shouting, the chief again told him to sit down and dealt with others. Close to midnight, the chief finally called him again, stating that as a special consideration, he wouldn't make him sleep there but was letting him go, with the condition that he return tomorrow morning at nine.
In any case, being released was fortunate and welcome, but the thought that he would miss work again tomorrow made it seem like the job was lost for good.
"Ah! Whatever happens, happens!"
Tŏkch'ang clicked his tongue, walking slowly along the dark street after the last streetcar had gone.
When he returned home, just as he had suspected, the old woman (로파) was sleeping with Wŏnhŭi in the main room. One night, one day.<268> The fantasy of truly going to the Han River Railway Bridge right then flickered again. But just like yesterday, he trudged along the clattering, dark path, heading desolately home.
As he rounded the alley entrance, something flashed before Tŏkch'ang's eyes in the darkness. Tŏkch'ang's heart leaped, and he stopped dead, as if doubting his own eyes.
(Did I imagine it? Was it a trick of my eyes?)
Tŏkch'ang looked around, but the alley was silent, without any sign of movement.
As he started towards the gate again, something rustled behind the neighbor's outhouse. Tŏkch'ang, whose nerves were frayed and hypersensitive, nearly screamed "Ugh!" in fright, his legs trembling uncontrollably.
What appeared before him, even in the darkness, was clearly recognizable as Puni. It seemed so much like an illusion (헷개비), a trick of his eyes, that he wanted to ask, "Are you really Puni?" But seeing the trunk (츠렁크) in her hand confirmed she was real, looking like someone who had just arrived from a trip by train.
"Where have you been?"
It was Puni's voice.
"Huh?"
The man managed only one syllable in response, staring blankly, drained of energy. Thoughts of anger, resentment, reproach, or even joy – nothing came to his mind.
"Let's go inside."
Puni took the lead, pulling her husband along.
Seeing Puni, the old woman turned deathly pale, startled as if seeing someone rise from the dead (not out of joy).
"What happened?"
"You probably know better what happened, Auntie."
Puni's expression was frosty as autumn frost (秋霜 갓다). Intimidated by this, the old woman didn't dare follow them into the side room. Puni hugged her daughter who ran to her (내닷는 딸년), and suddenly burst into tears.
That night, Puni apologized to her husband without sleeping.
"It was all my fault. But please don't ask questions," she repeated, only saying that she had gone to the countryside to earn money but returned because things didn't work out.
"Why did you get the family register copy?"
"There's a reason for everything. You'll find out gradually."
Puni brushed this off vaguely as well.
To tell the truth, she had made the harsh decision to become eternally estranged from this husband. Guided by the old woman in the main room, she had set out with an old rich man from the countryside, planning <270> to disappear from Seoul forever. The family register copy was also something she had provided because the old rich man demanded to see it to know her background. However, the result was, quite unexpectedly, that she had almost fallen into a terrible pit for life. Defiling her body with that man was hardly the issue. It was fortunate enough that she managed to escape from the tiger's den (호혈), having almost been dragged into a countryside brothel (시골유곽).
From the next day, Puni sat at the M Cafe, pouring drinks for customers as if nothing had happened. And Tŏkch'ang once again stayed home, looking after the child during the day and putting cold leftover rice in the pot to heat up in the evening. Thanks to this, the M Cafe owner, having only received a 100-wŏn debt payment, fawned over Midori even more. Midori, too, felt reassured by the prospect of being able to borrow another 100 wŏn whenever needed. (The End) <271>

**Summary**

Following the disappearance of his wife Puni (who works as a cafe hostess named Midori), Kim Tŏkch'ang is detained by the forceful cafe owner over a debt Puni incurred, for which Tŏkch'ang unknowingly stood guarantor. He spends a night at the cafe and most of the next day detained and questioned by the police, missing the first day of a new job. Released late at night under orders to return, he arrives home to find Puni has unexpectedly returned with a trunk. She vaguely apologizes, admitting fault but refusing to explain her absence or why she obtained her family register, only saying a trip to the countryside for money failed. In reality, she had planned to leave him permanently with a rich older man but narrowly escaped being sold into a rural brothel. The story ends with Puni resuming work at the cafe and Tŏkch'ang returning to his unemployed state, looking after their child, their precarious situation seemingly reset but unresolved.

From a historical perspective, the story vividly portrays the economic desperation and social vulnerabilities prevalent in colonial Seoul. The dynamics between the cafe owner, the indebted hostess (Puni/Midori), her unemployed husband (Tŏkch'ang), and the police reflect the harsh realities faced by marginalized urban populations. The casual mention of "selling" women (파라넘긴), the powerlessness against debt collectors backed implicitly by authorities, and the near-trafficking into a rural brothel (시골유곽) highlight the precariousness of women's lives and the limited options for the impoverished under colonial rule.

失職(承前)
						廉想涉
作家點描

半島文壇에 잇서 長篇작가로써 第一指에 곱히는 이가 氏라 하리만치 氏는 長篇에 손대인 것이 만하서 이미 발표한 것만 헤여도 「사랑과 罪」 「해바라기」 「萬歲前」 「三代」 「二心」 「모란꼿필 때」등 7,8편을 넘는다. 또 단행본으로 나온 것도 이밧게 「牽牛花」 「반지」등이 잇다. 그의 作風은 어디까지든지 寫實主義에 일관되어잇다. 원래 京城 社稷洞 출생으로 普成中學을 中途退學, 京都 第二中學을 졸업. 동경에 이르러 慶應大學 文科에 재학하다가 中途退學, 그뒤 동아일보, 조선일보 학예부장을 역임, 현재는 某신문 정치부장으로 재직하는 한편 창작의 붓을 꾸준히 날니고 잇다. 춘추는 40.

M「카페-」주인은 덕창이가 곱은곱은히 대웅하기 때문에 그랫든지 손님들이 꼬이면 주인으로서 왁자짓걸하는 것이 안되어서 그랫든지 태도가 일변하야지며 올러가 종용히 이야기하자고 이층으로 끌고 올러갓다. 이충에는 좁아터진 속에 그래도 방이 셋이나 터진 입구자로부터 잇고 저편 구석 방문압헤는 남자의 구두가 한 켜레 노혀 있다.
「그래 지금 어듸 묵으슈?」
덕창이를 충게를 올러서는 마즌방에 드리밀 듯이 하고 주인은 문을 막고 서서 뭇는다. 덕창이는 머리속에 또 한번 딸년이 기다릴 집을 그려 보앗스나 먼저 한 말이 잇는지라 주저주저하다가
「인제 친구집으로 차저갈 모양얘요.」하고 어름어름해버렷다.
「엇잿든 술이나 더 자시구 잠간만 기대려수슈.」
주인은 네 주머니에 돈이 잇거든 먹을대로 먹어달라는 수작을 남기고 미처 무어라고 할 새도 업시 문작을 후닥닥닷치며 훌쩍 나려가버렷다.<260>
덕창이는 취하랴든 술이 번쩍 깨이며 십촉 등이 흐릿히 내려다보는 써늘하고 우중충한 다다미 방안에 우둑헌히 섯섯다. 한가운데는 다다미만치나 떄에 씨드른 상에 잔득 찬 재터리를 언저노헛고 저편구석에는 난쟁이 가튼 경대엽헤 녀자들이 모욕가는 제구를 담은 조고만 주석 대야가 두서너개 흐터저 잇고 때가 흘으는 치마저고리가 얼룩얼룩 걸려 잇는 것을 보니 가운데 노힌 상만 미러 노흐면 저의 집 업는 게집애들의 침실이 되는 모양이다.
그는 엇지 되잇든지 이편구석에 두세개 포개 노힌 방석을 끄려다노코 상압헤 쪽으리고 안젓다. 그러나 잠간 기다리라든 주인은 물론이요 이 치운 방에 화로불 한아 아니 갓다준다. 저편 남자의 구두가 노힌방에는 아까 녀자의 구두소리가 통통나며 음식을 날라 드려가는 긔척이더니, 그 속이 얼마나 깁흔지 쥐죽은 듯이 아모 소리조차 업다.
덕창이는 물론 술을 먹을 용긔도 아니 나고 흥도 아니 낫다.
래일부터 새로 취직된 회사에 출근을 하랴면 여긔 역시 외교원이니만치 외투 한아는 고물상에 가서 사 입드라도 들써야 하겟는데 그것 역시 다만 한곳 분이를 미덧든 것인데 이 지경이로구나. 그건 고사하고 아츰을 손수 끄려 먹고 출근을 할 수 잇나. 나간 뒤엔 그 전과 달라서 원희년을 엇더캐 두고 다닌단 말인가-.
인제는 다라난 안해 생각보다도 당장 래일부터 절박한 사정을 얼업시 안저 생각하다가 문득 엇더면 분이가 그 동안 집에 도라오지나 안핫슬까 하는 일의 희망이 또 머리에 떠올은다. 나를 혼을 내랴고 한때 발끈한 마음에 그런 편지를 써노코 나갓다가도 일이 여의치 안케될 뿐 아니라 참아 발길이 도라서지지를 안허서 다시 드러오지나 안핫슬까? 적어도 자식이 잇거던! 이런 공상을 해보는 것이다.
(엇잿든 일이 잘 안됏기에 여긔도 씨음을 깡그릿드리지 못하고잇스니까- 그러나 엇더면 지금이라도 여긔를 올지 모르지.)
이런 생각을 하면 괘씸하고 엇저고 하는 생각은 다 니저 버리고 곳 알에칭에서 분이의 목소리가 나는 듯십다.
덕창이는 삼십분을 안젓는지 한시간을 안젓는지 멀건히 쭉치고 안젓고 알에서도 가만히 내버려만 둔다. 그러나 일년 잇해, 쭉치고 드러 안젓느라고 기운이 줄고 비굴하게 된 그는 감히 큰소리를 내서 주인을 불러 올린다든지 쪼차 나려가서 얼는 귀정을 내고 가겟다고 버티어 볼 용긔가 나지를 못하얏다.
(나를 감금을 해두랴나? 어듸를 보기로 피천샐립이나 나오겟기에! 하며 덕창이는 혼자 코웃음을 치다가 <261>
(오죽해야 게집년까지 노첫슬까?...) 하고 자긔를 조소하듯이 또 한번 흥하고 한숨을 쉬엇다.
저편 방에서는 인제야 인긔척이 나며 남녀가 깔깔대며 우당퉁탕 나온다. 덕창이는 지금 나려가는 녀자를 불러서 주인을 불러 달라고 할까하는 생각도 업지 안핫나 눈살이 저절로 찝흐려지며 목소리가 냅뜨지를 못하얏다. 자긔도 그런 오입은 해본 찍걱이지만 당장 이 집에 분이가 잇섯고 분이 역시 지금 저 녀자가 탓스리라는 생각을 하고는 눈살이 안 찝흐려질 수가 업섯다.
(그러나 그 분이가 지금 이러케 된 바에야 그런 생각을 하면 무얼하니...)
하는 생각도 고처 해보앗다.
덕창이는 인제는 아모도 업는 이칭속에 혼자 떨고 안젓는 것이 기다리기에 지친 것보다도 우스깡스런 생각이 들어서 벌떡 니러섯다.
「어딀 가우?」
그러지 안하도 주인을 차저서 안으로 드러가는데, 책상 압헤 화로를 끼고 유산태평으로 안젓든 뚱뚱보가 볼멘소리를 한다. 정말 촌에서 갓잡아 온 줄로 알앗든지 죄인이나 닥다르듯 하는 것이 아모리 궁축한 덕창이로도 참을 수 업다.
「날 붓드러두면 무얼 한다는 거요. 래일 오리다.」
덕창이의 어긔는 숨엇든 본성이 도저 나온 듯이 별안간 뾰룽뾰룽하얏다. 주인은 저편의 태도가 돌변한데에 어이가 업는 듯이 한참 눈을 홉뜨고 치어다보다가
「아니, 래일은 와서 무얼하겟소? 누구를 떠보랴 다니는 수작이란 말요? 둘이 맛붓들고 도망질을 치랴건 애국에 도망질을 치든지, 여긔를 어듸루 알구 설불리 드러 섯단 말야? 두말말구 180원 내놔요. 그게 안되건 지금 나하구 미도리한테를 가든지...」
골김에 공연히 뾰룽뾰룽한 말을 한 것을 덕창이는 금시로 후회하얏다. 그러나 내친 거름을 금시로 옴추랏드리기는 어려웟다.
「글세 당신이나 내나 찻기는 일반안요?」

「허지만 자식이 잇다니까 설마 다라나기야 할까」
「나으리두 망녕의 말씀이십니다. 자식은 벌서 처치를 햇겟죠만, 설사 잇기를서니 집이니 못 떼가지고 다닙니까 산이니 못 떼가지고 갑니까」 하고 주인은 너털웃음을 첫다.
순사는 사실 무심코 한 말이 경관으로서 어리석은 말이나 한 것 가태서 덤덤히 안젓스나, 엇잿든 자긔 담임도 아닌데, 밤을 도아서 취조를 하기도 성이 가시고, 또 만원이 된 류치장에 애를 써 집어 너키도 귀치 안핫다. 그래서 엇잿든 가티 나가서 노치지 말고 래일 데리고 드러오라고 일럿다.
그럭저럭 열시가 넘어서 경찰서에서 나온 덕창이는 또 다시 카페-로 끄들려갓다. 하로밤만 가티 새고 래일 경찰서에 가서 귀정을 내야만 노하주겟다는 것이다. 덕창이는 사람을 무단히 감금을 하는 법이 잇느냐고 얼러도 보고, 에미아비 업시 혼자 잇는 어린 것이 가엽다고 애걸을 해보아야 주인은 막무가내하다.
문을 다들때엔 그래도 내노흐려니 하얏스나 그것도 헛다방이엇다. 마즈막으로 그러면 어린애를 위하야 가티 가서 함께 자자고까지 하야 보앗스나, 그 역시 도리질이다. 그랫다가 사실로 분이가 어린것까지  두고 도망한 형편을 목도하고는 덕창이를 달고 칠 구실을 일허버릴까 보아서 그러는 모양이다.
꼼작 수 업시 하로밤을 그 무지렁이 가튼 주인놈의 발치께서 새우잠을 자고 나니 무엇보다도 큰 걱정이 오늘 아홉시에 첫 출사를 할 회사이다. 이러케 된 바에야 그놈하고 다시 니ㅅ삿을 어우리기도 실코  독안에 든 쥐모양으로 허는 대로 내버려두고 거동이나 보기로 하얏다. 회사엔 못 다니게 되면 못 가는거요 될대로 되어라고 슬몃이 자포자긔가 되는 것이엇다. 전화나 걸고 못 가게 된 사유나 꾸며댈까 하얏스나 첫날부터 그런말을 더구나 전화로 한 대서야 <267>도리어 연문의 ㅅ짓이다.
덕창이는 새벽부터 니러나서 떨고 안젓서야 겨울해가 퍼지도록 우알에칭에서 어리친개 색기한아 꼼짝달삭하지 안코 주인은 얼굴까지 입울 뒤집어 쓰고 천정이 물러안즐만치 코를 더렁더렁 고더니, 열시를 바라보니까 부억에서 인제야 떼그럭 소리가 들려오고 주인자도 부시시 눈을 떠 본다. 이자도 덕창이의  존재는 눈에도 안 띄우는 듯이 시침이을 뚝 떼고 잇다.
아츰이랍시고 한술 뜬 것이 오정칠때, 그리고도 가진 거레를 다한 뒤에 그 뚱뚱보의 자-나서라는 명령이 나린 것이 거진 한시, 경찰서 취조실 밧게 안젓는 것이 세시간...짜른해에 전둥불이 드러올 무릅에야 겨우 풀려서(?) 취조실에 발을 드려노핫다. 별둘 부친 부장압헤 섯슬 때, 덕창이는 그래도 마음이 조금은 후련해지고 무러만 보면 뻔한 일이니까, 당장 나가게 되리라는 희망이 잇는 것이엇다. 그러나 부장은 밥분 모양이엇다. 주인의 설명만 다-듯고 나더니 주소, 성명, 직업 가튼 것만 뭇고는 저리가 안젓스라고 기다란 나무걸상을 가르치고는 「카페-」주인더러는 나가라고 한다. 이 부장과도 역시 안면이 잇는 주인은 절을 열번이나 하고 신신당부를 하고 물러나가 버렷다. 그자와 떠러진 것은 호랑이굴에서나 벗어난 듯 십히 시원도 하얏스나 여긔서 풀려나갈 가망이 아득한 것 갓다. 뒤에 밀린 사건을 처리하느라고 새 사건은 건듸리지 안는 것인지 덕창이는 본체만체하고 밧게서 불러드리는 것, 류치장에서 끄러낸 것을 취조하느라고 한바탕 법석을 하더니 저녁밥 때가 되니까, 다-거더치우고 부장은 덕창이와 그외 두 사람만 숙직에게 맛기고 나가 버린다. 저녁밥을 먹으랴 나가는 모양이다. 덕창이도 저녁을 그 속에서 사먹고 여전히 엉뎅이가 박이는 걸상에 거러 안젓스려니까 여덜시가 넘어서 부장이 들어온다. 부장의 얼굴을 보니 반가웟다. 그러나 한시간동안 호통을 하야가며 취조를 하고 나서는 또 안젓스라 하고 다른 사람을 가라댄다. 자정이 갓가웟슬 때, 그제 부장은 다시 불러 세우고 특별히 여긔서 재우지 안코 내보내는 것이니, 래일은 아츰 아홉시에 다시 출두하라는 것이다.
하여간 노혀 나오는 것만은 고맙고 반가웟스나 래일도 또 회사에는 못가고 마는구나 하는 생각을 하니, 일은 영영 틀리는 것만 가타얏다.
「에잇! 될대루 돼라!」
덕창이는 전차가 떠러진 어두운 길을 타박타박 걸으며 혀를 찻다.
집에 도라와 보니 원희란 년은 그러리라는 짐작은 잇섯지만 안방에서 로파가 데리고 잔다. 하로밤 하로다.<268> 이길로 정말 한강철교에를 나갈까하는 공상도 떠올러왓다. 그러나 어제와 가티 역시 대그럭거리는 컹컴한 길을 타박타박 거러 쓸쓸하게 집으로 향하얏다.
막동구를 쓱 돌처슬제, 넘넘한 속에서 덕창이의 눈압헤는 무엇인지 얼신하고 지나첫다. 덕창이는 가슴이 덜렁하며 자긔 눈을 의심하는 듯이 웃둑 섯다.
(헷보앗나? 내 눈의 착각인가?)
덕창이는 압뒤를 살펴 보앗스나 아모 긔척도 업시 골목안은 잠잠하다.
다시 대문을 향하고 오랴니까 엽집 뒤간 뒤에서 무엇이 부스럭한다. 잔득 신경이 피로하고 예민하야진  덕창이는 하마트면 으앗! 소리를 질을만치 경겁을 하며 두 다리가 부르를 떨렷다.
압헤 나타난 것은 컹컴한 속에서도 분명 분이인 것을 알 수 잇다. 네가 정녕 분이냐? 고 다저보고 십흘만치 그것은 헷개비로만 보이고 자긔 눈에 착각가타얏다. 그러나 손에 츠렁크를 든 것을 보니 사라온 것이요 어듸를 려행을 하고 방금 긔차에서 나린 행색이다.
「어딀 갓다 오슈?」
분이의 목소리다.
「응?」
남자는 한마듸 대꾸만 하고 열이 빠저 물그럼히 바라만 보고 섯다. 분한 생각도 미운 생각도 남으랠 생각도 반가운 생각도 아무것도 머리에 떠올으는 것은 업섯다.
「어서 드러가시죠.」
분이는 남편을 끌고 압장을 서 드러갓다.
분이를 본 로파는 죽엇든 사람이 사라온 것 가티 (반가워하는게 아니라) 몹시 놀래며 얼굴이 햇슥하야젓다.
「엇더케 된 셈야?」
「엇더케 된 셈은 아즈머니가 더 잘 알겟지.」
분이의 얼굴빗은 추상 갓다. 거긔에 저긔가 되어 로파는 감히 건넌방으로 따라 드러오지를 못하얏다. 분이는 내닷는 딸년을 끼고 안다가 울음이 왁하고 터젓다.
이날밤에 분이는 남편에게 잠을 자지 안코 사죄를 하얏다.
「모두 내가 잘못햇서요. 그대신 뭇지는 마라 주세요.」 이러케 뇌이면서 다만 한가지 돈을 벌러 시골 갓다가 여의치 못해서 도로 왓다는 말만은 하얏다.
「호적등본은 웨 냇서?」
「다- 까닭이 잇서요. 차차 아세요.」
분이는 이것도 이러케 어름어름 해버렷다.
실토로 말하면 이 남편은 영영 남되티 타는 모진 마음을 먹엇섯다. 안방 로파의 지시로 시골 늙은 부자를 따라 나서서 <270>서울에서는 영영 자최를 감추랴는 게획이엇다. 호적등본도 그 늙은 부자가 근지를 알아야 하겟스니 보여지라는 대로 내어다 바친 것이엇다. 그러나 결과는 천만이외에도 하마트면 몹슬 구렁에 일생을 빠칠번 하얏든 것이다. 그 놈에게 몸을 더럽힌 것쯤야 문제가 아니엇다. 시골유곽에 끌려 드러 갈번한 것을 호혈에서 모면하고 나온것만 다행하얏다.
이튼날부터 분이는 M카페-에서 아모 일 업섯든 듯이 손님의 술을 딸코 안젓섯다. 그리고 덕창이는 또다시 드러 안저서 낫이면 아이를 보고 저녁때가 되면 찬밥뎅이를 솟속에 너코 불을 집히고 하얏다. 그덕에 백원빗만 바든 M카페-주인은 미도리를 한칭 더 떠밧들엇다. 미도리도 백원빗을 언제든지 더 쓸 여유가 생긴 것이 든든하기도 하얏다. (끗) <271>
