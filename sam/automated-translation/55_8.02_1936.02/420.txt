Publication Date: 1936년 02월 01일
Article Title: 英語 又는 에쓰語로 飜譯하야 海外에 보내고 십흔 우리 作品 (Our Works We Wish to Translate into English or Esperanto and Send Overseas)
Author:
Article Type: 설문 (Survey)

英語 又는 에쓰語로 飜譯하야 海外에 보내고 십흔 우리 作品
(Our Works We Wish to Translate into English or Esperanto and Send Overseas)

Yang Chu-dong
Two or three historical works by Ch'unwŏn [Yi Kwang-su].
10 poems by Chŏng Chi-yong.

Chang Tŏk-cho
Before answering this question, as I haven't read many works by others habitually, it's difficult to list them one by one, but just recommending what remains in my memory:
Mo Yun-suk's Poetry Collection, Pak Hwa-sŏng's "Paekhwa" (White Flower).

Yu Chin-o
I answer your question.
If well chosen from the works of Mr. Yi Hyo-sŏk and Mr. Yi T'ae-jun, there should be some worthy of introduction abroad.

Im Hwa
I answer your question simply.
Regrettably, we do not yet possess literature to boast of to the world. However, last year, out of some necessity, after comparatively reading acclaimed works like Sholokhov's "Virgin Soil Upturned" (開拓된 處女地) from the Soviet Union (蘇聯) and Hirata Shōroku's "Torawareta Daichi" (囚れた大地 - Imprisoned Earth), alongside Minch'on [Yi Ki-yŏng]'s "Kohyang" (故鄕 - Hometown), I thought that even if only "Kohyang" were translated, it would not fall short of "Torawareta Daichi".

Ch'oe Tok-kyŏn<210>
Respectfully answering, well, I don't really know.

Kim Kwang-sŏp
Although our literature surely possesses some kind of uniqueness globally, it still seems somewhat problematic to proudly declare, staking the honor of a nation, "This is the literature we possess that can be sent to the world without being inferior!" However, if we must attach a "still," then among Yi Kwang-su's early works, Kim Tong-in's short stories, P'a-in's "Night on the Border" (國境의 밤), Chi-yong [Chŏng Chi-yong]'s poetry, Ch'i-jin [Yu Ch'i-jin]'s plays... etc. Rather than doing this, it seems appropriate to aim for carefully selected collections like a "Chosŏn Poetry Anthology" or a "Short Story Collection."

Min Pyŏng-hwi
Minch'on [Yi Ki-yŏng]'s "Kohyang" (故鄕). Im Hwa's "Oppa and the Brazier" (옵바와 火爐).

An Sŏk-chu
Even if there are some, I personally don't want to say. As an aside, speaking of older works, I would say something like the "Ch'unhyangjŏn" (春香傳) might be good.

Chŏng Nae-dong
Mr. Yi T'ae-jun's and Mr. Yi Mu-yŏng's short stories<211>
Mr. Yu Ch'i-jin's plays
Mr. Yu Ch'i-hwan's and Mr. Chŏng Chi-yong's poetry

Chang Hyŏk-chu
Minch'on [Yi Ki-yŏng]'s "Kohyang" (故鄕), Hyŏn Min's "Professor Chŏng and Instructor Kim" (丁敎授와 金講師), Yi T'ae-jun's "Saeksi" (색시 - Bride/Young Woman), Puk Myŏng's "Min Po's Timetable" (閔甫의 生活表), etc., and many others.

Yi Il
Searching our literary circles for works to translate into English or Esperanto (愛世不可讀語 - *likely a typo or unusual term for Esperanto*) to send to the world might be a good idea, but first, we need to cultivate scholars of English and Esperanto. Even more so, I wonder how many broad-minded and leisured readers there might be in famous world nations who would read works with Chosŏn as their theme. I would like to know who among foreigners have read recent Chosŏn-themed works like "Ch'odang" (草堂) and "Japan's Korea" (日本의 朝鮮).

Hong Hyo-min
Mr. Yi Ki-yŏng's "Kohyang" (故鄕), Mr. Im Hwa's poetry, Mr. Chang Hyŏk-chu's various novels, and the outstanding works of both Mr. Yu Chin-o and Mr. Yi Kwang-su.

Yi Mu-yŏng
Chŏng Chi-yong's Poetry Collection and others; even if carefully selected, there would surely be a bundle of masterpieces.

Yu Ch'i-jin
It seems that some of Mr. Chŏng Chi-yong's poetry and Mr. Yi Kwang-su's novels would not be embarrassing to send to the world literary stage.

Sŏ Hang-sŏk
About 4 or 5 poems from Chi-yong [Chŏng Chi-yong]'s Poetry Collection.

Kim T'ae-jun
Yi Kwang-su's "Mujŏng" (無情 - The Heartless)
Ch'oe Hak-song's "Hongyŏm" (紅焰 - Red Flames)
Yi Ki-yŏng's "Kohyang" (故鄕 - Hometown)

Kim An-sŏ
Enjoying things by oneself is not necessarily a bad thing.<212> However, wanting to show them to others is also human nature. From this standpoint, if you ask me what works I would choose, there are only a few short stories.
Works like Tong-in [Kim Tong-in]'s "Arasabŏdŭl" (아라사버들 - Russian Willow?), "Mad Flame Sonata" (狂焰소나타), "Famous Painting Lydia" (名畵 리듸아); Pinghŏ [Hyŏn Chin-gŏn]'s "Housemaster B and the Love Letter" (B舍監과 러브레터), and a few others besides these. It is, of course, my own subjective appreciation.

Sim Hun
If this person produces a masterpiece, please have your company do so [translate and send it overseas] once.

No Cha-yŏng
Answering your question.
I would like to translate Mr. Chu Yo-sŏp's short story "The Houseguest and My Mother" (사랑손님과 어머니) into English and introduce it to the world.

Ham Tae-hun
It is extremely awkward to pinpoint and write something like this.

Ch'ae Man-sik
If we consider it merely like going sightseeing at an exposition in Tokyo (東京) wearing a topknot, it wouldn't be impossible, but if that's not the meaning and we are selecting works of world-class standard, then there probably aren't any yet.

Chŏn Yŏng-t'aek
The works I see as worth translating into a foreign language are:
Kim Tong-in's "Kamja" (감자 - Potatoes)
Yi Kwang-su's "Hŭk" (흙 - Soil)

Yŏm Sang-sŏp
Well now, this question should rightly be raised after works have emerged that wouldn't be embarrassing to put out into the world, but anyway, I really don't know.<213>

**Summary**

This 1936 survey from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine polls prominent Korean literary figures on which contemporary Korean works they would like to see translated into English or Esperanto for an international audience. Responses vary significantly: some confidently suggest specific novels (notably Yi Ki-yŏng's "Kohyang" and works by Yi Kwang-su), short stories (by Kim Tong-in, Yi T'ae-jun), poetry (by Chŏng Chi-yong), and plays (by Yu Ch'i-jin). Others, like Im Hwa, Kim Kwang-sŏp, Ch'ae Man-sik, and Yŏm Sang-sŏp, express reservations, believing Korean literature has not yet reached a "world standard" or produced works they could proudly present internationally. Practical concerns about translation quality and the existence of an international readership for Korean-themed works are also raised (Yi Il). The survey reflects a nascent desire for international recognition alongside a candid self-assessment of Korean literature's standing during the colonial period. For historians, the article reveals the complex mix of national pride, cultural ambition, and self-doubt among Korean intellectuals under Japanese rule, as well as the specific works and authors considered significant at the time. The frequent mention of Yi Ki-yŏng's socialist realist novel "Kohyang" and the debate around "world standards" are particularly noteworthy.
