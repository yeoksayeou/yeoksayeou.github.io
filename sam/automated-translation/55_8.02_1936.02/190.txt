**背叛한 그 女子, 그 男子** (The Woman, The Man Who Betrayed)

**그 女子와 紫金山** (That Woman and Zijing Mountain)

崔承一 (Ch'oe Sŭngil)

15 years ago, Nan'gyŏng (南京) was a leisurely and quiet city, like something seen on a landscape folding screen (山水屏風). At that time, we began to gather here in large numbers. There was one person at Kŭmnŭng University (金陵大學) and two in middle school—our students numbered no more than 4 or 5, but that spring, the number suddenly increased, and nearly a hundred of our students gathered. Among them were over 10 female students. <94>
Spring! One night—a spring night in Nan'gyŏng—back then, there were no sounds like the wheels of streetcars or the sirens of automobiles; instead, it was a night filled with sounds like the bells tied to donkey necks or the wheels of horse carts. There was a discussion meeting (討論會) for our students at the Kŭmnŭng University auditorium. I forget the topic, but it was a joint male-female discussion meeting, and I also took part, joining either the affirmative (可便) or negative (否便) side, and stood on the platform for the first time in my life. The audience appeared to be about a hundred people, and it seemed almost all the female students were in attendance. Standing on the platform, I was startled. Why was I startled? It was because I saw a female student sitting in the very back row of the female students' section. She was the female student I had traveled with on the train from T'ienchin (天津) a few days earlier to Hsia-kuan (下關), and then crossed the Yangtzŭ River (楊子江) ferry with. At that time, she wore Chinese clothes, so I had assumed she was a Chinese student, but tonight, she was sitting there neatly dressed in Korean clothes. She looked younger and prettier than when she wore Chinese clothes.
The next day, somehow or other, I found out her name, her place of birth, and that she was a woman (女性) who had secretly crossed over from the Korean mainland.
From then on, at the age of twenty, I began to long for a woman day and night for the first time—it was her. I thought she must be two or three years older than me. And I had a feeling she might have been married once; sure enough, I later found out that she had married young, her husband had died, and right after his death, she started studying, attended a church-affiliated girls' school up to the fourth year, then began "that work" and became active as a representative figure among them. After learning this, I came to love her character even more.
She and I occasionally met on the street, but we always met at church every Sunday. I would resolve to go stand near her on purpose, but once there, I would invariably stand at a distance, gaze at her, and then leave—this continued week after week.
I made up my mind. I would write a letter—this took several weeks. I'd start writing, then hesitate, hesitate, <95> and finally, one early morning, I got up alone without my roommates knowing and wrote a simple letter. It was the first time I had ever written "My dearest so-and-so (아모개씨)—" to a woman. Well, I wrote it, but delivering it was another big worry. I thought of a plan (게책). The following Sunday, after the service ended and she took a rickshaw back to her place, I would also hail a rickshaw, chase her down, so to speak, get the rickshaws side-by-side, swiftly toss the letter to her, and quickly return home. What would she think? What if this became a rumor? I lost my appetite, couldn't eat properly, and just waited for a reply. The next Sunday came. Wondering what would happen, I went out to the street early, took a rickshaw, and slowly headed home, still anxious, occasionally looking back, but no one was following. However, coming out of an alley and reaching a three-way intersection where the rickshaw turned, her rickshaw appeared from somewhere, came up beside mine, and she too tossed a letter to me, then, without looking back, had her rickshaw speed away. I quickly tore open the letter. The content of her letter was, at first, to regard me as a younger brother; in the middle, she advised me like a friend; and finally, she said she was busy with her work and had no time for such thoughts. At the end, she mentioned that she would soon be leaving Nan'gyŏng, below this—Zijing Mountain (紫金山)—to embark on another long journey, and told me (you) to, by all means, make my name shine beneath that Zijing Mountain and then return.
I never saw her face-to-face again, and if I happened to go where she was, I avoided her as much as possible. Sure enough, after a few months, she left for America. <96> And somehow or other, I ended up crossing over to Japan. However, even now, after fifteen or sixteen years have passed, I still find myself thinking of her from time to time. -The End-

**깨여진 마돈나像** (Broken Madonna Statue)

安夕影 (An Sŏgyŏng)

It was when I was eighteen, a very long time ago, but for me, it was before I awakened to reason (理性), an event from childhood memories that makes me laugh to myself.
At that time, I was a young Christian.
I had no particular ambition (野慾), only pure feelings. Back then, I attended middle school, was engrossed in "drawing," and when the church was empty, I would sit at the harmonium and immerse myself in contemplation (瞑想). And on the wall of my room, there was a picture of Mary holding the baby Jesus. How beautiful and holy that Mary was.
She was a very gentle woman. She also had eyes that seemed to peer into anyone's heart. At that time, my heart always felt joyful, as if touched by her hand.
However, I amusingly realized that it was nothing more than a piece of paper with a picture on it. I realized it was akin to looking at some kind of uncorrupted corpse (屍體).
So, the shadow of disappointment (失望) began to cover my heart.
I realized she was merely a woman painted by the brush of a skilled artist, someone who couldn't possibly exist in this world.
So I took the "Mary" <97> down from the wall and put it in my desk drawer. Another time, I took it out of the desk drawer and burned it.
I don't know why I did that back then; it was simply something from my age of pure feelings, something that can only be understood by recalling that time when I was eighteen.

From then on, I wanted to find my living Madonna. During this time, my heart ached for no reason. May Flower Sunday—
As I entered the church, I spotted a woman dressed in white clothes, holding a bouquet of flowers, coming towards the church from the alley opposite. I stood there blankly, watched her enter the church, and then I too went into the church and sat in a corner of the men's section.
I was surprised.
She was a woman I always saw when I went to church, but why, at this moment, did I see her as so beautiful?
Perhaps the May morning sunlight made her, holding the bouquet, shine, or perhaps it was a change in my state of mind at that time, having been disappointed by the Mary statue.
The woman held the bouquet, carefully walked to the front of the platform, placed the bouquet on top of the pile of flowers brought by others, blushed, bowed her head, and went to her seat.

One day—the lawn in early summer made all sorts of fantasies bloom in my young heart.
This day was the outing (野遊會) for the Sunday school teachers.
As the men climbed the hill and gathered on the lawn, the women following behind also came up.
That woman was among them. She climbed the hill, set down her folded parasol on the lawn, and looked at me. Whether she was actually looking at me then, I believe my face must have turned red. And as she bowed her head slightly and then stood up to sit down, a smile (微笑) filled her face.
My heart fluttered as if she were revealing some unknowable secret of hers to me.
Everyone seemed to be enjoying themselves immensely that day, but for some reason, my heart was burning. And that woman, for some reason, seemed shy about everything, and I saw that she had some kind of uncontrollable feeling.
That night, I couldn't sleep a wink.
The next day, on the spot on my room wall where the picture of Mary had been, there was now a portrait (畵像) of that woman, drawn with my clumsy skill. <98> However, even though this portrait was a clumsily drawn picture, I knew it as a living picture, the image of a living person, or a living Madonna. And so, having been disappointed by Mary, a new, truly intense passion surged like a spring for this new Madonna.
Then, I coincidentally fell ill. According to the doctor, it was a fever—probably typhoid.
For a month, two months, I hovered near death but survived. How joyful I was to have survived this serious illness.
On the day I first went out, a Western-style envelope addressed to my older brother had fallen by the house gate. Since my brother wasn't home (안 게시니), I picked it up, sat at his desk, saw that the envelope was unsealed (염여지지 안은 것임을 보고), assumed it was some kind of invitation, and casually took out the contents.
I was shocked. It was a wedding invitation for that woman and some man.
I was disappointed again.
And so, I burned the second Madonna portrait I had drawn and put up on the wall.
Tears flowed from my eyes as I burned the Madonna portrait I had drawn and put up with my own hands.
Thinking about it now, that woman was three years older than me.
I realize now that even the heartaches of youth seem amusing when I think about them now. Can one say this is love, and this is the pain of lost love?

**꿈 가치 단녀간 그이 그림자** (That Person's Shadow Who Passed Like a Dream)

卜惠淑 (Pok Hyesuk)

First love!
Just hearing the words makes it seem like something from a world so distant and remote that it's hard for me to even think about now. My personality hasn't changed much from the past to the present, nor has there been a significant change in my life, so there haven't been any particularly more interesting events, and consequently, no heart-wrenching incidents to recall with tears.
However, if I try hard to think and recall, there might be one thing—but because my attitude, stemming from my youth and lack of experience, was so ridiculous, I haven't told anyone about it. <99> Now that over ten years have passed, writing about a hidden old story probably won't bring much shame (羞恥) to myself; rather, I believe it could become a lasting topic of conversation (話題) for those who hear it.

I think it was twelve years ago from now. It was after the T'owŏrhoe (土月會) troupe had passed, when I was with the Chosŏn Kŭguhoe (朝鮮劇友會), so back then, eating, sleeping, and acting were my daily routine. I couldn't experience much of the outside world, nor did I intentionally seek out a different world myself. In other words, it was a time when I lived under the very simple (單純) conviction that I would spend my life as an actress (演劇人).
Regardless of East or West, we receive many incoherent letters from people whose names we don't know. However, even if not just one, but a hundred letters arrive, we can only be mentally grateful for their goodwill; we can't reply to even one. We know well that it's regrettable to simply accept others' goodwill (好意) on credit, but there are so many letters that we simply don't have the time to reply to them all. Even if we had secretaries (秘書) like famous actors abroad to handle everything, it would still be difficult. For us, whose public and private lives are not affluent (豐足), where would we find the time when we are constantly falling behind even with our own personal affairs? Not only that, even when there are occasionally letters that move us enough to want to reply, those very letters invariably lack an address (住所) or name (姓名). It's truly like sending something addressed 'care of Namdaemun Gate'; where could we send a reply? However, there was one thing back then that caught my attention (注目) and interest (興味). Among the many letters arriving daily, the letters from someone signed 'C' were not on the usual picture postcards or envelopes but written on plain white double envelopes and standard letter paper. The refinement (洗鍊) of the writing style (文章) and the gem-like (珠玉) brushstrokes (筆致) held an unknown charm (魅力) that made me read them two, three times over. Thus, as days turned into months, I, who used to reply maybe once every three or four times, started replying daily—and since mere letters couldn't possibly convey the feelings in my heart, we agreed to meet. You can well imagine the state of my heart (心情) during that time. He was, in today's terms, a modern type; his face was handsome enough to stand out, perhaps because my heart was so completely captivated. Another thing was that he had no wife and no money—though not the sort of person who would starve or go naked <100> if he did get a wife. All these things captured my heart again and again, and like a dream, one year, two years flew by, and the third year arrived. I was scheduled to go on a regional tour (地方巡廻) with a certain theater troupe. Though I didn't want to leave his side even for a moment, what could I do? Although I had occasionally gone away for a month or two before, the feelings upon leaving and reuniting were always beyond description (形言). That particular time, my heart felt as if I were embarking on a journey from which I might never return; I was especially anxious. However, since I had dedicated my body to the theater and my heart to him, the distance or duration (時日) wasn't the issue. I believed that as long as our hearts were true (眞實) to each other, that was enough. We had discussed things, even promised each other an eternal future, so I set off on the lingering journey, looking back two, three times, thinking about what would follow. Having written this much, you can probably guess what happened before this, and also anticipate what was to come.
To put it more simply, after wandering here and there for nearly half a year, when I finally returned to Seoul, he was no longer my 'so-and-so'. Furthermore, he was to become the father of a child in a few months. There must have been a path (經路) that led him to become like that. Of course, at first, trying to deceive me, he pretended to show unchanging love and affection, but how long can one deceive (잇슴니가)? His confession-like words to me were, "It's because I truly (眞實노) loved you that things turned out this way. After you left for the countryside, I was so heartbroken and lonely that I started carelessly hanging out with friends (친구에 얼녀 놀나 단이다가) and made a mistake." This was just an excuse to avoid my resentment. I don't want to say more about what followed. I can't even describe my state of mind (心情) at that time. I just endlessly resented 'myself' for having rashly given my heart away, and it was because I felt disillusioned with 'myself'. At that time, I couldn't figure out what to do (엇재야 조흘지), couldn't get my bearings, and started wandering the streets like someone sleepwalking (夢遊病). At times, visions (幻影) of suicides (自殺者), commonly seen, even came to mind. It might sound like boasting, but if I had been a weaker girl back then, it's questionable whether I would be holding this pen now. Although I, who used to act carelessly (덩벙대든) without considering the consequences (압뒤일), was not without the self-control (自制力) that a person can possess, I was too young (어렷섯기) to sort out the order of my life's program (푸로그람) that had been <101> turned upside down (뒤박귀게 된). Because of that, for a time afterward, I even tried living a life of utter abandon (放縱生活), or sometimes imitated towards other men what I had received (바든 고대로) from him.
However, now I neither resent him nor deliberately dwell on the past. I simply think that on the path a person walks, there will be stones to stumble over, sunken places (움푹), dry spots, and muddy ground (잇스리라구만). It's a phrase used too often (넘어 만히 쓰는 말이라), so it feels awkward to write, but I'll write it anyway.

『Life is short, Art is long』 (人生은 짧고 藝術은 길다)

That's right. Pok Hyesuk as a person (人生으로서의 卜惠淑) is not that long. Consequently, Pok Hyesuk in her private life (私生活노서의 卜惠淑) is not such a big issue either. Unpleasant memories (不快한 긔억) of the past fade away (무더짐니다) on their own (제절노) if you forget them (이즈면). However, I, standing on the front line of the art movement (藝術運動), have realized the truth that my artistic actions (藝術行動 如何에 잇서서) will remain forever, and I will advance with the conviction to do my utmost (최선을 다하리라는 신념으로) on this path until the very end (끗까지 끗까지).
-(The End)-

**차디찬 心鏡** (A Cold Heart's Mirror / Cold State of Mind)

李軒求 (Yi Hŏn'gu)

For me, who has lived like a weak greenhouse plant (溫室적 弱生) in a forbidden garden (禁斷의 花園), how could there be the thick dream (지튼 꿈) of azaleas enveloped in spring mist? Though the footsteps of a simple maiden (素朴한 處女), wrapped in a deep blue skirt (靑裳), are faintly heard from across the street in the gently seeping evening mist, where did I ever have the courage to look directly at that face, those bright eyes (明眸), and that profound fragrant smile (香笑)? Thus, day after day, evening after evening, while drawing worlds as radiant as several rainbows in my mind, I only loved the inner landscape of my narrow chest. I wanted to hide deep within it, and I wanted to fall into a very cozy sleep. Then dreams would come too. The tickling sensation (간지러움도) of the lapping sea waves would surely be heard.

I have no sister. No older brother, no younger sibling either. Thus, I had no older sister who should have been the very first <102> to receive my first love. The fence of the neighboring house (동니집), the village fence—even if one placed a decent ladder (새다리(梯)) against it, it couldn't be crossed, and even the ladder of the heart, some hooked hand (갈퀴진 손) pressed down completely (짓눌너 바렷다), making it impossible to move (꼼작못하게).

Goethe—Byron—Rousseau—these young troublemakers (不作亂군) skillfully stirred up (도처주엇다) the budding thoughts of loving stars and violets. But somehow, that budding thought in this land is not sweet and fragrant (긔롭지 못 하다). It's not the state of mind (心境) of a singing nightingale, but rather like the weeping of a mute. Could there possibly be a pure young nature (淸素한 어린 性) in a land that matures early? Flowers don't get to bloom (퍼여보지 못 하고), yet they bear fruit (열매는 맺는다). After bearing fruit (매즌 뒤에), they try to bloom (피여볼야고 한다). It's a tearful tragedy (悲劇).

Spring is hazy (아즐타). On the frozen mirror of my heart (어러부튼 마음의 窓鏡), the pale (핼쓱한), smileless face (우슴없는 얼골) of a woman is reflected (비친다). Is she a virgin (童貞女)? Ave Maria? The eternally young memory has already passed several years (멧해를 지내갓다). But why (웨) has her expression (表情) changed like that (저러틋)? Are you angry (성낫는가)? What are you trying to complain about (하소하려는가)? Tightly shut lips (다친 입술)—those lips that created the enchantment of all past beauty—tremble faintly as they try to swallow the hot tears rolling down (굴너떠러지는). But the frozen mirror of the heart (얼엇든 마음의 窓鏡) shows no sign of melting again (녹을 듯 싶지도 안타). If your round fist were to beat (뚜들기는 때) this mirror window until it bled, the fortress walls of memory would shatter into pieces with a sharp, piercing sound. There, your old dreams would remain meaninglessly (하잘 것 업시) as a handful of black earth. But aren't you trying to search again (차저 보랴고 하지 안는가) for something newly sprouting (움트는) within it? <103>

**Summary**

This collection presents four poignant memoirs from 1936, each reflecting on past experiences of love, admiration, and disillusionment, often intertwined with youthful idealism and subsequent heartbreak. Ch'oe Sŭngil recounts an unrequited love for an older, politically active woman he met while studying in Nanjing, highlighting the milieu of Korean students abroad. An Sŏgyŏng describes his adolescent infatuation, transferring his idealized image of the Virgin Mary onto a young woman from his church, only to face disappointment when she marries someone else. Pok Hyesuk, an actress, shares a painful memory of betrayal by a lover who married another while she was away on tour, leading to a period of despair before she rededicated herself to her art. Yi Hŏn'gu uses metaphorical language to express a sense of repressed emotion and stunted development in love, possibly reflecting societal constraints, ending on a note of lingering, perhaps unresolvable, past pain. From a historical perspective, these memoirs offer glimpses into the personal lives and emotional landscapes of Koreans during the colonial period. They reveal the experiences of educated youth (studying abroad in China, engaging with Western concepts like Christianity and literature), the challenges faced by women pursuing non-traditional paths (like Pok Hyesuk in theater or Ch'oe Sŭngil's love interest involved in 'that work,' likely related to independence activities), and the personal costs of societal expectations and individual betrayals within that specific historical context. The blend of modern aspirations (education, art, travel) and enduring emotional themes (love, loss, betrayal) provides valuable insight into the complexities of colonial modernity in Korea.

내가 열여덟살 때 너무도 오랜 일이였으나 나로서는 리성(理性)에 눈뜨기전 일이라 어렸슬 때 추억의 하나로 스사로 웃게하는 일이였다.

아무러한 야욕(野慾)이 없섰고 다만 순정이 있섰슬 뿐 나는 그 때에 중학을 다니면서 「그림」에 열중하엿섯고 교회당에 사람이 없을 때면 풍금앞에 안저 명상(瞑想)에 파뭇치고 하였다. 그리고 나의 방 벽에는 어린엽수를 안은 마리아의 그림쪽이 부터있었다. 얼마나 그 마리아는 아름답고 성스러웟는지.
그는 퍽 어진 녀인이였다. 또한 그는 아모나 사람의 마음을 드려다 보는 듯한 눈을 가지고 있었다. 있 때 내 마음에는 그의 손길이 닷는 것가치 늘- 즐거웟섰다.
그러나 그것은 우습게도 그림 쪼각에 지나지 안는다는 것을 늑기엿다. 일종 썩지 안는 그 어느 시체(屍體)를 바라보는 것과 가흔 것임을 깨다럿다.
그래서 실망(失望)의 그림자가 나의 맘을 덥기 시작하였다.
그것은 확실히 능난한 화가의 붓으로 그렷슬 뿐인 이 세상에는 도모지 없을 녀인이엿슴을 깨다럿다.
그래서 나는 벽에서 그 「마리아」를 <97>떼여 책상 속에 집어너헛다. 또 어느 때는 그것을 책상 속에서 꺼내여 불살러 버렷다.
나는 그 때 무슨 까닭에 그랫섯는지 다만 내가 열여덟 살 때인 그 때가 아니고는 아러낼 수 업는 순정시대의 일이엿슬 뿐이다.

나는 그 때부터 나의 산-마돈나를 찻고 싶엇다. 이 때에는 공연히 내 마음이 괴로웟다. 오월 꽃주일-
