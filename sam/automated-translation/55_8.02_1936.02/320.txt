Where Palm Trees Grow Thick (A Letter Sent to Gangnam)
						Yŏsan

Tonight, I take up my brush to write this letter to you, Brother, who enjoys life in the land of passion and dreams, on the riverbank where palm trees sway in the gentle breeze (微風), in that southern country far across the distant sea!

My Dear Brother X!
It has already been a year since we parted (作別)! Please forgive me for not having replied (回答) despite receiving several letters from you during this time. You might say a single postcard (葉書) would suffice, but there are letters meant for postcards and others that are not. How could one write a letter frankly confessing (告白) one's living conditions (生活상태) in an affectionate (多情한) human relationship on a mere postcard? I try to avoid writing dull (無味乾燥한), mechanical (機械的인) letters asking about the weather and your well-being in a foreign land (氣體候一向萬康하오며 他國生活은 如何乎). Even if I write only once a year or once every two years! I trust you have received the representative letter signifying that I have passed the year safely (無事히). That is my handwriting (筆蹟) inquiring after your well-being (問安) and congratulating (祝賀) you on the New Year on the very first morning of New Year's Day (元旦).

Normally, I don't have the leisure to hold a brush to write letters.
To others, newspaper or magazine reporters might seem like they are playing around, but if you looked behind the scenes, you wouldn't know how busy and complex our minds are. This is the life of a magazine reporter: wandering frantically all over Seoul (서울) as if the city<148> were too small, wondering how to collect (取集) good material (才料), and then having to produce the manuscript (原稿) in the evening. Sometimes, when manuscripts pile up, I can't sleep at night and only catch a wink when the morning stars twinkle before heading to the company (社); this happens frequently (터문터문함니다). Also, I often go on regional business trips (地方出張). Like lightning (電光石火格), I might be wandering the Chongno intersection (죵로 녜거리를) in Seoul one evening, but the very next morning, my steps move onto the Pusan (釜山) pier in the southern port. Before word even gets around that I was there, I might be back again, heading to Wŏnsan port (元山港口), Sinŭiju (新義州), P'yŏngyang (平壤), Sinch'ŏn (信川). When traveling busily like this, communication with the head office (本社) is by telegram (電報), and there's often no time to write even a long letter—or even a single postcard.

During the day, I have to rush around attending to business affairs on the trip, and in the evening, visitors come calling. When colleagues from the head office, whom I rarely see, want to talk, it's already eleven o'clock. After going to a Chinese restaurant (中國료리집), there's no time (여유) left to write letters. In the morning, absorbed (몰두해서) in the work ahead, writing a morning letter doesn't even cross my mind. Because of this, I constantly receive letters from friends saying, "You are heartless (無情하다)! Has your heart changed?" I probably get 5 or 6 such letters from different places each month. Sometimes, there are truly difficult situations. There was an instance where someone, completely misunderstanding (誤解) me, sent a letter akin to a final notice out of some nervousness, and at that time, I urgently (火急이) substituted a telegram saying, "It's not like that," for a letter. I wish I didn't have to do this, but my body doesn't afford me the leisure to write letters. And sometimes, when I try to write, some unexpected obstacle (장애) arises. Last year was truly a difficult year for me, a busy year, and a year of travel. I found scenic spots (勝地) and obtained good writing material. In return, I suffered absurd hardship in July. I went out with two companions (동모) to a stream bank (개첫가) and was bitten by a mad dog (밋친 개), suffering for two months. Also, my enjoyable work, raising chickens (養鷄)—they laid such large (굴고), fine eggs (알)—but in April and May, a hateful weasel/marten (?) (밉살서러운 먹구리) appeared from under the roof (집웅) and broke and ate them all (깨터러 먹고). Furthermore, what causes me anxiety is the lodging problem (宿所問題). I dislike moving between inns (旅館) and boarding houses (下宿屋). However, my wish is to live alone in a quiet, single room in a thatched house (草家집), just reading books. I dislike both honor (名譽) and wealth (富貴); all I desire is not to struggle for food, to have a healthy body, to leisurely (閑假히) wander 찾아 visiting myriad waters and thousand mountains (萬水千山), seeing my fill, getting drunk to my heart's content, and living together within Great Nature (大自然). Especially when I put down the pen I was using on the manuscript on the fourth floor (四層樓上) in Chongno and look down at the Chongno intersection, I just want<149> to run away (도망하여) to the shores of Ch'ŏnji Lake (天池畔) on Paektu Mountain (白頭山), or to where the East Sea (東海) glitters by Kŭmgang Mountain (金剛)—to the white sandy beach (白沙장) of Myŏngsa Simni (明沙十里)—to Haeundae (海雲臺) where white seagulls (흰갈매기) flutter (훨훨) above the blue waves—anywhere my feet take me. But then, looking down in front of Hwasin Department Store (和信百貨店), seeing couples walking arm in arm, and such plausible scenes, I just think, "Ah, whatever! (에라!) Fine, that's the pleasure of a lifetime." However, my * is even that...
So it is. When I trudge back, exhausted from the whole day, to what I call home, are there, in O-kun's words, any slender-waisted (허리 잘숙한) XX ladies, or dark-eyed (눗깔 색카만) XXs? So it is. When I open the door, a cold wind blows in, and manuscript paper and books are scattered in this corner and that. The time I feel especially sentimental is on autumn nights when the crickets (귀두람이) chirp intensely (심히). My mind, wandering (희메는) in the silence, runs off to unknown lands. At such times, more than once or twice, I wish someone were beside me who would understand (알니려니) this heart of mine!

My Dear Brother X!
Truly, I wish I could meet a somewhat sensitive, affectionate (多情하고), and humane (人間美 잇는) landlady (主人아저마씨). Until about a month ago, the house was bustling, but the landlady was not only good-looking but also cooked well (食事도 잘 하고). She sympathized (同情하여) with me being alone and, when I was out, would come into my room and wash even my underwear (內衣) and socks—a grateful landlady. So, I always trusted and regarded her as my own older brother's wife (親兄수). I admired (감탄하엿소이다) that she was a woman from Hamgyŏng Province (咸鏡道), and among Hamgyŏng Province women, she worked so well, was faithful (忠實하며) to her husband (男便), managed over 10 boarders without once showing her husband a face of hardship, always regarding him like the heavens. That's why I'm telling you this now, Brother, but it's been a month since I left that peaceful house. Although the housewife (主婦) of this house was so good, because over 10 boarders came and went, it was too bustling. While looking for a quieter house, I happened upon a pastor's house (牧師宅)! Thinking there wouldn't be noisy children (아희들) and, being a pastor, his wife would also be good-hearted, I moved there, which is where I am now. But then, Brother... I truly didn't know it would be like this. Previously, when I went to a temple in Samch'ŏng-dong (三淸洞) seeking a quiet place, the head monk (主持), who was a Buddhist monk (道僧), only pursued profit (利) and wouldn't serve meals without advance payment (先金) for the food. This house is the same. I stayed for a month; for the first few days, the food (음식) looked edible (먹음직 하드니), but after 4 or 5 days, the meals became unchanging morning and evening, utterly inedible. I realized for the first time that she was a stingy (인색한) woman, no different from a usurer (고리대금업자), nagging even when my companion came for one meal or when I washed my face. It was all because I couldn't<150> pay the full meal cost (밥갑) on time; early one morning, she told me to pack my bundle (봇다리) and leave immediately. This is the family of a pastor pretending to be virtuous today. During my struggling student days in Tokyo (東京), even when I couldn't pay the meal cost for 3 months, the landlady, understanding my situation, never spoke such inhumane (몰인정한) words. It's sheer rudeness (막행패). So how could my mood possibly be good? It's all the chatter (지저구는구려) of a frivolous woman in these degenerate times, saying things proper and improper! Tsk! His wife, preaching from the pulpit (강도상)? Indeed, on the last day of the last lunar month (섯달 금음날), my pocket (주머니), perhaps combined with the Shōwa (昭和) 10 bonus (보나스), contained only one small paper bill (紙貨). With this, I could have bought 20 glasses of makgeolli (막껄니). Barely receiving a meager salary (박봉) each month, my expenses are: 17 Won for monthly food (食代), 5 Won for 2 sections of tram fare (電車費), about 3 Won for city tram fare (used for collecting news material), 5 Won for newspapers and books (書籍갑), 3 Won for socks, towels, soap, bath, and haircut (리발비), and Western clothes (洋服) on a monthly installment (月賦) of 35 Won. As it turns out, without around 50 Won a month, city life, especially the life of a reporter who also does outside work (外勤), is a difficult livelihood. Despite this, relatives in the countryside think that since I'm an important executive (간부) at a magazine company, I must be earning a lot of money and tell me to save (貯金) or ask for help. It's truly exasperating. If I had lots of money, if I had hundreds of millions of Won, I would spend it freely! But saving money while living as a reporter is, for eternity, a blatant lie. Unless money pours in from somewhere else, it's impossible.

My Dear Brother X!
This year's new plan (풀랜) is to see the ancient capital Kyŏngju (慶州), Ullŭng Island (鬱陵島) playing in the blue waves (蒼波) of the East Sea (東海), the female divers (海女) of Cheju Island (濟州島), and the customs (風俗) of Hamgyŏng Province (咸鏡道), and perhaps pick out one of those brave (씩씩하고) and robust (健實한) maidens (處女). I don't know if it will happen though.
Brother! It's too much joking, but this year, the Samch'ŏlli Company (三千里社) has set several big plans (게획), and I am now making all preparations (쥰비) to implement (實行) them. You must have already received the New Year's issue (新年號). As you can see, we intend to make Samch'ŏlli better every month. Especially Mr. P'ain's (巴人先生) intellect and direction (指揮) in gathering news material—you wouldn't know unless you worked under him. It's truly brilliant (긔발적이고) and skillful (교모함니다). Also, I intend to try to perfect the internal and external affairs (內外事) of the company. Brother, what I boast of first is the grand plan ahead: establishing an Esperanto language section (에쓰페란트語部) in Samch'ŏlli to send our Samch'ŏlli to the whole world (全 世界), deliver news (消息) from the whole world to the Peninsula (半島), and make the Samch'ŏlli Peninsula known to the world. Again, my request (부탁은) to you, Brother, is to let us know the news from the Philippine Islands (比島) at least twice a month. I'll say this in advance: I'll be busy this year too, so I won't be able to write letters often (자조), but please, don't say my heart has changed or that I'm heartless (無情하니). I bow (허리 굽혀) and ask only this... The end<151>

**Summary**

The author, Yŏsan, a journalist for the magazine *Samch'ŏlli*, writes a long-overdue letter to his friend ("Brother X") living abroad (likely the Philippines). He explains his silence by detailing his extremely busy life as a reporter in Seoul: constant travel, deadline pressures, lack of sleep, and financial struggles despite perceptions of his job. He recounts personal difficulties like being bitten by a rabid dog, losing his chickens, dealing with unpleasant landlords (contrasting a kind former landlady from Hamgyŏng Province with a stingy pastor's wife), and feelings of loneliness and yearning for nature or companionship. He concludes by outlining ambitious plans for *Samch'ŏlli*, including an Esperanto section to connect Korea globally, and requests news from his friend.

From a historical perspective, the article offers insights into the life of an urban intellectual/journalist in colonial Seoul during the mid-1930s. It reveals the hectic pace, financial precarity (50 Won monthly budget breakdown), and emotional toll of such a life. The mention of specific locations (Chongno, Pusan, Wŏnsan, Hwasin Dept Store, Samch'ŏng-dong), travel patterns across Korea, lodging issues, the reference to the Shōwa era, and the ambitious plan for an Esperanto section in *Samch'ŏlli* to engage internationally reflect the complex realities and aspirations within the colonial context. The contrast between the idealized Hamgyŏng woman and the greedy Seoul landlady might also hint at regional stereotypes or social commentary.
