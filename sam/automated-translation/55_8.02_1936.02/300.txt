**Body Text Translation:**
Visiting a Street Women's School, the Textile Women's School Where Delicate Hands Weave
Eight Hundred Women Operating New-Style Spinning Wheels

On the 15th, at 1 PM, braving the biting cold wind that felt like knife blades against my cheeks, I set out to visit the textile women's school. Arriving at the office of Kyŏngsŏng Textile Company (경성방직회사), a large three-million-won company which also serves as this school's office, I unexpectedly encountered Mr. Ri Ŭn-sŏng (李殷成), a former teacher at the Women's Commercial School, whom I hadn't seen in a long time. He greeted me warmly. When I explained the purpose of my visit, he guided me to the person in charge, Mr. Ri Sang-u (李常雨). After exchanging greetings, I heard a brief explanation from him and then headed straight to the textile company's factory in Yŏngdŭngp'o (永登浦). While everything else seemed lethargic, as if unable to bear the cold, middle school students skating on the vast ice sheet of the Han River (漢江) were vigorously swinging their arms and legs like lightning, their spirits soaring (意氣衝天) like soldiers undergoing cold endurance training (耐寒練習). It was endlessly exhilarating. Being unskilled (無才通) and unfamiliar with skating, the thought arose like fire within me that I wanted to tumble around on that ice sheet with them, even if it meant breaking my backside or cracking my skull. However, my duties did not permit it, so I suppressed all such thoughts and boarded a bus heading to Yŏngdŭngp'o. The incessant cold wind, as if intending to torment people, skimmed across the river surface and struck the bus, making a whistling sound as it passed. While gazing at the mountain ridges meeting this winter and looking out over the river, I arrived before the textile factory before I knew it. Entering through the main gate (正門), I saw that the wide yard was neatly paved with gravel, and pine trees were planted here and there. It evoked a cozy atmosphere (氣魂), much like arriving at some school building (敎堂).<140> I asked the guard (守衛) where to go for the office, and he pointed, saying, "Please enter through the door you see right in front of you." I presented my business card at the reception (受付) and requested an interview with the administrative staff. Soon, a boy guided me to the reception room. Although I had anticipated that everything would be well-equipped, as is typical of endeavors by wealthy individuals, everything exceeded my expectations in its splendor. The reception room alone was spacious and clean enough for several dozen people to gather for a meeting. As I sat drinking the tea served by the boy, Mr. Hyŏn Tŭg-yŏng (玄得榮) entered. We briefly exchanged greetings, and when I stated the purpose of my visit, he began to explain and guided me to the factory.

800 Women in Uniforms and Caps

A distinctive feature of this factory, compared to others, is the large number of women from rural areas and the mix of ages ranging from 12 or 13 to 30 years old. Their number was also large, at 800. We stepped into the preparatory work (準備作業) room. All sorts of machines, steaming profusely, were placed here and there. Dressed lightly in pitch-black work clothes topped with pure white caps, people were busily engaged in their respective tasks, their hands moving too fast for the eye to follow: unwinding thread, winding thread, warping threads and sizing them, drawing threads through the heddles and reeds. Everyone was diligently doing their assigned job. Turning again, we crossed the threshold into the weaving room (織布室). Here, in a room so vast that the far end was barely visible, countless looms—impossible to know how many hundreds<141>—were packed in, weaving cloth. However, one particularly noteworthy thing here was the deafening roar of the clashing machinery, enough to make one lose one's senses. Hearing it for the first time, my ear canals felt completely blocked, and a tingling sensation ran through the bones of my entire body. The sharp, cracking sounds, like artillery fire, suddenly struck my chest like stones, jolting me. It shook my head this way and that, leaving no room for thought. If even a sturdy man's heart pounds at the tumultuous machine noise that sounds like the heavens collapsing and the earth caving in, how much more terrifying must it have been for these delicate women! Glancing furtively at me, they seemed pale, as if the flesh had withered from their faces and the blood had dried in their chests due to the dreadful roar of the machines. Unable to bear standing there any longer listening to that extremely loud noise, I turned towards the finishing (仕上) room. Here, the finished cloth was piled up and then loaded onto carts. At this point, something occurred to me. The lines "Yesterday I went to the capital walls (昨日到城廓), / Came back with my kerchief soaked in tears (歸來淚滿巾). / Those clad all over in patterned silks (偏身綺羅者), / Are not the ones who raise the silkworms (不是養蠶人)" came to mind. Though they weave 3,000 tan (unit of cloth length) of fine fabric daily, they cannot freely wear it themselves. What a gut-wrenching and heartbreaking situation this must be! The feelings (心境) of the person who returned home squeezing tears into a handkerchief after seeing someone in the market wearing silk clothes they themselves had woven, and the feelings of these women today, blankly watching the cloth they wove with their own hands being loaded onto carts and sent away, must both be filled with the same inconsolable sorrow. Gazing deeply at them, who seemed as pitiful and forlorn as frost-nipped flower petals, I headed towards the dining hall.

A Grand Dining Hall and Recreation Room Capable of Accommodating Over a Thousand People

This dining hall is exceptionally large, rarely seen elsewhere. The walls were plastered with theater (演劇) posters. When I asked the guide what these were, he explained that the entire factory closes from the first to the fifth day of the Lunar New Year, and during that time, the workers put on plays, which these posters announced. Not only that, but there are many other games played during the Lunar New Year holidays. He mentioned they play Yutnori (a traditional board game) with prizes, and indeed, prizes marked with first and second place scores were piled up in the dormitory supervisor's office, catching my eye. For recreation, there are occasional慰安映畵會 (comfort film screenings), for which a movie projector has been purchased, and ping-pong tables and other sports equipment are also provided. Of course, plays and film screenings are held in this dining hall, he said. Listening to the general explanation, we arrived at a small<142> lecture hall located in a corner of the dormitory, which also served as a study room and sermon hall. In this hall, he explained, staff members gather for an hour each evening for instruction in various subjects like ethics and language. Then, on Sundays, a Christian pastor comes to give sermons. Given that the site covers 20,000 p'yŏng (approx. 66,000 sq meters), the extensive facilities were impressive enough to stand comparison anywhere. While it's likely that factories run by other people in other regions might be even more splendid, considering the region and population ratio, an institution of this scale in this land couldn't help but be considered larger than institutions run by other people in other lands.

A Dormitory that Rivals Hotels and Its Inhabitants

It is a two-story Western-style building made of brick. There are several hundred rooms, each designed to accommodate about 10 people. The current number of residents alone is said to be 500. This dormitory (寄宿舍), neatly arranged within a walled enclosure that prevents easy entry or exit even for birds, is truly more splendid than any hotel. Stepping into the entrance hall, I saw people on break, some deeply asleep in the embrace of dreams (三昧夢) behind drawn curtains (카-뎅 - likely 'curtains'), while others were rustling about doing something. Peeking at us coming and going through the gaps in the doors, they seemed to have an expression deeply longing for the outside world (밧갓). More than that, as the holiday season approached, sounds of humming drifted faintly through the door cracks, as if homesickness was surfacing in their minds. Indeed, it must have been more than once or twice that they couldn't suppress the boiling resentment or the sorrow welling up inside them. When they compared their situation—spending their girlhood or youth rotting away in this factory due to unavoidable circumstances—with watching others adorned in fancy clothes, displaying all sorts of charms and coquetry to melt men's hearts while living idly, how many secret sighs must they have heaved? How many times must their tears have fallen heedlessly, wetting their sleeves and then drying? The more the machines turn, the more their fragrant (香氣) years wither and rust away. Every time they heard the waves of the Han River flowing noisily, every time the stars in the sky cast their laughter upon the earth (大地), they must have been unable to control the uncontrollable fluttering of their hearts. Gazing at the firmly shut dormitory doors, I returned to the reception room.

Monthly Salary: Maximum 30 Won, Minimum 6 Won

Drinking the tea brought by the boy and lighting a cigarette, I began a brief conversation with the factory manager.
"Me": What are the working hours generally like?
"Him": They come to work at 7 in the morning and finish at 6:30 in the evening.
"Me": What about the monthly salary (月給)?<143>
"Him": The maximum is 30 Won (圓) and the minimum is 6 Won. This minimum wage position involves two months of training as a trainee (養成生).
"Me": Then after training, they presumably receive a decent wage?
"Him": That's right. (Omitted section)
"Me": The facilities I just saw, like the bathhouse and recreation area (어락장 - likely 오락장, recreation hall), are ultimately provided free of charge to the workers, I suppose?
"Him": That's right. Things like night classes (야학), film screenings (映畵會), and bringing in a pastor for sermons are all for the workers' benefit.
"Me": What are the holidays generally like?
"Him": Sundays (日曜日), of course, are off, plus one day for Ch'usŏk (秋夕), five days for the Lunar New Year, and the first day of the solar calendar year.
"Me": If any romantic relationships develop, how are they handled?
"Him": If it involves a married person acting illicitly (非合法的), it's stopped. Otherwise, if both parties are unmarried, we help facilitate their marriage.
"Me": Well, thank you for your time. I'll visit again for the rest. That's all for today. After offering these words of parting, I put on my overcoat and stood up. He saw me off to the entrance hall. As soon as I stepped out of the textile women's school gate, the sound of an approaching bus made me quicken my pace hastily.

Watching the 800 women working diligently at this textile company today brought something to mind. Even just within Seoul city, there are countless people living in luxury and idleness, as well as those whose fortunes are poor, unable to find work and just consuming energy. Setting others aside, if we compare this to the fact that the workers in this factory weave 3,000 tan of cloth per day, even if we conservatively estimate the number of idle people at ten thousand, if these ten thousand people were to work, they could weave over 30,000 tan per day. What a staggering contrast this is! I implore you, wealthy sirs, who pile up money like mountains but don't know how to use it! Please, from now on, do not let your vast sums lie idle; use them meaningfully.<144>

**Summary**

The article recounts a reporter's visit to the Kyŏngsŏng Textile Company's factory and associated women's school/dormitory in Yŏngdŭngp'o in 1936. The reporter describes the large scale of the operation (800 female workers, large facilities), the harsh working conditions (long hours, deafening noise, low pay starting at 6 won/month), and the controlled lives of the young women, many from rural areas, living in a guarded dormitory. Despite company-provided amenities like recreation, classes, and holiday events, the reporter senses the workers' underlying sorrow, lost youth, and feelings of injustice, contrasting their labor with the silk clothes worn by others and the idleness of the wealthy. The article ends with an appeal to the rich to invest their money productively, highlighting the potential economic contribution of unemployed people if given work. From a colonial history perspective, the article offers a glimpse into early Korean industrialization under Japanese rule, particularly the role of female labor in textile manufacturing. It highlights the paternalistic yet exploitative nature of factory work, the stark social inequalities, the regimented lives of female workers drawn from the countryside, and the reporter's empathetic yet somewhat paternalistic observations on their plight and potential economic value. Historians might find the detailed description of factory conditions, wages (6-30 Won), working hours (7 AM - 6:30 PM), dormitory life, company welfare provisions (recreation, education), and the explicit contrast drawn between laborers and the idle rich valuable for understanding labor relations, gender dynamics, and social commentary during this period.
