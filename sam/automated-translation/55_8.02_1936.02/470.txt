잡지명	삼천리 제8권 제2호
발행일	1936년 02월 01일
기사제목	獸 (Beast)
필자	柳致眞
기사형태	희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

柳致眞 (Yu Ch'ijin)
Having previously published the play 『버드나무선 洞里의 風景』 (Scenery of Willow Tree Village), establishing himself as a notable playwright in our literary world, he has since studied the Tokyo theater scene and continued to steadily publish fine works such as 「土幕」 (Earthen Hut) and 「소」 (Cow). His hometown is T'ongyŏng, South Kyŏngsang Province. He previously went to Tokyo, attended Hōzan Middle School, and graduated from Rikkyō University's English Literature Department in Shōwa 6 (1931). He is currently a member of the Drama Arts Research Society (劇藝術硏究會). Age 32.

Early Spring.
A city in China.

Characters

Taesŏn (大善) (Older Brother, 20s)
Sosŏn (小善) (Younger Brother, 15-16)
Their Grandfather
Their Mother (Widow)
Their Maternal Uncle
Hongmae's Father (Neighbor)
Taesŏn's friends A, B
Unknown Man
Others passing by on the street <242>

Stage

Taesŏn's house, facing a Chinese street. A poor household. On the *k'ang* (캉, a raised heated floor used by Chinese for sleeping), Sosŏn is sitting up, being fed porridge by his Grandfather. Sosŏn is still pale, showing signs of having suffered greatly from illness. In one corner of the room is a tall chest. Beside it, a wooden horse (木馬). There, the grandfather does piecework, pasting labels onto matchboxes (內職). The pasted matchboxes are spread out in corners of the room to dry. Spread out like that under the dim electric light, they look strangely like dotted red and blue flowers.
Taesŏn is hiding in a corner, dividing bundles of paper with his friends A and B. In another corner is the Maternal Uncle's living space. From the night street outside comes the noisy clamor of vendors typical of a Chinese street. Among them, the sound of a beggar's *huqin* (胡琴) sounds quite exotic.
This street scene is barely visible through the entrance door of Taesŏn's house.

Taesŏn. (Lowering his voice as he distributes the paper bundles to A) Hey, you take charge of this side, across the bridge. Then I'll take the north side.
A. *T'ing hao!* (뜅하우! - Okay!)
Taesŏn. (To B) And you take the other side of the intersection, got it?
B. *T'ing hao!*
Taesŏn. Damn it! Hehehe... looks like we've divided the world into three. Right, Grandfather, keep this medicine and give it to Sosŏn. It's supposed to be taken after meals. And please hide these paper bundles here for a bit. (Hides the extra papers.)
(Taesŏn, A, and B stand up. Just then, the siren of a motorcycle (오토바이) is heard in the distance. Tension on the street. The beggar's *huqin* also falls silent.)
Taesŏn. (Exchanging tense looks) What's going on?
(The entrance door opens! Taesŏn, A, and B quickly take out the bundles they were dividing from their clothes, hide them swiftly in a corner, and pretend nothing happened. Taesŏn's Mother appears, carrying a bundle. They relax only after seeing it's her.)
A. Oh my goodness!
B. I thought it was someone else!
Taesŏn. (Sharply) Mother. Cough or something before coming in! (Takes the hidden paper bundles back into his clothes.)
Mother. What is that, you brats? Those paper bundles?
Taesŏn. It's none of your business, Mother!
Mother. (Throws down her bundle and raises a piece of wood to hit Taesŏn) Ech! <243> You damn beggars! Because of you lot, everyone else is going to die too.
Taesŏn. Shh! Why are you shouting!
Mother. How can I not shout? You damn brat! If you're going to pull stunts like this, just kill off this family first. You brat, why are you blocking others' way of living!
Taesŏn. (Covering Mother's mouth) Mother! Anyone passing by on the street will hear. Even if the house is like paper.
Grandfather. (Seeing Mother's bundle) Why, daughter-in-law. Why did you bring that back? Didn't you leave it at the company and collect the money--
Mother. The company gates were slammed shut! How could I deliver it! (Pointing at Taesŏn) It seems these brats are still messing with that match company.
Grandfather. Is that true, boys? Are you still doing that with the match company?
Taesŏn. The company is being too unreasonable. We tried to settle it amicably on good terms, but they wouldn't listen. What can we do? This time, we'll fight them to the very end.
Mother. Hmph! You fight day and night, but it's always you brats who get hurt. Has the company ever surrendered?
Taesŏn. This time, it looks quite promising. Just wait and see. Something will be settled tonight. (To A and B) Let's go quickly.
(Taesŏn, A, B exit)
Mother. (Watching them leave) You brats! (To Grandfather) Why are these printing workers meddling in someone else's match company affairs? Thanks to that company, we've been getting through these hard times. Now that even that company has closed its doors, what are we going to do? Hmph! Thanks to those brats, now we can't even paste matchbox labels anymore.
Grandfather. (Smacking his lips) Did they close the doors completely?
Mother. Yes. Policemen were blocking the way, you couldn't get near the company at all.
Grandfather. This is serious trouble!
Mother. Starting tomorrow, we'll have no choice but to starve. (Untying pasted matchboxes from the bundle she brought and setting them aside) Damn it! Even after pasting all these, there's no way to collect the wages (삭전).
Grandfather. (Feeding porridge) Oh dear, this is serious trouble...
Sosŏn. (Refusing the porridge Grandfather is feeding him) ...I don't want any more.
Grandfather. You don't want it?
Sosŏn. (Nods his head.)
Mother. What are you trying to do? Not eating more? <244>
Grandfather. There was only half a bowl in here, and he can't even finish that.
Sosŏn. (Stares weakly into space) Lay me down.
Grandfather. (Lays Sosŏn down.)
(Taesŏn's Maternal Uncle enters. He's returning from selling wire grills (석쇠) as a peddler (行商). He's a country farmer who hasn't been in the city long. Taciturn personality.)

Mother. Brother. Why are you so late today?
Grandfather. Hey, how were things today? Did you sell much?
Uncle. Damn it all! If I just had land to farm, I'd carry a night soil bucket right now! A farmer should be farming, whatever anyone says.
Grandfather. That's right. A shaman only gets inspired when holding the drumsticks.
Mother. (Serving him dinner) Do you think you can become like the merchants here in just a day or two? As you do business, you'll gradually learn the tricks.
Uncle. (Showing a fish) Here, I bought this. Grill it for Sosŏn. (Starts eating dinner)
Mother. (Pleased) Oh, how much did you pay?
Grandfather. Hehehe... Tomorrow morning, we'll get to see our Sosŏn eat with appetite. Always having only soybean paste stew (된장찌게) like these days, wouldn't even a healthy person waste away? Hey boy, eat well when this is grilled for you.
Uncle. (Eating silently, beast-like (獸獸히))..... I asked Taesŏn, about a job for tomorrow? Anything involving digging or moving earth is fine.
Mother. That brat is so caught up in someone else's company affairs, he knows nothing about his own family's matters. That damned kid.
Uncle. (Sighs deeply as if realizing the gravity of the situation.)
Grandfather. (Looking at Sosŏn's face) Daughter-in-law. Look at this.
Mother. ?
Grandfather. (Lowering his voice) Doesn't his face look unusually pale? His eyes are sunken?
Mother. (Looks closely at the lying Sosŏn) Still, doesn't it seem like some color has returned?
Grandfather. That... consumption (페병) illness, right before death, it's always like that...
Mother. (Startled) What?
Grandfather. I think I heard something like that somewhere.
Mother. Oh my goodness! Then what should we do?
Grandfather. Don't be alarmed, ... Didn't Hongmae's father say he'd bring some herbal medicine?
Mother. I'll go to Hongmae's house and check.
Uncle. (Has been standing blankly beside the *k'ang*, watching Sosŏn <245>) I'll go check. I need to buy some wire (철) too.
(Uncle exits)
Grandfather. Right, let's try giving him the medicine. Taesŏn left it. (Gives medicine to Sosŏn.)
(Just then, Hongmae's Father bustles in. Around 40, very frivolous and flighty.)
Hongmae's Father. (Entering) Have you had dinner?
Mother. (Pleased to see him) Ah, come in, Hongmae's father.
Grandfather. Good timing. We just sent his uncle to your house.
Mother. What about the medicine?
Hongmae's Father. This is it.
Mother. (Looks at the medicine.)
Hongmae's Father. How is Sosŏn today? Any improvement?
Grandfather. Same as ever. Not doing well.
Mother. Is this really that medicine?
Hongmae's Father. I asked others, and they all said it's definitely the one.
Grandfather. Anyway, thank you for your trouble. Here, take the money. Four *chŏn* (스픈첸 - 四錢).
Hongmae's Father. (Takes the money and puts it away) Thank you. Neighbors are like cousins, we live by helping each other like this. Hehehe...
Mother. How should this be decocted? Should I break off the parts with leaves like this?
Hongmae's Father. That's right. Just gather the roots like that and decoct them like any herbal medicine... Today the weather was cold, so I couldn't dig up many, but try using these anyway. If it proves effective after giving it to him, I'll dig up more.
Mother. Of course, if it's effective, we wouldn't just leave it. (Hurries to decoct the medicine.)
Hongmae's Father. (Suddenly lowers his voice)... By the way, look here. Hasn't Taesŏn come back yet?
Mother. He went out again earlier with those friends of his.
Hongmae's Father. What exactly happened with that commotion (수드러기)?
Mother. What commotion?
Hongmae's Father. Don't you know about the commotion? The thing that happens occasionally... Ah, that thing where the young folks gather their pals instead of thinking about work and just stir things up (수드럭대는 것).
Grandfather. (Laughing loudly) Hahaha... That thing... (Finally remembering) Right, a *sŭdŭragi* (수드라기 - strike/dispute). Our Taesŏn did that.
Hongmae's Father. *Kisŭdŭrŏgi* or *sŭdŭragi*, it's all the same. Anyway, what happened with that?
Mother. (Angrily) I don't know. Because of that brat's nonsense, we're all sick with worry. (Pointing to the mountain of matchboxes) Look at that. We made all those, and still can't collect the money.
Hongmae's Father. Damn it. We're in big trouble too. After begging and pleading, we finally got our Hongmae a job at that company, and just a few months later, this commotion happens again.
Mother. Well, your Hongmae works at that company, so it can't be helped. But look at our Taesŏn. He doesn't earn a single penny from it, yet he jumps into someone else's business and causes this trouble, doesn't he? These days, as soon as he gets back from the print shop, he doesn't even glance at his younger brother who's dying like this. That damn brat got swayed by those crafty female workers...
(Uncle returns with wire and starts making grills in his spot.)
Hongmae's Father. Don't say that. This incident too was apparently cooked up by that damn printers' union or whatever your son is involved with... Anyway, they say that damned union, may its butt break, is leading the charge even now.
Mother. Nonsense! It was cooked up by those crafty girls working at the company. Hmph... Thanks to your daughter, even we who work outside are suffering.
Hongmae's Father. Why are you saying this! Think about it! Would girls come up with such an outrageous plan? Moreover, they're being so clever about it this time that even though I live in the same house, I had no idea whether she was stirring things up (수두럭거리기) or whispering (쑤군거리기). I only found out yesterday. Until then, it was like a pheasant eaten without a trace. For a few days, she was going out often and sometimes came back late in the evening, so I thought, being her age, she was just giggling with boys. But lo and behold! As I looked into it, that girl had been lured by those union brats and was up to some mischief. That girl deserves to have her butt broken—damn her!
Mother. This is truly ungrateful. We live thanks to that company, but what happens if even that job disappears? Everyone, without exception, will just starve to death.
Uncle. (Making grills aside)... Still, it looks like the effort this time might pay off. What... they say the company, after resisting and resisting, has no choice but to grant this request. And the request isn't even that demanding...<247>
Grandfather. (Pasting matchbox labels) They're asking for a ten percent raise in daily wages, right?
Uncle. If things go as planned this time, the wages will go up across the board.
Hongmae's Father. Hmph, they'll become big tycoons, I suppose... Wait a minute. Let's see how much my girl's daily wage will be then. She earned fifteen *chŏn* a day, so ten percent of that is...
Grandfather. One *chŏn* and five *ri* (일전오리).
Hongmae's Father. One *chŏn* and five *ri*? Hmph! A spectacular raise.
Uncle. Don't talk big! That's how life gradually gets better!
Grandfather. Indeed, there's the saying 'Gather dust to make a mountain' (塵合泰山)!
Hongmae's Father. Stop it! Will one *chŏn* five *ri* become one *wŏn* fifty *chŏn*? Will it become one hundred fifty *wŏn*?
Uncle. You're thinking only of your daughter, that's why you say that. But think about all the girls working at the company.
Hongmae's Father. All of them? How many are there in total?
Mother. Around five hundred, they say.
Hongmae's Father. Then, even if each person gets a raise of two *chŏn*... Two times five is ten... Oh my goodness. That's exactly ten *wŏn*. Is it such a great feat for five hundred female workers at that big company to get ten *wŏn* more per day? It's not even enough for one executive's lunch.
Uncle. Don't talk nonsense! Do you think persimmons just fall into your mouth while you lie there?
Hongmae's Father. Well, there's no harm in getting even a little bit more. But we shouldn't just rejoice blindly. If the prison warden adds one more grain of rice to the prisoner's bowl, will the prisoner's stomach feel full?
Grandfather. Hehehe... That's an amusing way to put it.
Hongmae's Father. My words make sense, right, old man?
Grandfather. ... Well, hearing it one way, it seems plausible, but hearing it another way, maybe not. But still, one must get even one penny more.
Hongmae's Father. Look here. Today, while digging these medicinal herbs (藥草), I thought about this. What if I do this kind of work for others and put one *wŏn* per month from the wages into a piggy bank? How would that be?... Ah, I calculated it, look! That's twelve *wŏn* a year. And in ten years, one hundred twenty *wŏn*! In a hundred years, one thousand two hundred *wŏn*. Heh, damn it! Pigs might fly! By this calculation, I'd have to live to be one hundred forty-five years old from now just to see a thousand *wŏn*. Can we ever live with peace of mind like this? When will the time come when we can speak loudly and have our own proper meal table?
(Silence among them)
Grandfather. (Pasting matchbox labels, begins speaking slowly)... I too, as soon as I open my eyes each day, paste these matchbox labels until late at night like this, but pasting like mad all day long yields barely a thousand. A thousand translates to twenty *chŏn*. After deducting the cost of glue, a day's earning is barely ten *chŏn*. What on earth is this work?... If I die doing only this, I won't be able to lift my head in the afterlife for shame... What did you do before coming here? If the messenger from the underworld asks like this, what should I answer? (Laughs lonely) Hehehe... Should I show these fingers stained with glue.
Uncle. That's why we want to earn even one penny more before we die, to live just a little bit better, isn't it?
Grandfather. Yes, that's right. Ho... My hands are cold. Why is the fire like this?
Uncle. (Stirs the brazier fire as if annoyed.)
(From afar, the sound of bells announcing a newspaper extra (新聞號外) has been audible for some time)
Grandfather. (Listening to the bell sound, remembers something and nervously takes out the paper bundle Taesŏn left from under his seat, showing it to Hongmae's Father) Right, what is this? We don't have anyone literate in this house.
Hongmae's Father. (Tilts his head, trying hard to read the contents of the paper. Unfortunately, he is also illiterate (無識))... I can't tell. It looks like writing, all black marks. What on earth is this?
Mother. Our eldest son... (To Grandfather) Right?
Hongmae's Father. Taesŏn?
Mother. He went out with two other friends, hiding this in their clothes.
Hongmae's Father. (Startled) Hyo- Then this is big trouble! This is, this is exactly that stuff they scatter on the main street... what's it called...
Mother. Oh, that damn kid! I knew it was something like that.
Hongmae's Father. If that noisy motorcycle (탁탁 자전거) comes rattling along right now! Everyone will be swept up and dragged off to some dark place... I'm leaving. I'll go put on some warmer clothes and sit tight. At least I won't catch a cold if they take me away.
Grandfather. Ah, you're so easily scared. Hehehe...
Uncle. Damn it! A person dies once, not twice.
Hongmae's Father. Please, just get rid of those paper bundles! Safety first in all things. (Just then, the sound of a motorcycle is heard in the distance. Everyone tenses. Hongmae's Father stops hesitantly, unsure what to do.) ... Ech, that <249> motorcycle sound! See if my words were false. Now!

(Just then, the sound swooshes past the front of the house. Everyone gasps, startled. Hongmae's Father nearly collapses.)

Hongmae's Father. ... Oh, my back.
Mother. (Trying to hide her startled heart with laughter) Hehehe...... If you get that scared, you'll faint just seeing a pot lid in the dark.
Hongmae's Father. Never mind others, get a grip on yourselves. How can you just laugh when you don't know what might happen when? (Moves towards the entrance.)

(A whistle sound is heard. Soon, A and B, hats pulled down low, burst through the entrance door. Hongmae's Father jumps back startled. A and B quickly hide behind furniture. Immediately after, the Unknown Man follows, panting.)
Man. Didn't some young punks just come in here? The ones scattering stuff?
Hongmae's Father. *Pujit'awa!* (부지타와! - I don't know!)
Man. Damn kids, where did they hide! I just saw them turn that corner.

(The Man exits. Dumbfounded, they look at each other. Hongmae's Father and Uncle cautiously peep outside. A and B emerge from behind the furniture.)
Hongmae's Father. (Raising his fist) Ech, you damn beggars! There's a limit to scaring people!
A. (To Mother) Give us what Taesŏn left earlier. Please?
Mother. You brats, what are you trying to do?
Hongmae's Father. These kids won't come to their senses until they get dragged off once.
B. Give it quickly. He might come back.
Mother. (Gives the paper bundle. A and B divide it.) Where did our Taesŏn go? Wasn't he with you?
B. (Quietly) Taesŏn went to headquarters. He's drafting the document there for the final negotiation (단판) with the company side today. He said he'll probably stay there overnight or come back very late.
Grandfather. Coming back late?
B. Not necessarily. This matter should be settled before midnight.
Grandfather. (Pleased) Settled!
Mother. (Angrily) Damn kid. Even if it's settled, what good will come of it?
B. (To Hongmae's Father) Perfect timing. Uh... when you go home, please tell Hongmae to come there right away.
Hongmae's Father. Where is 'there'? <250>
A. If you just tell her that, she'll know. There's a place where we always meet (노모히는 데).
Hongmae's Father. There's a place where you always meet? You miserable brats! This is going to be big trouble!
A. Don't worry. The worry is that big trouble *hasn't* happened yet.
(A and B exit)
Hongmae's Father. (As if dumbfounded) Why do they do things like that?
Uncle. (Simply) They probably don't want to live like beasts (개짐승).
Hongmae's Father. So doing things like that makes one human? Damn it!
(Hongmae's Father exits)
Uncle. Damn! Hongmae's father is quite the coward for a poor man.
Mother. Still, when you think about it, Hongmae's father's reasoning doesn't seem entirely without merit.
Uncle. (About to resume work) What merit? He talks like that because he's a coward.

(Sound of a vehicle in the distance. The lonely sound of a flute (胡笛) from a blind Chinese beggar.)
(Mother tends the fire under the medicine pot.)
(Sosŏn, sleeping fitfully, coughs shallowly. Grandfather and Mother go closer.)

Sosŏn. (As if short of breath) Mother... Mother...
Mother. Did you sleep a bit? Sosŏn?
Sosŏn. Un, I think I dozed off... Help me sit up.
(Grandfather and Mother help Sosŏn sit up.)
Sosŏn. Where's the medicine Hongmae's father brought earlier?
Grandfather. Right, how is it? Daughter-in-law.
Mother. It's almost ready. (Strains the medicine.)
Grandfather. (Giving Sosŏn the medicine) You try so hard to take medicine like this, yet there isn't even a packet of medicine by your bedside. What should we do about this? (Wipes Sosŏn's mouth after he takes the medicine) Instead of medicine, you should eat well, but these days you have no appetite, which is worrying. Tonight, you just wet your lips. And at lunchtime, you didn't eat at all. Your uncle bought that fish, I'll grill it for you tomorrow morning, so you must eat some. With any illness, they say you only live if you have an appetite.
Sosŏn. ...Grandfather. Grandfather and Mother try so hard, but I still haven't gotten better... I... I... <251> don't think I'm going to live...
Mother. Don't say such useless things!
Sosŏn. ...If only I could die... if only I could die soon... then Grandfather and Mother could have some peace...(Wipes tears.)
Grandfather. Why do you say such things, Sosŏn? Not everyone who gets sick dies. And if you were fated to die from this illness, you would have died already, not lingered like this. You must not give in to the illness. Instead, you must firmly step on the illness and envision in your heart how strong and resilient a man you will become when you grow up.
Sosŏn. (Clutches his chest and coughs. Mother holds a bowl under his chin) ... Ai, I can't breathe! Ai, it's suffocating! It feels like lime powder (횟가루) is packed in my windpipe. Ai, my chest hurts.
Mother. (Worriedly) Let's lay him down quickly. He seems to cough like that because he's sitting up.
Grandfather. (Lays his grandson down.)

(A Policeman (巡警) is seen outside on the main street, warning a neighbor.)
Policeman. Don't defecate on the main street. It smells bad.
(The Policeman says only this and disappears.)

(Uncle carefully bolts the entrance door.)
(Pause.)

Sosŏn. (Having caught his breath, points to the stone lattice window (石使窓)) Open the window a little.
Mother. It's still cold outside.
Sosŏn. ...It's stuffy...
Grandfather. The weather outside hasn't warmed up yet. What if you catch a cold on top of this?
Sosŏn. I want to see outside.
Grandfather. (Opens the window. Pale moonlight streams in.)
Sosŏn. ... Ah, refreshing... Spring must have come...
Grandfather. (Touching the moonlight) The wind is chilly, but there's definitely a feeling of spring... On the street, they were all decorating their shops, saying flowers would bloom soon.
Sosŏn. (Clinging to Grandfather's arm, pleads) Grandfather, I want to walk... I want to walk around a bit.
Grandfather. You mustn't. Moving around when your body isn't well will make the illness worse.<252> ... Still, for a child like you to just lie here for over a year, you must want to walk. Now that the weather is starting to warm up, your illness will likely ease too. Your face is beginning to show such signs. Then, this grandfather and you can go see the flowers at the mineral spring outside the east gate...
Sosŏn. (Earnestly) ...Then I'll go work at the factory. Yes, I'll earn money.
Grandfather. Work again?
Sosŏn. Yes, there's nothing better than working when your body is healthy. Lying here like this, I realize that.
Grandfather. Still, I won't send you to that damned lime factory (석회공장). Living buried in that dust day and night, how could even a healthy person endure it?
Mother. Oh, don't even talk about it. It was truly awful. One day I went to the factory to bring him lunch, and even though it was broad daylight, I could hardly see ahead because of the thick dust. Everyone working, adults and children alike, were covered in lime powder, with only their dark eyes showing. You couldn't tell who was who at all.
Grandfather. Sending this young child to such a filthy place, and yet we rejoiced at the few pennies he brought home as wages. Thinking about it now, the pennies earned were no different from trading away lengths of his lifespan.
Sosŏn. (Earnestly) No, our factory isn't a dirty factory! Big brother's print shop is dirty, and Hongmae's match company is dirty too! (Proudly)... Anyway, at our factory, they use "masks" (마쓰크)...
Grandfather. "Mattŭkko" (마뚜꼬)?
Sosŏn. They say it's fine if you wear that while working. No matter how bad the dust is, it doesn't affect you at all.
Mother. The brat who says it doesn't affect him at all, why did he get so terribly sick?
Sosŏn. Idiot! I told you it doesn't affect you. Our supervisor said so.
Mother. That squinty-eyed guy, right? Damn him! If you employ people, employ them properly, why smear lies on your lips? Seeing you sick like this, what excuse will that brat come up with now?
Sosŏn. (Proudly) Still, that gentleman is apparently highly trusted by the boss.
Mother. Those who are cruel to subordinates are often well-regarded by their superiors. <253>
Sosŏn. Still, it's fine... I'll work at our factory.
Uncle. (Still making grills) What's going on? Tonight, Sosŏn seems remarkably lucid.
Grandfather. No. It's strange that the child is fussing so much tonight. I'm actually getting scared... (To Sosŏn) Hey, pull yourself together. Lie down and calm down.
Sosŏn. (Fussing, trying to get down from the *k'ang* to the floor) It's okay, I'll try walking... I'll walk... I'll walk to see if I can go to the factory or not.
Grandfather. (Strongly) No. Hmph! No!
Sosŏn. (Pleadingly) Grandfather...
Grandfather. (Clicks his tongue) I said no. (Unable to resist Sosŏn's pleading, earnest face) Alright then, have your wish, try walking.
(Supports Sosŏn and helps him stand below the *k'ang*.)
Mother. Oh my, what is this child trying to do?
Grandfather. Just leave him be. Since it's his wish...
(Sosŏn, supported by Grandfather, tries to walk around the room. Of course, he cannot even stand properly. Mother also helps support him.)
Mother. ...This child is really being difficult tonight, it's strange.
Sosŏn. Grandfather, let me see outside a bit...
(They show him the outside from beside the window. Bathed in moonlight, Sosŏn's face is exceedingly pale.)

(Sudden sharp knocking on the entrance door outside)
(Everyone freezes.)
(Mother unbolts the door.)
(Hongmae's Father rushes in frantically, looking for a place to hide. He has layered on so many clothes that he looks like a clumsy toad. Uncle checks Sosŏn's pulse.)

Hongmae's Father. (Looking for a hiding place) What should I do? It's big trouble... Oh, my back.
Mother. What's this all of a sudden?
Grandfather. What's wrong? Hongmae's dad?
Hongmae's Father. Please save me. Oh, my back.
Grandfather. What happened all of a sudden? You were fine just a moment ago... What's that in your clothes?
Hongmae's Father. (Showing papers) It's this.

**Summary**

The play "Beast" depicts the harsh life of a poor Korean family living in a Chinese city in the 1930s. The family struggles financially, relying on piecework (pasting matchbox labels) and meager factory wages. The younger son, Sosŏn, is dying from consumption, likely caused by working in a lime factory. The older son, Taesŏn, is involved in organizing a strike with fellow print workers to demand better wages for female match factory workers, distributing leaflets despite the danger. The play culminates tragically: just as Taesŏn triumphantly announces the strike's success (a 10% wage increase), his younger brother dies, and Taesŏn himself is immediately arrested by authorities who had been tailing him.

From a historical perspective, the play vividly portrays the precarious existence of Korean migrants in China during the colonial period, often relegated to dangerous labor and poverty. It highlights the nascent labor movement and activism among Koreans, even in diaspora, against exploitative working conditions, likely exacerbated by Japanese industrial presence or influence. The swift suppression of Taesŏn after the "victory" underscores the authorities' intolerance of organized labor and dissent, painting a bleak picture of the limited agency and constant threat faced by marginalized groups like Korean workers under the overlapping jurisdictions and political tensions of the time in China. Historians might find the depiction of inter-ethnic urban life (Chinese street sounds, vendors, the *k'ang*), specific labor conditions (lime factory dust, matchbox piecework), methods of activism (leaflets, union meetings), and state suppression particularly revealing.

紅梅父. 내버려 두어요. 그런 철 업는 애들은 좀 단단히 맛을 봐야 해요. 그래야 정신이 나죠.
