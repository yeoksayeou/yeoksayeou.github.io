Pulmonary Tuberculosis and Surgery
						Chŏng Ku-ch'ung

With the development of surgery, there is a trend towards treating internal diseases surgically. Especially diseases that cannot be treated medically are almost all attempted to be treated surgically. Appendicitis (盲腸炎), once treated in internal medicine, is now fundamentally treated by complete appendectomy (虫樣突起摘出) in surgery as its pathology has become clear and its cause precise. Stomach cancer (胃癌) is also difficult to completely eliminate the cancer itself, so the goal of treatment is pursued by surgically removing the site where the cancer occurs. Similarly, since the medical treatment of pulmonary tuberculosis (肺結核) is slow, there is an attempt to treat it surgically. Now, I wish to briefly discuss the cause and pathology of pulmonary tuberculosis and its treatment.

The cause of pulmonary tuberculosis is infection by the tubercle bacillus (結核菌). While it is sometimes transmitted directly through the respiratory system (in most cases), infections transmitted through the skin or intestines all invade the lungs (肺臟) to form tuberculosis.

Strangely, from the point of invasion, the bacteria travel through the lymphatic vessels (淋巴線) to the veins, invariably passing through the lungs before circulating throughout the body again. In other words, bacteria that have entered the bloodstream, regardless of their type, must pass through the lungs. Therefore, pyogenic bacteria (化濃菌) that cause acute diseases cause illness at the site of invasion, but bacteria that cause chronic diseases do not cause illness at the site of invasion but rather in distant locations (遠隔場所). Thus, it is often the case that tubercle bacilli invade through the tonsils (扁桃線) and cause tuberculosis of the lymph nodes in the head (scrofula), leading to pulmonary tuberculosis. Not only that, but it is believed by all scholars studying tuberculosis that hilar lymph node tuberculosis (肺門腺結核) leads to pulmonary tuberculosis. When we handle tuberculosis patients, thinking it is only transmitted through the respiratory tract and defending only the oral cavity means defending only a part; we must be careful that it does not invade any part of the entire body.

The initial symptoms of pulmonary tuberculosis, hilar lymph node tuberculosis, and apical catarrh (肺尖加答兒症勢) are only mild fever and cough. When such symptoms are present, if pulmonary tuberculosis is suspected first, it can be completely cured with simple treatment. However, if it progresses further and tuberculosis spreads to the lung parenchyma (肺實質), there is comparatively high fever and many various symptoms, making treatment difficult, and it often does not heal unless a long period has passed.

When it progresses further and becomes relatively widespread tuberculosis, there may be hemoptysis (咯血), and besides that, various side effects such as cough, bloody sputum, and fever occur, making it often difficult to expect a complete cure.

Generally, as human life gradually concentrates in cities and the need for communal living is felt, contact between individuals increases, and consequently, the spread of tuberculosis causes harm, while at the same time, immunity (免疫性) to tuberculosis develops within the human body. Children in cities become infected with tubercle bacilli unknowingly from the age of 4 or 5, developing immunity to it. During the period until immunity develops after infection with tubercle bacilli, the symptoms observed in young children include weakening due to loss of appetite, mild cough and fever, sometimes accompanied by mild pleurisy (肋膜炎) and peritonitis (腹膜炎), along with swelling (腫脹) of lymph nodes throughout the body, or sometimes symptoms of gastrointestinal catarrh (胃腸加答兒), and occasionally chest pain or abdominal pain. While such symptoms often persist for several years or 7-8 years and are unknowingly cured during that time, we see a considerable number who transition again to pulmonary tuberculosis and become victims (in our Chosŏn (朝鮮), this is sometimes called *pokhak* (복학) or *chaegŏt* (재것)). Those who have resisted tuberculosis in childhood like this have strong resistance to tuberculosis after becoming adults, their progression is relatively slow, and they are easily cured. People who had no relation to tuberculosis in childhood, if infected with tubercle bacilli after becoming adults, progress rapidly and meet an unfortunate outcome (歸轉). There are many actual examples of this seen when young men and women from rural areas engage in city life.

When one develops pulmonary tuberculosis, there are differences according to age, but generally, in the young, it progresses and worsens rapidly, leading to an unfortunate outcome even less than 3 months after onset; mostly, they suffer for several years. Those who develop it after age 30 have relatively slow progression, and those who develop it after age 40 suffer from pulmonary tuberculosis for a longer period, 10 years or more, but the suffering is comparatively less. Women have less resistance to tuberculosis than men, and not only that, but women often experience a rapid worsening of pulmonary tuberculosis due to blood loss after childbirth. Therefore, for female pulmonary tuberculosis patients, the issue of artificially inducing abortion always arises, as does the issue of contraception.

The need for prevention does not require our further comment now. The more pulmonary tuberculosis patients there are, the more severe the spread, causing not only suffering for the patients themselves but also misfortune for families and great loss for the nation. Once infected with a tuberculous disease, one must treat it for life and always be cautious, and since few regain productive capacity, it is inefficient from an economic perspective.

Contact with a pulmonary tuberculosis patient does not mean immediate infection. The gradual expansion of the patient's tuberculous changes and the external dissemination (外部散布) of tubercle bacilli is the most dangerous to the general public. Tubercle bacilli are dispersed externally in the sputum (咯痰) along with coughing. Therefore, this must be understood not only by the patients themselves but also by the general public to strive for prevention of spread. Patient sputum contains over approximately 300 million tubercle bacilli, harming the populace. Since tubercle bacillus infection can invade any part of the body, there should be no wounds on the surface, and care must be taken to avoid gastrointestinal disorders. Of course, since the chance of transmission through the respiratory tract is the greatest, special attention should be paid to the respiratory system. Spittoons (唾壺) used by patients with open tuberculosis must always contain disinfectant (such as sublimate solution [?] or Creolin [クレン-ル]); utensils used by patients should be sterilized by boiling (煮沸); clothing and bedding should be disinfected (液毒) by sunlight. Sputum should be collected in paper (조희) or cloth and disposed of by incineration (液却). Patients with open tuberculosis should, if possible, be admitted to a sanatorium for isolation, for the sake of their families and the public, which is necessary for tuberculosis prevention.

Tuberculosis sanatoriums should be established near cities, selecting locations with fresh air and mild climate, have skilled physicians treat patients, differentiate and accommodate severe and mild cases, and simultaneously implement thorough disinfection (액독) to prevent harm to the general public. In modern civilized cities recently, laws require the establishment of sanatoriums by the state. We believe that such laws will be implemented in the various cities of our Chosŏn (朝鮮) in the near future. <40>

**Summary**

This 1938 article by Chŏng Ku-ch'ung discusses the rise of surgical interventions for diseases like appendicitis, stomach cancer, and particularly pulmonary tuberculosis (TB), where medical treatment is slow. It outlines TB's transmission routes (respiratory, skin, intestinal), pathology, symptoms, and varying severity based on age and sex. The author emphasizes the concept of acquired immunity, contrasting urban children's early exposure with the high susceptibility of rural individuals moving to cities. Prevention is stressed, highlighting the danger of sputum from open TB cases and recommending sanitation measures (disinfection, boiling, sun exposure) and isolation in sanatoriums, which the author anticipates will soon be legally mandated in colonial Korea's cities.

From a historical perspective, the article reveals the penetration of modern Western medical concepts (surgery for internal diseases, germ theory, public health, immunity) into colonial Korea. It reflects contemporary concerns about TB as a major public health crisis, exacerbated by urbanization. The observation about differing immunity between urban and rural populations provides insight into the social and health impacts of migration patterns during the colonial period. The anticipation of state-mandated sanatoriums points to developing public health infrastructure and policy awareness, even under colonial rule. The mention of folk terms like *pokhak* and *chaegŏt* also offers a glimpse into local understandings of disease.

肺結核의 원인은 結核菌의 감염으로 생키나니 직접 호흡계통으로 전염되는 때도 잇고(대부분) 피부로 전염된 것이나 腸으로 전염된 것이나 모다 肺臟으로 침입되여서 結核를 형성한다.
이상한 것은 침입한 장소로부터 균이 淋巴線을 통하야 정맥으로 반드시 肺臟을 통해 다시 전신으로 순환된다. 환언하면 혈액중으로 침입한 세균은 그 종류의 여하에 불구하고 반드시 肺臟을 통과하게 된다. 그럼으로 급성질환을 이리키는 化濃菌은 침입한 장소에 병이 생기나 만성질환을 이리키는 세균은 침입장소에 병이 생기지 아니하고 遠隔場所에 병이 생긴다. 그럼으로 扁桃線으로 결핵균이 침입하여서 두부의 임파선결핵(연주나력)으로 肺結核이 되는 때가 만타. 그뿐 안이라 肺門腺결핵으로 肺結核이 되는 것은 결핵을 연구하는 학자간에 누구든지 밋는 바이다. 우리가 결핵환자을 취급할 때 호흡기로만 전염되는 줄 알고 口腔만을 방어할야는 것은 일부분만 방어하는 까닭을 몸전체 어느 방면이든지 침입치 안토록 주의하야 한다.
肺結核의 초기 肺門腺결핵, 肺尖加答兒症勢는 경미한 신열과 咳嗽뿐이다. 일어한 증세가 있을 때에는 먼저 肺結核을 의심하야서 간단한 치료로 전치할 수 잇으나 더 지나서 肺實質에 결핵이 파급하는 경우에는 비교적 고열과 여러 가지 증세가 만어서 치료하기가 어려울 뿐 아니라 시일에 경*가 길지 안으면 낫지 안은 때가 만타.
