With a Brazier and Riding a Horse Cart, from a Northern Manchuria Travelogue
						Mo Yun-suk

It was an untamed outdoors, a journey with no place to linger. For a moment, I couldn't suppress a sense of emptiness, but the sound of the swaying horse cart (馬車) and the spirit of the clattering, running horseman became one, somewhat easing my anxiety.

After about an hour, the wind blowing across the boundless plain (一望無提) was fierce.
The sky grew darker from the distance. My hands grew cold, my body became increasingly frozen, and I could no longer sit still.
Cold snow flurried and clung to the cart's wheels. They said it was still three hours to Kukchaga (局子街); how could we possibly get there? My head felt lonely, with nothing to lean on.
Unlike Turgenev's "On the Eve," my heart wasn't filled with great sorrow, nor could I recall Tolstoy's deathbed; I simply raced across the vast plain, feeling quite ordinary. The distant sky turned red, and the crimson-tinged snow looked even more beautiful.
Reaching a village full of dugout huts (움막), I felt temporarily grounded, thinking of it as a place to live.
First, I found hot water, drank a mouthful, and washed my hands.
Hearing it was still two more hours, I felt apologetic but asked the old man of the house to light a charcoal fire (숫불) in a brazier (화로), offering him some money.
Placing the brazier inside the cart, I sat alone, warming my hands. Snowflakes, like the manna (만나송이) the Israelites ate, poured down abundantly. Free snowflakes, with no one to watch them, no one to tread upon them. What worry could worldly anxieties be to me now? Alone, I listened to the clatter of the horse's hooves and met the rustling snowflakes, delving into my thoughts. All ideas had vanished; it was a primitive brain, childish. The charcoal fire died out. The ashes were cold. I crossed my arms and lowered my head. Sometimes I focused my mind on the sound of shifting sand, like a night prayer, and sometimes I lost my composure to the vibrations of the fierce blizzard. Suddenly, I thought of Thais (다이쓰). I pictured the gaunt face of Thais discussing virtue and the meaning of baptism with Paphnuce (빠프니쓰) as they walked through the long desert. Was the place where she hid the silver chalice for the altar in her pure white dress and drank from the desert spring also a field like this? In their struggle to grasp the highest truth, they couldn't hide their blissful passion.
Traveling this wilderness with a professional driver (마부) with whom I could have no conversation, nor felt any inclination to, was an act too devoid of central meaning. As if no incident would occur, we passed leisurely through the middle of the plain.
I wrapped my skirt around myself, turned over the embers in the dying earthenware brazier (질화로), and just waited for the house of my friend K to appear. I thought again of K's letter saying she was waiting with roasted pork (도야지고기) and the p’ŏngt’ahulla (펑타훌라) I liked, and rejoiced.
The fire was completely out; now there was nothing to do but cross my arms demurely and endure as if dead.
As the daylight grew dim (거스므레하니), the road felt tedious. I told the driver once more to hurry (다바나라고). The sound of the whip cracking echoed like a heroic roar and vanished.
I was lost in the fantasy of how joyful my state of mind would be upon meeting friend K.
"Stop the horse!" came a very sharp voice.
Even at first hearing, the voice possessed an incomparable wild quality. Being in a desolate (空漠) plain, I hesitated to look out immediately, clutching my shrinking heart and waiting for what would happen. Suddenly, the cart's canopy was flung open, and a person, face covered with a black hood and body wrapped in animal (즘생) skin, jumped in.
"I suppose I'm done for now," I thought, instantly finding it easy to give up on my life.
My familiar people who could save me were too far away. There was no telephone, no train. Perhaps I would lose my life, leaving behind a * that would never be known even after death. Without a mirror, I could still guess my face must have turned green (미상불). My liver and heart (간과 심장) all clenched together, falling into a state of coagulation (응고). I was already so dazed I wouldn't feel anything even if killed.
"I wish I could die before this fellow starts his interrogation (힐란)," I hoped, but dying in such an orderly fashion seemed likely to take some time.
"Where are you going?"
The voice was gentle and didn't sound like it intended harm.
My tightly shut lips wouldn't part. As I sat there for a while, he asked,
"Why no answer?"
He gently shook my shoulder.
Ah—he's Korean! I realized instantly. Even among Koreans, his voice sounded remarkably compassionate. I lifted my bowed head and looked at him once. A face with only eyes. There was fire in those eyes.
"Oh, how scary," I exclaimed like a child, covering my face with my skirt.
He chuckled, "Hahaha," for a moment, then said,
"Don't think of me as so frightening. You must take me for a Chinese. I am Korean, just like you. It's true I am someone who searches passing carts and people, interrogates them as I please, or takes them to where I am. They have always been Chinese or Russians (로시야). I even had a weapon capable of spotting this cart from afar, but for some reason, seeing a cart going slowly and nonchalantly on a snowy road seemed suspicious, so I followed it. When I arrived, I found a Korean woman. I am very glad to meet you."
I was somewhat impressed (多少感心) by his weighty story, and my heart fluttered as if his wilderness life touched a part of some longed-for idea.
"Don't be too scared, please answer me."
This time, he patted my back, soothing me with a voice distinctly like a father's.
"Then take off that mask (복면) and talk."
My head still wouldn't lift.
"I will. But it is my fate not to be able to remove this mask until my day comes. Please understand that, and instead of seeing my face, see the light in my voice. I believe it is enough if you can see through to the sincerity of my words. Where are you headed at this twilight hour, and for what purpose?"
"I work at the girls' school in Yongjŏng (용정). Since today is Saturday, I'm going to visit a friend in Kukchaga. I set out without knowing it was such a desolate plain with no villages."
Finding it easier for my mood not to look at him, I turned my head to the left as I answered.
"Did you study in Seoul?"
"Yes."
"When did you come?"
"I came last spring."
"Are you married?"
"No, I am not."
"Should such a precious person be traveling carelessly through a plain like this? There are many bandits (마적) here, and also communists (공산당) who set fires and flee. Even people who live here travel by train. At the T'udugogogae pass (투두고고개) up ahead, a band of bandits has formed a village."
"Please take me to Kukchaga." I knew this request was impolite to a man I had just met, a dictate of conscience. But I had no other words more urgent than these.
"I intended to do so even without you asking. But may I ask your name?"
"If you cannot remove your mask before me, how can I tell you my family name or first name? I will always remember you as a good person worth remembering for a long time. What need is there for my name in a place like this?"
I laughed lightly.
The snow was falling more slowly than before, but the wind blew fiercely, making it unbearable. He sat as if undisturbed.<34> Thinking it might be better if the cart cover was closed, I got up to close it. My hands were numb, and I fumbled with the metal edge, cutting my thumb. Red blood flowed onto my skirt. Seeing no scrap of cloth anywhere, I was truly flustered. I thought of tearing off a piece of my petticoat but couldn't bring myself to do it in front of him, and was worried. He seemed to notice my predicament and quickly reached into his waist. It looked like an undershirt (속샤쓰); he tore a piece straight from the middle of his chest and bandaged my hand. The reliability of his touch, like the feel (*) of the mountains, made my heart flutter.
"Hold it up like this," he said, supporting my elbow.
"It's alright."
"What do you mean, alright, while crying? Put this on," he said, taking off the leather coat he was wearing and draping it over my shoulders.
Made entirely of fur, it felt instantly warm (확근하다).
"I am so sorry [for the trouble]."
"      . (One line deleted - National Institute of Korean History) Forgive my rudeness in draping my coat over you. We're almost there now. See the lights over there? That's Kukchaga. When you return, take the main train."
"Yes, thank you."
"I miss Korea (조선). But I might spend my entire life in this distant place, repeating how much I want to go back. Please become a good female worker. Seeing your clothes makes me feel as if I'm touching my mother's skirt hem. Today has been excessively happy."
He pressed my hand tightly to his chest. The sounds of carts and fiddles (깽깽이) gradually grew closer.
"I turn back here," he said, jumping down from the cart.
"How will you walk at night?"
"Oh, a road like this is no problem. I shouldn't go near places like that with neon signs (네온싸인). I sustain my bones and flesh in the dark plains, feeding on blizzards. I wish you happiness." Like a swift bird, he ran off again, seemingly without any lingering attachment. Getting down from the cart, I watched his footprints for a long time. The footprints, pressed deep into the untrodden white snowfield, added a dark luster. I felt a distinct pang in my chest for reasons unclear.
"He's gone. A man who came and went like a dream! The wound wrapped around my finger feels feverishly hot. The masked man of unknown identity! If only he fights for a righteous cause (義)!"
Gradually, I became deeply aware of the fluttering in my heart. Is this right? Saying farewell to the driver, I headed towards friend K's house. K had apparently gone to the station, thinking I would arrive by train. Pork (도야지 고기), p’ingt’ang hulla (핑탕 훌나), steaming Chinese food came in.
My sense of * [taste] (각) vanished, rendering the food flavorless. Even K, whom I had expected to be so glad to see, seemed meaningless. On the pretext of a headache, I spread bedding in another room and lay down. His heavy voice seemed to drift in on the distant wind.
I want to open the window. I hate ** [likely 'this place' or similar]. If I just follow the footprints he left, will his passionate cottage be there? The piece of shirt torn from his chest—
It's stained red.

**Summary**

The author, Mo Yun-suk, recounts a winter journey by horse cart through the desolate plains of Northern Manchuria to visit a friend in Kukchaga. Feeling lonely and cold, her journey takes a dramatic turn when a mysterious, masked Korean man intercepts her cart. Initially terrified, she realizes he is Korean and compassionate. He shares insights about the dangers of the region (bandits, communists) and escorts her safely, bandaging her injured hand with a piece of his own undershirt and giving her his fur coat. He departs abruptly near Kukchaga, expressing longing for Korea and leaving a deep impression on the author, who finds herself preoccupied with thoughts of the enigmatic figure even after reaching her friend's house.

From a historical perspective, the article offers a glimpse into the precarious lives of Koreans in Manchuria during the Japanese colonial period. The mention of bandits (마적) and communists (공산당) reflects the instability and dangers of the region, a frontier zone contested by various powers and groups. The masked man, potentially an independence fighter or someone resisting authority ("until my day comes," avoiding neon signs), symbolizes the hidden struggles and patriotism of Koreans living under harsh conditions far from home. His longing for Korea and the author's emotional response highlight the shared sense of displacement and national sentiment among Koreans in diaspora.
