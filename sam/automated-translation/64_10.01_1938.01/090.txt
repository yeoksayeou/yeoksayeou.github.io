What are the Seven Military Classics (兵家七書)?

What are the "Seven Military Classics," which have been highly regarded since ancient times as the canon (經典) of military strategy and tactics? These seven books are truly great classics, the pride of East Asia (東洋), unparalleled in the world.

1.  *Sun Tzu* (*孫子*), authored by Sun Wu (孫武) of the state of Qi (齊). It is divided into 13 chapters ([篇]): Initial Estimations (始計), Waging War (作戰), [Planning] Offensives ([謀]攻), Military Disposition (軍形), Military [Power] (兵[勢]), Weaknesses and Strengths (虛實), Military [Combat] (軍[爭]), [Nine] Changes (九[變]), Moving the Army (行軍), Terrain (地形), [Nine] Grounds (九[地]), Attacks by Fire (火攻), and Use of Spies (用間). Its exposition of military matters is [profound] ([精妙]), the writing is also [concise] (簡[潔]), and especially the excellence of its theories on the use of troops and strategy is widely [revered] ([推仰]) across ancient and modern times ([古今通涉]).

2.  *Wu Tzu* (*吳子*), said to be compiled (撰) by Wu Qi (吳起) of the Zhou (周) dynasty, although some later people (后人) transmit that it might be falsely attributed (假托). Regardless, it is a genuine ancient book, [orderly] ([整然]), and *Wu Tzu* ([吳子]) is outstanding (卓拔). That it is mentioned together with *Sun Tzu* as "The Art of War of Sun and Wu" (孫吳의 兵法) is common knowledge (世人周知).

3.  *Six Secret Teachings* (*六韜*) and *Three Strategies* (*三略*). These two books are entirely separate. The *Six Secret Teachings* is said to be the [work] ([作]) of the Grand Duke Wang (大公望), and the *Three Strategies* that of the [Yellow] Stone Elder ([黃]石公). The *Six Secret Teachings* begins from when King Wen of Zhou (周文王) recruited Jiang Taigong (姜太公) and is divided into the Six Scabbards ([六韜]): Civil, Military, Dragon, Tiger, Leopard, [and Dog] ([文武虎豹犬龍]). The *Three Strategies* of the [Yellow] Stone Elder is also transmitted as the art of war that the famous Zhang Zifang (張子房), when he was a child, learned from an old man under a bridge after picking up and returning his shoe.

5.  *Methods of the Sima* (*司馬法*), compiled (撰) by Sima Rangju (司馬穰苴). *Sima* (司馬) was the official title in ancient China (支那) in charge of military affairs ([師旅]), so this could be called the Essentials of Military Science ([軍學綱要]). It is a standard text for educating soldiers. It is said there were 150 chapters, but only 5 chapters are extant today.

6.  *Wei Liaozi* (*尉繚子*), compiled (撰) by Wei Liao (尉繚) of Zhou (周). Discussing the art of war, it is very [essential] ([精要]) and can be compared to the [essentials] ([要略]) of the 13 chapters of *Sun Tzu*.

7.  *Questions and Replies between Tang Taizong and Li Weigong* (*李衛公問對*). This is a collection of discussions on the art of war between Emperor Taizong ([太宗]) of [Tang] ([唐]) and Li Weigong. <17>

**Summary**

This article introduces the "Seven Military Classics" (兵家七書), a collection of ancient Chinese military treatises highly revered as the canon of East Asian military thought. It briefly describes each text: *Sun Tzu*, *Wu Tzu*, *Six Secret Teachings*, *Three Strategies*, *Methods of the Sima*, *Wei Liaozi*, and *Questions and Replies between Tang Taizong and Li Weigong*, noting their purported authors, key characteristics, and legendary origins (like Zhang Liang receiving the *Three Strategies*). The article emphasizes these works as an unparalleled "pride of the East (東洋)." From a historical perspective, presenting these shared East Asian classics in a Korean magazine in 1938, under Japanese rule, is interesting. It highlights a common cultural heritage distinct from the West, which could subtly foster a sense of broader East Asian identity or pride during a period dominated by Japanese imperialism, while also potentially serving as a nod to classical knowledge respected by the colonizers themselves.
