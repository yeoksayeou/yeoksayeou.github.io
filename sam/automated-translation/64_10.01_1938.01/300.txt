Kyŏngsŏng Music Academy, in a German's House in Chŏngdong-chŏng

An Kiyŏng, Yi Sŭnghak, An Sŏnggyo, Kim T'aeyŏn, and Mr. Kim Kwan gathered, appointing Mr. Kim Chaedong, who returned after completing his studies in Germany, as the director. They have founded a music school called Kyŏngsŏng Music Professional Academy (京城音樂專門學院), renting a German person's house in Chŏngdong-chŏng, and began classes on December 1st last. Mr. Kim Chaedong will teach composition method, German language, and violin; Mr. An Kiyŏng will teach harmony and voice; Mr. Yi Sŭnghak will teach music fundamentals (樂典) and voice; Mr. An Sŏnggyo will teach intervals (音程), ear training (聽音), and violin; Mr. Shimizu Kanzō will teach piano; Mr. Kim T'aeyŏn will teach cello; and Mr. Kim Kwan will be responsible for teaching music theory, music history, foreign languages, etc.

The curriculum is divided into three courses: the main course (본과), the specialized course (專修科), and the preparatory course (예과). The preparatory course is for preparing before entering the main course. The specialized course is, naturally, for those who wish to study music further after completing the main course, but it is also designed so that graduates from other schools, those currently studying elsewhere, those with jobs, or even those at home can pursue research. For this reason, for commuting convenience, only the specialized course has [*]afternoon or evening classes[*].

Admission Qualifications
For the preparatory course, applicants must have completed the third year of middle school for boys or girls, or possess qualifications equivalent to or higher than this level.
For the specialized course, regardless of academic background or age, anyone recognized as capable of undertaking music studies can be admitted.

**Summary**

This news article announces the establishment of the Kyŏngsŏng Music Professional Academy (京城音樂專門學院) in Chŏngdong-chŏng, Seoul, in December 1937. Headed by Kim Chaedong, recently returned from studies in Germany, the academy offers preparatory, main, and specialized courses taught by notable Korean musicians like An Kiyŏng and Yi Sŭnghak, as well as a Japanese instructor, Shimizu Kanzō. The article details the faculty, subjects taught, admission requirements, and tuition fees, highlighting the specialized course's flexibility for working individuals or those pursuing further study. From a historical perspective, the article reveals the burgeoning interest and institutionalization of Western classical music education in colonial Korea, led by Korean intellectuals educated abroad (Germany, in this case). The inclusion of a Japanese instructor and the mention of German language instruction also reflect the complex cultural interactions of the period. The establishment of such an institution signifies a Korean effort to cultivate modern artistic expertise despite the colonial context.
