**Article Title:** 서울의 上流社會, 入會金만 三百圓 드는 꼴푸場 (Seoul's High Society, the Golf Course Where the Entrance Fee Alone is 300 Won)
**Author:** N/A
**Article Type:** 소식 (News/Report)

In every aspect, Seoul's high society is splendid, to the point where one might say, "This truly is Seoul." It shines brilliantly in all respects. In other words, within Seoul, there are many high-ranking nobles (堂上귀족) of esteemed status, and many great millionaires and multi-hundred-thousandaires, whose way of life differs from ordinary folk like us—it is luxurious, grand, and broad in scope. Now, we shall unveil, step by step, the outline and details of the places considered the playgrounds of all this Seoul high society:
The Golf Club, the Riding Club, the Aristocrats' Hall.

While there are high-class social clubs in Chosŏn whose members include many high officials and top prominent figures, such as the Chosŏn Industrial Club (朝鮮實業俱樂部) centered around Mr. Han Sang-nyong (韓相龍) and the Kyemyŏng Club (계명구락부) centered around Mr. Yun Ch'i-ho (尹致昊), the one that captures the most public interest is likely the Kyŏngsŏng Golf Club (京城골프구락부), which has His Highness King Yi (李王殿下) appointed as Honorary President. Looking at the committee members and members of that club, it includes not only His Highness King Yi and high officials of the Chosŏn Government-General but also nearly all the top prominent figures in Seoul, major business magnates from the provinces, and the British and American residents in Chosŏn. When one speaks of golf, everyone knows it is the unique recreational sport of top prominent figures and great capitalists belonging to the upper class, a sport they can enjoy, but one might not realize how highly golf is valued in upper-class society. We often hear tales like the Takarazuka golf team coming to Tōkyō for a match, or so-and-so flying to Shanhai from Tōkyō to play golf. Therefore, a certain high official in Sinŭiju coming to Seoul every Sunday to play golf would be considered a more than natural event for golf enthusiasts. Thus, any day at the endlessly vast Kyŏngsŏng Golf Course in Nŭng-ni, across the river from Ttuksŏm outside Kyŏngsŏng city, the cheerful laughter of Chosŏn's top prominent figures never ceases, and every Sunday, matches are held between amateur golf players gathered from all over Chosŏn. Spending the one Sunday out of seven running around the nature-embraced golf course all day long, returning home with a tired body but a refreshed mind, could be called a happiness only they can savor. Perhaps the main point that wins their favor is that golf is a gentlemanly sport that can be played without an umpire (엠파이), allowing them to compete without damaging their self-esteem. So, what kind of person is qualified to become a member of the Kyŏngsŏng Golf Club, enjoying this labeled (레텔붓흔) gentlemanly sport, and how can one join? With the recommendation (취천) of two or more members, anyone who is affluent in terms of time and finances can join by paying an entrance fee of 200 Won. Once admitted, one must pay an annual fee (年会費) of 60 Won and monthly expenses ranging from 20 to 30 Won. These seem to be the conditions (修件 - likely typo for 條件) that allow only top capitalists to engage in the sport.

**Summary**

This 1938 article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine describes the Kyŏngsŏng Golf Club as a symbol of Seoul's elite high society. It highlights the club's exclusivity, noting the high entrance fee (300 Won mentioned in title, 200 Won in text) and annual/monthly costs, restricting membership to the wealthy and influential. The club's members include high-ranking Japanese officials, prominent Koreans (businessmen, former officials, professionals), and Western residents, with King Yi Un as Honorary President. The article emphasizes golf as a "gentlemanly sport" favored by the upper class for recreation and social networking. It concludes by listing numerous prominent Korean members, identifying them by profession (industrialists, Central Council members, mine owners, lawyers), underlining their status within colonial society. Historians might find the article valuable for its glimpse into the social stratification and leisure activities of the colonial elite in Korea, including both Japanese and collaborating Koreans. The list of Korean members, including well-known figures like Han Sang-nyong, Pak Hŭng-sik, Kim Yŏn-su, and Yu Il-han, provides concrete data on the composition of this upper echelon and their participation in institutions associated with the colonial power structure. The cost of membership also offers insight into the economic disparities of the time.
