The True Meaning of the War, An Interview with Prime Minister Konoe and a US U.P. Correspondent

While we are currently undertaking military action in China, some third parties might see it as contradictory if I say this now, but our goal is peace and cooperation. To emphasize this again, it can be said that we are fighting China because we desire peace and cooperation. Why is that? It is because unless the immediate obstacles are removed by some method, we cannot establish the preferential, mutually beneficial relationship we aim for. Despite this, China's anti-Japanese sentiment has intensified to the point where it completely negates all our efforts to remove the immediate obstacles through peaceful means.

What do these immediate obstacles refer to? They are the anti-Japanese and expel-Japanese policies as China's national policy, the advance of communist forces in China, and other dissatisfactions and complaints existing between Japan and China. It is our greatest regret that we have no choice but to resort to military force to resolve these. The only comfort we have in this is our firm conviction that this struggle is not an aggressive one, but a struggle against an enemy, a struggle against a mistaken national policy that endangers the foundations of peace. Although endlessly pitiful, things have come to this pass, and we cannot help but think that we are now paying an unavoidable price to build a better future and to fundamentally adjust Japan-China relations, bringing about the balanced, mutual prosperity of both nations.

-Many researchers of Japan-China issues observe that regarding the current incident, there is a justifiable position that general foreign countries cannot understand. What is the general outline of the circumstances that led to the occurrence of the current situation?

-To understand the current conflict, one must go back and consider the time of the Russo-Japanese War. The Russo-Japanese War was a life-or-death struggle <19> we undertook, risking the fate of our nation against Russia's aggression in Manchuria. As a result of that achievement, we established legitimate rights and interests in the land of Manchuria. However, in 1931, several years of nationalism and the intensification of anti-Japanese sentiment led China to directly attack these rights and interests, and they paid no heed whatsoever to our conciliatory attitude aimed at removing the friction during that period. The result was the Manchurian Incident. The subsequent developments are already well-known to the world. Therefore, while it is not fair [프외아] to place the blame for the outbreak of the current incident solely on China's anti-Japanese policy, it is also by no means a fair judgment to assert that it was always Japan's actions that provoked China's anti-Japanese sentiment. Among the various debated facts, we must not forget one extremely clear fact that cannot be overlooked.

That is the intensity of China's anti-Japanese policy. In recent years, China's policy has concentrated solely on denouncing Japan to its people and the entire world, portraying Japan as the sole enemy they must fight. Attempting to build the project of national unification upon the foundation of hatred [惡] as a slogan against one particular country cannot be seen as sound. Moreover, instilling aversion towards the people of another nation while educating innocent children is truly beyond words. China has previously adopted anti-American policies, anti-British policies, and policies of sequentially rejecting other foreign countries. For several years, Japan has been sacrificed by the same type of policy. Japan's turn, in particular, came last and met the worst fate. Underlying this, it is extremely clear that the Chinese people's hatred for foreign peoples is severe. If we investigate its roots, the feeling of hatred that China holds towards foreign peoples is by no means the sole responsibility of Japan; if there is blame to be assigned, each country should bear the blame equally. It is undeniably a tragedy that when China's anti-foreign sentiment targeted Japan, it took the most extreme form of expression. We can generally understand why anti-foreign thought took the most radical form of expression when directed at Japan. Originally, feelings of inferiority are felt most acutely towards nations of the same race or those geographically closest. In any case, when the Chinese people's hostility towards the Japanese people finally became deep-rooted, leading them to think that fighting Japan was their only way to survive, and when this hostility caused them to make a grave miscalculation regarding the strength of the two countries, and further, when China found itself bound multiple times over by its own most destructive [...] <20> self, the fight was already an unavoidable fate for both countries. The dissatisfactions and complaints that had piled up for many years required a fundamental, uprooting adjustment. However, only outstanding wisdom [*智] could rescue such a situation from armed conflict, but such outstanding wisdom cannot be found in the world today.

-What actions will Japan take henceforth? After the current military actions succeed [...], will Japan withdraw its troops from China? What is Japan's policy towards China?

-I believe my response to your very first question has already addressed that. The purpose of military action in China is neither territorial acquisition nor the partitioning of China. Therefore, it is well understood that our forces are not in China to be stationed there permanently. It is our sincere hope to withdraw the troops as quickly as possible. However, there is just one condition for that. That is for the Chinese government to abandon its mistaken policies. Our specific policy depends greatly on the direction China intends to take. Only one thing is clear: our objectives are always fair and just, and will be good for all those who have an interest in the peace of East Asia.

-Are there individuals in China currently who sympathize with your country's great ideals?

-I am convinced that there are many Chinese who understand Japan's true motives and sympathize with the ideals common to both Japan and China. I believe I can find such people even among the ruling class. Moreover, I believe there are undoubtedly even more in the economic sphere. Today, their voices are drowned out by the noise and do not reach the outside, but I believe the time when their voices will be properly heard is not far off. <21>

**Summary**

In this January 1938 interview, Japanese Prime Minister Fumimaro Konoe justifies the ongoing war in China (Second Sino-Japanese War) to a U.P. correspondent. He frames Japan's military actions not as aggression, but as a necessary measure to remove "obstacles"—namely China's "anti-Japanese policies" and communist influence—in order to establish "peace and cooperation" and a "mutually beneficial relationship." Konoe blames Chinese nationalism, historical anti-foreign sentiment (which he claims is unfairly focused on Japan due to proximity and an inferiority complex), and a miscalculation of strength for the conflict. He asserts Japan has no territorial ambitions and will withdraw troops once China abandons its "mistaken" policies, claiming many Chinese secretly understand and sympathize with Japan's "true motives." For Korean colonial history, this article is significant as it showcases the official Japanese justification for its expansionist war, disseminated within colonized Korea through a Korean-language magazine. It reveals the propaganda narratives used—portraying aggression as a path to peace, blaming the victim (China), and appealing to a supposed shared destiny in East Asia—which were intended to shape public opinion in the colony and legitimize Japanese imperialism.
