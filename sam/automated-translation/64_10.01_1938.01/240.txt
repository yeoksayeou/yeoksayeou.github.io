Memories of the "Eight Fairies," When the World Seemed Only Joyful
						By Song Kŭm-sŏn

Saying I cannot understand the editor's mind in asking me to dig up moldy old stories from well over 15 years ago,
yet, perhaps my own mind, taking up the pen <35>, is even harder to understand. When I think about it, it seems humans must be animals that extremely enjoy reminiscing.

At seventeen, after graduating from Sungmyŏng Women's Higher Normal School, I went to Tokyo with just one other person, Dr. Cho Kyŏng-hŭi, who is now a teacher at Jissen Women's Professional School in Tokyo. Although the world I was hearing about and seeing for the first time was dazzling and novel, perhaps because the aspirations I held in my heart were large, there was absolutely nothing fearful or hesitant; it just felt as though the whole world would bend to my will.

That said, it wasn't as if I wrapped my head and delved into studies until my brain hurt; rather, for no particular reason, my heart was simply cheerful and happy.

At that time, the school and dormitory rules were extremely strict, and there weren't any particularly noteworthy interesting events among teachers or friends, but there were eight students in total who had come over from Chosŏn. We always played together in a group, and whenever there was any trouble, sadness, or joy, we would talk amongst ourselves, comfort, and encourage each other. Because of this, we eight were always together like shadows wherever we went, so our school friends called us the Eight Fairies (팔선녀).

These "Eight Fairies" all held equally grand ambitions. If someone dressed extravagantly or neglected their studies, we would invariably advise each other, saying, "Shouldn't we achieve our goals?" However, we couldn't achieve those great goals. Two of those friends have already passed away from tuberculosis, and of the remaining six, there are those who married, had children, and are housekeeping; those working as school teachers; one who became a widow; another who became unhappy in a different way; one who went mad, lost touch with the world, and died; and then there's me, living a busy life, bustling around at school every day, then coming home to attend to my husband and deal with the children's demands, living such a complex and busy life that I can hardly tell night from day.

15 years ago, back then—when I believed the whole world would go my way and was joyful—who could have even imagined it would turn out like this? We dreamt of being heroines, and whenever we gathered, sitting or standing, our conversations were only about "What will you do?" and "What will I do?" when we returned to Chosŏn. How endearing these talks were.

Even if there were gatherings just for women, the majority of the talk was about achieving our goals. Pak Sŭng-ch'ŏn, Han So-che, Hwang Sin-dŏk, and others were people I often met during those times. These individuals were even more radical than us, and when they gave speeches at meetings, they would speak with passion and force, pounding the table—it's still fresh in my memory.

Perhaps it was after the Great Kantō Earthquake. The Tokyo Association of Korean Students held a memorial service for Koreans, and I can still remember Han So-che speaking at length with her very eloquent delivery. Although I was young then, it seems true that my spirit was no less than theirs. Otherwise, I wouldn't have been so moved by their words.

Whatever one says, it's already a bygone past, so what use is there in constantly digging it up and talking about it? But along with the memories of playing well, chatting well, and laughing well back then, the memories of the friends I always met also do not fade from my mind.

There's no need to say more about Ch'oe Ŭi-sun, who was at the Tonga Ilbo, as she was in the same school and one of the Eight Fairies. Yi Suk-chong, principal of Kajŏng Women's School; Nam Su-hŭi, a dentist; Yi Pong-hŭi of Ihwa Women's Higher Normal School; Kim Hyŏn-sil of Sungmyŏng Women's Higher Normal School; Im Suk-chae of Taegu Girls' High School; and Cho Kyŏng-hŭi—these are the friends I can never forget. Sometimes, in the evening when the children are all asleep, I think of each of their faces from that time and smile to myself inwardly. Now, their faces are all wrinkled and gaunt, but back then, those faces were as lovely and endearing as plump garden balsams. <36>

**Summary**

Song Kŭm-sŏn reminisces about her youth, specifically her time studying in Tokyo over 15 years prior, around the early 1920s. She recalls the initial excitement and naive optimism, feeling the world was hers for the taking. She describes her close-knit group of eight Korean female students, dubbed the "Eight Fairies" (P'alsŏnnyŏ), who shared grand ambitions for their future roles in Chosŏn. Song contrasts this youthful idealism with the harsh realities of their adult lives—premature death, unhappy marriages, mental illness, and the demanding routine of domestic life or teaching, far removed from their heroic dreams. She fondly remembers specific friends and other notable Korean women in Tokyo at the time, like Han So-che, known for her passionate speeches. The piece highlights the poignant gap between youthful aspirations and eventual life paths. For historians, the article offers a valuable first-person perspective on the experiences, ambitions, and social networks of educated Korean women studying in Japan during the colonial period. It sheds light on their sense of purpose, their engagement with nationalist sentiments (implied through discussions of "achieving our goals" and mentions of passionate speakers), and the sobering realities faced by these pioneering women upon returning to colonial society or navigating adulthood.

八仙女의 追憶, 세상이 그저 즐겁기만 하더니
						宋今璇
15년도 훨신 지낸 곰팽이가 쓸도록 옛일을 뒤저내라는 편집자의 심리를 알 수 없다고
하면서도 붓을＜35＞ 드는 내 심리는 더 알 수 없다구나 할까요 생각하면 사람이란 추억을 지극히 좋아하는 동물인 탓인가 봄니다.
열일곱에 淑明女子高等普通學校를 졸업하고 지금 東京서 實踐女子專門學校에 선생으로 있는 理學博士 趙慶喜氏와 단둘이서 東京에 갔읍니다. 처음 듯고 보는 세상이 화려하고 신기하기는 햇지만 가슴에 품은 뜻이 컷든 까닭이였든지 무서운 것 주저되는 것이란 도모지 없고 세상이 모다 내 뜻대로 될 것 같기만 생각됐읍니다.
그렇다고 해서 머리를 싸매고 골이 빠지도록 공부를 파고들었는가 하면 그러치도 않고 거저 까닭없이 마음이 愉快하고 즐거웠을 뿐입니다.
그때 학교나 기숙사는 몹시 규측이 완고할 뿐으로 선생이나 동무들 사이에 특별히 기록할 만하게 재미있는 일이 없었으나 朝鮮서 건너간 학생이 모다 여듧이 있어서 늘 함께 떼를 지어 놀고 무슨 괴론 일이나 슬픈 일이나 즐거운 일이나 간에 있으면 서로들 얘기하고 위로하고 격려하고 했읍니다. 이러는 사이의 우리 여듧 사람 사이는 어디를 가든지 그림자 모양으로 서로 따르게 되여서 학교 동무들은 우리들을 팔선녀라 불너 주었읍니다.
이 「八仙女」들은 똑같이 크다-란 포부를 가젔읍니다. 옷을 사치하게 입는다든지 공부를 게을니 한다든지 하면 으례 서로들 충고해서 하는 말이 「우리의 뜻을 일우워야 하지 안늬?」하는 것이였읍니다 마는 큰 뜻을 일우지도 못하고 그 동무중 두 사람은 벌서 폐병으로 세상을 떠나 버리고 여섯 사람 남은 것은 시집가고 아이 낳고 살님사는 사람, 학교선생 노릇하는 사람 과부된 사람 또 달니 불행하게 된 사람, 미처서 세상을 모르다가 죽어버린 사람이고 나는 또 이렇게 날마다 학교에서 분주히 지내다가 집에 도라오면 남편의 시중을 들나 아이들 성화 바들나 밤인지 낮인지도 분별할 수 없을 만큼한 복잡 多事한 생활을 하고 있읍니다.
15년 전 그 때-세상이 모다 내 뜻대로 될 줄 알고 즐거워하든 그때-야 이렇게 되리라고 누가 상상인들 했겠읍니까? 영웅을 꿈꾸고 조선에 도라가면 너는 뭘 하고 나는 뭘 하고 안즈나 서나 한데 모이면 이런 얘기들 뿐이였으니까요 얼마나 귀여운 이얘기입니까.
혹 무슨 여자들끼례 모임이 있어도 대부분의 이얘기가 우리의 뜻을 일우자는 것이였읍니다.  朴承天氏 韓小濟氏, 黃信德氏 등도 그 때 시절에 종종 맛나는 이들이였는데 이분들은 우리들보다도 더 과격한 편이여서 회합같은데서 연설을 하게되면 열과 힘을 다한 말세로 테-불을 때려가며 하는 것이 아직 새롭슴니다.
아마 진재 뒤의 일이였든가 바요. 朝鮮사람 追悼會를 東京유학생회에서 하게 되였는데 그때 韓小濟씨가 아주 잘하는 말솜씨로 길게 이얘기하든 것도 지금 기억할 수 있읍니다. 나도 그때 나이는 어렷지만 그분들만 못도지 않은 마음을 가젔든 것만은 사실이였든가바요. 그러치 못하면 그분들의 하는 이얘기에 그리 감격을 하지 않었을 것임니다.
뭐니 뭐니 해야 벌서 지내간 옛일인데 작구만 둘처내여 이야기하면 무슨 소용이 있겠읍니까 마는 그 때 잘 놀고 잘 떠들고 잘 웃고 하든 기억과 한가지로 늘 맛나든 동무들의 기억도 머리에서 사라지지 안습니다.
東亞日報에 게시든 崔義順씨는 한 학교에 같이 있었고 또 八仙女 중의 한 사람이였으니까 더 말할 것 없고, 家政女學校 校長 李淑鍾, 齒科醫師 南壽熙, 梨花女子高等普通學校 李鳳姬, 淑明女子高普에 金縣實, 大邱女高 任淑宰, 趙慶喜 諸氏들은 내* 늘 못 잊어하는 동무들입니다. 아이들이나 다-잠든 저녁같은 때로는 그분들의 그 때 얼골 하나 하나를 생각해보고 마음속으로 혼자 웃는 일로 있지요. 지금은 모다 주름쌀이 잡히고 여위고 한 얼골이나 그때의 그 얼골들은 살진 봉송아처럼 귀엽고 사랑스러웠습니다. ＜36＞
