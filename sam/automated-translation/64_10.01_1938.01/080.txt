Magazine Title: 삼천리 제10권 제1호
Publication Date: 1938년 01월 01일
Article Title: 呂運亨씨는 무엇하고 게시나 (What is Mr. Lyuh Woon-hyung up to?)
Author: Unspecified reporter (기자)
Article Type: 소식 (News/Tidings)

What is Mr. Lyuh Woon-hyung up to?
The Latest News of the Former Newspaper President (Part 1)

It has already been two years since the newspaper *Chungang Ilbo* was suspended, and it remains unpublished to this day. Readers are curious about the fate of the *Chungang Ilbo*, and many, not just this reporter, must want to know what Mr. Lyuh Woon-hyung (呂運亨先生)—who once stirred not only Korea but the wider world with his eloquence like a rushing river (懸河같은 웅변), and who worked tirelessly day and night (不分晝夜) for the survival of the ever-struggling *Chungang Ilbo*—is thinking and how he is spending his days.
Thus, hoping to get a glimpse into President Lyuh's recent life, this reporter braved the fierce northern wind blowing over Pukak Mountain (北岳山) and made his way to the teacher's private residence (私宅) in Kyedong-chong (桂洞町) one morning late last year as the year drew to a close.

Entering the alleyway that winds around the brick wall of Posŏng High School (微文高普) and nestled at the foot of a hill sits a modest semi-Western style building (半洋式건물)—this is the residence of President Lyuh whom the reporter sought.
The teacher, greeting me warmly, appeared as vigorous (元氣) as ever. "The nickname 'eternal youth' truly fits him," the reporter thought, and after a brief greeting, began the professional questioning.

What is your current relationship with the *Chungang Ilbo*?
- I heard a rumor recently that the *Chungang Ilbo*'s publishing rights (版權) were revoked. Is this true?
- Yes, it seems that is the case. But whether the rights were revoked or not, the newspaper is utterly unable to come out.
- Could you elaborate on that?
- What more is there to say? It's just as the world knows. Right after the newspaper's suspension (停刊), I tried very hard, thinking I had to revive the newspaper somehow. But starting this year, seeing the surrounding circumstances, it seemed hopeless, so I finally gave up. Of course, reviving the newspaper was the main focus (主眼), but on the other hand, there was a sense of responsibility for the over 200 employees whose livelihoods (糧道) were suddenly cut off. For the sake of those employees who lost their jobs and were adrift, I wanted to revive the paper and did everything I could. So, last year, I went to the newspaper office daily and frequently met with related people, but from the beginning of this year, I stopped that too. Even now, though I say I've given up, whenever I think of the employees currently wandering the streets (街頭), I always feel depressed.
- By the way, some people seem to criticize you, saying that your influence in the revival movement weakened because you returned all the shares (株券) you received from Mr. Ch'oe Sŏn'ik (崔善益) immediately after resigning as president. Why did you hand over the shares so quickly?
- Ha-ha... Well, since you brought it up, I did hear some criticism or complaints about that from a few young friends, but that's because they don't know the full situation. Why would I have thought of handing over the shares without reason? But at the time of my resignation, Mr. Ch'oe expressed his hope that I would return them, so I had no choice. If holding onto the shares would have actually helped in reviving the paper (續刊), that would be one thing, but since it wouldn't, refusing to return them would have made me look greedy for someone else's property, so what could I do? In fact, whether I returned the shares or not, I still held my position as a director (取締役), so it was never the case that the movement lost strength because I handed over the shares. But anyway, now that even the publishing rights have been revoked, what's the point in talking about it?
- So, is there absolutely no hope for the newspaper to come out now?
- In my opinion, there seems to be no hope for revival at all.
- And you too, of course, have completely severed ties with the newspaper company?
- Yes, it seems to have turned out that way. Now, all I can do is wish in my heart that the former employees who haven't yet found jobs will find them as quickly as possible.
- Let's stop talking about the newspaper company. Now, please tell us a bit about how you are spending your days recently. What do you do to pass the time (소일) daily after leaving the newspaper?
- Nothing special. I occasionally attend meetings of various sports organizations I was involved with before, read a bit, go hiking sometimes, and nowadays, I find great pleasure in listening to the radio at night.

What do you do to pass the time recently?
- What kind of reading do you mainly focus on?
- Given the times, I mostly read books related to the current situation. But I plan to read as many other kinds of books as possible. Actually, I haven't read that many books until now. When I was in China before, the atmosphere was always tense, and I myself led a constantly busy political life, so I rarely had the leisure or peace of mind to read. I really haven't read many books with focus. The time I read comparatively much was <16> when I was in prison after returning to Korea. In terms of the number of books, I probably read more during those three years than all the books I had read up until then combined. And this year, I feel I'm putting quite an effort into reading again. Reading has become such a habit lately that if I don't read for a day, I feel empty, as if I've forgotten something.
- About how many hours a day do you read, and where? Do you often go to the library?
- Well, I don't really go to the library. I read at home whenever I have time, but I usually go to the Maruzen Bookstore (丸善書店) in Honmachi (本町). Due to economic issues, I can't buy every new book, but at Maruzen Bookstore, because I had many dealings with them when I was president of the *Chungang Ilbo*, they treat me with special kindness. So, I borrow a chair there and sit at the storefront, reading whatever books I want for about three or four hours. I usually go around 1 PM and stay until about 5 PM. Mr. Hong (洪君), if you want to meet me next time, you can probably find me there any day.
- Isn't it uncomfortable to stay like that in someone else's shop?
- It is a bit uncomfortable, but I have no choice but to endure it. Just like someone who drinks goes to a standing bar if they have no money, since I have no money, I have no choice but to go to the bookstore and read while standing (다찌요미), so to speak.
- By the way, if you have written any poems or something similar, please give me one.
- Poems? Haha, am I some kind of poet? To write poems...
- Still, isn't your state of mind (心境) these days such that it might inspire poetry?
- Truly, if I had even a little poetic talent (詩才), my current melancholic state of mind would indeed be suitable for composing poems. But I'm utterly inept in that field... When I was in prison before, I managed to compose a few clumsy verses, but now... well...
- You mentioned you often go hiking. Do you enjoy hiking that much?
- Yes, I truly enjoy hiking. Like reading, it wouldn't be an exaggeration to say hiking is my only pleasure (行樂) right now. The moments spent wandering from mountain to mountain with the children, carrying backpacks... it's truly like 'another world, not for ordinary humans' (別有天地非人間). All worldly worries (雜念) disappear, and it's incredibly pleasant. Hiking is a precious way to pass the time (消日) that allows me to forget my gloomy feelings, even if only for a moment.
- Where do you usually go?
- I can't go too far, just around Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul). I mainly hike around Pukak Mountain (北岳山) here and there, and sometimes I've gone as far as Paeg'undae (白雲臺).

Recent days spent with young people
- By the way, what kind of people do you mostly associate with these days?
- Well, there isn't really a specific type, but I mostly meet young people. Most of them are around 30 or younger. People in their 40s or 50s, my age group, I don't meet often unless it's absolutely necessary. When I was involved with the newspaper, I had to associate with older people due to work, but these days, I have almost no contact with the older generation.
- Why is that? Is there a reason you deliberately avoid socializing with older people?
- No, it's not that I consciously avoid them; it's just how my heart naturally leans. Firstly, people in their 40s and 50s just don't understand my thoughts or opinions. Their ideas and everything are outdated. For example, when discussing current affairs (時局談), which is common these days, their opinions are mostly devoid of any principled view on the development and progress of human society or any clear theoretical basis from a policy perspective. Instead, they argue back and forth based solely on their own insignificant personal experiences or temporary calculations of profit and loss (利害打算), so it's frustrating, how can one even have a conversation?
- Then, the reason you primarily associate with young people must be the opposite?
- That's right. Of course, not all young people are like that, especially when the situation is as complex as it is now. However, most of the young people I interact with now are largely people who possess firm convictions of their own, and when thinking about our future problems, many have grand ideals and aspirations (抱負) that are not merely temporary. Though they suffer much mentally and materially because of the times, they are people who possess the spirit (意氣) to somehow overcome these difficulties and move forward, without falling into despair and giving up. When I meet such young people, I somehow feel like I become young again myself. When I meet older people, I feel like I myself become outdated like them.

How do you view the Sino-Japanese Incident and Chiang Kai-shek?
- Ha-ha... You really seem to dislike the older generation. Let's stop there. Regarding the recent Sino-Japanese Incident (日支事變), which has become quite grim, please tell us your thoughts.
- Regarding the Sino-Japanese Incident, I personally think Britain (英國) is entirely responsible. In the end, it's Britain's crafty (老獪) maneuvering (策動). As for Chiang Kai-shek (蔣介石), the suffering he is enduring now can be said to be the punishment (罰責) for betraying the true National Revolution (國民革命) during the Northern Expedition. It seems to have happened like this: Chiang Kai-shek's regime grew entirely under Britain's absolute support (擁護) and its foundation (基礎) solidified to that extent. But once Chiang Kai-shek felt his regime's foundation was somewhat secure, Britain's influence became rather bothersome. So <17> he showed signs of insubordination towards British policy. Seeing this, Britain likely thought, "This won't do," and maneuvered here and there, perhaps instigating this incident. Therefore, from Chiang Kai-shek's perspective, if he hadn't betrayed the National Revolution before and had achieved a true revolution, British influence wouldn't have become so immense, the nation's foundation would have been more solid, he wouldn't be suffering such hardship now, and the innocent masses wouldn't be suffering like this either. It all came to this because Chiang Kai-shek, as an individual, sought to satisfy his own desire for political power (政權慾).
- Do you not feel like going back to China (中國)?
- Why wouldn't I? I think about wanting to go back a lot. But now, rather than for any specific task, it's more out of a desire to go quietly, visit all the places where my footprints (足跡) once were, and reminisce about the past. As you know, my youth, which is my first half-life (前半生), I spent entirely in China. During that time, I briefly visited Russia (露西亞), the Philippines (比律賓), and India (印度), but I spent the longest time in China, so China left the deepest impression and, so to speak, I long for it. In fact, the local scenery and everything feels more familiar and suits my mood even more than Korea (朝鮮). Because of that, I can say I know the situation in China quite well.
- Among the prominent figures active in China now, who do you know personally?
- Well, it's not about naming specific individuals; almost all (擧皆) are my friends. Starting with Chiang Kai-shek (蔣介石) and Wang Ching-wei (汪兆銘), figures like Mao Tse-tung (毛澤東) and the late Hu Han-min (胡漢民) are all people I suffered together with at one time. I was also quite close to Sun Yat-sen (孫文) during his lifetime. Compared to when I first associated with them, they have all, so to speak, achieved success. Among them, I saw with my own eyes how Chiang Kai-shek was dearly loved by Sun Yat-sen during Sun's lifetime.
- What are your feelings now?
- Well, should I say deeply moved (感慨無量)? When I first associated with them, they and I were nothing more than masterless wanderers (浪人). In the meantime, whether they did well or poorly, they all became leaders of a country, while I am now in this state, worrying even about managing a single household...
- Thank you for speaking with me for so long. Lastly, what are your plans for the future?
- Well... I intend to just continue living in this current state. As Dr. Kawakami (河上博士) said, I suppose I have no choice but to spend the rest of my life buried in a corner so as not to become an obstacle to social development. As you know, my first half-life was, from beginning to end, a record of failures. I don't want to repeat failures anymore. From now on, I'll just pass the time leisurely, making reading my hobby.

After finishing this long interview here, the reporter apologized for the intrusion and finally got up to leave. President Lyuh, perhaps also a bit tired, saw me off outside. The reporter's chest felt somehow full, unable to sense the chill of the biting wind, and hurried back towards the company office.
(End) <18>

**Summary**

This 1938 interview captures Lyuh Woon-hyung, the former president of the suspended *Chungang Ilbo*, reflecting on his life after the newspaper's demise. He discusses his unsuccessful attempts to revive the paper, attributing its permanent closure partly to the revocation of its license and his own resignation, though defending his return of company shares. Lyuh describes his current quiet life involving reading (often standing in Maruzen bookstore due to lack of funds), hiking, listening to the radio, and preferring the company of idealistic youth over his own "outdated" generation. He offers a critical analysis of the ongoing Sino-Japanese War, blaming British manipulation and Chiang Kai-shek's past betrayal of the "true National Revolution" for China's predicament. Recalling his past in China and close ties with figures like Sun Yat-sen, Chiang Kai-shek, and Mao Tse-tung, he expresses nostalgia and a sense of personal failure compared to their rise, resolving to live quietly henceforth. From a historical perspective, the article is valuable for Lyuh's candid assessment of the colonial press situation, his critical (and somewhat conspiratorial) view of British influence in China, his personal disillusionment juxtaposed with his nationalist past, and his insights into the pre-communist revolution relationships among key Chinese figures. His preference for youth's "spirit" (의기) over the perceived stagnation of older Koreans also reflects generational tensions and aspirations under colonial rule.

呂運亨씨는 무엇하고 게시나
前新聞社長의 그 뒤 소식(其1)
停刊된지 햇수로 이미 두해, 금일까지 나오지 못하는 신문 中央日報의 뒷 소식도 궁금하거와 한때는 그 懸河같은 웅변으로 조선은 물론 널리는 세상을 떨치엇고 또 늘 빈약한 中央日報의 유지를 위하야 不分晝夜 맹활동을 하시든 呂運亨先生은 그 뒤 매일 무엇을 생각하고 어떻게 소일을 하고 계신지 기자만이 아니라 독자 여러분도 알고 싶은분이 많으리라.
그래 呂社長의 그 동안의 생활상황을 엿볼까 하고 기자는 北岳山 넘어로 불어치는 凜烈한 삭풍을 무릅쓰면서 이해도 저무러 가는 지난 초나흔날 아츰 桂洞町 선생 私宅으로 발을 옴기엇다.

微文高普의 벽돌담을 안고도는 골목길을 들어서 언덕밑에 그리 넓지 못한 자리를 잡은 半洋式건물 이것이 기자가 찾어 뵐려는 呂社長의 저택이다.
반가히 맞어주시는 선생의 자태는 여전히 원기 왕성하시다. 만년 청년의 別號가 정말 적절하구나 하고 기자는 생각하면서 간단한 인사를 여쭌 후 곧 직업적 질문을 시작하였다.

中央日報와는 지금 엇던 관계에 게신가
- 요즘 소문을 들으니 中央日報의 판권이 취소되였다는데 정말입니까?
-녜- 아마 그리된 모양입니다. 그러나 판권이야 취소되든 않되는 신문은 아주 못 나오는 신문이지요.
- 그 말슴을 좀 자세히 해 주섰으면
- 뭐 얘기랄께 별반 딴 얘기가 있켔읍니까. 그저 세상에 전해진 그 정도로 나두 신문 정간 직후에는 어떻게 하든 신문을 다시 살려 볼 마음으로 애도 퍽 써 보았읍니다만 금년을 들어서면서 부터는 주위의 사정이 암만해도 가망이 없을 것 같애 그만 단념을 하고 말었지요. 첫재야 물론 신문을 살려놓는 것이 主眼이나 한편은 당장 糧道가 끊어진 200여명 사원을 위한 책임감도 있고 해서 직을 잃고 헤매는 그들 사원만을 위해서라도 신문을 살려볼까 하고 할 수 있는데 까지는 활동 해 보았읍니다만. 그래 작년 1년은 날마다 신문사에도 나갔고 관계인물들과도 자주 만나보기도 했읍니다만 금년초부터는 그것도 중지하고 말었지요, 그래도 지금도 단념은 했다구 하면서도 현재 街頭에 방황하는 사원들을 생각할때면 늘 우울해지군 합니다.
-그런데 선생님께서 처음 사장취임시에 崔善益씨에게서 맡었는 株券을 사장사임후 곧 전부 반환하섰기 때문에 그후 속간운동에 있어서의 선생의 활동력이 미약해젔다고 비난하는 사람이 더러 있나본대 어찌하야 그렇게 곧 株券을 내놓구 말었읍니까?
