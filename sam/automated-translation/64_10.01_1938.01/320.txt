Magazine Title: 삼천리 제10권 제1호
Publication Date: 1938년 01월 01일
Article Title: 女社長 裵貞子 登場, 東劇의 신춘활약은 엇더할고 (Female President Pae Chŏng-ja Appears, How Will the Tongyanggŭkchang Fare in the New Spring?)
Author: Not specified
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Discussion)

When Mr. Hong Sun-ŏn (洪淳彦), the owner of the Tongyanggŭkchang (東洋劇場), suddenly passed away, there were rumors that his wife, Ms. Pae Ku-ja (裵龜子), would take over the management, or that the management rights would be entirely handed over (*nŏk'e toenda*) to someone else. However, according to accurate reports and inside information, last August, Pae Ku-ja, Hong Sŏk-t'ae (洪錫泰), Pae Chŏng-ja (裵貞子), and Mr. Ch'oe Sang-dŏk (崔象德) gathered a large sum of 100,000 won to form a joint-stock company (合資會社), and Ms. Pae Ku-ja was recommended as the representative member. However, because Ms. Pae Ku-ja is busy touring various regions within Japan proper, she entrusted the position of representative member to her mother, Ms. Pae Chŏng-ja (裵貞子). Therefore, the reporter decided to visit Ms. Pae Chŏng-ja at the Tongyanggŭkchang office one day.

Ms. Pae Chŏng-ja is a stout woman in her 40s who speaks with a Yŏngnam accent. The first impression was quite haughty and blunt, but after sitting and talking for a while, she gave a friendlier impression different from the initial one.

"Ms. Pae Chŏng-ja, have you completely cut ties with this theater?"

"No. Pae Ku-ja is my daughter, and since she is currently busy with entertainment tours (興行) in Japan proper (日本內地), I am essentially filling in for her. When she returns (*torawassŏ*) and takes over the work, I intend to step down (*mullŏsellyŏmnida*) anytime. That's why, although I come here every day like this, I don't know the details well. I only handle general matters concerning obstacles. If you want to know the details, it would be better to speak to the manager (*chibaein*)."

Ms. Pae thus cut off her words and entrusted the conversation to Mr. Ch'oe Sang-dŏk. The reporter had no choice but to start asking questions facing (*majojŏ*) Mr. Ch'oe from then on.

"I heard talk that the Tongyanggŭkchang plans to establish a film studio in the future. Is this true?"

"Yes, it is currently under consideration (*anjung'e issŭmnida*)."

"Then it will happen before long?"

"However, it seems difficult to achieve within this year. We plan to either purchase <44> an existing film company or establish a new one."

"Currently, you are mainly focusing on plays, correct?"

"Yes, that's how it is (*toeyŏ issŭmnida*), but in the near future, we also plan to screen films to serve the general audience at a low price."

"How many theater troupes do you currently own?"

"Right now, we have Ch'ŏngch'unjwa (靑春座) and Hwahwasŏn (豪華船), but in the future, we plan to directly manage the Pae Ku-ja Orchestra (裵龜子樂團) as well."

"Are the plays performed day and night?"

"We used to (*haesŏssnŭnde*) perform day and night, but doing so tired out the actors, so it wasn't working. Therefore, until we start showing films for a while, the theater is often idle during the day. Sometimes, we stage performances like Korean folk dances (*Chosŏn Minyo ch'um*) or Ch'ang'gŭk (*唱劇*) by the Korean Vocal Music Research Society (朝鮮聲樂硏究會)."

"What is the relationship with the Korean Vocal Music Research Society? For instance, do they simply rent the theater?"

"No, we have a truly deep relationship with them. That is, they are an affiliated organization (*paesok kikwan*) of our theater. So, when they perform in Kyŏngsŏng city, they always do so at our theater, and when they go on regional tours, all negotiations are handled through our theater. In other words, the Vocal Music Research Society receives leadership (*ri-dŏ*) from our theater. People like Mr. Yi Tong-baek (李東白), Mr. Song Man-gap (宋萬甲), Mr. Chŏng Chŏng-nyŏl (丁貞烈), and Mr. Kim Ch'ang-nyong (金昌龍) are all elders (*ŏdŭl*) now in their 80s. If they pass away, Korea's unique musical art might disappear along with them. Therefore, our theater tries its best to cherish (*aekkigo*) and popularize this precious and regrettable (*akkawun*) art, often staging ch'ang'gŭk."

"Are there any other businesses you are involved in?"

"Besides that, we intend to stage anything that has entertainment value and is profitable."

"How many employees does the Tongyanggŭkchang have in total?"

"About 150 salary envelopes go out each month, amounting to over 4,500 won."

"How many actors do the Ch'ŏngch'unjwa and Hwahwasŏn troupes have combined?"

"There are about 70 actors in total."

"Ms. Pae Ku-ja is the representative member; what is the reason for that? Wouldn't a man be better (*natji*) suited for managing a theater like this? Especially since she isn't directly managing (*mat'ŏ hajidu*) it now?"

"That's because the person who contributed the most capital (*manhi naen saram*) naturally becomes the representative member. Unless she recommends someone else, it cannot be helped." <45>

**Summary**

The article discusses the management transition at the Tongyanggŭkchang theater following the death of its owner, Hong Sun-ŏn. His wife, Pae Ku-ja, became the representative member of the new joint-stock company formed to run the theater, but due to her touring commitments in Japan, her mother, Pae Chŏng-ja, is acting as the interim head. The theater manager, Ch'oe Sang-dŏk, outlines future plans, including establishing a film studio, showing movies at low prices, and managing the Pae Ku-ja Orchestra alongside existing troupes Ch'ŏngch'unjwa and Hwahwasŏn. The theater also actively supports the preservation of traditional Korean vocal music (ch'ang'gŭk) through its close affiliation with the Korean Vocal Music Research Society, featuring elderly masters of the art. The theater employs around 150 people with a monthly payroll of over 4,500 won. This article is interesting for colonial history as it showcases a woman, Pae Chŏng-ja (albeit temporarily), in a prominent business leadership role within the cultural sector. It also highlights the theater's dual strategy of embracing modern entertainment like film while simultaneously acting as a patron for preserving traditional Korean arts like ch'ang'gŭk, reflecting the complex cultural dynamics of the period.
