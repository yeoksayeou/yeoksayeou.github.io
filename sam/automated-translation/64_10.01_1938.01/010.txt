Memoirs on Various Problems of Chōsen, Roundtable Discussion with Former High Officials of the Government-General

The Maintenance of Public Order at the Time and the Hardships of the Police Authorities Then

Reporter - I imagine the duties of those of you who were responsible for governance after 1919 (Taishō 8) must not have been easy. We can vaguely grasp the situation at that time through newspapers, but hearing detailed accounts directly from those who were actually involved would not only serve as a reference for the future but also become historical material. Therefore, please tell us in as much detail as possible. If there are no sensitive matters, we would appreciate it if you could speak in detail about the revision of the Chōsen Government-General organization system at the time, extending even to personnel matters.

Former Vice Governor-General Mizuno spoke as follows:
In March 1919 (Taishō 8), the Manse Disturbance broke out across the entire peninsula. This was a disturbance where Koreans opposed Japan's rule over Chōsen and hoped for Korea's independence (韓國의 獨立), and because the entire populace, everywhere throughout Chōsen without exception, loudly chanted "Manse," it was called the Manse Disturbance. At that time, it was the Hara Cabinet, and Prime Minister Hara, deeply concerned about this situation in Chōsen, thought that fundamental reforms to Chōsen governance were necessary. Furthermore, as this situation became known in foreign countries, including America, various criticisms regarding Japan's rule over Chōsen arose from abroad, and it was discussed in newspapers and magazines. In particular, Dr. Gulick of America, having friendly relations with Japan, sent a letter to Prime Minister Hara hoping for the reform of Chōsen's administration. Prime Minister Hara replied that he held the same thought and planned to implement reforms sooner or later. At that time, the Cabinet first discussed revising the Chōsen Government-General organization system, subsequently decided it in a Cabinet meeting, and underwent consultation with the Privy Council.
The main points of that revision were:
First. Previously, only an Army or Navy General could become the Governor-General of Chōsen. However, responding to the situation at the time and for the future, it was deemed unnecessary to necessarily appoint a military Governor-General to Chōsen; rather, it was necessary to appoint a suitable person regardless of whether they were a civilian or military official. Based on this principle, the provision in the previous Government-General organization system stating that the Governor-General must be appointed only from Army or Navy Generals was revised, abolishing the restriction on appointment qualifications, thus enabling individuals from civilian backgrounds to also become Governor-General.
Second was the reform of the police system.
Previously, the gendarmerie (헌병) formed the fundamental structure of the police, its chief, the Police Superintendent General (警務總長), was filled by the Commander of the Gendarmerie Stationed in Chōsen, the Provincial Police Chiefs (警務部長) in each province were appointed from Gendarmerie field officers (憲兵佐官) who were the Gendarmerie Chiefs of each province, and Gendarmerie commissioned officers, warrant officers, and non-commissioned officers could be appointed as Police Superintendents (警視) and Police Inspectors (警部).
However, the results (實結) of this system were not favorable, and especially in local offices, because the Provincial Governor (道長官) lacked police authority, cooperation between the Provincial Governor and the police officers below the Provincial Police Chief was often lost, frequently causing hindrances in handling affairs. Therefore, upon this revision, the Police Station Organization System (警察官署官制) was abolished, its central affairs were transferred to the Government-General, a Police Affairs Bureau (警務局) was newly established in the said office, <5> and local police affairs were handed over to each Provincial Governor, deciding to unify police affairs under the Government-General.
Third was the reorganization of the Government-General's departments and bureaus. Previously, the structure had Department Chiefs (部長官) under the Governor-General and Vice Governor-General, with Bureau Chiefs (局長) below them. However, because this structure was complex and hindered the progress of affairs, the Department Chiefs were abolished, and it was decided that Bureau Chiefs would execute the affairs directly. These were the main points of the organizational revision. As this revision was considered most appropriate for the situation at the time, the Privy Council also agreed to it, leading to the promulgation of the revised organization system on August 20, 1919 (Taishō 8).

The Situation Upon Taking Office
Reporter - Mr. Akaike, as the Police Bureau Chief at the time, you undertook the great responsibility of maintaining Chōsen's public order under the most difficult circumstances. Please share your story of hardship regarding the Chōsen situation back then.

Former Police Bureau Chief Akaike spoke as follows:
First, I will speak about the situation when I took office. Since Mr. Mizuno had become Vice Governor-General and was facing the difficult situation of Chōsen governance, I felt I had no choice but to accept the trend of the overall situation and responded to the call (**). Afterwards, personally listening to the opinions of various figures in and out of government in Tōkyō, I became extremely concerned. This was because intellectuals in society were leveling extreme criticism against the previous Governor-General's administration, even making irrational remarks recklessly, and also because one part of the government argued that if the system was reformed and the treatment of Koreans improved, the disturbances would soon be suppressed. Having previously served as Colonial Affairs Section Chief (拓殖課長) and been a member of the Colonial Affairs Committee (拓殖委員會), and having also toured Chōsen and glimpsed an aspect of its governance, I well knew how much our country had strived for the development of Chōsen and the happiness of the Korean people since the time of the Resident-General (統監), and I had seen the actual progress of that course.
Therefore, thinking it was extremely ignorant of the actual situation to say things like being greatly disconcerted by criticism from some quarters - especially foreign criticism - and feeling compelled to apologize to foreign countries for the misconduct (不行屈) of Chōsen governance. This disturbance stemmed from the Koreans' inherent Sadae ideology (事大思想), that is, pro-American sentiment (親米觀念). Unless the root cause was addressed and dealt with appropriately, it would not easily calm down.
Especially since it is human nature to desire independence (獨) after obtaining something (*), and some publicly stated that something could be gained just by causing disturbances, hoping for immediate calm through system reform seemed, in a way, likely to fall into optimism (낙관), I feared (嬚).
Therefore, I thought that the restoration of Chōsen's public order could only be realized once the authority (威信) of the Government-General was established and they gave up their unreasonable desires (非望) before long. Then, while accompanying Vice Governor-General Mizuno, after paying respects at the Great Shrine of Ise and the Momoyama Imperial Mausoleum, when we returned to Kyōto, news arrived that the unstable (不穩) situation in Chōsen was frequent. Unexpectedly (不意여), I conferred with Mr. Nishimura, and the two of us decided to hurry to Chōsen ahead of the Governor-General, departing Kyōto that night.
However, unfortunately, due to a storm, the Shimonoseki-Pusan ferry (關釜連絡船) did not depart, and we were forced to stay at a hotel for a day. Because of this, that morning we had the opportunity to meet former Vice Governor-General Yamagata (山縣前政務總監) who had come from Chōsen, and the new Vice Governor-General Mizuno at the hotel, and receiving very useful insights that night was unexpected good fortune.
The information coming from Chōsen reported a graver situation with each message, indicating perhaps anti-Japanese (排日) intentions or perhaps plotting to assassinate (暗殺) the Governor-General; it was truly chilling (몸사리짓치). At that time, a telegram arrived saying that Police Affairs Bureau Chief Noguchi (野口警務局長) was in critical condition and seemed unlikely to last the night. Thinking that in today's critical situation, the post of Police Affairs Bureau Chief could not be vacant even for a day, I voluntarily offered myself for the position and immediately obtained His Excellency's (각하) permission. That night, the ferry carried the Governor-General's party across the Genkainada (玄海灘), but the rough seas from the previous day had not yet subsided, and the raging waves washed over the ship (怒濤澎湃船을 싯처); it was truly a rare and difficult voyage, seeming to foretell the grimness of the path ahead.
All the police-related personnel gathered on the deck, put their heads together, and discussed countermeasures for Chōsen's public order. One person said this, another said that (甲一語 乙一語); each spoke what they knew and stated their thoughts. As the discussion progressed, everyone keenly felt that the situation's severity was beyond expectation and that the responsibility was extremely grave.
Thus, when the ferry arrived in Pusan, Official Tokinaga (時永事務官) met us and explained the situation in detail.
In short, his words were not greatly different from the telegrams, but hearing directly from him about things like bombs (爆彈) and assassinations (暗殺) evoked a different kind of feeling (감동). As things progressed, what made me feel uneasy was the insufficiency and countless flaws in the preparations (회비). So, Mr. Maruyama and I departed Pusan and arrived in Kyŏngsŏng (京城) the next morning ahead of the Governor-General's party, immediately went to the Government-General building, and consulted with the relevant personnel. Right after the system reform, due to personnel changes (人事異動), very few were settled in (*착하야) and handling affairs. The Governor of Kyŏnggi-do had been newly appointed, and the Honmachi Police Station Chief was primarily responsible for security.
So, Mr. Maruyama exerted tremendous effort (비상한 馬力) in command and supervision, and his hardship (고심) at that time was truly beyond words (現言할수 없섯든).
Thus, according to the system reform, the gendarmerie no longer held responsibility for public order, and the police alone were tasked with it. However, police funding was not allocated at all, and with insufficient personnel, everything was in a state beyond description. Especially preventive policing (豫防警察) was full of flaws, and they didn't even try to seriously investigate rumors of assassination (暗殺). Therefore, we devised the best possible measures, and beyond that, there was no other way but to slowly entrust things to fate. Time flowed by, moment by moment. The Governor-General's train arrived at Kyŏngsŏng Station, and as the Governor-General exited Namdaemun Station (남대문역), a loud explosion roared, shaking heaven and earth (轟然天地를 진동하는 폭음이 파열되였습니다). It was truly a thunderbolt from a clear sky (청천벽력).

Chōsen's instability had become a reality and manifested itself thus. However, the Governor-General's calm and composed (悠悠若若) demeanor at this time indicated that the Governor-General's administration would not be shaken in the slightest by vicious acts (흉행) or violence. At that time, leaving the Governor-General, I checked on the Vice Governor-General's safety (無事) and barely managed to stand at the scene. Next, I stopped by the Governor-General's official residence (관저) and returned to the Government-General building. Then, as twilight deepened (暮色蒼然), the surroundings grew dark. But the electric lights did not turn on. It seemed that lighting had been prohibited until then. So, having no choice, we lit candles (燭불). At this time, feeling the need not only to report this incident (椿事) to the government offices but also to announce it widely to the public and inform them of the atmosphere in Chōsen, I immediately took up my brush and wrote the full story (眞末), and Mr. Maruyama telegraphed this to all government offices as well as newspapers in Tōkyō and Ōsaka. This was the incident that occurred on the first day of arrival in Chōsen. Sitting in an empty (탕비인) room with four or five people, lighting candles late at night (심야), and striving for public order is an impression unforgettable even today. At that time, Official Tokinaga, feeling deep responsibility for the bomb explosion (爆彈破裂), forgot to eat and sleep (寢食을 잇고) while striving for the arrest (捕縛). Whenever I recall him, I cannot help but express my respect for his strong sense of responsibility.

Public Sentiment Stabilization and Relief Measures (民心安全 求療施設)
The bombing incident was unexpected if you call it unexpected, and expected if you call it expected. Due to this, morale became tense. That is, there was no choice but to take a last stand (배수의 진) and charge forward (突進) with the determination to fight to the death (결사의 각오). Now, whether the new administration (新政) would succeed or fail attracted not only the attention of Koreans but also the attention of the world<6>. If unfortunately it failed, rash (經率한) people might deliver the harsh criticism (혹평) that Japan lacked the ability to govern Chōsen, whether with a military or civilian official. Therefore, the newly appointed officials had to make utmost effort (粉骨碎身) out of responsibility to the nation and absolutely strive for success.
They had to vow (맹서하지 않을수 없섯습니다) success. Therefore, somehow (엇지 하든), they had no choice but to focus their efforts (주력하지 않을 수 없섯든 것입니다) on maintaining public order and stabilizing public sentiment. At this time, Koreans held suspicions (의혹) about the Governor-General's administration but did not trust it at all. Even good (선량한) people did not go beyond sitting and watching the outcome (성패를 보는). Malcontents (不逞者), of course, hoped for the failure of the new administration, so they engaged in various maneuvers (策動).
When lacking authority (威信), even if orders were given, there was no reason for them to be obeyed, and even if forced (강제한 댓자), it would only suddenly increase resistance (반항). If propaganda (宣傳) was used, it would likely become nothing more than a topic of tea conversation (茶話의 화제). Therefore, the urgent task (焦眉의 時務) was solely this: braving hundreds of difficulties (百難을 무릇쓰고) to establish the police, thereby suppressing reckless actions (맹동), restoring order (질서), and letting people know the direction towards peace and stability (안주의 歸向).
It is clear (확연한) that once authority (威信) is gained and the inclination towards stability (歸向) becomes firm, not only industrial development (殖産) and education (교육) but all administrative affairs (庶政) will proceed smoothly (順調로). However, if efforts were focused (*) solely on the police at this time, public criticism (物議) and censure (비난) would inevitably arise frequently (百出). But since this was originally an expedient measure (便宜의 처치) taken in response to the crisis (變), we would push forward single-mindedly (一路邁進할 것이입니다), paying no heed (귀를 기울니지 않고) to any kind of attack (여하한 공격) until everything went as intended (뜻대로 될 때까지는). Especially since the new administration had the revision of the gendarmerie system as one of its main principles (要綱), if the police could not maintain public order, that itself would be the failure of the new administration. In this sense, establishing the police was most crucial at this time and could not be delayed (緩延할수 없는 것입니다) for even a moment. Seeking a suitable person (적임자) for Police Affairs Bureau Chief from the mainland (內地) might have been reasonable, but thinking it undesirable (滋味없다고) to have the responsible person absent (缺한다는) even for a moment in such a crisis (위급), disregarding (不願하고) my unworthiness and lack of talent (不肖不才), I volunteered (自進) to become the Police Affairs Bureau Chief. Accordingly, I requested 500 additional police officers and immediately received permission. That was September 3, 1919 (大正 8년 9월 3일). At that time, Chōsen's instability (不穩) was tremendously propagandized.
The same was true within the police. To give examples: first, within Kyŏngsŏng, most shops had closed their doors (문을다덧든). This is, according to Chōsen custom, an expression of protest (抗議) against the administration. There were prisoners (囚徒) shouting Manse inside prisons (XX내에서 - *likely 감옥내*). Interactions (왕래) between Japanese and Koreans (內鮮人) completely ceased (두절되야), and even those who were previously close (친밀하든) had no news (소식). And Koreans taking a hostile (敵對的) attitude towards mainlanders (內地人) increased greatly. In the provinces (지방), those demanding the withdrawal (撤收) of mainlanders and taking violent (XX의) actions became extremely numerous. Not a few trusted the Provisional Government (假政府) in Shanghai (上海) and hoped for its success. 1. Belief (念) strongly trusting in things like national self-determination (民族自決), Korean independence (朝鮮獨立), Korean autonomy (朝鮮自治), etc., was strong, and among Christian believers, those frequenting the churches (회당) of the American Presbyterian Church (米國長老派) increased markedly (顯著히). Various rumors (풍설) flowed out from those centers (其處). Thus, throughout the entire peninsula (全鮮), reverence (尊崇) for America and trust in Americans were indescribable, absolutely impossible to judge (律할수 없섯든 것입니다) by today's common sense. Especially since the theory of national self-determination was advocated by American President Wilson (우윌손), they believed it would surely be implemented through America's power. Also, because America considers the liberation (해방) of nations its heavenly duty (천직), they propagandized that America showed more than enough (十二分) goodwill (호의) towards the independence (XX) movement. In extreme cases, some believed Wilson would come to Chōsen (來鮮) because of this and were preparing to welcome (환영할) him. Contrary to this, doubt (의혹) regarding our (我) national power and disregard (輕視) for the Government-General were beyond words. Truly, the Government-General's administration faced a major crisis (일대위기).
On top of this grim situation, North Chōsen (北鮮) suffered an unprecedented drought (旱災), causing many to wander in starvation (기아), and cholera (코레라) also spread, with deaths piling up (死者累累). Due to traditional superstitions (慣信), there were cases where cholera patients were simply abandoned (遺棄) and people fled (도주), creating an appalling (언어도단) tragic situation (참상).
Furthermore, encountering a great storm (대폭풍우), the first in decades, gave the feeling that a hundred disasters (百災) had struck all at once (일시에). Treating all patients afflicted by such epidemics is the duty of the administrator (위정자), so saying it could not be delayed even a day, on August 3rd (*error in original text, likely September 3rd or later based on Akaike's arrival*), the Vice Governor-General invited the Government-General Hospital Director, Dr. Haga (芳賀博士), to the official residence, held a meeting regarding relevant management (당해 관리) and disease prevention (防病), and as a result, urgently finalized a policy (방침), allocated a budget (예산), and initiated vigorous relief activities (極力 求療에 맹활동).
This relief effort (求療濟) yielded great results (효과), popular sentiment (民情) welcoming the Governor-General's administration gradually arose (漸起되야), and public sentiment also gradually calmed down (온화되엿든 것입니다).

Police Reinforcement and Concentration (警察의 充實과 集合)
At the time of the police system's establishment (*度創設), its incompleteness and imperfection (不備不完) were beyond description.
For example, although there were said to be 10,500 police officers (경관), half of them were Korean constables (조선인 순사). Amidst the agitation of public sentiment (인심동요), they developed suspicion/doubt (*감심), and could not be trusted (안심 할 수 없섯든 것입니다) under any circumstances. Their performance (근무) was also not precise (정확하지 못하엿든 것입니다). Also, the 3,000 mainland Japanese police officers were the only ones available (일휴뿐이니), so new recruitment became necessary. However, as it happened suddenly (돌연이), not only did we lack any kind of plan (**) for recruitment, but there were also no preparations and no budget (예산).
On top of that (우에), as reports (소래) of Chōsen's instability grew louder, even those who had said they would apply submitted cancellations (취소를 申込하는 형세이니), making it completely unpredictable when the required personnel (요원) could be replenished (보충할 수 잇슬지). Meanwhile, the situation (시국) grew grimmer day by day (날노 날노), and the need for police officers became more urgent (適切하야) moment by moment. Kyŏnggi-do and the other 13 provinces (道) all pleaded urgency, and requests (催促) demanding police deployment (배치) came like arrows (화쌀갓하엿든 것입니다). The Police Affairs Bureau was preoccupied with responding to these demands, but there were simply no police officers (도대체 경관이 없섯든 것입니다). So, there was nothing that could be done (엇더케 할 수가 없섯습니다). After considering every possible way (百方苦慮하야) and earnestly requesting (간청하야) various gentlemen (제씨에게) on the mainland, we finally obtained applicants, but there were no uniforms (被服) or swords (패검) to give them.
So, they were made to work in plain clothes (평복), making it impossible to identify them as police officers at a glance (일견 경찰관인 것을 식별할 수 없섯든 것입니다). The appearance (體載) was also not good (조치 못하엿든 것입니다). Some (**) spoke of the police's powerlessness and incompetence (무력무능) because of this, or criticized the lack of discipline (불규율); it was truly pandemonium (百鬼夜行否百鬼*行).
Many said things could not go on like this and spoke of the police's failure. What was most distressing (심통한) for the authorities was that the Korean constables, who constituted about half of the Chōsen police force, mostly just watched the situation and did nothing (정세를 보고 안할 뿐 아니라), and in severe cases, colluded with (통모하고) malcontents (不逞의 徒) and either watched their actions idly (방관) or aided (원조하는) them. This was because they were misled by propaganda (宣傳에 의혹되야) and pessimistic (비관하엿든) about the future prospects (前途) of the administration.
Therefore, even if there were mainland Japanese police officers, it was like walking without a guide (안내자 없시 길걷는 격으로), and they could not fully exercise their capabilities (기능을 발휘 할 수 없섯든 것입니다). In a word (一言), the police at that time were in a state of hemiplegia (半身不隨). Because the situation was as described, the urgent task (시무의 급한 것) was, for the sake of the Government-General's authority (威信), to rapidly reinforce (충실을 급히 할것) the police and restore police functions to their normal state (평상시 상태에 회복 할 것이엿습니다). Doing this one day earlier brought a day's benefit (利), and delaying it one day caused that much harm (害). Therefore, the police authorities agonized day and night (日夜肝膽을 태우며), forgetting to eat and sleep (寢食을 잇고) while handling affairs (*掌하야), managing only 3-4 hours of sleep (睡眼) over 2-3 months. However, since perfection (만전) could not be ensured, rather than a policy of preparing for emergencies (急에 備하는 방침), we first adopted a principle of concentration (集中主義). We concentrated all efforts (전력) on Kyŏngsŏng, striving to prevent bombings or other disturbances of public order there. Next, we focused efforts on the provincial capitals (도청소재지), and decided to leave other places alone (방임하기로) even if there were unstable incidents.
However, along with the reinforcement of police officers, even when preparing (예비할때에도) for the provinces, we also adopted the concentration principle, having 3 to 5 officers work together so they could take emergency measures (응급조치) if a critical incident occurred. The police came under suspicion of being incompetent because the police substation system (주재소제도), deploying single officers in localities, exposed (暴露하엿기 때문에) their complete inability to resist violence. Fortunately (當幸히), blessed by Heaven's help (天佑), the police were reinforced in a shorter time than expected, the instability was removed surprisingly quickly, the plan progressed steadily (착착), and we were able to fulfill the duty of security (경비의 任) without major breakdown (破綻). We saw this as what a miracle (기적) points to (가라침이라고). Thus, we could not help but be grateful for the protection of the gods (天佑神明의 加護). However, we must not forget (이저서는 않될 것입니다) that Heaven's help was not obtained by chance but because all the authorities concerned worked together with heart and strength.

Third Department Chiefs and Security Maintenance (第3部長 治安維持)
The Government-General, waiting for the arrival of the Third Department Chiefs (제3부장 - Provincial Police Chiefs), hastily convened the Third Department Chiefs' meeting even though security arrangements were not yet organized (정리되지 않음에도 불구하고). While preparing for the meeting, everyone keenly realized (절감하야) the gravity of their responsibility and resolved (각오하엿든 것입니다) to fulfill their duties (직책) desperately (결사적으로). However, the Third Department Chiefs at that time were all young (年少하고) and had newly come to Chōsen, so they were not at all familiar (통하지 못하엿든 것입니다) with the Chōsen situation. Public opinion (여론) largely held that it would be impossible to overcome (타개하야 나갈 수 없겟다는) this difficult situation this way. Upon hearing this (듯자), driven by manly spirit (남자의 의기), they developed the feeling (기분) <7><Continued on page 45> that they absolutely had to produce results (성적을 내어) and restore public order (치안을 회복하지 않으면 안되겟다는). Suddenly becoming tense (突然緊張하야저), their morale became high (사기왕성하엿든 것입니다).
Every one of the Third Department Chiefs took upon themselves (自任하야) the maintenance of public order not just for their own province (도의 치안) but for all of Chōsen (朝鮮 전도), reaching the point where there was no one who did not approach it as a national duty (국가로써 任하지 않은 자 없서슴에 至하엿든 것입니다). Therefore, each word was more scathing (통렬히) than the last, and every single word and phrase (言言句句) boiled up from their innermost feelings (肺肝으로부터 끄러오르는 것이엿끼 때문에), so there was not even a fragment of speech (片言隻語) that did not touch the heartstrings (琴線을 촉진하지 않는 것이 없섯습니다).
Especially since they were resolved to die (죽엄을 각오하고) and did not expect a long life (일생을 期하지 않는 것이니), their heroic splendor (장렬함) was beyond words, truly enough to make ghosts weep (귀신을 울닐만하엿든 것입니다). A meeting so full of pure feeling (순정) and earnestness (眞劍하엿슴) was a sight never seen before.
Thus, when the meeting ended (끈나슬 때), a firm (**) resolve shone between the eyebrows (眉字의 사이에) of the provincial chiefs, and their faces shone (얼골은 빛낫든 것입니다), full of solid (張固) conviction (신념).

**Summary**

This roundtable article features former high-ranking Japanese officials of the Chōsen Government-General, primarily Vice Governor-General Mizuno Rentarō and Police Affairs Bureau Chief Akaike Atsushi, reflecting on the period immediately following the March 1st Movement in 1919. They discuss the political and security crisis, the rationale behind administrative reforms (allowing a civilian Governor-General, centralizing police under a new bureau replacing the gendarmerie system), and the extreme difficulties faced in restoring order. Akaike details the chaotic arrival, the bombing targeting the new Governor-General, the severe lack of police resources and personnel, the unreliability attributed to Korean officers, compounding crises like famine and cholera, and the strategy of concentrating police power in key areas while acknowledging the initial neglect of others. The officials emphasize the need to restore the Government-General's authority (威信) above all else, justifying the focus on police power despite potential criticism, and portray their eventual success in stabilizing the situation as a near-miraculous outcome achieved through desperate effort and "Heaven's help."

From a Korean colonial history perspective, the article is revealing for its candid portrayal of the Japanese administrative viewpoint. It shows their interpretation of Korean resistance as stemming from "Sadae ideology" and misguided "pro-American sentiment," their deep sense of crisis and concern for Japan's international reputation following the March 1st Movement, and their justification for prioritizing police control and authority restoration as the prerequisite for any other governance. Historians might find Akaike's detailed account of the practical challenges (resource shortages, unreliable subordinates, simultaneous natural disasters) and the psychological state of the officials (desperation, determination, self-perceived righteousness) particularly interesting as a primary source illustrating the colonial power's mindset and operational difficulties during a critical period.

記者 - 大正 8년 이후에 통치의 任에 당한 여러분들의 임무는 용이한 것이 아니엿스리라고 생각합니다. 그 당시의 일은 신문지상을 통하야 어렴푸시 알수 있사오나 실제 그 局에 當한

大正 8년 3월 全鮮的으로 萬歲騷動이 이러낫습니다. 이것으로 朝鮮人이 日本의 朝鮮統治에 대하야 반대하고 韓國의 XX을 희망하는 騷擾로 全朝鮮 어디나 어느곳 할 것 없시 전 민중이 만세를 高唱하얏끼 때문에 萬歲騷動이라고 하였든 것입니다. 그 당시 原內閣이였는데 原首相은 朝鮮에 잇서서의 이 상태를 깊이 우려하야 朝鮮統治에 근본적개혁을 가하지 않으면 안되겟다고 생각하엿든 것입니다. 또 이 情勢가 아메리카를 위시하야 외국에 알녀젔기 때문에 諸外國서는 日本의 朝鮮統治에 관하야 여러 가지비난을 하고 신문잡지에도 논의되엿든 것입니다. 특히 아메리카의 규-릭博士는 日本과 친교있는 관계로 原首相에게 朝鮮政治의 개혁을 희망하는 서면을 보내엿든것입니다. 原首相은 이에 대하야 자기도 동일한 생각을 갓고 잇스니 조만간 개혁을 할 작정이라고 회답하엿습니다. 그때 內閣에 잇서서는 우선 朝鮮總督府官制를 개정할 것을 의논하고 이어서 閣議에서 결정하야 樞密院의 諮詢을 經하게 되엿습니다.
그 개정의 요점은
제1. 朝鮮總督은 종래 陸海軍大將이 아니면 될 수 없섯는데 당시 또는 금후의 정세에 응하야 꼭 武官總督으로써 朝鮮에 임할 필요없시 文官武官을 불문하고 적임자를 總督으로 함이 필요하다는 취지하에 종래 總督府官制에 總督은 陸海軍將軍만으로서 식힌다는 규정을 개정하야 임용자격의 제한을 철폐하고 文官出身者라도 역시 總督이 될 수 잇게하엿든 것입니다.
제2는 警察制度의 개혁입니다.
종래는 헌병으로써 경찰의 근본조직으로하야 그 장관인 警務總長은 朝鮮駐在憲兵司令官으로써 이에 충당케 하고 각 도의 警務部長은 각 도 憲兵長인 憲兵佐官으로써 이에 임명하고 憲兵將校 及 淮士官下士는 警視 及 警部로 임용될수 잇게 되엿습니다.
그리한 것이 이 제도는 實結이 양호하지 못하고 특히 지방청에 잇서서는 道長官에게는 경찰권이 없기 때문에 道長官과 警務部長 이하의 경관과의 사이에 왕왕 협조를 일허 처무상의 지장을 이르키는 일이 많엇든 것입니다. 그래 금번의 개정에 際하야 警察官暑官制를 폐하고 그 중앙 사무는 總督府에 이임하야 同府에 警務局을 신설하고＜5＞ 지방경무사무는 각 道 長官에게 인계하야 總督府 하에 경찰사무를 통일하기로 하엿든 것입니다.
제3은 總督府部局變更을 하엿습니다. 종래는 總督政務總監下에 각 部長官이 잇서 그 아래에 局長이 잇기로 한 조직이엿는대 이 조직은 복잡하고 사무의 진보를 방해하는 실정이 잇섯기 때문에 部長官을 폐하고 각 국장으로써 사무를 집행하기로 하엿든 것입니다. 이것이 官制改正의 주요한 점이엿습니다. 이 개정은 당시의 실정에 가장 적당하다고 하엿기 때문에 樞密院도 이에 동의하야 大正 8년 8월 20일에 改定官制의 발포를 봄에 至하엿든 것입니다.
