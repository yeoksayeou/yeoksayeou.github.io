Artist of the Streets
						Grass Private (草兵丁)
"What do Westerners say in criticism of your dancing, Miss?"
"In Seoul, there's an international club organized by people like the British and American consuls and other Westerners, called the Rotary Club (로-타리 구라부). They asked me once to come and dance. It was probably for some general meeting..."<56>
"And so?"
"So I danced, and whether it was just empty compliments, I don't know, but they welcomed me greatly, saying I danced very well. The person who was the president, whatever his name was, even encouraged me to advance further in the field of dance."
"That's understandable—so, do Westerners give generous payments?"
"What do you mean? They're rather more stingy. Isn't that usually the case?"
"How about expanding your dance studio, Miss, and starting a movement to express valuable old folk arts like Arirang or Banga Taryeong as dance?"
"Well... I wish I could at least build an eight or nine-pyeong barracks-style house in the woods of Sindang-ni (新堂里), but even building something like that requires about 700 won. But I need the money. If I just had the building, the occasional costs could be covered by performances in spring and autumn, and I would also devote myself fully to the movement to artistically render folk songs."
"Now that your reputation is so high, you mean there's no one who would throw even 700 or 800 won your way?"
"If there were, I suppose there might be. But such people should be giving it to properly nurture the 'art,' specifically 'dance.' Those kinds of people usually have other motives. I don't like it."
"In that case, as a means to raise funds, how about going to perform somewhere like Onjŏng-ni (溫井里) in the Kŭmgang Mountains (금강산), where many foreign bourgeoisie (뿌르조아들) gather in the summer? How about creating a fund for the dance studio that way?"
"It probably wouldn't work. Those bourgeoisie (뿌르조아들) just want to play around with 'ero' (eroticism), where would they...?"
And her face flushes slightly. (Remainder omitted - original text)

On the Chogŭk Stage, Ms. Pae Ku-ja (裴龜子女史)
"You said you were setting out on a dance pilgrimage to London (론돈) and Paris (파리), so why did you come back to Chosŏn? Did you miss Chosŏn that much?"
"......"
"Seeing that dance called 'Arirang,' imbued with the mystique of quietly walking out to the well (움물가) with camellia oil on your hair, carrying a water jar, gazing up at the half-moon hanging in the tree branches—the blood of this land's many youths raced. That's right, if you are a Chosŏn woman, you should dance Chosŏn dances. Watching those Western-style dances (양코백이 딴스), jumping about wildly, looks vulgar."
"......"
"Do you read many literary books, Miss? Novels are good, poetry is good too. You should read many of the newly published works. Even if you don't have time to read Western ones because you lack the opportunity, you should always read Chosŏn ones. All artists lacking literary cultivation—for example, musicians, painters (그림장), or those engaged in dance—look quite shallow, I find."
"......"
"Don't you feel the passion and impulse to go to places like the Left Wing Theatre (좌익극장) and take on roles like the mother in works such as Gorky's (꼴끼-) 'Mother'? I'm asking if you lack the passion and impulse of a stage artist."
"......"
"You kept saying 'Humoresque, Humoresque'... It seems you've really become a humoresque yourself."
Still, not a single word of reply escaped the lips of dancer Ms. Pae Ku-ja (딴서- 배구자씨). That's how it is; as I sit in the audience of the Chosŏn Theatre (조선극장) and she dances on the stage, how could there possibly be an answer to these questions I ask in my heart! <57>

**Summary**

This article presents two perspectives on female dancers in 1931 Chosŏn. The first part is an interview with an unnamed dancer who discusses the financial hardships of pursuing traditional Korean dance, her desire for an independent studio, and her refusal of patronage tied to "other motives." She expresses skepticism about performing for potentially lewd foreign "bourgeoisie" even to raise funds. The second part is the author's reflection while watching the famous dancer Pae Ku-ja perform. The author silently questions Pae's return from Europe, critiques her perceived preference for Western dance over Korean forms, suggests she lacks literary depth, and wonders if she has the passion for socially engaged theatre, like the Left Wing Theatre, ultimately comparing her silent performance to a "humoresque." For historians, the article offers insights into the economic struggles of artists, the tension between preserving traditional Korean culture (folk songs, dance) and embracing Western influences, the moral dilemmas faced by female performers seeking patronage, the presence of class critique ("bourgeoisie") and leftist cultural movements ("Left Wing Theatre"), and contemporary criticism of prominent figures like Pae Ku-ja regarding artistic direction and national identity.
