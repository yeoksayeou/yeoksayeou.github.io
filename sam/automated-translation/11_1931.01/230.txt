上海의 新春
林元根

Unlike other days, this morning (December 31, 19XX) I woke up a bit early.
Taking a holed *yŏpchŏn* coin, I went out and bought a basin of hot water (cash)—washed my face, and then simply made do with a 7-chŏn loaf of bread for breakfast. Though time was a bit early, for some reason this morning, I really didn't feel like trudging over to the eatery. I disliked it almost to the point of feeling unwell.

After washing my face, I considered going out, but then, unable to overcome my inner feelings, I suddenly got angry and just sat down—damn it, is this cursed fate of mine always going to be like this day and night? Last year or this year, still unable to establish even one warm home or family, drifting abroad in a foreign land, always like this. Ugh, this wretched situation. When will this humiliating life ever end?—Such thoughts came to mind just now.

The clamor of the noisy streets, starting early this morning, grew even louder. The sounds of trams and horse carriages running, the sounds of freight carts and peddlers hawking cheap goods, the hurried steps of passersby—all these things heralded the New Year tomorrow morning and spoke of today, the last day of the year.

Around ten in the morning, I left the house heading for Jessfield Park. Forsaking the French Park, which could almost be called our outdoor meeting place, I specifically chose Jessfield because I wanted to find a quieter place like that to fully savor and feel the sentiments of bidding farewell to the old year.

Inside the spacious park, human figures were extremely scarce. It wouldn't be an exaggeration to say it was less than a third of the usual crowd. I settled myself on a bench in a corner. In such situations, almost as if by second nature, I took out a cigarette from my overcoat pocket and lit it. My surroundings were extremely quiet.

My mind filled with memories of the old days. Then, the present and future, private life and public life, myself and our future destiny, resurrection, ruin... all these tangible and intangible illusions raced through my mind like a panorama, coming and going.

Looking back, it feels like a dream that three years have already passed since I left my beloved hometown and set foot on this perilous path. While it's true that the feeling of time flowing like water is always more acute at the year's end and beginning, it seems like such shortened days await us as we walk the path of young life.

Yes—three years ago—that night was a fiercely cold winter night with a blizzard raging. I roughly <54> gathered only the simple necessities of daily life into my luggage and resolutely set off on the journey to XX. My old mother and young siblings, hearing that I wouldn't be returning home for a long time, initially tried forcefully to detain me, but later, realizing my determination was unshakable, helplessly let me go with sorrowful tears. The earnest plea of an old mother for her young son, setting off on an aimless path over mountains and across seas, was only to pray day and night for unchanging health and unending news. How could such sincere pleas end only with those of my mother and siblings? From trusted comrades with whom I had firmly pledged my life and from ordinary friends I met daily, there were also the same requests.

Since then, during the three years that have passed, there have indeed been many bitter experiences and rough waves in this humble life of mine in exile. Although today, on this day of this year, I happen to be seeing out the old and welcoming the new year here in Shanghai, in the meantime, my footsteps, without any particular gain, have wandered aimlessly like rootless duckweed floating on water, sometimes to Chungnyŏng, sometimes across the Siberian plains. Where will this humble body stop its steps on this day next year? And again, in the coming year, will "I," people called Koreans, continue this life just like today? As the days and months go by, it is an undeniable, stark fact that our (Omitted - original text) and in direct proportion, our society becomes ever more (Omitted - original text).

"Dear brothers and sisters in my hometown! Mother! Sisters! The year has already drawn to a close. The year 19XX closes its curtains with this single day, New Year's Eve. Having left the embrace of the nostalgic and beloved old home, I am spending the old year and greeting the new in a lonely guest window in a foreign land ten thousand *li* away. Though usually indifferent, when faced with such an occasion, a myriad of sentiments yearning for my hometown hills well up uncontrollably like a spring. I am now sitting on a bench in a corner of a spacious foreigners' park. Over there on the street beyond the park, cute children dressed in 'New Year's clothes' are led by their mothers and fathers, running here and there in groups. From the kitchens of nearby houses, the smell of rich cooking for the holiday wafts on the wind, piercing the nose of a hungry person. In this foreign land, abundant hope and sweet happiness, accompanied by joyous laughter, drift through the entire city to greet the new dawn of tomorrow's New Year.

Dear brothers and sisters! But as for me—only our pitiful group—we weep ever more in solitude, weep in annihilation, weep in misfortune. I do not know, but I believe that you, brothers and sisters in our hometown, probably have suffering and sorrow no less than ours. Brothers and sisters! What New Year's greeting could there be for XX people like us? Whether in the old year or the new year, carrying on this life is still the same.

However, brothers and sisters, let us all remember! We must strive only to combine and unite all the strength we possess, to forge our path ahead even more resolutely. On this last day of 19XX, we must wash away and let flow all the misfortunes and sorrows of the past year, whether small or large, accomplished or not, and greet the New Year with more carefully prepared new plans and new hopes.

Brothers and sisters! If your pockets permit, brew delicious food and alcohol, and eat and drink your fill! And if you wish to cry, embrace each other and cry to your heart's content until dawn! We lonely brothers wandering here will also try to create an opportunity tonight to cry and laugh, enjoying ourselves to the fullest. Here, at least, we have the freedom to cry at the top of our lungs as much as we want. Wishing new happiness for all the brothers and sisters in our hometown..."

Today is January 1st— Thanks to drinking more alcohol than I could handle with several colleagues at XX-ru on Sama-ro until past midnight last night, my nervous head became extremely agitated, and I overslept this morning, only opening my eyes when it was nearly noon. Looking at the antique clock placed on the bedside table, its short hand unerringly pointed to 11:30 AM.

My eyes wide open, I remained lying on the bed, lost in thought for some time. (Continued on page 53)—<55>

The thick rays of the New Year's first day shone directly into the south-facing window, warming the air of the gloomy room. Today's weather was indeed splendidly clear, rarely seen recently. In the clear sky, there wasn't a speck of cloud nor a gust of wind, and beyond the horizon, even spring-day hazes shimmered under the purple-golden sunlight.

On this day when others were jumping for joy, I didn't wash my face, skipped breakfast, and remained lying in the chilly bed until evening approached. Everything felt bothersome. So I skipped breakfast and didn't wash up during the day. In truth, my life is utterly irregular.

Some days I wake up and bustle about from the early dawn around five or six, and other days, just like today, I don't eat and just lie sprawled out all day long. When my pockets were empty, I skipped meals for several days at a time, and when I had plenty of money, I chose quite splendid dishes to eat. Sometime last autumn, while gazing at the moon reflected in the south window with friend X, who is now in Europe, I felt so nostalgic for the mountains and rivers of my homeland and so mournful about our situation that we embraced each other and wept aloud all night. Gazing at the clear moon hanging mid-sky, we sang tear-soaked songs of longing for home. Then we also sang solemn XX songs.

On those nights when the quiet moonlight secretly seeped in below the south window and the sound of indifferent insects created the orchestral music of an autumn night, the lonely wooden pillow of the XX person was always soaked with its owner's tears.

From around 2 PM, fluttering snowflakes began to fall, making my somber mood even darker. From the street, various sounds symbolizing the joy of the New Year—firecrackers, laughter—could be heard incessantly. But I remained lying alone all day on a bed in a corner of a gloomy foreigner's rented room. Only for today, this form of life suited me.

January 3rd— Today, starting from noon, there was our meeting at XX-kwan on XX Street. Many representatives of X peoples gathered there, almost like an international gathering. (Middle part omitted - original text)

The spirits of the various colleagues were very vigorous and brave. Among them, the impassioned, fervent speech of a young Turkish woman deeply moved everyone. Moreover, I was almost captivated by that woman. Although our common language was English, she was particularly fluent.

This day's meeting was indeed extremely pleasant. Before dispersing, we devoted about an hour to entertainment, enjoying ourselves together interestingly. (Latter part omitted - original text)<53>

**Summary**

Writing from Shanghai on New Year's Eve 1930 and New Year's Day 1931, the author, Im Wŏn'gŭn, describes his feelings of loneliness, poverty, and despair as a Korean exile. He contrasts his bleak existence—skipping meals, living in a cold rented room, reflecting on three years of aimless wandering since leaving home—with the festive atmosphere outside. He expresses deep sorrow for his plight and that of his fellow Koreans ("brothers and sisters"), both in exile and back home. Despite the gloom, he calls for unity, renewed hope, and resolute effort for the future. He briefly mentions attending a multi-ethnic meeting on January 3rd, finding inspiration in the participants' spirit, particularly a Turkish woman's speech. The article offers a poignant glimpse into the emotional and material hardships faced by Korean exiles during the colonial period. Historians might find interest in the raw portrayal of exile life, the expressions of nationalist sentiment (veiled by "XX"), the sense of solidarity among displaced Koreans, and the mention of international connections with other marginalized or anti-colonial groups (indicated by "X族" and the Turkish woman) in Shanghai, a hub for such activities. The use of "XX" and omissions suggests self-censorship or external censorship regarding politically sensitive topics like the independence movement.

上海의 新春
						林元根
오늘 아츰(19XX년 12월 31일)에는 例와 달리 좀 일즉이 이러낫다.
구멍뚤닌 葉錢 한 푼을 들고 나가서(캐슈)―더운물―한 박아지를 사다가 얼골을 씻고 나서는 간단히 7전짜리 食빵 한 개로서 아참을 치르고 마랏다. 시간이 좀 일는 관계도 잇지마는 오늘 아참에 이르러서는 엇전 일인지 덜넝덜넝 밥집까지 달려가는 것이 몹시 실혓던 까닭이다. 거의 불쾌를 늣길만치 실혓다.
세수를 마치고 나서 나가 볼가 하다가는 나는 고만 내심 술을 못 이겨서 화를 더럭 내고 주저 안저 바렷다―이런 제미 그저 이 놈의 八字는 밤낫 이 모양이람. 작년이나 금년이나 그저 집 한間 家庭하나도 따듯하게 이루지 못하고 風土다른 海外로 流離하면서 늘 이모양이람. 에이 이놈의 身勢. 이놈의 屈辱生活은 어느 날에나 끗이 날 모양인가― 이가튼 생각이 今時로 머리 속에 떠올낫섯다.
요란한 거리의 소음은 오늘에 이르러는 일은 새벽부터 더 한層 놉하젓다. 電車와 馬車닷는 소래 荷車와「싸구려」賣子소래 往來人의 밧분 步調 등도 이 모든 것들은 그 어느 것이나 다가치 내일 아참의 新正을 報하는 것이며 이 해의 마지막인 오늘을 말함이엇다.
나는 이럭저럭 오전 열시 가랑이나 되여서「쩨스필公園」을 향하야 집을 떠낫다. 거의 우리들의 野外集會場所라 하여도 無妨할 만한 佛蘭西公園을 버리고 특히 쩨스필을 택한 것은 그가치 좀 靜寂한 곳을 차자가서 忘年感懷를 충분히 맛보아 늣겨보자는 까닭이엇다.
넓다란 公園 안에는 人影이 극히 稀少하엿다. 平時의 3분 1에 지나지 못한다 해도 過言이 아닐 만하엿다. 나는 한편 모통이 뻰치우에 자리를 정하고 안젓다. 이런 경우에 잇서서는 거의 第二本能的이랄만치 나는 外套뽀켓트 속으로부터 담배를 끄내어 부처물엇다. 나의 주위는 극히 고요하다.
나의 머리 속에는 녯날의 추억이 가득히 떠올낫다. 그러고는 現在와 未來, 私生活과 公生活, 나와 우리들의 將來運命, 復活, 滅亡...... 등 이 모든 有象無象의 幻想이 마치 파노라마와 가치 머리 속을 달려가고 왓다.
회고하건대 내가 사랑하는 故鄕을 떠나 이가튼 險路울 밝게 된 것도 언으듯 꿈결가치 三星霜이 지나처 바렷다. 光陰의 流水性을 늣기는 것이 항상 年末年頭에 잇서 더욱 切實한 것도 사실이지마는 젊은 인생의 행로를 거러가기에는 너무도 短縮한 時日이 우리를 期待하고 잇는 것 가치 생각된다.
그러타―3년 전 녯날―그날 밤은 눈보라치는 몹시 치운 게울밤이엇다. 나는 대강대강＜54＞ 일상생활의 必需品만으로써 간단한 나의 行李를 收拾하야 가지고 斷然히 XX의 旅程에 올낫다. 늙은 어머니와 어린 동생들은 내가 오래동안 집에 도라오지 안는다는 말을 듯고 처음에는 仰制로 挽留하다가 나종에는 나의 결심이 움죽이지 안음을 깨닷고는 할 일 업시 恨心겨운 눈물로써 나를 떠나보내고 말엇다. 산넘어 바다 건너로 定處업는 발길을 내여 드듸는 어린 아들에게 대한 늙은 어머니의 切實한 付托은 오즉 변함업는 건강과 끗치지 안는 소식을 밤낫으로 축수하야 빈다는 것 뿐이엇다. 이가튼 功實한 付托은 엇시 어머니와 동생들의 그것 뿐에 끄칠 것이랴. 일평생을 굿게 言約한 信任하는 同志들과 朝夕相逢하는 평범한 친구들게로부터서도 역시 同一한 付托이엇섯다.
그 후로부터 오늘까지 3년이란 세월이 지나치는 동안에 이적은 나의 亡命生活 중에는 실로 쓰라린 체험과 거츠른 波浪이 만히 잇섯다. 이해 오늘에 當해서야말로 내가 이 上海 한 곳에서 送舊迎新을 하게 되지마는 그동안 나의 발길은 별스러운 收獲도 업시 그저그저 말하자면 물에 뜬 浮萍草와도 가치 혹은 中嶺으로 혹은 西伯利벌판으로 정처업시 放浪의 길을 헤매이엇다. 내년 이날 이때에는 이 적은 몸이 또 다시 어느 곳에 발길을 멈추게 될 것인가. 그러고 또 오는 해에도 역시 오늘 이와 한가지로「나」라는 朝鮮人이라는 사람들은 依然히 이 생활을 계속하여 나갈 것인가. 날이 가고 달이 갈사록 우리들의 (略 - 원문) 그와 正比例로 우리들의 사회는 더욱 (略-원문) 하여가는 것이 숨길 수 업는 엄연한 사실이다.
