Regarding the Martyrdom of Catholics, as the Author of “The Death of Ich’adon” (異次頓의 死) (*Chosŏn Ilbo*)

Ch’unwŏn (李光洙)

I like martyrs. I believe martyrs, who disregard personal gain, honor, or disgrace, and sacrifice even their lives solely for truth and righteousness, are the most beautiful flowers of life.
Therefore, I have always had the thought of trying to write about martyrs. In the past, I wrote a script called *Martyr*, and I also tried writing a novel about the martyrdom of the founder of Catholicism. Although circumstances prevented me from finishing it, I also wrote a novel called *The Gold Cross* (金十字架) for the *Tong-a Ilbo*.
Writing *Ich’adon* originated from such motives. Ich’adon was the first martyr in Korean history. That is what captured my interest.
I respect the tens of thousands of Catholic martyrs. It is regrettable that their history is not well known, but I consider it an immortal pride that Koreans produced tens of thousands of martyrs. When I think that the blood of such martyrs might flow in my veins, I feel reassured and take great pride.

Writing about the Ideals and Anguish of the Progressive Strata in “Dawn of the Northern Country” (北國의 黎明) (*Chosŏn Chungang Ilbo*)
Pak Hwasŏng

Just as I mentioned as the author when the notice for my humble work, *Dawn of the Northern Country*, was published in the *Chosŏn Chungang Ilbo*, I intend to introduce to you the turbulent half-life of one woman.<74>
Unlike Kŭmbong, the protagonist of Mr. Yi Kwangsu's *That Woman's Life* (그 여자의 일생) – who seems strong-willed but is extremely weak, pretends not to be driven by vanity yet her entire being is woven from dreams of vanity, clutches fiercely at her chastity yet is truly open, bearing two children with different fathers while married, indulging in wanton behavior before fleeing to Kŭmgangsan (金剛山), and finally, on a whim, deciding to shave her head and become a nun to end her life – a woman as lewd as the mist and empty as a scarecrow, whose profound existence readers deeply understand through Mr. Yi's powerful pen; I wish to show that there can also be valiant women like Hyosun, the heroine of *Dawn of the Northern Country*.
As some might be reading this daily, I won't introduce the content tediously here, but all her actions, from her youth to the point where her half-life concludes, were nothing short of revolutionary against family, religion, institutions, customs, and masculinity.
The story is still in progress, so I won't speak of its conclusion, but what I desire is to depict, even if just a little, the ideological tendencies of the male and female intelligentsia of the time, spanning the ten years from Taishō 15 (1925) to Shōwa 10 (1935), their ideals and anguish, and also to portray, however thinly and narrowly, aspects of the sexual and private lives of pioneering and progressive male and female comrades, aspects of life in Tokyo (동경), etc., allowing you, the readers, to grasp even a tenth of the various facets of that era.
However, more than all the points above, my main effort is specifically directed at showing our female readers that even a woman, whom both she and others acknowledge must spend her life as an appendage to a man, can fulfill her duties and natural calling as a daughter, wife, and mother,<75> while completely breaking free from male subjugation and familial subordination. She can also, as an individual human being, break through deeply entangled emotional ties (情理) and myriad difficulties for her ideas and work, forging ahead valiantly until the end.
However, class consciousness – which was most active and highly developed during this time – has seen many ideological conversions (思想的) recently, enough to create the new buzzword 'ideological shift' (사상전향) from years of experience. This aspect threatens to mar almost the entire work with points I wish to speak of but cannot fully express (欲言未吐). Nevertheless, I trust and reassure myself that if you do not discard my clumsy and incomparable humble work but read it to the very end, perhaps there will be those who understand my hopes and also comprehend all these grievances.

With the Attitude of a Sculptor – As the Author of “The Dense Forest” (密林) (*Tong-a Ilbo*)
Kim Malbong

A sculptor poured his heart and soul for several years into creating a marble sculpture. When it was about to be sold, the buyer, for some reason, demanded the nose be shaved down because it was too high, the back of the head ground down because it protruded, and the hands and feet cut off because they were too large. The sculptor, weeping, scraped and carved away parts of the sculpture into which he had poured his life. But the sculpture reflected in his eyes had become utterly ugly. He picked up the mallet<76> he held and smashed the sculpture to pieces.
The words I offer to you, dear readers, are that when I wrote *Millim*, I put forth all my effort.
However, being a Korean, and it being a newspaper novel – these two factors have shaved the nose and cut off the hands of my work. The wounds visible on *Millim* to my eyes are truly dreadful. If I had an artist's conscience, I ought to rightfully rip the manuscript of *Millim* to shreds.
But I am not an artist (藝術家). I am merely a poor woman of Chosŏn.
There is a heart I entrusted to *Millim*, even in its cut and shaved state. If there is anyone among you, dear readers, who might lend an ear to the clumsy melody that *Millim* sings hoarsely, then *Millim* will courageously continue its song.

Amidst Emotion and Tension – As the Author of “Red Clouds, White Clouds” (紅雲白雲) (*Maeil Sinbo*)

Pang In'gŭn

First, I thank all of you who avidly read my humble work. I always feel that an indescribable affection is formed between an author and their readers. This connection, woven through the work without ever meeting face-to-face or speaking to each other, is undeniably one of the joys of living in this world. Often, the author writes not so much for the sake of the work itself, but thinking of the readers<77> who will read this writing. Imagining the tens of thousands of readers, men and women, young and old, while writing is quite enjoyable and has a sense of tension. Readers, too, likely find much enjoyment in imagining the author while reading the novel. This is undeniably one of the pleasures of life.
Since *Hongun Paegun*, which I am currently writing, could not be written all at once and is being written daily, I know there will likely be many flaws. Furthermore, due to demands such as being reader-centric and taste-oriented, there may be parts that deviate from my original intentions, compounded by the complexity of my own life recently, preventing me from writing with sufficient leisure. The title *Hongun Paegun* itself was hastily conceived; looking up at the sky while searching for a title, I saw red clouds and white clouds and came up with it, so the content is likewise spontaneous. As it is still being written, I don't know how it will unfold or how substantial a work it will become, but in any case, I only regret not being able to present a masterpiece to you all.
Now that things have settled down and I have a study where I can sit and write, I believe it will improve going forward. Moreover, living in a very secluded place outside the city allows me to write with peace of mind. Behind me is a mountain, and before me lies a wide field; autumn deepens, stirring poetic thoughts (詩想). It is also becoming a good season for all of you to read. Please continue to avidly read *Hongun Paegun* to the end, offering your critiques (鞭韃), and I hope that through this connection, we can become literary friends for life. I wish for your health and happiness as I put down my brush.

I Want to Hear the Voice of the True Reader – As the Author of “The Evergreen Tree” (常綠樹) (*Tong-a Ilbo*)

Sim Hun

Though repeatedly asked to write a letter to beloved readers, I already wrote the author's remarks, albeit briefly, when<78> the notice was published, so there seems no need for further repetition. Moreover, presenting oneself self-importantly about a work that has only been published for about twenty installments cannot but be embarrassing. Therefore, it suffices to consider the entire work as a long letter addressed to the beloved readers. Within *Sangnoksu*, the author's intentions, though immature (未熟), should be expressed to some extent (幾分間). Even if it fails to scratch the itchy spots precisely with sharp fingernails, one should be able to glimpse, here and there, the creator's thoughts or attitude towards the reality of Chosŏn.
Therefore, since the author is essentially writing a one-sided letter to the readers daily for almost half a year, isn't it this side that wishes to receive a reply? Although I receive two or three letters daily, excessive deference is impolite (過恭이 非禮), and overly effusive praise feels rather like an insult. I want to hear the stern, cold, truly frank voice of the reader. While it goes without saying that I will heed the words of discerning critics (具眼), what I truly wish to hear are the unreserved impressions and criticisms of the rural youth who are actually engaged in movements of a similar nature to the content of the work within such environments.
I believe that by establishing close contact between the author and readers, exchanging opinions, and receiving constant encouragement, mutual growth can be achieved. Accordingly, for a kind of ascetic who continues creation through painstaking effort (彫骨縷心), encountering the "true voice" (참다운 聲咳) of beloved readers who share their innermost thoughts (肝膽을 相照) is the sole joy and simultaneously a great driving force for pouring energy into the work. November 1st (At Tangjin Pilgyŏngsa).

Only Through the Work – As the Author of “Holy Mother” (聖母) (*Chosŏn Chungang Ilbo*)

Yi T'aejun

I recall reading somewhere – I forget who said it – "The only thing that truly praises<79> the author is his work." The author only speaks to the reader through the work; what other words could there be apart from the work? Whether it receives praise or censure, one must simply be faithful to the work.

I Ask the Masses in Return – As the author of “When Dawn Breaks” (먼동이 틀 때) (*Tong-a Ilbo*)

Yi Muyŏng

There is perhaps no act more embarrassing than discussing an unfinished work – especially one's own. This is not only because a work does not necessarily proceed according to the intentions held during its creation, but also because the author themself is in no position to pronounce upon the value that the third-party masses will assign to the work. Furthermore, one runs the risk of imposing a "value" upon the masses that they do not acknowledge.
If I can say anything here, it would likely be a more concrete extension of what was said in the "author's remarks."
For someone intending to pursue literature with a sincere (眞摯) attitude, taking up the pen for a newspaper novel is already a sign of wavering, and that attitude itself is already less than beautiful. Thus, whenever criticized for writing newspaper novels, it hurts like touching a raw wound. However, realistically thinking, in Chosŏn, where one can only write novels of 30-40 pages [manuscript pages] if not for newspaper serialization, one naturally comes to<80> rationalize one's own writing of newspaper novels.
I do not hope for *When Dawn Breaks* to become a masterpiece – (no, I cannot hope for it) – nor do I hope for this work to last even two or three years, let alone ten. However, this by no means implies that the attitude of myself, the author writing this work, is lax. To write a work that might remain for 10 or even 100 years is a common instinct not just for me, but for anyone who writes. It's just that, knowing myself too well, I cannot even hope for such a thing myself.
But perhaps I can say this much: I harbor the ambition, with this opportunity, to try and raise the level (레-벨) of existing newspaper novels somewhat. Rather than catering (迎合) to the readers' lowbrow tastes, I wish to take a step forward and help cultivate their appreciation (鑑賞力) for "Art" (藝術).
However, in truth, perhaps even these words were not mine to say.<81>

**Summary**

Seven prominent Korean authors (Yi Kwangsu, Pak Hwasŏng, Kim Malbong, Pang In'gŭn, Sim Hun, Yi T'aejun, Yi Muyŏng) writing serialized novels for major newspapers in 1935 address their readers via the magazine *Samch'ŏlli*. They discuss their motivations, themes (martyrdom, progressive women, artistic struggle, rural movements, everyday life), the challenges of newspaper serialization (deadlines, reader expectations, potential censorship implied by Kim Malbong and Pak Hwasŏng), and their desire for genuine reader feedback, particularly from those whose lives mirror the stories. The letters reveal a complex relationship with their audience and the constraints of their medium, alongside aspirations for artistic integrity and social relevance. Historically, the article provides valuable insights into the colonial Korean literary scene, authorial self-perception under Japanese rule, the pressures and opportunities presented by mass media (newspapers), and the ongoing negotiation between popular appeal and artistic/ideological goals during a period of significant social and political change. The anxieties expressed about censorship ("shaved nose," "cut hands," "欲言未吐") and the discussion of ideological shifts ("사상전향") are particularly noteworthy.

天主敎徒의 殉敎를 보고 「異次頓의 死」(朝鮮日報)의 作者로서

春園

나는 순교자를 좋아합니다. 利害榮辱을 도외시하고 오직 진리와 의리를 위하야 생명까지 희생하는 순교자는 인생의 가장 아름다운 꽃이라고 믿습니다.
그래서 나는 순교자를 테마로 글을 써보러는 생각이 늘 있어서 예전 순교자라는 脚本도 하나 써보고 또 天主敎祖의 순교도 소설로 써보고 또 사정있어 끝은 못내고 말았지만은 東亞日報에 金十字架라는 소설도 썼읍니다.
異次頓을 쓰게 된 것도 그런 동기에서 입니다. 異次頓은 조선의 역사에서는 첫 순교자었읍니다. 그것이 내 흥미를 끈 것이었읍니다.
나는 천주교도의 數萬명 순교자들을 존경합니다. 그 역사를 잘 알 수 없음이 恨이어니와 조선인 수만의 순교자를 내었다는 것은 不朽의 자랑으로 알며 내 혈관에도 이러한 순교자의 피가 흐르거니하면 마음이 든든하고 큰 긍지를 느낌니다.

進步層의 理想과 苦悶을 「北國의 黎明」(中央日報)을 쓰면서
朴花城

鄙作 『北國의 黎明』의 예고를 朝鮮中央日報 紙上에 냈을 때 작자로서의 말슴을 드린 것과 마찬가지로 나는 波難많은 한 여자의 半生을 여러분께 소개하려는 것입니다.＜74＞
李光洙씨작 『그 여자의 일생』의 주인공 금봉이와 같이 心志가 강한 듯하게 보이면서도 극히 유약하고 허영에 달뜨지 않은 체 하면서도 前身이 허영의 꿈으로만 뭉쳐있으며 정조를 아끼는 척 바득 바득하면서도 그야말로 개방적이어서 人妻의 몸으로 아비 달은 자식을 둘씩이나 낳고 방종할 대로 방종하다가 金剛山으로 망명한 후 나종에는 일시적 기분으로 削髮爲僧하야 일생을 마치리라는 그러한 암개와 같이 음탕하고 허수아비처럼 등신 뿐인 여자가 있다는 것을 氏의 雄筆로서 심각히 알고 계시는 讀者 諸氏에게 또한 『北國의 黎明』의 여주인공 효순이와 같은 씩씩한 여성도 있으리라는 것을 보여드리려 하는 것입니다.
