기사제목	三十萬圓을 新聞에 너흔 崔善益氏 (Mr. Ch'oe Sŏn'ik, Who Poured ₩300,000 into Newspapers)
필자	三千里社
기사형태	대담·좌담 (Interview/Discussion)

Mr. Ch'oe Sŏn'ik, Who Poured ₩300,000 into Newspapers
						Samch'ŏllisa

Going up the small alley in Kahoe-dong where "Ch'wiunjŏng" (翠雲亭) is located, and turning slightly into the narrow side alley of Hwa-dong, one finds a large, purely Korean-style, splendid residence, grand enough to make one wonder, "When was such an Arabian Nights-like palace built in an alley like this?" This house is none other than the residence of Mr. Ch'oe Sŏn'ik, the wealthy man from Kaesŏng currently residing in Seoul, whom I came to visit. Stepping through the wide-open main gate, a weeping willow tree, its slender green leaves still vivid, hangs down heavily, dancing in the passing breeze.

Amidst the quiet morning air at 8 o'clock drifting through the flower-filled courtyard, I climbed the high stone steps. Past the main gate was a middle gate, and past the middle gate was another detached gate. Within these layers of three gates, I discovered a purely Korean-style house, still exuding the fresh scent of wood, blocking the view ahead.

Guided by a young housemaid, I passed through yet another locked main gate and walked for quite a while across a courtyard far larger than I had imagined, finally settling my small frame into a rattan chair in a large hall-like room serving as the reception area.

This house, built in a purely Korean style, is perhaps the most worth seeing in Seoul.

The master of the house, appearing dressed in white Korean attire from top to bottom, presents himself as a young man in his 30s with handsome features befitting the dignity of this house.

On one wall hangs only a single, long plaque with the inscription 『己所不欲勿施於人』 (What you do not want done to yourself, do not do to others), said to have been written by Hŏ Misŏ (許眉叟), a famous master calligrapher during the time of King Sŏnjo. Apart from this, not a single painting or potted plant could be seen, offering a glimpse into the character of the master of the house, who extremely favors "simplicity."<57>

Mr. Ch'oe needs no introduction from my pen, as the world already knows him well. Born as the cherished only son into a family renowned as wealthy figures in Kaesŏng, he experienced the loss of his father before even reaching the young age of 20. Consequently, he had to abandon his studies midway, inherit a vast fortune, and has continued as a young cultural entrepreneur to this day.

That Mr. Ch'oe, at such a tender age, barely past his teens (弱冠), could manage the million-won fortune inherited from his parents without issue, speaks to his exceptional talent and intellect.

Having long held aspirations in cultural enterprises, Mr. Ch'oe, who had invested significant sums and effort in newspaper companies since before he turned 30, has recently turned his back on society to rest his complex and strained mind and body, which had troubled him since childhood. For the time being, he lives in a manner akin to "seclusion" (杜門不出), spending quiet and leisurely days (閑日月).

Mr. Ch'oe, rumored by the world to possess one million won or even five million won, has long devoted much effort to social and cultural enterprises. With the experience and knowledge gained, he is currently calming his complex mind during this temporary period of leisure (閑日月), mostly sitting in his study flipping through books, and occasionally strolling through the suburbs or fields with two or three friends for recreation (逍遙). The day for this young millionaire, this spirited social entrepreneur, to re-emerge is surely not far off.

It is likely true that before long, Mr. Ch'oe is quietly planning in his mind the opportunity and scheme to once again reveal some unusual, surprising, and significant news to the world.

How much vast wealth does this young entrepreneur possess, and what has he felt about our society?

And how much money has he spent to date? Also, what is his current life like? What path has he taken until today, and what path will he take in the future?

In any case, one is curious about the recent news of this prominent and vigorous entrepreneur, and even more eager to know about his future from now on.
Now, having arranged these various points methodically in my mind beforehand, I began to ask them one by one.

Recent Private Life
Me: "Since withdrawing from the newspaper company, what do you occupy yourself with for enjoyment in your daily life?"
Ch'oe: "Well—there's nothing really worth calling a hobby. I'm just resting my weary mind and body, truly wasting time, so to speak. I don't really have any particular hobby to speak of."
Me: "Still, surely you can't be without any hobbies at all? Then perhaps you just hide away quietly in your study at home<58> and immerse yourself only in reading?"
Ch'oe: "Reading? I don't do much reading either. Since I had to quit school midway due to family circumstances, I can't really read specialized books. So, when I'm bored, I just flip through new magazines or newspapers. Occasionally, I browse bookstores in Ponjŏng (本町) and look through new books coming from the Tokyo area, but—. Since my middle school days, I've avidly read literary works. I still tend to do so, but I have no particular ambition to advance in that field; I just find enjoyment in it. I also really enjoy listening to music performed by others, but I don't know how to play myself. The same goes for calligraphy. Literature is also similar. If these are my hobbies, perhaps they could be called hobbies."
Seeing a baduk board placed on one side of the wooden floor, I thought this gentleman might pass the time playing baduk and continued my questions.
Me: "You seem to find great enjoyment in playing baduk."
Ch'oe: "Baduk? Yes, the board is placed there, but I don't particularly like it. Why would a young person like me sit around playing baduk when there are things I cannot do? I haven't even learned 'changgi' (장귀 - Korean chess)."
Me: "What kind of sports do you enjoy?"
Ch'oe: "I don't know any. I don't know how to play tennis, nor golf or horse riding."
Me: "Excuse my asking, but are you good at drinking (酒)? Haha."
Ch'oe: "Yes, I quite like alcohol. (Heh heh heh) I end up drinking quite often."
Me: "What do you think about men drinking? Should men definitely drink, or should they not drink?"
Ch'oe: "Drinking alcohol cannot necessarily be called bad. However, if anyone overdoes it, they make mistakes and lose their senses, which is unfortunate—. But nowadays, most politicians and people with many social interactions often drink, so one could say there are good occasions and times for drinking."

The Path Walked Before
Me: "How old were you, sir, when your father passed away?"
Ch'oe: "I was just a young boy attending the fourth grade at Chungang School. If my father hadn't passed away then, I would have graduated from the school I was attending, and perhaps even attended university. Moreover, being the only son, what choice did I have when my father passed away? I just stayed at home and looked after the inheritance."<59>
Me: "When did you first get involved with the newspaper company?"
Ch'oe: "It was when I was twenty-one. I became involved with the Chosŏn Ilbo (朝鮮日報) and stayed with that newspaper for three years, until I was twenty-three."
Me: "You must have spent quite a lot of money."
Ch'oe: "Yes, I suppose I spent about 300,000 won."
Me: "And after that..."
Ch'oe: "After leaving there, I went down to my home in Kaesŏng for a while and just stayed there."
Me: "Since when have you been with the Chungang Ilbo (中央日報)?"
Ch'oe: "Let's see—it must have been around the 8th year of Shōwa (1933). I ended up spending quite a lot of money here too."

View on Gold (Money)
Me: "How do you view money? Do you consider it the most important thing in human society?"
Ch'oe: "Well, you'd have to ask society about that; how can I say what it is? If it's used valuably and effectively for society, then it's a precious thing."
Me: "Sir, in what field do you intend to spend your great wealth next?"
Ch'oe: "I don't have any money. People talk about how much I have, but in reality, I have very little. Besides, are there any truly great fortunes among Koreans? At most, it's around one million won. Even if someone has several hundred thousand won and is called rich, that amount is barely enough to manage a household. And some people say if they only had a certain amount of money, they would start this business or that, but I don't engage in such fantasies. If I have money, I spend it valuably; if I don't, I can't spend it. Therefore, since I don't have money now, I haven't even begun to think about which field I will spend money in or how I will spend it in the future."
Me: "Don't you want to accumulate more money than you currently have?"
Ch'oe: "I've only ever known how to spend money; I have absolutely no idea how to accumulate it, so I don't even entertain such thoughts. Even though I'm from Kaesŏng, I don't know how to do business or save money."

View on Inheritance
Me: "Sir, you inherited a large fortune from your parents early on. Traditionally, in wealthy Korean families, the issue of inheritance has been considered quite necessary and important. How do you view this?"
Ch'oe: "Of course, due to traditional Korean customs, large wealthy families still consider passing down inheritance to descendants a major affair. However, I believe this inheritance has both advantages and disadvantages (一短一長). It seems that if a young person receives an inheritance from their parents, their individuality might not be fully developed. Looking at past examples, there are many cases where people rely solely on the inheritance,<60> lose their unique character, and simply entrust everything to the family property. However, if one is to receive an inheritance, one must receive it. The issue then is simply how to use it beneficially and appropriately according to societal demands. If this distinction is made well, it would be incredibly good. But even looking at myself, before receiving the inheritance, my young mind and idealistic mood were large, thinking, 'In the future, I will undertake such and such projects.' But once I received the inheritance, due to all the surrounding circumstances and family situations at that time, things didn't go as planned. In any case, while a young person receiving an inheritance might be necessary socially depending on whether the money is used well or poorly, from the individual's perspective, it cannot be called a good thing."

Reason for Using a Purely Korean-Style House
Me: "Looking at the residences of wealthy people these days, many tend to build in a Western style (洋屋風). Why did you specifically choose to build and live in a purely Korean-style house?"
Ch'oe: "For the lifestyle of Korean people, the Korean-style building format seems the best. Firstly, it's convenient to use, sunlight enters well, and air circulates freely, making it truly an ideal housing system (理想的家屋制度), in my opinion. Especially for us who eat kimchi and kkakdugi, and wear baji and jeogori, this type of house structure seems best. Looking at the houses of upper-middle-class people nowadays, most are built somewhat like Western houses, but that's neither fish nor fowl (죽도 않이고 밥도 않이지요). Compared to those barrack-style cheap Western houses, how much better is the traditional system suited to Korean life? Looking at the Western-style houses being built in Tokyo now, even the smallest ones usually cost around 10,000 yen, and decorating just the reception room costs 4,000 or 5,000 yen; otherwise, you can't even call it a Western house. Whether viewed economically, or from the perspective of traditional Korean sentiment or classical beauty, this system seems better."

What Path Lies Ahead
Me: "How long do you plan to continue this period of silence and recuperation (靜養)?"
Ch'oe: "I haven't thought about it yet. I just need to 'cultivate myself' (修養) for some time. After that, I'll figure out what to do."
Me: "Do you have any thoughts of stepping back into cultural or social enterprises?"
Ch'oe: "If I only had the money, that would certainly be a good thing. However, for the time being, I need to stay away from society and rest my mind. After that, the day will come when I gather my thoughts again about the path I should take." (Continued on page 56)<61>

While exchanging words with Mr. Ch'oe, the morning sunlight had, at some point, spread across the vast, shimmering wooden floor of the main hall, brightening the entire room.

Rising from my seat and stepping out of the main gate, I looked back once more; it was a magnificent and large house, like a "palace" (宮闕).

With light steps, I descended the stone stairs, wondering curiously about the timing of this young cultural entrepreneur's comeback, and exited the alley, enveloped in the slightly chilly late autumn wind.<56>

**Summary**

This 1935 Samch'ŏlli magazine article interviews Ch'oe Sŏn'ik, a young millionaire from Kaesŏng famous for investing heavily (₩300,000) in newspapers like the Chosŏn Ilbo and Chungang Ilbo. Having withdrawn from public life to rest, Ch'oe discusses his current quiet existence, his lack of specific hobbies besides reading literature and drinking, his views on money (valuable only if used well for society, denies having much despite rumors), inheritance (potentially stifling individuality), and his strong preference for traditional Korean housing over Western styles for practical and cultural reasons. He remains non-committal about future plans but hints at a possible return to social or cultural activities after a period of reflection.

From a historical perspective, the article provides a fascinating glimpse into the life and mindset of a wealthy Korean intellectual during the colonial period. His significant investment in Korean newspapers highlights the role of media and potential nationalist or cultural undertones, even if not explicitly stated. His discussion on inheritance reflects traditional concerns alongside modern individualistic thought. Furthermore, his articulate defense of Korean-style housing against the trend of Westernization offers insight into cultural identity struggles and preferences among the elite under Japanese rule. The sums of money mentioned (hundreds of thousands of won) give context to economic scales of the time.

기사제목	三十萬圓을 新聞에 너흔 崔善益氏
필자	三千里社
기사형태	대담·좌담

三十萬圓을 新聞에 너흔 崔善益氏
						三千里社
「翠雲亭」이 잇는 가회동 골목으로 작고 올너가다가 花洞 좁은 엽 골목길을 조곰 드러서느라면 「이런 골목에도 저런 아방궁이 언제 생겻든가」하리 만치 아라비안, 나잇트에 나오는 궁성가튼 크다란 순조선식 화려한 주택 한 채가 노엿스니 이 집이 바로 내가 차저드러가는 開城富豪로 서울 와 잇는 崔善益氏 宅이라 활작 열어제친 대문안으로 드러서니 칭칭 느러진 수양버드나무 한 그루가 아직도 생생한 초록입을 가느다라케 드리우고 가고 오는 바람에 춤을 추고 잇다.
아츰 8시의 조용한 공긔가 떠도는 화초 욱어진 뜰안으로 놉다란 층층대 돌층게를 밟고 올너가니 대문 안에 중문이오 중문 안에 일각문이라. 삼대문이 첩첩이 노힌 속에 나뭇내음새 아직 生新한 맛을 전하는 순조선식 한 채가 압흘 가로막고 선 것을 발견한다.
어린 하녀 아이에게 안내를 받어 잠겨진 또 한 대문을 넘어서야 나의 상상 이상으로 넓다란 뜰안을 한참이나 거러서 응접실로 된 커다란 마루방의 藤倚子에 비로소 나는 적은 몸을 기대엿다.
순전한 조선식으로 된 이 집은 아마도 서울서는 제일 갈 만하다.
아래 우에 흰 조선의복을 입고 나오는 이 집 주인은 이 집의 격에 알맛게 수려한 眉目을 가진 30대의 청년몸으로 나타난다.
한쪽 벽 우에는 宣祖 때에 글 잘쓰는 名筆家로 유명한 許眉叟가 썻다는 『己所不欲勿施於人』이라는 기-다란 額板 한 장이 붙엇슬 뿐이지 그밖에는 아무 그림 하나 화분 한 개를 어더 볼 수 없음은 지극히 「簡潔」함을 조와하는 이 집 어룬의 기품을 엿볼 수 잇다.＜57＞

氏는 내 붓을 기다릴 것 업시 이미 세상이 다 아는 바라. 개성에서 일즉이 한다하는 부자로 이홈이 잇든 집안에서 귀여운 외아들로 태여나 20의 아즉 어린 고개를 채 다다르기도 밧부게 이여 부친을 사별한 氏는 학업도 그만 중도에서 지버치우고 그 많은 재산을 물여바더가지고 오늘날까지 내려오는 젊은 문화사업가이다.
씨가 그렇게 어린 弱冠의 몸으로 부모가 물여준 백만의 대금을 무난히 누림도 氏의 뛰여난 재조와 두뇌를 가젓슴을 알겟다.
일즉붙어 문화사업에 뜻을 두고 30미만에서붙어 신문사에 많은 돈을 쓰시며 퍽으나 애써오든 氏가 근자에 와서는 어려서붙어 시달니든 복잡하고 긴장하든 머리와 심신을 좀 쉬우기 위하야 사회에서 머리를 돌여 당분간은 「杜門不出」격으로 지내며 조용하고 한가한 閑日月을 보내고 잇다.
백만원을 가젓느니 오백만원을 가젓느니하며 세상에서 말하는 씨는 일즉붙어 사회사업과 문화사업에 많은 힘을 기우려 왓든 경험과 지식으로 당분간의 閑日月로 복잡하든 머리를 가다듬고 잇서 오즉 서재실에 앉어 서적을 뒤적이기도 하며 틈틈이 2, 3의 友人들과 벗하야 교외나 들로 산책하는 것으로 逍遙하고 잇스니 이 젊은 백만장자 이 혈기 발발한 사회사업가의 두 번 다시 내여드딀 재기의 날도 머지않을 것이다.
아마도 오라지 않어 氏는 긔여히 또 무슨 심상치 않은 놀납고 큼직한 소문을 세상에 내여놓을 긔회와 푸란을 고요히 머리속에 그리고 잇슬 것이 사실이다.
이 젊은 사업가는 얼마나 많은 巨富를 가지고 잇스며 또한 우리 사회에서 그 무엇을 늣겻든가.
그리고 얼마나한 돈을 오늘날까지 써왓든고. 또한 氏의 요즘의 생활은 어떠한가. 금일는 어떤 길로 장차의 거러나갈 길은 그 무엇이엿든가.
어찌되엿든 쟁쟁하고 발발하든 이 사업가의 저간의 소식이 굼굼하며 이지붙어의 앞날이 더욱 알고 십흔 일이다.
이제 나는 이러한 여러 가지 점을 미리붙어 머속리에 차국 차국 개여넛코 한가지씩 뭇기를 시작하엿다.

最近의 私生活은

遺産을 어떻게 보는가
나 「선생이 일즉이 많은 유산을 부모께서 받으시고 햇스니, 재래로 우리 조선에서 부자집이라면 이 유산문제를 그 중 필요하고 중대하게 녁여왓섯는데, 선생은 이것을 어떻게 보심니까.」
