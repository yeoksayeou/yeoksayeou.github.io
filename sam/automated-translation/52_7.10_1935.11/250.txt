Memoirs of the Hardships of Composers and Lyricists Who Produced the Ten Great Singers, the "Nightingales of the Street"

One million two hundred thousand copies.
This is the number of records sold annually in the Chosŏn market. Of these, Chosŏn folk music records account for about one-third. In any case, the record world, which has four to five hundred thousand buyers each year and hundreds of thousands, even millions, of people listening to and enjoying its songs, cannot but be called an enviable and brilliant presence. So, who are the ones moving these 1.2 million records? They are none other than the singers, the "nightingales of the street."
However, even among singers, there are various types and classes. As a result of a popularity poll conducted by our company, ten great singers were selected. These individuals can be seen as the ones wearing the laurel wreaths. When we think about it this way, how much hidden, tearful effort must have gone into producing such famous singers by the composers and lyricists? It is with the intention of peeking behind this screen to view the flowers that I move this brush.

Composing "Sidŭrŭn Ch'ŏngch'un" for Mr. Ch'ae Kyuyŏp
Chŏn Kiyŏn-
Mr. Ch'ae Kyuyŏp's fame was already high even before we entered the record world. When I composed "Sidŭrŭn Ch'ŏngch'un" (Withered Youth) for him, he indeed performed it well. No matter who you name, Mr. Ch'ae Kyuyŏp truly possesses not just his own voice, but a wondrous voice (妙音) capable of changing according to the moment. Mr. Ch'ae Kyuyŏp's voice, though perhaps not booming for a man, is perfectly suited for records. Even if an amplifier is used before hundreds of thousands of youths, it doesn't matter. It's not about shouting loudly like an army commander; a loud shout is utterly unnecessary for the way he delicately draws out, breaks, and turns the notes. His blessed-looking face and round physique give viewers a gentle feeling, and the wondrous technique of gracefully turning each note is something first found in Mr. Ch'ae Kyuyŏp. I heard from his friend that his hometown is Hamhŭng and he loved music since his youth. He also studied voice at the Osaka Music School (大阪음악학교) in Taishō 15 (1926), returned to Sŏul and served as a music teacher at Kŭnhwa Girls' School (근화녀학교), and then, harboring greater ambitions, crossed the Genkai Sea again to enter the Tokyo Central Music School (東京中央音樂學校). By that time, he was already refined as the first Chosŏn youth to become an exclusive singer for Columbia Records, and his advancing voice spread beautiful melodies to the populace through records and broadcasts. Thinking back, it seems Mr. Ch'ae Kyuyŏp has been a Columbia singer for over 8 years now. During that time, he has recorded over 300 records and is also greatly welcomed in the Japanese popular song world under the stage name Hasegawa Ichirō (長谷川一郞). He was also warmly welcomed when he visited Chosŏn the year before last. Indeed, as the strong attract many enemies, there are those who criticize Mr. Ch'ae, but I hope people will just appreciate his art. (Photo is Ch'ae Kyuyŏp)
Is there anyone without flaws? Since everyone, you and I alike, has faults, trying to eliminate great art because of minor flaws stems from a lack of consideration for the artist, I believe. While not claiming he is faultless, it is publicly acknowledged that in terms of his singing, he is indeed one of the unique figures in this field as an Eastern singer.
Mr. Ch'ae's voice has characteristics that popular song genres, especially record companies, would favor. The essence of popular songs is that they are liked by the general public and sell well. Since Mr. Ch'ae's songs incorporate tunes likely to become popular according to the trends of the time <152> and make them sell, they are likely super-speed, cutting-edge popular songs.

Writing the Lyrics for Sŏnu Ilsŏn's "Kkoch'ŭl Chapko"
Kim Ansŏ

It was a late spring day. Flower petals were falling ceaselessly, and only the idle green spring awaited the hot summer.
Thinking of the past, one cannot help but have various thoughts. People too will likely wither away, leaving only sorrowful thoughts in their hearts. When you think about it, everything is like fleeting clouds and passing winds, but are human affairs really like that? When the spring breeze blows, everyone enjoys it and feels cheerful, but before that cheerfulness ends, don't they forget everything? Thus, when looking back at bygone days, who can help but feel pity for that sorrowful sentiment? Holding such thoughts, I wanted to try writing something like a song. To put it simply, it's about looking back on past days as the spring wind whirls by, finding them unforgettable, and weeping in idle sorrow.

It wasn't written as poetry, but as lyrics, and Mr. Yi Myŏnsang came by, saw it, said it was interesting, and took it. He took it, composed music for it, and it became what is now called "Kkoch'ŭl Chapko" (Holding the Flowers). The song "Kkoch'ŭl Chapko" wasn't written with the intention of having someone specific compose it or sing it. However, it happened coincidentally that way and became known to the world.
Hearing that song sung by Sŏnu Ilsŏn's beautiful voice, I too cannot listen to it indifferently. I cannot suppress the thought that it is interesting.
The song itself is one thing, but without Mr. Yi Myŏnsang's composition and Sŏnu Ilsŏn's beautiful voice, I couldn't imagine my amusing little song becoming so popular.
Sŏnu Ilsŏn's singing should not be seen merely as ordinary popular song; her voice possesses the high quality (品價) to sing our Chosŏn folk songs that express the Chosŏn sentiment (朝鮮情調).
No matter who you name, it is impossible to fully express Chosŏn folk songs without Sŏnu Ilsŏn's voice.

Composing "Kodoŭi Chŏnghan," Sung by Wang Supok
Chŏn Kiyŏn

Just as the newspapers and magazines <153> made a huge fuss about Wang Supok moving from Columbia to Polydor, and since she was a major singer at Columbia, Polydor intended to make her the queen of popular song. Thus, her first work there, "Kodoŭi Chŏnghan" (Island of Solitary Sorrow), was born from great effort by the composer and lyricist.
When I first heard that song, my initial impression was that it heavily imitated the kouta (小唄 - short songs) sung in Tokyo. Just as Wang Supok's physique is robust, her voice comes out strong, energetic, and powerful. Especially since she graduated from the P'yŏngyang Kisaeng School (平壤藝妓學校), her way of turning notes is truly admirable. Although it's not her natural vocal cords but purely crafted ones, it was welcomed by the public, became popular, and sold unprecedentedly well.
What was difficult for me as a composer while composing "Kodoŭi Chŏnghan" was changing the melody (變曲) suddenly in the middle and drawing out a thin sound; this was truly hard for me. I wondered, "Will this succeed? Can Wang Supok handle such an abrupt melodic change?" Unexpectedly, she broke and changed the melody more wondrously than the composer had imagined, drawing out the thin sound in a way that truly surprised me.
Thus, through Wang Supok's "Kodoŭi Chŏnghan," my composition became known to the world, and people started calling me a composer of sorts. To reiterate, in the Chosŏn record world, there has probably been no record that sold more than "Kodoŭi Chŏnghan." That's how much my effort, Mr. Wang P'yŏng's effort, and Wang Supok's effort combined in "Kodoŭi Chŏnghan."

When Writing "Aesanggok," Sung by Ms. Kim Pokhŭi
Yi Hayun

Normally, the lyrics should be written first, then the composition, and finally the song should be sung, but this "Aesanggok" (Song of Sorrow) was done completely backward. Hearing Kim Pokhŭi's voice, I was asked to write lyrics that matched it, with suggestions on the meaning, but no matter how I thought about it, nothing good came out.
Firstly, since this was Kim Pokhŭi's debut after joining the company, we tried hard to create something unique.
So, it was very difficult trying to compose lyrics that matched her mournful, most sorrowful voice.
Her voice is not ordinary; it seems mixed with tears and sighs from deep within. One discovers a subtle mystery, like a frail woman standing alone under the moonlight, looking into a deep blue pond. Therefore, I thought about it for days and days while writing the lyrics, but matching them to Kim Pokhŭi's voice required numerous revisions, making it one of my more difficult lyrical works. (Photo is Mr. Yi Hayun)

After finishing practice, recording, and listening to the test pressing, it was deemed good. <154> The sorrowfully drawn-out "Aesanggok" heard from record shops on the streets makes listeners shed tears.
I believe Kim Pokhŭi has not yet fully revealed her wondrous voice (妙聲). When the unknown depths of her voice will be unlocked is something we cannot help but anticipate in her future releases. (Photo is Mr. Kim Surin [error in original, should be Chŏn Surin])

Composing "Aesanggok," Sung by Ms. Kim Pokhŭi
Chŏn Surin

Kim Pokhŭi's "Aesanggok" truly squeezed out my utmost effort. When Kim Pokhŭi first came to our company and sang, listening to her song, it seemed her voice, like her swaying figure – perhaps like the weeping willows on Nŭngna Island – followed her body's style. Listening to her vocal cords, they were delicate, drawn-out, and trembling, which felt sorrowful. So I went back and tried composing a melody that matched this feeling. Then, matching it again with Kim Pokhŭi's singing, I thought it was very suitable. I initially presented my idea, but it didn't work out, so I finally asked Mr. Yi Hayun for the lyrics. That drawn-out melody expressed the sentiment (情調) of Chosŏn. It seems the public quite liked the way the song gently flowed in "Aesanggok," but even if my own abilities were lacking, I believe the day will come when Kim Pokhŭi will fully display her wondrous qualities in future songs. (Photo is Mr. Wang P'yŏng)

Writing the "Chosŏn March," Sung by Mr. Kim Yonghwan
Wang P'yŏng

We could be called the first people in the Chosŏn record industry. Before us, records were not produced in Chosŏn as they are today. So, the ones who first established the Polydor-Chosŏn branch were Kim Yonghwan,
Yi Kyŏngsŏl, and a few others like myself. Back then, it was a dark age for the Chosŏn popular music scene, with nowhere even to request lyrics or compositions from others. So, we few friends joined hands and moved forward. Mr. Kim Yonghwan is not just a singer but a genius who also composes and writes lyrics. Since he originally loved and studied music, he now mostly composes the songs he sings himself. However, among the songs I wrote, for the "Chosŏn March," I put a lot of effort into figuring out how to make him sing it smoothly and coolly, like flowing water from a green mountain, with that endlessly powerful voice of his. I believe it turned out well not because I wrote the lyrics <155> well, but because he sang it well. For music, good health is paramount. Mr. Kim Yonghwan seemed somewhat frail in his youth, but after improving his health, he has been a picture of health since joining our company, never falling ill, and has a large physique (富大). That's why his vocal volume (聲量) is excellent. Even if he sings all night, he doesn't get tired or struggle; the sound just resonates from his chest. This is a special talent (特才) others don't possess. More so than what I wrote, songs like "Tumangang Paessagong" (Tumen River Boatman) or "Kukkyŏngŭi Pam" (Night at the Border) display his remarkable talent (驍特) for praising the three thousand ri of mountains and rivers. When I give lyrics I've written to Mr. Kim Yonghwan, he composes them according to his vocal range and goes into recording. When he sings as if weeping or pleading, even those beside him are moved to tears. When he draws out notes long, thinly, and with turns, he does it effortlessly, singing powerfully and vigorously. Also, he never uses an amplifier, even before large audiences. Some struggle due to insufficient vocal volume, but Mr. Kim Yonghwan never uses an amplifier. Here, one can hear his true singing voice directly. Like a cuckoo in a green grove, he truly makes the three thousand ri of mountains and rivers tremble, and once Mr. Kim's record is released, it inevitably makes the hearts of young men and women leap.

"Chosŏn T'aryŏng," Sung by Mr. Kang Hongsik
Chŏn Kiyŏn

Joyful! Let's bless Chosŏn.
This was the first song that satisfied my heart. Although I composed many songs among many, I always desired something manly. Then, around that time, just after I moved to Columbia, the first thing I tackled was the composition for "Chosŏn T'aryŏng" (Chosŏn Ballad). Since I had come from Polydor after composing up to "Kodoŭi Chŏnghan," I felt I had gained some experience by then. So, "Chosŏn T'aryŏng" was a powerful work (力作) by the poet Yu Tosun; it was truly a masterpiece by Mr. Yu Tosun that depicted the spirit of Chosŏn without reservation. This song is a prayer for the happiness of our three thousand ri peninsula. From the spirit emanating from Paektu Mountain down to Halla Mountain on Cheju Island at the southern tip, the three thousand ri of mountains and rivers form a fertile, brocade-like landscape (錦繡江山), a beautiful garden 자랑 (pride) of the world. This is what we possess, and we are the people who should use what is ours. Thus, the lyrics written by poet Yu Tosun were lively, and perfectly matching them was Mr. Kang Hongsik. With his booming, energetic, and masculine voice, <156> he displayed absolutely no affectation aimed at receiving praise from others, just the straightforward singing of a vigorous man, resulting in a famous piece, a superb song (名曲絶唱). As soon as it came out, the peninsula was shaken.
The fact that it is currently being taught at a certain girls' school in the city likely makes Kang Hongsik's "Chosŏn T'aryŏng" the only popular song used this way. As each phrase and note passes, one's shoulders naturally start to dance. The song, sung coolly like flowing water from a green mountain with a powerful voice, is indeed one that gives strength and revitalization (蘇生). You can tell just by looking at the lyrics.

When Writing "T'ahyangsari," Sung by Mr. Ko Poksu
Kim Nŭngin

Leaving my hazy hometown Kimch'ŏn in Hwanghae Province, it's already been 11 years! Last autumn, when I walked through a "foreign land" (他鄕), it marked exactly the 11th autumn of my wandering life (漂泊生活). Sending my poignant homesickness (鄉愁) towards the blue sky and flowing clouds, I leaned against the train window. It happened to be dusk, the streetlights seemed lonely, and the fallen leaves on the roadside trees were so desolate. Tears flowed without reason, wetting my collar. Thus, the poem "T'ahyangsari" (Life in a Foreign Land) was purely my honest confession. I have written lyrics for over 100 popular songs so far, but most were done out of responsibility, largely being what you might call literary games (文字遊戲). Among them, however, regardless of its poetic value, "T'ahyangsari" was written according to my conscience, without inserting beautiful phrases or clever words (美文妙句). Fortunately, this too gained the sympathy of my close friend and colleague Son Mongnin, resulting in a fitting melody. Furthermore, perhaps because Mr. Ko Poksu found similarities with his own situation, he sang it very plaintively, which led many people to know this song. The fact that "T'ahyangsari" sold well makes me think that there are unexpectedly many young people leading wandering lives like mine, which doesn't leave me with a very good feeling.

Composing "T'ahyangsari," Sung by Mr. Ko Poksu
Son Mongnin

Our situation, where whether you or I have a home, it doesn't feel like we do!
Seeing the lyrics for "T'ahyangsari," feelings unexpectedly surged, and I thought of the sentiments of some poet I saw abroad, singing by the Nile River. So, out of empathy, I looked at the poem again and again, never tiring of it, and decided to compose music for it. I believe I composed it over several days with a weeping heart. This happened to be the first test <157> for Mr. Ko Poksu. For my part, I composed it by associating it with some ideal image of a hometown.

Composing "Pommaji," Sung by Yi Nanyŏng
Mun Howŏl

When I saw "Pommaji" (Welcoming Spring) from Mr. Yun Sŏkchung, the lyrics seemed truly good. So, wanting to sing it with the feeling of spring and become a garden of renewal (蘇生), I was brimming with excitement. Just then, I called Yi Nanyŏng for practice, and her voice seemed to fit perfectly. Listening to her with the feeling of being immersed in a spring garden, and thinking of the wondrous melody (妙曲) while excited, I composed it right then. It seems that composing in that state drew more popularity than composing over two or three days. I suppose things must arise from that specific mood. Honestly, when the timing or mood is bad, no matter how hard I try, it doesn't work. After the lyrics and composition are done, the singer must perform well. As they say, even thieves need coordination. Although these are songs sung by the nightingales of the street in Chosŏn, since they are presented to the public, perhaps if the lyricist, composer, and singer all share the same feeling, the listeners also experience that same feeling. I hope everyone becomes "Pommaji" (welcomes spring) from it.
Mr. Yun Sŏkchung's "Pommaji" truly depicts a spring garden where skylarks sing, small flowers sprout amidst the rising haze, old worries disappear, and a sweet, cheerful melody is infused throughout. Yi Nanyŏng's singing is young and tender. And her clever, crisp singing is like rolling jade beads (玉珠), a naturally beautiful voice (美音玉聲).
Upon first hearing this song, anyone feels intoxicated by spring, cheerful, energized, and happy. Such a voice is considered good even in our education circles, and Nanyŏng's voice is perfectly suited for children's plays (童劇).
Anyone hearing Yi Nanyŏng's singing realizes her youth and feels affection for her.
Abroad, many children's songs and plays (童謠童劇) are recorded, but not yet in Chosŏn. It seems Yi Nanyŏng is poised to contribute to the Chosŏn education world by covering children's songs and plays in the future. The more I hear this young, delicate, lovely, slender voice, the more I want to listen. As flower buds are about to open and the grass turns green, the spring welcome of youth sings of joyous happiness and pleasure. Indeed, no one but Yi Nanyŏng could infuse the spring welcome of youth ("Pommaji") with such joy, as if intoxicated. <158>

Composing "Kaŭlbam," Sung by Ms. Chŏn Ok
Chŏn Kiyŏn

I lament that I never learned composition systematically. When I was 15, there was a teacher who gave me private lessons, who was then in Chosŏn but is now a conductor (콘탁) for the Osaka Civic Music [Association?]. After that, due to life circumstances, I entered the Inch'ŏn Municipal Office (仁川府廳) and stayed for 7 or 8 years. After returning, while studying composition methods, I happened to mention my ambitions to Mr. Wang P'yŏng, currently at Polydor, and he introduced me, leading to my joining Polydor. So, it was my first time joining the company, and also Ms. Chŏn Ok's first recording upon joining. Listening to Ms. Chŏn Ok's voice, it seemed gentle, just like her personality. Her voice was gentle, unadorned, without embellishment (꾸임), and pitifully lovely (애련한). At that time, I had gone to Sinch'ŏn Hot Springs in Hwanghae Province. It was an autumn night, the autumn moon hung high in the middle of the blue sky, a gentle breeze brushed my sleeves, and countless stars twinkled – an autumn night! Drawing upon the association (련상) from that night, I wrote the lyrics myself. Holding onto that association, I composed the music to match Ms. Chŏn Ok's pitifully lovely voice. Fortunately, my composition matched Ms. Chŏn Ok's voice, and when we tested "Kaŭlbam" (Autumn Night), it sounded truly good. So, they said it sold and sold, claiming their hands were busy (두손이 밧벗다) at that time. Did "Kaŭlbam" only sell well then? No, they say it still sells steadily even now. Indeed, Ms. Chŏn Ok's singing isn't the type to receive polarizing critiques like "Wow, it's great!" or "Bah, it's bad!"; her voice is that of an eternal songstress (萬年歌姬). Whenever you listen, you don't tire of it, and even decades or centuries later, it will likely still be generally well-liked.
Through Ms. Chŏn Ok's song, the world came to know that I compose, and Ms. Chŏn Ok also became known to the world through my lyrics and composition, so "Kaŭlbam" was the launching pad for both our careers.
For this reason, as it was the first piece I composed upon first joining the company, it is a work into which I put much effort.

Composing "Chŏnghanŭi Pŏdŭl," Sung by Mr. Ch'oe Namyong
Yi Kiyŏng

I don't have much particular to say, but since your company asks, I will offer a few words.
Mr. Ch'oe Namyong, who already possessed brilliant fame <159> as a key figure in the Peninsula's record scene (歌壇), resigned from his position at a certain company and joined hands with T'aep'yŏng Records (태평회사), where I am, just this past March. When he first joined, he released works under the pen name Ku Yongp'o (具龍布), so everyone must have watched his works with particular attention (括目) out of a kind of curiosity. Thus, for his first work upon joining, he released a popular song called "Chŏnghanŭi Pŏdŭl" (Willow of Sorrowful Regret) with my humble composition, and the fact that it received considerable positive reviews is likely due to the power of his popularity.
At that time, I decided to compose for him. As any composer commonly experiences, not knowing the singer's vocal range (聲量) and special skills (特技) causes considerable hidden difficulty.
Since then, as he has sung many of my works, I now fully understand his capabilities (기량). His vocal cords are extremely clear and powerful yet gentle, and he possesses skillful talent (재조) in beautifully expressing the melody's flow (施律).
Some time ago, he began composing at the request of the Osaka head office, and we released our first collaborative work, "Kori Tatsunute" (古里たつぬて - Visiting the Old Hometown), as a Japanese record. Unexpectedly, it created a sensation (센세이슌). Ultimately, this sounds like boasting about myself, but anyway, composing is not that easy, as anyone who has walked this path (斯道) surely knows. From now on, I intend only to devote my humble and insufficient abilities to the best of my power so that he can fully display his fine talent. <160>

**Summary**

This article compiles memoirs from several prominent Korean composers and lyricists of the mid-1930s, reflecting on the process and challenges of creating hit songs for ten top singers identified through a magazine poll. Each contributor discusses a specific song, detailing the inspiration, the collaboration with the singer (Ch'ae Kyuyŏp, Sŏnu Ilsŏn, Wang Supok, Kim Pokhŭi, Kim Yonghwan, Kang Hongsik, Ko Poksu, Yi Nanyŏng, Chŏn Ok, Ch'oe Namyong), and their assessment of the singer's unique vocal qualities and appeal. The memoirs highlight the significant role of the burgeoning record industry (selling 1.2 million records annually in Chosŏn), the creative struggles behind popular hits, and the personal connections between creators and performers.

For historians, the article offers valuable insights into the cultural landscape of colonial Korea, particularly the popular music scene. It reveals the scale and commercial dynamics of the record industry, the interplay between Japanese and Korean musical influences (mention of Japanese training, stage names like Hasegawa Ichirō, imitation of kouta), the emphasis on expressing "Chosŏn sentiment" (朝鮮情調) even within popular genres, and the personal stories behind nationally recognized songs like "T'ahyangsari," which resonated with the widespread experience of displacement and longing for home during that era. The discussion of singers' vocal styles and the composers' efforts to match them provides a window into contemporary aesthetic standards.

「거리의 꾀꼬리」인 十大歌手를 내보낸 作曲·作詞者의 苦心記
一百二十萬張
이것이 일년 동안에 조선시장에서 팔니는 레코-드 수효다. 이 중에 조선소리판이 그 1/3쯤은 된다. 엇잿든 매년 4, 50만장의 구매자를 가지고 잇고 그 노래를 돗고 즐기는 사람을 수십만명 수백만명을 가지고 잇는 레코-드게는 참으로 부럽고도 빗나는 존재라 아니 할 수 업다. 그러면 이 120만장을 움지기는 이는 누구누구들인가. 그는 오직 「거리의 꾀꼬리 」인 가수들이다.
그런데 가수라 하여도 여러 종류와 여러 게급이 잇다. 이제 본사에서 인긔투표하여 본 결과 10대가수가 당선되엿다. 이분들이 월계관을 쓴 분이라 볼 것이다. 이러케 생각하여 볼 때에 이러한 유명한 가수를 낫키에 얼마나 작곡자와 작사자들의 숨은 눈물겨운 로력이 잇섯든가. 이 평풍 뒤의 꼿구경을 엿보려 이 붓을 옴겨보는 바이다.

全基玹-
