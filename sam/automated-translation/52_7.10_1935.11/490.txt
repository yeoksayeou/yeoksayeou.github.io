Magazine Title: 삼천리 제7권 제10호
Publication Date: 1935년 11월 01일
Article Title: (戱曲) 巴里의 그 女子 ((Play) That Woman in Paris)
Author: 羅蕙錫
Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

羅蕙錫 (Na Hye-sŏk)
Act 1
Place: A hotel in Paris (巴里)
Characters
A Her husband
B She
C Friend
D Friend

Someone knocks firmly on the door of D's hotel room.
D: "Who is it? Come in."
(C quickly opens the door and steps briskly inside.)
D: "Who is this? When did you arrive?"
(The two shake hands as if to crush them.)
C: "I just got here."<280>
D: "You should have sent a postcard at least. Anyway, it's very good to see you."
(Shakes his hand again)
(Clumsily plops down onto a chair.)
D: "But what brings you here? It's not even vacation time, and so suddenly."
C: "I'll tell you gradually."
(The two are silent for a moment. C first catches his breath.)
D: "So, how have things been?"
C: "What fun is there in a widower's life in a foreign land? It's always tasteless."
D: "Why, don't you even have a female companion? You're still stubborn."
C: "What use are a hundred of those types who just act cute to try and squeeze money out of me?"
D: "Although you say that true love arises only when circumstances and situations match, your kind of love is scientific."
C: "Well, love also differs according to the times. Can one sit in the 20th century and practice 19th or 18th-century love?"
D: "Hey now. Does love have eras? Does it have ideology and ideals? Isn't love just passion (熱) and affection (情), nothing else?"
C: "No, you've misunderstood. Why wouldn't love have ideology and ideals?"
D: "Then that love is already a calculated love."
C: "Can it continue if it's not calculated?"
D: "That sounds a bit disappointing."
C: "It's all a headache. One needs to have tasted it at least once to have the right to discuss it this way or that."
D: "Well, it can be fun, and also upsetting."
C: "By the way, friend, which hotel are A and his wife staying at now?"
D: "They left yesterday."
C: "Just yesterday? (With a look of shock) Oh no... then B went too?"
D: "Of course. Doesn't the thread follow where the needle goes? But why are you so surprised?"
C: "No, it's just..."
(For a moment, he says nothing, chin in hand, staring at the floor, looking disheartened.)
C: "Where did they go? They must have gone to America (米洲), right?"
D: "It seems so."
C: "Did many people see them off?"
D: "I wouldn't know. I didn't."
C: "What do you mean?"<281>
D: "Look here. What connection do we have with people who frequent those kinds of circles?"
C: (Pauses briefly, respecting D's opinion.) "No, I'm saying she's not someone to be treated like that. By the way, did you meet A and his wife the day before they left?"
D: "I did meet them the day before they left."
C: "And what was B's demeanor like?"
D: "That B, who used to be so cheerful, was strangely disheartened."
C: "Hmm. A pitiful woman."
D: "Why do you say that?"
C: "The worry is she has too much talent (재조)."
D: "So what?"
C: "Because of that talent, she lacks decisiveness in one thing and misses all the precious opportunities."
D: "Talent? What talent? Who doesn't have that much talent? She couldn't even speak a word clearly."
C: "Verbal talent is just one part."
D: "Why do you say that?"
C: "Speech requires connecting various ends of words, so someone focused intensely on one thing might not do it well."
D: "Anyway, she must have considerable experience."
C: "Yes, considerable. Moreover, her past actions are all original and artistic. Anyway, she's not a woman to be taken lightly."
D: "What's the big deal? How outstanding can she be? A is also at fault. He should have left his wife at home to manage the household and come alone."
C: "There are specific people for that."
D: "What use is it for a woman to see splendid things? It just increases her vanity."
C: "Korean people, regardless of gender, need to increase their vanity a lot."
D: "You always say things differently from others."
C: "The outward words are convention and form; the inner words are truth and life."
D: "Here comes the philosophy again. Meeting you is tedious yet interesting."
C: "Knowing that makes me grateful."
D: "But why is B praised so highly?"
C: "It's not praising, it's recognizing her worth. She's a lovable woman."
D: "But when did you get to know B so well? It seems suspicious no matter what."
C: "One can know much sufficiently through her words (言語), actions (行動), and writings (文字)."
D: "No, when A and his wife went to England (英國) last time, something good must have definitely happened."<282>
C: "No, absolutely not. Those are dangerous words."
D: "Dangerous? What's dangerous? Where do you think this is? It's Europe (구라파)."
C: "Of course, we understood and respected each other, but..."
D: "Alright. It's almost all out now."
C: "But not being able to meet them this time is a great setback." (Ignoring D's words)
D: "So that's why you came. All the way from London (론돈)."
(As if he finally understands for sure.)
C: "To be honest, I rushed here because I wanted to advise A to let B stay and study in Paris (巴里) or London for another year."
D: "Is there a need for that? She's not at an age to study now, and being separated from her husband isn't fun. Isn't it ultimately a matter of tuition fees?"
C: "The money for returning to America (미국) would be sufficient for the tuition, so it would have been fine if only A had agreed. That's why."
D: "What's the need to stay longer?"
C: "As someone with a supposed specialty, how much could she have learned in just a few months? Having come to this difficult-to-reach place, isn't it a loss, both personally and socially, if she can't complete something?"
D: "Then B could have stayed in Paris, why London?"
C: "Because when B came to England, she showed interest in women's issues. If she had spent some time in London researching women's issues before leaving, whatever she wrote would have been beneficial to Korean society, wouldn't it? Anyway, if I had come just one day earlier, whether it worked out or not, I could have at least suggested it. That's why."
D: "In my opinion, B did well to follow her husband. Middle age is the most difficult time for couples. Being in an intermediate stage, neither this nor that, she must have many unspoken pains. If B is as intelligent as you think, she wouldn't be able to keep her composure in a place like this. If she could grasp a firm rope there, she would become an exceptional person, but is that easy? If she just drifted along, she would only become a ruined woman. It's fortunate she left this place at that juncture."
C: "That's true. I wanted to see B overcome that juncture. I hoped that an invaluable woman could emerge in Korea (조선) as well. B has that potential, so it's regrettable."
D: "It still seems suspicious. Tell me everything."
C: "Don't talk such nonsense. What kind of affair is it with someone else's wife?"
D: "One should transcend such things."
C: "That may be true, but still..."
D: "Even now, if you took a plane and chased after them, you could meet them. Don't you have that much courage?"<283>
C: "I have it, but I endure."
D: "It seems so. Stop hinting little by little and speak up like a man."
C: "There has to be something to say. But on what grounds are you teasing me like this?"
D: "The relationship between a man and a woman can be cut cleanly into two, like with a knife."
C: "Alright. And so?"
D: "Between a man and a woman, there's only mutual liking or mutual dislike. Thus, finding fault with each other is due to a lack of love. Praising each other is because of mutual love. One observes that and makes conjectures."
C: "So, things like friendship or comradeship don't exist?"
D: "Those are all buried within that one love."
C: "That's too simple and clear."
D: "That's why. I'm saying I can guess the degree of love between you and B."
C: "So, what degree is it?"
D: "If it were a ladder, you're about halfway up."
C: "Isn't there a chance it could reach one hundred percent? Hahahaha."
D: "Well, it could, but since the other party has left, how can it?"
D: "Why don't you praise B some more?"
C: "Look here. Doesn't everyone, after wandering around, unconsciously sit back down in the seat they were in before? I find this quite strange. People fundamentally have a conservative heart. B too will ultimately fail to escape her environment and end up back where she started (도로묵이)."
D: "That's not a simple issue. Besides oneself, one has family, society, and life, so one can't just live stubbornly according to one's own will."
C: "Then if everyone goes back to square one, where is social progress? Is there anyone but those who stubbornly push forward? There need to be people who take leaps."
D: "You mean stubborn people like you."
C: "No, that's different. I'm not saying I'm like that, but it's frustrating to see young people who are neither cold nor hot, just lukewarm."
D: "Right. So you're saying B is also lukewarm. It seems she hasn't received passionate love yet."
C: "Once women get older and understand the ways of the world, they lose all flavor."
D: "Aren't men the same?"
C: "Women are faster. But B is different."
D: "You're quite taken with her."<284>
C: "Absolutely not."
D: "Why are you playing innocent like this? There are ways to figure it all out."
C: "Don't be ridiculous. The only regret is not meeting A and his wife before they left. It would have been good if B could have stayed for another year."
D: "Listening to you, it sounds plausible."
C: "If she even knew English properly, she could read good books and introduce them, couldn't she?"
D: "That's true. With languages, you first learn to use them and speak them, and only then does reading become easy."
C: "That's true, but can one be good at everything? If you go to a museum and look at the specialty displayed in one corner, you realize how little people know. If you meet Einstein and talk to him, outside his specialty, he's a complete fool."
D: "That's how modern people are."
C: "Right. Because modern culture requires knowing not just broadly, but also deeply."
D: "Yes. There's no bigger fool than a person with just one specialty."
C: "Right. It's a world where you can't pretend to know something you only know a little about."
D: "It's already past one. Let's sleep now."
The two lie down in one bed, turn their backs to each other, and enter the land of dreams.

Act 2
Place: E's apartment in New York (뉴-욕)
Characters
E
F E's wife
G Friend
H Friend
E, F, G, H sit scattered here and there, casually chatting.
G: "Did another letter come from A, Mr. H?"
F: "Where are A and his wife now?"
H: "They are probably on the Pacific Ocean (太平洋) by now. I received a letter this morning that they sent upon leaving San Francisco (쌘푸란시스코)."
E: "Was there any particular news?"
H: "He said he had many reflections, and it seems he saw all the articles about his recent incident in the Sinminbo in San Francisco."
E: "So, what did he say about seeing them?"
H: "He said he felt sorry towards us."<285>
E: "Tell him to learn how fearsome the world is and pull himself together a bit."
G: "He's too good a person."
H: "Yes, there needs to be a sharp person behind him, constantly giving him pointers."
E: "He has his sharp wife B, what's the worry?" (Somewhat mockingly)
G: "If she were sharp, would she have caused this incident?"
F: "That's right. What kind of behavior was that? Not protecting her husband properly."
H: "When women learn a bit of scholarship (學問), such setbacks often happen." (Half-jokingly)
F: "Would everyone really do that?"
G: "Indeed, this incident was a major mistake by A and B."
E: "A mistake, absolutely. Why did B take a friend along, leaving only her husband, and why did A attend that gathering instead of going?"
H: "Well, that's all in the past, let's stop talking about it and think about what to do in the future."
E: "If we, as supporters of A, try to defend him, the matter will only escalate. There's no choice but to support K and apologize to the Koreans in America."
H: "Then aren't A and his wife being abandoned?"
E: "What? It'll be a temporary uproar, and since it's a Korean affair, it will likely fizzle out."
H: "That may be true, but A and his wife are people who should be cherished from any perspective. After all, they are people who have done work and will do work."
G: "Absolutely. Making noise here and working back in the homeland will be entirely different."
H: "Absolutely."
G: "Anyway, I hope it gets resolved well."
E: "It seems B hasn't calmed down yet."
G: "Even in the words of Confucius (孔子), 'At thirty, I stood firm.' If she still can't calm down, what is she to do?"
H: "Her having learned a bit is the big problem. Scholarship is something one learns, creates, and then digests. But when it just goes up and down the throat, it's frustrating, stifling, and irritating. A person needs to have an open quality for conversation to be enjoyable and for influence to occur. Such people need the Bible, but..."
G: "Do artists (藝術家) need religion?"
H: "Just as Jesus was crucified for all mankind, didn't artists like Shakespeare or Tolstoy agonize, suffer, and try to resolve things concerning all mankind and the entire universe? Truly, their hearts were great, their art beautiful, and also magnificent."<286>
H: "Therefore, it's a matter of people rather than scholarship. Koreans (조선사람) all need to live piercing one fold deeper. They need to find something to grasp tightly and live by its strength. If Koreans continue to degenerate like this, their spirit will completely die out. (Everyone is silent) People pass away, heaven and earth change; in this world where enduring, unmoving things exist, what should we hope for, what should we rely on to live? How beautiful it is to firmly grasp just that one light that illuminates our inner person, and gain joy, strength, and infinite value from it."
G: "Those are correct words."
"It's late, let's go."
(Everyone stands up.)

Act 3
Place: Wŏnsan (元山) Beach
Characters
J B's Lover (愛人)
L J's Disciple
M L's wife
B: "The scenery is very nice, isn't it?" (Walking side by side along the shore)
J: "Yes. So, how do you feel being back in your home country?"
B: "It feels like I've come to a foreign country again."
J: "That must be so. By the way, why were there no letters?"
B: "One doesn't write letters to someone they trust."
J: "Eloquent, aren't you?"
B: "Look at this seawater. It's blue, deep, and plentiful like this, but the cause must lie in the waters flowing together from various mountain valleys, right?"
J: "And so?"
B: "So, I mean, my heart is like this seawater after touring Europe and America (歐米)."
J: "You have a very promising future."
B: "Why?"
J: "Because you say you're deep, vast, and blue like that ocean; what's inside must be boundless."
B: "That's not what I mean. I mean it's all jumbled up (뒤죽박죽)."
J: "You mean like bibimbap."
B: "So to speak, yes."
J: "If you only know the taste of bibimbap, it's fantastic, though."
B: "Exactly."
J: "Understanding like that is what 'exactly' means."
B: "They say scholars (샌님) only look down on servants (종)."<287>
J: "That's not necessarily true."
B: "Right."
J: "Anyway, you must have gained much."
B: "Just as the colors of the mountains and rivers (山川) become clear after rain, something to firmly grasp for the future has emerged."
J: "For whom, might I ask?"
B: "That's right." (Looks at J and smiles faintly. J gently pats B's cheek.)
B: "What are your impressions, Teacher?"
J: "Before I toured Europe and America, I had infinite hope, but after returning, infinite despair arose."
B: "What caused that?"
J: "Compared to the countries of Europe (구라파), Korea (조선) has nothing. It's barren land (荒蕪地), a desert (沙漠). The time until seeds sown there sprout is pitch dark, and during that time, they will advance ceaselessly. While there must be people in Korea who take one step, two steps, there also needs to be someone who takes one tremendous leap. That's why figures in a transitional period have good opportunities, but leaping is very difficult. Especially since there's a populace (民衆) binding them tightly from front and back, trying to push them into a ditch."
J: "Why don't you take a leap first?"
B: "Then, exactly."
J: "You once told me, 'I want my life to be a masterpiece.' Didn't you?"
B: "Yes. I did. My present (現在) life is like that, and my future (將來) life will be too."
J: "How so?"
B: "In the world, there are things that seem small but are great, seem weak but are strong, seem mundane but are magnificent, and seem strange yet lack substance. The general Korean populace still hasn't realized the small yet strong, the mundane yet magnificent. Westerners realized that thousands of years ago after the Greek (希臘) and Roman (羅馬) civilizations (文明). Building upon that foundation is today's civilization. Therefore, their thoughts are not fantasies but systematic, and their actions are not vanity but practical (實際的)."
J: "That's true. I agree completely."
B: "That was rather presumptuous, wasn't it?"
B: "No."
B: "Truly, that time was wonderful."
J: "Yes. That time was the best for me too."
B: "There was never a time when all conditions and feelings were as free as then."<288>
J: "Now, the pressure from the outside is severe, so how can it be as happy as then?"
B: "It was truly happy. Moreover, love in middle age (中年期) is a fearsome thing."
J: "How so?"
B: "Love in youth is blind; love in middle age is conscious. There might be no difference in passion (熱) and affection (情), but knowing one's own actions seems uniquely interesting, empowering, and stylish."
J: "You greedy thing."
B: "I'm not being greedy; fate makes it so."
"I'm greedy too, but Teacher, you're also greedy, despite your age (나히)."
J: "Yes. I am very happy. Until now, I have thought as I pleased hundreds, thousands, tens of thousands of times. But reality mercilessly blows all that away. Even so, I continued to fantasize ceaselessly. No matter what, it was fantasy, not reality. Then I saw you. I thought that when I saw you again, my illusion would disappear. But that person is now before me, and I am saying I love her. This is too unexpected an event. Isn't this too overwhelming a reality?"
B: "I have no words to say. Shall we drown ourselves in that water?" (Takes J's arm)
J: "Hearing those words makes me happier. Being alive feels like a magic lantern (幻燈), and realizing that one is dead upon opening one's eyes feels real."
B: "When the eyes are plucked out, it feels like death."
J: "Being alive feels like a dream."
"Let's die, let's die. Just as one feels ashamed of fearing death, I feel ashamed of fearing life."
B: "Ah, the wind is refreshing too."
(Feeling the sea breeze)
J: "Water is a good thing."
B: "Absolutely. Water is something always welcome whenever seen."
J: "Water gives a feeling of intimacy."
B: "Yes. I wish I could possess eternity like the seawater."
J: "It depends on one's mindset."
B: "That's true, but the human heart (人心) changes from morning to evening (朝夕變), so what's the point of living?"
J: "Can't you start by practicing not being like that yourself?"
B: "Well..."
J: "Look, L and his wife are coming over there."
B: "I see."<289>
(L and M approach quickly.)
LM: "Hello, Teacher."
(They bow towards J and B.)
J: "Ah, this is fun. A harmonious couple."
L: "Teacher, you are the one having fun."
J: "What fun is there for old people? It's your youthful days that are fun."
L: "What are you saying? Stylish Teacher. Stylish Teacher B. How could it not be fun?"
J: "Excessive style is rather bland."
M: "Did you swim a lot? There were many clams over there, are there any here too?"
B: "We caught this many too."
(Shows the clams wrapped in a towel.)
J: "Let's combine them and make soup."
L: "Agreed. That sounds good."
M: "It will probably taste even better then."
J: "It's hot. I need to get in the water."
B: "We'll go in too."
The four people, dressed in revealing bathing suits, enter the churning sea, swimming, some skillfully, some awkwardly. Harmonizing with the sound of the waves, a chorus rings out.
Ehera dehera
Ehee ehra
Eogiyeocha the boat departs
I am going, I am going<290>

**Summary**

This three-act play by Na Hye-sŏk depicts the experiences and inner turmoil of B, a Korean woman, presumably representing the author herself or the "New Woman" ideal. Act 1 shows her friend C lamenting B's departure from Paris with her husband A, believing she missed a chance for further self-development in Europe. Act 2 shifts to New York, where acquaintances discuss a scandal involving A and B, debating their worthiness of support despite societal judgment and reflecting on the challenges faced by returning intellectuals. Act 3 finds B in Wŏnsan, Korea, with her lover J, discussing freedom, societal constraints, the intensity of middle-aged love, and her intellectual and emotional growth after her time abroad, culminating in a shared moment of liberation by the sea. The play offers a glimpse into the complex lives of early 20th-century Korean intellectuals, particularly women, navigating tradition, modernity, personal desires, and societal expectations in colonial Korea. Historians might find the candid discussion of marital dissatisfaction, extramarital affairs, the critique of Korean society's limitations by those returning from abroad, and the articulation of a modern, independent female consciousness particularly interesting, reflecting the social and intellectual currents of the era.
