A Roster of Noblewomen on the Streets of Seoul
						Yi Sŏ-gu

You asked me to write about the young ladies of Seoul, didn't you? As the deadline passed two, three times and I was cornered, I had no choice but to follow precedent and rush off to Yŏnsu-gak (延壽閣) outside Dongdaemun. At Yŏnsu-gak, there's a kind old woman who specially prepares a quiet room, manuscript paper, and writing tools for my inadequate writing, and welcomes me whenever she sees me, bringing them out gladly. Although she's a Tokyo woman who doesn't even know Korean writing, she rejoices as if her own work has been published whenever something I wrote comes out on a record (『레코-드』) or appears in a magazine. Therefore, while worldly philanderers seek out Yŏnsu-gak to quietly dally with their lovers, I alone often dash off there feeling like the young master of a millionaire going to his villa just to write. Instead, if I were to have a female companion, it would usually be someone of the ‘relative’ (親戚) class, and if a woman were to stay late into the night, it would only be my wife – the one who tends to my house, my younger siblings, and the shrine of my ancestors (시하라바님). Thus, this time too, because the people from Samch'ŏlli-sa (三千里社) came looking for the manuscript too many times and left empty-handed, my foolish wife (愚妻), feeling sorry for me, suggested,
"Honey, why don't you go out to Yŏnsu-gak?"
"Well... the autumn leaves on the back hill should be worth seeing."
"The cosmos must be in full bloom too."
"Right. Shall I become the grave keeper for Princess Myŏngon (明溫公主) again?"
This is because Yŏnsu-gak was a house attached to the tomb of Princess Myŏngon.

Sŏnin hyŏng! <192>
And so, I found myself setting foot in Yŏnsu-gak again after what felt like two months. It had been two months since I scribbled seven song lyrics for Lucky (『럭키』) Records here in one night, during which time I was mostly traveling to Tokyo or the provinces and wasn't in Seoul. I took along a Lucky Record, released during that time, as a gift for the proprietress.

*Holding back the bitter sorrow in my heart,*
*Smiling outwardly is what a man does.*

*Meeting, pretending not to see, passing by,*
*Turning back and crying is what a man does.*

Though she couldn't read the words, when I translated the meaning, she deeply resonated (共鳴) with the short song (小曲) called “A Man’s Love” (사나히 사랑). Thinking of this, I picked out Lucky’s “A Man’s Love” and set out.

Sŏnin hyŏng!
Going alone was boring, taking a lover would be troublesome, dragging my wife along felt cumbersome, and male companions were no fun. Anyway, I headed out towards Jongno. Half my life has been spent rushing back and forth on this street. How many times have I given my love on this street, and how many times have I forgotten it? Today too, thinking that surely someone would join me if I just went out onto this street, I strolled down from Hwasin Department Store, swinging my walking stick (『스데끼』). Until Nakwŏn Annex, there was no gain.

"Oh well. Let's go into the Annex." Thinking maybe the princesses of Nakwŏn might be gathered, whispering amongst themselves, I entered, and surprise! A Western-dressed beauty was making a phone call. Peeking over her shoulder – what a pretty young lady! A beauty of the eyes, a beauty of the lips, a beauty of the nose.
"This girl is quite charming (妙)."
Tapping her shoulder,
"Isn't it frustrating talking to someone whose face you can't see?"
"Hmph! Why don't you turn me around then?"
"Because I'm going to the front."
"That seems a bit frustrating."
Here, I was completely defeated.

Thinking, "This one's worthwhile, quite good at repartee," I reached out and firmly grabbed her hand, leaving only the thumb showing from the five fingers dangling whitely from her red sleeve cuff.
"Ouch! Who said I dislike you?"
"Let's sit down."
"After making me sit,"
"Are you scared of me?"
"Hmph—"
"Anyway, sit down."
"Alright—I'm sitting, so what's the next program..."
"Appetite—"
"Hasn't the autumn season just passed? <193>"
"Let's eat."
The meal began. She didn't ask my name, nor I hers, yet we finished the meal like an interesting married couple.
"Alright—let's go."
"Where!"
"Are you scared of me?"
"Hmph—"
"Then how about the suburbs for autumn leaf viewing?"
"Let's go."
"Call a car."
I told the errand girl, but
"Oh no. Never mind."
She immediately followed up with the cancellation.
"Let's go out, walk a bit, and hail one." From this remark, I gathered this girl hadn't been back long from a big city like Tokyo or Osaka.
"Whatever you say."
And so, the two of us arrived quite cheerfully at the main gate of Yŏnsu-gak.

Sŏnin hyŏng!
This is merely the preface. The story of the woman I intend to tell is actually about a young lady I newly met upon arriving at Yŏnsu-gak, not this young lady. If, within the stories of these two young ladies, you can catch even a small glimpse of the path modern women walk, there would be no greater joy.

Sŏnin hyŏng!
Caught by the hand of the welcoming old woman, I settled into the North Annex (北別室). I clapped my hands, and a bobbed-hair girl (斷髮娘) I hadn't seen before entered.
"Wow—this one's a genuine article. What's your name?"
"Uh—Akiko!"
Her head bowed, her reply was faint.
"Where did you come from?"
"Kyushu (九州)."
"Where in Kyushu?"
"Yamaguchi (山口)."
"What did you say? Isn't Yamaguchi in Honshu (本州)?"
At this, she burst out laughing and ran away. She was of medium height, her voice clear, her face chocolate-colored, her nose long and high, and her eyes sparkled with spirit (英氣). No matter how I looked at her, she didn't seem like a woman who would be working as a maid in such a mountain place. I asked the old woman about her, and she said she was a young lady who had been staying there for fun for about 10 days.
"She's so mischievous, it's killing me," she said, though without much of an annoyed expression.
"Hmm... her way of speaking isn't vulgar."
"Yes, rumor has it she's the daughter of a wealthy country family, but ran away because of her fiery temper and is wandering around."
"That's quite common among young ladies these days."
After lunch, as I was about to head to the back mountain, she was already up on Princess Myŏngon's tomb (明溫公主墓), eating roasted chestnuts with specialized school (전문학교) students from over the mountain. She apparently goes to the Maeil Pot'ong Hakkyo (每日普通學校) grounds every day to learn bicycling, falling and stumbling as she wanders the country roads, <194> though recently she's stopped falling. When the specialized school lets out, she borrows the gardener's (園丁) Western clothes (洋服), rides her bicycle to the front of the school, picks up the square-capped (四角帽) young gentlemen, brings them back, plays with them, and feeds them. One day, a young man named Yamamoto in the kitchen (廚房) jokingly asked,
"Akiko-san, just how many boyfriends do you have?"
She reportedly flew into a great rage (大怒), grabbed a knife, and threatened to cut off his head, causing a huge commotion. At night, too, if it's just drizzling, she'll get a car ride into the city center (市中), wander this street and that, pick up men, drag them around to her heart's content, then abandon them at a street corner and run off.
"Compared to the young ladies who put on a prim and proper act (시침을 딱 떼고 순진한 체하는), such a tomboy (말괄량이) is actually physically sounder (몸은 정말 성하답니다)."
The old woman seemed to have become firmly partisan to Akiko. When new guests arrived, Akiko (秋子) would stride into the guest room, pretend to be a maid, and took pleasure in watching the guests and beauties interact. Since she was fluent in Japanese (內地말), she would deceive guests by saying she'd only just arrived from Kyushu a few days ago, listen to men and women talk freely amongst themselves in Korean, then clap her hands and laugh alone. She too had a lover. Just one—but the way she treated even that lover was so tempestuous that onlookers felt unbearably sorry for the man. One day, her lover came out and they talked late into the night, then got into a fight. The man said he was going back to the city, but the old woman and the household staff managed to detain him with effort. At that time, Akiko just sniffed contemptuously, "Not even manly enough—saying you'll leave and then sitting back down—hmph, *obakasarata ne* (おばかさらたね)..." and didn't even glance his way. Yet, if he was even a day late in coming, she would face towards Seoul, throw stones, and repeatedly yell "Baka— Baka—" saying, "He's the only one in the world I know, and this is how things are." When it was time for dinner, I invited her to eat with us, but she protested, "No... no... the pretty lady will be angry," and ran off. When my companion followed to grab her, she yelled, "*Iyada! Yasegaman shinasaruna!* (イヤダ ヤセカマソ シナサルナ)" and dashed into her own room.
My companion was utterly dumbfounded,
"She's quite the radical."
"Hmph. So there are young ladies who can surprise even you."
"My dear! Someone like me is still in kindergarten."

The next day—unable to write the manuscript, I practically fled back home, vaguely put off the person from Samch'ŏlli-sa who came for the manuscript, and headed into the city center (市中) again. Feeling hungry in the evening sun (夕陽), I stopped by the Nakwŏn Annex, and surprise! Wasn't it Akiko?
"Hey!"
"Hey! <195>"
An unhesitating handshake!
"What brings you here?"
"Came for a drink."
"Starting in the daytime?"
"Can't I have even one cup of coffee?"
"I've lost again."
She sat down squarely, possessing a vitality greater than a man's. However, there was something about her that seemed deliberately put on. It felt as though, if she didn't act this way, there was some unbearable resentment she couldn't forget, and consequently, in situations where demure young ladies would cry, our Akiko would run out into the street and make noise.
"Don't you miss your Mama?"
"I keep them neatly folded."
"Keep what?"
"Photos..."
"Have you never felt sorry?"
"Hmph... Who am I trying to make cry?"
"Crying is good too."
"Don't wanna!"
At this, I grabbed her wrist, made her sit, sent word to Chŏngja-ok (丁字屋), and brought back a puzzle toy where you arrange number tiles (數字牌) from 1 to 15 in order. This puzzle takes at least 30 minutes, during which time one must calm the mind and focus the eyes on one spot.
"Here—play with this puzzle just once a day. Then your temper might calm down a bit."
"Thank you."
She took it and tried to solve it once, then said,
"How can anyone do this tedious thing?"
"No back talk, just do it once a day."
"Hmph, who are you trying to gently coax?"
"Alright! Want to make a bet with me?"
"About what?"
"Who finishes first."
"As if I can't solve this thing."
"That's why I suggested a bet."
"Hmph, *Makerumono ka* (マケルモノカ)."
Her aversion to losing stirred, she began the tedious puzzle. 5 minutes, 10 minutes, 15 minutes—as the numbers increasingly fell into place, she seemed to find it interesting.
"Wow—this is fun."
"That's why, take it with you and do it just once a day. Then your temper should calm down."
"O, K!"
She ran off again. The next day, I met the old woman from Yŏnsu-gak. She told me Akiko had brought the puzzle back that night and rejoiced, saying,
"Now, if I just have this thing, I too can calm my mind. I'll become a gentle young lady."
When she was around, she would accost anyone, yell about being hungry or there being no side dishes, making her a nuisance, but she also had a childish charm and sincerity, so the proprietress seemed to have grown deeply fond of her. "Sometimes I feel like I hear Akiko yelling and coming in from somewhere, and I can't stand it," the mistress said, her eyes welling up with tears. Akiko, who had vanished without a trace! Now (Continued on Page 200) <196> where might she be, shaking her fist at someone and yelling? She had definitely promised to appear at Sŏ Chŏng-gwŏn-gun's Boxing Match (徐廷權君拳鬪會場) in Seat No. 14, but there was no way to trust that either. If, on October 21st at 6:30 PM, at the special ring in Kyŏngsŏng Stadium (경성운동장), a strange woman is sitting in Designated Seat No. 14, please know that it is Akiko. If she doesn't show up, that seat will be left empty as for an absent host. <200>

**Summary**

The author, Yi Sŏ-gu, struggling with writer's block, recounts his recent experiences seeking inspiration. He first encounters a modern, witty young woman in Western dress at Nakwŏn Annex, whom he takes to Yŏnsu-gak, a retreat near Princess Myŏngon's tomb. There, he meets another young woman, Akiko, a rebellious Japanese girl staying at the inn. The narrative focuses on Akiko's boisterous, unconventional behavior – learning to bike, picking up male students, getting into fights, wandering the city – contrasting her with more traditional expectations. The author finds her intriguing, attempts to calm her fiery nature with a puzzle, and reflects on her sudden departure and unpredictable character, wondering if she will appear at an upcoming boxing match. From a historical perspective, the article offers insights into the changing social landscape of 1930s Seoul under Japanese rule. It portrays the emergence of the "modern girl" archetype, challenging traditional norms, interactions between Koreans and Japanese residents (including leisure spots like Yŏnsu-gak catering to both), the adoption of Western fashion and activities (bicycles, cafes, boxing), and glimpses of urban life and landmarks in colonial Seoul.
