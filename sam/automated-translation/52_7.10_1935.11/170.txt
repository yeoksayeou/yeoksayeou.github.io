Gold Deluxe Edition, Former Nurse Kim Chŏng-suk Who Still Holds Over Sixty Claims, The 300,000 Won Gold Mine Queen
						By Yŏsan

Although there are countless surprising and rare occurrences in the world, Ms. Kim Chŏng-suk (金貞淑), the Gold Mine Queen who, as a former nurse (看護婦) and telephone operator (電話交換手), endured all of life's hardships, then encountered a good gold mine, received the fortune of yellow gold pouring out like a bonanza (nodaji), and amassed 200,000 Won in just a few years, cannot but be considered one of the heroines of our time in many respects. To hear about the experiences and aspirations of this extraordinary lady, I knocked on her door on the afternoon of October 15th, around 6 PM, as autumn deepened.

Walking slowly up the dimly lit alleyway of Ch'ŏngjin-dong under the electric lights, one passes Susong-dong and Sungmyŏng Girls' School. A couple of houses further up from there, a splendid and elegantly decorated Korean-style house, striking enough to catch the eye of any passerby, appears before you as if demanding attention.

Arriving at the gate of this house and looking up at the nameplate, one sees the three characters 「金貞淑」 clearly written on a slender, white stone plaque.

Guided by a servant, I entered the inner room (anbang) situated deep within the house.＜120＞

Seated in the warmest part of the room near the ondol floor heating was none other than Ms. Kim Chŏng-suk.

The room was spotlessly decorated, the floor paper gleaming. To one side stood a magnificent three-tiered chest, and on the desk, besides a telephone, the room was quite neat and simple.

Me: "I apologize for disturbing you when you must be busy, but I heard that you experienced hardships from a young age that others haven't, so I came seeking to hear your detailed story."

Kim: "Oh my, what are you saying... I have indeed gone through much hardship, but is there really anything worth telling?"

Me: "Where is your hometown?"

Kim: "It's Yŏngyu-ŭp, P'yŏngwŏn-gun, P'yŏngan Namdo... As I mentioned on the phone earlier, what need is there to know about a money-grubber like me? However, thank you truly for coming all this way to see me."

Me: "Please tell me your story step-by-step, starting from when you attended school as a child. How old were you when you attended primary school?"

Kim: "I was exactly 13 years old. At that time, I learned the Thousand Character Classic (千字文). Then, as the private school became a public primary school, I learned the so-called new academics. But something I still think about now is that the daughter of Magistrate Yi from Maengsan was in my class. That girl was a bit better at needlework, but her studies weren't as good as mine. However, she ended up ranking first in grades, and I was second. I was so infuriated at losing to someone else that I just quit the primary school I was attending because of it."

To Sericulture School

Me: "And after that...?"

Kim: "Right after that, I went to P'yŏngyang and enrolled in a sericulture school. While I was studying there, the Yŏngyu primary school kept asking me to come back, saying their top student was attending the sericulture school, but I disliked the biased treatment favoring the magistrate's daughter, so I didn't go back and just finished that school."

Next, as a Nurse

Me: "What happened after you graduated from that school?"

Kim: "After that, I immediately went to work as a nurse at the P'yŏngyang Chahye Hospital."

Me: "What was life as a nurse like?"

Kim: "What can I say? To the extent that my young mind saw things like corpse dissections, I couldn't eat or sleep for two or three days. And when the patients cried out 'Aya, aya' in agony, the hearts of those watching ached. Goodness, that was no job for me."

Me: "So, you quit?"

Kim: "It wasn't that I quit, but my family had arranged a marriage, so they kept telling me to come home! Having no choice, I went back home."

Me: "How old were you then?" ＜121＞

Kim: "I was fifteen. After getting married, I did some housekeeping for a while, and then I quit that."

Seeking a Mine

Me: "So, after that...?"

Kim: "It was the year I turned 18. I followed my husband to the Ch'angsŏng gold mine in P'yŏngan Pukto and spent day after day in terrible hardship. After five months passed, we inevitably decided to leave that place to find a way to live, but where would we get the travel money (路費)! We walked all day, hungry, feet blistered, and the road ahead seemed endless! But along the way, we happened to meet someone, and when I explained our detailed situation, that person gave me 3 Won. Ah, that 3 Won! I still haven't forgotten it, nor have I forgotten his name, Mr. Kim Pyŏng-hyŏn (김병현)."

Life with In-laws Again

Kim: "So, on the way, we went to stay at the house of my sister-in-law's relative [literally: sister-in-law's husband's younger brother's wife] in the mountain valley behind Sukch'ŏn. Since it was a mountain village, everyone carried sheaves of millet (조이ㅅ단) down the slopes. As I was receiving meals there, I couldn't just sit idle, so I had to work alongside them, and it was no ordinary hardship. Thinking back on that time, one amusing incident comes to mind again. It was my husband's birthday, which fell on the Ch'usŏk day in August. Normally, we only ate corn and millet mixed rice, but because it was a holiday and his birthday, they cooked white rice. Being young, I couldn't eat freely with so many people watching, so I wet my hands, made rice balls from the bowl placed on the water jar, and ate them when no one was looking! How desperate must I have been! It really seems funny now. That's how I experienced life with my in-laws (싀집사리)."

Me: "Hahaha... How did the rice taste then? Compared to now..."

Kim: "Back then, it wasn't rice, it was honey. How could the fine delicacies of today compare to the millet rice of that time?"

Back to Girls' High School

Me: "What happened next that led you to enroll in girls' high school again?"

Kim: "Even while enduring such terrible hardship living with my in-laws, the desire to study never left me, so I went back to P'yŏngyang and enrolled in the Girls' High School."

Me: "At what age...?"

Kim: "It was the year I turned 19. Then, when I turned 20, in my second year, I had to quit school. A woman can't help it. My desire to study, even to the point of cooking meals for my teacher to afford it, all came to nothing. As the wife of one husband, what choice did I have? At that time, I was pregnant with my first child, so I quit!" ＜122＞

As a Telephone Operator!

Me: "So, did you manage the household from then on?"

Kim: "Not at all! My husband's younger brother (싀아젓씨) said he'd set me up in business, so we went to Kyŏmip'o. But what business could possibly succeed? So, to make a living, I had no choice but to enter the Mitsubishi Company as a telephone operator, earning a daily wage (일금) of 36 chŏn, and my husband worked as a foreman (십장) on a road construction site, earning 65 chŏn a day. That's how we lived day by day."

Me: "Since you both worked hand-in-hand then, it must have been an interesting life."

Kim: "Well... working at the company during the day and being so exhausted at night that I would sometimes just collapse... I wonder if you could call that interesting!"

Seeking the Chŏngsŏn Hwaam Gold Mine

Me: "When did you go to Kangwŏn-do?"

Kim: "Yes. It was right when I quit Mitsubishi, when I was 23. My husband went to Kangwŏn-do first in April of that year, and I followed him in July or August."

Me: "You must have suffered a lot going there at that time."

Kim: "Suffering is an understatement! Crossing Mun Pass (문제) and Chŏn Pass (전제), reaching Taehwa Pangnim, and then crossing Pyŏlp'yŏn Pass, I truly cried. Looking back, the road I had come stretched over a thousand ri, and the path ahead was still deep in the mountains, unclear where the road even was. The few people I met all seemed like bandits, making my heart pound, and tears welled up in my eyes. Oh, don't even talk about the mountain valleys of Chŏngsŏn, Kangwŏn-do!"

Climbing Mountains Carrying an A-frame

Me: "How did you live after that?"

Kim: "Though I had never done such work in my life, from the time I got there until winter came, I climbed the mountains carrying an A-frame carrier (jige) to gather firewood for burning. In such a remote mountain village (드멧산골), this kind of work was just something one naturally did. Modern new women probably can't even imagine such hardship. So, we would mine a little gold, sell it, buy rice, and somehow get by. That must have been the Kimi Year (긔미년 - 1919). It was a time when one mal of rice cost 2 Won 50 chŏn."

Me: "When did you discover a new vein (신혈) at the mine?"

Kim: "While mining like that, little by little, a new vein appeared, and just as we started making a bit more money, my husband's older brother (싀아주버니) came and asked to take it over, saying he would run it. I gave it to him, and he ended up losing everything (잡혀먹고 말앗슴니다). So after that, we were left empty-handed again. Then another vein appeared, and after we recovered what had been seized, he asked for it again! At that time, my husband had come up to Seoul. My husband had relinquished the gold mine, for which he had been paying 150 Won per month in taxes, in November of Shōwa Year 1 (소화 원년 - 1926).＜123＞ After that, I was alone in the mining hut (수체막), and when darkness fell, it felt as if death itself was approaching. The fence made of sorghum stalks (수수깽이) would rustle and creak (웃석웃석) when the wind blew, and one night, a thief must have come to the door. It was winter, snow was falling constantly, and being a woman alone, already unable to sleep well, I locked the door, grabbed a club, and shouted. The thief fled. Later, I found out it was a neighbor (니웃놈) who knew a woman was alone and thought there was gold in the upper room (웃방). It was truly terrifying. That was when my daughter, now nineteen, was being weaned (젓떠러질 때). When I think of the trembling and goosebumps that night, I shudder (진저리가 납니다). Even after that, whenever the sun set and the wind blew, making the sorghum stalks rustle (버석버석), I trembled in fear. And since I had gone there in early autumn, I hadn't brought winter clothes, so I endured the cold winter scantily clad. But it would have been better if we had at least always had something to eat."

Me: "So, were you unable even to have three meals a day?"

Kim: "There were more times we starved than times we ate. To buy rice, you need money. I went from house to house, asking them to lend me money, promising to repay it after mining and selling gold. With 3 or 4 Won, we barely survived (련명햇지요) for two or three months. Living like this, when the little children cried from hunger, how must a mother's heart have felt!"

Me: "Truly, you lived an appalling (기맥힌) life."

After Childbirth, Not Even a Handful of Rice

Kim: "When I gave birth to Sunhŭngi in Chŏngsŏn, there was no one to help support me, not even rice for the first meal, nor even seaweed soup. Is that all? They say you shouldn't be exposed to wind after childbirth, but living without money to buy even a single sheet of paper to patch the door holes, I went out after giving birth to fetch water and did all sorts of strenuous work. Not eating properly, my face was puffy and swollen (푸석푸석 붓고), and it was a miracle I survived when I should have died from illness in that state. Oh dear, let's not talk about this anymore. It's too upsetting. If I were to tell all my hardships, it would become a long novel."

Me: "So, when did things gradually start looking up?"

Kim: "After entrusting the mine to my brother-in-law for the second time, we went to the Ch'ŏnp'o Gold Mine. But things didn't go as planned there, and in December of Shōwa Year 6 (소화6년 - 1931), even my young child died, so feeling distraught (살난하기에), I just came up to Seoul. After that, I received notice from two of our 'workers' that a new vein had been found. I immediately applied for the rights (출원), and with the 'nodaji' pouring out, things took off. So, in January of last year, I sold the Ch'ŏnp'o mine for 200,000 Won."

Still Over Sixty Mining Claims (鑛口)

Me: "How many mining claims (광구) do you have now?" ＜124＞

Kim: "Perhaps about 60? Including the ones I've applied for."

Me: "Goodness, if nodaji pours out from all 60 of those places, what will you do with all that money?"

Kim: "That's nothing. Even if there were as much gold (金) as all of Chosŏn, I could manage it all, hahaha..."

Me: "What do you plan to do? Buy up the fields of the Naktong River basin, survey the plains of Manchuria? Or pass it down to your sons and daughters?"

Kim: "What's the use of buying fields? If I had even 100,000 Won in cash in my hands, I wouldn't want to refuse anyone—parentless children, childless elderly people, poor people suffering after childbirth like I did, those who couldn't study when they wanted to like me, or those who want to start a business. If it's a worthwhile cause, I want to help. If it's a project for the benefit of the people, I intend to do anything."

Me: "By the way, I heard you were taking over a certain girls' school. What happened with that?"

Kim: "We'll have to wait and see. It's not finalized yet, so I'll tell you next time."

Me: "How many children do you have now?"

Kim: "Four sons and four daughters, eight siblings in total."

Me: "How old are you this year?"

Kim: "Me? I'm thirty-eight."

Me: "During all the time you suffered so much, did you ever resent your husband?"

Kim: "I think of him more as a companion than just a husband. We are a couple who untied my childhood hair ribbons [i.e., married young], so no matter how angry I get, it just disappears after 30 minutes. Our marital relationship is based on mutual understanding (리해). And as a couple married since youth, if one were to complain about hardship, lack of care from the husband, lack of enjoyment, and talk of divorce (리혼), how could that be the way of a married couple? Whether we eat or starve, it's one's own fate; there's no point blaming the husband or wife. As we grow older, our sympathy for each other grows, and our love deepens."

Having once lived in a respectable yangban family, although she endured hardship, her marital affection stemming from that moral framework (관렴), her slow and deliberate way of speaking, her appearance seated comfortably in a white jeogori and grey chima, her roundish face showing faint traces of hardship yet looking blessed with fortune—all contribute to her image. Hearing her tale of hardship—working as a nurse and operator, repeatedly entering the workforce, carrying firewood on an A-frame—one can truly sense that her life has been full of ups and downs and many critical moments. The frequent phone calls during my interview indicated her busy schedule.

And following the thread of each haltingly spoken phrase would lead to an endless story. As it was already nearly eight o'clock, I limited my questions. Looking again at her face, marked by hardship, a wish for her health (Continued on page 129)＜125＞ and happiness naturally arose within me.

Her virtuous heart, never having resented her husband despite such suffering! Also, her benevolent heart, openly expressing her intention to dedicate what she has to charitable works! What man could match this lady's heart?

I looked at her face again, twice, three times.

As I left the house after two hours and walked away, I replayed her chronicle of blood and tears (血淚錄) in my mind and wished for her future to shine brightly! ＜129＞
＜120-129＞

**Summary**

This 1935 interview profiles Kim Chŏng-suk, dubbed the "Gold Mine Queen," who rose from extreme poverty to immense wealth through gold mining after enduring significant hardships. The article details her life journey: truncated education, distressing work as a nurse, an early arranged marriage, severe deprivation while following her husband to various mines, physically demanding labor (including hauling firewood in remote mountains), near starvation, losing mining discoveries to relatives, enduring danger alone, suffering child loss, and working low-wage jobs like telephone operator. Eventually, a major gold strike ("nodaji") led to her selling a mine for 200,000 Won, and she now holds over 60 mining claims. Kim expresses a strong desire to use her fortune for philanthropy, aiding the vulnerable and supporting education and enterprise, while emphasizing the strength of her marriage built on shared suffering and mutual understanding.

From a historical perspective, the article is significant for its detailed portrayal of a woman's life trajectory during the Japanese colonial period, highlighting the limitations and challenges faced (education, marriage, gender roles) alongside opportunities presented by economic shifts like the gold rush. It provides a vivid account of extreme poverty, the physical and emotional toll of labor and hardship in remote areas (Kangwŏn-do), family dynamics (obligations to in-laws), and the emergence of new wealth. Kim's story offers insights into social mobility, entrepreneurial spirit amidst adversity, and philanthropic sentiments during that era, making it a valuable source for understanding the social and economic history of colonial Korea.

黃金豪華版, 一看婦護로 아직도 六十餘區를 가진 金貞淑女史, 三十萬圓의 金鑛女王
						如山
세상에 놀납고도 희귀한 일이 하도 만치만 일개 간호부(看護婦)의 몸으로서 또 전화교환수(電話交換手)의 몸으로서 인생의 가진 고생을 격든 나머지 조흔 금광을 맛나서 누러케 쏘다지는 노다지의 복을 입어 불 줄 수년에 일획 20만원을 모아 쥔 금광여왕 김정숙녀사는 여러 가지 의미로 당대 녀걸 중 한사람이 아니랄 수 업다. 나는 이 히한한 녀사의 경역과 포부를 듯고저 씨의 문을 두다리기는 가을도 깊어가는 10월 15일 오후 6시라.
전긔ㅅ불이 어슴푸레한 청진동 골목길을 터벅터벅 거러 올너가노라면 이여 수송동이 나지고 숙명녀학교가 나진다. 여긔에서 두어집 더 올너 가노라면 길가는 사람의 눈을 끄을 만한 훌융하고도 얌전하게 꾸며진 조선식 주택 한 채가 날 보아라 하드시 눈압헤 가로 나타난다.
이 집 대문 앞에 이르러 문패를 처다보면 갤죽한 하이얀 돌판으로 된 문패에는 「金貞淑」이란 삼ㅅ자가 또렷이 쓰여저 잇다.
하인에게 안내를 받어 바로 집쑥한 이 집 안방으로 들어갓다.＜120＞
방 아래ㅅ목에 앉은 이가 바루 金貞淑녀사이다.
방안은 깨끗하게 꾸며 젓는데 장판지에서는 윤이 번득번득한다. 한편 쪽으로 으리으리하게 장치한 3층의 장과 책상 우에는 전화긔가 느여 잇슬 뿐 아주 단아하고 간결한 방이여다.
나 「분주하실 터인데 대단 미안합니다마는 듯자오니 부인께서는 남이 맛보지 못하는 고생 을 젊어서 붙어 겻거 왓다기에 그에 대한 자세한 이야기를 듯고 십허서 찾어왓습니다.」
金 「아니 참 별말슴을... 고생이야 무척 해왓슴니다만 뭐 이야기 할 것까지야 잇습니까.」
나 「고향이 어데심니까.」
金 「平安南道 平原郡 永柔邑임니다... 앗가도 전화로 말슴하엿슴니다만 무엇 나같은 금젼군이야 알아서 필요 잇서요. 그러나 이렇케 찾어와 주시니 참으로 감사함니다.」
나 「어려서 학교 단이실 때붓어 차곡차곡 이야기해 주십시요. 보통학교는 맻살 되든 해에 단엿슴니까.」
金 「내가 바루 13살 되든 때이엿지요, 그때는 千字文을 배웟담니다. 그러다가 사립학교가 공립보통학교로 되면서 소위 신학문을 배웟슴니다. 그런데 지금도 생각키우는 일은 그때 孟山서 온 李군수의 딸이 나와 한 반이엿지요. 그 애는 수예가 좀 낫지만 공부는 나만 못햇지요. 그런데 그만 그 애가 성적이 첫재요 내가 둘재되는 바람에 남에게 지는 것이 어떻케 분하든지 그만 그 바람에 단이든 보통학교를 지버 치웟담니다.」

다시 싀집사리
金 「그래서 오든 도중에 저- 肅川 뒷산골에 잇는 싀누이동서 집에 가 잇는데 산골이라 모두 조이ㅅ단을 이고 비탈을 내려오는데 나도 거긔서 밥을 얻어 먹는 몸이라 그냥 이슬 수 없어 같이 일을 할야니 여간 고생이 안이엿담니다. 참 그 때의 일을 생각하면 우수운 일 한가지가 다시금 생각키움니다. 바루 저- 우리 아희 아버지 생일인 즉 8월 추석날인데 보통 때는 강냉이 조밥만 먹든 것이 그 날만은 명절겸 생일이래서 흰밥을 햇는데 어린 마음에 보는 이가 많어서 마음놓고는 못먹고 물동이 우에 놓인 밥그릇의 이밥을 손에 물칠을 하여 주먹밥을 만들어 남이 보지 안는 틈에 먹누라니 여북햇겟서요! 참 지금 생각하면 우수운 일이엿지요. 참 싀집사리를 이렇게 해왓담니다.」
