Article Title: 수탉 (Rooster)
Author: 李孝石
Article Type: 소설 (Fiction/Novel)

Rooster
						Yi Hyosŏk

Lately, with his gloomy heart, Ŭlson had become negligent in tending to his chickens. The chickens he had raised so devotedly didn't catch his eye at all, nor did they pull at his heartstrings. When he saw them milling about the front yard searching for feed, he would find himself picking up a wooden stick. The inside of the coop, left uncleaned, was incomparably messy.

Selling four of them would cover one month's tuition. The gradual decrease in the number inside the coop wasn't particularly regrettable. Rather, the sight of the four chickens who had escaped their month-long fate, unlike those still wandering inside the coop unable to meet their timely destiny, caught his eye offensively. The tuition for that month he wasn't attending school had been extended.

Among those four chickens was one unsightly rooster – it had the most wretched appearance. On top of its shabby exterior, whenever it fought with the neighbor's chickens, it was invariably defeated. Its comb, bitten and torn, always seemed to have fresh blood flowing. Besides having dull eyes, it limped on one leg. The feathers on its wings were ruffled, and even its tail was short. Sometimes, it was even chased by the hens. <274> A rooster unable to fulfill its duty as a rooster was pitiful to see, but lately, it had gone beyond pitifulness to become unpleasant to look at. Moreover, the fact that it had attached its month-long fate to the coop seemed only hateful and grotesque to Ŭlson.

His heart felt terribly stifled for not being able to go to school.

Being cast out of paradise for picking an apple is legend, but being cast out of school for picking an apple is reality.

The apples in the farm were forbidden fruit.

Ŭlson and the others had violated that rule.

Rather than saying he was swayed by his friends' coaxing, Ŭlson himself had fallen prey to the temptation of the apples. Apples were not a luxurious desire; they were a necessary appetite.

There were five on duty. It was after the silkworms had all been put up on the racks. Perhaps the idleness, with not much work to do, was the beginning of the trouble. Waiting for midnight while chatting idly, they all left the room together, hid themselves in the darkness, and climbed over the wire fence of the orchard.

Tossing the leftovers into the fireplace seemed like a clean getaway, but hiding the last one among the mulberry leaves in the corner of the room was a mistake.

The next morning, when the footprints in the orchard became an issue, that one apple hidden among the mulberry leaves was unfortunately discovered.

The path of the search was obvious. The five who were on duty the previous night were interrogated one by one before the head resident supervisor.

Despite having firmly made a pact, as is always the case, human solidarity seems to subtly crumble from somewhere. It appeared that the truth finally slipped out from the mouth of one weak friend. They were called in one by one again.

When the second round of summons began, Ŭlson was in a peculiar place.

He hadn't entered that place because he felt physically unwell, but had deliberately chosen such a place to avoid the unpleasant time for a while.

That narrow, square space where one person could barely crouch down and sit – though uncomfortable, it was also the most peaceful place. Sitting there felt as light as being submerged in seawater.

From the outer yard, the sounds of friends chatting, laughing, and running, mixed with the light sounds of tennis and soccer balls rolling, flowed ceaselessly, making his body feel as if it were floating along with those delightful sounds.

Ŭlson forgot even the situation of his friends currently being interrogated and his own predicament, calmly took a cigarette from his pocket, and lit it. In truth, cigarettes, like apples, were also forbidden by the rules, but violating rules was a beautiful <275> virtue passed down by human ancestors. Moreover, smoking a puff in that place was, Ŭlson thought, a supreme joy.

Also part of the peculiar customs of that place, the walls were scribbled with various primitive postures of men and women without clothes, drawn with childish strokes. Though the lines were simple and the drawings clumsy, they provided a kind of joy.

Feeling an inexplicable temptation, Ŭlson searched his pocket for a dull pencil and, while exhaling fragrant smoke in long puffs, began to draw, exercising his creative imagination.

Eating apples, then smoking cigarettes, then doodling – while repeating these "violations," Ŭlson suddenly felt an aversion to school spring up. – If a teacher, after questioning a student about stealing apples at school, returned home to find his own young son had stolen apples from a neighbor's field, how would he punish him? And when recalling his own boyhood days of stealing apples, what feelings and reflections would arise? Or, how should one explain the case of a teacher, who preaches the virtue of moderation at school, himself falling into unrighteous passion? – Just like a pastor preaching the Ten Commandments while groaning under the sin of adultery.

Thinking closer to home, wasn't he in a situation where he had no farmland of his own to utilize even if he learned specialized science and skills?

It's pathetic. It's wretched. Rather than receiving so much humiliation for such trivial rewards, he wanted to break free from the narrow, uncomfortable bridle and run off anywhere into the wide world.

Ŭlson's thoughts, like a horse with loosened reins, knew no bounds.

It seemed quite some time had passed.

The bell signaling the end of the school day rang chaotically.

The next day, his father, wearing his only outing durumagi coat, was summoned to the school.

It was an indefinite suspension.

His father looked utterly dumbfounded – unable even to discipline his beloved son.

Ŭlson felt an irrepressible urge to round up all the chickens in the coop, sell them, and run away, but unable to do even that, he left home empty-handed.

After wandering through neighboring villages, he returned home three days later.

With no inclination to help with the farm work, he could only spend several days like an idiot.

The flock of chickens in the coop came into view. Among them, the appearance of the wretched rooster looked even more pathetic. Even when eating rice mixed with red pepper paste, its pitiful state of losing to the neighbor's chickens was unbearable to watch. <276>

That wretched rooster – isn't that just my own situation? – Ŭlson suddenly flared up in anger.

Since it was an idle time, he was in a position to meet Poknyŏ often, but feelings of awkwardness made him hesitate instead.

Poknyŏ certainly didn't seem to view Ŭlson's situation favorably.

Poknyŏ was a woman of determination. Having completed a half-year apprenticeship at the sericulture seed production center, she was set to become a village sericulture instructor starting next spring. It was Poknyŏ who encouraged and urged Ŭlson, who tended to neglect his studies. They had promised to work together once he finished school, but Ŭlson's recent mistake had surely disappointed Poknyŏ. An incompetent man – nothing was more meaningless to Poknyŏ than this.

One evening when he visited Poknyŏ, everything became perfectly clear to Ŭlson.

It wasn't Poknyŏ who came out, but her mother.

"It's terribly sad to think that from now on, we won't be able to visit each other often."

As he stood there dumbfounded, not understanding her meaning, Poknyŏ's mother continued.

"We finally found a suitable person."

While he was stunned, a weight like a thousand pounds of iron struck his spine.

"I heard there's a decent person at the cooperative, so I didn't inquire further and just decided."

Without even thinking of looking for Poknyŏ, Ŭlson rushed out, feeling empty and dazed.

"Was it Poknyŏ's wish or Ch'unhyang's mother's doing?"

There was no need to ask.

His vision blurred, and the world felt like it was collapsing.

For several days, nothing registered in his eyes.

Reality felt like a gaunt chestnut burr.

Even after more than a month, there was no notice of reinstatement from the school.

It was evening.

When the chickens had entered the coop and settled into their respective roosts, the rooster, which had been in the village, came staggering back.

It looked like it had fought again.

Blood was fresh on its torn comb, and the feathers on its battered wing stuck out backward.

Its limp was the same, but the direction it walked was unsteady. When he looked closely, one of its eyes was crushed. Blood flowed from the closed eye, staining its feathers.

It was a ghastly sight. (Continued on page 299) <277>
However, the feeling of pity instantly turned into hatred. A fiery anger flared up in Ŭlson. – What's the point of living looking like that?

His hand, filled with murderous intent, trembled violently. He grabbed whatever came to hand and threw it at the chicken.

Shockingly, it hit its mark. As the chicken momentarily stretched its legs and floundered, Ŭlson quickly averted his gaze. The pitiful cries, stopping and starting, seemed to shake Ŭlson's very core. <299>

**Summary**

The story follows Ŭlson, a young student indefinitely suspended from school for stealing apples from the school farm with friends. Dejected and unable to pay tuition (which he used to cover by selling chickens), he feels trapped and compares his situation to a wretched, frequently defeated rooster he owns. His relationship with Poknyŏ, an ambitious young woman, also collapses due to his perceived failure. In a fit of rage and self-loathing projected onto the injured rooster, Ŭlson kills it, seeing it as a reflection of his own miserable state. The story poignantly captures the despair and limited prospects faced by Korean youth under colonial rule, highlighting the harsh consequences of minor transgressions within rigid institutional systems and the resulting psychological turmoil. Historians might find the depiction of rural life, the specific mention of sericulture training (a common colonial-era industry), the casual violation of school rules reflecting youth rebellion or desperation, and the protagonist's sense of hopelessness indicative of the socio-economic pressures and lack of opportunity during that period.

수탉
						李孝石
을손은 요사이 울적한 마음에 닭 시중도 게을리 하게 되엿다. 그 알뜰히 기르든 닭들이 도모지 눈에도 들지 안으며 마음을 댕기지 못하얏다. 모이는 새퉈 뜰 압을 어른거리는 꼴을 보면 나무개피를 집어들게 되엿다. 치우지 안은 우리 속은 지저분하기 짝업다.
네 마리를 팔면 한 달 수업료가 된다. 우리 안의 수효가 차차 줄어짐이 그다지 애틋한 것은 아니엿다. 도리혀 제 때 가질 운명을 못 가지고 우리 안을 헤매는 한달 동안의 운명을 벗어난 네 마리의 꼴이 눈에 거실렷다. 학교에 안가는 그 한 달 수업료가 늘녀진 것이다.
그 닭의 네 마리 중에서도 못난 한 마리의 수닭 - 가장 초라한 꼴이엿다. 허울이 변변치 못한 우에 이웃집 닭과 싸우면 판판이 젓다. 물너 뜻기운 맨도람이에는 언제 보아도 피가 새로히 흘러 잇다. 거적 눈인데다 한 쪽 다리를 겻는다. 쭉지의 깃이 간지런하지 못하고 꼬리조차 짧다. 엇떤 때면 암닭에게까지＜274＞ 쫏겻다. 수닭 구실을 못하는 수닭이 보기에도 민망하엿스나 요사이 와서는 민망한 정도를 넘어 보기 실흔 것이엿다. 더구나 한 달의 운명을 우리 안에 더부치게 된 것이 을손에게는 밉살스럽고 흉측스럽게 보일 뿐이엿다.
학교에 못가는 마음이 몹시 답답하얏다.
능금을 따고 락원을 쫏기운 것은 전설이나 능금을 따다 학원을 쫏기운 것은 현실이다.
농장의 능금은 금단의 과실이엿다.
을손들은 그 률측을 어긴 것이다.
동무들의 꼬임에 빠젓다느니보다도 을손 자신 능금의 유혹에 빠젓든 것이다. 능금은 사치한 욕망이 아니다. 필요한 식욕이엿다.
당번은 다섯 명이엿다. 누에를 다 올닌 후이다. 별로 할 일 업시 한가하얏든 것이 일을 저즈른 시초일는지 모른다. 잡담으로 자정이 되기를 기다렷다가 일제히 방을 나가 어둠 속에 몸을 감추고 과수원의 철망을 넘엇다.
먹다 남은 것을 아궁지 속에 너흔 것은 깜족가텃스나 마즈막 한 개를 방구석 봉닙 속에 간직한 것이 실책이엿다.
이튼날 아츰 과수원 속의 발자최가 문제되엿슬 때 공교하게도 뽕닙 속의 그 한 개가 발견되엿다.
수색의 길은 빤하다. 간밤의 다섯 명의 당번이 차례로 급주임 압헤서 취됴를 밧게 되엿다.
굿게 언약을 해 노코서도 어느 때나 마찬가지로 사람의 단결이란 그 어대로부터인지 교묘하게 부서지는 것 갓다. 약한 한 사람의 동무의 입에서 긔어코 실토가 된 모양이엿다. 한 사람식 거듭 불녀 들어갓다.

두 번재 호출이 시작되엿슬 때 을손은 괴상한 곳에 잇섯다.
몸이 무거워 그 곳에 들어간 것이 안니라 얼마동안의 귀치 안은 시간 피하러 일부러 그런 곳을 골은 것이엿다.
한 사람이 들어가 간신히 웅크리고 안젓슬 만한 네모진 그 좁은 공간 - 거북스럽기는 하야도 가장 마음 편한 곳도 그곳이엿다. 그곳에 안젓스면 마치 바다물 속에 잠겨 잇는 것과도 가티 몸이 것분한 까닭이다.
박 운동장에서는 동무들의 짓거리는 소리 우슴 소리 닷는 소리에 석겨 정구 축구의 공 구르는 가벼운 소리가 쉴새 업시 흘러와 몸은 그 질거운 소리를 타고 뜬 것 갓다.
을손은 현재 취됴를 밧고 잇슬 당번의 동무들과 자신의 형편조차 니저 버리고 유유히 주머니 속에서 담배를 한 개 집어 내서 불을 붓첫다. 실상인즉 담배도 능금과 가티 금단의 률측이엿스나 률측을 어김은 인류의 조상이 끼처 준 아름다운＜275＞ 공덕이다. 더구나 그곳에서 한 목음 피우기란 무상의 깃붐이다고 을손은 생각하는 것이엿다.
이것도 그곳의 특이한 풍속으로 벽에는 옷을 닙지 안을 때의 남녀의 가지가지의 원시적 자태가 유치한 필치로 락서 되여잇다. 간단한 선 서투른 그림이면서도 그것은 일종의 깃붐이엿다.
을손도 알 수 업는 유혹을 바더 주머니 속에서 무딘 연필을 차저 향긔로운 연긔를 길게 뿜으면서 독창적 상상을 기우려 그림을 그리기 시작하얏다.
