**Body Text Translation:**
A Tour Report of the Kyŏngsŏng Sanitarium Surrounded by Green Pines
						By Sŏngdaebongin

On the afternoon of October 7th, the air in Seoul city was as polluted as it could be. Leaving the office, I walked to the temporary office of the Kyŏngsŏng Sanitarium (京城療養病院) in Insadong, and perhaps like a single lotus flower, a nurse came out and asked me to come in. The office was bustling with activity; the Western doctor was out on a house call, while Dr. Ch'oe and the accountant, Mr. Ch'oe Yŏngch'an (崔永贊), were busy with brushes over the ledgers (帳薄). I suggested to Mr. Ch'oe that, since the office hours must be half over, we should take a walk out to suburban Ch'ŏngnyangni and see the newly constructed hospital. Mr. Ch'oe asked me to wait just a moment, then put aside his work and stepped out the door with me.

The time was 4:30 PM. We caught a streetcar in front of Pagoda Park, transferred at Tongdaemun, and rode the streetcar out towards the suburbs. Gradually, the polluted air cleared. Getting off at Ch'ŏngnyangni, we took a bus bound for Hoegi-ri, alighted at the last stop, and tapping my cane, crossed the Kyŏngwŏn Line (京元線) railway crossing. Here, the scenery was purely rural, just as it was: lush green forests (蒼林) grew thick, a clear breeze (淸風) blew, and there stood a splendid three-story house of white jade (白玉) built purely with white stone, its magnificent view reflecting brilliantly in the crimson evening glow – a sight unseen within the city of Seoul.

Following Mr. Ch'oe who was guiding me, we came upon a wide front yard where turf, planted not long ago, filled the space. There was also a fountain, and various flowers planted all around the left garden swayed in the autumn <148> breeze. As we entered the main gate, Mr. Ch'oe explained, "The floor below is for hospital management, like the dining hall and storage rooms, and from the 2nd floor upwards are the patient wards." Walking along the sparkling balustrade paved with unnamed stone, we looked into room after room. To the right of the entrance was the pharmacy, to the left was the accounting office, next to the accounting office was the nurses' station, and then the hydrotherapy room (水治療室), electrotherapy room (電氣治療室), and in one quiet corner, the third-class inpatient ward. Opposite the main entrance were the examination room and the X-ray room (x光線室). Two bright rooms on the east side (東便) were also third-class inpatient rooms (三等入院室).

Then we went up to the 3rd floor. These rooms were even quieter and designed to let in plenty of sunlight. The triangular room at the very back was the operating room, and the rest were filled with large and small inpatient rooms. "How many people can be admitted?" I asked. "Well, in special circumstances, we could probably accommodate 100 people. However, the 3rd floor is divided into special rooms, first-class rooms, and second-class rooms. And if there are many patients, each first-class patient will have one dedicated nurse assigned to them."

"This hospital is basically managed by the Seventh-day Adventist Church (第7日安息日 耶蘇再臨敎會). There are over 60 hospitals of the same affiliation worldwide. The Tokyo Sanitarium (東京위생병원), Mukden Sanitarium (奉天요양병원), Shanghai Sanitarium (上海요양병원), and Sunan Hospital (順安병원) in P'yŏngnam are receiving notably high praise. Anyone in the Sŏsŏn [Western Korea] region would know of the benefits brought by Dr. Russell when the Sunan hospital was first established in Korea. Now, this Kyŏngsŏng Sanitarium, you could say, has facilities beyond ordinary hospitals, such as light therapy (光線), heat therapy (熱), electrotherapy (電氣), massage (맛싸자), and hydrotherapy (水治療). Just because it's called a sanitarium (요양병원) doesn't mean it should be thought of only as a sanatorium for tuberculosis (肺結核) patients like the Haeju Sanitarium. It combines natural therapies (자연치료) with surgical procedures, including abdominal surgery, selecting the best academic methods."

Listening to this, we climbed to the 4th-floor rooftop. A vast plain (一望平野) stretched out; far off, Samgak Mountain, Seoul city, and Namsan were visible. The surroundings were encircled by green pines (蒼松), and a cool, clear breeze seeped through the pine forest (松林). Sparrows chirped, insect sounds cried out – wouldn't anyone's spirit (心靈) become clear, even someone from the midst of complexity, immersed in this nature? Flocks of birds hidden within the nearby Hŭigyŏngwŏn Royal Tomb fluttered back and forth.

"When will it open?" I asked. "We'll probably move everything from Insadong in November and install more facilities. And next spring (明春), we plan to hold a grand opening ceremony for the new building." "How much did the construction cost?" "It probably cost over 100,000 wŏn, and the land cost about 50,000 wŏn. We bought all the surrounding pine forest, and we plan to build doctors' residences within the pine forest in the future. We've started this large project, and permission for fundraising has been granted. Already, starting in Seoul, we are immensely grateful that many people approve of our work, beginning with the generous donations (喜捨) from certain philanthropists (某某 篤志家)." I raise my two hands and pray that this enterprise brings joy to the weeping and the light of health to the suffering, thereby contributing to Chosŏn society. <149>

**Summary**

This travelogue recounts the author's visit to the nearly completed Kyŏngsŏng Sanitarium in the Ch'ŏngnyangni/Hoegi-ri area outside Seoul in October 1935. Guided by an accountant, Mr. Ch'oe, the author describes the modern, three-story building with advanced facilities like hydrotherapy, electrotherapy, X-ray, and an operating room, set amidst serene pine forests. The hospital, run by the Seventh-day Adventist Church, aimed to provide modern medical care, including surgery and natural therapies, distinct from typical sanatoriums. The author notes the significant cost (over 150,000 wŏn), ongoing fundraising efforts, and expresses hope for the hospital's positive impact on Korean society.

For historians, this article offers insights into the development of modern healthcare in colonial Korea, particularly through foreign religious missions like the Seventh-day Adventists. It highlights the introduction of Western medical practices and technologies (electrotherapy, hydrotherapy, X-ray), the substantial investment involved, the blending of modern medicine with "natural therapies," and the perception of such institutions by contemporary Koreans. The mention of other Adventist hospitals in East Asia (Tokyo, Mukden, Shanghai, Sunan) places this development within a broader regional context of missionary activity and modernization. The contrast drawn between the polluted city and the clean suburban environment chosen for the hospital is also noteworthy.
