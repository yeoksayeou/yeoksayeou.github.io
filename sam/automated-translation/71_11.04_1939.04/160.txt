An Impression of Chen Youren
						Yi Chŏng-sŏp

It was the first month of Showa 2 (1927). Having secretly arrived in Shanghai via Nagasaki, I met a certain friend and spoke of going to Wuhan. This person, scoffing at newspaper reports, advised me strongly to give up the idea entirely, saying, "Revolutionary turmoil is currently unfolding across the Yangtze River region, bullets are flying back and forth, and you want to go to Wuhan?" Brushing aside this counsel, I traveled up the great river (大江) for three days and nights and reached my destination, Wuhan.

At this time, Chiang Kai-shek was at the Jiujiang encampment, so due to the itinerary, I couldn't meet him. After meeting government figures such as Sun Fo, T.V. Soong (Song Ziwen), Ye Chucang, Xu Qian, and Deng Yanda, I lastly requested an interview in writing with Foreign Minister Eugene Chen (Chen Youren). A reply came immediately the next day, clearly stating the place and time and saying he would gladly meet me. Although I generally didn't trust their punctuality regarding appointments, since I was the one seeking the meeting, I went to the Foreign Ministry before the designated time and was just waiting idly (lit. yawning). But what do you know! Exactly at the appointed time, a secretary came out and guided me to the Minister's office; I couldn't help but be startled (一驚* 喫). Taking a step into the room, I quickly glanced around (얼* 삺여보니); the way all the documents on the bookshelf and desk were arranged in perfect order immediately gave the impression of a modern office politician. In fluent English, he offered condolences for my long journey and then asked if the French-language interview request letter I had sent earlier was written by me. I felt slightly displeased by this question, but discerning that there was no malicious intent (惡意의 바늘) behind Mr. Chen's query, I politely affirmed that it was. He seemed somewhat impressed and asked thoroughly about my school background, major, how long I had been in France, and so on, making me feel as if I were undergoing some kind of interrogation. At this point, I interrupted Mr. Chen, saying it was now my turn, and laid out the list of questions I had prepared<132> beforehand. After a while, Mr. Chen asked, "Excuse me, is the script you are using to take notes on my English Korean?" When I replied yes, this time it was clear that Mr. Chen was quite impressed. When the interview ended and I was about to take my leave, Mr. Chen asked, "So, must you really go back to Korea?" His expression felt as though he was making some kind of 'appeal' (앱피-) to me. "Of course, I must go back to Korea; what would I do staying in China?" I replied. "I suppose so!" Mr. Chen said, looking somewhat disappointed, as if there was something he regretted.

Compared to Mr. Chen, Sun Fo and T.V. Soong were extremely arrogant. Sun Fo, commonly known as the "Crown Prince" (太子), perhaps because he grew up presumptuously relying on his father's authority (父威), made me doubt if he even considered reporters like us as human beings. He told me to come at 10 AM, so I went to the Ministry of Communications as appointed, but he kept saying he was busy and didn't meet me easily until 4 PM – ah, I was hungry! When he finally granted an audience, his first words were to keep the questions brief. But this was Sun Fo's miscalculation (誤算). I too was a newspaper reporter who had come from afar, and having finally secured an interview, there was no way I would easily comply with the other party's demand. No, even just to vent my pent-up frustration (짜증 난 憤푸이) from waiting six or seven hours, I intended to deliberately drag out the questions and make it longer, yet he told me to be brief – it made no sense. Sun Fo repeatedly pulled out his watch, and finally, almost pleadingly, said, "Look, I have an important meeting, please don't ask any more questions." Only then did I finally lift my backside from the chair.

In short, my impression at the time was that in terms of magnanimity, agility, meticulousness, and level of knowledge, in all aspects, Eugene Chen (Chen Youren) stood out among Chinese politicians, as I remembered him.<133>

**Summary**

This 1939 memoir by Korean writer Yi Chŏng-sŏp recounts his 1927 visit to Wuhan, China, during the Northern Expedition. He contrasts his positive impression of the professional and courteous Foreign Minister Eugene Chen (Chen Youren) with the arrogance he perceived in Sun Fo and T.V. Soong. Yi highlights Chen's punctuality, organized office, fluent English, and genuine interest (even asking about the Korean script), contrasting it sharply with Sun Fo's dismissive attitude and making Yi wait for hours only to demand brevity. The author concludes that Chen was an outstanding politician in China at the time. From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article is interesting as it shows a Korean intellectual engaging directly with key figures of the Chinese Revolution, assessing their character and political capacity. It provides a rare firsthand Korean perspective on the internal dynamics of the Kuomintang during a critical period, revealing Yi's own standards for political leadership and perhaps a subtle commentary on the kind of international engagement he valued, especially notable given Korea's own struggle for sovereignty at the time. Historians might find the specific interactions and the author's judgments valuable for understanding Korean intellectual views on China and the nature of political encounters during this era.

陳友仁 印象記
						李晶燮
그것은 昭和 2년 正月이였다. 비밀히 長崎를 거처 上海에 도착하야 某某친구를 맞나 武漢에로 갈 이야기를 하니 이 사람 신문보도고 코*먹이고 시방 혁명난이 揚子江 일대에 전개되여 탄환이 왔다갔다 하는데 武漢으로 가다니 아주 단념허게 이렇게 권고하는 것을 박차고 大江을 三晝夜間 올나가니 목적하던 武漢에 이르렀더라.
이 때 蔣介石은 九江陣營에 있었기 때문에 路程上 관계로 맞나지 못하고 孫科, 宋子文, 葉楚傖徐謙, 鄧演達 등 정부요인을 맞난 후 맨 마즈막으로 외교부장 陣友仁에게 서면으로 회견을 신청하였더니 그 翌日에 즉시 회신이 왔는데 장소와 시간을 明記하고 반겨 맞나겠다는 것이었다. 내 원래 이듵의 시간약속에 신임을 두지 못하였지만 어째피 나는 구하는 사람이라 지정시간 전에 외교부에 가서 하폄만 하고 있노라니 웬걸! 꼭 지정시각이 되자마자 비서관이 나와서 나를 大臣室에 안내함에는 一驚* 喫하지 안을 수 없었다. 실내에 一步를 옴겨 놓으면서 얼* 삺여보니 書架와 卓床의 모든 문서가 질서정연하게 놓인 법은 얼는 현대식의 사무 정치가라는 인상을 주웠다. 그는 유창한 영어로 나의 遠來를 위로하여 준 후 일전에 그대가 보낸 佛文會見書簡은 그대가 쓴 것이냐고 뭇는다. 나는 이 무름에 슬그머니 아니꼬운 생각이 났으나 陳씨의 이 무름 속에 惡意의 바늘이 백여있지 않음을 간파하고 나는 공순히 그러타 하였더니 다소 感心하는 듯한 표정을 하면서 학교 출신이며 전공 학과에 佛蘭西에 얼마나 있었으며 두루두루 뭇는 것이 내가 마치 무슨 심문이나 받는 것같었다. 나는 여기서 陣氏의 말을 중단하고 이번은 내 차레오 하면서 미리 적어 가지고<132> 간 질문 사항을 開陣하였어니 이윽고 陣氏는 여보 그런데 그대가 지금 내 영어를 書取하는 그 글이 朝鮮語인가요 하기에 그렇다 하니 이번에는 陣氏가 자못 感心하는 것이 분명하였다. 회견이 끝나고 하직하려 하니 그런데 정말 朝鮮가서야 하겠소 하는 陣氏의 표정에는 나에게 어떠한 『앱피-』을 하는 것 같이 느껴젔다. 그럼 朝鮮 가야지 내가 支那에 있어서 뭘 하겠어요 하니 陣氏는 그렇겠지오! 하면서 다소 섭섭히 녁이는 무엇이 있는 듯이 보였다.
陣氏에 비하면 孫科와 宋子文이는 몹시 건방진 편이였다. 孫科는 俗稱 太子로서 父威를 배경으로 방자하게 잘어난 것이 원인인지 대체로 우리 같은 기자도 사람으로 취급하느냐가 의문이었다. 아침 10시에 오라 하기에 지정대로 교통부에 가서 받부다 하면서 좀처럼 맞나지 안타가 오후 4시- 아! 배가 곺았다-에 겨우 引見하고서는 첫 말이 질문을 간단히 하라는 것이었다. 그러나 이것이야 말로 孫科의 誤算이지. 나 亦 신문기자로 멀니 온 사람이라 모처럼 회견을 어든 바엔 상대방의 注文 그대로 쉽게 쫓을 理 萬無이었다. 아니 6, 7시간을 기리 기대리든 짜징 난 憤푸이로라도 우정 질문을 더 끌고 더 오래할 참이었는데 간단히 하라니 말이 않된 말이었다. 孫科는 연해 시계를 끄집어 내여 보면서 마즈막에는 비는 듯이 여보 중요회의 있어서 이 이상은 뭇지 마서요 하기에 나도 겨우 椅子에서 엉뎅이를 들었다.
요컨대 당시의 인상으로는 포용력으로 敏捷하기로 精細하기로 또는 지식정도로 모든 점에 있어서 陣友仁이 支那에서는 出衆한 정치가 이였다고 記憶되였다.<133>
