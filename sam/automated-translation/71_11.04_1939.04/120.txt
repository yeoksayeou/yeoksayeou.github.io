An Open Letter to Unfortunate Women
It seems there are many in this world standing at the crossroads of fate, tormented by worries, as letters inquiring about such matters often arrive at our office. To guide these unfortunate women towards a path of light, our company has requested responses from various renowned ladies, which we publish together here. We intend to receive more such letters from women weeping in distress in the future. (Editorial Department)<109>

Should I follow as the Second Wife of an Executive? Will Happiness Come to Me?

Ch'oe Su-ryŏn, Pusan
I am a maiden who attended a certain vocational college up to the second year. To speak frankly, I might be what the world calls an "old miss." I am already twenty-three years old. After long torment, having no other recourse, I have taken up my pen seeking the guidance of my seniors. Although my parents are alive, they are not only elderly, but my father, who was known as a millionaire until 10 years ago, has completely withdrawn from worldly affairs after business failure. Thus, even if I were to confess my feelings, there would likely be no insightful answer, so I thought it better to handle it myself, but alone, I simply cannot figure out what to do.
My family's situation is, of course, dire. What more can be said of a family that fell from prosperity? It might have been better if we had been poor from the start. I cannot bear seeing my parents suffer. This is also why I missed my chance for marriage. Since leaving school, I worked as a primary school teacher, then as a company clerk, and now I am at a bank, but it is difficult to support a family of five on my salary alone. Above me are my aging parents, and below me are all my younger siblings. Recently, I received a marriage proposal to become the second wife of a certain millionaire. Of course, if I consent, he says he will guarantee the livelihood of my parents and siblings, and also grant me separate land and property. I heard this dazzling proposal directly from him. He is old. Fifty-seven, they say. Old enough to be my father's peer. And it's as a second wife. In other words, a concubine. Thinking of saving my parents and siblings, and of my own body finding comfort, I should happily run to my parents and tell them, but with these heavy conditions dangling one after another, I have been agonizing alone for two months since receiving the proposal, wondering whether to do this or that, and haven't told my parents. If I tell my parents, they will surely tell me to marry him. Therefore, I am afraid and cannot tell my parents. Yet, I do not want to simply reject the proposal either. The desire to sacrifice myself to save <110> the entire family arises. At other times, the desire arises to leave this dreadful hardship behind, regardless of what others say, and live a life of ease. Teacher, what should I do? Is it better to marry and save my parents and siblings? Or should I endure the hardship as it is and wait for a better marriage proposal? Is there any day when light might shine upon my future?

(Reply) Happiness Does Not Come to a "Concubine," Become a Laboring Woman

Hwang Sin-dŏk
I sympathize with your family situation. I also fully understand your feelings regarding the marriage problem you currently face.
However, it would be best for you to give up on that marriage proposal.
In today's Korea, which is in a transitional period (過渡期), we cannot definitively say that being a concubine is uniformly immoral. However, generally speaking, polygamy becomes a cause of discord within a family and misfortune for generations of descendants (子孫累代), and brings about results that disturb social order.
Of course, if one holds a view of love that transcends national borders, ethnicity, and family registers, and carries it out thoroughly, it is a separate issue. But your current situation and desires are different from that; the desire to resolve your heavy family responsibilities while simultaneously seeking a comfortable life for yourself is stirring within you. This psychology is certainly not unreasonable. Ultimately, poverty (貧寒) has weakened your mind and body, even paralyzing your judgment.
Since you even have experience as an educator, please try to think differently from a more objective, third-person perspective.
A millionaire offers to divide land, permanently solve your financial problems, and so forth. Many are deceived by this and become second wives, third wives.
However, not only might these promises merely be means to make you his possession, but even if they were fulfilled, they would by no means lead you to a happy life. You must understand that it drags you into eternal misfortune. That complex environment and the slave-like life of gold will make you long for the noble life of labor you live today.
Your spirit of wanting to sacrifice for your parents and siblings is admirable,<111> but to do so by becoming a concubine, selling your body for gold, is, I declare, a gravely mistaken thought. Even if you ask a thief, they will have their reasons. If there are points in this answer you disagree (不肯) with, please come for a direct consultation. There are things I wish to discuss with you quietly. (End)

Writer or Marriage? If I Become a Housewife, Will My Husband Not Restrain Me?

Kim Suk-kyŏng, Kyŏngsŏng
Teacher, I am a twenty-three-year-old maiden who will graduate from the English Department of a certain Women's Vocational College this spring. Thinking that I will soon leave school torments me to the point of sleepless nights. My ambition is literature, what I have studied is literature, and therefore, what I should do in the future must naturally be literature. However, my parents, who I thought understood my aspirations until now and said little, are suddenly rushing me, saying I must get married. They say that when a woman enters a household, she becomes tied down by domestic affairs and cannot do anything else. Especially for someone like me who intends to pursue literature, how can I get married? I have studied with the ambition for literature for nearly 10 years. Even if I die, I do not want to do anything other than literature. Marriage seems like something I could do without until I die. Nevertheless, my parents do not listen to me, so what should I do? My parents say that one can pursue literature even after marrying, but the question is whether I can truly establish my will after marriage. Yet, I am in a position where I cannot completely disregard my parents' wishes. Therefore, what should I do? I can only await your wise guidance, Teacher.

(Reply) Marry When You Are Forty; Marriage and Being a Writer Are Not Mutually Exclusive

Mo Yun-suk
You must be greatly tormented. I find it regrettable that you have come to think of literature and marriage as separate. Literature and marriage are flowers of life that should bloom together in one garden (花園) by destiny.<112> If there is a woman who tries to separate and resolve these individually, it proves that this woman is certainly situated in some imperfect social environment. There is no principle that dictates one must fear marriage to succeed in literature. Rather, isn't complete literature established where these two consciences harmonize?
However, because you are a woman living in the reality of Korea, you possess this unreasonable anguish (煩苦). That's right. In the current reality, it is true that marriage and literature cannot be completely combined to achieve harmony.
Unless you meet a good husband, your hopes and talents will die midway. Therefore, I dare to advise you: If your aspirations are noble and sound, turn a blind eye to marriage for now and study literature. Then, after your will and emotions have matured, perhaps after turning 40, marry the person in your heart. I believe it is natural to give yourself the intention to rely on someone who suits your heart when you feel inclined. If the literature you currently possess triumphs over your feelings for marriage, please follow that triumphant feeling. I believe your parents' command depends on how you yourself manage it. Marry late, very late. If there is no suitable partner when you are older, wouldn't it be fine to forgo it? As long as you produce valuable works...

A Foreign Youth's Courtship; He Asks Me to Cross the Pacific with Him

Pak Sun-hŭi, P'yŏngyang
I pray for your health, Teacher.
Teacher, I am a young maiden of nineteen. Judging by my age and knowledge, I am still little more than a child. I wonder if it is alright for someone like me to boldly ask you such an outlandish question, Teacher, but after agonizing alone for a long time, I now take up my pen to ask for your esteemed opinion. Please guide me well. Guiding one person like me will surely become guidance for the many women who suffer in similar difficult situations.
Now, let me tell you the truth of the matter. It was the night before Christmas last year. I attended a Christmas party of some foreigners with Mr. So-and-so, a friend of my deceased father.<113> Of course, several foreign friends of my father were there. They are people who had been invited to our house several times while my father was alive. Unable to forget their friendship with my father, they still invite me in his stead whenever there is a party, even though it has been over two years since he passed away. This is likely because my father often took me along when he was alive, fostering a certain degree of intimacy between them and me. That night too, I could laugh and talk with them without feeling the slightest awkwardness. However, a foreign youth, the son of a certain missionary, who had shown no such signs even at the Christmas party the year before last, or even when we met at a certain Summer Bible School last summer, began paying strange attention to me, I realized. He seemed to watch everything I did – laughing, talking, chatting – without missing a thing. That's why, whenever my eyes happened to drift towards that young man, wouldn't his gaze fiercely pierce mine, right upon my face? I don't know how many times I lowered my face because of that. Somehow, I couldn't help but feel my heart pound and my face flush hotly. It was a strange feeling, the first I had ever experienced since being born. The guarded feeling I had hitherto held towards foreigners disappeared, and somehow, I couldn't help but feel particularly affectionate and tender towards him. I cannot describe here the feeling of regret when, late at night, my father's friend and he escorted me home. He too kept looking out the car window (車窓), waving his hand in farewell several times. From that night on, I couldn't sleep thinking of him, and he apparently couldn't sleep thinking of me either. According to his letters, his existence depends on my existence in this world. Without me, the world and everything else means nothing, he said. It seems to be the same for me too, but when he talks about marriage, I somehow become worried. While I love him, marrying him feels unsettling. What if he suddenly leaves Korea, or takes me far away across the Pacific Ocean to his country? At times, out of my overwhelming love for him, the feeling that I could go anywhere with him is not entirely absent – but when I think of completely leaving the land that raised me, my mother, siblings, and friends, I become sad enough for tears to well up.
Teacher, what should I do? Should I marry him? He urges me to marry him as soon as possible.<114>

(Reply) If It Is Stronger Than Death, Then Go with Him

Yi Sŏn-hŭi
A Christmas party.
A foreign youth's courtship.
Thinking of these things suddenly throws my mind into confusion. Frankly, if I were faced with such a situation myself right now, I would likely just weep and wander, unable to find any special solution.
Judging from your letter, you seem quite accustomed to Westerners and adept at their way of life.
Also, if your appearance is attractive enough to capture the attention of foreigners, who generally have high standards, I imagine you must be beautiful first and foremost. If you possess culture, both physically and mentally, sufficient not to be inferior to those foreigners, and if an immortal love exists between the two concerned individuals, then marriage would be fine.
And following him across the Pacific could also be happiness.
However, foreign youths can easily feel an exotic affection (엑쏘틱한 戀情) and curiosity towards daughters of a nation they look down upon as inferior.
This is shown in numerous movie stories, but the blond youth might feel a peculiar dream and love precisely because you are an Oriental girl.
If it becomes like this, when that dream shatters in the distant future and only naked life is revealed, the two might separate and flee far from each other.
In such a case, marriage with a foreign youth is not much fun.
In any case, take a longer time to sound out the depths of both your hearts. If, after settling everything else, what remains last is a love stronger than death, then, without question, the two of you must marry. <115>

**Summary**

This 1939 article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine presents an "agony aunt" column where three young Korean women seek advice on major life decisions, receiving replies from prominent female figures (Hwang Sin-dŏk, Mo Yun-suk, Yi Sŏn-hŭi). The first woman weighs becoming a wealthy older man's concubine to save her impoverished family. The second struggles between pursuing a literary career and parental pressure to marry. The third is torn between her love for a foreign missionary's son and the fear of leaving her homeland. The advice given generally prioritizes personal integrity, long-term happiness, and self-reliance over immediate financial security or traditional expectations, though acknowledging the difficult realities faced by women in colonial Korea.

For historians, this article offers valuable insights into the social pressures, economic hardships, and evolving aspirations of educated Korean women during the late colonial period. It reflects the tension between traditional Confucian values (filial piety, marriage) and modern Western influences (individual desire, career ambitions, romantic love, international relationships). The specific dilemmas discussed – poverty forcing morally compromising choices, the perceived incompatibility of marriage and career for women, and the complexities of intercultural romance against a backdrop of colonial power dynamics – highlight the unique challenges faced by the "New Woman" in 1930s Korea. The advice itself reveals contemporary progressive female thought, emphasizing self-worth, labor, and cautious consideration in matters of marriage and love, while subtly acknowledging the constraints of their social environment.

薄倖女性에게 公開狀
이 세상에는 운명의 기로에 서서 번민하는 이가 만흔듯 하야 본사에 그 문의의 편지가 각금 여러 장이 드러온다. 이 薄倖女性에게 광명의 길를 가르치기 위하야 본사에서는 名流 女性 諸氏의 答書를 청하여 여기 倂載하기로 한다. 번민에 우는 여인 諸氏의 이같은 편지는 앞으로도 더 밧으려 하노라.(編輯局)<109>

重役 第二夫人으로 따라 가면 一身에 幸福이 오리까?
