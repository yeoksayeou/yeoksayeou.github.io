Folk Song Collecting Journey (2)
						Yi Sangin

Day 3. Today, a drizzling rain fell, and the day ended with me confined to my room.
Reflecting on the poetic line by Master T'oedo, "客中愁思雨中多" (Worries during travel multiply in the rain).

Day 4. The rain that fell until dawn had vanished as if washed away, and only the clear blue sky was perfectly bright.
Where should I head today? I pondered, but nothing particularly good came to mind!
While hesitating, I met an awkward drinking buddy who guided me to an unremarkable *makgeolli* (rice wine) house. After a few cups of wine, the awkward friend needlessly explained my purpose.
Hearing this, the tavern keeper (酒母), as if finding her moment, raised her voice and sang what she called the "Jealousy Song" (嫉妬歌) and the "Orphan's Lament" (孤兒寃). I didn't write them down on the spot. Nor did I press her for details, yet the tavern keeper, adding explanations, chattered on, half story, half song.

Living in a palace-like tiled house,
With sea-like fields,
Having a husband like Ondal,
Having a cherry-like daughter, a pomegranate-like son,
Having a stork-like mother-in-law,
Having a loft-like servant,
He says he's leaving, he says he's leaving,
To marry a concubine, he says he's leaving.
On the day he goes to marry,
If he rides a palanquin, may the palanquin pole break.
If he walks the road, may the road split open.
If he enters the wedding hall (行禮廳),
May his formal hat and gown (紗帽冠帶) be torn.
When they exchange bows,
May the leg of the large ceremonial table break.
When the side table (赤床) is brought in, may it break this way and that.
When the bridal chamber (新房) is prepared,
May it become his final parting. Go, go,
Go marry a concubine, go, go.

This is the "Jealousy Song" (嫉妬歌). It's said that the wife of a certain prominent household, burning with jealousy as her husband<175> was about to take a concubine, sang this. Somehow, it became popular for a time among tavern maids (酒幕 酌婦). Only this faintest of legends remains; the era of its origin and detailed explanation are obscure.
Having written this much, let's move on to the next—

Poknam, oh Poknam, don't cry. Every time you cry, my heart melts.
Don't cry, don't cry. If you cry, my eyes bleed.
Poknam, oh Poknam, our mommy comes to give milk. Mommy, mommy, our mommy, what deep sleep holds you? How can you not know our Poknam is crying?
Our mommy sleeps under the green grass on the front hill.
Where did our daddy go? He sleeps in the wooden coffin (나무독).
Where did our daddy go? He sleeps in the blue pavilion room (靑樓房).
Mommy, mommy, our mommy, quickly wake up, hold this child, and give our Poknam milk.
Sad, so sad, our siblings. Poor siblings of ours, losing parents within our first year, how will we live? Will there ever be a time when flowers bloom for us too?

This is the song called "Orphan's Lament" (孩兒寃). Although this song lacks even an origin or legend, its content speaks for itself, requiring no explanation.
It expresses the innocent and pitiful sentiments of a child. What a sorrowful song of tears, sung out of longing for parents, surpassing mere sentimentality!
This single song, in terms of quality, can be considered a significant harvest in any sense.
Reflecting here once more, I feel that the true essence of general social culture is to be found, above all, in folk songs. Why? Because folk songs arise from popular sentiment (民情) and local customs (土俗), which are the inner crystallizations of social life. Also, their rhythm is natural, rich in local color (로칼, 칼라) and flavor, and they are always frank expressions of emotion, free from falsehood, masks, and affectation.
Ah, but one flaw is that collecting these important folk songs with clumsy methods and without any system is, more than anything, regrettable and cannot but be a shortcoming, can it?
Today, I returned with unexpected gains.

Day 5. I met Mr. K. Mr. K is a very gentle and refined young friend.<176>
Mr. K seems to have great interest in these folk songs and apparently collects them sometimes.
Recalling the old poem "司恨晩相逢" (Regretting the late meeting), I explained my purpose to him and asked him to make an effort. He pulled out a notebook and told me to choose whichever I liked.

Loom Song (베틀노래)

Baby, baby, playing baby, coming into the world
to wander, without anything special to do,
Let's set up the loom, let's set up the loom, by the Oknam River (玉郎江),
Let's set up the loom. Loom legs, four legs,
The front legs propped up, the horizontal and vertical pieces
Are placed across the sitting frame.
The *bit'aeilshin* (a loom part) tied around the waist,
My two legs hidden underneath.
Wearing wooden clogs (나무말코) and a hair ribbon (댕기),
Dressing a dead mulberry branch (죽은애), hanging a pair of *kkuri* (shuttles),
The *mulchi* (temple) and *chŏjŭlgae* (reed?)
Are hung by the Oknam River. The jujube wood
*Yŏnsaengbuk* (warp beam?) holds black eggs within.
Naturally bent, worn-out shoes (헌신짝)
Are tied by the neck. The sound of lifting the *padŭijip* (heddle frame?)
Is like the Jade Maiden (玉皇仙女) in heaven striking bamboo.
The *mulchi* and *chŏjŭlgae*
Dart here and there. The *ing'et'ae* (lease rods?)
Are three brothers. Sharpening the *osatch'im* (reed hook?),
The naturally bent *chwich'wal* (bow temple?)
Spreads between the eyebrows. The way the cloth rolls onto the beam (베가리)
Is like surrounding the Hongmun Banquet (鴻門宴)
With a hundred flowers. The nodding *nunsŏpttae* (treadles?)
Are like offering a cup before parents.
The meaning of begging for alms (施主)—
The much-entangled cloth beam (도트마리),
With no way to make a living, falling down and getting up,
The countless threads of the warp (배빛대)
Are like military police whips? Thrown here and there.
The sound is good, the dragon-head part (용두머리)!
In the ninth and tenth moons, amidst autumn maples, a lone wild goose,
A pair of wild geese, having lost their mate, search.
Three days after setting up the loom, a bolt of cotton cloth
Is cut off. Boil it in the front cauldron,
Rinse it in the front stream,
Bleach it in the back stream, make starch like pear blossoms,
Apply it to the cloth beam. The covered wagon (난장마차)
Washes it fold by fold. Let's make it, let's make it,
Let's make clothes for my beloved. Dry them with a silver goose (은가새 - iron?),
With nine-strand cotton thread,
Doing the upper and lower stitches, setting them aside,
With the middle stitch, blinking twice, it's done. Bring the silver *taribi* (iron)
And iron them. Try them on, try them on, my beloved,
Try them on. Looking from the front, it's my beloved,
Looking from the back, it's someone else's beloved. Look, look,
A purple jacket (*tongjŏguri*), jade buttons, layered clothes (*ch'ŏpch'angot*),
Wearing a *kulch'u* silk sash,
Wearing a formal knife (紗모粧刀), let's go, let's go,
Let's go to the concubine's house. Crossing pass after pass,
Plucking flowers on the front southern mountain,
Brushing off dew, brushing off mist, there it is,
There it is, the concubine's house is there.
It's tied, it's tied, the swing rope (鞦韆)<177>
Is tied. Let's swing. One push, anxious,
Two pushes, anxious.
Fell off, fell off, the swing rope
Fell off. Torn, torn,
The jade-buttoned layered clothes are torn.
"Great, great Mother-in-law (큰어만님), what are you patching?"
If she asks, say "A green thread got caught
On a lacquer tree branch and tore slightly."
Great, great Mother-in-law, having come
To the main house, what will you eat before leaving?
Rice mixed with millet like Yi clan's (*isshi*), with beans like cherries,
Three-year-old kimchi (*ch'ojirang*),
And beef ribs (*soeppul kat'ŭn tŏrŏkchi*). Eat,
Eat, eat when urged.
Hey, you wicked wench! Even if your house
Has walls built with yeast (*nuruk*),
And a fence of *ch'ilgi ch'ŏngch'ŏng* (?),
Even if my house doesn't exist, its walls are built with wood (*t'ong*),
Even if my house doesn't exist, its fence is woven with strong thread (*tangdaesil*).
Cannot live, cannot live,
Cannot live alone in an empty room. Spinning Wheel Song (물래노래)

Spinning Wheel Song (물래노래)

Little baby in the front house, big baby in the back house, let's go play, let's go play, To the water place of Kimhae Kimsan (김해김산).
At the hairpin bend (살가락) of Yŏnghae Yŏngdŏk (영해영덕),
A change occurs, a change occurs,
At the crab's head (게머리), a change occurs.
Squeeze it, squeeze it, sesame oil,
Squeeze it. It's soft, it's soft.
Won't you spin? Won't you spin? Quickly grasp and
Won't you spin? The desired thread (*baran nŭi*)— mix water
Into the porridge (*pŏrijuk*) and wait for me.
On the twelve-strand fine thread (*myŏngkarak*), three heavenly maidens (仙女)
Place the wooden frame (*namuna t'ŭl*) before them, arrange the warp threads (*ori ori sach'ŏnoko*).
Elder sister-in-law in the front house,
Elder sister-in-law in the back house, thread it for me,
Thread it for me, thread our loom for me.
Tie it for me, tie it for me, with ten hands,
Tie it for me.

*Ilgu Yŏngdŏk i mulneya* (?)
At the apricot hairpin bend (살구영덕 살가락애), where does the change occur?
Try dabbing sesame oil. The *yŏlsaegŏri* (part of spinning wheel?),
The *yŏlsaegŏri* turns. Where does the change occur?
At the crab's head (게머리), the change occurs.

Swing Song (鞦韆歌)

*Iburŏi suna* (refrain?)
Planting pines on the back hill, half are *tabak* pines (?).
Sowing hemp in the dung field, half are *wadagari* (?).
*Ŏburŏi suna* (refrain?)

Planting rice in the deep paddy, half is chaffy rice (*aengmi ssal*).
Sowing mugwort on the slope, half is blighted mugwort (*kkambuk suk*).

*Ŏburŏi sura* (refrain?)
Front mountain, push! Back mountain, pull!
It's the voice of a very old woman. Painting her white hair black,
Putting red beans on her pockmarked face,
Putting rouge on her blue complexion, putting pumpkin seeds where teeth are missing,
Putting a hairpin (*tonggot*) where the nose isn't straight,
Tucking up her hemp apron (*haengju ch'ima*), putting plucked flowers<178>
In her hair, tearing leaves to make a flute (*ch'egŭm*),
Breaking off pine needles to hold in her hand, taking off her socks (*posŏn*)
And putting them on her head, finding leggings (*ŏbŏktari makchoch'im*)
And putting them on, entering the children's room (*ahaedang*) shufflingly,
That too is quite a sight.
Twirling this way, tottering that way, "You too
Will get old, look at your legs," she says.
Turning back on the path she wasn't taking,
The way she comes back is imposing. Nevertheless,
Hanging a *kkalttak* hoe around her neck, slinging a small basket (*chongdaraekki*),
Let's go dig *pulminari* (water parsley?).

*Ŏburŏi suna* (refrain?)
Where did the guest come from? From Kyŏngdan River (경단강).
What horse did you ride? Rode a white horse (*paektae mal*).
How did you come as night fell? Slept at a reed-hatched house (*satkat chip*).
What bedding did you sleep on? Slept on silk bedding (*kongdan yo*).
What blanket covered you? Covered by a stage blanket (*mudae ibul*).
How did you come, being hungry? Ate at the east-west house (*tongsŏ chip*).
Where did they serve the rice? Served rice in the bowl base (*sikkikup*).
Where did they serve the soy sauce? Served sauce in the plate base (*chŏpsi kup*).
How did you come, it being cold? Wrapped in fallen leaves (*karangip*).
Who gave you that ring? A Seoul gentleman (*Sŏul yangban*) gave it and left.
Stand up, let's see your build.
Sit down, let's see your face.

Mr. K said he hasn't yet found the origins of the folk songs recorded above.
There are various trifling legends here and there, but when examined with strict observation, they turn out to be nothing more than baseless conjectures of later people, and a genuine historical explanation has not yet been discovered.
Today, with this, I thanked Mr. K for his kindness and departed towards the Ch'ŏngsong (靑松) region.

Day 6. Ch'ŏngsong. From Ch'ŏngsong, I sought out a village called Chigyŏng (地境), which is even more of a farming and mountain village.
Here, I looked for Mr. J. He was currently taking medicine due to illness.
After exchanging a few words of greeting with Mr. T (?), I got straight to the point. Mr. T tilted his head a few times and said,
"Where would any be left now? Even just a few years ago, there were some, but now? Now, popular songs! Since they've become the trend, it seems the shadows of genuine local folk customs and folk songs are gradually disappearing."
He frowned, looking somewhat troubled. Through Mr. T's indirect guidance, all I obtained throughout the day was:

Rice Seedling Planting Song (모숭기노래/移秧歌)

Lift it up, lift it up, this seedling tray!<179>
Take it away, messengers from the underworld (저승차사), esteemed young master, take this seedling tray away.
This paddy field like the sea floor, planting seedlings makes it a vast ocean (滄海).
What kind of sea is it itself? It's the East Sea, the sea.
On Yudarak Mountain (?) in Seoul, a golden dove lays eggs.
If you give me that egg, this egg, I will pass the state exam this year.
A smart and tall maiden sits perched on a mulberry branch.
Pick the leaves, pick the leaves, I'll pick them for you. Make a hundred-year promise with me.
At Konggal Pond (公갈못) in Sangju Hamch'ang (상 함창), tall girl picking lotus seeds (*yŏnbam*),
Lotus seeds, chestnuts, I'll pick them for you. Make a hundred-year promise with me.
The sun sets, on a darkening day, what beauty is weeping?
Parted from parents and siblings, weeping with nowhere to sleep.
The sun sinks, on a darkening day, what merchant departs?
The parting merchant whose first wife died, Yi T'aebaek (이태백 - Li Bai?), departs.
It's getting late, it's getting late, the midday meal (*chŏngsim ch'am*) is late.
Busy turning around in ninety-nine kitchen spaces, thus it's late.
Coming out, coming out, the half-moon shaped lunch bowl (*chŏngsim pakol*)
The full moon shape comes out. What kind of half-moon is it?
It's the crescent moon of the month's end and beginning.
Look at that chest, like a ramie jacket (*mosi chŏksam*) torso.
Looking too much brings illness, let me see just a fingernail's worth and go.
On our parents' grave mound, pines planted become a pavilion (*chŏngja*).
Strip the *chaengp'i* (?), put it in a basket, give me a pair of stems.

This water outlet, that water outlet, torn down,
Where did the Munnae gentleman (무내양반) go? Holding a Munnae abalone (?)
In his hand, he went to play at his concubine's house.

Planting here, planting there, planting is planting,
But that shady spot grows weeds (*mibal*).
Planting seedlings in this paddy field, golden, golden, flourishing.
Meeting the time of fine harvest and favorable weather (歲和年豐),
Glory for our village. When spring comes, azaleas;
When autumn comes, the five grains.

Today ended with this!
Not just today, but my personal circumstances absolutely could not afford such leisurely time for long.
Therefore, I concluded this objective, albeit meagerly, for now, decided to await the next opportunity for the rest, and turned back.
I express my sincere apologies to the general readers and specialists in this field (斯界專門家) for the many regrettable points due to my clumsy collection methods.

Finally—since folk songs themselves already belong to the category of local culture, their vocabulary uses dialects and archaic words, so there are quite a few places difficult to understand upon reading. However, gathering them without any preparation, I considered interpreting only the difficult phrases, but worried that if the interpretation were mistaken, it might instead become a major stain on the folk songs. Therefore, I decided to postpone発表 (announcement/publication) until after some research on a later day, and thus lay down my pen.<180>

**Summary**

This article, part two of Yi Sangin's "Folk Song Collecting Journey," documents his experiences on days 3 through 6 of his trip in April 1939. He records several folk songs obtained from a tavern keeper and a friend (Mr. K), including a "Jealousy Song," an "Orphan's Lament," a "Loom Song," a "Spinning Wheel Song," and a "Swing Song." He laments the difficulty of finding authentic folk songs due to the rise of popular music and the lack of documented origins for the songs he does collect. He concludes by noting the linguistic difficulties in transcribing and interpreting dialectal and archaic language found in the songs.

For historians, the article offers a glimpse into cultural life in rural Korea under Japanese rule, specifically the active effort by intellectuals like Yi Sangin to preserve traditional folk culture amidst modernization and the influx of new cultural forms (like popular songs). The content of the songs themselves reflects social themes (jealousy over concubines, the plight of orphans, women's labor at the loom and spinning wheel) and provides valuable linguistic data on regional dialects and older Korean vocabulary from the late 1930s. The author's explicit concern about the disappearance of folk traditions highlights the cultural anxieties of the period.

民謠採輯行脚(二)
						李相寅
第 3日. 이날은 비가 부슬부슬 나려서 방에 들어앉은 채 해는 저무렀다.
「客中愁思雨中多」라는 退陶 선생의 詩句을 返想면서-.
제 4日. 새벽까지 나리든 비는 어느듯 씻은 듯이 개고 오즉 새파란 하늘이 동그랗게 맑았다.
