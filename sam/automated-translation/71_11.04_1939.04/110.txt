Article Title: 政治家 李栗谷論, 東方의 先驅者의 그 思想 (A Discussion of the Statesman Yi Yulgok, The Thought of an Eastern Pioneer)
Author: 柳子厚 (Yu Chahŭi)
Article Type: 논설 (Editorial/Essay)

A Discussion of the Statesman Yi Yulgok, The Thought of an Eastern Pioneer
						Yu Chahŭi

Consensus System and Yulgok

Master Yulgok was someone who greatly respected people's opinions. During his time as Minister of Personnel (吏曹判書), he implemented a consensus system (合意制度) where officials, high and low, mutually discussed matters, regardless of the issue.
Originally, it was the Minister of Personnel who handled the personnel appointments register (都目). This register system, the *Tomok* (都目), was divided into upper and lower periods (上下 兩都目), corresponding to June and December. In modern terms, it was a system of upper and lower half-years.
In our Chosŏn, the original number of officials was 3,000 inner posts and 800 outer posts, meaning 3,000 officials in central government positions and 800 provincial governors and magistrates (外任方伯守令). The advancements and retreats of these 3,800 inner and outer officials were decided during the upper and lower *Tomok* periods. To decide this, the Minister of Personnel employed his own people as Ministry of Personnel clerks (吏曹執吏), and these clerks selected and decided. When Master Yulgok became Minister of Personnel, he abolished this system of selection by Ministry of Personnel clerks. Instead, using the clerks' selections as a basic draft, he respected and broadly gathered opinions from the officials of the Six Ministries (六曹官員) and the public discourse of Chosŏn (朝鮮公論). After mutually discussing the matter and personally gathering what he heard and saw, he appointed upright and wise scholars (方正賢士). This consensus system was unseen before Yulgok's time as Minister of Personnel, demonstrating how the sage's administrative handling of affairs always involved appointing based on ability and employing the worthy (任能用賢).

Chosŏn Sage Transformation Movement

Master Yulgok infinitely endeavored to have all the people of Chosŏn assimilate into sages (聖人). However, he deeply lamented seeing that the people of his time thought the learning of sages was not found in everyday activities (日用動靜) but in some profound, mysterious, marvelous, lofty, and difficult-to-practice realm,<100> thus indulging in wild thoughts (馳心妄思). It wasn't just the masses of Yulgok's time who thought this way; the general populace before and after Master Yulgok similarly indulged in wild thoughts, not seeking sage-like learning in their everyday activities but letting their minds race towards the highest, most distant, and profound places, ultimately showing, and continuing to show, the terrible downfall of self-deprecation and self-abandonment (自景自棄).
Master Yulgok, thinking that while the past couldn't be helped, in his present and future, he must release the numerous masses from the shackles of self-neglect and self-abandonment (自暴自棄) and lead them all to return as one to sagehood (聖人으로 一是同歸). He radiated this eternally indestructible great ideal. Whether serving in court (立朝) or residing in the countryside (在野), his singular, devoted heart (惟惟丹丹 一心) worked infinitely through the ruler, the state, and the masses, making the unified return to sagehood and worthiness (聖賢同歸) his ideal and his practice.
In the year he turned 41, he completed the construction of a study hall (精舍) at Sŏktam Kugok (石潭九曲) in Haeju (海州), Hwanghae Province (黃海道). He then composed an eternally unchanging propaganda text (宣傳文) for the nationwide Sage Transformation Movement (全國民 聖化運動). This was the *Kyŏngmong Yogyŏl* (擊蒙要訣 - Essentials for Dispelling Ignorance), promulgated in the Chŏngch'uk year (丁丑年) when he was 42. This *Kyŏngmong Yogyŏl* consists of 10 chapters: 1. Setting Aspirations (立志), 2. Reforming Old Habits (革舊習), 3. Cultivating Oneself (持身), 4. Reading Books (讀書), 5. Serving Parents (事親), 6. Mourning Rites (喪制), 7. Ancestral Rites (祭禮), 8. Managing the Household (居家), 9. Interacting with Others (接人), and 10. Conducting Oneself in the World (處世). Becoming a sage is nothing special; it is accomplished by mastering these 10 articles. Being a sage is achieved amidst everyday affairs (日用事物), not in some profound, mysterious, lofty place. I will now cite what the master declared for the sage transformation of the masses to clearly prove this.
In the *Kyŏngmong Yogyŏl*, Chapter 1, Setting Aspirations, it says:
"Beginners in learning must first set their aspirations, resolving to become sages. Do not harbor even a speck of thought that diminishes or excuses oneself. Fundamentally, the essential nature (本性) of all people and sages is the same, without any difference. Although in terms of vital force (氣運) and material endowment (바탕), there might inevitably be differences in clarity and turbidity (淸濁), purity and mixedness (粹駁), if one truly knows how to practice sincerely, casts off old stains (舊染), and recovers the beginning of one's endowed nature (性初), that is, the nature endowed by heaven (稟性), then without adding even a hair's breadth, all ten thousand goodnesses (萬善) will be sufficient. Why shouldn't everyone resolve to become sages themselves? Therefore, Mencius (孟子), speaking of the goodness of human nature (性善), always cited Yao (堯) and Shun (舜) as proof, saying, 'All people can become Yao or Shun.' How could these words deceive me?
So, masses, do thus. Always rouse yourselves<101> and say, 'Human nature is originally good; there is no difference in wisdom between ancient and modern times. Why then did sages alone become sages, and why have we alone become [ordinary] people? This is truly because our aspirations are not established, our knowledge is not bright, and our conduct is not sincere (敦篤). Establishing my aspirations, brightening my knowledge, and making my conduct sincere all depend on me. If I do not seek this within myself, what else and where else shall I seek it?' Yan Hui (顔淵) said, 'What kind of man was Emperor Shun? What kind of man am I?' Take the heart and spirit with which Yan Hui looked up to Shun as my model (範바다 法하야), and resolve firmly to become a sage. If you solidify this aspiration and only advance without retreating (惟進不退), becoming a sage happens amidst everyday activities (日用動靜). It is not something that comes from some lofty, distant, profound, and mysterious place." This was the declaration of the oath of the spirit for becoming a sage (作聖精神).

Ruler-People Unity, Advocacy of Frugality

Whether an individual or a ruler, in founding, maintaining, and bringing prosperity to their household or country, without the virtue of advocating frugality (崇儉) as the fundamental spirit, maintenance and security cannot be achieved. In other words, diligence and frugality (勤儉) bring forth all kinds of prosperity, while luxury (奢侈) invites ruin in a hundred forms. Then as now, it is easy to go from frugality to luxury (從儉入奢), but difficult to go from luxury to frugality (從奢入儉). However, the customs of Yulgok's time appeared excessively extravagant and luxurious (奢靡侈極) to Master Yulgok's sagely eyes. To reform these customs, he advocated social reformism in his "Ten Thousand Word Memorial" (萬言對事), stating that only by uniting ruler and people (君民一致) to discard luxury and advocate frugality (去奢崇儉) could the deterioration of society and the ruin of the state be saved. That is, concerning "Advocating Frugality (崇與儉) to reform the trend of luxury," he said: "The poverty of the people and the exhaustion of finances (民窮財盡) have never been worse than today. You must emulate the virtue of frugality (崇儉의 美德) of the sage kings and ancestors (聖王祖宗), reduce tribute goods (貢物), and exercise fiscal discipline in balancing income and expenditure (量入爲出). If you do not emulate the sage laws of the ancestors (祖宗聖法), balancing income and expenditure will fail, like putting a round lid on a square container (方底圓蓋). Furthermore, considering the lives of the masses groaning under tribute demands (貢物進上) and other exactions (誅求), it is utterly indescribable. Yet, looking at the lifestyle customs of the middle and upper classes today, they indulge in extreme luxury (窮奢極侈) as if the day is not long enough. Eating is not for filling the stomach (充腹) but for boasting of delicacies and sumptuous feasts (珍需盛饌). Clothing is not for covering the body and observing appropriate decorum (儀式) but for competing with dazzling garments (燦燦衣服). The cost of one meal (一卓) could exceed several months' food for the hungry (飢者), and the cost of one set of clothes (一襲) could exceed the clothing for ten cold people (寒者). If the fields cultivated by ten people (十人에 畊田) are insufficient for one person's food (一人의 食), and the cloth woven by ten people (十人에 織布) is insufficient for one person's clothing (一人의 衣), and if those who cultivate and weave (畊織) are few<102> while those who eat and wear clothes (衣食) are many, what will be the result? How can the people avoid hunger and cold? The ancients' saying that the harm of luxury is worse than natural disaster (天災) is indeed true. If Your Majesty does not first strive for self-discipline (節操) to rescue [us] from this calamity (禍患), no matter how strict the penal laws (刑法) or frequent the commands (號令), it will be futile effort (徒勞). I once heard words from old records (故志): When a great minister (大臣) of King Sŏngjong's (成宗) time was gravely ill, another minister went to inquire. He was lying in the inner palace (大內), covered with a reddish-brown silk quilt (明紬 이불) that was almost worn out, yet he had not changed it. Those who heard this—even now—cannot help but admire him. I wish Your Majesty would emulate the ancestors (祖宗), cut back all palace expenses (宮中用度), set an example for inside and outside [the court] (中外), reform the extravagant habits (侈習) of the populace, and cultivate the people so that they become ashamed of delicacies, sumptuous feasts, and dazzling garments. Guide them so that everyone knows how to save, allowing the people's strength (民力) to relax and expand (暢舒)."
There is an old poem:
"Yesterday I went out to the walled city,
Returning, tears filled my chest.
Those dressed head-to-toe in silks and brocades (綺羅者)
Are not the ones who raise silkworms (養蠶人)."
Seeing the extravagant scenes of his era, Master Yulgok's sagely eyes must have brimmed with tears (盪盪). Although this "Ten Thousand Word Memorial" (萬言封事) was a petition (上䟽) submitted to King Sŏnjo (宣祖大王), its fundamental spirit serves as an eternal sagely teaching (聖訓) admonishing the wealthy and arrogant (富貴騰慢) multitude, making the significance of the memorial even clearer when adopted.

Seeking the Position of Head Teacher for Children (童蒙山長)

Master Yulgok emphasized children's education (兒童敎育) above all else. This was because they were the future bearers of society and the state, the second citizens (第2 國民). In a memorial submitted to King Sŏnjo resigning from office (辭職封事), we see:
"Schools (學校) are the foundation of culture and customs (風化). But looking now, schools are dilapidated (荒廢), and the spirit of education (敎育에 정신) has been corrupted for a long time. From where will culture and customs arise? Internally, the National Academy (成均舘) exists in name only (일홈 뿐이오), lacking practical education (實務敎育), so the spirit of promoting learning (興學) is negligible (蔑如). Externally, the county schools (鄕校) are even more pitiful. Recently, the establishment of private academies (書院) has been of no small help (不淺) in nurturing scholars dedicated to learning (志學에 士), but due to the lack of suitable head teachers (師長), Confucian students (儒生) gather together, indulge themselves freely (放意自肆), offer nothing worthy of emulation (矜式), and cannot achieve the effects of diligent study (藏修). There is no matter more lamentable and regrettable (可痛可恨) than this. Originally, the founding spirit of the state's establishment [of schools] was not like this. Some critics (議者) say it is right to abolish the Sŏwŏn indiscriminately,<103> but this is said out of mere indignation and is not a sound argument (正論). Your humble servant (愚臣) thinks that if we adopt and incorporate the system of Chinese Sŏwŏn (中國에 제도) to create a proper educational system (學制) for our Eastern Country (東邦), establish the positions of village head (洞主) and academy head (山長), provide salaries similar to those for teaching young children (童蒙敎授), and select individuals with learning and conduct (學行) who can serve as models (師表) or retired officials (休官隱退한 사람) with literary ability to lead them, the educational effects will be remarkable (可觀). Consequently, the state will gain considerable talent (得人) in the future. A person like me, useless both inside and outside the home (捨內 捨外에 百無所用의 인물), nevertheless has some expertise (專工) in parsing sentences and explaining texts (章句訓話). If you allow me to take charge of the position of academy head (山長) at the Haeju Sŏwŏn (海州書院), I will teach the young children (童蒙). Please do not trouble me with burdensome calls [to office] but allow me to live contentedly (安分하게); it would be my great fortune. In a sagely dynasty (聖朝), there are no discarded things (棄物). Your humble servant will not eat idly (徒食) but will engage in children's education." Indeed, in that year, Master Yulgok was summoned to important posts such as Third Minister of Personnel (吏曹叅議), Inspector General (大司諫), Right Royal Secretary (右承旨), and Governor of Chŏlla Province (全羅監司), but he declined them all, returned to Haeju, built the study hall at Sŏktam, and began educating children. The following year, he authored the *Kyŏngmong Yogyŏl*. Master Yulgok was truly an advocate for the sage transformation of children (兒童聖化運動者). That is, he devoted himself to the vocation of an elementary school teacher (小學校 訓導). Exchanging high positions like Royal Secretary, Inspector General, and Governor of Chŏlla Province for teaching young children is something only a sage could do. Contemporary elementary school teachers should deeply realize they are colleagues of Master Yulgok, who was the Head Teacher for Children (童蒙山長) at the Sŏktam Study Hall in Haeju during King Sŏnjo's era, and must inherit and thoroughly implement Master Yulgok's movement for the sage transformation of children. If children become sages, the national character (國格) will consequently be completely and eternally transformed into sagehood.

Master Yulgok's Theory of *Ki* Issuing and *Ri* Riding (*Ki Pal I Sŭng*)

In our East, the period when doctrines concerning *Ri* (Principle, 理) and *Ki* (Material Force/Vital Force, 氣) were most flourishing, forming a golden age, was the era of Master Yulgok. This was because many sages, worthies, and scholars emerged before and after his time. Among the numerous scholars who advocated *Ri-Ki* theories, summarizing the main points:
Master Cho Kwangjo (趙靜菴) seems to have advocated a theory of the single origin of *Ri* and *Ki* (理氣一原說), but in reality, it was not a theory of *Ri* and *Ki* being one substance (理氣一物說). Master Yi T'oegye (李退溪) advocated dualism, where both *Ri* and *Ki* issue (*Ri pal Ki pal* 理發氣發), a dualistic theory (二元論). Master Sŏ Hwadam (徐花潭) advocated the theory of the eternal persistence of the single *Ki* (一氣長存說). These masters, whoever they were, were all outstanding sages (聖者) or worthies (賢人) of our East, whose attainments were profound (玄奧), each establishing their own views. Thus, it was difficult to easily judge their theories right or wrong. However, only our Master Yulgok reviewed all these doctrines, refuted them, and established his unique,<104> unprecedented theory of the single origin of *Ri* and *Ki* (理氣一元論). Briefly stated:
"Fundamentally, what is this thing that scholars argue about, calling it Principle (*Rich'i*, 理致) or Vital Force (*Kiun*, 氣運)? Looking at the recent theories of the three masters Cho Kwangjo, Yi T'oegye, and Sŏ Hwadam, Cho Kwangjo's words are the highest among them. It can be said that the master clearly saw the entirety of *Ri* and *Ki*. However, there are some slightly incomplete aspects. Next is Master T'oegye's theory. Although the master also spoke from a full understanding, following Chu Hsi's (朱子) theory, he said that when Principle issues, Vital Force follows (理致가 發하면 氣運이 도라온다). This can be called a flaw (病痛) arising from excessive belief in Chu Hsi's doctrine. Next is Master Hwadam, who advocated the theory of the eternal persistence of the single *Ki*, meaning that one stream of Vital Force exists eternally and infinitely (永遠無窮) in this universe, not passing away from the past nor continuously coming from the future. This line of argument was Hwadam's unique creation (創說), a theory that contemporary scholars could not challenge. However, this is problematic when speaking of Principle, a flaw that fortunately Master T'oegye perceived, thereby rescuing later scholars. Generally, Master T'oegye's theory had much adherence to Chu Hsi's doctrine, showing much caution (謹愼). Master Hwadam's theory had much of his own assertion, showing much optimism (樂觀) in his words. Thus, Master T'oegye's words had fewer errors, while Master Hwadam's words perhaps had somewhat more errors. It is better to adopt T'oegye's cautious approach (謹愼主義) rather than finding a need to emulate Hwadam's optimistic approach (樂觀主義).
In short, the words of these three masters each have strengths and weaknesses (一短一長), and I cannot accept them as complete *Ri-Ki* theories. As Sakyamuni said, 'Although gold dust is precious, if it falls into the eye, the eye cannot see.' Similarly, although the words of sages and worthies are precious, if viewed incorrectly, they can instead cause harm. Chu Hsi's *Ri-Ki* theory is by no means dualistic. Therefore, concerning this *Ri-Ki* theory, I intend to take what I have thought and attempt to correct the theories of the three masters." Thus Master Yulgok spoke.
"And originally, Principle (*ich'i*, 이치) is the controller (主宰) of Vital Force (*Kiun*, 기운), and Vital Force is what Principle rides upon (所乘). That is, without Principle, Vital Force has nothing to be based on (根抵), and without Vital Force, Principle has no place to adhere to (依著). Therefore, Principle and Vital Force cannot be divided into two things, nor can they be called one thing. In other words, because they are not one thing, one becomes two; because they are not two things, two become one.
What does it mean that Principle and Vital Force are not one thing? Although Principle and Vital Force cannot be separated, in the midst of the profound and subtle (玄玄하고 妙妙한)<105> state, Principle is intrinsically Principle, and Vital Force is intrinsically Vital Force; they are not mixed (混同) or jumbled together (混雜). Therefore, they are not one thing. What does it mean that Principle and Vital Force are not two things? Although Principle is intrinsically Principle and Vital Force is intrinsically Vital Force, this Principle and this Vital Force cannot be separated and distinguished individually; they form an inseparable, unified whole (渾然한 一體). Therefore, Principle and Vital Force have no precedence or sequence (先後), nor do they ever separate even for a moment and then reunite, or reunite and then separate. They exist eternally together as one, hence they are not two things. Therefore, movement and stillness (動靜) are endless, Yin and Yang (陰陽) have no beginning (始初), Principle has no beginning, and thus Vital Force also has no beginning.
Fundamentally, Principle is only one. Thus, it originally has no distinction between partial/deviant (偏辟) and correct, no distinction between penetration (通) and blockage (맥키는), no distinction between clarity (맑은 것) and turbidity (흘인), no distinction between purity (純粹) and mixedness (雜駁). However, regarding the *Ki* that it rides upon (所乘에 氣運) – that is, when Vital Force issues (發), Principle rides (타고) the Vital Force, ascending and descending (升降), flying about (飛揚), operating without rest or pause (休食과 정지), creating all things (萬物) in various forms (여러 가지 형상) between heaven and earth through transformation (造化). This manifests sometimes as correct, sometimes as deviant, sometimes penetrating, sometimes blocked, sometimes clear, sometimes turbid, sometimes pure, sometimes mixed. Therefore, although Principle is one, when Principle rides upon Vital Force, its divisions and differentiations become ten thousandfold different (萬 가지로 달나지는 것이다). This becomes the Principle of Heaven and Earth (天地에 理致) in heaven and earth, the Principle of All Things (萬物에 理致) in all things, and the Principle of Us Humans (우리 사람에 理致) in us humans. In this way, although heaven, earth, humans, and all things each have their Principle, the Principle of Heaven and Earth is the Principle of All Things, and the Principle of All Things is namely the Principle of Us Humans. This is what is called the convergence into the one Great Ultimate (太極), the unified whole (統體 즉 統一合體). To state it once more: Principle is that which is above physical form (形而上), being formless and inactive (無形無爲). Vital Force is that which is below physical form (形而下), being possessed of form and activity (有形有爲). The formless and inactive Principle becomes the controller (主掌) of the formed and active Vital Force, and the formed and active Vital Force becomes the vessel (그릇(器)) for the formless and inactive Principle. Thus, the penetration of the formless Principle into the formed Vital Force is called *Ri* Penetration (*Ri t'ong*, 理通), the principle's universalizing function (理致에 通化作用). The confinement of the formed Vital Force by the formless Principle is called *Ki* Limitation (*Ki kuk*, 氣局), the vital force's particularizing function (氣運에 局限作用). That human nature (人性) is not the nature of things (物性) refers to the limiting function of Vital Force. That the Principle of humans and the principle of things<106> are the same refers to the universalizing function of Principle.
To give a concrete example (實例): a square vessel (모진 그릇) and a round vessel (둥근 그릇) are not the same, but the water inside the vessels is the same. A large bottle (큰 甁) and a small bottle (자근 甁) are not the same, but the emptiness (空 즉 빈 것) inside the bottles is the same. Therefore, the 'one root' (一本) of Vital Force refers to the reason why Principle universalizes (理致가 通化하는 然故), and that Principle differs in ten thousand ways refers to the reason why Vital Force particularizes (氣運이 局限하는 然故)." This was Master Yulgok's theory of *Ki* issuing and *Ri* riding (*Ki pal i sŭng*, 氣發理乘). In essence, it was a theory of *Ri* and *Ki* as one path (理氣一道), *Ri* and *Ki* as one substance (理氣一物說), a monistic theory of *Ri-Ki* where 'the myriad particularizations share one principle' (萬殊一理). His statement in the *Riil Punsu Pu* (理一分殊賦 - Essay on Principle is One, Its Manifestations are Diverse), "understanding the one root of the myriad particularizations" (悟萬殊之一本), represented the concise summary of his entire *Ri-Ki* theory.
Regarding this *Ri-Ki* theory, it stood in opposition to T'oegye's theory, the theory of mutual issuance of *Ri* and *Ki* (理氣互發說), leading to debates (甲論乙駁). Scholars of the Namin (南人) faction in the Yŏngnam (嶺南) region supported T'oegye's theory, while the Noron (老論) scholars in the Kiho (畿湖) region supported Yulgok's theory, creating two major divisions in the *Ri-Ki* theory among Confucian scholars (斯文理氣說). Thus, Yŏngnam came to be called the T'oegye school (退溪學派), and Kiho came to be called the Yulgok school (栗谷學派). <107>

**Summary**

This 1939 article by Yu Chahŭi discusses the political thought and philosophy of the 16th-century Joseon scholar-official Yi Yulgok. It highlights Yulgok's emphasis on consensus in governance (abolishing patronage in personnel appointments), his belief in the potential for all people to become sages through practical ethics outlined in his *Kyŏngmong Yogyŏl*, his advocacy for frugality (崇儉) as essential for social and state stability (criticizing luxury), and his deep commitment to children's education as foundational for the nation's future (even seeking a humble teaching post over high office). Philosophically, the article explains Yulgok's *Ki pal i sŭng* (氣發理乘說 - Ki issues and Ri rides) theory, arguing it corrected earlier theories by proposing a nuanced monism where Ri (Principle) and Ki (Material Force) are inseparable yet distinct, with Ki being the active force and Ri its inherent controller, ultimately converging in the Great Ultimate (太極). The article contrasts this with T'oegye's dualistic theory and mentions the resulting regional scholarly factions (Yŏngnam vs. Kiho). From a colonial history perspective, the article is interesting for its focus on a pre-modern Korean thinker known for practical statecraft, ethics, and national education during a time of Japanese rule. Highlighting Yulgok's ideas on consensus, universal potential, frugality against elite excess, and the importance of educating Korean children could be seen as subtly promoting Korean agency, cultural identity, and perhaps even social reform relevant to the contemporary situation.

合議制度와 栗谷

栗谷 선생께서는 대단히 사람에 의사를 존중히 하신 분이섰음니다. 선생은 吏曹判書 시대에 無論某事하고 上下官員이 互相論調하는 合意制度를 실시하섰음니다.
원래에 吏曹判書-라야 都目를 쓰는 것이 있음니다. 즉 都目은 上下 兩都目의 제도로 되여 있섰으니 6월 12월이였음니다. 현대어로 말삼하면 上下 兩半期의 제도이였음니다.
우리 朝鮮에 元 官吏 數가 內3천 外8백 즉 內職官吏가 3천 명이오 外任方伯守令이 8백 명 이였섰는데 이 內外官吏 3천8백명에 進退가 上下 兩都目에 결정되는 것임니다. 이것를 결정함에는 吏曹判書가 자기 사람으로 吏曹執吏를 채용하야 이 吏曹執吏가 物色決定하든 것임니다. 이렇게 하든 것을 栗谷 선생께서 吏曹判書가 되서서는 그러한 吏曹執吏에 物色制度를 폐하시고 吏曹執吏에 物色을 基礎案으로 가지고 六曹官員과 朝鮮公論을 尊重 廣採하야 互相論調한 후에 당신께서 친히 그 聞見을 收攬하신 후에 方正賢士를 등용하게 하섰으니 이 合議制度는 栗谷 吏判 이전는 보지 못하든 것으로 聖人의 行政處事에 任能用賢이 每每如是 하섰음니다.

朝鮮聖化運動

栗谷 선생은 전 朝鮮사람으로 하야금 모다 聖人으로 同化하시랴고 무한히 노력 하섰음니다. 그러나 당시에 모든 사람들은 聖人에 학문이 日用動靜하는 사이에 있지 아니하고 무슨 玄妙奇效한 高遠難行에 있는 것 같이<100> 馳心妄思함을 보시고 더욱 애석하기 마지 아니 하섰음니다. 이것은 栗谷 당시에 大衆많이 그렇게 생각한 것이 아니라 율곡 선생 前 시대 또는 栗谷 선생 후 시대에 일반 대중이 또한 同然한 馳心妄思로 자기들에 聖的 학문를 日用動靜하는 사이에서 찻지 아니하고 가장 高遠深邁한 곳에 馳心之思하야 결국 自景自棄에 무서운 墮落을 보왔으며 또는 보는 것임니다.
栗谷 선생께서는 당신에 과거는 하는 수 없스려니와 당신에 현재와 당신에 미래에는 반다시 그 허다한 대중에 自暴自棄에 桎梏를 解說식켜 聖人으로 一是同歸 하시랴는 영원불멸에 大理想을 煥發하사 立朝하시거나 在野하시거나 惟惟丹丹 一心이 君國大衆를 통하야 聖賢同歸를 당신에 이상으로 당신에 實行으로 무한히 노력하섰음니다.
41세 되시는 해에 黃海道 海州 石潭九曲에 精舍를 落成하시고 全國民 聖化運動에 萬古不易의 宣傳文를 究成하시니 42세 되시든 丁丑年에 發布하신 擊蒙要訣이 이것이였음니다. 이 擊蒙要訣은 第1은 立志-오 第2는 革舊習이오 第3三은 持身이오 第4는 讀書-오 第5는 事親이오 第6은 喪制오 第7은 祭禮-오 第8은 居家오 第9는 接人이오 第10은 處世로 凡10章으로 되어 있음니다. 聖人이 별 것이 아니라 이 10草를 완수하면 聖人이 되는 것임니다. 聖人이라 하는 것이 日用事物에 사이에서 이루는 것이지 무슨 玄妙高遠한 곳에서 이루는 것이 아임니다. 지금 선생께서 大衆聖化에 宣言하신 것을 擧述하야 明證하기로 하겠음니다.
擊蒙要訣 立志章 第1에 보면
처음 배호는 이는 먼저 모름즉이 뜻을 세워가지고 반다시 써 聖人이 되기를 自期하라. 그리하야 一毫라도 자기를 축소하거나 退托하는 念思를 두지마라. 대저 뭇 사람과 더부려 聖人이 그 本性이 한가지로서 조금도 다름없다. 비록 기운과 바탕에 있어서 혹 淸濁과 粹駁에 다름이 없을 수 없으나 진실노 능히 참으로 실천할 줄 알아 그 舊染을 바리고 그 性初 즉 하날에서 稟賦된 稟性에 처음를 회복한則 毫末도 더함이 없이 萬善이 卽 일만 가지 착함이 是足할 것이다. 衆人이 무었었어하야 자기들이 다-聖人되기를 自期치 아니할 것니냐. 그런 고로 孟子께서 性善를 일카라 말삼마다 堯舜을 반다시 일크르사써 實證하야 가라사되 사람이 다-可히씨 堯舜이 될 수 있다 하섰다. 이 말삼이 었지 나를 속히신 말삼일 것이냐.
그러니 大衆아 이렇게 한라. 맛당히 항상 스사로<101> 분발하야 가로되 사람에 성품이 본래 착하서 古今知慧에 殊別이 없거늘 聖人은 무슨 연고로 홀노 聖人이 되었으니 우리는 홀노 무슨 연고로 人이 되었는가 이는 진실로 참 우리에 뜻이 서지 못하고 우리에 알미 밝지 못하며 우리에 행실이 돈독치 못한 까닭에 연유된 것이다. 내 뜻이 서고 내 알미 밝고 내 행실이 敦篤함이 다-나에게 있는 것이다. 이것을 내에 求하지 아니하고 내가 또 무었에 어듸에 구할 것이냐. 顔淵이 가로대 舜임금은 었던 사람이며 나는 었더한 사람이냐 하였으니 이 顔淵이 舜임금을 바라든 마음과 정신를 내 範바다 法하야 기어히 聖人이 꼭 되겟다 하여라. 그리하야 이 뜻을 견고히 하야 惟進不退하면 日用動靜하는 사이에에서 成聖하는 것이지 무슨 高遠玄妙한 곳에서 聖人 오는 것이 아니라는 作聖精神에 誓詞를 선포하신 것이였섰음니다.

君民一致 崇儉主義

개인이나 人君이나 그 집안을 그 나라를 創業 守成 繁榮함에 있어서는 다-같이 崇儉에 美德를 근본정신으로 하지 아니하고서는 維持安保되지 못하는 것임니다. 다시 말하면 勤儉은 萬種에 번영를 齋來하는 것이오 奢侈는 百狀에 멸망를 초래하는 것임니다. 예나 지금이나 從儉入奢는 易하고 從奢入儉은 難한 것임니다. 그런데 栗谷시대에 풍속이 너무나 栗谷 선생에 聖眼에 奢靡侈極하게 빛치여 이 풍습를 개량하시랴고 萬言對事에 사회개량주의를 鼓唱하야 君民一致하야 去奢崇儉하여야 사회의 惡化와 국가의 멸망을 구제할 수 있다 하섰음니다. 즉 所謂 崇與儉하야 奢侈의 風를 개혁한다 함은 民窮財盡이 今日에서 심한 적이 없음니다. 聖王祖宗에 崇儉의 美德를 法하사 貢物를 減하시고 量入爲出을 節操있게 하서야 함니다. 만약 祖宗聖法를 法效치 아니하시면 量入爲出이 맛지 못하와 마치 方底圓蓋가 맛지 못하는 것같이 될 것 임니다. 또 貢物進上其他誅求에 우는 대중의 생활은 도저히 형언할 수 없는 오날 中層 이상의 생활풍속을 보오면 窮奢極侈를 猶日不足하야 먹는 것은 充腹를 위하는 것이 아니라 珍需盛饌를 서로 자랑하며 입는 옷은 몸을 가려 그 相當한 儀式를 위하야 하는 것이 아니라 燦燦衣服을 서로 경쟁하야 1卓에 비용이 可히써 飢者에 數月 糧食를 지나고 一襲에 비용이 可히써 寒者에 10人에 의복에 지내니 10人에 畊田이 1人의 食에 부족하고 10人에 織布가 1人의 衣에 不足하니 畊織하는 자는 적고<102> 衣食하는 자는 많으면 이 결과가 어떻게 되겠음있가. 백성이 쥬리고 춥지 아니할 수 있음잇가. 古人의 말에 사치의 害가 天災보다 심하다 함은 과연 진리에 말임니다. 만약 대왕께서 스사로 먼저 節操를 힘써 이 禍患을 救치 아니하시면 형법이 아모리 엄하고 호령이 아모리 자저도 徒勞 무익한 일임니다. 臣이 일즉 故志에 言를 듯자오니 成宗 大臣께서 병환이 심중하서서 大臣이 入問한즉 大內에 누어 게신데 덮흐신 다갈색의 明紬 이불이 거진 해진 것이였섰는데 박구어 덮지 아니 하섰다 하야 듯는자-지금까지 欽想하야마지 아니하는 것임니다. 원컨댄 전하는 祖宗을 法效하사 宮中用度를 일체 절약하사 中外에 모범를 보아사써 민간에 侈習을 개혁케 하야 사람으로 하야금 珍需盛찬과 燦燦衣服을 부끄러하도록 되게 敎化하소서. 그리하야 사람사람의 액길줄 알아 써 民力이 暢舒케 되도록 引導하시옵소서 하섰음니다.

栗谷 선생의 시대에 그 奢靡한 광경를 보시고 聖眼에 눈물이 盪盪하섰을 것임니다. 이 萬言封事는 宣祖大王께서 올닌 上䟽이지만 封事에 元 근본정신은 영원히 富貴騰慢한 무리에 대하야 警戒하신 聖訓으로 採用함에 있어 더욱 封事에 意義가 闡明되는 것임니다.

栗谷 선생께서는 무엇보다 兒童敎育을 절대로 중시하섰읍니다. 장래에 사회국가에 담당자인 第2 國民인 까닭이였읍니다. 宣祖大王께 올니신 辭職封事 가운데에 보면

대저 이 세상에서 理致니 氣運이니 하고 학자들 사이에서 論難하는 것이 무엇이냐. 근래에 趙整菴 李退溪 徐花潭 3先生의 說를 보건댄 趙整菴에 말삼이 그 중 놉다. 선생은 분명히 理氣에 전체를 다 보섰다 할 수 있다. 그러나 조금 未盡한 곳이 있고 다음은 退溪 선생에 說이다. 선생께서도 물론 전체를 다 아신 말삼이나 朱子에 학설을 衣樣하야 理致가 發하면 氣運이 도라온다 하였으니 朱子에 학설을 너무 밋는 데서 나온 病痛이라 하겠고 그 다음은 花潭 선생으로서 이 분은 一氣長存說를 주장햇는데 즉 氣運에 한줄기가 永遠無窮하게 이 우주 사이에 있어서 과거로 지나감이 없고 미래로서 계속하여 오는 것이 아니라 하였다. 이 論法은 花潭에 독특한 創說노서 現今 학자들노서는 抗敵할 수 없는 論이였으나  이것은 理致를 말함에 있어서 病痛이 있는 말노서 으즉 退溪 선생께서 그 病痛을 간파해서 뒤에 오는 학자들을 救하섰다 하겠다. 대개 退溪 선생은 朱子에 학설에 衣樣한 맛이 만아 근신함이 많고 花潭 선생은 자기 주장에 맛이 만아 그 말에 樂觀이 만았었다. 그리하야 退溪 선생은 말삼에 過失이 적고 花潭 선생은 말삼에 過失이 점 만치 아니한가 한다. 차라리 退溪에 謹愼主義를 取할지언정 花潭에 樂觀主義는 번바들 필요를 인정치 아니하는 바이다.
요컨댄 이 세 선생의 말에 다 一短一長이 있어 완전한 理氣論이라고 나는 허락할 수 없다. 釋迦의 말에 금가루가 비록 귀한 것이나 이것이 눈에 떠러지면 눈이 보이지 안는다는 말과 같이 聖賢에 말삼이 비록 귀하나 잘못 보면 도리혀 害가 되는 것이다. 朱子에 理氣學說은 결코 二元主義가 아니다. 그러므로 나는 이 理氣學說에 대하야 나의 생각한 바를 가저 세 선생에 학설를 訂正하여볼가 하는 바이다. 이렇게 栗谷 선생은 말심하섰읍니다.
그리고 원래에 이치라 하는 것은 기운에 主宰-오 氣運이라 하는 것은 理致에 所乘이다. 즉 이치가 아니면 기운이 根抵할 바가 없고 기운이 아닌즉 理致가 依著할 곳이 없다. 그러므로 理致와 氣運을 갈나서 두 가지라 할 수 없고 또한 한 가지라고 말할 수 없는 것이다. 다시 말하면 한 가지가 안임으로 하나로서 둘이 되며 두 가지가 안임으로 둘노서 하나가 되는 것이다.
