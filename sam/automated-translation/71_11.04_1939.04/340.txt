加藤淸正(2)
						張赫宙 (Chang Hyŏkchu)
II. The Battle of Gyeongju (Kyŏngju) State

At dawn the next morning, the entire army, led by Katō Kiyobē and Shōrin Hayato, began advancing towards the streets of Tongnae. Banners fluttered from spear tips, the armor of the samurai glittered gold and peach, foot soldiers (足輕級) wearing only helmets stood at the front of the force, and soldiers in leather armor (皮具足) followed in the rear. Slightly behind the vanguard, the Chief Accountant (主計頭) Kiyomasa, wearing a silver helmet and a black leather cuirass (胴丸) dotted with gold front and back, bearing the great sword Ōgikukō, sat high upon his famed horse Hauzuki-ge, displaying his majesty to the entire army. Before his horse, the banner of Myōhō (妙法 - Lotus Sutra) fluttered in the blowing wind. Flanking him were two pages (小姓) and two mace bearers (鎚持), with Hyōbu-dayū and Matazō following slightly behind. Kiyomasa's force of 10,000 led the way, Nabeshima's force of 12,000 formed the center, and Sagara's force of 800 guarded Kiyomasa. They were able to enter Tongnae Fortress (도구네기) within a few hours, but inside this fortress too, the inhabitants had all fled the disaster, and no people could be seen.

"Ah, this is tough without horses. Shouldn't we try to find some horses somewhere?" one of the soldiers said to his similarly unfortunate companions.
"Can't be helped. Let's go requisition some horses." <287>
They kicked down the doors of private houses and searched, but houses with stables were rare. Even if there was a cowshed, there were no horses, only cows that tried to charge the soldiers upon seeing them. The soldiers ran here and there along the narrow paths but could find no horses.
"Can't find any."
"Couldn't find any anywhere."
The soldiers said this, growing anxious as the departure time drew near.
"Whatever happens, happens. Let's just ride the cows," one soldier said and burst out laughing.
"Shall we try? It's not like we don't need them until we march," other soldiers laughed and agreed.
"Alright then, let's ride them." Having decided, they each ran off to houses where they remembered seeing cows. The cows, seemingly crazed once their halters were untied, tried to charge. Some went into the yard on their own to find grain. When the soldiers gave them feed and water, the cows ate ravenously. Once their bellies were full, the cows finally obeyed the soldiers.

When the soldiers rode the cows into the camp, the other soldiers laughed at the strange sight. As the march was fast, those riding cows were inevitably slower.
"Ha, this is really strange! I've never heard of going to battle riding cows. Are you planning to tie torches to their horns and send them into the enemy camp?" a cavalryman teased the cow-riding soldiers.
"Of course! You must be really frustrated that your horses don't have horns," the cow-riding soldiers retorted.
"Hahahahaha! Truly frustrating, but since you're riding cows, you can't be called cavalrymen."
"That's right. You guys are cow-cavalry (騎牛兵)! Since you rode cows!"
Laughter erupted among the soldiers. The cow-cavalry soldiers laughed along with them.

After leaving Tongnae Fortress, an old man of about sixty waited by a private house for the troops to approach.
The vanguard soldiers, suspecting him to be a Korean (高麗) scout, ran up to him, but the old man, dressed entirely in white Korean clothing (高麗服), bowed to the soldiers.
"Who are you?" asked one of Katō Kiyobē's men.
"Yes, I am!" the old man began in Japanese (大和語). <288>
"I am originally an inhabitant of Hirado (平戶), and my name is Tokugorō (德五郞)," he said.
"What? A person from Hirado?"
"Yes, yes. Why would I lie? There's no reason to. Exactly 35 years ago, I went out fishing, encountered a storm, and after several days adrift, the place I washed ashore was right here on the east coast of Korea..."
Tears were already flowing from the eyes of the old man named Tokugorō.
"So, what is your business?"
"Yes. Hearing the rumor that the Japanese army (大和軍勢) had invaded, I was overcome with such longing that I ran through the night just to meet my compatriots from my homeland, even if only for a moment."
"Ah, is that so. Truly pitiful," the soldier said, then added,
"But our army is in a hurry, so we don't have time to talk with you," and began to march on.
"No, please wait just a moment. I have one request," Tokugorō shouted, following the soldiers.
"What is it?" Kiyobē asked his soldiers. A soldier ran back and relayed Tokugorō's story.
"Ask him what his request is." When the soldier asked Tokugorō, he replied that he wanted to at least guide them along the road to repay his debt to his homeland. Kiyobē reported this to the Chief Accountant (Kiyomasa). Permission was granted, and Tokugorō marched with the vanguard soldiers. The road was generally narrow, cutting through mountains and crossing plains. The mountains were quite high, but not ruggedly steep, extending grandly in smooth curves. Low hills slept peacefully on the plains like cows. Entering a forest through a valley, the sky became invisible due to centuries-old pines. In the fields, barley showed its green ears, rippling like silver waves in the blowing wind. However, neither in the fields nor in the villages could a shadow of a person be found. The soldiers, feeling oppressed, began to sing. Having landed but not yet fought a single battle, the soldiers felt as if they had entered a primitive land as they walked through this unpopulated territory. They had come to the Korea they had wanted to see, but they were disappointed not to witness the cultural prosperity they had always heard about. Stone Buddhas and stone pagodas were visible along the roadside and in the valleys.
Reaching a point where a temple was visible on a distant mountain slope, they turned their gaze to the right and saw a large town that seemed to have several thousand households. The soldiers prepared their weapons and tensed, expecting a battle, but this place was Yangsan (야구상) Fortress, which had already been captured by Konishi's forces. Realizing this, the soldiers were disheartened. Tokugorō told the soldiers what he knew about the temple visible on the right mountain slope. <289> When the soldiers learned that the temple was T'ongdosa (通度寺), one of the three main temples of the Kingdom of Korea (高麗國), they went to see it under orders not to loot treasures from the temple. The soldiers knew well that Kiyomasa, a devout follower of the Nichiren sect (日蓮宗), tried to avoid attacking temples as much as possible.
The temple consisted of numerous pavilions and halls; the entire mountain was filled with stone pagodas and gilt-bronze Buddha statues. Buddhist halls and hermitages of various sizes and colors were competitively built beneath cliffs, in valleys, and on high platforms. The soldiers knelt before the brilliantly golden Sakyamuni Buddha statue in the Main Hall (大雄殿) and performed Gassho (合掌 - palms together) worship. However, they discovered traces that several temple buildings had been burned in the war and that treasures inside the halls were missing.
Konishi Yukinaga (行長), being a Christian, likely considered Buddhist temples enemies and enjoyed destroying and looting them. Only a few elderly monks remained in the monks' quarters (僧庵). The monks sat with closed eyes in meditation, seemingly ignoring the soldiers. When the soldiers reported the state of the temple after leaving, Kiyomasa showed a look of disapproval towards Konishi's anti-Buddhist actions. He urged his entire army forward, ordering a rapid advance.
Entering the fortress town, they saw traces that the residents here had already suffered harm. They encountered fleeing residents carrying bundles, leading children and the elderly by the hand. Kiyomasa ordered his interpreter (通辭) to issue a proclamation promising protection to the residents, but the refugees fled even more frantically, stumbling as they went. The soldiers rested briefly and then continued their march. The day was drawing to a close; although they were soldiers accustomed to battle, marching endlessly through uninhabited territory was extremely tedious, and they began to feel considerable fatigue.
Passing Yangsan and advancing several ri (里 - leagues), they encountered peasants leisurely riding cows, perhaps because news of the war had not reached this area. Upon reaching Hwangsan (黃山), the army decided to rest there for the night. The next day, the 19th, the army fought its first battle, lasting several hours. However, the fortress fell without much resistance. The following day, the march continued again. The soldiers found the sight of peasants riding cows peculiar. Some peasants were frightened and fled, while others stared curiously at the gleaming peach and gold armor and banners. Seeing a fleeing peasant strike a cow's rump, the soldiers laughed.
"Hey, look! There's one of your kind, you cow-riders! That's what you'll look like when you flee. Take a good look!"
Seeing this, a cavalryman made this taunt.
"What do you mean! You're the ones who'll flee!" A cow-rider drew his sword. The military column fell into disarray. Seeing the commotion escalate, Shōrin came over, asking, "What's going on, what's going on?" and called the two men out. After hearing the story, he reprimanded the cavalryman and ordered him to apologize to the cow-soldiers. Thus, the disturbance ended, and the army resumed its march. The soldiers felt relieved for a moment from the tedium due to the commotion, <290> but less than half an hour later, under the warm sun, they even began to feel drowsy.
Kiyomasa, ignoring the soldiers' disturbance, remained silent throughout, only gazing ahead. Even when the march didn't proceed quickly, he didn't impatiently rebuke his officers and men. He had wanted to march straight on without a moment's rest from the night he landed in Pusan. However, he had refrained, wanting to prepare countermeasures thoroughly for everything. At the time of the Korean expedition (高麗 出陣), the Taikō's (太閤 - Hideyoshi) order was for Konishi and himself to alternate as the vanguard, but Konishi, from the time they arrived at Iki (一岐), had been looking for an opportunity to go ahead of him. That's why Konishi ultimately left him behind and went alone. Disobeying the Taikō's command to advance while waiting for the rear guard, wasn't Konishi just marching steadily towards the Royal Capital (王城)? Whether it was secretly setting sail in the middle of the night despite such rough seas at P'ungbŏn (風本?) or his actions after landing, Konishi's ambition was as clear as day (觀火自若). Now that he understood Konishi's true intentions, he must not yield even an inch to Konishi. He recalled the past Amakusa Rebellion (天草亂). The principal figure in that rebellion was Konishi. However, the military merit had gone instead to Kiyomasa, who had gone as reinforcements. Konishi's jealousy towards him must have started back then. Konishi undoubtedly intended to avenge the shame of Amakusa in this Korean campaign. Therefore, Konishi planned to seize all the military glory for himself before Kiyomasa could. Konishi was originally a merchant. Observing his actions in every matter, he seemed like a cunning merchant. Furthermore, regarding this Korean campaign, Konishi had the geographical advantage. Konishi, having visited China (支那), Southeast Asia (南蠻), and even this Korea during his merchant days, would be familiar with the geography, but he also had connections through marriage with the Sō family of Tsushima (對島) and ties with the Ukita family (宇喜多家). They were all well-versed in the geography of this country. Besides them, many of his subordinates were, so to speak, Konishi's faction. But I have sincerity and sufficient military strength. The delay of these past few days couldn't be helped, but from now on, I will not yield future military glory to Konishi. Until I am the first to strike the Royal Capital, I will not shrink from any hardship or fierce battle, no matter what. Kiyomasa fiercely commanded himself thus. When he arrived at Pusan Harbor (釜山浦) and heard the news that Konishi had already captured not only Pusan Fortress and Tongnae Fortress but also neighboring fortresses, he naturally became angry and even considered landing at Ungch'ŏn (熊川) on the outer sea rather than Pusan where Konishi had landed. He intended to attack Ungch'ŏn and Angol (安骨) Fortress, which Konishi had not yet passed through, and then advance towards the Royal Capital from there. However, he thought that his resentment towards Konishi wouldn't be appeased by such minor matters, and considering this rather childish mischief, he decided to leave the Ungch'ŏn area to the rear guard and take the right path to advance. But neither at Yangsan nor at Ŏnyang (彦陽) could he engage in a proper battle. While reaching the Royal Capital first might be fortunate in this way, the thought of Konishi saying something like, "Didn't you pass even one strong fortress on the way to the Royal Capital?" was vexing. He wished there was at least one decent fortress. He suddenly remembered the man named Tokugorō <291> and called him out.
"They say there's a Royal Capital in Silla (新羅), right?" Kiyomasa asked, still looking straight ahead.
"Yes, there is. If you follow this road, I believe you will undoubtedly reach it," Tokugorō's words were not precise.
"Are you sure?"
"Yes."
"Is that fortress the largest in this region?"
"Yes, I have certainly heard that it is."
"Then, what is the name of that fortress?"
"It's called Kyŏngju (게구슈 / Gyeongju) Fortress."
"How large is its army?"
"Well...!" Tokugorō was at a loss for words. Sweat beaded on his face. If he were in his homeland, he thought, speaking with a feudal lord (城主) like this would be unimaginable even in dreams; feeling overwhelmed, his whole body broke out in sweat.
"You don't know," Kiyomasa said after a pause.
"Is that fortress still far?" he asked.
"No, sir. I think it's about 1 or 2 ri away now, but—"
"Alright then, let the soldiers set out together," Kiyomasa commanded. Led by Tokugorō, several soldiers scouted ahead on the road. Meanwhile, forests and plains stretched endlessly across their path. Entering the forest, they found three stone Buddhas nearby. Reading the inscription, they learned it was a structure from the Silla Dynasty (新羅時代). The army rested briefly in the forest. As evening approached, the scouting soldiers rushed back, reporting that about 2 ri ahead, near a forest and river, several hundred banners and approximately 3,000 troops were waiting. Kiyomasa was pleased. He immediately ordered the advance. Passing through forests and plains, they gradually walked on, and indeed, unfamiliar triangular and square banners were flying. Kiyomasa ordered the entire army to prepare. He assigned the right vanguard (右先手) to Katō Kiyobē and Shōrin Hayato, the left vanguard (左先手) to Katō Uma-no-suke and Kuki Shirobē Hirotaka, placing Nabeshima Kaga Naoshige's forces on the right wing behind them and Sagara Kunai Shōsuke's forces on the left wing. Kiyomasa addressed the entire army, boosting their morale and stating his expectations for each man's meritorious service. Since Gyeongju (게구州) was said to be a Buddhist capital (佛都), <292> he specifically warned them not to damage Buddhist temples or engage in wanton looting. The army charged forward, spreading out from the foot of the reddish hill (赭山) on the right to the plain on the left. Dust clouds soon soared to the heavens. Seeing the army charge towards them, a war cry suddenly erupted from the Gyeongju camp, and the sound of gongs and drums was heard. Kiyomasa's army advanced to the riverbank and faced the Gyeongju army. Kiyomasa had his interpreter convey a message in a loud voice towards the Gyeongju camp:
"Our army, by order of His Highness the Taikō, intends to invade Ming (明). Therefore, your country, as befits a neighboring nation, should open its gates, lend us the road, and act as our guide. This was the promise made between your King and our country some years ago. As long as you do not resist, we will ensure the common people do not suffer the hardships of war." However, the Gyeongju forces replied that without a royal command, they could not open the road, asking why bandits (賊) were invading a neighboring country.

Kiyomasa ordered the vanguard to charge. The sound of gongs and drums rang out clamorously from both sides, and war cries and dust clouds covered the sky.
The Gyeongju forces were commanded by the Prefectural Magistrate (府使) Pyŏn Ŭngsŏng (邊應星) and five other commanders (部將). The vanguard under Katō Kiyobē and Shōrin Hayato took position by the riverbank and fired arrows and guns.
About 5,000 men from the Nabeshima army's vanguard, led by Narizumi Jūemon and Nabeshima Heigorō, crossed the river and charged into the Gyeongju lines at the edge of the pine forest. As Narizumi's troops climbed onto the road, an enemy commander, urging his soldiers on, charged the right flank of Narizumi's force. Under the rain of arrows, Narizumi's troops could not hold and began a retreat.
Seeing this, Uma-no-suke and Kuki charged forward to provide support. However, facing the emboldened Gyeongju forces, Kuki too was gradually pushed back and had no choice but to retreat. It was then that Katō Kiyobē leaped from his horse, raised his cross-shaped spear (十文字の手鎚 - likely a jumonji yari), and charged alone towards the Gyeongju forces. Seeing this, his soldiers followed Kiyobē. Kiyobē used his spear to stab down the enemies who pressed close upon him. Shōrin, watching this scene from afar, mounted his dapple-grey horse (糟毛) and, leading about 20 cavalrymen, charged straight into the enemy lines. Because of this, the Gyeongju forces split into two groups and shot arrows at Shōrin. Shōrin resolved to fight a warrior who was fighting valiantly at the head of the Gyeongju forces. He dashed through the enemy lines, searching for that warrior. The warrior wore armor embroidered with gold, silver, blue, and red. His horsemanship was superb, darting around the battlefield almost too fast to see. Shōrin pushed back the attacking enemies and galloped towards the warrior. His body was pierced by arrows and swords, but paying them little mind, he rode on, chasing the warrior. The warrior, brandishing a large sword, charged towards Shōrin. The two then grappled and fell from their horses. As the two fought fiercely, the 3,000-strong force of Yamaguchi Yosōuemon and Ōki Tosa-no-kami, positioned on a hill slightly behind the front line, <293> plunged into the chaotic battle. Kiyobē also drew his long sword (大長刀) and spurred his horse forward. About a thousand of his personal troops followed their lord. Kiyomasa chased the Gyeongju forces, swinging his long sword left and right.
Shōrin, his right hand seized by the warrior, lost his freedom and desperately tried to avoid the enemy general's sword aimed at his neck. Fortunately, however, the enemy general was stabbed by Shōrin's sword and fell. Seeing this, the morale of the Gyeongju soldiers plummeted. Kiyomasa's opponent had been Prefectural Magistrate Pyŏn Ŭngsŏng's force, but Shōrin and Katō Kiyobē joined the fray there. Sagara, Nabeshima, and Narizumi also continued the melee, and the Gyeongju forces were ultimately forced to begin retreating. Shōrin's vanguard pursued them, raising clouds of dust. When they pursued them to the edge of the village, the Magistrate's force of about 5,000 men finally turned back and fired arrows from the rooftops of houses, treetops, and atop earthen walls. Attacked from both sides of the road, many soldiers fell. The forces of Katō Yozaemon and Yoshimura Kichizaemon, desperate not to fall behind Shōrin's force, charged forward firing their guns. Gunshots and war cries echoed off each other, while dust and smoke obscured the sky. Although the Gyeongju forces retreated, they could not withstand Shōrin's charge and eventually began to scatter in disarray.
When they rushed into the fortress gate, the iron gate was slammed shut. Kiyomasa ordered fire arrows shot into the fortress. Soon, smoke rose from the gate, and flames began to blaze.

Kiyomasa had trees felled and ropes attached to breach the south, west, and east gates. Realizing this, the fortress residents and soldiers all fled towards the north gate. Pyŏn Ŭngsŏng and the other commanders, mounted on swift horses, escaped towards An'gang (安康) before Kiyomasa's forces could catch them. One commander was pursued by Sagara's chief retainer (家老), Indō Hyōbu Shōsuke; they grappled and fell from their horses. That warrior was immensely strong, and Indō found himself pinned underneath, in dire straits. However, Indō fortunately managed to stab the warrior, escaping the crisis. Much of the fortress gate and the lower town (城下) were burned, and the residents all fled towards Mohwaganch'ŏn(?) (毛火幹川) and An'gang. Kiyomasa established his headquarters (本陣) at the foot of Sŏndo Hill (仙桃小麓), inspected his troops, and then regrouped his forces. He ordered the army to rest, had Hyōbu-dayū write down the full account of the Gyeongju fortress capture, and dispatched Shōrin Kibe'emon back to Japan to report to Hideyoshi (秀吉). The next day, it rained. Kiyomasa, accompanied by his generals, made the Tōkyōkan (東京舘) pavilion by the fortress gate his headquarters. He posted notices on the four gates stating: "Our army will not harm non-combatants. Residents may return home with peace of mind." Residents emerged from storehouses and hidden closets (壁藏) to read the notices. The news quickly spread throughout the surrounding area. Women, holding children, even wept with joy. <294> The children were still in a half-dead state. Perhaps it was due to suffocation in narrow chests, but also because the terror of the previous day had chilled them to the bone. The residents who couldn't flee were either peasant soldiers (農軍) or slaves ordered by their masters to defend the houses to the death. The masters had threatened to behead them if they didn't protect the houses. The upper-class people had known of the Japanese army's (大和兵) impending attack since Kiyomasa's forces attacked Ŏnyang Fortress, warned by signal fires (烽火), and had fled to the eastern mountains and distant mountain villages. The residents spent the night half-believing, half-doubting. When soldiers happened to pass by the street corners, they watched intently from hiding. When the soldiers gestured for them to be at ease, they gradually grew accustomed and eventually even came out into the alleys to watch the soldiers. However, they probably couldn't feel completely safe as long as the soldiers wandered the streets speaking in their strange language. The residents asked each other when the soldiers might withdraw. Some said the Japanese army would defend the fortress and not withdraw, while others claimed they wanted to leave but couldn't move because the Silla gods (新羅神神) were collectively enraged. Herdboys stealthily climbed P'ungwhang Hill (風凰?), peered through the trees at the distant camp, and marveled at the various battle flags, banners, and tents.
"Do you know why the Japanese soldiers (倭兵) can't leave the foot of the mountain?" asked a long-braided herdboy.
"I know. It's because those bastards can't move even if they want to advance."
"Why's that?"
"Because General Kim Kyŏngsin (金庚信 - likely a confusion/corruption of 金庾信 Kim Yusin) is angry."
"Hmm! Maybe so. They say General Kim Kyŏngsin is still alive."
"Of course. They say he often comes out of his tomb and wanders this whole area."
"Is that really true?"
"Anyway, since he was the general who pacified Paekche (百濟) and Koguryŏ (高麗 - here likely meaning Koguryŏ/Silla context, not the Goryeo dynasty), his spirit must still be alive."
"So he hates the Japanese army and is keeping them from leaving?"
"Of course. All their horseshoes are stuck fast to the ground, so they can't take a single step."
"That's why Kiyomasa holds rites morning and evening at the general's tomb, trying to appease his anger."
"Hahaha! Those crazy fools! Why did they have to set up camp right next to the general's tomb?"
"It's a sign they're doomed." The herdboys laughed cheerfully. But realizing their laughter had become loud, they quickly scanned their surroundings. <295> Checking if any soldiers were nearby, they slipped stealthily among the trees. This place was an egg-shaped hill (卵形) about 40 or 50 ch'ŏk (尺 - feet) high. It was said to be a structure built during the Silla Dynasty, the tomb of a meritorious official from that time. There were many such mounds (台) below the fortress; those without trees were all royal tombs (王陵) or tombs whose occupants' names had been passed down. The tomb of Kim Yusin (金庾信) was in the forest to the right of the Japanese camp (大和陣). The general was a man employed during the reigns of two Silla kings, Munmu (文武) and Muyeol (武烈), in the mid-Silla period, who unified the peninsula by defeating the kingdoms to the west and north. Though a thousand years had passed, various legends persisted among the residents to this day, claiming the general's spirit was still alive. However, no matter what miraculous powers the general's spirit might possess for the residents, the soldiers visible before them were a more terrifying presence. The soldiers wandered through the villages seeking provisions. Several soldiers went from the South Gate towards the Ulsan (蔚山) road. They had heard there was a large village near the foot of Namsan (南山). Passing the Twin Phoenix Mound (雙兒鳳凰台) and heading south from where King Mich'u's Tomb (味趨王陵) was located, they reached a village. On a table (床), there was pork (豚肉), grilled fish (燒魚), and an earthenware bottle (土甁) containing liquor. The soldiers discussed whether it was safe to eat. They thought it might contain poison.
"What, it's fine. Eat up," one soldier said, picking up a side dish and putting it in his mouth.
"It's delicious. You guys try some too." An old man poured liquor, urging the soldiers, "Lots, lots." "What? Lots?" a soldier asked, mimicking the old man, and the old man kept telling them to eat. The soldiers ate cheerfully. Hearing the laughter, young men and women gradually emerged from the doorways. The young people came closer, saw the soldiers' smiling faces, and laughed themselves. After eating, the soldiers gestured that they had come to borrow rice. Unable to communicate the meaning, they resorted to writing (筆談). The old man read it, then with a troubled expression, consulted with the young people of the village gathered there. He seemed to command them sternly and then coax them. After a short while, the old man gave some hemp (?) (삼) to the young men and told them to follow the soldiers. The soldiers followed the old man and 3 or 4 young men. Earthen walls (土塀), built regularly with roof tiles and stones, stood high like fortress walls on both sides. Beyond the walls, magnificent tiled houses (기와집) were visible. At the four corners, turtle-shell pattern (龜甲型) carvings and animal figurines (燒物) fascinated the soldiers with their exoticism. The soldiers were guided to a large tiled house, marveling at the unfamiliar storm(?) style (폭풍?) architecture. The old man watched the soldiers with an anxious face. The soldiers counted on their fingers, asking for about ten som (섬 - sacks) of rice. The old man nodded as if he had no choice and had the young men bring out the rice sacks. The soldiers asked the young men if they could carry it to the fortress. The young men were reluctant, but one of them, wearing a white headcloth (白頭巾), nodded vigorously, saying, "Don't worry. We'll take it." He said something to the others, and they each brought carrying frames (지게). They followed behind the soldiers. <296> Many soldiers who hadn't gone out to the villages wandered around the fortress. Wooden pagodas (木塔) and stone Buddhas were everywhere. Smoke still billowed from the burned areas. Soldiers examined corpses as they walked, then entered empty houses and peered into chests (櫃). Kiyomasa showed some satisfaction with this initial military success. One point of dissatisfaction was that the fortress had fallen too easily. The Gyeongju soldiers (게구바兵 - Gyeongju soldiers?) were unexpectedly weak. The soldiers, who had been surprised by Konishi's rapid advance, now seemed to understand the reality. It wasn't that Konishi was strong; the enemy was too weak. The Korean army (高麗軍) was more of a disorganized mob (烏合之衆) than a proper army. They thought they were no match for the battle-experienced Japanese army (大和 軍勢). However, wondering what kind of strong fortress might lie on the road to the Royal Capital, he diligently reviewed the merits of his officers and men and paid attention to future military preparations. After finishing the records of the military merits with Hyōbu-dayū and others, Matazō came to the tent.
"A resident of the fortress has come seeking an audience," he reported.
"A resident?" Kiyomasa looked back at Hyōbu-dayū and smiled, saying that issuing the proclamation had turned out well.
"Show him in." Feeling goodwill towards the Koreans he was seeing for the first time, his spirits lifted. He ordered the room cleared and guest seating prepared. The man who entered the tent had the air of an elder (長者風), with white hair, close to sixty years old. Two young men followed behind him. The old man looked towards Kiyomasa and Hyōbu-dayū, seemingly searching for Kiyomasa. Kiyomasa stood up and bowed his head slightly (目禮) to the old man. The old man came before Kiyomasa, clasped his hands together (雙手), and bowed respectfully. The old man and the young men all wore broad-brimmed, parchment-colored hats (冠) and flowing yellow robes (袍衣) tied with blue silk cords (靑絹細). Round jade ornaments (玉) gleamed on both sides of the old man's head-net (網巾). The old man tried hard to maintain a solemn expression. However, because of this effort, his face took on a rather tragic look. He seemed to have come prepared for death. The two attendants were terrified, trembling at Kiyomasa and his men's every move. Once seated opposite Kiyomasa, the old man received paper and brush (紙筆) from an attendant and wrote a message to Kiyomasa. Kiyomasa had Hyōbu-dayū write a reply.
"Which of you is General Katō Kiyomasa?" the old man first asked this way.
"That would be me," Kiyomasa replied, trying to be as polite as possible.
"The general's bearing is like that of King Xiang of Chu (楚王), truly a hero; how could the four classes of people (四民) not fear him? However, in the general's eyes, there is a hint of mercy (慈氣), so I believe you are not merely a monstrous general (怪將)."
"Thank you. I have long believed in Buddhism (信佛) and trust that I possess a Buddha's heart (佛心). Why would I harm innocent people (無辜之民)?"
"Hearing the general's words, I feel relieved." The old man stroked his beard and laughed, "Haha." <297>
"You have the air of an elder. Are you perhaps an official (官人)?" The old man waved his hand, saying, "Not at all."
"I am a resident below the fortress, just an obscure Confucian scholar (儒生). Seeing the proclamation below the fortress, I have come with a request (所願)."
"What is your request?"
"Currently, the fortress residents are suffering hardship. They have no homes to live in, no food to eat, and they all wish to return to the fortress."
"Excellent. I have been hoping for that for some time."
"However, the residents fear the general as they would a tiger. Until the general leaves the fortress, they cannot return with peace of mind. I request that you relinquish the fortress and continue your advance." Kiyomasa lifted his face and laughed heartily (哄笑).
"Very well. Our advance is urgent, so we plan to depart the day after tomorrow, on the 23rd."
"The general's kindness is as vast as Mount Tai (泰山)." The old man turned to his attendants and sighed in relief. Kiyomasa mentioned leaving a small number of troops in the inner fortress (內城) of Gyeongju for rear communications for the Japanese army and spoke about the surrender (歸順) within the fortress.
"Your country's troops were surprisingly weak. What was the reason?"
"Indeed, you are right. Our country's politics are currently in turmoil, and the people have become effete (文弱). The royal court (朝廷) is plagued by factional strife (黨爭) and struggles for the throne; treacherous ministers (亂臣) run rampant. The people (人民) have even sunk into drunkenness and luxury (酒奢). Truly, the end result is lamentable. Under the pretext of military preparations for the Japanese invasion (倭軍 來襲), they absorbed the people's wealth (民財) and forced hard labor (苦役), so the people's resentment reached the heavens. How could they possibly win a battle?" The old man closed his eyes sadly.
"The people's situation is truly pitiful. I make protecting the populace (護民) my military rule, so please rest assured."
"The general's heart is like that of the Buddha. I shall convey this to the people." The old man took up his brush again.
"I wish to offer hospitality to the general and his commanders. If you would honor us by attending even a humble feast (粗饗), I would be grateful."
"Thank you," Kiyomasa replied with a pleased look.
That night, Kiyomasa, accompanied by Nabeshima, Sagara, and their respective commanders, attended the lavish banquet (富席). The venue was the main hall (大廳房) of the official guesthouse, Tōkyōkan. Kiyomasa sat at the head of the table (上座), surrounded by the old man, the young men, and several other Gyeongju people. Beside him sat Hyōbu-dayū and Katō Kiyobē, with kisaeng (기생 - female entertainers) interspersed among them. The kisaeng were all official entertainers (官妓). They wore pink upper garments (상의 - jeogori?) and long blue skirts (청치마) that trailed as they walked. Their hair shone blackly, pinned up (쪽진 머리), with long gold and silver hairpins (비녀) stuck prominently in place. The generals and soldiers (將土) became drunk. Their bodies and minds, weary from battle, grew pleasantly intoxicated. <298>
"Is Kangnyŏgae (姜女皆?) your esteemed daughter (息女)?" Hyōbu-dayū drunkenly asked the old man.
"No, she is an official entertainer," the old man and Hyōbu-dayū burst into laughter.
"But could you perhaps introduce us?"
A young man sitting next to Hyōbu-dayū secretly passed a slip of paper without the old man noticing.
"Of course, I can introduce you. I can even arrange a tryst (다-래두)," the young man replied in writing. Only after seeing the reply did they relax and laugh loudly. The young men spoke quietly among themselves in their own language.
"The Japanese (倭人) are catching on to our scheme (作亂 - mischief/plot)."
"Well, they're human too, so how could their feelings be different?"
"Exactly my thought. Kiyomasa also has the bearing of a great man (大人)."
"Moreover, we seem to share exactly the same feelings."
"But their writing (文章) is poor. Hyōbu-dayū's writing is also childish. Shall we propose composing poetry (作詩)?"
"Ah, forget it. What's the point of embarrassing them? Getting them to leave quickly is the best plan (上策)." When a kisaeng began to sing, the soldiers laughed, as it was likely the first time they had heard such music.
No one could understand the kisaeng's melody. They probably found the tempo slow and lacking variation, making it unpleasant. However, when the kisaeng danced, everyone clapped and enjoyed it. They called the flowing, linear movements (線의 躍動) graceful (優美). At the end of the table, soldiers named Hosokawa and Kimura Matazō pulled kisaeng beside them, touching their hands and patting their waists. Both were drunk and overcome by the fragrance (薰香) emanating from the kisaeng. Hosokawa whispered sweet nothings to his kisaeng. The kisaeng understood Hosokawa's intentions but only smiled. While pretending to enjoy herself, she thought resentfully, "If he defiles me, what more can happen than death?" The kisaeng had hated coming to this banquet but had done so following the old man's persuasion to sacrifice themselves for the sake of the townspeople. They had all made their final resolve. Narizumi, seeing the indecent behavior (醜態) of Hosokawa and Kimura, reprimanded them. Their spirits dampened, the two moved away from the kisaeng. The banquet continued until midnight. **Summary**

This excerpt from Chang Hyŏkchu's novel depicts Katō Kiyomasa's army advancing through southeastern Korea during the 1592 invasion. After easily passing through Tongnae and Yangsan (already taken by Konishi Yukinaga), they encounter Korean resistance near Gyeongju. Despite an initial setback, Kiyomasa's forces, particularly led by commanders like Katō Kiyobē and Shōrin Hayato, overwhelm the Korean defenders under Prefectural Magistrate Pyŏn Ŭngsŏng. Gyeongju falls quickly, and Kiyomasa issues a proclamation promising safety to civilians, leading some to return. An elderly Confucian scholar approaches Kiyomasa, pleads for the army's swift departure, and arranges a banquet for the Japanese commanders, attended by kisaeng. The narrative highlights Kiyomasa's ambition, his rivalry with Konishi, the perceived weakness of Korean defenses, the suffering of civilians, and tentative interactions between the occupiers and the occupied. For historians, the piece is interesting as a Korean-authored work from the colonial period depicting Japanese invaders with some nuance (Kiyomasa's complexity vs. Konishi's perceived cruelty, the soldiers' behavior) alongside the expected themes of invasion and Korean suffering. The portrayal of Korean reactions, from flight and fear to the scholar's calculated diplomacy and the kisaengs' reluctant participation, offers insight into narratives circulating during Japanese rule.

二. 게구州의 戰

밝는 날 아츰 未明 全軍은 加藤淸兵衛 庄林阜人을 선두로 도구네기 街頭를 행하야 진군하는 것이였다. 旗幟이 펄펄 槍끝에서 날고 侍級兵士의 갑옷이 금빗과 桃色으로 빛나고 투구만 쓴 足輕級의 병사가 軍勢의 전반에서고 皮具足의 병사는 후반에 따라섰다. 先半軍勢보담 조곰 뒤떠러저서 主計頭淸正은 은색 투구를 쓰고 前後에는 金點이 백힌 거문 가죽 胴丸을 입고 大菊光의 大刀를 차고 名馬羽月毛에 높이 앉어 全軍勢들의게 偉容을 보히고 있었다. 馬前에는 妙法의 旗가 부러오는 바람에 펄펄 날니고 있었으며 그 좌우에는 小姓 두 사람과 鎚持 두 사람이 곁에 서고 兵 太夫와 又藏이 좀 뒤떠러*서 따르고 있었다. 淸正軍勢 1만을 선두로 鍋島軍勢 1만2천을 中頭로 相良軍勢 8백이 淸正을 호위하였었다. 도구네기(東萊城)에는 數刻을 안 지나서 入城할 수 있었으나 이 城內에도 주민들은 모다 難을 피하야 사람들은 볼 수가 없었다.
「말이 없이는 원 고생스러워서 어데 말을 求하여보지 않겠나」 그 중의 한 병사가 같은 不運패에게 이같이 말하었다.
「할 수 없지. 말을 어데 가 좀 徵發해 봄세」<287>
그들은 민가의 문을 차 깨트리고 단니면서 찾어보았으나 馬舍가 있는 집은 稀少하였다. 오양간이 있는 집이 있대도 말은 없고 소가 병사들을 보기만 하면 바들려고 덤비는 것이였다. 병사들은 오솔길을 이리저리 뛰여단였으나 말은 어더볼 수가 없었다.
「구할 수 없어」
「아모 데도 어더볼 수가 없는데」
병사들은 이같이 말하면서 출발시간도 임박하여 와서 모다들 焦燥하였다.
「될 대로 돼라. 소라도 타자」 한 병사가 이렇게 말하고 깔깔 우섰다.
「타 볼까. 행군할 때까지는 안 필요한 것도 아니야」 *른 병사들도 우스면서 동의를 하였다.
「자 그러면 타보게」 그들은 그렇게 결정된즉 각각 생각나는 대로 소 있는 집으로 뛰여갓다. 소는 밋친 듯이 곱삐를 푸러놓은즉 바들여고 덤비였다. 제절노 뜰에 가 곡식을 어더보는 것도 있었다. 병사들이 먹을 것과 물을 준즉 소는 밋친 듯이 막 먹어대였다. 배가 불으니 겨우 소들은 병사들의 말을 들었다.
병사들이 소를 타고 陣으로 들어선즉 다른 병사들은 珍妙한 꼴들이라고 우서대였다. 행군이 빠름으로 소 탄 사람들은 어쨋든 더듸였다.
「야 이것 참 珍奇하구나. 소 타고 出戰한다는 말은 들은 일이 없는데 뿔*다 홰불이라도 달고 敵陣에 내놓을 생각들인가」 騎馬兵이 소 탄 兵을 보고 놀여대였다.
「그렇구 말구. 그대들은 말에 뿔이 없어 참 憤켔구려」 소 탄 병사들이 반박하였다.
「화화화화화화화 참말 憤하*하나 그대들은 소를 탓스니 騎馬兵이라고는 할 수 없어」
「그렇치. 그대들은 騎牛兵이야. 소를 탓스니까!」
병사들 사이에는 우슴이 터저 야단들이였다. 騎牛兵士들도 함께 우서대였다.
도구데기城을 나오자 民家 앞에는 한 사람의 60된 노인이 軍勢들이 가가히 오기를 기다리고 있었다.
先手의 병사들은 高麗側의 偵察인가하야 의심하고 뛰여가 본즉 그 上下 白色의 高麗服 노인은 병사들의게 절하였다.
「웬 사람이야」 加藤淸兵衛軍勢의 한 사람이 물어 보았다.
「네. 저는!」 노인은 大和語로 이렇게 말하면서<288>
「저는 본래 平戶의 주민인데 성명은 德五郞이라고 합니다」라고 말하였다.
「뭘? 平戶 사람이야?」
「네. 네. 웨 거즛말을 하겠읍니가. 있지도 않습니다. 지금부터 바로 35년 전 고기잡이를 나갓다가 풍파를 맛나 數日만에 표류한 데가 바루 이 高麗의 東海岸이였읍니다...」
德五郞이라는 노인의 눈에서는 벌서 눈물이 흘넜었다.
「그래 용건은 무어요」
「네. 風說에 듯건대 大和軍勢가 처들어온 것을 알고 너무도 그리운 나머지에 잠간만이라도 故國의 同胞들을 맛나보고저 밤을 새우면서 뛰여온 길임니다.」
「아 그렀오. 참 가엽구려」라고 병사는 말하고서
「하나 우리들 軍勢는 갈 길이 밥뿌니깐 그대와 이야기할 사이가 없오」라고 행진을 시작하였다.
「아니 잠간만 기다려 주세요. 한 가지 願이 있읍니다」 德五郞은 병사들을 따라가면서 고함처 말하였다.
「웬 일이냐」 淸兵衛가 병사들의게 물었다. 병사들은 뛰여와서 德五郞의 이야기를 하였다.
「무슨 願인지 물어보고 와」 병사가 德五郞의게 물은즉 德五郞은 하다못태 길 안내라도 하여 故國에 恩報하겠다는 것이였다. 淸兵衛는 主計頭에게 보고하였다. 허락이 나서 德五郞은 先手병사들과 進軍하였다. 길은 대개 좁고 산간을 뚫서 평원을 지나였다. 산들은 상당이 고산이었으나 峽險突ꞗ의 정도는 아니고 평평한 곡선으로 거대하게 뻔치고 있었다. 나즌 구릉은 소같이 고요히 평원에 자고 있었다. 谷間을 지나 삼림 가운데로 들어온즉 수백년전 老松으로 하날을 불 수가 없었다. 바테는 보리가 퍼런 이삭을 내들고 부는 바람에 銀線의 물결을 치고 있었다. 하나 바테도 부락에도 사람의 그림자라고는 찾어볼 수가 없었다. 병사들은 답답한즉 노래를 부르기 시작하였다. 上陸하야 아직 한 번도 合戰 못한 병사들은 人影없는 地帶만 거러서 原始國에 오지나 않었는가 하는 착각을 이르키기까지도 하였다. 한 번 와보고 싶던 高麗國에 오기는 왔으나 늘 듯던 文化繁榮을 보지 못하는 것이 섭섭하였다. 路傍과 溪谷에는 石佛 石塔이 보였다.
먼 山*에 寺院이 보이는 데까지 와서 시선을 右便으로 돌닌즉 수천 戶는 됨즉한 大邑이 보였다. 병사들은 合戰을 豫期하고 무기를 준비하고 긴장하였으나 이곳은 야구상(梁山)이란 城인데 여기도 小西軍勢가 벌서 공략한 곳이였다. 병사들은 그것을 알고 落膽하였다. 右便 山*에 보이는 寺院에 대하여 德五郞은 자기가 아는 데까지 병사들께 이야기하여 주었다.<289> 병사들은 그 寺院이 高麗國의 三大本山인 하나의 通度寺인 것을 알자 그들은 寺院에 가서 寶物을 掠奪하여서는 안 된다는 명령하에 가보았다. 日蓮宗信者인 淸正은 寺院을 공략하는 것은 될 수 있는 대로 피할여고 하는 것을 병사들도 잘 알고 있었다.
寺院은 다수의 樓閣과 敎堂으로 되어 全山이 모다 석탑과 금동의 불상으로 찻고 大小色色의 佛殿과 佛庵은 岩下와 谷間과 高臺에 競建하여 있었다. 병사들은 大雄殿의 金色이 찬란한 釋迦如來像 前에 와서 무릅을 꿀고 합장 예배하였다. 하나 佛殿의 幾棟은 兵火에 타고 殿內의 보물이 없어진 자최를 발견하였다.
기독신자인 行長은 佛寺를 도리여 敵으로 생각하고 질겨 破壞하고 掠奪를 하였을 것이였다. 승려는 僧庵에 老僧이 數人이 있을 뿐이였다. 승려들은 瞑目에 잠겨서 병사들을 보는 체도 하지 않었다. 寺院에서 나려서 寺內의 모양을 보고한즉 淸正은 行長의 排佛的 행위를 詰難하는 표정이 보였다. 그는 全軍을 督勵하여 급속한 進軍을 명하였다.
城內에 와본즉 여기도 벌서 주민들은 화를 입은 자최가 보이였다. 피난하는 주민들이 보퉁이를 들고 어린애와 노인들의 손목을 이끌고 도망하는 것을 맛났었다. 淸正은 通辭에게 布命하야 주민의 보호를 언약하는 것이였으나 피난민은 도리여 顚倒하면서까지 도망하였다. 병사들은 잠간 휴식하고 또 행진을 계속하였다. 날은 저무러 싸움에 들어난 병사들이였으나 無人의 지경을 작고 행군하는 데는 너무도 지란하여 적지 않은 피로를 늣기게 되였다.
야구상을 지나서 數里를 進한즉 이 곳은 戰報가 傳치 않었는지 농민이 소를 타고 유유히 단니는 것이였다. 黃山에 도착하였을 때 軍勢들은 밤을 여기서 쉬기로 하였다. 다음 19일에 軍勢들은 처음 數刻의 전투를 하였다. 하나 城은 멋 없이 함락되고 말었다. 다음 날도 또 다시 행군은 계속하였다. 소 탄 백성들을 보고 병정들은 珍奇하게 생각하였다. 백성들은 겁내고 도망하는 것도 있었으나 桃色과 金色으로 번쩍거리는 甲胃와 旗幟을 보고 기이하게 바라보는 것도 있었다. 도망하는 백성이 소 궁뎅이를 때리는 것을 보고 병사들은 우서대였다.
「여보들 소 탄 이 지기 그대들의 한 패가 있구만. 그대들도 도망할 땐 저 꼴이 되겠구만. 잘 보와 두어요」
그것을 보고 한 사람의 騎馬兵이 이같이 揶揄하였다.
멀 어째 도망하는 것은 그대들이야 하고 기마 병사는 *을 빼들었다. 軍列은 흣터젔다. 소동이 크게 된 것을 보고 庄林이가 차저와서 웬일이야 웬일이야라고 두 사람을 불너내였다. 이야기를 듯고 그는 기마병을 叱責하고 牛兵士들께 사과하라고 명하였다. 그리하야 소동은 끈치고 다시 軍勢들은 행진하였다. 병사들은 얼마쯤 소동으로 一時는 답답증은 면하였으나<290> 半시간도 지나서 따뜻한 해볏테는 조을니기까지 하게 되었다.

「과연 올슴니다. 我國은 방금 정치가 문란하고 인민은 文弱하여젔슴니다. 朝廷은 당쟁과 왕위의 쟁탈 亂臣의 跂扈人民은 또 酒奢에까지 빠젔으니 참으로 그 말로가 慨嘆될 일이지요. 倭軍이 來襲한다고 軍備를 구실로 民財를 吸收하며 苦役은 강제하야 인민의 원한은 하날에까지 미첫으니 었지 싸움에 이길 것임니가」 노인은 슲흔 듯이 눈은 감었다.
「인민의 정상이 참 가엽구만. 저는 護民하는 것으로써 軍規를 삼은즉 안심하시요」
「장군의 마음은 佛心과 갔읍니다. 저는 이것을 인민의게 전하겟습다」 노인은 또 다시 붓을 들어
「제가 장군과 部將* 환대코저하니 粗饗의나마 와 주시면 감사하겟슴니다」
「감사합니다」 淸正은 깁븐 얼골노서 대답하였다.
그 날밤에 淸正은 鍋島相良 及 각각의 部將들을 다리고 富席에 참석하였다. 會場은 官家 東京舘의 大廳房이였다. 淸正은 노인과 젊은 사람 외에 여러 제구州 사람들과 한 床에 둘너 않자 上座에 않젔다. 곁데는 兵太夫와 加藤淸兵衛가 않고 그 사이에는 기생이 끼여 안기도 하였다. 기생은 모다 官妓였다. 분홍색의 상의는 쩌르고 청치마는 길어서 끄을고 단였었다. 머리는 거머케 빛나고 쪽진 머리에는 金銀의 비녀가 기루 길게 꼿첫었다. 將土는 취하였다. 戰陣에 피곤한 身心은 기분 좃케 취하여가는 것이였다.<298>
「姜女皆는 貴殿의 息女입니가」 兵太夫는 취하여 노인에게 물었다.
「아니-요. 官妓람니다」노인과 兵太夫는 깔깔 우서대였다.
「하나 소개할 수는 없음니가?」
兵太夫곁에 잇던 젊은 사람이 노인 몰내 가만 수종이 쪼각에다가 보내였다.
「소개하구 말구요. 다-래두 하여 드리지요」 젊은 사람들은 返書를 보고서야 마음올 놓고 크게 우섯다. 젊은 사람들은 자기들 말노 가만가만 말하였다.
「倭人들도 우리들 作亂을 아라채이는데」
「어째든 같은 인간이니깐 인정이 다를 리야 있겠나」
「꼭 동감이야. 淸正이두 大人의 풍격을 가추었는데」
「더구나 우리들은 꼭 같은 인정을 가진 듯한데」
「그러나 문장은 틀였어. 兵太夫의 문장도 유치하구만. 作詩라도 한 번 提議해볼가」
「아 그만 둬. 챙피를 주어서 꼴내게 할건 뭐야. 그보담도 어서 떠나게 하는 것이 상책이지」 기생이 노래를 시작하니 將卒을 처음 보는지라 우서대였다.
