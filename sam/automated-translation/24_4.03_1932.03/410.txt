自叙傳代로
						金一葉

파인 선생―
선생의 請은 좀더 정성스럽게 늣겨저서 좀처럼 거절할 수 업시 생각되엿섯스나 요전 말슴은 엇재 홀녀 듯고 잇섯슴니다.

그런데 來日도 설 모레는 정말 설날이라니 남들 갓치 차리지는 안치만 그래도 무슨 붓침질도 좀 하여야겟고 고깃점이라도 쟁여야겟고 남편의 하다가둔 저고리도 맛처야 겟는데 엇저자고 來日 아츰 차즐 原稿를 지금 기벌함닛가 自叙傳이요. 쓸資格이나 때가 잇는것도 안인지는 모르지만 나의 自叙傳 갓흔 것을 무엇에 쓰게요.

平凡하고 보잘 것 업는 內容밧게 적을것이 업는 것을―남보다 좀 다르다면 5代獨子의 無男獨女로 早失父母 하니만큼 외로온 身勢이오. 離婚과 戀愛사건으로 세상 사람에게 욕설과 批難을 밧은것과 허풍선이의 新女子라는 別名 隨筆 수십편 掌篇이랄지 短篇이랄지의 조각 소설 몃개로 女流文士 라는 일홈을 드럿을뿐 세상에 도음과 흥미를 끌―아모것도 업는 걸이요＜104＞

***

In Lieu of an Autobiography
						Kim Il-yŏp

Teacher P'ain—
Your request (請) felt quite sincere, making it seem difficult to refuse, yet somehow I only casually listened to your previous words.

However, since the day after tomorrow is truly New Year's Day (Seol), although I won't prepare as elaborately as others, I still need to do some frying [of festive foods], stock up on some meat, and finish the *chŏgori* my husband left unfinished. Why on earth are you rushing me now for a manuscript due tomorrow morning? An autobiography (自叙傳), you say? I don't know if I even have the qualifications (資格) or if it's the right time, but what use is something like an autobiography of mine?

There's nothing to write about other than commonplace and insignificant content (內容)—if there's anything different from others, it's that I am the lonely figure (身勢) of an only daughter with no brothers (無男獨女) to an only son for five generations (5代獨子), having lost my parents early (早失父母). Add to that being subjected to insults and criticism (批難) from people due to divorce (離婚) and love affairs (戀愛사건), the nickname (別名) of a "New Woman" (新女子) who is a braggart (허풍선이), several dozen essays (隨筆), and a few pieces of fragmented fiction, whether you call them long short stories (掌篇) or short stories (短篇), which merely earned me the name of a female writer (女流文士). There is nothing that would provide help or draw interest from the world.<104>

**Summary**

Kim Il-yŏp writes informally to someone called "Teacher P'ain," expressing mild exasperation at a last-minute request for her autobiography, especially just before the Lunar New Year holiday (Seol). She claims her life is too ordinary and insignificant for such a piece, listing her unique status as an only daughter from a long line of only sons, her early orphanhood, the public criticism she faced for divorce and love affairs, her reputation as a "New Woman" (though she adds the qualifier "braggart"), and her literary output (essays, short fiction) as the only points of note, ultimately deeming them insufficient to warrant public interest.

For historians, this short piece offers a glimpse into the self-perception of Kim Il-yŏp, a prominent figure often labeled a "New Woman" in colonial Korea. Her candid mention of societal criticism regarding her personal life (divorce, affairs) alongside her literary activities highlights the pressures and scrutiny faced by women who deviated from traditional norms. Her self-deprecating tone, questioning her "qualifications" for an autobiography despite her known public profile, might reflect both genuine modesty and the complex reality of female intellectuals navigating a patriarchal society during that era.
