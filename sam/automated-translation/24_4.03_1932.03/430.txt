First Day of Street Contact
						Song Kye-wŏl

Just now—the siren signaling the lunch break wailed. All the comrades in their factory uniforms (제사복), like pitiful young creatures first released into our cage, were thrilled and jumping about, clutching cold lunch boxes (벤도) from their book bags (책보) to their chests as they flocked to the east-side cafeteria. Until yesterday, I too used to join the group heading to the cafeteria, unconsciously jumping about as if merely filling a hungry stomach was the most important action for us humans. However, at this hour, burdened with a far greater mission, watching the comrades jump about like that, their actions seemed so pitiful and wretched. Realizing this deepened the sense of responsibility for this mission in my heart. Lost in these thoughts, I slipped out in the opposite direction from them, passed through the back gate of the factory section, and came out onto the main street. Once on the main street, I looked back one more time at the factory where I lived. The red chimney, covered in coal ash and black smoke that confess XX’s deeds! The loud sound of machinery echoing from within! To my ears today, everything sounded like the groans of a mother lion nearing death. Immersed in these thoughts, I found myself standing before the Sinseol-ri streetcar stop. Then, I heard the sound “Stop!” I quickly threw myself onto the bus heading for Dongdaemun. Honestly, taking the streetcar would have been safer and more comfortable given my weak disposition, but I took the bus fearing I wouldn't make it in time. Huddled in a corner of the bus, my entire body was paralyzed with a strange excitement, like someone who had lost all judgment and comprehension. I had little desire to look at the people around me or the scenery outside. On—ly occasionally, whenever the bus reached a stop, the female conductor’s (녀차장) calls of “Al—l right” and “Stop-” would reach my ears. And each time, my attention would focus on the female conductor. Working people! Yes! Comrades who are suffering XX everywhere exist. Thinking this, I wanted to run over right then, grab her wrist, and shout “Oh! Tovarishch (다바리싯치)!” sending her my strongest handshake. Just wait and see! The time will surely come soon when they too will understand. No, I felt that we must surely strive to help them rise up—realizing this anew, I felt my heart pounding wildly. While this was happening, the words “Chŏ—ngno!” “This is Chŏngno” reached my ears. Startled by something, I hurriedly stood up and got off in front of the Donga Department Store. Then, wondering if my behavior might have looked strange to those around <110> me, I glanced around once more. My ears and eyes were further sharpened by the bustling clamor of the department store. It seemed new, and even newer still to me, seeing Chŏngno, which usually seemed asleep day and night, now bustling as if unaware of the recession, with a department store having appeared, causing the high-heeled shoes of modern Korean women (조선 신식 녀성), who previously only knew places like Chŏngjaok (정자옥) or Samwŏl Obokchŏ (삼월 오복저), to stop here—a novel sight for me, out seeing the world after a long time. An even rarer scene was the faint voice of a young boy (뽀-이) bowing and saying, “Welcome!” amidst the noblewomen (귀부인) and new women (신녀성) bundled in fur coats entering and exiting the doors; this was exceedingly rare and then some. I turned my steps away from this scene at the department store. My task begins now! The watch on my wrist read twelve thirteen! There were still exactly seven minutes left until the contact time. Wondering if my watch might be wrong, I checked it against the clocks hanging on shop pillars two or three times. Between Chŏngno Intersection—and Hwanggŭmjŏng (黃金町) Intersection! This is our contact point! I have little experience; today is my first. The thought that I must now fulfill my important first mission made my head spin even more—and my heart pound harder—. I tried infinitely hard to keep my composure as I had to walk from Chŏngno Intersection, past the Dong-il Bank (동일 은행), keeping to the left side. And I needed to pay attention—once more—to my clothes and posture. A black durumagi coat and black socks! White rubber shoes! And I must not forget to link my left arm through the front flap of the durumagi. Trying to perform all these actions consciously felt unnatural and forced. Might I look like a strange woman to others? What if Mr. Pi (彼이公) finds out about my actions? I glanced around several times, trying to gauge people's reactions. But the people on the street all seemed busy going their own way. Feeling relieved for the first time, I patted my pounding chest and comforted myself, thinking, “There are no issues on the Chŏngno X line (종로X선).” However, my anxiety did not end there. The instructions emphasized at the cell meeting (분회)! Start walking from Chŏngno towards Hwanggŭmjŏng from about fifteen minutes past the hour, and you should meet the other courier/reporter (레포-터-) roughly halfway. The other courier/reporter—is male! Black fedora hat, black tortoiseshell glasses (대모테 안경), shorter than average height! Yellow shoes, carrying a navy blue book bag (남색 책보) in his left arm, and so on. But I didn’t think this was very certain. Among so many people on this street, how could there be only one person dressed like this! Of course, one cannot say there are no other people with black fedoras, tortoiseshell glasses, yellow shoes (노란구쯔), and navy blue book bags. But the issue lies in the behavior. Thinking this over again, what if I fail to recognize him and walk past! And also, what if he fails to recognize me? I considered this. Wondering if I had perhaps already passed him, I looked back a second and third time, and also paid close attention to the behavior of people coming and going. Was that all? What if he got caught somewhere in between? What if there’s a spy (스파이) at our meeting place? All sorts of fears surged uncontrollably. But I pulled myself together again.—I can't work like this. I tried my best to think determinedly about everything. While walking distractedly like this, I had come quite a distance from Chŏngno Intersection. Regretting having been lost in thought, I hastily looked at my wristwatch. Twenty minutes! The minute hand pointed exactly to twenty. Twenty minutes! Oh! The contact time! But what happened to him? Looking across the street, a man met my gaze! He seemed to recognize me, nodding (목레) as he approached my side. That young man! When I rubbed <111> my eyes and looked again, he was none other than the person I had been searching and wandering for—the black fedora! Tortoiseshell glasses! And yellow shoes, with the navy blue book bag at his side! It was him, without a single discrepancy. Yet even here, I was too inexperienced in everything to immediately offer a comrade's greeting to the other side. Rather than feeling happy that I had met the one I was searching and waiting for and that things were somewhat safe, my heart pounded even harder—as if I was facing some new, great difficulty. However, the comrade in the black hat didn't seem awkward at all! He treated me with a calm and cool demeanor. Smiling slightly on both cheeks as he spoke sometimes, he came closer to me. And when we passed each other! He swiftly pressed a small wad of paper (조희 뭉치) into my hand, saying, “Be careful!” He continued, “It’s better not to go back the way you came. It’s dangerous.” Like someone in a hurry, he quickly crossed the street towards the Spinning Company Office (방적 희사 사무소) on the other side and disappeared into a narrow alleyway over there. Unaware of the danger of my current situation, I stared blankly at his retreating figure. Our warrior (용사) on the front lines. Choking back tears of emotion, I watched his retreating figure and prayed for his well-being (족복). And I resolved that I too must gain valuable experience like today and become a splendid—XX like him. Only eight minutes remained before work started at the factory. Hurriedly following his advice, this time I decided not to go via Chŏngno but to take the bus from Hwanggŭmjŏng. While on the bus—and until I got off the bus and entered the factory gate, my excited heart, my pride in having fulfilled a new mission, churned in my chest even more than before (앗가). This report (이러포) must be taken to the cell meeting (분회) as quickly as possible—that was my only thought. By the time I entered the dormitory (긔숙사) gate, the siren was already wailing. (End) <112>

**Summary**

This short story by Song Kye-wŏl recounts the narrator's first experience acting as a clandestine liaison or courier. Likely a young female factory worker involved in an underground movement (implied socialist/communist by the use of "Tovarishch"), she describes her nervousness, sense of mission, and heightened awareness as she leaves her factory during lunch break, navigates Chŏngno, and successfully meets her contact near Hwanggŭmjŏng to receive a message. The narrative contrasts her newfound political consciousness and the perceived danger of her task with the mundane routines of her fellow workers and the bustling consumerism of the department store, highlighting her feelings of alienation and commitment. Historically, the story is significant for its portrayal of underground resistance or organizational activities during the Japanese colonial period in Korea. It offers a glimpse into the methods (coded descriptions, specific meeting points, warnings about routes), the psychological state of participants (fear, excitement, sense of duty), the class consciousness informing such movements, and the ever-present threat of surveillance ("spy"), all conveyed through a personal, first-person perspective, likely reflecting real experiences or common narratives within such circles, even with censored terms ("XX", "X워야", "종로X선").
