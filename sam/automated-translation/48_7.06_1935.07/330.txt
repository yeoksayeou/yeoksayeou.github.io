Poetic Talents of Women Poets
Hwang Chin'i

The green mountains are my heart; the blue waters are my love's affection.
Though the blue waters may flow away, how could the green mountains change?
The blue waters too, unable to forget the green mountains, weep as they go.

The sun sets behind the western hills, boundless is the world.<193>
The moon shines white on the pear blossoms, thoughts of my love arise anew.
Cuckoo, for whom do you long, crying throughout the night?

Maehwa

Though they say a love seen in dreams is without connection,
When I desperately long for him, if not in dreams, how else could I see him?
Though a dream is but a dream, may it come ever so often.

Spring returns to the old stump of the plum tree (梅花);
It seems likely to bloom on the branches where it once bloomed,
But the spring snow falls like scattered powder, so it may bloom, or it may not.<194>

**Summary**

This article presents four traditional Korean Sijo poems, attributed to the renowned kisaeng poet Hwang Chin'i and another poet, Maehwa. The poems explore classic themes of enduring love compared to unchanging nature, longing under the moonlight, the solace of dreams, and the tentative return of life (symbolized by plum blossoms) in spring. From a historical perspective, the inclusion of these Sijo poems by female poets in a popular Korean magazine during the Japanese colonial period is significant. It demonstrates an effort to preserve and celebrate traditional Korean literary forms and highlight the contributions of women writers within the national culture, potentially as a subtle assertion of Korean identity amidst pressures of assimilation.

黃眞伊

靑山은 내뜻이오 綠水는 님의 情이
綠水 흘너간들 靑山이야 변할소냐
綠水도 靑山을 못이저 우러 예여가는고

서산에 日暮하니 천지에 가이업다<193>
梨花에 月白하니 님생각이 새로워라
杜鵑아 너는 누를그려 밤새도록 우는고

梅花

꿈에 뵈는 님이 인연업다 하건만은
탐탐이 그리울제 꿈아니면 어이하리
꿈이야 꿈이언마는 자로자로 되어라

梅花 녯 등걸에 봄철이 도라오니
녯 피든 가지에 피염즉도 하다마는
春雪이 亂粉粉하니 필동말동 하여라<194>
