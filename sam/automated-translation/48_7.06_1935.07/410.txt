Jeju Island of Myriad Cool Flavors (凉味萬斛의 濟州島)
						By Kwŏn Tŏkkyu (權悳奎)

By a Thousand *Ri* of Waterway

Let us sit apart and think about Jeju Island (濟州島). Jeju Island, as is often said, is a thousand *ri* by land and a thousand *ri* by sea, an island nearly two thousand *ri* from Seoul. It is a large island with a circumference of over four hundred *ri*, surrounded on all four sides by the sea. In the center of the island stands a great guardian mountain called Halla (한라), and its spurs spread out in all directions, forming countless smaller mountains. Human villages are spread along the slopes of all these mountains. The livelihoods of these people follow the terrain: agriculture is predominant in the mountain regions, and fishing predominates along the coast. Also, because there are many hills and plains, animal husbandry is practiced concurrently in both mountain and coastal areas, regardless of location. Thus, the mountains abound with fruit, the fields are productive with grain, and furthermore, all kinds of marine products are inexhaustible. Being islanders with little contact with outsiders, the people might seem unsophisticated (貿貿), but they are likely exceedingly simple and honest (淳淳). Thus, the women are likely chaste and faithful (貞信), and the men diligent workers (力作).

Since Hallasan (한라산) stands in the center and spreads its ridges in all directions, valleys form between the mountains, and through the middle of the valleys flow streams, sometimes becoming waterfalls, sometimes deep clear pools (澄潭), sometimes swift rapids (激湍). They are sometimes sufficient for sightseeing, sometimes suitable for washing one's feet (濯足), sometimes sufficient for irrigation (灌漑), so regarding water, there should be no dissatisfaction whatsoever.<209> Also, the coastline is deeply indented, sometimes suitable for harbors, sometimes appropriate for fishing grounds (漁場), sometimes suitable for boating (船遊), so regarding the sea, there should also be no dissatisfaction. Is that all? As revealed even in Pak Yŏnam's (朴燕岩) *Hŏ Saengjŏn* (許生傳), which could be called a treatise on making wealth (貨殖傳), Jeju Island is the foremost producer of horsehair (말총) in the country. Although in this modern world where shaved heads (중대가리) are common, the demand might have declined somewhat, the fine crafts of mesh hats (網市), brimmed hats (岩市), and women's winter hats (갓냥) are one type of byproduct of horse breeding (牧馬) with substantial exports. Furthermore, speaking of the climate, in spring a hundred flowers bloom profusely, in summer ten thousand trees provide dense shade (陰濃), in autumn the five grains and hundred fruits are yellow and red, and even in winter, camellias, rhododendrons (萬病草), and evergreen trees are lush and green (울울창창), fully exhibiting the warmest temperate climate among temperate zones. Not only is it not cold in winter, but the fact that it is not so hot in summer is due to the accompanying oceanic climate. Indeed, the climate is like that. Although it is now the formidable summer of the three peak heat periods (三伏), I do not feel it is as hot as on the mainland. Since I have never been here in winter, it is difficult to say, but it is said that in the southern part of the island (本島), one can manage without padded clothes even in winter.

However, the Jeju imagined and the Jeju seen upon arrival differ in quite a few aspects. First and foremost, the most surprising thing is that among the four essentials for human life – fish, salt, firewood, and water (魚鹽柴水) – the first is abundant beyond words, but the latter three are extraordinarily scarce. It is said that the forests were once dense, but as a characteristic of a declining nation, the trees have all been cut down, so now, except for the shoulders of Hallasan and the shiitake mushroom farms (椎茸場) managed by Japanese, it is completely bare wilderness (野地). The disappearance of trees is a recent event, and perhaps cultivation (培養) could restore the vast mountain ridges, but the scarcity of water and salt has been inherent from the beginning. Regarding this, one cannot help but furrow one's brow and wonder what can be done (未嘗不 어찌하나 하고 니마를 집흘 수 밧게 업다).

Hearing there is no water, those who only hear might say, "The mountain must be large. If the mountain is large, the valley must be deep, and if the valley is deep, the stream will naturally be large, so how could water be scarce? In such a place, how good must the spring water be?" However, this is merely empty theorizing (空論) by someone who has not seen Jeju Island; the reality is starkly different. Since Jeju Island is a mass formed from solidified volcanic lava (火熔岩), the rock is pockmarked like smallpox scars (마마), rough and uneven (울멍줄멍한 얼음박이 돌), with no granite type whatsoever. This rock crumbles like poorly steamed rice cake (선떡덩이) and piles up high to form mountains and low to form valleys. Unless the water volume is considerable, it all flows underground (隱伏). Even if there are springs, unless they are quite large, it is truly difficult for them to well up; perhaps they seep out below. Thus, not every village (洞里) has a proper spring, and to put it starkly, there are quite a few places where fetching water from about 5 *ri* away is considered relatively close (갓갑다).<210>

The first place we landed was called Sanjŏp'o (山底浦) outside Jeju Castle (濟州城). Here, indeed, there is good water called "Sanjŏ water" (山底 물). Thus, in the early morning or late afternoon, the sight of women – old, middle-aged, young, and children – bustling about drawing this water is truly a magnificent spectacle (壯觀). As for salt, if it were merely boiled down at the seaside ports (海隅浦邊), it should be inexhaustible until the sea itself runs dry. However, the coastline (浦邊) is not like the west coast with tidal flats (개펄) stretching for tens of *ri*, but rather a coast interrupted by rocky outcrops (石脚). Thus, there is absolutely no location suitable for salt fields (鹽田). Can the salt boiled down in small quantities at Kŏup'o Bend (苦于浦曲) in Chŏngŭi (旌義) and Taejŏng (大靜) possibly meet the demand of a population of over 210,000?

Jeju Island, Have You Been Well?

There is a saying about Jeju Island's "Three Abundances" (三多). There are many stones, much wind, and many women. Written in Chinese characters, this is 石多風多女多 (Sŏkta P'ungda Yŏda). Abundance itself is no fault, but if useless things are abundant, it is a worry; how much more worrisome must it be when necessary things are lacking? In contrast to the "Three Abundances," I coined the phrase "Four Lacks" (四無). These are: No Springs (泉無), No Rice Paddies (稻無), No Fruit (果無), and No Salt (鹽無). Saying "lacks" doesn't mean they are utterly non-existent (씨알머리도 업다는 것), but that they are scarce relative to the proportion that should exist. As mentioned before, the mountains and fields are rugged and barren (崎嶇磽노), with not even half an *acre* (半畝) of fertile flatland (腴厚). Since water is inherently scarce, the area suitable for cultivation as paddy fields (水田) is small. As for rice cultivation (稻作), a bumper crop for the entire island yields merely 20,000 *sŏk*. For a population of over 210,000, how astonishing is the figure of 20,000 *sŏk*? Also, sitting and hearing about it, it sounds like fruit should be quite common. One imagines fruits cultivated in the fields and wild fruits (山果) blooming and falling on their own in the mountains. Just thinking about it, picturing colorful fruits – red, dark, yellow – hanging heavily, bending the branches season after season, makes one feel content even before eating. However, this too is vastly different (大相不同) from reality. Pine nuts (栢子) are entirely absent, miscellaneous types like pears (梨) and chestnuts (栗) are extremely rare (絶稀). Persimmon trees (柿木) exist occasionally (間有), but the fruit pulp is not rich (豊濃). Furthermore, due to the custom of dyeing summer clothes (夏衣) with persimmon tannin (柿潻), persimmons are rarely used for food (食用). Therefore, among common fruits, the "lacking" side outweighs the "existing" side. Having written this much, my aforementioned "Four Lacks" does not seem too severe.

If I keep writing only such things, it might seem like I am deliberately finding fault with Jeju Island, but the reality is already so, and since I have started writing, I will add a few more words. Also, for the reader, whether good or bad, if it is newly heard information, it will at least be curious news (奇聞). Therefore, I will continue writing without deliberately avoiding tedious details. Like the aforementioned "Three Abundances," there is also the saying: Because the mountains are high, there are many stones (山之高也石多 故로). Because there are many stones, the mountains are high; because the mountains are high, there is much wind. And due to this wind, disasters are frequent. The wind on Jeju Island is such that, unlike on the mainland, houses with grass roofs (草蓋 집웅) do not just have spiderwebs hanging, but are tied down with ropes thick as anchor lines (닷줄가튼 동아바), woven like netting (數* 뜨듯이). (*Hanja obscured, likely '촘촘히' meaning densely).

Jeju Island also has "Three Disasters" (三災). Because the mountains are high (山高), wind damage (風災) is frequent. Because the soil is thin (土薄), drought damage (旱災) is frequent. When it is parched dry and water is scarce, if a heavy rain falls, the water does not pool and flow somewhere (停溜), but rushes straight down the deep valleys, causing severe flood damage (水災). Then, when the sun shines brightly again,<211> only rocks stand out starkly, and pebbles roll around (돌작알만 대글대글), thus drought damage (旱災) is also severe. As mentioned before, although there are many rocks, these rocks are not hard like granite but crumbly masses of volcanic lava (火熔岩). Also, some are like the clay from a brazier's chimney (땜장이 굴뚝 흙), fired and refired by heat. Therefore, stone slabs (돌ㅅ장) cannot be quarried from Jeju rock. Even if some could be quarried, laying them for underfloor heating (구둘) would cause them to catch fire, it is said. For this reason, rooms on Jeju Island cannot have *kudŭl* (구들 - traditional underfloor heating). Thus, there is absolutely no *ondol* (온돌) in any room. Even in the inn (여관) at Sanjŏp'o where I stayed, because the owner was from Chŏnju (全州), only one room out of four was equipped with *ondol* to accommodate mainland guests.

So how do the island natives (本島 사람) manage in winter? People here prepare their rooms not by laying *kudŭl* stones, but by arranging stones flatly. In the warmest part of the room (알의목깨), they place a small brazier enclosure (함실 한 張) large enough for one person to sit on, plaster over it (맥질), cover it with oiled paper flooring (장판), and then, for heating – firewood being scarce – they burn dried horse dung under the brazier enclosure, barely getting by (간신간신 지낸다). Also, considering things absent as local products (土産): among mountain vegetables (山菜), *ch'ul* (朮 - Atractylodes), ginseng (인삼), *tanggwi* (당귀 - Angelica gigas), and bellflower root (吉更 - *kilgyŏng*) are absent. Among marine products (海産), laver (海衣 - *haeŭi*), octopus (絡蹄 - *nakche*), oysters (牡礪 - *moryŏ*), herring (청어), and croaker (석수어) are absent. Pottery (도기), porcelain (사기), brassware (鍮), and ironware (철) are naturally absent. As mentioned before, due to scarce rice cultivation (稻作), refined rice wine (淸酒) is entirely non-existent (絶無). Among birds, cranes (鶴) and magpies (鵲) are numerous, while pheasants (雉) and crows (烏) are most abundant. Although deer (獐, 鹿) were said to be plentiful before, they are now rarely seen. Foxes (狐), rabbits (兎), tigers (虎), and bears (熊) have been absent from the beginning, so there is absolutely no worry about tiger attacks (虎患), which mainlanders fear so much. Consequently, among the hundreds of tiger tales, there are no Jeju Island tiger tales; if anyone tells one, it is a lie.

Curious Customs (奇異한 風習)

The previous two sections seem to have spoken only of Jeju Island's shortcomings, which is quite regrettable. Now, I intend to change the subject. When talking about Jeju Island, everyone recalls the three surnames Ko, Pu, and Ryang (高夫良三姓), and the story of the Three Surnames naturally follows. Mohŭnghyŏl (毛興穴 - "Hair Raising Cave," usually Samseonghyeol 三姓穴) is located on a flat bank west of Karak Stream (嘉樂川 - Karakkotnae 가략곳내) in North Jeju. This "hole" (穴) is not shaped like any particular cave, nor is it situated at the base of some cliff, but is a hollow depression in the middle of a flat bank, about two *pyeong* (두어평 - approx. 6.6 sq meters) in circumference. Seeing this famous Samseonghyeol, one cannot help but stand there dumbfounded. Is this truly the den of the three demigods (三神)? What kind of pit was this? What kind of place was this? The more one looks, the deeper the doubt grows.

Even considering that the pit might have filled in somewhat over the long years (年月이 久深), was this really the burrow where three people jostled together (복작이를 치든 구렁)?<212> No matter how one looks at it, it is impossible to know. However, this place, passed down unchanged for thousands of years, must have some reason for its existence, though it doesn't quite feel like a historical site (歷史地). Yet, I do not mean to deny it, nor can I deny it. Even in remote antiquity (上古), where people supposedly lived in caves summer and winter (夏*冬穴 - *Hanja obscured, likely 夏巢冬穴 meaning nesting in summer, caving in winter), did they really live in such a pit? Anyway, since this is called Samseonghyeol, let's accept it as Samseonghyeol. Even so, how did the King of Byeokrangguk (碧浪國王) know that the Three Surnames had descended and send his three princesses (王女)? Let's leave that unquestioned (勿問에 부치고) too. But did the demigods (神人) really compete so cleverly with archery to become chieftains (君長)? These are all unknowable matters. Perhaps it is best to simply accept the fact that the Three Surnames emerged, each married, each claimed territory, and lived there, and then gather the surrounding circumstances to piece together the story.

If so, when did this Jeju Island come into existence? Consulting history (*), in the 5th year of King Mokchong (穆宗) of Koryŏ (高麗), a mountain on Jeju Island developed four holes, and red water (赤水) gushed out for five days before stopping, the water turning into roof tiles (瓦石). Also, in the 10th year [of Mokchong], a mountain emerged in the South Sea (南海). Clouds and mist obscured everything (雲霧가 晦冥), and the earth rumbled like thunder (地動이 울에갓치) for about seven days (한 닐헤 - *nilhe* means 7 days) before finally clearing. The mountain was over 100 *jang* (丈 - approx. 300 meters) high and about 40 *ri* (里 - approx. 16 km) in circumference, devoid of vegetation, with smoke covering its top, appearing like burning sulfur (石硫黃). This is said to be Kunsan (軍山) in present-day Taejŏng (大靜). Thus, Jeju Island is undoubtedly a volcanic island formed by earthquakes, and its emergence date cannot possibly be before the Three Kingdoms period (三國), as volcanic eruptions occurred sporadically even until the early Koryŏ period. Therefore, as stated in the geography records (地志), the time when the Three Surnames emerged corresponds exactly to the Samhan (三韓) period. The claim by Kim Chosun (金祖淳) that the emergence of the Three Ŭlla (三乙那 - another name for the founders) was contemporaneous with Tan'gun (檀君) is not even close. Furthermore, the statement in the *T'aengniji* (擇里志) that in the 8th year of Yongp'ing (永平) under Emperor Ming of Han (漢明帝), corresponding to the year Ŭlch'uk (乙丑 - 65 AD), a purple aura (紫氣) appeared over the South Sea (南溟), might be supporting evidence (傍證) for the timing of the Three Surnames' emergence. The claim in the Ko Clan Genealogy (高氏家譜) that the emergence of the Three Demigods (三神湧出) occurred in the 2nd year of O-pong (五鳳) under Emperor Xuan of Han (漢宣帝 - 56 BC) further supports this. Considering that the legend of the Three Surnames Ko, Ryang, and Pu (高良三姓說) corresponds to the legend of the three Silla surnames Pak, Sŏk, and Kim (朴昔金三姓說), the legend of the three women floating ashore (三女浮來說) is similar to the legend of Sŏk T'arhae crossing the sea (昔脫解渡來說), and the legend of divining land by shooting arrows (射矢卜地說) is similar to divining a residence by burying charcoal (埋炭卜居* - *Hanja likely 說, meaning legend), the time of the Three Demigods' descent cannot possibly be before the Samhan period, let alone contemporaneous with Tan'gun. The mention of the Samhan period in the *Jiji* likely refers not to Mahan (馬), Jinhan (辰), and Byeonhan (弁), but to the Three Kingdoms of Silla (旬麗 - likely 新羅 Silla), Paekche (百濟), and Silla (新羅 - repeated, maybe Koguryŏ was intended but written as Silla?). Thus, the time of the Three Demigods' descent, the origin of Tamna (耽羅), seems to be contemporaneous with or later than the Three Kingdoms period. Furthermore, even if Jeju Island were a remote island in the sea (海中絶島), if its origin were before the Three Kingdoms or even much earlier than Samhan, it is unlikely that its connection with the mainland would have been established so late, only in the 2nd year of King Munju (文周王) of Paekche (百濟). Therefore, the time of the Three Demigods' descent, the origin of Tamna, is likely after the Three Kingdoms period. If so, did the descendants of these Three Demigods, the Three Surnames, spread and multiply to become the people of Jeju Island? That cannot be said for certain. While venerable figures like the Three Surnames initiated the pioneering of this island, people who migrated from the mainland form the majority (大部) of the island's population. There were also those who came across from Tokyo (東京 - meaning Japan?) in the East, those who entered from China (支那) in the West, and finally, those who came down from Mongolia (蒙古). As the races mixed, language and customs followed suit.<213> There are many such instances. To mention just one or two examples: proficiency in horse breeding (牧馬) and the industriousness of women are customs of the northern lands (北陸 - perhaps Manchuria/Mongolia). Ending interrogative sentences with a noun when asking back is a pattern from Chinese grammar (支那語法). The way women hold a needle when sewing is similar to Japanese women (和女), and the widespread use of the sentence-ending particle *masŭ* (마스) after nouns, verbs, adjectives, interrogatives, and declaratives is said to be a Japanese influence (日本의 風氣). For example, when asking "Is it this?" they say "이것말슴" (*igŏt malsŭm* - literally "this word/speech"), and for "It is a person," they say "사람마스" (*saram masŭ*), and for "There is/are," they say "잇서마스" (*issŏ masŭ*).

Searching for the Magistrate of this Province (本州牧使)

Although there is no place where this wasn't true in ancient times (古代), there were few regions with as much superstition (미신) as Jeju Island. How prevalent was superstition? It is said there were about 1,000 shrines (詞) and altars (堂). This can be guessed even from the saying passed down today of "500 shrines, 500 altars" (詞500 堂500). Also, even now, male and female shamans (男巫女巫) called *sŭngdang* (승당) swarm everywhere. In places like the Taejŏng region to the south, it is said that snakes are still worshipped as the Seven Stars (七星 - Chilseong, the Big Dipper). If so, it's not just snakes being worshipped; traces of Seven Star worship can be clearly seen. This indicates that the people's level (程度) was still in the stage of worshipping tangible objects (有形的物). On a remote ocean island (海洋絶島), not the mainland, looking up at the sky on a pitch-black moonless night, the countless stars (星辰) twinkling distinctly, maintaining their positions (*) without change whenever observed, must have seemed mysterious and awe-inspiring, making such worship natural. This custom likely persisted later here than elsewhere. In connection with this, there is something else to mention. Within Jeju Castle, there is a Chilseong Diagram (七星圖 - Seven Star Diagram). It is said this exists because the Ko, Pu, and Ryang clans divided the area into three sections and settled in a formation resembling the Big Dipper (北斗形). However, it is difficult to readily believe this; this Chilseong Diagram might have been a site for rituals (祭) to the heavens or the Big Dipper. If not, one might suspect it was a place for rituals to the snake god (蛇神) believed to be the Seven Stars. In any case, it is clear that this Chilseong Diagram was a site for rituals, whether to the stars or something else. The story that the Three Surnames lived imitating the shape of the Big Dipper seems unlikely. Whether the Big Dipper was likened to a snake because it is curved, or a snake was likened to the Seven Stars because it wriggles, let's leave that aside. Considering that even until the reign of King Sukchong (肅宗), magistrates (牧使) suffered from the mischief (作戱) of demonic snakes (妖蝺) to the point where their movements (行止) were hindered, one can grasp how abundant snakes were on the island (本島) and how much fear they instilled in the people. Thus, when an upright and great figure like Yi Hyŏngsang (李衡祥) became magistrate, saw this situation, and became greatly concerned, he expelled the snakes (驅除), demolished several hundred illicit shrines (淫祠), burned sacred trees (*木 - *Hanja obscured, likely 神木 shinmok, sacred tree), and collected the shamans' (巫覡) metal bells (金鐸) to forge military equipment (軍器). The reduction of illicit shrines in Jeju began with Yi Hyŏngsang,<214> and the awakening from superstition in Jeju dates from the time of King Sukchong. After the descent of the Three Demigods (三神下降), how many people must have bowed down (納拜) before demonic snakes, monstrous trees, and illicit shrines? And how many must have lost their lives to them? Ah, how frightening, the superstitions of Jeju Island! Such superstitions have mostly disappeared now, and there are few people who practice them, but even today, superstition is prevalent among Jeju people.

Perhaps it is because Chŏlla Province (全羅道) has more superstition than anywhere else, and Jeju Island is the southern tip of Chŏlla Province, but there are no people as superstitious as Jeju people. Believing the saying that a "True Man" (眞人) will emerge from among the sea islands (海島), they point to the northern foot of Hallasan as South Chosŏn (南朝鮮) Peak, claiming "this is South Chosŏn," and vigorously promote the South Chosŏn theory (南朝鮮說). To prove this, they claim Emperor Taizu of Song's (宋太祖) birthplace was Jeju, and his ancestors' tombs (先墓) are in Jeju, among other things. Though different from past superstitions, superstition is still superstition. Utter nonsense (엉털이도 업는 미신). Not a few people carry iron pieces shaped like a chicken's comb (닭의 멀덕운이 가튼 쇠), fuss about auspicious sites for graves (陰宅) or houses (陽宅), and consult fortune-tellers about the Inch'ŏn (仁川) rice and bean market (米豆판), asking "How about today? How about tomorrow?"

There are many unbelievable stories originating from Jeju people recorded in historical annals (史冊) from not-so-distant eras. First, when the Crown Prince of Ryukyu (琉球太子) arrived at Sanjŏp'o (山底浦) on the island, the provincial magistrate (本州牧使) demanded valuables (實貨を討索). All the prince had were a "Wine Spring Stone" (酒泉石) and a "Mountain Covering Canopy" (漫山帳). The Wine Spring Stone was a square stone (方石) with a depression (凹處) in the center; pouring clear water into it immediately turned it into wine (酒). The Mountain Covering Canopy was woven (織成) from spider silk (蜘蛛絲) dyed with medicine (藥); when folded small (小張), it could cover one room (一間), and when spread large (大張), it could cover a large mountain (大山), without letting rainwater leak through (雨水가 漏치 아니한다). During the reign of King Chungjong (中宗), Magistrate Sŏ Ryŏn (判官徐憐) killed the demonic snake in Kimnyŏnggul Cave (金寧窟). As he rode back alone towards the castle (單騎로 성에 驅入), a streak of red energy (一道赤氣) pursued him closely (直趕). Upon reaching the government office (官衛), he collapsed unconscious (昏倒不省) and finally died after about ten days. Snakes gathered from all directions, causing such a commotion (어찌 야단이든지) that during the preparation of the body for burial (歛襲入棺), fires had to be lit on all sides, and special instructions (申飭) were given for a separate coffin (別棺) to seal it (結棺). However, when the time came to transport the coffin (運柩) for the funeral, they opened it, and lo and behold – a most strange thing! A snake had gotten inside and eaten out one of Mr. Sŏ's eyes (徐씨의 한 눈을 파아먹엇더라). Thus, it is said that his descendants for generations are blind in one eye (한눈이 굿는다). As for my acquaintance Mr. Sŏ (然我徐君), my memory of whether his descendants have any eye condition is hazy (몽롱하다). I intend to look closely when I go to Seoul.

Where are the Ten Sights of Yŏngju (瀛州)?

Writing a travelogue (紀行) of Jeju has turned into something like a storybook. A storybook filled with unclear tales, at that. However, that is what I saw and what I heard. Also, as mentioned before, I am not writing this travelogue in the order I visited places, but giving a general overview. That is why it turned out this way. Leaving everything else aside, if one looks at Jeju Island solely for its scenic beauty (景勝), there are the Ten Sights of Yŏngju (瀛洲十景 - Yŏngju is an old name for Jeju, evoking the mythical Isles of the Immortals).

The Ten Sights are:
Sunrise at Sŏngsan (城山日出) / Sunset at Sabong (紗峯落照)
Evening Snow at Noktam (鹿潭晩雪) / Strange Rocks at Yŏngsil (靈室奇岩)
Spring Flowers at Yŏnggu (瀛丘春花) / Autumn Colors of the Tangerine Groves (橘林秋色)
Sanbanggulsa Temple Cave (山房窟寺) / Chŏngbang Waterfall (正房瀑布)<215>
Grazing Horses at Kosu Pasture (古藪牧馬) / Night Sails at Yongyŏn (龍淵夜帆)
These are they. Sometimes, Fishing at Sanp'o (山浦釣魚) or Returning Sails at Jop'o (渚浦*帆 - *likely 歸帆 Kwibŏm) is substituted for Sanbanggulsa Temple Cave. Sunrise and sunset are not unique to Jeju Island, so let's omit them. Spring flowers and autumn colors are out of season, so they are not appealing now. Noktam and Yŏngsil are best viewed in summer. The rest are fine anytime. I will now talk about these sights (景). Though out of season, I visited the Tangerine Grove (橘林) and Yŏnggu (瀛丘). The Tangerine Grove is inside the South Castle Gate (城南門). In the past, it wasn't just this grove; Jeju Castle (濟州城) itself was said to be a Poncirus trifoliata ("bitter orange") fortress (탱자성), meaning tangerine orchards surrounded the entire castle site (城址圍). Jeju Island is the origin (원산지) of tangerines; setting aside the claim that even Japanese tangerines (日本橘) originated from seeds transmitted from Jeju, the tangerine groves were once this prosperous, and there were many varieties (種類). To list the main ones:
*Kŭmgyul* (金橘 - Kumquat) / *Yugam* (乳柑 - Nipple orange) / *Tongjŏnggyul* (洞庭橘 - Dongting orange)
*Ch'ŏnggyul* (靑橘 - Green tangerine) / *San'gyul* (山橘 - Mountain tangerine)
*Kamja* (柑子 - Mandarin orange) / *Yuja* (柚子 - Yuzu) / *Tang'gam* (唐柑 - Chinese mandarin) / *Tang'yuja* (唐柚子 - Chinese yuzu/pomelo?)
*Pyŏnggyul* (甁橘 - Bottle(-shaped) tangerine) / *Sogyul* (酥橘 - Butter(?) tangerine) / *Waegyul* (倭橘 - Japanese tangerine)
Besides these, there were several others. Discussing the tangerine varieties: *Kŭmgyul* ripens earliest, in the 9th month. *Yugam* ripens around the end of the 10th month. *Tongjŏnggyul* ripens around the same time as *Yugam*; it is smaller than the first two and has a stronger sour taste (酸味). *Kamja* and *Yuja* are commonly known. *Tang'yuja* is as large as a big quince (木苽), but its taste is inferior to *Yuja*. *Pyŏnggyul* is shaped like a gourd bottle (葫蘆甁). *Waegyul*'s size and taste are both inferior to *Tang'yuja*. *San'gyul* is smaller than *Sohyul* (袖子 - sleeve(-sized) tangerine? perhaps a typo for Yuja 柚子?) but has much sweetness (甘味). *Ch'ŏnggyul* becomes very sour (되우시어서) from autumn onwards and inedible, but then in the 2nd or 3rd month of the following year, its sourness and sweetness become well-balanced (酸甛이 적중하여지며). By the 5th or 6th month, the old fruit (舊實) and new fruit (新實) are mixed on the same branch; the old fruit is ripe yellow (爛黃) and the new fruit is tender green (靑嫩), truly marvelous (奇絶). Its taste is sweet as honey (꿀가트며). If preserved in vinegar (醋), even the seeds dissolve (씨까지 물이되야), and the taste becomes indescribably good (아주 형용할 수 업시 조타). Then, by the 8th or 9th month, the color turns greenish again (돌우푸르며), seeds form anew, the taste becomes sour again, making it indistinguishable from the new fruit. Thus, for the "Autumn Colors of the Tangerine Groves" (橘林秋色), without this green tangerine, it lacks its true character, they say. However, these fine tangerines are rapidly disappearing. Also, since there is a policy of eradicating (撲滅) anything considered indigenous to Chosŏn (朝鮮自來), the will to cultivate them is lacking, and cultivation itself is said to be impossible.
One more thing to add here: ordinary pharmacies (약국) roughly chop and dry common tangerine peels (밀감껍더기) and call it *jinp'i* (陳皮 - aged tangerine peel), but the real *jinp'i* is the peel of the *san'gyul* (산귤), called an "island product" (出物) by the locals. And *chigak* (椇角 - immature bitter orange) is made by picking and drying young *Tang'yuja* (唐柚子); those picked on the 5th day of the 5th lunar month (端午 - Tano) are considered the best. Anyway, the once-famous Tangerine Grove (귤림으로)... (Continued on page 225) <216> (Continued from page 216) ...inside the South Gate, famous just like that, has transformed into a neglected, dirty, ownerless (依支 업는) public latrine (共同便所), and a few oak trees (참나무) preserving the old atmosphere are old, sick, and rotting away.
At the end of this talk, I will mention a few more good local products (토산). Among mountain fruits (山果), there is something called *mŏng* (멍 - written in Hanja as 桎應 *chirŭng*), the size of a quince (木果), with reddish-black skin. When split open, it resembles *ŭrŭm* (으름 - Akebia quinata fruit), and its taste is very rich and delicious (濃美). Not only are shiitake mushrooms (표고 - 香蕈) abundant, but their quality (品) is the best in the country. The *omija* (오미자 - Schisandra berries) berries are as large as well-ripened wild grapes (큰머루 - 山葡萄) and incredibly potent (濃하기 짝이 업서), matching the description in the *Bencao* (本草 - Materia Medica): "Those produced in Chosŏn (朝鮮) are good, and also sweet" (『産朝鮮者良又味甘』).<225>

**Summary**

The author, Kwŏn Tŏkkyu, reflects on Jeju Island in 1935, contrasting preconceived notions with reality. While acknowledging its natural beauty (Halla Mountain, coastline) and potential abundance (fishing, historical horsehair trade), he focuses heavily on its perceived deficiencies: scarcity of water, firewood, salt, arable land (especially for rice), and common fruits. He discusses the "Three Abundances" (stones, wind, women) and his own "Four Lacks" (springs, paddies, fruit, salt), along with the "Three Disasters" (wind, drought, flood). He touches upon unique customs, the Samseong origins myth (critically examining its timeline), linguistic influences (Chinese, Japanese), prevalent superstitions (past snake worship, modern fortune-telling), local legends (Ryukyu Prince, Magistrate Sŏ Ryŏn), and the famous "Ten Sights of Yŏngju," particularly lamenting the decline of the historic Tangerine Grove and its diverse varieties. He concludes by highlighting some prized local products like *mŏng* fruit, shiitake mushrooms, and *omija* berries.

From a colonial history perspective, the article is revealing. Kwŏn mentions the deforestation as characteristic of a "declining nation," possibly a subtle critique or observation reflecting the resource exploitation under Japanese rule. The reference to Japanese management of shiitake farms (일본인의 경영하는 표고밧) and the claim that indigenous Chosŏn tangerines face eradication (撲滅) due to policy implicitly point to colonial economic and agricultural interventions. The observation of Japanese linguistic influence (*masŭ* ending) reflects the ongoing cultural assimilation pressures. The author's focus on Jeju's "lacks" and superstitions, while perhaps reflecting genuine observations, could also align with colonial narratives emphasizing Korea's underdevelopment and need for modernization/guidance, although Kwŏn's tone seems more descriptive and melancholic than overtly biased. Historians might find the detailed description of Jeju's perceived state in 1935, the blend of traditional lore and contemporary observation, and the subtle hints at colonial impact valuable.

凉味萬斛의 濟州島
						權悳奎
水路千里로

따로 안저 濟州島 생각을 하야보자. 濟州島는 흔히 하는 말로 육로로 천리 수로로 천리 서울서 近 2천리에 잇는 섬이다. 사면이 바다로 둘린 周廻四百餘里의 큰 섬이다. 그 섬 가운데에 한라라는 큰 鎭山이 잇고 그 山ㅅ발이 사면으로 버더 무수한 작은 산이 되엿다. 또한 이 모든 산기슭으로 人村이 벌여 잇다. 이 사람들의 생애는 지형을 딸하 산지로는 농업이 主張이요 해변으로는 어업이 주장이다. 딸호여 丘原이 만흠으로 산지나 해변을 물론하고 목축을 겸행한다. 그리하야 산에는 과실이 만코 들에는 곡물이 풍산하며 또한 겸하야 온갖 水産이 무진장이렷다. 사람은 섬사람이라 外人과 접촉이 적음으로 혹 貿貿할 법 하되 淳淳하기 짝이 업슬 것이요 그리하야 여자는 貞信하고 남자는 力作하렷다.
그리* 한라산이 중앙으로서 사방으로 뼈첫스니 산 사이에는 골이 지고 골 가운대는 내가 흘러 혹 폭포가 되고 혹 澄潭이 되고 혹 激湍이 되어 혹 구경에 足하며 혹 濯足에 可하며 혹 灌漑에 足하야 물로 하야는 무슨 불만이 업슬 것이요.<209> 또한 바다의 굴곡이 심하야 혹 항구에 可하며 혹 어장에 宜하며 혹 船遊에도 宜하야 바다로 하야서도 또한 무슨 불만이 업스렷다. 그나 그뿐이랴 朴燕岩의 貨殖傳이라 할만한 許生傳에도 나타나는 말총의 다산지로는 濟州島가 국내에 제일이니 중대가리 世上 지금에는 좀 시세가 낫바젓다 할지라도 網市 岩市 갓냥의 纖作은 한가지 牧馬의 副産으로 수출이 多大한 것이며 또한 기후로 말하야도 봄에는 百花가 만발하고 녀름에 萬樹가 陰濃하며 가을에는 오곡백과가 누럿벍엇하며 겨을에도 동백 萬病草常綠樹가 울울창창하야 온대중의 가장 더운 온대의 기후를 온전히 들어내는데 겨을에 칩지 안흘 뿐만 아니라 녀름에도 그리 덥지 아니한 것은 해양적 기후를 겸한 것이엇다. 과연 기후가 그러하다. 지금 이 삼복의 무서운 녀름이지마는 육지에서처럼 그리 더운줄은 모르겟다. 겨을에는 내가 잇서본 적이 업스니까 말하기 어려우나 本島의 南方에는 겨을에도 솜옷을 안닙어도 괜치 안흘만 하다 한다.
그러한데 생각하는 濟州와 와서 보는 濟州는 서로 틀리는 점이 적지 아니하다. 爲先 제일 놀라운 것은 사람 생활로 하야서 필요불가결할 魚鹽柴水 네 가지에 압서의 한 가지는 넉넉하야 말할 것이 업거니와 뒤의 세 가지는 여간 부족한 것이 아니다. 전에는 산림이 울창하엿다는데 망하는 나라의 특징으로 수목을 죄다 깟가 먹어서 지금은 한라산 엇개와 일본인의 경영하는 표고밧(椎茸場) 외에는 아주 밝안 野地이다. 수목이 업서진 것은 근래의 일이요 넓은 산릉에 培養 곳 하면 되려니와 물 소곰 두 가지는 본래부터 부족한 것이니 이에는 未嘗不 어찌하나 하고 니마를 집흘 수 밧게 업다.
물이 업다면 말로만 듯는 이는 산이 크것다 산이 커야 골이 깁다고 골이 깁흐면 자연 내가 클 것이니 물이 부족할 리가 업스며 이러한 곳에 샘물인들 여북 조켓느냐 하리라. 그러나 이는 濟州島를 보지 안이한 사람의 한 空論뿐이요 실제에는 썩 다르다. 濟州島라 하는 것이 한덩이 火熔岩으로 엉긔인 섬이니까 돌이라는 것이 마마(天然痘)만히한 울멍줄멍한 얼음박이 돌이요 花崗石 종류는 하나도 업스며 이 돌이 선떡덩이 부스러지듯 울우를 문허저 놉게 싸힌데가 산이요 낫게 싸인데가 골작이니 여간 큰 물이 아니면 죄다 밋흐로 隱伏하야 흐르며 샘이 잇다 하야도 여간 큰 샘이 아니면 밋흐로나 소슬는지 우으로 솟기는 참으로 어렵다. 그리하야 洞里마다 다 샘이 제법 잇는 것이 아니매 심하게 말하면 물을 5리쯤에 가 길어 오면 오히려 갓갑다 할만한 데가 적지 안타.<210>
우리가 처음 나린 곳은 濟州城 밧 山底浦라는 대인데 여긔에는 과연 山底 물이라는 조흔 물이 잇다. 그리하야 이른 아츰이나 저녁 나절쯤에 老壯少幼의 여자들이 이 물을 깃노라고 어즐어히 드나드는 것은 참 장관이다. 소곰으로 말하면 海隅浦邊에서 구어만 내면 바다 물이 업서지도록 무진장일터이데 逋邊이라는 것이 서해안 모양으로 幾 10리씩 연장된 개펄이 아니라 石脚이 두절된 해안인즉 염전으로 될만한 위치가 도모지 업다. 旌義, 大靜의 苦于浦曲에서 얼마씩 구어 내는 소곰으로야 21만여 인구의 수요에 供하기에 가망이나 잇슬것이냐.

濟州島야 잘 잇섯나

濟州島에는 3多라는 말이 잇다. 돌이 만코 바람이 만코 여자가 만코 이것을 한문으로 쓰면 石多風多女多이다. 만흔 것이야 무슨 험이랴마는 만흔 것도 쓸대업는 것이 만흐면 걱정이어든 허물며 잇슬 것이 업는대야 정말 걱정이 아니랴. 나는 三多라는 반대로 四無라는 말을 하얏다. 곳 泉無稻無果無鹽無이다. 업다하면 아주 그야말로 씨알머리도 업다는 것이 아니라 잇서야 할 比例보다 적다는 말이다. 전에 말한 바와 갓치 산야가 崎嶇磽노하야 腴厚하다 할 平土는 半畝도 업스니 본래 물이 흔치 아니한대다가 水田으로 하야 경작할만한 면적이 적으며 稻作이라고는 일년 풍작이 全島에 겨우 2만석, 아야제주 인구 21만여에 대하야 2萬石이라는 말이 얼마나 놀라운 소리냐. 또한 안저서 들으면 과실이 썩 흔할 것 갓다. 들에는 業하는 果*산에는 自開自落하는 山果 생각만 하야도 시기를 딸하 울긋붉읏 검어 누릇한 것이 가지가 축축 늘어지게 줄엉줄엉 매여달린 양 먹기도 전에 속이 느긋하다. 그러나 이도 실제와는 大相不同, 栢子는 전무, 梨栗 등 잡종이 絶稀하며 柿木은 間有하나 果肉이 豊濃한 것이 아니요 또한 풍속에 夏衣를 柿潻에 染하야 의하는 고로 감을 식용에 供하는 일이 매우 드믈다. 그러하면 과실로도 보통 과실로는 잇다는 편보다 업다는 편이 이긴다. 이만큼 쓰고 보면 나의 니른 바 四無라는 말이 그리 심한 것은 아니다.
이런 말만 작구 쓸 것 가트면 濟州島의 험만 일부러 잡아내는 것 갓지마는 실제가 이미 그러하고 기왕 쓰는 끗이니 몃마듸 더 쓰려한다. 또한 보시는 이에게도 좃튼 그르든 새로 듯는 말이면 아무튼지 奇聞이 될지라 구태 지리한 것을 피치 아니하고 계속하야 쓰노니 이 우의 三多라는 말과 가치 또한 山之高也石多 故로 돌이 만흐매 산이 놉고 산이 놉흐매 바람이 만흐며 이 바람으로 하야 재앙이 만흔데 濟州島의 바람이야 어떠케서든지 육지 모양으로 草蓋 집웅에 거미줄을 늘이는 것이 아니라 닷줄가튼 동아바로 數* 뜨듯이 얽어매엇다.
濟州島에는 또한 三災가 잇다. 山高하매 風災가 만코 土薄하매 旱災가 만타. 밧작 말나 물이 업다가도 큰 비 곳 나리면 물이 어대가 停溜하야 흐르는 것이 아니라 깁흔 골작이로 막우 쏫치는 고로 水災가 심한 것이다. 그러다가 또 날이 번쩍<211> *면 바위만 웃득 서고 돌작알만 대글대글하는 고로 또한 旱災가 심한 것이다. 이미 말한 바와 가치 바위가 만흐나 이 바위라는 것이 花崗石 가튼 단단한 것이 아니라 푸석푸석할 火熔岩 덩이인 고로 또한 어떠한 것은 불에 달꼬 달은 땜장이 굴뚝 흙가튼 고로 濟州島 바위로는 그 돌ㅅ장을 뜰수가 업고 약간 뜬다 하드라도 이 돌로 구둘을 노호면 불이 붓는다 한다. 그런 故로 濟州島에는 방에 구들을 놋치 못한다. 그리하야 방치고 온돌이라고는 도모지 업다. 내가 들엇든 山底浦 여관에도 주인이 全州 사람인 고로 육지의 손을 치기 위하야 방이 네 개에 온돌은 겨우 한 間을 마련하엿다.
그러하면 本島 사람은 겨을에 어찌 지내는고. 여긔 사람은 방을 꾸미되 구들돌을 놋는 것이 아니라 돌을 저다 평평히 고르고 알의목깨에 사람하나 안즐만치 함실 한 張을 언꼬 그 우에 맥질을 하고 장판을 한 후 불을 땐다는 것이 나무조차 귀해서 함실ㅅ장 밋헤다 마른 말똥을 피우고 간신간신 지낸다. 또한 土産으로 업는 것을 치면 山菜에 朮, 인삼, 당귀, 吉更 등이 업스며 海産에 海衣, 絡蹄, 牡礪, 청어, 석수어 등이 업스며 도기, 사기, 鍮, 철이 업슴은 물론 먼저 말한 것 갓치 稻作이 적음으로 淸酒가 絶無하다. 새로는 鶴鵲 등이 만코 雉, 烏가 제일 만흐며 그 전에는 獐, 鹿 등이 만헛다하나 이제에는 거의 볼 수가 업스며 狐, 兎, 虎, 熊 등은 본대부터 업스니 육지 사람의 그리 무서워하는 虎患 가튼 것은 아주 염려가 업는 것이다. 딸하서 호랑이 이야기 몃 백종에 濟州島 호랑이 이야기는 업는 것이요 만일 이야기하는 사람이 잇다면 그것은 거짓말이다.

奇異한 風習

먼저의 2회는 濟州島의 결점만 말한 것 가타야 大段 안되엇다. 이제부터는 문제를 돌리려 한다. 濟州島 이야기를 하면 누구든지 高夫良三姓을 연상하고 三姓 이야기가 자연히 딸하온다. 毛興穴은 濟州北嘉樂川(가략곳내)西 편편한 언덕에 잇스니 그 穴이라는 것이 무슨 穴처럼 된 것도 아니요 어느 절벽 밋헤 가 잇는 것도 아니라 편편한 언덕 가운대에 두어평 둘레나 되게 움욱이 파인대이다. 그리 유명한 三姓穴 이것이든가 하고 처음보는 사람으로 하야금 고만 물끄럼이 섯슬 수밧게 업시한다. 과연 이것이 三神이나 신구덩인가. 이것이 무엇하든 구덩인고 이것 무엇하든 자리인고 하고 보고 보아도 보면 볼수록 의심만 깁게 한다.
年月이 久深하야 구덩이가 얼마쯤 메엇다 하드라도 그러나 세사람식이나 복작이를 치든 구렁이 이 자리가 과연 이것이든가<212> 암만 하야도 알 수 업다. 그러나 또한 수 천년동안 변함업시 나리 전하야 오는 이 자리가 무슨 까닭이든지 잇기는 잇슬터인데 그러나 이것이 무슨 歷史地갓지는 아니하다. 그러타고 부인하자는 것도 아니요 부인할 수도 업다. 아무리 夏*冬穴하든 上古이기로 이가치 정말 구덩이에서 살앗든고. 아무튼지 이것이 三姓穴이라는 것이라니까 三姓穴이라 하야두고 그는 그러타 하드라도 三姓이 나린줄을 어찌 알아서 碧浪國王이 王女 3人을 보내엇는고. 그도 勿問에 부치고 만다 하드래도 神人들이 그리 영특하게 활을 쏘아가며 君長 되기를 다투엇슬가. 모두가 알 수 업는 일이니까 그저 순탄하게 三姓이 나서 각기 장가를 들고 각기 토지를 점령하야 살앗다는 한 사실로 보아 두고 그 주위의 사정을 거두어 맛추어 보는 것이 조흘가 한다.

한마듸 이에부처 말할 것은 보통 약국에서는 밀감껍더기를 숭덩숭덩 썰어 말여가지고 陳皮라고 쓰지마는 정말 진피는 여긔 사람의 出物이라고 닐컷는 산귤의 껍질이며 椇角이라는 것은 唐柚子의 어린 것을 따아 말린 것인데 5월 단오에 딴 것이 제일이라한다. 엇잿든 귤림으로 (以下第225頁續) <216> (第216頁에서 繼續)그저 유명하든 南門안이 덧거칠고 더러운 依支 업는한 공동변소로 化하고 녯빗을 직히는 참나무 몃 그루가 늙고 병들어 썩어간다.
이 이야기하는 끗헤 토산의 조흔 것 몃 가지를 더 이야기하겟다. 山果에 멍(한자로 질應이라 함)이라는 것이 잇는데 크기 木果만한 것이 껍질이 검붉고 쪽애면 으름가튼 것이 맛이 매우 濃美한 것이며 표고(香蕈)가 다산할 뿐 아니라 그 品이 국내에 제일이며 오미자는 열매가 잘닉은 큰머루(山葡萄)만콤 한 것이 濃하기 짝이 업서 本草에 닐은바 『産朝鮮者良又味甘』이라 한 것이다.<225>
