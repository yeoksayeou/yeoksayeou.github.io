나의 女敎員時代
						羅蕙錫
This happened 20 years ago. It was when R was studying abroad in Tonggyŏng.
Because R’s father was a nobleman (yangban), wealthy, and known for his sharp character, he initiated marriage talks with M and urged R to return home quickly, even refusing to send school expenses. Thus, R had no choice but to return home, but at that time, R already had a lover and they shared a promise as firm as iron and stone.
After R returned home, R's father nagged her daily to marry M, even resorting to fetching a switch to beat her, demanding she get married. However, R couldn't dare tell her strict father that she had already promised herself elsewhere.
"I will live alone."<126>
she would say.
"You wench, how can you live alone?"
"I will earn money and live by myself."
"What an absurd world,"
he would say, finding it ridiculous and not worth listening to, and the matter would fizzle out inconclusively. R wrote a letter to Teacher Y at her alma mater, C School, asking her to visit for a short while. Teacher Y was a teacher who had extremely cherished (貴愛) R during her school days.
Teacher Y came down at once.
"Teacher, please grant my request."
"What is it? I will certainly listen."
"I cannot stay at home like this, so please send me somewhere as a teacher (敎員)."
"That won't be difficult. Fortunately, there is a place requesting (請求) one."
"Then, please do this and that."
R told Teacher Y what to say to her father. A little later, her father came in.
"Ah, Teacher Y. When did you arrive?"
"I arrived this morning."
"Then have you had breakfast?"
"Yes, I have eaten. But I came because there is something urgent I need to discuss."
"What is it? Please speak."
"It's about this. The Bureau of Educational Affairs (學務局) asked me to select a female teacher (女敎員), and since your daughter (令孃) has happened to return home and is idle, I came to ask if you would send her."
"You mean my daughter (딸년)? Now I need to teach her women's skills (女功) and get her married."
"Women's skills are things one masters when faced with them."
Father, in response, asked,
"If I send her, where would she be sent?"
"It is Yŏju (驪州)."
"Then for how long?"
"How could I possibly say that now?"
"Hey, OO (R's given name)."
"Yes."
"Come out here."
R had gone inside during the conversation between Teacher Y and her father.
"Do you want to go be a teacher at Yŏju Public Common School (驪州公立普通學敎)?"
"Since Teacher Y asks me like that, how can I not go?"
"Then go."
R and Teacher Y winked at each other and smiled.
A few days after Teacher Y returned to Seoul, the letter of appointment (辭合書) and travel expenses (旅費) arrived.
R stayed (留宿) at the house of County Magistrate (郡守) K in Yŏju public ****************ing<127> It became the first month of the year. School Affairs Commissioner (學務委員) O's house invited all the teachers for a treat of rice cake soup (tteokguk). R, being the only female teacher, went inside. Mrs. C had passed away, and only young people were there. Among them were two cousins, H and I, who were the same age as R.
From then on, the two young women vied with each other to show affection for R. If they made soup, they invited her over to eat; if they made rice cakes, they had their younger brother T wrap them up and send them over. Affection grew between R, lonely in a strange place, and the two young women who had lost their mother, and they met almost daily.
One night, it was moonlit. As R lay down to sleep, the moon had risen high in the sky, shining into R's window. Just then, someone tapped lightly on the window. At first, R thought it was the window paper rustling in the wind, but realizing it wasn't, she opened the window frame. There stood I, her hair, like the lushness of ninety spring days, neatly braided and hanging down.
"What is this?"
R jumped up and grabbed her wrist.
"Surprised?"
"No, but what brings you here in the middle of the night?"
"I missed you so much I couldn't stand it."
R, finally relieved, said,
"Come in."
"Shall I?"
"Of course, you should come in."
I entered the room.
"But how did you get here? The gates must have all been closed."
"I climbed over where the wall is broken."
The back wall of the magistrate's house was low and slightly damaged.
"What if you were a thief, climbing over the wall so recklessly (백죄)?"
"Who cares."
"Hahaha hee..."
"Eat this."
"What is it again?"
"We made some songpyeon (송편) at home, and thinking of my friend, I brought some."
I unwrapped a bundle containing a bowl piled high with songpyeon, covered with a lid.
R sat looking intently at the deeply affectionate I, without saying thank you or anything.
"Why are you looking at me like that?"
I pinched R's knee.
"Because I'm so grateful."
"I brought this because I was afraid the rice cakes would harden by morning, so hurry and try some."
"I will."
R ate deliciously, smiling faintly.
"You're killing me again." (colloquial expression of affection/charm)
"I don't know who's killing whom."<128>
"Drink some water and eat."
She lifted the water bowl that was there and offered it.
"It tastes good, and I was feeling a bit hungry."
"Eat a lot, okay?"
I was satisfied.
At that moment, the sound of a rooster crowing could be heard from afar.
R was startled.
"I, you should go now. It's late, it'll be big trouble if your father finds out."
She remembered hearing that I had once been scolded for visiting R and walking back and forth on the main street like a big girl (even though she wore a head covering (씨게)).
"It doesn't matter."
"It'll be big trouble if he hears the gate opening."
"Father went to a banquet and will come back late, and besides, I arranged everything with H before coming, so it's fine."
R, still uneasy, said,
"Just go. We'll meet again tomorrow."
"I want to sleep over."
"I want to hold onto you more than you want to stay, but just go."
Under the moonlight, the two young women parted, unable to let go of their deep affection.
They missed each other when apart and couldn't bear to separate when together. They came and went, and came and went again. For R, meeting the two young women after school was a great joy (樂), and I and H waited eagerly for the afternoon. A year passed like this, day by day.
R saved her monthly salary to prepare to return to Tonggyŏng and continue the studies she had been pursuing. After one year, she submitted her resignation and left.
The two young women and R cried every day. But the fact of departure was relentless. She had no choice but to tear herself away from the two crying and clinging young women. All R left behind for the two were a photograph of herself and a piece of paper with her address written on it.
Thereafter, letters were exchanged several times, but R, focusing all her energy on her studies and getting involved in this incident and that affair, had no time to think of past friends amidst the pressing matters of the present (卽 現在). Thus, communication naturally ceased (自然絶信). Even occasional thoughts of them faded until they were quite forgotten.
Over 10 years later, when R was living in Sungi-dong (崇二洞), Kyŏngsŏng (京城), I's younger brother T unexpectedly came to visit. The little boy he once was had grown into a mature young man. R was very glad to see him. T stayed at R's house for several days, and through the arrangement of R and her husband, he was able to enter a commercial school. At this time, she heard news of H and I; they had long since married and even had children.
After that, there was again no news. R bounced from this life to that life, that life to this life, and eventually sought out her hometown again, coming to Suwŏn (水原). She was recuperating from illness (身病), lying low in a five-kan thatched house (五間草屋). One day, after dinner, as she was about to lie down, her nephew (甥姪) came in holding a name card (名卸).
"Aunt, this person came looking for you."<129>
On the name card was written:
Hwasŏng Financial Cooperative (華城金融組合) Vice Director (副理事) COT.
R did not recognize it immediately. Then, thinking again,
"Ah, I know, I know! Where is this person?"
"He's standing right outside the gate now."
R rushed out flustered.
"What brings you here?"
T looked at her intently without saying a word. R's appearance, aged and sick, must have seemed shabby.
"Please come in." She guided him into the room.
"Have you been well all this time?"
"Yes, I have been well. You've become quite the gentleman."
"Yes, I've grown up."
"But how long has it been?"
"I haven't seen you since I met you at your house in Sungi-dong."
"It's been about 10 years, hasn't it?"
"Yes, about that."
"By the way, how are your older sisters?"
"Older sister H lives in Seoul; she is the wife (夫人) of the deputy manager (代理) at the Tongdaemun Branch (東大門支店) of Tongil Bank (東一銀行). Older sister I lives in Ich'ŏn (利川); she became a widow at the young age of thirty."
"Is that so? What a pity for such a person."
"So, do you enjoy working at the Cooperative (組合)?"
"I'm always busy. In places like the city, you just need to keep office hours, but in the countryside, you become responsible for even minor administrative tasks, so it's interesting if you find it interesting, and troublesome if you find it troublesome."
"If you make an art of all matters, there is no hardship (辛酸)."
"That's right."
"By the way, would it be possible to see your sister?"
"If I ask her to come, she will come right away."
T stayed and chatted for about an hour before leaving.
The very next day, R wrote a letter to I.
My beloved friend,
Has it not been 20 years? Thanks to your faithful (信義) younger brother (季氏) T finding me, I have learned news of you. I cannot express how grateful I am to him. Your brother says if you are asked to come, you can come right away. Please come to see me soon. Let's talk about the rest when we meet.
Two or three days later, notification came that she had arrived. R ran out. She was not the young woman with plump flesh, radiant complexion, smooth face, and long flowing braids, but a middle-aged woman with wrinkles, a dark complexion, and her hair tied in a bun (쪽찐).
"Oh my goodness!"
The two held hands, tears welling up in their eyes.
They embraced tightly. Their cheeks touched.
"How many years has it been?"
"Exactly 20 years."<130>
"How could we have lost touch like that?"
"It just happened naturally."
"Through newspapers, magazines, or hearsay, I heard about my friend's great success, but how could I know where you were? I would occasionally look at your photograph and cry and laugh by myself, that's all."
"So, you've been well? But I heard you became alone."
R stroked I's cheek. Tears welled up in I's eyes. Tears also welled up in R's eyes. They were silent for a while. They came and went daily, sometimes circling Suwŏn Castle (水原城), sometimes sightseeing at Sŏho Model Farm (西湖模範場), sometimes visiting a temple and stopping for a bath on the way back, sometimes visiting R's sketching spot (寫生處) and carrying her art supplies, sometimes walking along paddy ridges (논두렁) and field banks (밭두렁) picking mugwort to make rice cakes, sometimes sleeping over at each other's place. Thus, the friendship between the two deepened day by day.
I brought her younger sister and said they were going to Onyang Hot Springs (溫陽溫泉) for an itching ailment. R also happened to be feeling itchy and accompanied them. After bathing in the hot spring and lying down side by side at night, the three women burst into uncontrollable laughter (사접씨를 깨틔리엇다). R woke I's younger sister, who was lying down cuddling a child.
Like this, they spent two nights laughing until their sides ached, and then returned.
Due to her household affairs, I could not stay long and returned home. Longing once again filled the space between R and her.
There are often friends in the world. Friends for work, friends with similar hobbies. The friendship between I and R is a friendship that transcends all these conditions. It is simply the beautiful friendship formed by the joining of R's affection with nowhere to pour and I's affection with nowhere to pour.
However, the regrettable thing is being far apart and unable to see each other often (자조). If everything in the world went as desired, one could live comfortably without dissatisfaction, complaints, or resentment towards others. But the way of the world is that things do not go as one wishes.
However, we do not intend to maintain the current state indefinitely. We don't want to live too closely (넘오 조혀). When living too closely (갓가이) for a long time, alienation inevitably arises. We want to see with big eyes, deeply and broadly. We want to see all directions (四方八面). While we are not seeing each other, I and R will again wander through and find (허매여 엇고차질) a world of ignorance.
We have a world of the flesh (肉) and a world of the spirit (靈). While the world of the flesh is narrow and shallow, the world of the spirit is wide and vast. We live in the world of the flesh, but the existence of the world of the spirit beyond it gives meaning to living as a human being. Who knows where the friendship between I and R, playing in this limitless (무진장) world of the spirit, connected as I and R, acquainted as I and R, possessing the lonely and longing affection of the flesh, I and R, bound by the spirit, will go to help each other's lives in the future?
R is merely painting in silence. Clever I, amidst difficult married life with her cluster (올망졸망) of children, cherishes some silent resolution, pursues hope, and works daily. God (하누님), please bestow long, long health (建康) upon these two lonely daughters. <131>

**Summary**

This memoir by Na Hye-sŏk recounts her brief period as a teacher in Yŏju around 1915. Forced to return from studies in Tokyo by her father who disapproved of her relationship and wanted her to marry, Na Hye-sŏk arranged through a former teacher to take a teaching post to escape home. In Yŏju, she formed a deep, almost romantic friendship with two local young women, particularly one named I, sharing moments of intense affection and secret meetings. After a year, Na Hye-sŏk returned to Tokyo, losing touch with her friends for nearly two decades. They reconnected later in life after Na Hye-sŏk had settled in Suwŏn, finding their bond remained strong despite the passage of time and changed circumstances (I was now a widow). The essay reflects on the nature of their transcendent friendship, rooted in mutual affection rather than shared activities, and contemplates the separate paths their lives have taken, sustained by a spiritual connection despite physical distance. For historians, the article offers insights into the constraints faced by educated "New Women" in early 20th-century Korea, particularly familial pressure against pursuing education or choosing one's own partner, and the importance of female friendships as a source of emotional support and escape. It also subtly touches upon the life paths available to women (teaching, marriage, widowhood) and the social dynamics in provincial towns under colonial rule.
