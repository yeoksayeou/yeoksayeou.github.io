Kim Chŏngp'il, Released from Prison After 12 Years, Does She Intend to Remarry?

Closing one's eyes and thinking back, it was already 12 years ago, the scene at the Kyŏngsŏng District Court (京城地方法院) in Chongno (鍾路), Seoul (now the site of the Chongno Police Station), where the "Beauty of Pukkwan" (北關美人), Kim Chŏngp'il, was being tried.

As the trial progressed, deciding whether to kill or save this lovely woman, every newspaper in Seoul published her photograph and reported her testimony item by item. They even issued extras to report her sentence. Meanwhile, knowledgeable figures in society sent petitions to the judge, some pleading for sympathy and a reduced sentence (減形願), others offering factual references (事實參考論). Tens of thousands of words were sent to newspaper offices regarding Kim Chŏngp'il's death sentence, striving to stir public opinion back then. Thus, the name of Kim Chŏngp'il, who poisoned her own husband (本夫毒殺), became known to everyone, young and old, male and female, at that time.

Time flows on.

Kim Chŏngp'il, who had received a life sentence and was serving her time, was released after 12 years due to three pardons (恩赦) and has returned to her hometown in Myŏngch'ŏn (明川), Hamgyŏng North Province (咸北). So, what has become of her, who once caused such a sensation, after her release? Did she get married? Or did she become a Buddhist nun?<175>

Reporter K from the Myŏngch'ŏn branch of Samch'ŏllisa (三千里社) recently met Kim Chŏngp'il after her release. K fortunately grew up in the same village as Kim Chŏngp'il, so he knows her family situation better than anyone. When Kim Chŏngp'il went up to Seoul for her trial, K was an elementary school student attending the local common school. His heart pounded many times due to the rumors that swept through the entire village. Now, he met the Kim Chŏngp'il of those days for the first time as a member of society. Below is the Q&A between Reporter K and Kim Chŏngp'il.

One thing to add: upon her release and return to her hometown, Kim Chŏngp'il soon left her parents' place and came up to Myŏngch'ŏn town, where she is working as a maid (하녀) at an inn called Songch'ŏn Inn (松川旅館) (pseudonym). On the day she met Reporter K, she was wearing a *haori* (하오리) and dragging *geta* (게다) on her way to the neighboring Yamamoto (야마모도) (pseudonym) general store to buy something for a guest's errand. Unable to even go out as she pleases, she works as a dutiful maid, obeying only the commands of her master and the guests. As if avoiding the world, she stays hidden in this Songch'ŏn Inn and doesn't try to meet people often. While working as an inn maid, she strives to save even the small monthly wage, just a few pennies.

Bitter Twelve Years

Reporter: I'm very sorry to ask you for an interview like this when you must be busy.
Kim: Not at all. But if you have business, please see to it quickly. It would be awkward if someone saw us, and moreover, I'm not that free right now,” she said, showing a very worried expression.
Reporter: Well, what I want to see is nothing else. It's to hear about your experiences during the 12 years in prison.
Kim: Ah, is that so? Thank you. When I was released, I wanted to go to any newspaper office and publish the stories piled up like a mountain in my heart (胸中), but circumstances didn't allow (許), so I had no choice but to return to my hometown (還故鄕).
Reporter: Then please tell me about the courthouse.
Kim: Oh dear, how can I tell you that in such a short time? Truly, if I were to write my prison memoirs, it feels like it would take many days and still not be finished. Let's talk in detail at a good opportunity later. I have to go back home.<176>
Reporter: No, please just tell me the main points briefly.
Kim: I feel constrained (꿀려서), so it's uncomfortable to speak. I'll speak well when we meet quietly later.
Reporter: No, please. Then I'll ask a couple of questions in order. Please tell me about learning to read and write in prison.
Kim: Well, as for learning letters, I studied the common school curriculum for 3 years.
Reporter: From what age did you study? Is it true you studied from age 19 to 21?
Kim: Yes! That's true.
Reporter: What kind of work did you do while in prison?
Kim: I did sewing (재봉), mostly sewing *kimono* (キモノ).
Reporter: And, after living such a life for truly 12 long years, when you were released, in other words, when you stepped out of the prison gate and headed towards your old hometown of Myŏngch'ŏn, please tell me your feelings.” As I looked up at Kim’s face, perhaps recalling her past crimes, or perhaps repenting (改過) each and every event of the 12 years prior, she lowered her long face deeply with a lonely expression, thought for a moment, then slowly opened her mouth and began to speak.
Kim: It's uncomfortable to describe it in words. Stepping out of the prison gate without any particular thought, thinking of meeting my longed-for parents and siblings as I headed towards my old hometown brought joy, but on the other hand, thinking about it, sadness (슯흠) overflowed, and I barely held it back,” she managed to reply, her tone enveloped in sorrow (비감).
Reporter: Then, since you're being called and time is short, lastly, please talk about the issue of marriage.
Kim: Marriage issue? What are you talking about?” she asked back strangely, both surprised and somewhat frightened.
Reporter: It's not something to be startled about. Please speak about the future marriage prospects for Kim, who spent her blooming youth, the flower of her late teens and early twenties (이팔청춘), in prison, and is now lonely like a flower in a spring garden without butterflies.
Kim: Haha, my future, you mean? For now, I wish I could live a single life (독신생활). But thinking about *that* (거시기를)... *kinotokutakarane* (キノトクタカラネ - it's pitiful, isn't it?)” she blurts out fluently. Then, perhaps yearning for her former husband, now a guest in the Yellow Springs (黃泉客), a lonely, cold wind seemed to blow across the face that had been smiling as she answered.
Reporter: Then, do you intend to spend your entire life single?
Kim: Well, *sore wa shirimasen ne* (ソレハシリマセンネ - I don't know about that),” she says quite fluently in the speech patterns learned during her 12 years in prison. She continues, “As for me being a maid (女中) now, my parole period (보석기간) lasts until December 18th of next year, so until then, I definitely plan to remain single. But I don't know what will happen after the parole period ends. So, regarding the marriage issue, please ask me after my parole period is over.”
Reporter: Well, with my shallow knowledge (천박지식), it's difficult to portray this adequately with just these main points; it would be better to write in detail at that opportunity. However, it's an urgent order from my company (本社), so I have no choice (할 수 업슴니다).
Kim: Is that so? If you're going to publish it in the magazine, please publish it in detail. And what I earnestly<177> ask is to express my gratitude to Mr. Yi In (李仁선생), who defended (변호) me when I was facing corporal punishment (體刑). Thinking of how various social organizations in Kyŏngsŏng showed me affection makes me feel eternally indebted (백골난망). When I was released, I wanted to visit each of them to offer my greetings, but circumstances didn't permit, so I've just remained silent until now, but I always keep them in my heart (심중).” Expressing deep emotion (감개무량), she continues, "*Yoi tokoro ga areba yuku kamo shiremasen desu ne*" (ヨイトコロガアレバユクカモシリマセンデスネ - If there's a good place/opportunity, I might go), repeating it several times. On Kim's face, as she clears away the past, a light seems to appear, dreaming once again of a new partner (이성). Meanwhile...
Reporter: Did you receive any awards while incarcerated (在獄)?
Kim: Yes. Three years after entering prison (入獄), I received an award, and again after another 3 years. I also received an award when I was released. And I received pardons (恩賜) three times as well.
Reporter: And, excuse me, but do you have a recent photograph? Could you give me one?
Kim: I don't have one (업슴니다).
Reporter: Then, since it's urgent, could you have one taken tomorrow?
Kim: I don't think I'll have the leisure (여유) for that.
But if you need it, please arrange for it to be taken tomorrow,” she said. As if thinking anew of past events, her long face and shining eyes held a touch of sorrow (비감), and she let out a sigh unknown to others. Then, as if in a hurry,
Kim: Excuse me,” she said, offering a silver coin (은전) to buy drinks or something. When I absolutely refused, she apologized profusely (백배사례), saying she was sorry. Wearing a *haori* over her *hisashigami* (히사시가미 - likely a type of clothing or hairstyle, specific meaning unclear in context), dragging her *geta* (ケタ), she disappeared from view. I watched her retreating figure for a while.<178>

**Summary**

This 1935 article interviews Kim Chŏngp'il, a woman notorious for poisoning her husband, shortly after her release from prison after serving 12 years of a life sentence, commuted due to pardons. The reporter, who knew her from their shared hometown, finds her working as a subservient inn maid in Myŏngch'ŏn, avoiding public attention. Kim recounts her prison experience briefly, mentioning learning basic schooling and sewing *kimono*, expresses gratitude towards her lawyer Yi In and supportive organizations, and speaks cautiously about her future, particularly marriage. She intends to remain single until her parole ends the following year but hints at openness to remarriage afterwards, notably using several Japanese phrases learned during her incarceration.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting for several reasons. It highlights the sensationalism of media coverage of crimes involving women in colonial Korea ("北關美人"). It provides a glimpse into prison life under Japanese rule, including vocational training (sewing *kimono*) and basic education. Kim Chŏngp'il's integration of Japanese phrases into her speech reflects the linguistic environment of the era and possibly the prison system. Her cautious attitude towards remarriage and societal reintegration underscores the difficulties faced by female ex-convicts, compounded by her notoriety. The reporter's framing and Kim's deference also subtly reflect the social dynamics of the period.

12年만에 出獄한 金貞弼, 그는 다시 結婚하려는가
눈을 감고 생각하면 벌서 12년이란 녯날이엇다, 서울 鍾路의 京城地方法院(지금에는 종로경찰서가 된 자최) 법정에서 北關美人 金貞弼이 재판을 밧든 광경이.
그때 어엽분 이 여성을 죽이느냐 살니느냐 하는 재판이 진행되어감에 따라 서울 각 신문사에서는 그의 사진을 내고 그의 답변을 一條一目드러 보도하엿스며 심지어 호외까지 내어 그의 형기를 報하엿스며 한편 사회의 식자들은 혹은 동정하는 減形願을, 혹은 事實參考論을 써서 재판장에게 탄원하는 글을 보내엇고, 신문사에도 金貞弼의 사형에 대하야 누누 數萬言의 글을 보내여 여론을 이르키기에 애쓰든 그때 일을. - 이리하야 本夫毒殺 金貞弼의 일홈은 그 당시 남녀노소할 것업시 모르는 사람이 업섯섯다.
세월은 흐른다.
그리하든 金貞弼이 무기징역의 선고를 밧고 복역하든 중, 세 번의 恩赦를 입어 12년만에 출옥하여 그의 고향인 咸北 明川으로 도라가 잇다. 그러면 그러케도 한때는 쎈세-숀을 이르키든 그는 출옥뒤 엇더케 되고 잇는가, 시집갓는가? 혹은 중이 되엇는가?<175>

三千里社 明川지국 기자 K군은 출옥 후의 金貞弼을 요지음 맛낫다. K군은 다행히 金貞弼과 가튼 동리에서 자라낫다, 그럼으로 그의 가정형편 등은 누구보다도 잘안다, 金貞弼이 서울까지 올너가 재판을 밧고 잇슬때 K군은 그곳 보통학교에 다니는 소학생이엇다. 온 동리를 휩슬든 그 소문에 가슴을 두군거리기를 여러 번 하여 왓다. 그리든 金貞弼을 이제 사회인으로써 최초에 맛낫다. 이하는 K군과 金貞弼의 일문일답이다.
한가지 첨언하여 두거니와 金貞弼은 출옥하여 고향에 내려가자 친정부모 잇는 곳을 곳 떠나서 明川읍으로 올나와서 이곳 松川여관(假名)이란 여관집에 와서 하녀로 잇다. 이날 K군과 맛나든 날도 「하오리」를 입고 「게다」를 끄을고 그 이웃 「야마모도」(假名) 잡화상으로 손님 심부름으로 물건사러 가든 길이엇다. 그는 제 마음대로 나 다니지도 못하면서 오직 주인과 손님의 명령을 순종하는 순직한 「하녀」로 일하고 잇다. 그는 세상을 피하는 듯 이 松川여관에 무치어 잇스면서 남과 줄기어 맛나려고도 하지 안는다, 그는 여관집 하녀로 잇스면서 한달에 몃 푼안되는 조고마한 월급이나마 모으기에 힘쓴다.
