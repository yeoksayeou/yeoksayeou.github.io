Life, Art, and Love of Popular Singers
						Yŏsan

Kang Hong-sik of Manly Beauty - Wants to Return to the Theater World

In a neatly built two-story Western-style modern house at the foot of Naktasan, inside Seoul's East Gate! Here resides Mr. Kang Hong-sik, a popular singer who provides good entertainment to the people of the peninsula with his resonant songs. His lover, Ms. Chŏn Ok, sharing his aspirations from early on, formerly dedicated themselves together hand-in-hand to the theater world, and now both husband and wife boast their beautiful voices on the platters of records. (Photo is Mr. Kang Hong-sik)
Setting out to visit the "sweet home" of this popular artist <161> was on the twenty-eighth of May, when the midsummer heat had already descended upon the streets of the capital, a sweltering climate that stifled one's breath.
Taking the streetcar bound for Tongdaemun, it was difficult finding No. 1-27, Chongno 6-chŏngmok after riding for a while. Perhaps it was fortunate that I happened to meet a mailman who could find his house without hesitation.
A little later, I sat face-to-face with Mr. Kang Hong-sik. Seated in the reception room, cleanly decorated in a blended Korean-Western style (和洋折衷式), I lifted my eyes afar. Over Naktasan, dense green pines and bamboos, looking as if uneven drops of water might fall, were packed together. From within them, an endlessly cool breeze rustled, refreshing my chest. On the other hand, the ten thousand houses of the capital, Seoul, dotted the entire vista like overturned clam shells. It was truly a superb view that enchanted both host and guest.

A Wandering Artistic Pilgrimage

You: I hear you undertook an "artistic pilgrimage" for a long time. Please tell us about your "Tōkyō Period" first. Indeed, moving from theater troupes to the film world and then again to the record industry, you've walked a complex and varied "Path of Youth," so what else could one call it but an "artistic pilgrimage"?
Kang: Yes, that's right. The "Path of Youth" and "artistic pilgrimage" have continued like this and that for nearly 10 years, during which I've experienced various things. The first place I jumped into after bidding farewell to my school days was Mr. Ishii Baku's (石井漠) "Dance Research Institute." I went there with the passion of wanting to learn dance and thereby bring joy to my brethren through dance. However, at that time, I had one unavoidable circumstance. While attending P'yŏngyang Higher Common School, I got into a fight with the art teacher one day and ended up dropping out of school. So, I secretly stole 60 won from home and fled to Tōkyō. I was sixteen then.
Once I actually got to Tōkyō, money had to be sent from home. My family was wealthy back then, so sending about 50 or 60 won monthly for school expenses would have been quite easy. But my father, who had been waiting for me to graduate from P'yŏngyang Higher Common School, thinking I'd become a county magistrate (郡守), was angry that plan had gone awry. Besides, he thought, 'If the rascal runs out of money, he'll come crawling back,' so he didn't send school fees either. Thus, while attending Seisoku English School, I shared the school fees sent to my P'yŏngyang friend, Kim Pyŏng-ch'ŏl, between the two of us. Did that money even arrive sufficiently? So, I threw away my hopeful student life and jumped into the artistic field I had always loved. Since Mr. Ishii Baku's popularity was at its peak then, it seems it was perhaps inevitable that the flame of my hope turned towards him.
You: So you were much earlier than Ch'oe Sŭng-hŭi?
Kang: Much earlier. Then, when Mr. Ishii Baku went far away to Germany to study dance, I changed direction again and jumped into the Nihonkan (日本舘), an "opera" house in Asakusa (淺草).<162>
You: An opera singer, then.
Kang: That's right. So, the first thing I performed on stage was that "Teaboro" (데아보로), which the T'owŏrhoe (土月會) group, including Yi Paek-su, vigorously performed at Kwangmudae in Hwan'gŭmjŏng, Seoul, until a few years ago. The dashing chivalrous thief, "Teaboro"!
But back then, the general social atmosphere was such that stage actors were considered base fellows, while singing actors appearing in opera were treated as somewhat higher-class humans. At that time, searching the wide expanse of Asakusa, I was the only Korean there, so I felt a bit lonely. Coupled with various other reasons,
I bid farewell to being an "opera" singer too! and pulled my foot out.
You: So that was the first and second pilgrimage. Then the third?

A Nichikatsu Actor for Six or Seven Years

Kang: A movie actor!
You: A motion picture actor?
Kang: Yes. And not just any motion picture company, but the very "Nichikatsu" (日活), Japan Motion Picture Company, which reigns supreme in today's film world. Haha.
You: How old were you then?
Kang: The year I turned twenty-one. I quit Nichikatsu when I was twenty-seven, so I was there for five or six years in total. People like Kataoka Chiezō (片岡千惠歲), Natsukawa Shizue (夏川靜江), and Ōkōchi Denjirō (大河內傳次郞) were all there then.
While I was acting at Nichikatsu for a while, Mr. Chi Tae-sŏp <163> decided to shoot a Korean film, and I came to Korea as the lead actor. During the course of this and that, I lost the desire to go back to Tōkyō, so I simply resigned and stayed in Seoul.
You: And then did you immediately enter the record industry?
Kang: No, there was another step in between. That was setting foot in the theater world. Just when I was thinking I wanted to start a Korean Theater Movement in Seoul, Kim Ki-jin (金基鎭), pen name Palbong (八峯), and Yŏn Hang-nyŏn (延鶴年) happened to join hands and organize the "Comprehensive Arts Association" (綜合藝術協會).
You: So, what was your first production?
Kang: We performed "He Who Gets Slapped" (뺌맛는 그 자식 - なぐろられゐ彼奴) by the famous Russian writer Andreyev (안드레푸).
You: How were the box office results? Were you successful?
Kang: Successful? It was disastrous, enough to make one sigh. But we couldn't just stop because of that. We needed to find a financial backer who understood theater, but such a person had to appear. Moreover, theater isn't done alone; at least 30 or 40 people have to combine efforts, so expenses are high. After struggling for some time, seeing no prospect of success staying like that, I spun around once more and changed direction.
You: How so?
Kang: I entered an established commercial theater troupe. The Kim So-rang (金小浪) troupe. A money-making group. There was no "art" there. Also, because the level of understanding among the general audience was low, rough-and-tumble plays where pistols went bang-bang, killing this guy and hitting that guy, were welcomed, so I advocated for Western-style plays (洋劇).
You: Did you find satisfaction there?
Kang: The world is strange; wherever you go, there are bound to be hateful people. Even in that scene, there were despicable characters scrambling to earn more money than others, so I got angry and stormed out. I took the lead and organized a new theater troupe. We didn't have a penny. With no money, we were a miserable performing troupe without the funds to make posters or print flyers (삐라).
You: The result?
Kang: At that time, thinking there were comrades in Hamhŭng who might understand our intentions, we went there first to perform. We didn't even have the strength to make a banner, so we wrote the troupe's name large with ink on mosquito netting and hung it up. As luck would have it, it rained, washing away the ink and turning the characters into mere streaks (川). The promotional flyers written with ink on white paper (白露紙) also just became ink smudges. Opening night proceeded like this, with total receipts barely reaching 80 won. How could we even pay the theater rent with that? However, since we were a group of youths burning with spirit, we received favorable reviews from the next day onwards. Daily income reached 300 won, so from then on, <164> we properly made banners and newly organized a band, doing well.
You: They say if you fail, actresses are pawned for meal money. Did you experience such hardships?
Kang: For the next 6 years, leading this troupe around every nook and cranny of the 13 provinces, I truly endured many hardships. But though I wanted to do artful theater, the audience didn't understand it, so eventually, I came to feel pangs of conscience and a sense of meaninglessness.

Recording Hit a Hundred Doubles

You: So then, you pulled your feet out of theater and came into the record world.
Kang: That's right. That was January the year before last. My friend Wang P'yŏng (王平), who understands me, asked me to record songs for the Polydor (포리돌-) record company. At first, I thought of recording plays. But the company wanted me to record popular songs (유행가). Did I know how to do popular songs? I messed up ten times on one record, and the German engineer named "Pure" (푸레) asked if that was even music. Eventually, we fought, and I left Polydor. What I recorded then was "Spring on Manwŏltae" (滿月臺의 봄), which was the very first thing I recorded. It's also famous for not selling a single copy.
Anyway, vowing "Let's meet again in three years," I left Polydor. After that, I truly underwent bloody training, asking anyone better than me, be they women or children, to teach me tempo. Later, I joined Columbia (콜럼비아) and have reached where I am today.
You: How many records have you recorded in total up to today?
Kang: It should be about a hundred doubles (백따불 - 100 records, likely meaning 50 double-sided discs).
You: Among them, which are you most confident in?
Kang: The biggest seller was "Maiden and Bachelor" (처녀총각). About 30,000 copies. And one that somewhat satisfies my heart is "Korean Ballad" (조선타령).
You: You've succeeded.
Kang: My conscience troubles me. Since this has also become a profession, the company asks me to record things that will sell well, so I can't avoid recording even low-quality songs against my will. Conscience and money are always hard to reconcile, but it's worse in the "record" world. Generally, popular songs are more like mere nasal humming than art. Hum-humming fellows, and if it's a woman, bringing in someone with a pretty face makes it sell well, so they use many kisaeng too. Truly trained vocalists don't record popular songs.
Looking at the "Popular Singer Popularity Poll" your company (貴社) is conducting this time, I would have less guilt if my position were much lower than it is now. There should be many better than me. Among those people, I think Mr. Kim Yong-hwan (金龍煥) is the best. He spent a lot on tuition to learn vocal music and has learned much, so I think he is the best.<165>

You: Can popular songs be looked down upon? They are the most popular and most effective art form. It's just that they become vulgar because singers mistakenly record base lyrics.
Kang: I agree with that opinion too. I also wanted to study more and went to Tōkyō, but returned because my health was weak.

Is Love a Game?

You: Next, how do you view the opposite sex? What about your "View of Women"?
Kang: Advocates of love-supremacy will curse when they hear my words, but this is how I see love. Perhaps it's because all the women I've associated with were either kisaeng or actresses, but I generally believe that love, for a hundred out of a hundred people, is nothing more than playing games. Love? It's a plaything.
Women have a lot of vanity, enjoy famous people, and dislike "sincere" people.
I've never truly experienced love, and the number of women I've associated with is countless. All from the perspective that it's a game.
You: Why do you hold such nihilistic views? When did you first associate with the opposite sex?
Kang: Due to my parents' compulsion, I married a 14-year-old bride when I was 9. So, what marital affection could there have been? I still remember wetting the bedclothes on my wedding night.
You: What kind of romance was there with your current lover, Ms. Chŏn Ok, with whom you share a sweet home?
Kang: Romance? Is there any particular romance? As individuals who had both dedicated ourselves to the Korean theater world, she was lonely, and I was lonely, so our souls probably just harmonized. That's how we moved beyond a teacher-student relationship to become husband and wife.
You: What is your monthly income?
Kang: About 200 won.
You: Living expenses?
Kang: It takes at least 100 won to live. I have four children: a 19-year-old son studying in Tōkyō, and a 16-year-old daughter also studying. Then there's a 4-year-old girl and a 2-year-old baby. Including my younger sibling and a housemaid, we can't live on less than 100 won.
You: In the future, if you make a lot of money, what field do you aspire to?
Kang: Without a second thought, I will return to the theater revival movement. I will pour my life into the Korean theater world.
And finally, my wish is for everyone in the popular song world, both companies and singers, to disregard money and strive towards a more refined direction with the spirit of reviving art. Then, Mr. Kang guides me through the rooms of the modern house, built at a cost of 6,000 won, showing me around. A large physique, a baritone voice – what a talented individual! Wishing him even greater fame in the future, I step out the gate. White clouds drift over Chongmyo Shrine in the sky.<166>

Kim Pok-hŭi of "Elegy" (哀想曲)

It is already early summer. On Nŭngnado island in P'yŏngyang's Taedonggang river, weeping willows like fine silk threads hang their long tresses over the water, and from within the willow grove, thin strands of white smoke from cooking fish porridge rise idly, one wisp, two wisps.
Seeing this scenery, one imagines how Kye Wŏl (桂月), filled with timeless literary genius, could have shed the heart-rending famous lines when parting from his lover Yi Kwang-dŏk (李光德):

*Parting from my love on the Taedonggang,*
*A thousand willow strands cannot hold back the person.*

*Tear-filled eyes gaze upon tear-filled eyes,*
*A heartbroken person sends off a heartbroken person.*

Not only that, but it feels as if the inspired famous lines, said to be composed by some local official, flow forth:

*Willow blossoms before Yŏnjalu Pavilion,*
*Willows, swallows, the setting sun slants.*

*Swallows kick the flying blossoms, blossoms kick the swallows,*
*Scattering into the east wind, into ten thousand homes.*

At times like these, just like a herdboy seeking Apricot Blossom Village in Jiangnan, I cannot suppress the spontaneous excitement welling up in my mind and body.
Watching the women of this western capital busily coming and going near the third floor of Taedongnu Pavilion, with the Dano festival approaching in May, I walked step by step along the river towards Yŏn'gwangjŏng Pavilion.
This was to write the second installment of the interviews with popular record singers hosted by our magazine (本社).
Finally, I stood before the gate of a modest house at X18 Kyŏngjae-ri.<167>
On the ceramic gate plaque (沙器門碑), "Kim Pok-hŭi" (金福姬) was written.

Passing through a flower bed, following the guidance, I moved my feet towards her room. Just then, I saw two pairs of white rubber shoes, shapely like melon seeds, placed neatly before the door, and next to them, a pair of shiny, enameled men's shoes stood shoulder to shoulder.
Perhaps the most elegant yet most devastatingly charming (惱殺) scenery is something like this. It spontaneously brings to mind the story of a certain king (王公) who summoned an erotic painter (艶畵家) and ordered him to draw the most intense scene of relations between a man and a woman. The painter drew a vast field with a single thatched hut (草屋), and in front of the door of the modest room in that hut, he drew two pairs of men's and women's shoes placed neatly side-by-side. Presenting this, he received high praise (激賞).
Now, should I abruptly enter that room where a young man and woman are sitting?
Thinking, "This isn't fun," yet being someone who came purposefully from Seoul and pressed for time, unable to linger even a moment, I decided to close my eyes tight and commit the impoliteness.
Looking at her, Ms. Kim Pok-hŭi was a beauty (佳人) aptly described as fresh and pure (淸楚). Perhaps because it hadn't been many hours since she applied makeup, her eyebrows were drawn like spring mountains (春山), and her lips bore a faint touch of rouge (臙脂). And what was surprising was the painting (畵額) of
"Jesus"
hanging on the front wall of this room. If it were a nice landscape painting, it would be seen as natural. But one couldn't help being surprised by the hanging of the Savior's portrait. And below the picture, in a corner of the room, stood a large "organ" (올강), a dressing table, and a pink mosquito net hung askew, having been pushed aside.

Classmates with Sŏnu Il-sŏn

You: Among the kisaeng school classmates you attended with, who became the most successful, and with whom were you closest friends?
Kim: Sŏnu Il-sŏn (鮮于一扇).
You: With Ms. Sŏnu Il-sŏn, exclusive to Polydor?
Kim: Yes. We attended school together, and our school grades always competed for the top spot. So if I wasn't first, Sŏn was. If Sŏn wasn't first, I was. We were good friends too.
You: So now you are competing for popularity on the same record scene.
Kim: No, no. We should both become famous together.
You: Was P'yŏngyang Kisaeng School your only education?
Kim: I also finished common school in the countryside. My hometown is Ipsŏk, Anju. I entered common school when I was 8. My teacher was very fond of me too, and for me, who struggled with school fees due to poverty, the teacher even paid my monthly tuition (月謝金), so I finished elementary school amidst the warm affection of others.
You: Then, since you were talented, why didn't you finish elementary school, middle school, and university to become a female doctor (女博士) or female principal (女校長), instead of <168> entering Kisaeng School?
Kim: Well... if there hadn't been winds and rain in life, perhaps such dreams might have been realized. But after actually graduating from elementary school in the countryside, was there a way to make a living? My father had passed away – so I came up to P'yŏngyang and, after much thought, decided to become a kisaeng and jumped into Kisaeng School.
You: How old were you when your father passed?
Kim: When I was 12, from stomach cancer. Since he suffered from illness for a long time, we sold the house, paddies, and fields we had for his treatment, but he eventually passed away from that illness.
You: How many family members now?
Kim: My mother, my younger sibling, so three of us.
You: Where is your younger sibling?
Kim: Attending Sŏngmo School (聖母學校 - Holy Mother School).
You: So you became a kisaeng to pay for your sibling's education.
Kim: That was part of it, and there were various other circumstances too.
You: Ms. Pok-hŭi, how old are you now?
Kim: Eighteen. Sŏnu Il-sŏn is also eighteen. We're the same age.

"Elegy" is the Most Satisfying Work (會心曲)

You: When did you first set foot in the record world?
Kim: Last June, when I joined Victor Company (삑타회사) as an exclusive singer, was the first time.
You: How many records have you put out to date?
Kim: About 20 or so, I think.
You: Among those, which do you think turned out the best?
Kim: Well... relatively satisfying ones are "Elegy" (哀想曲). And perhaps "Affection Bound That One Night" (저-하로밤 매친 情).
You: Alright, now please tell us about your love life.
Kim: Oh, teacher (선생님 - polite term for interviewer). I don't have a lover yet.
And she lowers her head. Beside her sits a young man. Well, however much I wanted to convey her pink romance to the readers, I lacked the courage to ask further. Chanting "Long live youth" silently in my heart, I came back out.<169>

**Summary**

This 1935 article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine features interviews by Yŏsan with two popular Korean singers, Kang Hong-sik and Kim Pok-hŭi. Kang details his winding artistic path from dance (studying under Ishii Baku in Tōkyō) and opera to Japanese film (Nichikatsu), Korean theater (both serious and commercial), and finally, popular music recording (Polydor, Columbia), expressing disillusionment with the commercial demands that compromise artistic integrity. Kim Pok-hŭi discusses her background, entering the P'yŏngyang Kisaeng School after her father's death due to economic hardship, her friendship with fellow singer Sŏnu Il-sŏn, and her recent start as a Victor recording artist. The interviews provide glimpses into their personal lives, artistic views, and the burgeoning, Japanese-influenced popular culture scene in colonial Korea.

Historians might find Kang Hong-sik's detailed account of his career across multiple artistic fields in both Japan and Korea particularly interesting, as it illustrates the transnational cultural flows and the challenges faced by Korean artists navigating Japanese cultural industries (like Nichikatsu) and the nascent Korean entertainment scene. Kim Pok-hŭi's story exemplifies the socio-economic pressures that led some women into the kisaeng system, which then served as a pathway into the modern entertainment industry. Kang's candid comments about the commercial pressures in the recording industry and the perceived low quality of popular songs versus "art" also offer insight into contemporary artistic debates and audience tastes.

人氣歌手의 生活과 藝術·戀愛
						如山
男壯美의 姜弘植-다시 劇界에 나오고십다고

서울 동대문안 낙타산밋(洛陀山下) 숙새하게 지은 이층양제의 문화주택! 여기에는 지금 우렁찬 노래를 불너 반도 인사에 조흔 오락을 제공하고 잇는 인긔가수 姜弘植씨가 잇다. 그의 애인 젼옥(全玉)씨도 일즉부터 뜻을 가치하야 예전에는 극게에 손을 맛잡고 두 분이 헌신하드니 이지음은 내외가 모다 레코ㅡ드의 譜盤우에 그 아름다운 목소리를 자랑하고 잇다.(사진은 姜弘植씨)
이 대중적 예술가의 「쓰윗트, 홈」을 차즈러 나서기는<161> 오월 스무하롯날 장안바닥에는 벌서 삼복여름이 닥처와서 무더운 일긔가 사람의 숨을 막어 놋는 때다.
동대문행 전차를 타고 한참 가다가 종로 6정목 1번지의 27호를 찻기는 힘이 들엇다. 마츰 우편배달부를 맛나 그의 집을 서슴업시 차즐 수 잇섯슴이 다행이랄가.
나는 조곰 뒤에 강홍식씨와 마주안젓다. 깨긋하게 화양절충식(和洋折衷式)으로 차린 응접실에 안저 멀니 눈을 드니 저리 낙타산우으로는 叅差한 물방울이 떠러질 듯한 蒼松綠竹이 가득하여 그 속에서 한이 업는 서늘한 바람이 우스스 불어와 가슴을 시언하게 하며 한편으로는 서울의 만호장안이 조개껍질을 업허노흔 듯 眼界一帶에 點綴하고 잇다. 실로 주객을 황홀케하는 절경이다.

轉轉하든 藝術的 放浪

나, 드른즉 「예술적 방랑」을 오래하섯다니 어듸 「東京時代」 이약이부터 말슴하여 주서요. 실로 극단으로 영화계로 다시 레코-드계로 복잡다단한 「靑春行路」를 하여 오섯스니 그를 「藝術的 放浪」이라고 밧게 더 하겟습니까.
姜, 네 올슴니다 「靑春行路」와 「藝術的 放浪」이 이럭저럭 근 10년을 게속하여 왓스니 그동안 여러 가지 경험을 격거보앗슴니다. 내가 학창생활을 하직하고 처음 뛰어든 곳이 石井漠씨의 「舞踊硏究所」엇소이다. 춤을 배와서 나는 무용을 통하여 내 형제들을 깁부게하여 드리고 십다는 열정에서 그리로 다른 것이지요. 그러나 그때 내게는 부득이한 사정이 한가지 잇섯담니다. 平壤高等普通學校에 다니다가 하로는 圖畵선생과 싸우고는 학교를 퇴학하고 말엇습니다. 그래서 집엣돈 60원을 몰래 훔처가지고 東京으로 도주하엿슴니다. 그때 내 나이 열 여섯.
정작 동경을 와 보니 집에서 돈을 보내주어야지요. 그때 시절에 우리 집은 부자엇스니까 다달이 5, 60원 정도의 학비를 섬겨주기야 퍽으나 쉬운 일이엇스나 아버지 생각에는 평양고보만 졸업하면 「군수」나 하려니 하고 기다리든 터에 그만 그것이 틀려지니 화 나신데다가 제 놈이 돈이 업스면 도로 기어나오겟지 - 하시어서 학비도 아니주어요. 그래서 正則英語學校에 다니면서 평양동무 金炳喆군에게 오는 학비를 둘이 썻지요. 어듸 그 돈조차 유족하게 옵뎃가 그래서 희망 만튼 학생생활을 집어 뿌리고 평생 조와하든 예술방면으로 뛰어든 것이니 당시 石井漠씨의 인기는 절정에 달하든 때라 나의 희망의 불꼿이 그리로 쏠닌 것은 아마 피치 못할 일이엇든 듯 함니다.
나, 崔承喜씨보다도 먼첨이섯군요.
姜, 훨신 전이엇지요. 그리다가 石井漠씨는 무용연구차로 멀니 독일로 유학을 가게 되자 다시 방향을 옴기어 뛰어든 곳이 아사꾸사(淺草)의 日本舘이라는 「오페라」 座엇서요.<162>
나, 가극배우구먼요.
姜, 그러치요. 그래서 처음 무대에 나간 것이 웨 저 몃해 전까지 서울 黃金町 光武臺에서 李白水군 등 土月會 일파에서 盛히 하든 「데아보로」 엇지요. 쾌걸, 협도인 데아보로」!
그런데 그때 시절에 일반 사회의 공긔가 엇던고하면 그냥 무대배우라하면 천한 년석으로 알엇지만 가극에 나오는 노래하는 배우는 좀 고급한 인간으로 취급하엿서요. 그때 조선사람이라고 아사꾸사 너른 천지를 차저보아도 오직 나 한사람 뿐이엇스니 좀 쓸쓸도 한데다가 이럭저럭 다른 까닭도 잇서
「오페라」 가극 배우도 너 잘잇거라! 하고 발을 빼어버렷지요
나, 그러면 그것이 제1차와 제2차의 방랑이엇구만요.

日活俳優로 六七年

姜, 영화배우!
나, 활동사진배우로요?
姜, 올해요. 활동사진회사도 이만 저만한 것이 아니고 실로 오늘날 영화계에서 제왕노릇하는 저 「日活」 다시 말하면 日本活動寫眞株式會社의 배우엇지요. 하하하
나, 그 때가 몃살?
姜, 스물한살 나든 해엇지요. 내가 스물일곱살에 日活을 그만두엇스니까 전후 5, 6을 잇섯서요. 지금 片岡千惠歲이니 夏川靜江이니 大河內傳次郞이니 모다 그때 잇든 사람들이여요.
일활배우로 한참 잇다가 그때 池大涉氏가 조선영화를<163> 촬영하기로 되어 내가 주역배우가 되어 조선에 나왓다가 이럭저럭하는 사이에 나는 다시 동경 갈 생각이 업서 그냥 퇴사하여 서울에 머물너 잇섯담니다.
나, 그고는 곳 레코ㅡ드계로 출마하섯는가요.

金, 제가 12살 때에 위암으로요. 오래 병중에서 신고하엿느니 만치 잇든 집깐이나 논밧 전지를 모다 팔아서 치료하엿건만, 끗내 그 병으로 도라가시고 말엇서요.
나, 지금은 몃 식구서요.
金, 어머니 내 동생 그래서 셋이지요.
나, 동생은 어듸잇서요.
金, 聖母學校에 단니고 잇서요.
나, 그러면 동생을 공부 식히려 긔생되섯구만.
金, 그 일도 잇고 이러저러한 사정도 잇서서요.
나, 福姬씨는 지금 몃 살이요.
金, 열여덟이얘요. 鮮于一扇이도 열여덟이지요. 동갑이여요.

哀想曲이 會心曲

나, 언제부터 레코-드 界에 발을 드려 노으섯서요.
金, 작년 6월에 삑타회사에 전속 가수로 드러간 것이 처음이엿서요.
나, 오늘까지 몃 장이나 너으섯서요.
金, 한 20여 장될걸요.
나, 그 중에 가장 잘 되엇다고 생각하는 것이 무엇 무엇이엇슴니까.
金, 글세요. 비교적 마음에 맛는 것이 「哀想曲」. 그리고 「저-하로밤 매친 情」등이라 할가요.
나, 자아, 인제는 연애하든 말슴이나 하시구려.
金, 아이 선생님두. 아직은 애인이 업담니다.
