白墨 (White Ink/Chalk)

My heart is like stone, hard and so very cold,
Not knowing if this spring comes or goes.
Flowers, carried on the wind, fill the yard.

How many times have the garden leaves turned green?
Standing on the stone steps, gazing for a long while,
I hold white chalk in one hand, my heart aching.

다람쥐 (Squirrel)

That one squirrel inside the jar,<271>
How suffocating must it be to pass a whole day?
Its only plaything is just that one wheel.

Solely devoted, that tiny wheel,
A thousand, ten thousand times a day, it turns and turns.
Is it a body fallen into a trap, knowing no escape?

Where ten million ancient pines grow thick and dense,
Like a flying cloud, darting here and there,
In deep valleys and on high peaks, there was no place to fear.

Why did you enter this narrow place?
Saying that enduring life is better anyway,
Do you await a time that may or may not come?<272>

**Summary**

The document presents two poems by Yi Pyŏnggi (pen name Karam), published in the Samch'ŏlli magazine in July 1935. The first poem, "White Ink/Chalk," uses the imagery of a stone-cold heart contrasted with spring flowers and the act of holding chalk to convey a sense of emotional numbness or sorrow. The second poem, "Squirrel," describes a squirrel trapped in a confined space, endlessly running on its wheel, contrasting this with its former freedom in nature, evoking themes of confinement, lost freedom, and perhaps futile endurance. From a historical perspective, these poems are interesting as potential allegories for the Korean condition under Japanese colonial rule. The themes of confinement, suppressed feelings ("stone-like heart"), loss of freedom ("squirrel"), and repetitive, seemingly meaningless action ("wheel") could reflect the frustrations and lack of agency experienced during that period.
