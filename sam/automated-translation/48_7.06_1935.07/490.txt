How Does Department Store Crime Occur?

1. Crimes Against Department Stores

Among crimes against department stores, *manbiki* [萬引] "shoplifting" and *wasŭri* (pickpocketing) are the most common. Consequently, to prevent these crimes, department stores employ two or three plainclothes detectives requested [from the police], in addition to five to twenty full-time surveillance personnel.

The names used for shoplifting also differ by department store. At Mitsukoshi (三越), it's called *Kinzaemon* (金左衛門), while at others like Washin (和信), Chōjiya (丁子屋), Mitsunakai (三中井), or Hirata Shōten (平田商店), it's called *25?* or *Rijyo* (利調). Regarding shoplifting, the stance of each department store is apprehension-focused, with surveillance personnel always in uniform. However, one department store adopts a prevention-focused approach, employing plainclothes surveillance personnel. It's hard to say which method is more effective, but it's true that the prevention-focused approach is more ideal. Among shoplifters, amateurs (*sirouto*, 素人; clumsy people) account for 70%, while habitual professionals account for 30%. <226> The ratio is 30% male to 70% female. The majority of stolen goods are daily necessities. Pickpockets (*sŭri*) often use the elevator. Something to watch out for is that ordinary people face the door when they get on an elevator, but pickpockets stand facing the opposite direction. Besides this, there is also snatching (*somaech'igi*). Crimes involving stealing handbags or bundles left aside while customers are selecting items are quite common. Investigating crimes utilizing department stores further reveals the following cases:

A. Caught by the Tail = This is truly nonsense. One winter, a woman with the appearance of a noble lady tried to steal a fox fur scarf from the fur section by tucking it into her sleeve and leaving. However, the fox's tail (尻尾) protruded from under her sleeve, leading to her discovery. It sounds like a lie, but it's true.

B. Becoming a Habitual Offender = Regarding the path by which an ordinary customer becomes a habitual shoplifter, department stores themselves must bear considerable responsibility, which is why this real example <227> is mentioned. A certain dry goods store (*Obokchŏm*, 吳服店) held a pop-up sale at a certain building. At this time, an attendant (*sain*, 差人) of a certain noble family (*Hwajok*, 華族) came to buy goods. However, it was so crowded (*chaptap*, 雜踏) that no matter how long they waited, the clerk did not wrap the items. The attendant inevitably held the items and wandered here and there, and before they knew it, they had unknowingly exited the store. Once outside, it suddenly occurred to them, "Oh, shoplifting is this easy." Thus, they committed their first offense. The next day, they went back to the same place and took children's clothes. And so, they eventually became a habitual shoplifter. Several months later, at the cosmetics counter of a certain department store, a perfume bottle disappeared right after that attendant had passed by. When followed, the person was a distinguished-looking white-haired old man. Thinking he might be a good customer, they let him go. A few days later, the old man entered the store again. They tailed him and saw him stealing items while walking around the hat section. He was immediately caught. Learning about the circumstances under which this old attendant became a criminal, it was thought that the department store also bore considerable responsibility. <228>

C. Employee Crime = This is the most ingenious crime among those committed through collusion between employees and outsiders, having been committed many times already and still ongoing.
At the fabric counter (*p'omokchŏm maejŏm*) of a certain department store, there was an employee named A who conspired with an outsider named B. The method was as follows: B is a customer of that department store. Using, for example, the name Kim Something, B approaches A saying, "I live in Sŏdaemun, please show me 5 or 6 rolls of fabric for my wife and myself." A replies, "Yes, I will do that," takes out 6 or 7 rolls, gives them to B, and fills out the sales slip (*chŏnp'yo*). Then, the next day, A brings fabric identical to what B took from another section to their own counter and tells the accounting clerk (*changbugye saram*), "Mr. Kim from Sŏdaemun didn't like the fabric and returned it all," handing over a return slip (*panp'um chŏnp'yo*). With this, everything is OK. Bringing the items * taken away from another section is done through subtle methods that arouse no suspicion at all. This is discovered when it's noted that an unreasonable number <229> of items sold by A are returned entirely due to dissatisfaction.

D. *Strange Request* = This happened one day shortly after a certain department store opened. A man rushed into the general affairs office (*sŏmu*). It was just three days after the opening. He claimed that after eating "ebi furai" (fried shrimp) in the upstairs dining hall, he took the streetcar to a hot spring (*onch'ŏn*). He started having stomach pains (*pokpyŏng*) on the streetcar and ended up not being able to enjoy the hot spring properly. Therefore, he demanded compensation for damages. A nearby clerk asked if perhaps it wasn't another store, but he insisted it was definitely this store. So, they called the dining hall manager to investigate. The manager said, "I apologize, but are you sure it wasn't another store? Actually, for the first three days after opening, the dining hall was so busy that we suspended serving ebi furai..." At this, the man became embarrassed and quickly slipped away (*kkokmuni ppida*).

E. The Snatch = Before this type of fraud (*sagi*) became well-known, the gift certificate counter (*sangp'umgwŏn'gye*) of a certain department store received a phone call urgently requesting three 500-won gift certificates. The employee sent was a clever man. Intending to exchange them for cash, he put the 500-won certificates in his pocket (*hoejung*, 懷中) and carried an empty box (*kong sangja*, 空箱) wrapped in a cloth (*po*). When he arrived in front of the customer's house, an unknown man came out asking, "Why are you so late? Hurry up and give me the goods!" <230> When the employee handed over the box, the man fled via the garage (*chadongch'a koban*, 自動車庫便). The employee went into the house and asked, only to find that no such order had been placed. "He only got away with an empty box, so thinking it wasn't a big deal, he returned to the store and laughed it off." It definitely pays to use clever employees. <231>

**Summary**

This 1935 article from "Samch'ŏlli" magazine details various types of crimes occurring in department stores, primarily shoplifting (*manbiki*) and pickpocketing (*sŭri*, *wasŭri*, *somaech'igi*). It notes different store strategies (apprehension vs. prevention), the demographics of shoplifters (mostly female amateurs stealing daily goods), and specific methods used by criminals, including elevator pickpocketing techniques. The article provides anecdotes illustrating how amateurs become habitual offenders, sophisticated employee collusion involving false returns, and attempted scams like fraudulent compensation claims or fake gift certificate purchases. The stores mentioned (Mitsukoshi, Washin, Chōjiya, etc.) reflect the Japanese commercial presence in colonial Korea.

For historians, the article offers insights into urban consumer culture, social issues like petty crime, and the operational challenges faced by modern businesses in 1930s Korea under Japanese rule. The prevalence of Japanese loanwords for crime types and the mention of Japanese department store names underscore the cultural and economic landscape of the period. The anecdotes, while possibly embellished, reflect contemporary anxieties and perceptions about crime, class (mention of a noble family's attendant), and the impersonal nature of new commercial spaces like department stores.

1. 百貨店에 對한 犯罪

백화점에 대한 범죄는 만빗기「흠치기」와 와스리(소매치기)가 제일 만타. 따라서 백화점에서도 이 범죄를 방지하기 위하야 2명 또는 3명의 請願私服刑事 외에, 5명내지 20명의 전임 감시원를 둔다.
만빗기에 대한 명칭도 각 백화점에 따라 다르다. 三越에서는 金左衛門, 기타 和信이나 丁子屋이나 三中井이나 平田商店 갓흔데는 25?, 利調, 라고 불은다. 만빗기에 대하야 각 백화점의 태도는 검거주의로서 감시원은 언제나 제복을 하나 모 백화점에는 防止主義로서 사복의 감시원을 두고 잇다. 어느 편이 효과적이라고는 말하기 어려우나, 防止主義편이 이상적인 것은 사실이다. 만빗기 범죄자를 素人(서투른 사람) 7할에 대하야<226> 상습의 전문가 3할. 남 3할에 여 7할의 비례다. 상품의 多部分은 일용 百貨다. 스리는 흔히 에레베-타를 이용하는 것으로 척보고 경계하지 안으면 안될 것은, 보통 사람은 에레베-타에 올으면 문 여다지쪽을 향하야 서나 스리는 반대방향으로 서는 것이다. 이밧게 소매치기라는 것이 잇다. 손님이 물품을 선택하고 잇슬 사이에 핸드빡이나, 보재기 노흔 것을 도적질하는 범죄가 상당히 만타. 이하 백화점을 이용한 범죄를 조사하여 보면
A. 꼬리를 잡혓다=이것은 참으로 넌센쓰다. 엇떤 겨을 엇떤 귀부인 풍채을 가진 여자가 毛物 매장에서 여호의 목도리를 흠처서 옷소매 속에 너어서 나오려 하엿스나 그 소매 밋흐로부터 여호의 尻尾가 나와서 발견되엿다고 한다. 거짓말갓흔 참말이다.
B. 상습자가 되기까지=보통 손님이 만빗기 상습자 되기까지의 경로에 대하야는 백화점 자신도 상당히 책임을 지지 안으면 안되기 따문에 이런 實例를<227> 말하는 것이다. 엇던 吳服店에서 엇던 빌틩에 출장 판매하엿든바, 이때 엇떤 華族의 差人이 물건 사려 왓다. 그런데 너무 雜踏하야 얼마 기달여도 점원이 물품을 싸서 주지 안엇다. 差人은 할 수 업시 손에 쥐고 이곳저곳 거러 단이는 사이에 어느새 몰으고 밧게 나오게 되엿다. 밧게 나와서 문득 생각한 때 온냐 이럿케 하면 십게 만빗기가 된다고 생각하엿다. 그리하야 그대로 제1회의 범죄를 범하엿다. 翌日 다시 가서 갓흔 곳에서 아해들 의복을 가지고 돌아왓다. 그리하야 듸듸여 만빗기 상습자가 되여 버렷다. 그때부터 數月后 엇던 백화점의 화장품 매장에서 그 差人이 단여간 즉후에 향수병이 업서젓다. 따라가보니 백발의 휼융한 노인이다. 조흔 고객일넌지도 몰나서 근양 보내고 수일후 노인이 다시 店에 들어왓길래 미행하여 본즉 모자 매장에서 걸으면서 물건을 흠치길내 즉시 발견하여 본즉 이 老差人의 범죄자 될 때의 경로는 이러하엿기로 백화점에도 상당히 책임이 잇다고 생각하엿다.<228>
C. 점원의 범죄. 이것은 점원과 외부인과 연락하야 행한 범죄중 가장 교묘한 것으로서 이미 만히 하엿고 현재 하고 잇는 범죄다.
