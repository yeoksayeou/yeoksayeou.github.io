Hugo and the Panthéon

Marking the 50th anniversary of Victor Hugo’s death on May 22nd, there is a clamor demanding that his remains be removed from the honorable Panthéon. The proponent of this view is Rémon Sū-abu. His reason is that it is too regrettable to bury a poet of the mountains, sea, and sky like Hugo in a cave-like pyramid.

He argues that a poet's grave, unlike those of soldiers or politicians, must be upon the earth, which was the poet's friend and mother, and must be a place where anyone can freely and intimately approach. In this sense, Shelley's grave and Alfred de Musset's grave, valuable with its willow tree, are said to be more ideal.

**Summary**

This article reports on a contemporary debate in France surrounding the 50th anniversary of Victor Hugo's death. A proposal by Rémon Sū-abu (Raymond Schwab) suggested moving Hugo's remains from the Panthéon to a more natural setting, arguing that a poet of nature should rest in the earth, not a mausoleum. This idea sparked considerable discussion in major French literary newspapers. For historians studying colonial Korea, this article shows Korean intellectuals' awareness of and interest in Western European cultural debates, even minor ones, during the 1930s. It reflects the content profile of magazines like *Samch'ŏlli*, which aimed to keep its readership informed about global affairs and cultural trends despite the constraints of colonial rule. The incomplete final sentence might hint at censorship or simply a printing issue.
