Article Title: 舊文明의 崩壞·新文明의 建設, 二十年만의 東京을 보고와서 (The Collapse of the Old Civilization, The Construction of the New Civilization: After Seeing Tōkyō for the First Time in Twenty Years)
Author: 安在鴻
Article Type: 문예기타 (Literary Miscellany)

The Collapse of the Old Civilization, The Construction of the New Civilization: After Seeing Tōkyō for the First Time in Twenty Years
						An Chae-hong

It has already been several tens of days since I left the freezing Kyŏngsŏng. The climate in Tōkyō is exceptionally warm, stirring the sentiments of being in a foreign land and bringing forth reflections of a traveler. Yesterday, however, large snowflakes began pouring down from the morning, and the streets of Tōkyō were covered in 3 or 4 *ch'on* [unit of length, approx. 3cm] of snow, transforming it into a silver world. Standing at the train window and looking out, even the great city of Tōkyō, bustling with people and vehicles (people and vehicles are so crowded in Tōkyō's streets that cows and horses are not visible, so one cannot write 'carts and horses' (車馬)), appeared refreshingly pure and clean across my field of vision, as if it were a pristine Pure Land due to this blanket of white snow. Although this trip to Tōkyō came about somewhat by chance, for me, making this first step now, 22 years after leaving Tōkyō where I studied, it evokes endlessly profound emotions. Since I slept through the night journey past Shimonoseki and Himeji, I cannot say whether they were good or bad, but as I pass Himeji, go through Kōbe, Ōsaka, and Kyōto, travel along the Tōkaidō line, and wander around this Tōkyō cityscape, my impressions are irrepressible. These impressions are, to put it simply, feelings of envy, more envy, and beyond envy, naturally leading even to sad thoughts. As I witness the progress and prosperity of this country, I find myself alone<34> generating feelings of infinite regret and remorse, as if our collective circumstances have become so unfortunate due to something we ourselves poorly understood.

Nearly 30 years ago, when I first landed at Shimonoseki as a fresh young man, the bright morning revealed nature and the human world spread out clearly – the lush green forests, the clear and refreshing bays, the clean and orderly cities, fields, transportation systems, and the lively citizens moving about – 보고 seeing all this stirred considerable emotion in my vibrant youthful heart. Today, however, I find myself generating even more profound and far-reaching emotions welling up from the marrow of my bones than back then.

Looking out from near Himeji along the railway line, one sees the wealth of the cities, their tall factory chimneys signifying extreme modernization. The development of the so-called Kei-Han-Shin (Kyōto-Ōsaka-Kōbe) region as commercial and industrial cities seems likely to result in the three cities connecting before long. Passing Kei-Han-Shin and continuing for the thousand *ri* [unit of distance, approx. 0.4km] to Tōkyō, except for the rural areas around Gifu*, major factory facilities, starting with large cities like Nagoya, are lined up within sight of each other, presenting a well-ordered appearance of a modern, capitalist nation built on commerce and industry. The mountains, fields, rice paddies, dry fields, streams, seashores, roads, embankments, bridges (橋梁), and dikes (防築) are neatly organized, appearing fertile and thriving. Even rural houses are all smartly kept, and aside from some traces of the previous autumn's wind and flood damage in the Kansai region, there is absolutely no feeling of desolation or shabbiness. The clear appearance of wealth, orderliness, and cleanliness encompassing the villages, cities, mountains, and fields is undoubtedly several steps advanced compared to what I saw over 20 years ago. The San'yō and Tōkaidō main lines are the primary arteries for international traffic in this land and major conduits for economy and culture, so it is natural that housing construction and general facilities are restricted and controlled by government ordinance (政令). Since external influences also tend to converge here, this level of development might be expected, but as a traveler from the backward society and desolate land of Chosŏn, I find myself welling up with fresh feelings of amazement.<35>

Over the past 20-odd years, Tōkyō has expanded to nearly 6 or 7 times the area of the old city center. Present-day Greater Tōkyō has a population approaching 6 million, attempting to compare its standing with London and New York. During this period, Japan experienced the World War, which brought about a great leap in national development, and also suffered the Great Kantō Earthquake of Taishō 12 (1923). Having passed through these two great turning points (兩大劫運), both good and bad, witnessing this degree of transformation and development is more than natural. Following the unprecedented great destruction of the earthquake, the urban planning for reconstruction involved rather revolutionary large-scale facilities, built upon the revolutionary great leap in economic development during the Great War period. This indeed turned it into the orderly, cultured metropolis of today. If the fate of humanity is strange (奇), then it is strange indeed. As a mere Chosŏn student-drifter (書生浪人), belatedly coming to see this newly risen metropolis and struggling alone in a new torment (新魔障) of vexation and anguish (懊惱煩悶) by the train window, my whole situation seems laughable to myself. Laying out the streets as they pleased, fantastically, radially, or as twenty-odd-*kan* [unit of length, approx. 1.8m] wide roads, the transportation network – national (省線), municipal (市營線), private lines (私營線), elevated lines (高架線), subway lines (地下線), etc. – with various trains, trams, buses (뻐쓰) of various lines, automobiles, bicycles, and trucks (튜럭), rushes and circles in four or five layers. Its speed and liveliness, along with its clamor (喧噪) and chaos (騷亂), are quite overwhelming. The sight of men and women of all classes and types bustling about makes one discover here the bustling metropolises of Europe and America (歐米). The Marunouchi area in front of Tōkyō Station, where I am staying, is also a vital heart (心臟部) of Tōkyō. It has the most large buildings (삘딩) of 4-5 stories, some even 8-9 stories, and its traffic volume is one of the most representative areas. Centered around Tōkyō Station, during peak times, it's not uncommon for about four trains<36> – departing and arriving – to overlap. Automobiles and buses swarm like dense ant colonies, following one another closely, while trams also stand imposingly in front and behind amidst them. The number of male and female citizens moving daily via all these modes of transport is staggering, and when they pour into the city streets, there is truly a sense of a vast human wave (人波萬頃). Most of them are people working diligently and studying assiduously – employees of government offices (官公署), large and small schools, banks, and companies, along with various students, artisans (職人), and laborers (工夫). They look healthy, cheerful, energetic, tense, agile, and also have an air of neatness and balance (均整). There is hope, vitality, and joy in their society. I hear that they aim to rival the great nations of Britain and America (英米) in all their facilities – politics, economy, industry, transportation, etc. – striving for parity (伯仲). The area around Tōkyō station is certainly impressive, but even areas outside the city I frequented during my student days – beyond Ueno Park (上野公園), the Takinogawa (瀧野川), Ōji (王子), Itabashi (板橋) areas, and beyond Shinjuku Station (新宿驛), the Yodobashi (淀橋) and Nakano (中野) districts – places that were then sites for student excursions (遠足生徒) and strolls (逍遙), grounds for cavalry (騎兵隊) exercises, or spots for autumn foliage viewing and chestnut gathering, have now become splendid residential districts, much like Tabang-gol (다방ㅅ골) or Chaha-gol (紫霞ㅅ골) in Sŏul. Their prosperity and bustle are incomparable. I hear that Shinjuku Station, which used to be adjacent to the suburbs, perhaps comparable to Ch'ŏngnyangni (淸凉里) near Kyŏngsŏng back then, has now become a center of Tōkyō, with traffic volume several times that of Tōkyō Station. It goes without saying that there are many large buildings housing schools, art galleries (美術館), banks, companies, and department stores (떼파트). For instance, the famously fast subway (地下鐵道) has entrances/exits (出入口) at various points (各端), sometimes leading directly into the middle of department store floors (廛房) like Shirakiya (白木屋) or Mitsukoshi (三越), or connecting to entertainment and pleasure grounds (遊樂 享樂) like the Asakusa district (淺草區) and Ginza-dōri (銀座通), making modern life extremely convenient (至便) for the people. If you happen to wander down the main street of Ginza-dōri or visit the bookstore street (冊廛거리) in Kanda district (神田區) from around five or six in the afternoon onwards,<37> you will see tens of thousands of men and women from various classes, boasting all sorts of luxury (奢侈) and splendor (豪華), flocking back and forth. This also contributes overwhelmingly to the prosperity (殷盛) and bustle (雜踏). The brilliant glow (燦煉한 光彩) of neon signs (네온싸인), radios, records (레코-드), and the various orchestras (오케스트라) playing everywhere entice and seduce people with modern, sensational (煽情的) melodies (멜로디), turning modern men and women into city fanatics, intoxicated by extravagance (華奢). Should one call it a seductive, enchanting allure (蠱惑의 妖艶色)? Or a fantastical symphony (狂想的 交響樂)? Seeing this far, one inevitably feels envy and regret, but if this is one side of national development and the fanfare (엄파레드) of a prosperous era, compared to desolation (蕭條) and dispersion (散亡), it evokes a deep, dream-like clear sorrow, intensifying the remorse of the backward person. Long, long ago, the two princes Piryu (沸流) and Onjo (溫祚), who left the north and moved south, ever south, seeking to pioneer new lands, were drawn to the Han River basin (漢江流域). One established his new kingdom in Mich'uhol (味鄒忽), the other in Wiryesŏng (慰禮城) in Hanam (河南). Later, King Piryu, who had failed bleakly (蕭瑟하게), came to Hanam and saw his younger brother King Onjo's burgeoning nation already entering a phase of prosperity and comfort. He felt terribly envious and resentful, and finally died, it is said. Am I, a mere commoner student (平民書生) of Chosŏn, perhaps being utterly presumptuous (僣濫至極) in harboring such groundless envy and sorrowful resentment (愁恨)?

I have visited various upper-middle-class tea houses (茶亭), restaurants (料亭), dining halls (食堂), and other establishments and shops in Tōkyō, accepting invitations from our various acquaintances there. The so-called smart, modern style permeates the professional ranks of men and women, even extending into religious settings. However, one thing that draws the attention of the discerning observer (食者) is that dining halls, tea shops, and other brightly decorated places equipped as 'restaurants' (레스토랑) become extremely busy during the lunch hour (after noon until 2 PM) and dinner time (around 4:30 PM to 6 or 7 PM),<38> catering to men and women of all classes arriving for work. Generally, the mealtime passes in a quiet and cheerful atmosphere, quite different from the rowdiness and mischief found in 'bars' (빠) or 'cafés' (카페). As for 'cafés,' my only experience was entering a famous one on Ginza through the front door with a certain magnate leading the way, and exiting through the back – a mere glimpse, like a white heron against black clouds. Nevertheless, the male and female staff (點員) serving in these dining halls, tea houses, and restaurants, no matter how 'smart' and 'modern' their attire (裝身), setting aside their private lives (이면의 생활), demonstrate in their official conduct (公式의 생활) a rather strict and refined (都雅) manner, trained like attendants (侍女 侍者) from a noble house (華胄) serving honored guests (貴賓). They already constitute fine models (모텔) for the typical lifestyle of the upper-stratum citizens of modern capitalism.

One might say the distinguishing features of Japanese culture are its martial spirit (尚武精神) serving as the cornerstone, its combative disposition (戰鬪的 氣習), and the sharpness (峻銳) of its sense of responsibility, transformed from core values like loyalty and filial piety (忠孝). Added to this are the characteristics of modern Western capitalist civilization – concentration (集中), control (統制), and the discipline (規律) of division of labor (分業) – which embellish it. Currently, influenced by the circumstances of a 'national emergency' (國家非常時), everything tends to take on a somewhat militaristic or combative form. This too should not be overlooked and is an interesting point. Even watching the recently established Takarazuka Girls' Revue (實塚少女가劇 - *likely 寶塚 'Takarazuka'*), not to mention overtly military-themed performances (歌演), one sees entirely the formations (編制) and maneuvers (機動) of military campaigns (征陳戰鬪) even in ordinary, splendidly beautiful (尋常華美) comic operas (喜劇的 가극). For instance, when 51 performers line up in a single row (一列) on the main stage centered around the lead singer (首歌妓), with 7 or 8 more lined up on the left and right as support (助演), it resembles the frontal assault (正面進攻) of an advance guard (前衛部隊). When separate groups perform distinctively on the left and right, with song and dance advancing together (並進), it's like the flanking maneuvers (策動) of the army's left and right wings (左右翼). And those singing and playing spiritedly (氣勢조케 歌戱) separately, far behind and on the left and right sides, seem like various artillery units (各種砲兵) providing massive covering fire (巨大한 掩護射擊) or fierce crossfire (猛烈한 十字砲火) for the advancing main force (主力部隊). <39> Those moving actively in between are like cavalry skirmishers (騎兵인 遊擊隊). The way they move in organized chaos (混混沌沌하게 동작하는 것) is like watching a field army (野戰軍). While the opera is the most extreme example (最인 者), even down to the manager (支配人), accountants (會計), clerks (書記), and bellboys (뽀이) in any decent hotel (호-텔), the chain of command (系統) in their movements follows this style (流儀) and taste (趣味), expressing their characteristic control (統制), balance (均整), and neatness (精潔). What moves within this is the manifestation of responsibility and the sense of responsibility. Discussing this anew might provoke cold smiles (冷笑) from some, but looking back at the situation and moral tendencies of modern Koreans (現代 朝鮮人), who seem to know yet do not truly know, whose ears and eyes are accustomed to the old and worn-out, but whose practice (實踐) exhibits the infantilism (小兒病) of a newly hatched chick ('곤닭의 알' 되려는 - *idiom meaning immature/just starting*), the regret of the backward person – the failed nation (失敗民族) – arises afresh. And reflecting upon the whole situation of Chosŏn just by coming back and forth to Tōkyō these days, the infighting (反目) and friction (軋轢) that only lead down into the dark pit of self-consumption (自己耗損) and self-harm (自己戕害) are secretly lamentable and bitter.

On the other hand, there are also various phenomena that require the discerning eye's attention (着眼). Although Tōkyō is this prosperous, remnants of the devastation (慘狀) from the Great Earthquake remain here and there. Sitting on a high floor of the Takashimaya (高島屋) department store in the Kyōbashi district (京橋區), one can see right across the way, behind the storefronts (行廊뒤ㅅ골목), shabby single-story houses (외층집) with tin roofs (양철집웅) lined up in an empty lot (뷘마당), being used to bring out potted plants from the damp interior. Elsewhere too, there are patches where weeds (茂草) grow thickly in vacant lots, quite evoking a scene of widespread desolation (滿目荒凉) in places. According to a certain elderly thinker (某老思想家), even the farmers along the Tōkaidō line, who appear so wealthy (殷富하여 보히는), faced a shortage of 350,000 *koku* [unit of volume, approx. 180L] of food rice (食糧米) last year. On the bustling streets of Ginza-dōri, starving beggars (거지) huddle (매대기를 치며) and murmur against the cold,<40> yet the cheerful and glamorous modern men and women do not even glance at them awkwardly. Is it out of fear of spoiling the scenery and mood? On a certain entertainment street (某遊樂의 거리), it is said that among those staggering around after a drink and causing trouble for officials (務吏?), women students (女生) outnumber male students (男生). There's also an anecdotal story (笑話的인 사실) about a certain earnest bachelor scholar (女學士 총각 - *likely meaning a male scholar*) who, while having drinks at a bar (酒場) in a love triangle with the charming daughter (令孃) of a certain wealthy man (某富豪) and another man, failed to tactfully support the drunken daughter's arm during a walk (散步). Consequently, he was immediately subjected to cold ridicule (冷嘲) and reprimand (핀잔) by the second, more tactful man, who snatched away his beloved. Though now part of Tōkyō Prefecture (東京府下), the crater (火口) of Mount Mihara (三原山) on 'Volcano Ōshima' (御神火의 大島) broadcasts (放送) the sensational 'good news' (好뉴-쓰) for newspapermen (新聞장이) about the desperate screams (悲鳴) of young girls committing forced love suicides (억지 情死), casting a gray melancholy (灰色의 悲愁) over countless young men and women across the land. And why is it that among the melodies (陰調) and rhythms (韻律) expressed through the popular songs (謠曲) and lyrics (歌詞) emerging from the shadow of their cheerful activity, there are not a few that carry such feelings of sorrow and resentment (悲愁悽怨)? They cause even a carefree traveler (無心한 遊子) like me to be immersed in the underlying current (底流) of anguish and indignation (煩惱憂憤). Anguish and indignation are not necessarily entirely inauspicious (不祥). Perhaps they stem from the instinctive accumulation (本能的인 積蓄) of a desire to break through the obstacles of anguish (煩惱障) facing the era and run wild freely? Or perhaps not? The people of this land have latently accumulated (潛蓄하여 온) national vigor (民族的 精力), unharmed for several thousand years in the sea. Thus, one believes their future must surely be bright (前途洋洋). However, when I harbor feelings of infinite regret and remorse (無限悔恨) deep in my bones (骨髓), listening to those tones of anguish and indignation makes me feel even more intensely moved (恍然히 感激) beyond measure. Alas! Is my sentiment futile (無爲)? Rather, I feel impatient with those who see but feel nothing.<41>

**Summary**

An Chae-hong describes his impressions of Tōkyō revisited after 22 years, struck by its immense development, modernization, and vibrant energy, especially compared to the state of Chosŏn. He observes the advanced infrastructure, bustling populace, and disciplined societal spirit seemingly modeled on Western powers. However, his admiration is deeply mixed with envy, sorrow, and a profound sense of regret ('落後者의 悔恨' - remorse of the backward/failed one) over Korea's stagnation under colonial rule, drawing parallels to the ancient story of Piryu and Onjo. He also notes the underlying darkness beneath Tōkyō's glittering surface – lingering poverty, social issues like suicide, and a pervasive melancholy hinting at deeper societal anxieties. From a historical perspective, the article is valuable for revealing the complex psychological state of a prominent Korean nationalist intellectual grappling with the stark contrast between the colonizer's progress and the colonized's perceived backwardness. It highlights the painful mixture of admiration, envy, and nationalistic sorrow experienced by Koreans observing Japanese modernity, as well as An's critical eye noting both the strengths and potential weaknesses within Japanese society itself.

舊文明의 崩壞·新文明의 建設, 二十年만의 東京을 보고와서
						安在鴻
沍寒의 京城을 떠난지도 벌서 數旬이 각가웟읍니다. 東京의 기후는 一屬溫暖하야 異鄕의 情操가 구을러 覊旅의 회포를 자어내는데 작일에는 아츰부터 함박가튼 눈송이가 쏘더저서 東京의 市街는 積雪 3,4寸의 銀世界로 도섭햇읍니다. 旅窓에 서서 내어다보니 人車가 붐비는 (東京市街에는 人과 車가 매우 雜沓하야 牛馬가 눈에 안뜨이니 車馬라고 쓸 수 업습니다) 大東京의 시가도 이 白皚皚한 자연의 包裝으로 인하야 居然純潔한 淨土와 가티 眼界에 걸처 爽朗하게 보입니다. 이번 東京行은 우연처럼 왓엇지만 學窓의 東京을 떠난지 22년만에 지금 이 첫 거름인 나에게는 하염업시 무한한 感慨를 자어냄니다. 下關 姬路의 새는 밤중에 자면서 왓으니 좃코 그르고를 모르려니와 姬路를 지나고 神戶 大阪京都를 지나고 東海道의 沿線을 지나 이 東京市街에 두르 단일스록 나의 감상은 눌를 수가 업습니다. 이 감상은 말하자면 부럽고 또 부럽고 부럽다 몯하야 자연히 슬푼 생각조차 들고 무엇인지 자기가 잘 몰하야 우리들의 다닥친 집단적 遭際가 하도 불행하게 되기나 한듯이 이나라의 진보와 번창을 볼스록에 제홀로<34> 無限悔限의 정을 자어내는 것임니다.
거의 30년전에 내가 새파란 청년으로 下關에 처음 상륙할 대 아츰해에 명랑하게 넓으러진 自然과 人寰 - 푸르게 살진 삼림과 맑고도 시원스런 海曲과 깨끗하고 반듯한 시가와 전원과  교통기관과 및 생기잇게 走動하는 시민 - 들을 보고 팔팔한 소년심에 퍽은 만흔 감격을 일으켜엇드니 오늘날이 되어서는 그때 보담도 일층에 骨髓에서 울어나오는 심각하고 淸遠한 감회를 자어내는 것임니다.
姬路부근에서부터 沿線지대를 내어다보며 도시의 殷富함을 그 높이 솟은 공장의 굴둑과 함께 가장 현대화함을 보겟는데 神戶서부터 大阪 京都에까지 소위 京, 阪, 神지방의 상공도시로서의 發展은 멀지 안허 3도시 연접되는 현상을 나타낼 것같고 京阪神을 지나 東京까지 천수백리의 동안 岐阜*의 드메ㅅ속 일대를 제하고서는 名古屋과 가튼 대도시를 필두로 중요한 공장시설이 멀리 서로 바라보일만큼 늘어서서 商工立國의 현대 자본주의화한 외관이 아주 정제된 세음이오. 산과 들과 논과 밭과 개울과 바다ㅅ가와 길과 뚝과 橋梁과 防築이 깨끗이 정리되고 기름저 자라는 듯한데 비록 농촌의 주택이라도 모다 번뜻하게 整頓되어 지난 가을 關西지방 水災風의 자취를 약간 남긴 외에는 쓸쓸하거나 구지레한 맛이 도무지 없읍니다. 그 농촌과 도시와 산과 들과를 아울러 殷富 整齊 淸潔의 빛이 또렷하게 보이는 것이 20여년 전에 보든데 비하면 數段의 躍進을 한 것이 의심업습니다 山陽 東海南鋨道는 이땅에 잇어서도 가장 국제교통의 根幹線이오 경제와 문화의 大動幹線인만치 그 주택의 건축과 일반시설을 모다 政令으로써 제한 통제하는 것이오. 外他의 사위도 이 고장으로 집중되는 형편인즉 이만한 것이 당연하겟지만 낙후된 사회 蕭條한 조선의 천지로부터 이땅의 나그내가 되매 새삼스리 놀나운<35> 생각이 샘솟고 잇습니다.
