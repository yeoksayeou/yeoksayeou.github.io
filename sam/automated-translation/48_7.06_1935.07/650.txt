발행일	1935년 07월 01일
기사제목	故鄕 (Hometown)
필자	치리코프(作), 憑虛(譯) (Author: Chirikov, Translator: Pinghŏ)
기사형태	소설 (Fiction)

Hometown
						By Chirikov, Translated by Pinghŏ

The steamship carrying me, making my heart pound violently, is approaching the wharf of a small country town where I spent my younger days. The gentle and quiet summer evening, as if sunk in idle sorrow, softly covered the lazily undulating Volga River, the mountains along the banks, and the distant view of the green haze of the forest across the river. A languid fatigue, an ineffable, sad feeling, seeped into my soul from this evening, from the dreamlike face of the water, from the shadow of the dense woods lying within it on the mountainside, from the setting sun dipping beyond the mountains, from the lonely fisherman's small boat, and also from the white seagulls and the distant steam whistle... Already, I can see familiar places where I once wandered with a fishing rod, lit fires, and caught crabs. On the very rock where I always used to cast my line, someone is fishing. How strange... sitting in the exact spot I used to sit, fishing just like me! Suddenly, overcome by sad thoughts, I felt like crying. I started thinking about how I now have white hair and wrinkles, and how, even if I felt my heart pound following the movement of the float (浮子), or even if a fish got caught as that person is doing now, I wouldn't jump into the water to grab it. And so, my heart began to ache for the life that, once gone, never returns... I came here expecting joy and excitement, but what greets me is sorrow. Turning a bend, from the high bank of the Volga, the familiar round dome of the church and a cluster of houses with green or grey roofs peek into view.—Tears welled up in my eyes... Since then, this town has suffered fires so severe they could be called total destruction twice; could the old house where we lived still remain? I desperately wanted to see the house enclosed by the green fence where I lived happily with my parents. Father is gone. Mother is gone. Not a single sibling remains in this world. Yet, poignant recollections <287> flickered before my eyes as if alive. Somehow, I hated to believe they were no longer in this world. When I get off this steamship, I’ll take the shortcut with the puddle – I used to take that way often to cut across.— Over the hill, past a couple of houses, our house's fence will come into view... Such thoughts arose.
『Mother, Father!』
Then, on the entrance steps, the joyful faces of Mother, Father, younger brother, younger sister appear...
『Just arrived!』
『Yes, I just arrived...』

The steamship, letting out a long blast, began to cry. It traced a wide circle, gradually drawing closer to the wharf. The wharf was crowded with people. As if starved, I looked searchingly at face after face, wondering if anyone I knew from the old days was there, but there was no one. Not a single person. Strange. Where did all those people go? But wait, that woman standing there holding a parasol—her face looks familiar. No, that woman isn't her! That woman was already 25 back then, so she must be close to fifty now, but this woman doesn't look even thirty yet. If so, when I lived in this town, she must have been only five or six years old, so there's no way we would know each other. These five or six young women... they probably weren't even born when I was here.

『Old sir, shall I carry this luggage for you?... 』
『Ah, yes. Please take it.』

I can't meet anyone. Not a single soul makes my heart leap. No one kisses me, no one asks, 『Have you arrived?』 Only, as if hostilely, as if distrustfully, and with full curiosity, people just stare at me.—『Who is that fellow—where might he be going?』
『Who would I go to? I don't know. No, I won't go to anyone. Oh, rough and pitiful land that saw my youth, I came here seeking you. No matter how much you've changed, surely we wouldn't fail to recognize each other.』

I won't take the shortcut with the puddle. There's no need for me to hurry anywhere now. There is no one waiting for me with joyful concern (念慮) like in the old days...
『I'll need to find a carriage...』
『That won't work. There are only two carriages here in total; the chief (署長) just took one, and the other one, for some reason, hasn't come out today. No need for that, I can carry it myself. Old sir, where are you going?』
『Me? Uh, there must be an inn, right?』
『Of course! There's an absolutely splendid one! <288> It's called K'ŭrimoba (크리모바) Inn.』
『K'ŭrimoba! Is she still alive then?』
『She died long ago. But it's still called that, just like before.』
『So, is her son running it?』
『Nope. Iwanobŭ (이와노브) runs it, but it's still called the old name. The son died too.』

Following behind this porter, I thought: Beloved land, could even you be entirely alive? Would even a single dog remain?
『Old Sir, where have you come from?』
『Me?... I'm just a traveler... Came from St. Petersburg (彼得堡).』
『The sights must be better where you live, sir. Did you perhaps come on some business?』
『No.』
『That makes sense, the only business here is selling flour. Well then—did you come on some official government business?』
『No, no, I just stopped by briefly for sightseeing. I used to live here before, so I somehow wanted to stop by...』
『Then you probably won't recognize it much. It burned down, there's hardly anything old left...』

We walk into the town. As if starved, I looked for places I used to know. The town had changed completely. The new houses did not greet me warmly (情다이).
『What is the name of this town?』
『It's called Simbilsŭk' (심빌스크).』
『Simbilsŭk'! Ah, is that really so?』
『Yes, it is.』

In Simbilsŭk', there was a priest's house, and staying there was a young maiden, a relative of the priest. Her name was Sashin'ka (사신카). A very simple, short romance suddenly comes to mind. A noisy group with fishing gear and a samovar heads towards the watermill... In the stream (川), whose waters roared hitting the rocky riverbed (石河床), there were many trout (鱒魚). Oh, how beautiful Sashin'ka must have looked, wearing a red kerchief on her golden hair, holding a fishing rod, revealing her lovely legs through the grass! We sat quietly, watching our floats. And so we waited for the call, 『Time for tea (茶)!』
Back then, the samovar took forever to boil. My Sashin'ka had already been heating the samovar with pinecones for some time. Because she hated worms, I used to bait the hook for her. Oh, how beautiful she was then, that Sashin'ka.........
『Eek, it ate it again... Could you put another one on for me? <289>』
『Of course, of course.』

I went over to her side and tried to bait the worm over her shoulder. But the worm would straighten itself rigidly, then curl up squirming, refusing to cooperate. Sashin'ka turned her head, looked up at me from below, and said,
『Put it on quickly.』
『You little rascal, why won't you go on nicely?』

I sat down beside her. Looking at her face from the side, I thought,
『What would happen if I kissed this girl right here...』
Our eyes met. Perhaps she sensed my wicked thoughts. She blushed. Not long after that, I did manage to bait the worm, but I didn't go back to where my own fishing rod lay. Right beside Sashin'ka, not far away, I lingered near her neck.
『Go away. Your float is twitching.』
『I'm not going to go... I won't go...』
『Why?』
『I can't leave your side...』

She was silent. Head bowed, mouth shut. She didn't tell me to go away.
『Alexandra Viktorovna (알렉산도라 윅트로브나)!』
『Yes? What is it?』
『Try to guess what I'm thinking...』
『Am I some kind of magician? How would I know what you're thinking...』
『If you knew what I was thinking right now, you would surely be angry...』
『Who can forbid what others think...』
『Do you know what I'm thinking?』
『I don't know. What is it?』
『You'll get angry...』
『Just say it anyway!』
『Have you ever loved anyone?』
『I don't know about such things.』
『What about now?』
『It's the same.』

She turned as red as a peony (杜丹).
『Then I...』
『Well?』
『I am in love...』
『With whom?』
『Guess?』
『I don't know...』
But she herself blushed more and more, lowering her head further and further. I lay down on the grass right beside Sashin'ka. <290>
She didn't move away. Chewing on a blade of grass she had plucked, I myself was tormented by the irresistible desire to kiss Sashin'ka.
I let out a sigh.
『Why are you doing that?』
『Judge for yourself...』

The girl blushed again. Let happen what may... I got up, leaned over, and kissed Sashin'ka. The girl covered her face with both hands and sat there silently (黙黙히). I kissed her once more, then asked softly,
『Yes or no? (얘쓰 오아, 노오?)』
『Yes! (얘쓰!)』 Sashin'ka whispered, barely audible.
『Lower your hands!... Look at me!』
『I don't want to.』

Just like that, Sashin'ka didn't budge... Sitting beside her, I buried my head in her lap. Her hand, gently placed on my head, slowly stroked it...
『The samovar is boiling!』
Sashin'ka, as if suddenly awakened from sleep, jumped up with a start and dashed towards the watermill. There, we sat together again and drank tea (茶). Even so, we didn't look at each other. Both of us were afraid to look at each other. Late at night, entering the town, when I said goodbye at the priest's gate (門어구) and helped Sashin'ka down from the carriage, I looked into her face for the first time. It was a bewildered, anxious (念慮스러운) face. Her hand, extended to me, was trembling. And I could keenly feel her barely responding to my handshake. After that, day after day, eagerly hoping to meet Sashin'ka, I walked past the priest's gate. As the days went by, my affection for her deepened and grew stronger, but she seemed to have submerged herself in water; neither her shadow could be seen nor any news heard. Sometime later, I learned that Sashin'ka had gone to Simbilsŭk' the very next day. Because a telegram had arrived saying her father had passed away...
After that, I never met Sashin'ka again. Where is she? Surely she must have married the priest. By now she must be the priest's wife... She too must be over forty already...

『There was a priest named Nik'orai (니코라이), is he still alive?』
『He died.』
『Then his house?』
『It burned down—his house was over there... right where that monopoly bureau (專賣所) is now...』

The building is new, but the stone gate (石門) remains as it was. As I gazed quietly at the gate, it suddenly seemed <291> as if the young and lovely Sashin'ka, wearing her red kerchief—just like when she went to the watermill—would run out from inside the gate, blush, and ask,
『Do you remember when we caught trout (鱒魚) at the watermill?』
A servant came out of the monopoly bureau and began knocking the wax seal (封臘) off a bottle's neck (甁ㅅ부리) against the stone pillar...
『Why are you doing that... Old fellow (老兄), this isn't the place for you to do such things...』
『What business is it of yours?』
『Indeed, it's true... There's no need to tell that man that Sashin'ka stood at this gate 20 years ago. My relationship with Sashin'ka probably has nothing to do with him.』

The temple (寺院) still retained its old appearance. As before, lush white birches (白樺) surrounded it. White-billed birds (白嘴鳥) had built nests in the trees, and their noisy cries echoed throughout the town. Just like a merchant's wife at the market. They wouldn't even leave a sentimental traveler, excited by memories, in peace. The bell for the all-night vigil (徹宵祭) rings out. Tomorrow is Sunday. It's the same bell sound I used to hear in the old days. The tolling ding-dong sound evokes in one's soul the sorrow that once gone, one can never return, making one think how short human life is and how human affairs are entirely transient (常中)... Again, I remember going to the temple to see Sashin'ka... Back then too, the bell rang just like this, but then, the end of life was not yet in sight. So its ringing was quite different from now.

『Well, we're here...』
Alone in a welcome place. Desolate and vast, like death.
Only a clock ticks lazily on the dimly lit wooden veranda (툇마루). The memory of kissing Sashin'ka at the watermill recedes further and further away. It doesn't stop. It doesn't cease. From the window, the police tower is visible. Everything is as it was. Even the correct greyish color is still yellowish. It must not have burned. It couldn't have burned.
『Come in!』
『Terribly sorry to bother you, but could you please show me your residence certificate (居住證明書)?』
『Ah, the certificate, you mean... This is an indefinite travel permit (旅行券). I can travel anywhere, anytime, without limit.』
『Security (警戒) is extremely tight here these days.』
『Is it that strict here too?』
『Yes, it is. Since that incident happened, we haven't been allowed to let anyone stay without a travel permit.
Saying this boastfully, gesturing with his hands, a Cossack (코삿코) came... So, old fellow (老兄), did you get beaten too?』
『Of course, I did. Beaten to death! <292>』
「His spirits are sky-high (氣高萬丈). He looks very satisfied.」
「So, this beating?」
「It's all settled (平定) now. The new chief (署長) is a very strict gentleman, truly a good chief.」

I couldn't understand what kind of voice it was. I waved my hand dismissively. The official (管店 - likely meaning 管家/official, or maybe 店主/innkeeper involved?) left. I sat blankly under the window for a while, then went out into the street. There is a bridge (橋) on a street overgrown with hemp (*麻). Below that street, a silvery stream winds its way; its name is "Bulbisŭk'a" (불비스카 - likely a local stream name). On the hill beyond that street should be the house where we lived, but I'm afraid even to go look at it. My heart might just shrivel up. I stopped walking on the bridge. I couldn't even breathe. Over the bridge railing, the middle of the ditch is visible. There, my younger brother was waging war with the hemp (*麻). He was cutting down the hemp (*麻) with a wooden sword. The memory of a boy with mocking eyes, plump and nervous, comes back.
「Mojiya! What are you doing there?」 (Модя? A Russian diminutive?)
「Waging war!」
「Lunch is ready, come here!」
「Can't, the enemy is pursuing!」

It seems like yesterday. Where is that boy now? Could the boy who played war games with hemp (*麻) in the countryside really be the Mojiya who was killed near *Ko'o (**고오 부근)? It's unbelievable! I let out a sigh, bowed my head deeply, and dragged my weary feet. Climbing up the hill, thankfully, everything remained as it was. Neither flame nor war had touched this place so precious to my memories. Look, there's that fence—ah, the forsythia is so overgrown! The windows are barely visible. Someone is playing the piano there. Stopping across from it, I listened intently. It sounded like an old, broken piano. We used to have one like that in our house back then. Hearing that sound, I suddenly felt like I had returned to the old days of my youth, and it seemed as if Mother was playing that piano. And I am thinking about Sashin'ka, whom I kissed at the watermill yesterday. What is she playing? Ah—right, it sounds like some old tune I know.
Then the wind blows. What tune could that be? Ah, right, that's "The Maiden's Prayer" (처녀의 기도). Right, that's it... Closing my eyes and listening, the twenty years that separated me from my youth instantly vanished. I felt as if I were still a university student, returned home for the summer vacation (暑中休暇). Relatives are gathered, the house is bustling (득실득실한다). It's just after having drunk plenty of tea (茶) in the middle courtyard (中廷). Father, holding a cigarette (卷煙) in his mouth, sits beside the already cooled samovar, reading the newspaper. Mother is playing the piano. My rival, Pogoyabŭrensk'i (보고야브렌스키), a theology student who admired Sashin'ka, has come and is pestering me to go swimming in the Volga. He was always preparing his proposal (申堤) to marry Sashin'ka. He had consulted me about it. While we were swimming <293>, we talked only of Sashin'ka. He took off one boot and tapped the sole of the shoe (구쓰).
「Getting married isn't like buying shoes (구쓰).」
「True!」
「So, what do you think?... How does she seem to you?」
「Who?」
「Why, Sashin'ka, of course—」
「Hmm, I haven't really thought about it.」
「I think she's devastatingly beautiful! I'd regret it even if I gave my all for her. I just don't know whether it's right to speak now, or if I should postpone it until graduation. I'm just worried someone else will snatch her away. Because she's devastatingly beautiful...」
He took off the other boot, tossed it aside, and jumped backwards off the raft into the water.
「I've decided. Tomorrow, I will definitely propose.........」

So, that man must have come today too, tempting me to go swimming and wanting to talk about Sashin'ka. Crazy fool! He doesn't even suspect that at the watermill yesterday, his Sashin'ka was lost irrevocably.
「Hey, pal. Aren't you going swimming?」
「Did you propose?」
「No, not yet. What's the rush?」
「That won't do. By the way, do you believe Sashin'ka loves you?」
「Her?」
Pogoyabŭrensk'i nodded vigorously, his eyes wide, then slapped my shoulder.
「The beautiful Sashin'ka is already mine—」

I felt both amused and angry. Leaving aside other things, I felt nauseated (욕지긔) primarily for Sashin'ka's sake. So I wanted to shout, "Yesterday we kissed each other. And Sashin'ka said 'yes' to me."
「Hey, pal, go away! I don't want to go swimming. And about Sashin'ka, don't face a crushing defeat. No matter how confident you are, it's no use!」
「What do you mean by that?」
「Well, just wait and see.」
「What am I supposed to wait and see? What will you do if I get her consent?」
「Stop talking nonsense—. Sashin'ka is already my wife (안해)...」
「Ah, this is serious! (이것 큰일 낫군?)」
「Go quickly! If you don't go, I'll slap your face (빰따군이)!」
「Ah!... This is serious! (이것 큰일낫군!)」

Where is that Pogoyabŭrensk'i now? Perhaps he married Sashin'ka. <294> Perhaps he became a priest. But did she tell her husband about the incident at the watermill? The piano stopped playing (울름). I returned to my senses, desperately wanting to go into that house and see all the changes. Who lives in this house now, who plays the piano, and what have the dining room, parlor, and study become? However, just barging in without warning and saying,
「Please let me see the house. I spent my youth here once. So now, seeing this house, I unbearably want to return to my youth.」 felt somehow... what did I do yesterday? (어재 무얼 하엿다 - This phrase seems out of place, perhaps meaning 'it felt awkward/presumptuous' or is a fragment).

I couldn't quite make up my mind. After circling the house several times, I slipped into a side alley and peeked over the fence into the yard (뜰). In this yard, I once picked and ate plump, firm fennel (茴香) berries from the tree. This is also the yard where Mother used to make jam (쨤) and share the foam with my siblings. There were many good hiding places there among the forsythia and strawberry bushes. I often read good books and thought amidst them.
My self from that time seemed so small and insignificant to my present self. Ah, life passes by in the meantime. You*, you are still as small (微小) and powerless as you were back in those distant old days. No, you have become even more insignificant and worthless than before. Why? Because you no longer have the old confidence in your own strength, nor even the hope of seeing happiness in the future.

Carrying dejection (悒鬱) and sorrow in soul and body, I returned to the inn. The official/innkeeper (管店) brought the samovar. Not long after, he turned to leave, closed the door, and seemed to be quietly listening for sounds, eavesdropping...
「Everything is the same! Only I am not the me of yesterday... I no longer believe in manifestos (檄文) nor get my hands stained with the black ink of the mimeograph (등사판)... Hey, proprietor, eavesdropping is useless. Do you suspect I came here to start a revolution again?... They say there's a strict chief in this town...」

It's the same thing again. Early in the morning, a policeman came with a summons (호출장) stating—Appear at the police station today (本日警察署에 出頭할 事).
「Ah, just seeing a summons gives me a headache. But it's better to go from this side than to have them come from that side. Let's go to the police station. Let's see... that strict chief.」

I arrived at the police station. I was led to the deputy chief's office. Contrary to my inner feelings (胸中), I entered with a frown.
「Please sit here. I apologize for asking you to come for no reason. However, I would like to know for what purpose you came here...」
「I have no particular purpose. I just came because I thought of it. Don't I have the right to freely wander wherever my feet take me? <295>」
「A perfectly reasonable thing to say (맛당히 하실 말슴이올시다)... But when do you plan to leave?」
「I haven't thought about that yet.」
「Forgive my curiosity, but you are... aren't you a writer?」
「I am a writer. Unfortunately, I am a writer.」
「I am very happy to make your acquaintance.」
「Are you really happy?」

The deputy chief looked disconcerted.
「I too was once a university student. I was at the university with you. When you were in your graduating year, I was in my third year.」
「Ah! Is that so!」
「Yes, it is. Please have a cigarette. I too was one of those who participated in that commotion (소동)... With you... Perhaps you don't remember, my name is P'enjensŭk'i (펜젠스키).」
「P'enjensŭk'i—Somehow, the name sounds familiar...」
「Yes... Wasn't it I who slapped the secretary's (幹事) cheek!」
「Was that you? (그랫습듸가?)」
「Yes............ It was me! I swear it was!」
「You! I really didn't know...」

The deputy chief eagerly tried to prove to me that he had slapped the secretary's cheek during that commotion. He seemed to have completely forgotten that he was now working at the police station. He became more and more animated, starting to recount the details of the commotion (顚未). His face changed strangely, losing any trace of a policeman's shadow; finding it would be impossible. Perhaps the university commotion was his most cherished memory... I looked at him with unconcealed curiosity and thought. Perhaps he guessed (推察하엿슴이리라) my thoughts.
「Look here, please don't look at me like that—Indeed, I am wearing a policeman's uniform now, but this sort of thing isn't such a big deal...」
And then he brought up the story of the commotion again.

A man with a dog-like, subservient face peeked into the room. Probably a clerk (書記). The deputy chief frowned, knitting his eyebrows together, and shouted sternly.
「Don't enter my room without permission. I'm busy with official matters right now.」
The clerk withdrew (몰러갓다).
「Ah, there were all sorts of people in those days!」
Suddenly, the deputy chief said this and paced the room back and forth, seemingly agitated.
「Perhaps you have torn my heart apart... Do you remember Uliyanobŭ (우리야노부)? The one who was executed? I was in the same class (반) with him!」
「By the way, why exactly did you summon me?<296> Please tell me the reason.」
「Ah, that... Remembering things from youth, from university days, I wanted to see how you had changed... Because I knew you from university days, so...」
「So it was to pay respects, then—」
「Are you angry? Please forgive me with a generous heart! I couldn't bear it, thinking of those times... And since I've been an avid reader of your writings, I wanted to meet you...」

He suddenly shut his lips. Then he turned towards the window and stood motionless... He quickly turned back to look at me, and his face (언골) was clouded. An embarrassed smile played on his lips.
「I won't detain you any further.」
He spoke kindly and let out a sigh. After thinking about something for a moment, he extended his hand. 「Well then, friend (한우님)! May blessings descend upon you... There probably won't be another chance to meet again. Unless...」
「Unless I'm summoned to the police station again?」

He chuckled (失笑). Then, with an embarrassed smile,
「Our lives are short (쩔읍니다). Everything passes away with us...」

I left the police station. For a while, I couldn't sort out my thoughts.
Was it because he, like me, had white hair scattering (헛날리게 되고) on his head, and on the rambling path of life, had lost the end of life in sorrowful (애닯은) bygone days? <297>

**Summary**

A middle-aged narrator returns to his provincial Russian hometown on the Volga River after a long absence, filled with nostalgic anticipation. He finds the town drastically changed by time and fires, recognizing almost no one and feeling alienated. He revisits old haunts, triggering bittersweet memories of his youth, particularly a brief romance with a girl named Sashin'ka and interactions with friends and family, now deceased or gone. The pervasive melancholy of lost time is heightened by an encounter with a former university acquaintance, now a police official, who summons him. This official surprisingly reminisces about their shared rebellious student past, including knowledge of the executed Ulyanov, before reverting to his official duties, leaving the narrator reflecting on the passage of time, changed circumstances, and shared mortality.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, the translation and publication of this Russian story in 1935 Korea is interesting. While depicting a Russian experience, the themes of profound nostalgia for a lost past, alienation in a changed homeland, the bittersweetness of memory, and the confrontation with disillusionment in middle age could strongly resonate with Korean intellectuals and readers experiencing the rapid, often traumatic, changes and losses under Japanese rule. The narrator's feeling of being a stranger in his own hometown, the vanished landmarks, and the sense of a irrevocably lost youth might mirror Korean sentiments about their own cultural and physical landscape transformations. Furthermore, the subtle yet pervasive presence of state surveillance (the police chief, the summons, the innkeeper eavesdropping) could reflect the realities of life in colonial Korea.

故鄕
						치리코프(作), 憑虛(譯)
나를 실흔 기선은, 나의 가슴에 극렬한 鼓動을 일으키면서 내가 저믄 날을 보내인 어떤 족으마한 시골의 부두에 다긔여가고 잇다. 閑愁에 잠긴듯한 다스하고도 고요한 녀름 저녁은 게을리 흔들리는 보을가 강물과 연안의 산들과 강건너 저편 수풀 푸른 내끼인 원경을 스르륵 덥헛다. 나릿한 疲勞 무에라고 말할 수 업는 구슬픈 느낌은, 이 저녁으로부터 꿈결가튼 물얼골로부터, 그 속에 가루 누은 산우의 蒼鬱한 나무 숩의 그림자로부터 산 저쪽으로 뉘엿뉘엿 넘어가는 석양으로부터 적막한 어부의 扁舟로부터 그리고 또 흰 갈멕이와 먼 汽笛으로부터 나의 넉속속이 숨이어 들어온다... 내가 일즉이 낙시ㅅ대를 가지고 헤매기도 하며, 불을 노키도 하며 게(蟹)를 잡기도 하든, 낫 익은 장소가 벌서 보인다. 내가 노상 낙시를 드리고 잇든, 그 바위돌 우엔 누구인지 고기를 낙고 잇다. 이상한 일이다... 내 안젓든 꼭 그 자리에 나 모양으로 안저 고기를 낙고 잇다! 문득 나는 슬픈 생각이 자아치어 눈물이 날 듯 하엿다. 나는 나에게 흰 털억과 줄음살이 잇는 것, 그리고 또 낙시씨(浮子)가 움즉임을 딸아 가슴을 두근거리기도 하며 시방 저 사람이 하고 잇든 고기가 걸리어도 물속으로 뛰어들어 그것을 잡으랴 안흘 것을 생각하기 시작하엿다. 그리하매 나의 심장은 한번 가고 다시 돌아오지 아니할 생애로 하여 압흐기 비롯하엿다... 歎喜를 기대하고 여긔를 왓거든 나를 맛는 것은 비애이엇다 한구비를 돌아 나가니 보을가의 놉다란 연안으로부터 눈에 익은 교회의 둥근 꼭대기와 혹은 초록 혹은 회색의 집웅을 인(戴) 집 한떼가 엿보인다.-나의 눈에는 눈물이 고이엇다... 그 후 이 고을이 두 번이나 可謂 전소하다십히 화재를 격것스니 우리가 살든 녯 집이 그대로 남아 잇슬가? 내가 부모님과 즐겁게 지내든 그 초록색 울타리로 에돌리인 집을 나는 못견듸리만큼 보고십헛다. 아버지도 업다 어머니도 업다. 형제하나 이 세상에는 업다. 하건만 애닯은 회상은<287> 산 것가티 눈압헤 얼른거리엇다. 암만해도 그들이 이 세상에 업다고는 밋기 실혓다. 내가 이 기선을 내려, 저 웅덩이 잇는 지럼길을 거처간다. 그-때 길을 질르랴고 행용 그리로 단이엇섯다.- 언덕을 넘어 두어집만 지나면, 우리집 담이 보인다... 이런 생각이 낫섯다.
