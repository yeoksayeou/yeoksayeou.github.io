Prose Poem - The Disturber of the Garden
						Yi Ŭng-su

Walking along a narrow path overgrown with weeds, passing through some wasteland, there was a small hill facing south. Bathed in the warm spring light of ninety days (90春光), adorned with haze like silver threads sent down by spring angels and goddesses of beauty, and with flower pollen (花粉) for makeup, covered overhead by light white clouds and auspicious clouds (瑞雲) billowing, stood this little garden.

Once, a pure (純潔), elegant (高雅), and beautiful (美麗) maiden lived in this garden, so the neighbors called this hill "the Maiden's Garden."

Indeed, she lived embraced by peace, hope, ideas, happiness, and joy in a very cool villa (舍莊) within the garden.

Thus, alone (단홀로), guarding the secrets of her youth unknown to anyone, not even a soul dared to peek into this maiden's garden. In the courtyard, various flowers and grasses of a thousand forms and charms (千態萬艶) profusely emitted their fragrance (芳香), and above them, the clear and resonant (劉喨한) chorus (코-러스) of bees, butterflies (蜂蝶), and birds alternated noisily.

The clear spring water carrying flower shadows, willow branches drooping like green threads (絲絲綠) into the water, an oriole perched upon them, mesmerized (陶醉) by its own golden face reflected in the water!

Everything seemed to exist for the maiden, the owner of this garden, and to her, everything was peaceful and joyful.<273>

It was at this time.

Even in mild (和暢한) spring weather, there can be harsh rain and wind that scatter (散散이) the flowers, and even in a tranquil (靜寂한) lake, there can be rough waves (激浪).

From between the dark (컹컴하고) and damp (직직한) thickets (雜林) a short distance from this garden, a young man, withered (枯槁) in appearance and haggard (憔悴한) in complexion, emerged, slouching (어슬렁어슬렁). Leaning on his wound-filled cane, he walked with weak, listless steps toward the maiden's flower garden, did he not?

His hair hung down disheveledly (蓬蓬하게), tangled (헐클리고); his face was pale (창백하고) and filled with melancholy (憂鬱), a sight that would make anyone recoil in fear (공포). All the young man (산아이) possessed were worn-out clothes, a cane, and a tattered, sewn-up (뀌여진) old book tucked under his arm; this was his entire fortune. In his mended book, the following song could be found:

"Darkness's (暗黑) Illegitimate Child (私生兒)"

Oh! I hate to see the sky
Will there never be a day when that arrogant blue sky shatters?
Occupying half my field of vision (眼界) and the world
Above my head, staring disgustingly (징글스례) at me
Mocking the smallness of my body
Spitting upon (춤뱃고) the worthlessness of my life<274>
Rebuking (질책하고) the darkness of my heart
Offering light, yet only showing ugly (醜한) reality
Oh—is there no way to avoid that blue sky?

Thus I enjoy rainy days and cloudy (호린), dark (暗黑) nights
On rainy days, that arrogant sky weeps
On cloudy, dark nights, he who boasts of light becomes blind
Seeing the weeping sky and the blind sky
I possess (갓노라) the victor's (승리자) sneer (조소), having succeeded in my revenge (복수)

But what can I do? There are more clear days
So I often enjoy the forest depths
Finding only my own image there within
Otherwise, I hang my head listlessly
Glancing around with fearful eyes like a thief
Seeking a dark land, heading towards a dark land

Oh, quickly cover my 5-foot body with earth<275>
Let me return to that land that is eternally sky-less
Oh—I hate to see that sky

The young man was without laughter or words; not even a hair's breadth (터럭만치도) of cheerfulness could be found.

However, he certainly possessed some hidden charm (매력), a charm derived precisely from his pale and melancholic appearance, something seemingly obtainable only from paleness (蒼白) and melancholy (우울).

He stood outside the garden's hedge (生籬) and, spotting the maiden tending (다르고) the flowerbeds inside, addressed her with a single phrase.
"I am terribly cold. I am one who seeks darkness. I am terribly cold. And weary. May I rest my legs just for a moment before I go on?"
She was a woman of warm and compassionate nature. As she gazed steadily at the man's appearance, hot (뜩어운) teardrops rolled down (구을고하야) her eyes, and finally, the sympathy of a gentle and beautiful (柔美한) woman was moved.

And also, the woman was drawn to the charm emanating from the man's paleness.

Thus, the woman inevitably let the man inside the gate and took him to her clean room to care for (介抱) him.

It is human nature to long for (동경하고), find novelty in, and cherish (애호하는) opposition (상반) and contrast. This cheerful and optimistic (낙천적인) maiden felt an inexplicable attraction (引力) to the young man's paleness (蒼白), melancholy (우울), silence (無言), and stillness (정숙).

But who could have foreseen it! From the moment he entered, disorder (散亂) and desolation (荒廢) came to the flower garden, and the young man would eventually become the arsonist (방화자) who set fire to the garden, the destroyer of happiness... <276>

Like a ghost (幽靈), like a sleepwalker (몽유환자), the man wandered silently among the flowerbeds and trees, morning and evening, showing no intention of leaving the garden. Some days he lay on the grassy lawn gazing at the sky all day; some days he did not rise from his bedchamber all day; some days he sat all day with his head bowed at his desk. And the woman, without realizing it, was drawn into this man's wings, breathing in the same atmosphere (분위기) as him. Her heart grew anxious, her eyes were downcast, her breath (호흡) became rapid (갑버젓으며), her movements (동작) slowed, and her face became quiet. And eventually, she ended up revealing all the secrets of her maiden kingdom to the man.

In the meantime, the flowerbeds of this garden became overgrown with weeds, blurring the distinction between flowers and grass. Fallen leaves settled in the spring, clouding the water. The paths between the beds and among the trees became obscured. Day by day, the garden grew wilder, transforming into a site of ruins (폐허) where the former glory and splendor could no longer be found. Scars began to appear, layered one upon another, on the walls (壁) of the neat villa (舍莊). One day, the maiden sat alone by the spring, lost in reminiscence.

"That day was truly an unlucky day (惡日) for me. Perhaps I had an ominous dream the night before I let him into this garden! But it can't be helped. I can only pity and grieve (설어할) my own character, unable to step outside being a 'Daughter of Oriental Ethics (東洋綸理의 딸),' regarding (녁이고) it pitifully (민망이)."

As she thought this, watching the white clouds drift across the sky, trying to glimpse the wings of past happiness and hope fluttering among them, and dreaming of such days to come, she heard the soft rustling (자작자작) sound of footsteps above her. Turning around, she saw the usual (例의) gloomy young man standing there, observing her back with his net-like gaze (眼光).

Letting go of her fantasies among the white clouds, the maiden strove to erase the unpleasant (불쾌한) look from her face. She went to stand beside him, put her arm around his waist, and they disappeared (살어저) into the villa.

For some time after that, a relatively settled (단락한) life continued. But strangely, one night, the maiden secretly slipped out of the garden<277> without the man noticing and fled somewhere, directionless (지향없이).

Several hours after she left, the man emerged from the door, dragging his old cane (短杖), and followed after her.

After a long time, the garden of youth lay desolate and empty, only the traces of the two figures' footsteps, handprints, and breath remained within, poignantly evoking old memories (녯기억).

It was a hazy moonlit night. Spring night—a beautiful name, but for the man and woman who had turned their backs on this garden of peace, it was instead a terribly sorrowful night.

As the man slowly climbed over the small hill blocking (가루막휜) the path ahead, there lay a great river!

Like a great serpent (大蛇) one might see in the age when ancient (太古) snake-dragons (蛇龍類) thrived, or like the Milky Way where the Cowherd and Weaver Maid met on the seventh day of the seventh month, a single river flowed, whispering of eternity, its surface furrowed like a silver field (銀田) in the moonlight.

"A good place."

The young man muttered, descending to the embankment (坊築) there. He gazed steadily at the brilliant sparkle of the jewel-like night dew settling to dream a sweet (甘密) spring dream on the grass, and at the red and yellow flowers embroidering the grassy carpet, whispering to himself:

"Grass is the dew's boudoir (閨房) and bedroom.
But dawn is not far off.
Dawn is the time when dew trembles (戰慄)."

He inhaled the clear fragrance (晴香) radiating (放散하여오는) from all around the moonlit riverside adorned with flowers (花月江畔). Immersing himself in the hazy (朦膿한) moonlight descending from the heavenly palace (天宮), unfolding soundless (無音) music of five strings and six pipes (五絃六律) and the fairy dance (仙舞) of Xi Wangmu, he moved (옴겻음니다) with a slow pace (步調). But the maiden's shadow was still nowhere to be seen. On the riverbank hill stood three or four huge, seemingly century-old weeping willows, and from their branches hung two pairs of swing (鞦韆) ropes.<278>

"The riverside is lonely (孤寂하고), and the spring night is sweet (담니다). 'Deep fallen night (落夜沈沈) in the swing courtyard, sounds faint and fine (聲細細) from singing pipes and pavilions (歌管樓台),' whispered Su Dongpo (蘇東坡) of the spring night (春宵) precious as a thousand gold pieces (千金같은)."

It seemed as if the swings awaited the warm wrists of the village maidens who would return tomorrow to grasp them.

Occasionally, the "three quintessential sounds (三絶聲) of midnight (夜半)" – the sound of dogs barking (犬吠聲), the sound of pounding clothes for washing (禱衣聲), and the sound of cocks crowing (鷄鳴聲) – came alternately from the distant village (遠村).

Even then, the young man could not find the maiden's shadow.

Having no other choice, the young man broke off a willow branch and fashioned a flute with his knife. With it, he played the melody (곡조) he always hummed (콧노래), ever so sorrowfully (구슬프게).

It was a song the maiden knew well, so perhaps hearing its sound (음향), the mistress of the villa would come looking for him...

The sound of the spring flute, said to disturb (동떠나게한다는) even village widows (村寡婦), gained the young man's exquisite touch (妙音) and sounded even more clear and bright (淸朗하게). Like weeping, like complaining (如泣如訴), like yearning (如慕), like crying out (如鳴), its lingering sound (餘音) reverberated (溺溺하는), like the sound of the vertical flute (퉁소) in the *Red Cliff Rhapsody* (赤壁賦), enough to break (끈어 놓앗음니다) a person's winding heart (九曲肝臟).

One minute, two minutes, five minutes, ten minutes passed.

Then the young man spotted a black shadow moving indistinctly (음즛음즛음즛) under the willows on the opposite bank of the river. He realized it was the dim (어슴푸레하고), ghost-like (幽靈같은) shadow of the maiden, standing quietly with her head bowed, as if intoxicated (陶醉하듯), dead drunk (酪酊한듯), or praying.

He could also see the hem of her skirt fluttering gracefully (飄逸하게) like fairy clothes (仙裳).

The young man stopped playing the flute.

"How did she cross over there? Where is the bridge?" he wondered. Just then, from the other side, she pulled out a boat from under the willows and began rowing towards his side. The maiden silently took the young man aboard and rowed (밋*러저 갓음니다) back to the other side of the river.<279>

In the boat, the young maiden said,
"You too left that garden empty? Are we finally to abandon both the blessed (밧을) spring garden of life, the paradise of youth? I came here wondering if I could disappear from this world alone. How could you change my character, my environment, my state of mind so frighteningly, at such a sharp angle? I find spring tiresome. I find the world tiresome."

"Let's leave the world," he replied. "I was already dead in spirit."

The two exchanged (밧꾸엇음니다) an embrace and a kiss, quite coldly (冷然이).

The next morning, the corpses of the man and woman hung (듸리웟음니다) from the willow branches.

On one side of the river, two pairs of swing ropes hung down... <280>

**Summary**

This prose poem tells the tragic story of a pure and happy maiden living in an idyllic garden whose world is irrevocably altered by the arrival of a dark, melancholic, and destitute young man. Drawn to his contrasting nature, she lets him into her sanctuary. His presence gradually brings decay to the garden and despair to the maiden. Feeling trapped and changed, she flees, but he follows. They meet by a river, express their shared weariness with the world, and ultimately commit suicide together by hanging themselves from riverside willows. From a colonial history perspective, the story is interesting for its allegorical potential. The "Maiden's Garden" could symbolize a pure, pre-colonial Korea or an idealized inner world, while the melancholic young man, an "illegitimate child of darkness" who hates the light (perhaps representing imposed modernity or the despair under occupation), acts as a disruptive force leading to destruction and death. The maiden's lament about being a "Daughter of Oriental Ethics," unable to escape her fate, hints at the constraints of tradition and possibly the helplessness felt under colonial rule. The pervasive themes of gloom, decay, and suicide reflect a pessimistic mood potentially prevalent during that era.

한대 이 동산에는 한 純潔하고 高雅하고 美麗한 처녀가 살고 잇엇는대 해서 近邊 사람들은 이 동산을 『처녀의 동산』이라고 불럿음니다.
실로 彼女는 평화와 희망과 사상 그리고 행복과 환희에 쌔여 동산속에 잇는 아조 淸凉한 舍莊에 살고 잇엇든 것이외다.
그리하야 단홀로 아모도 몰으게 청춘의 비밀을 간직하고 잇는 이 처녀의 동산을 어나누 감이 엿보는 사람조차 없어 뜰에는 千態萬艶의 各色花草가 芳香을 馥郁하게 날리고 그 우로 蜂蝶과 새들의 劉喨한 『코-러스』가 요란스러이 交替하고 잇엇을 뿐임니다.
꽃그름자를 실고 흘러가는 맑은 샘물 물속에 絲絲綠으로 내려듸리인 버들가지 그 우에 앉은 꾀꼬리가 물 속에 빛이인 자기의 황금빛얼골에 스스로 陶醉하는 姿態!
그 모든 것이 이 동산의 주인인 처녀를 위해 잇는 것 같아야 그 녀자에게 모든 것은 평화롭고 즐거웟음니다.<273>
