A Journey of Three Thousand *Ri* · 15 Years of Flying—My Reflections as an Aviator
						Sin Yong-swa
"Opening the window, the sky is so high! If only I could fly freely like a bird into that boundless, high, wide, and clear blue sky!"
This was the image I constantly daydreamed about while sitting in the classrooms of the Law College (法學專門學校) and Hwimun School (徽文學校).
Childhood hopes dominate a person's life.
The hope I had cherished continuously from my middle school days, I finally realized during my time at the professional school.
Thus, I ultimately became an aviator. It has already been 15 years since I started pulling the airplane I cherish more than the clothes I wear or the house I live in, more than anything else, out of the hangar and flying into that blue sky where clouds and fog drift morning and evening, and the sun and moon travel. How many thousands of *ri* has that flight distance been!
However, saying 15 years is easy, but during this time, I experienced much sorrow, joy, anguish, and effort. Thinking back on those past days now,<138>
My hometown is a place called Sach'ŏn-ri, Koch'ang-gun, Chŏlla Province (全羅道 高敞郡 沙川里). When I was a young boy in the countryside, after finishing Hŭngdŏk Public Normal School (興德普通學校), the heart of the child who played far away on stilts began to pound towards the road to Seoul. Thus, finally carrying my books (笈を負하고), I came up to Seoul at the age of 14. Upon arriving, I entered Hwimun High School (徽文高等普通學校) in Kyesan (桂山), spent four years wrestling with books, and after finishing middle school, the place I went to as a higher school was the Chŏnsu School (專修學校), the predecessor of the current Law College (法學專門學校). I would study law, stand in court, and save pitiful people crying from wrongful convictions; I would go out into society and become a pillar of society through law. All these hopes and fantasies pressed upon my heart.
Even while attending school as a ruddy-faced youth (紅顔청년), what never left my mind for a moment and excited my head was, as I mentioned earlier,
The fantasy of "an aviator flying through the sky."
Determined to realize this, I finally threw away my Chŏnsu School cap and uniform in the year I turned twenty-one. It was a complete decision.
When I set out to become an aviator, dreaming of being the "King of the Air" (空中王), what my mother said while trying hard to dissuade me is still clear in my ears.
"My child, aren't you the precious son raised by a widowed mother's hand alone since you lost your father at the age of three? I live relying only on you. It's dangerous even on the ground, let alone flying around in the sky; what kind of perilous act is that? Give it up. If you don't give up, I might as well die beforehand."
No matter how much I explained that it wasn't so, my elderly mother, unfamiliar with new scientific civilization, had no reason to understand.
"What if something goes wrong..."
It seemed as though my mother truly feared, seeing in her mind's eye the image of her son crashing in an airplane and meeting a tragic end. However, I ultimately said,
"Grandfather, farewell. <139>
Mother, I will go and return.
In the near future, I will succeed and come back to bow before you."
Thus, bidding farewell to my hometown mountains and rivers, and all friends and relatives, the place I arrived at was
Tokyo. At first, I entered an aircraft factory and became a mere worker, wearing oil-stained clothes, running errands ordered by others, and finding joy even in smelling gasoline (까소링). During that time, I watched others fly, heard rumors about who flew well, and rushed to the aircraft factory as soon as morning broke, only returning to my chilly boarding house at sunset!
A cold lamp in a guest window! Even under this lone lamp (孤燈), the dream woven in my heart every night was solely of becoming a world-famous aviator, flying a fine airplane over Paektu Mountain (白頭山) and the Pacific Ocean (太平洋). Often, I would wake up from such dreams of flying well to the applause and cheers of tens of thousands of spectators.
After being a worker like this for some time, I finally managed to grasp the intricacies (妙理) of airplanes and at last became someone who could board an aircraft.
However, one thing I still remember is that aviation skills in Tokyo at that time were rudimentary. Crashes occurred frequently, and the airplanes, often purchased secondhand from the Ministry of War (陸軍省), were prone to breaking. Many people were injured, and if one managed to gather money to buy an airplane and fly, it would break down after just a few days. Some were damaged even before flying.
How have these airplanes, once so disastrous and primitive, become today? Truly, they show astonishing progress, comparable without the slightest inferiority (조곰도 손색이 업슬만치) to those of the most civilized great powers of the world.
What is astonishing is solely effort, strength, passion.
Thus, during the several years I was there, the money I spent on buying training aircraft and on all sorts of school supplies reached the enormous sum of
140,000 or 150,000 won. Such a tremendous amount of money was the price for becoming an aviator. It was astonishing; the majority of my family's fortune was poured into this. As there is a limit to an individual's financial capacity,<140> and too much money was being spent, my grandfather at home started saying I was acting senile ("노망난 짓을 한다고") and from then on, stopped sending even living expenses, cutting off all school funding entirely.
My hardships became indescribably severe from this point on. However, with the thought, "Should I abandon a man's great ambition just because school funds from home stopped coming?", I resolved to work my way through school. Having made this decision, from the next day, I shouldered an ice cream container and went out onto the streets. I had become an ice cream vendor. That was in Taishō 14 (1925). Setting up 3 won worth at a street corner, I would shout all day long,
"Hey, Ice Cream!"
"Hey, hey, welcome! Ice Cream!" (エイツ, アイス クリーム エイツ, エイツ, イうツシヤイ アイスクリーム - Japanese shouts)
Calculating my wallet in the evening sun, I would easily have 12 or 13 won. If 3 won became 12 or 13 won in a day, that was quite a decent business. In this way, I procured my school fees and made a living. Moreover, there was even a day when I went to a festival (オ察リ) and sold 18 won worth in one day.
Thinking back on those times, another unforgettable event concerns Mr. An Ch'ang-nam (安昌男君), who later passed away after going to Shanghai. Around the time Mr. An was suffering from illness in bed, since we were both studying with the goal of becoming aviators, I helped him by earning money through this "ice cream" business. That Mr. An, now in a different world, alas!
Indeed, Mr. An also suffered greatly from illness, financial difficulties, and lack of school funds. Furthermore, when I was in Tokyo, there was an incident where Mr. An, while practicing flying at Chiba Beach (千葉海岸), made a piloting error, crashed, and miraculously survived (구사일생). That was in May of Taishō 13 (1924). Speaking of crashes, I too, while flying over Kaesŏng (開城), had my engine suddenly fail in mid-air, made an emergency landing, and luckily escaped death. That was in the spring of Shōwa 5 (1930).
The place I fell into then was a barley field. The spectators, who had gathered like a sea of people upon seeing the plane fall, all screamed "Aigu!" thinking I was dead, and fled.
At the moment of the crash, I too thought, "Now I'm going to die." Miraculously, although the airplane was heavily damaged,<141> my body was without a single scratch. Perhaps this might set a new world record for surviving a crash.
Anyway, having entrusted my body to the airplane like this, flying south and north, my beloved wife passed away, leaving two sons behind in this world. An aching heart, still unable to find solace.
And the times I practiced flying were almost always, it could be said,
days with rain and storms. What pride is there, what difficulty is there, in flying in fair winds and good weather? With this thought, I deliberately chose rainy and stormy days for practice, repeatedly undergoing harsh training, and eventually, I believe, managed to obtain the honorable First-Class Pilot and Second-Class Aviation (이등항공상) licenses (免狀). Speaking of storms, last year, for the Chosŏn Ilbo newspaper, I flew my plane through the great floods in the three southern provinces (三南大水災) to the disaster sites. At that time, flying from Taejŏn (大田) to Taegu (大邱), rain poured down from the sky, making it truly hard to distinguish anything nearby (지척을 難辯), and the wind was also severe. However, with the earnest conviction (拳拳한 일념) that if I sacrificed myself, I could save the hundreds of thousands of flood victims in the three southern provinces, I pushed through the pouring rain and flashing lightning.
In any case, I have spent 7 years in Tokyo and 8 years back in Korea, passing 15 long years as an aviator. Finally, what comes to mind is that the need for airplanes and aviators in our society is gradually intensifying. Aviation civilization now almost represents the entire scientific civilization of a country.
Passenger planes carrying guests, mail planes transporting communication materials like postal items! In this era of speed (스피ㅡ드시대) advancing day by day,<142> what is most precious is this airplane. However, it is most lamentable that among Koreans, the enthusiasm for aviation is weak, and the awareness of aviation is scarce.
I sincerely hope that in Korea too,
Establishing Aviation Departments (航空部) in every school above the professional school level to strive for learning aviation should be the top priority. Aviation enthusiasm needs to spread among young students, and there should be many comrades taking the initiative to grasp the propeller (푸로페라), but the current situation cannot but be deplored. Currently, there are barely five Korean aviators actively working, and only about 30 students learning.
For reference, the names of the five aviators are:
◇ First-Class Seaplane Pilot Mr. Chang Tŏk-ch'ang (張德昌씨) (currently in Ōsaka)
◇ Mr. Pak Pong-ji (朴奉趾씨), although it's uncertain if he has taken the First-Class Pilot exam, he is in Tokyo as a Second-Class Pilot.
◇ Mr. Kim Tong-ŏp (金東業씨) is a Second-Class Pilot, working with me at the Kyŏngsŏng airfield.
◇ And the only female aviator, Ms. Yi Chŏng-hŭi (李貞喜양), still a young woman of twenty-five, is a Second-Class Pilot (First-Class licenses are not yet given to women).
Including myself, there are five people in total.
I intend to devote my full energy to the aspiring aviators who will grow in the future. Anyway, recalling the past 15 years of flying here and there, I feel deeply moved (감개무량함을 늣겨진다).<143>

**Summary**

Sin Yong-swa recounts his 15-year journey to becoming one of Korea's pioneering aviators in 1935. Driven by a childhood dream, he abandoned law studies, defied his mother's wishes, and moved to Tokyo. There, he faced rudimentary training conditions, financial hardship (working as an ice cream vendor after family support ceased), and witnessed or experienced near-fatal crashes, including his own miraculous survival. He reflects on the rapid advancement of aviation technology, laments the lack of aviation interest and infrastructure in contemporary Korea, listing the mere five active Korean pilots (including himself and Yi Chŏng-hŭi, the sole female pilot). The article highlights the personal sacrifices (cost, family opposition, loss of his wife) and immense determination required to pursue aviation in colonial Korea. For historians, the article offers a personal perspective on the nascent stage of Korean aviation, the challenges faced by pioneers under Japanese rule (training in Japan, reliance on Japanese systems like the Ministry of War for planes), the socio-economic hurdles, the surprisingly low number of Korean aviators even by 1935, and a glimpse into early flight experiences and dangers. The mention of specific individuals like An Ch'ang-nam and Yi Chŏng-hŭi adds valuable biographical detail to the history of Korean aviation.

航程三千里·飛行15년-나의 飛行家로서의 感懷
						愼鏞瑣
『창을 여니 하늘이 놉기도 하다, 놉고도 넓고 깨긋한 저 無邊蒼空으로 새와 가치 훨-훨- 나라봣스면!』
이것이 내가 法學專門學校와 徽文學校의 교실에 안저 늘 공상하든 영상이엇다.
소년시대의 희망은 사람이 일생을 지배한다.
나는 중학생 시대로부터 계속하여 희망하든 바를 전문학교때에 까지 와서 끗끗내 실현하고야 말엇다.
그리하야 나는 끗끗내 비행가가 되엇다. 내 몸에 입은 옷보다도, 내 몸이 쓰고 사는 집깐보다도 무엇보다도, 가장 애지중지하는 비행기를 격납고에서 끄내어 타고, 구름과 안개가 아츰 저녁으로 흐르며 해와 달이 다니는 저 창공으로 날닌지 이미 15년, 그 비행里數 몃 3천리든고!
그러나 말로 15년 하면 쉬우나, 나는 이동안 슬품도, 깁움도, 고심도, 노력도 만하엇다. 지금 지나간 날을 생각하여 보면<138>
내 고향은 全羅道 高敞郡 沙川里라는 땅이다. 시골에서 조고마한 소년으로 잇슬때 興德普通學校를 마치고 멀니 죽마타든 少童의 가슴은 서울길을 향하야 두군거리기 시작하엿다. 이리하야 끗끗내 笈을 負하고 서울에 올너오기는 14살 때라. 울나와서는 桂山에 잇는 徽文高等普通學校에 드러 4년을 서책과 싸우며 지나다가, 그 중학교를 마치고서 다시 발을 도리켜 상급학교라고 간 곳이 지금의 法學專門學校 전신인 專修學校이엇다. 법률을 배워 법정에 서서 원죄에 우는 불상한 사람을 구해주리라, 사회에 나와서는 법률을 통하야 사회의 기둥이 되리라, 이러한 모든 희망과 공상이 내 가슴을 눌넛든 것이다.
이러케 紅顔청년으로 學窓에 드나들면서라도 일시를 이처지지 안코, 내 머리를 흥분케 하는 것은 오직 앗가 말한,
「하늘을 날려다니는 비행가」
의 공상이라. 긔어히 이를 실현하고저 끗끗내 스몰한살 되든 해에 專修學校 모자와 교복을 집어 던젓다. 완전히 결심한터이다.
내가 「空中王」을 꿈꾸면서 비행가가 되겟다고 나섯슬 때 그때 어머니께서 몹시 말니시며 하는 말삼이 아직까지 귀에 뚜렷하다.
「얘야 너는 세 살 나슬 때에 아버지를 여인 후로 어미 혼자 손에서 길너난 소중한 자식이 아니냐. 나는 너만 밋고 사는데 땅 우에도 위험하거든 하물며 공중으로 나려다닌다니, 그게 무슨 위태한 짓이냐. 그만두어라. 네가 그만두지 안으면 내가 진작 죽든지 하여야, 하겟다」
하심이라. 내가 그러치 안타는 설명을 아모리 하여도 연로하고 신과학문명에 어두운 어머니께서 이를 이해하여 주실 까닭이 업스섯다.
「그러다가 잘못되면 엇절나구...」
어머니의 안전에는 비행기에서 추락하여 참사하는 자식의 모양이 보이시는거나가치 진정으로 두려워하시면서 말슴하엿다. 그러나 나는 끗끗내
「할아버지 안녕히 게십소. <139>
어머니 갓다 단여오리다.
머지안은 압날에 성공하여 다시 슬하에 와서 절하겟습니다」
하고 고향 산천과 朋友親叔을 모다 하직하고 다다른 곳이
東京이라. 처음엔 비행기 공장에 드러가 일개 직공이 되여 기름 무든 옷을 입고 남이 식히는 심부름이나 하면서 「까소링」냄새맛는 것도 낙이라고 지내엇다. 그 사이에 남들이 비행하는 모양도 구경하고, 누가 잘 타더란 소문도 어더 드르며 아츰에 날이 밝기와 가치 비행기 공장에 달려가, 일몰할 때에야, 차듸찬 하숙에 돌아오군! 하엿다.
客窓寒燈! 이 孤燈아래서 일지라도 그때 내 가슴속에 밤마다 매저지는 꿈은 오직 내가 세계적으로 유명한 비행가가 되어, 조흔 비행기를 타고 白頭山 우에도, 太平洋 우으로도 날느는 꿈이라. 이러케 잘 날느다가 수만 군중이 격찬하는 박수와 환성소리에 깨어 이러나군, 나군 하엿다.
이려케 직공으로 얼마 잇다가 그제는 겨우 나도 비행기의 妙理를 解得하여 가지고, 비로소 비행기에 몸을 올려놋는 신세가 되엇다.

이러케 참혹하고 유치하든 비행기가 오늘은 엇더케 되엇는고. 실로 세계의 가장 문명한 열강에 비하여도 조곰도 손색이 업슬만치 놀라운 진보을 보이고 잇다.
놀라운 것은 오직 노력이다, 힘이다, 열성이다.
