Kasil, A Collection of Masterpieces from the Peninsula's New Literary Scene of the Last Twenty Years
						Yi Kwang-su

1
The time was the end of Silla, when Kim Yu-sin was in his prime. In the yard, brightly lit by the autumn sun as if being ironed, stood stacks of rice sheaves, bean sheaves, and buckwheat sheaves. In one corner of the yard was a pile of logs for the winter fuel. Beneath that pile of wood, a beautiful and sturdy girl of about sixteen or seventeen sat perched on a log, gazing towards the southern main road and crying. Just then, a young farmer, carrying a large axe, entered the yard, saw the girl sitting and crying, and stopped abruptly. "Miss, why are you crying?" he asked in a gentle voice. The girl, as if startled, looked up from the main road with tear-filled eyes towards the young farmer, stood up slowly, and said, "The country has summoned my father." Wiping her tears with the ties of her skirt, she turned away as if to hide her crying, revealing her long, braided black hair.
"Summoned by the country?"
"Yes, a government official came earlier and told him to gather at the village office tomorrow morning, and then left."
The young farmer seemed to ponder something. "They say Koguryŏ soldiers are invading Pukhan Mountain Fortress, is that why they're calling him?" He put down the axe there, went to another house and came back. "They say many people have been called." "Damn it, is there ever a day of peace? All the young men are dead, and now they're sending the old men out to die too? When will a world without war ever come?" he said, looking at the girl's weeping shoulders. Without turning her head, the girl asked, "Kasil-ssi, weren't you chosen?" Kasil was the young farmer's name. <192>
"They'll probably call me next spring," he said. "I'm still one year short, so they must be leaving me out for now." Folding his arms, he thought for a moment, then asked, "Where has your father gone?"
"He went into the village. To try talking to the magistrate. He said he'd plead to be excused because he's old, sick, and has only a young daughter left. He went just a while ago. It's about time for him to come back..." she said, looking towards the main road again.
"Will talking do any good? Does the country care about personal circumstances?" he said, picking up the axe and starting to chop firewood from the logs in the pile. The girl, seemingly surprised, opened her tear-soaked eyes wide. "Why are you chopping firewood?" she asked, taking a step closer to Kasil.
"We just finished chopping all our firewood. I thought I'd chop some for the old master since it must be hard for him," he said. With a grunt, he raised his crimson, muscular arms high above his head and brought the axe down with a "Chwae!" sound. A cracking sound followed as the log split, sending pieces of firewood flying sideways. The girl stood blankly, watching Kasil's sun-tanned back bend and straighten, his crimson forearms rise and fall, and the pile of white firewood quickly grow before her. As if struck by a thought, she darted through the brushwood gate. Soon, the girl came back out carrying a large bowl filled with cloudy makgeolli (막걸리; rice wine), waited until Kasil finished chopping the log he was working on, and said, "Have a drink of wine," offering the bowl to Kasil with both hands. Kasil leaned the axe against the log pile, wiped the sweat beads from his forehead with one forearm, and took the bowl with the other hand.
"Where did this wine come from?" he asked, gazing down at the wine with his strong yet gentle eyes.
"There was a little left at the bottom of the jar from the wine we made on the day we harvested the beans, so I drew fresh water and filtered it. I left some for Father to drink..." Wiping her wet hands on her skirt, the girl smiled faintly, seemingly content.
Kasil put the bowl to his lips and gulped it down in one breath, then wiped his mouth with his fist and handed the bowl back to the girl. The girl took the bowl, looked at Kasil intently for a moment, then darted through the brushwood gate and into the kitchen. Kasil watched her run off, gazed at the kitchen door she had entered for a while, then picked up the axe again and resumed chopping firewood. After some time, the girl ran out holding something wrapped in her skirt <193> and stood beside Kasil. Taking the opportunity when Kasil looked back at her, the girl said, "Have some chestnuts. I gathered these fallen nuts myself and buried them. They are king chestnuts!" She scooped up a large handful with her small hands, her fist turning red, and gave them to Kasil. Kasil leaned the axe against his leg and began peeling the chestnut shell with his teeth. The girl also peeled one with her mouth and ate it.
"Father's coming!" the girl exclaimed, dropping the chestnuts wrapped in her skirt onto the ground and running out to the main road to meet him. Kasil turned his head and looked out towards the main road. Under the shade of an old weeping willow, Old Man Sŏl (설녕감), his beard white, walked wearily. Entering the yard, the old man saw Kasil and showed a look of gratitude. "Did you chop the firewood?"
"Yes. After chopping all of ours..." Kasil replied with a shy yet satisfied smile.
The old man sat down on a piece of firewood and let out a long sigh. The girl had already gone into the kitchen and came out with a bowl of wine. "Father, have some wine," she said, giving it to her father.
"Oh. Was there wine left?" he asked, taking the bowl from his daughter. "Give this man a cup too."
"I gave him a bowlful. I saved some for you, Father," the girl said, looking at Kasil. Kasil said, "I had plenty, thank you. Please, drink up. It's still hot if you have more work to do," looking at the old man's seemingly tired face. The old man slowly drank the bowl, sucking the wine clinging to the tip of his upper mustache with his lower lip, then picked up a chestnut that had fallen on the ground and began to peel it. The girl wanted to hear the outcome of her father's visit to the village office today but couldn't ask in front of someone else, hoping Kasil would ask for her. Kasil sensed this, squatted down beside the old man, and asked, "So, did things go well at the village office?"
"No, they didn't. It seems I must leave tomorrow morning."
There was silence for a moment.
The girl couldn't hold back her tears anymore and turned away, burying her face in her skirt. Kasil also lowered his head deeply. The old man, too, lowered his head, then suddenly looked up at his daughter crying with her back turned, and said to Kasil, "Actually, I was planning to look for you anyway." He gazed intently at Kasil and continued, "As you know, if I leave, this young child will be left alone. It breaks my heart for her! Her mother died young, her older brothers all went off to war and died... If I <194> leave now, how can I hope to return alive? If I don't die in battle, I'll die of illness, and if not from illness, won't I die of old age? I too was drafted at twenty and only returned home at thirty to find my parents had both passed away... but what's the use of talking about that? Anyway, there’s no chance I’ll return alive this time... She is the only flesh and blood I have left. How can my heart rest leaving her behind?" The old man struggled to hold back his tears. The girl collapsed onto the firewood pile, sobbing. Kasil also cried. The old man blew his nose and composed himself. "But it's all fate, what can be done? ... I see that you are a good man! Therefore, take my daughter as your wife. And take this house, earn a living, and reside here. With the rice paddies, fields, and the woodlot, the two of you can make a living without worry if you work hard. Do this." He stood up, took the arm of his daughter weeping on the firewood pile, helped her up, and said, "Child, go inside and prepare dinner. Catch a chicken, make plenty of side dishes, and filter some more wine. Kasil will eat dinner with us too, let's talk for the last time." The girl stood up, wiping her tears with both hands as she went inside. The old man watched his daughter go in, turned back, and sat down beside Kasil again. "Kasil! Will you do as I say?" he asked, patting Kasil's sweat-soaked back with his hand. Kasil looked up at the old man, hesitated as if finding it difficult to speak, then simply but firmly said, "I am overwhelmed with gratitude!"
The old man stood up, picked up the axe lying beside Kasil, and began to chop a log. When Kasil said,
"I'll chop it," the old man replied, "Stay put. This is the last time I'll get to do this," chopping with "Hssi," "Hssi" sounds. Though old, his former skill remained. He couldn't chop as powerfully as Kasil, but he did it more skillfully. After chopping the entire log, he gave the axe to Kasil, saying, "Eh, chopping wood for a while gives me energy," wiping his sweat. "You know those two *maljigi* (말지기; unit of land area) of rice paddies over the hill? I made all that with my own hands. I was planning to spread some new soil there this fall and clear another *maljigi* next to it, but now I can't. You should do it, starting tomorrow. And move that cowshed over to that side," he said, smiling cheerfully as if without a care. Then, suddenly, a worried look appeared. "It's a pity I won't get to see the wedding, but <195> after you've threshed all this rice, invite the villagers, pick a good day, and have a proper feast," he said, showing a look of great displeasure. Kasil merely listened without any reply.

2
The next day, waking at the first cock's crow before dawn, the girl hulled rice in the mortar, drew water, caught a chicken, and cooked rice. The previous night, she had stayed up late sewing a set of padded winter clothes for her father, then briefly fell asleep lying beside him, only to be woken by the first rooster. The father, repeatedly touching his daughter sleeping beside him, had barely slept at all.
By the time the old father and young daughter sat facing each other, eating rice mixed in chicken soup, the eastern sky was already brightening. Before sunrise, a mounted official, carrying a bow and flashing a sword, had ridden around shouting, "Soldiers, come out!" The girl didn't even clear the table; she packed her father's bundle of clothes and mended the holes in his worn traditional socks (보선). There was no time for tears while preparing for the journey. While his daughter packed, the father fed the cow, cleaned the yard, did his usual morning chores, checked the farming tools, the cow, the chicken coop, and the grain stacks, and even cleared the weeds from the path to the well his daughter always used.
The sun rose. The rooftops were covered in silvery frost. Crying sounds came from the village. Roosters crowed from all directions, greeting the morning sun, and dogs barked loudly. Finally, it was time to leave. The father shouldered his bundle, stood down in the yard, and stroked his crying daughter's hair, touching her cheek. "Don't worry about anything. Kasil is a good person, so marry him, have many sons and daughters, and live well. Listen well to your husband and work hard, then you are my daughter," he said, stepping out the gate. The daughter clung to her father's sleeve, weeping.
At that moment, a large figure came running over the hill in front, bathed in golden sunlight. It was Kasil. Kasil wore straw shoes (집세기) and leggings (감발), his trousers hitched up high, carrying a small bundle (찜짐). Arriving at the gate, he bowed to the old man and said, "I will go in your place. They say I'll be back in a year." Steam rose from his face.
"How can you go?" the old man asked in astonishment.
"How can an elderly person like you go to war now? So, since yesterday, I decided I would go instead," he said, bowing again and starting to run off. The girl grabbed Kasil's hand <196> and asked, "Are you going to war in Father's place?"
"Yes," Kasil replied, looking down at the girl's upturned face. The girl pressed her tear-streaked face against Kasil's chest. "Then please go. I will repay this kindness until the day I die. Please go!" she said, looking at Kasil's face once more.
The old man, knowing he couldn't dissuade Kasil from his decision, gave the bundle of clothes he had been carrying to Kasil.
"I will never forget your kindness, even in death. Then, go and return quickly. Trust me as your father-in-law. Please, please return safely."
And so, Kasil went off to war.
He entered the village, joined the several hundred men drafted as soldiers, and was led by several dozen cavalrymen to the capital, Seoul. Along the way, they met men drafted from various other villages. By the time they crossed Ch'isullyŏng pass, their numbers exceeded a thousand. On the mountain slopes, old men, wives, and mothers stood in white lines, pointing, calling out, stamping their feet, and wailing as they watched their fathers or sons pass by.
When Kasil entered the East Gate of Seoul, the sun was already settling on the western mountain ridge, and from the eight hundred and eight temples, the evening bells rang out, boom, boom. The citizens of the capital had all come out to the roadside to watch the drafted soldiers arrive. Only when the distant figures were out of sight did they finally reach the military barracks in front of Punhwangsa Temple.
Kasil reported to the commanding officer and entered a room. Inside the large room, about ten *kan* (칸; traditional unit of space) wide, over a hundred men sat packed like bean sprouts, talking amongst themselves—some with acquaintances from the same hometown, others with strangers. Kasil sat alone in a corner, feeling a sense of fear about going to war, yet also happy at the thought that when he returned this time next year, he would marry the person he had long yearned for and live happily.
Soon, the sound of music drifted from somewhere. People stood up and looked out the windows. A bright light could be seen to the southwest. One person looking out the window exclaimed, "That's the palace! Where His Majesty resides!" Hearing this, those who had only heard the word 'palace' but never seen one rushed over. "Huh? Which one is the palace?" they asked, craning their necks through the crowd and standing on tiptoe.
"There, the place with lots of lanterns lit, that's the palace, Imhae Palace (림해궁)! <197>" someone explained as if they knew well.
Kasil also squeezed into the crowd and looked out. Countless lanterns, perhaps thousands, hung in the air like fireflies, and around the center, something like a large torch could be seen.
"They've lit so many lanterns," someone said. "It would be nice to thresh grain under such bright lights," said another. "It would be great to set up a wrestling match there," chimed in a third.
Among them, a man who had served as a soldier in Seoul for a long time scoffed at their ignorant remarks.
"What are you people talking about? His Majesty is holding a banquet right now with all the court officials. Generals Yongch'un and Yu-sin will lead us to Nangbi Fortress tomorrow, and this feast is to wish them victory and a safe return," he explained.
The sounds of drums, pipes, flutes (져소리; likely refers to a type of flute like daegeum), and gongs could be heard intermittently. The bright, full moon of the fifteenth day of the ninth month hung over Namsan mountain like a round sheet of ice, and Panwŏlsŏng Fortress and Hwangnyongsa Temple appeared as large shadows in the moonlight.
One by one, people moved away from the windows and collapsed in various corners, using bundles as pillows. Some, exhausted from the day's long walk, were already snoring loudly. These peasants, like children forced to leave their homes, wives, children, fields, paddies, and cows to go die in war, thrashed their limbs and snored, dreaming of the homes they left yesterday. Meanwhile, the music from the Imhae Palace banquet, sometimes faint, sometimes loud, sometimes stopping, sometimes resuming, drifted on the moonlight and seeped through the cracks in the walls. Kasil, too, couldn't sleep for a while at first, but exhausted from chopping firewood all day yesterday and walking all day today, he fell asleep so soundly he wouldn't have known if he were tied up and carried away.
When the moon was nearly setting over the western mountains, temple bells rang out simultaneously from all directions. Among them, the bell from Punhwangsa Temple, right next to the barracks, shattered the dreams of the sleeping soldiers.
Bugle calls and trumpet sounds arose within the barracks. The sleeping soldiers, like bees whose hive had been shaken, poured out of the various rooms and gathered in the courtyard. In the center of the courtyard, bows and quivers were piled high like mountains, and colorful flags fluttered in the torchlight.
At sunrise, about a thousand soldiers, formed into the first contingent, left through the South Gate and headed west. There were mounted soldiers and carts carrying supplies. All the soldiers carried bows and quivers, and some carried large spears. Kasil also carried a large bow and quiver <198> and wore a dyed military uniform. The farmers who, until yesterday, had peacefully lived holding hoes, sickles, and wood-chopping axes, had in one morning turned into soldiers carrying bows and swords, going off to kill people.
"Where do you think we're going?" someone behind Kasil asked, seemingly to no one in particular.
"Who knows? We just follow where they lead us," answered an unknown voice.
"Have the Paekche bastards invaded again?"
"This time, I heard it's the Koguryŏ bastards."
"Why can't those damned fools just stick to farming instead of constantly stirring up trouble for people who just want to live in peace?"
"Exactly. And they probably say the same thing about us Silla people stirring things up." Such talk emerged, and at another point, someone asked, "Seriously, what are we going marching off for?"
"What do you mean, 'for'? We're going to fight."
"Yeah, but what are we fighting *for*?"
There was no answer for a while, then someone laughed bitterly, "Does anyone go because they have a reason to? We go because they tell us to."
This remark seemed very amusing, as someone else laughed louder, "Who tells us to go fight? Us, who barely even listen to our own fathers!"
"Right, who told us to go?" someone else laughed again.
"We go because they'll catch us and kill us if we don't!"
At this, everyone fell silent, as if acknowledging, "Yes, that's true." Kasil thought, "I'm going in place of my old father-in-law," and felt happy alone.
In this manner, camping at night and walking by day, crossing unfamiliar rivers, passing through strange fields, climbing formidable high passes, somehow, after about ten days since leaving Seoul, they crossed the vast Nodol Ferry, wide as the sea, and reached Hanyang. During that time, some deserted, some were caught deserting and beheaded, some died of illness, some drowned crossing rivers; thus, their numbers dwindled, and of the thousand soldiers who left Seoul together, fewer than six hundred crossed the Nodol Ferry.
The day Kasil and his fellow soldiers crossed the Nodol, a northwest wind blew fiercely from Samgak Mountain, and fine sleet, like millet grains, fell thick and fast. The soldiers originally stationed in Hanyang <199> were all dressed in ragged clothes, their faces pale and gaunt. The clothes they brought from home were worn out, and they stood shivering in blackened, grimy uniforms. The newly arrived soldiers, like Kasil, felt a chill run down their spines upon seeing this sight.
"Why do they all look like that? Like skeletons?"
"You only look like that if you haven't died."
Talking like this, everyone felt disheartened as they entered the barracks made of straw mats.
That day, because the soldiers from Seoul had just arrived after a long journey of over twenty days, several cows were slaughtered, much wine was provided, and a great feast was held. Koguryŏ cavalry often peeked over Moak Pass, reinforcements from Seoul hadn't arrived, and the garrison soldiers here hadn't had a day of peace or a night of sound sleep. Seeing the new soldiers arrive this time, everyone rejoiced. Stuffing their long-starved stomachs full of beef and drinking wine, they forgot the cold and their homesickness, and everyone became lively, chatting and enjoying themselves. Kasil also got drunk. An old soldier who ended up in the same room took a liking to him, getting him lots of wine and meat. This old soldier had been a soldier for twenty years, had spent a long time in Seoul, knew the barracks well, could play the flute (퉁수; a type of traditional flute), sing, and dance, and having been in many battles, he regarded fighting lightly. After much merrymaking, this old soldier began beating time on his knees and sang a song. The lyrics went like this:
"E-he-ya—Mountains strange and waters strange, whom did I follow to come here?" After this opening line finished, five or six other old soldiers, also beating time on their knees, responded: "E-he-ya—yo—We didn't come following a lover. We didn't come for sightseeing. We came to hunt Koguryŏ bastards with the Dragon Spring Sword (룡천검; Yongch'ŏn Sword). E-he-ya—yo—"
The old soldier, even more spirited, danced with swaying shoulders, singing, "E-he-ya—yo—New soldiers, let me ask you. How are things in your home mountains and streams? How are your parents? Are the wives and children you left behind well? E-he-ya—yo—" Then the other old soldiers, also dancing with swaying shoulders, sang the refrain they had sung earlier, "We didn't come following a lover."
Soldiers with flushed faces from other rooms heard the singing and gathered around. The room became packed, and those who couldn't fit stood outside the door, enduring the sleet. The singers became even more excited; some stood up to dance, while others beat rhythm with their hands <200> or feet. With each line the lead old soldier sang, more people joined the response. Kasil, too, quietly tried to follow along, eventually learning the tune and joining the group singing the refrain.
The old soldier raised his voice: "E-he-ya—yo. Let's go hunting, let's go hunting. When day breaks, let's go hunting. Over Moak Pass, across the Imjin River, let's go hunt Koguryŏ soldiers."
"E-he-ya—yo—We didn't come following a lover. We didn't come for sightseeing. We came with the Dragon Spring Sword to cut off the Koguryŏ king's head and offer it to our Great King."
"E-he-ya—yo. A hundred years of life are but a dream. Where did we come from, where do we go? Though we live and play today, who knows what tomorrow brings? Alas, perhaps we are destined to be spirits dead upon the stony fields of Pukhan Mountain."
"E-he-ya—yo—" everyone sang the refrain in mournful tones. When the refrain ended, the crowd fell silent, watching only the old soldier's lips. The old soldier's wrinkled face and disheveled white hair hung down in a thousand strands. Still dancing with swaying shoulders, he sang, "E-he-ya—yo. Don't speak of Pukhan Mountain's stony fields. Lest you disturb the scattered white bones. Alas, if we too should meet our end, we shall become scattered white bones." As he sang, tears glistened in the eyes of the old soldier standing nearby. The soldiers who had been singing the refrain were about to sing again but choked up and wept. Kasil, unable to hold back the rising sobs, cried out loud.
At that moment, suddenly, the sound of the reveille bugle echoed from the barracks courtyard. The soldiers were all startled. But everyone knew. Koguryŏ soldiers were attacking Hanyang Fortress under the cover of night.
Kasil, doing as others did, slung his bow and quiver, picked up a sword, and went out. In the barracks courtyard, thousands of soldiers stood lined up in long rows. At the front, a mounted commander holding a flag waved it around, giving orders to the soldiers. "Koguryŏ soldiers are now crossing over Muak Pass. Go out and fight them. If the Koguryŏ soldiers retreat, pursue them all the way to the end of Pukhan Mountain," he commanded. Just then, an arrow from nowhere grazed the ear of the commander's horse and flew past. The thousands of soldiers let out a unified roar, rounded the corner of Inwang Mountain, and charged towards Muak Pass.
At dawn, when roosters crowed in the villages, the soldiers reached the end of Pukhan Mountain. The Koguryŏ soldiers had abandoned their dead, their horses, and their arrow-stricken, fallen comrades, fleeing back towards Nangbi Fortress <201>. Among the Silla soldiers, over two hundred had died. The old soldier who had led the singing was nowhere to be seen. Kasil searched here and there the next day and asked around, but no one knew what had happened to him.

3
About ten days after establishing camp here, the eight-thousand-strong army led by Generals Yongch'un and Yu-sin began to arrive. The Silla soldiers, all invigorated, boasted that this time they would surely push all the way to P'yŏngyang. However, things didn't go as planned. They would advance about thirty *li* (里; unit of distance), only to be pushed back fifty *li*, then advance again about seventy *li*, oscillating back and forth between the Han River and the Imjin River. Spring came, summer came, autumn came, winter came, and spring and summer came and went again several times. During this time, soldiers died of old age, sickness, arrows, and swords; some deserted, and some were caught deserting and killed. The army gradually shrank. When the numbers dwindled, they would retreat several tens of *li* and wait for new recruits. When new soldiers arrived, they would again boast of marching straight to P'yŏngyang, advance perhaps a hundred *li*, and then retreat again when numbers dwindled. Day and night, this pattern of advancing and retreating repeated endlessly, with no end to the war in sight. Kasil, who had left expecting to return in a year, had already spent three years, and the way back home seemed remote. He occasionally heard news from home through newly arrived soldiers, but there was no way to send news back. People arrived, but no one left, so how could news be sent? He heard news of the young woman's family in the spring of his third year. He heard the old man was still healthy and that his daughter was still unmarried, waiting for him. However, it was also said that she was arranged to marry a certain nobleman (량반) there, with the wedding planned for the autumn. Hearing this, Kasil felt terribly sad. But with the way home so uncertain, what could he do? The soldiers who had left Seoul with him three years ago had disappeared one by one, and now he couldn't see any familiar faces. He couldn't trust that his own life, like dew on a blade of grass, wouldn't vanish at any moment. Furthermore, this autumn, both Silla and Koguryŏ were said to be mobilizing all their strength for a major battle. It seemed unlikely he would survive that clash. The soldiers spoke of a flying general in Koguryŏ who could move unseen, and it was said this general would lead the attack this time, making it seem even less likely he would live to see next spring. Around the fifteenth day of the ninth month of his third year, the order came to attack Nangbi Fortress. The soldiers were all weary and homesick <202>, lacking the will to fight. But tempted by the promise that they would be sent home after this one last battle, they resolved to fight one final time, whether they lived or died. They ate their fill of meat and wine, blew bugles, beat drums, raised dust, and charged towards Nangbi Fortress. Kasil, losing himself in the moment, shot arrows while swinging his sword, pushing forward. From Nangbi Fortress, arrows rained down like hail. Charging soldiers fell one by one with a thud. Kasil repeatedly stepped over fallen soldiers, some dead, some not yet dead but gushing blood, tripped and stumbled, but just kept pushing forward, forward. Heaven and earth seemed to spin, making it impossible to distinguish directions even close by; heaven and earth were filled with the clamor of war drums and horns, making it impossible to hear any specific sound. He just kept moving forward along the path he was on. With a "sshik" sound, an arrow pierced Kasil's left arm. Kasil stopped abruptly and quickly pulled it out. As Nangbi Fortress gradually drew nearer, the arrows flying past him increased. Before long, without knowing when it happened, an arrow struck Kasil's right leg. "Aigo!" Kasil cried out and fell. Using all his strength, Kasil pulled out the arrow embedded in his leg, but blood poured from his arm and leg, the pain was intense, and his strength drained, leaving him unable to move. Kasil managed to staunch the wounds with his clothes and lay collapsed as if dead. He vaguely saw Silla soldiers shouting "Uak!" and running past him. After a while, something grabbed his leg and lifted it. Opening his eyes, he saw two Koguryŏ soldiers standing over him, holding swords. One of them kicked him in the side, asking, "Isn't this bastard dead yet?" "I'm not dead," Kasil replied, looking up at the soldiers. The other soldier aimed the sword in his hand at Kasil's chest. "You damn Silla bastard! Your army is already dead at our hands, only a few survivors fled. You bastard, I'll kill you too, just like this, stab!" he threatened, about to thrust the sword into Kasil's chest. Kasil gestured with his hand as if asking him to wait a moment. "Hey, what grudge is there between you and me? Did I steal your cow? Why are you trying to kill me, a stranger you've just met? I too have elderly parents and a young wife. If I die, what will happen to them?" At these words, one soldier grabbed the arm of the sword-wielding soldier, signaling him to wait. "You bastard. Then why did you come into our country carrying a bow? If you had come empty-handed, we would have caught a chicken and cooked you rice, you bastard <203>. Why did you come carrying a bow and kill our people? All you Silla bastards deserve to die. Needlessly stirring up trouble with Koguryŏ, which was just minding its own business, forcing us to come out to war like this..." Kasil asked doubtfully, "But they say the Koguryŏ people needlessly stir up trouble with Silla, which is just minding its own business!" "Who said that?" the soldier with the sword demanded angrily. "Our king says it's the Silla bastards who start the trouble first." Kasil countered, "Our king says the Koguryŏ people don't stay quiet and needlessly stir up trouble with others." The three men stood silently, looking at each other. Kasil struggled to sit up. He was extremely thirsty. He asked the soldier with the sword, "I'm dying of thirst, please give me a drink of water." The soldier hesitated, bewildered for a moment, then sheathed his sword, went to a stream, fetched water, and gave it to Kasil. Kasil gulped it all down. Then he said to the two soldiers, "Don't kill me. I shot arrows all day today, so maybe a few of your people died from my shots, but did I kill them because I wanted to? I shot because they gave me a bow and told me to shoot. Isn't it the same for you? Why do you just stab people to death for no reason?" He reached for the bow lying beside him, pulled it, and snapped it. "There, now I'm a man who entered your country unarmed, without a bow, aren't I?" The two soldiers looked at each other silently. "What should we do? Spare this guy?" "I don't know. They ordered us to kill everyone." "Let's spare him... What this guy says is right." "Yeah. Should we say we captured him?" "Okay. Let's take this guy, capture him, and hand him over to the barracks. Don't kill him." Thus, the two soldiers helped Kasil up and took him captive to the barracks, presenting him to the commander. 「자네가 엇더케 가나?」하고 로인은 놀래어 뭇는다.

「네.」하고 가실은 처녀의 처든 얼굴을 나려다 본다. 처녀는 눈물 무든 굴을 가실의 가슴에 무드며 「그러면 가 줍시오. 그 은헤는 내 몸이 죽기까지 갑겟습니다. 그러면 가줍시요!」하고 한 번 더 가실의 얼굴을 본다.
로인은 가실의 결심을 휘지 못 할 줄을 알고 자긔가 젓던 옷집을 가실에게 주며
「자네 은혜는 내나 죽어도 못 닛겟네. 그러면 갓다가 속히 돌아오게. 나를 자네의 장인으로 밋게. 부듸부듸 잘 다녀오게.」
이리하야 가실은 전장으로 나가게 되엇다.
골에 들어가서 여러 백 명 군사로 뽑힌 사람들과 함끠 마병 수십명에 끌리어 서울로 갓다. 가는 길에 여러 골로서 군사로 뽑혀오는 사람들을 만나 치술령을 넘어 올 때에는 천명이나 넘엇다. 산빗탈에는 늙은이 부인네 안해들이 하야케 늘어 섯다가 자긔네 아버지나 아들이 지나가는 것을 보고는 손으로 가르치고 부르며 발을 구르고 우짓는다.
가실이가 서울 동문을 들어 설 때에는 벌서 해가 서형 산마루에 올라 안고 팔백 여듧이나 된다는 여러 절에서는 저녁 쇠북소리가 둥둥 울려 나온다. 군사로 뽑혀가는 사람들이 들어오는 것을 보려고 장안 사람들은 모두 길가에 나섯다. 먼데 사람이 안 보일만 할 때에야 겨오 분황사 압 영문에 다달앗다.
가실은 장관의 점고를 맛고 방에 들어갓다. 열간 통이나 되는 큰 방안에 백 명이 넘는 사람들이 콩나물 모양으로 안저서 혹은 갓흔 고향에서 온 아는 사람들끼리 혹은 모르는 사람들끼리 니야기들을 한다. 가실은 방 한편 구석에 우둑허니 안저서 전장에 나아가는 것이 무서운 듯한 생각과 그러나 명년 이 때에 돌아오면 오래 그리워하던 사람을 안해를 삼아 재미잇게 살 것을 생각하고는 혼자 깃버한다.
이윽고 어듸서 풍류소리가 울려온다. 사람들은 닐어 서서 창으로 내다본다. 서남편으로 환한 불빗이 보인다. 창에 부터서 바라보던 사람 하나이 「저게 대궐이야. 상감님 게신데야.」하는 소리를 듯고 대궐대궐하는 말만 듯고 보지는 못한 사람은 일쩨히 그리로 밀려 「응 어느 게 대궐이야?」하고 사람들 틈으로 고개를 내어 밀고 발을 벗듸듼다.
「저긔 저 등불 만히 켜논 데가 대궐이야 림해궁이야?＜197＞」하고 누구 잘 아는 드시 설명한다.
가실도 사람들 틈에 끼어서 내다 보앗다. 멧 천인지 모른 등불이 반듸불 모양으로 공중에 걸리고 그 한 가운데쯤 해서 커단 홰빗 갓흔 것도 보인다.
「등불도 만히도 커노앗다.」하는 이도 잇고 「저러케 환하게 불을 켜노코 타작을 햇스면 조켓네.」하는 이도 잇고 「거긔다가 씨름을 한 판 차려 노앗스면 조켓네.」하는 이도 잇다.
그 중에 서울서 오래 병정 노릇하던 사람 하나이 이 사람들의 무식한 소리를 비웃는 드시
「이 사람들 그게 무슨 소린가. 지금 상감님이 만조백관을 모흐시고 연락을 배설한 것이야. 내일 룡춘장군 유신장군이 우리들을 거느리고 랑비성으로 간다고 가서 승전해 가지고 오라고 잔치하는 것이라네.」한다.
북소리 피리소리 져소리 쇠소리가 간간히 들려온다. 밝듸 밝은 구월 보름달이 둥글한 어름장 모양으로 남산 우에 걸리고 반월성과 황룡사가 달빗 속에 큰 그림자 모양으로 보인다.
사람들은 하나식 둘식 창에서 떨어저서 구석구석이 목침을 베고 쓸어진다. 엇던 이는 벌서 종일 걸어온 로독에 코를 드렁드렁 군다. 집을 바리고 처자를 바리고 논과 밧과 소를 떠나서 전장에 죽으러 나가는 어린 아희 갓흔 백성들이 팔다리를 탕탕 둘러치며 코를 드릉드릉 굴고 어제 떠난 집을 꿈꿀 때까지 가늘엇다 굵것다 끈첫다 닛겻다 하는 림해궁 대궐 풍악소리는 달빗에 떠와서 장틈으로 숨여 들어왓다. 가실도 처음에는 한참 잠이 안들엇스나 어제 종일 장작을 패고 오늘 종일 길을 것던 로독에 동여가도 모르게 잠이 들엇다.
달이 거의 서산에 걸린 때 사방 절에서 일제히 종소리가 울어오고 그 중에 바로 영문 졋헤서 치는 분황사 종소리는 곤해 자던 군사의 꿈을 모도 깨트려 노코 말엇다.
라발소리 주라소리가 영문 안에 닐어난다. 자던 군사들은 둥지를 흔들린 벌 모양으 여러 방으로서 쏘다저 나와 마당에 모혀선다. 마당 한가운데는 활과 화살통이 산덤이갓치 싸히고 울긋불긋한 긔빨이 횃불빗에 나비긴다.
해뜨게 천여 명 군사가 제일대로 남대문을 나서서 서를 향하고 떠낫다. 말탄 군사도 잇고 짐실은 수레도 잇다. 군사들은 모도 활과 살통을 메고 엇던 군사는 큰 창을 메엇다. 가실도 큰 활과 살통을＜198＞ 메고 물들인 군복을 입엇다. 어제까지 호미와 낫과 장작패는 도긔를 들고 화평하게 살던 농부들은 하로 아츰에 활을 메고 칼을 차고 사람을 죽이러 가는 군사로 변하엿다.
「어듸로 가는 모양이야?」하고 가실의 뒤에 오는 한 사람이 누구더런지 모르게 뭇는다.
「누가 아나. 끌고 가는 데로 따라가지」하고 누군지 모르는 사람이 대답한다.
「백제ㅅ놈들이 또 처들어 왓나?」
「이번에는 고구렷놈들이라든가.」
「그 망할 놈들은 농사나 해 먹고 자빠젓지 웨 가만히 잇는 사람들을 들수성거려서 못 견듸게 굴어.」
「글세나 말이지. 또 그 놈들은 우리네 신라 사람들이 들수성 거린다고 그러겟지.」 이러한 말도 나오고 또 엇던 때에는 「글세 우리는 무얼 먹겟다고 터불거리고 가?」
「먹긴 멀 먹어. 싸흐러 가지.」
「글세 무엇 먹겟다고 싸와!」
한참 대답이 업더니 누가 「누구는 갈 일이 잇서서 가나. 가라고 그러니가 가지.」하고 성난드시 픽 웃는다.
이 말이 대단히 재미나는 모양으로 누가 「우리더러 싸흐러 가라는 사람은 누구야? 아버지 말도 잘 안들으려고 드는 우리더러!」
하고 더 크게 웃는다.
「참 누가 가라기에 가는 길이야?」하고 누가 또 웃는다.
「안가면 잡아다가 죽인다니 가지!」
이 말에 모도 「참 그러타.」하는드시 아모 말들이 업다. 가실은 「나는 늙은 장인 대신 나가는 길이야.」하고 생각하고 혼자 깃벗다.
이 모양으로 밤이면 한둔하고 나지면은 걸어 낫선 강을 건너 낫선 벌을 지나 어마어마한 큰 령을 넘어 이렁구렁 서울을 떠난 지 10여 일에 바다 갓치 넓은 노돌나루를 건너 한양에 다달앗다. 그 동안에 도망한 사람, 도망하다가 붓들려 목을 잘려 죽은 사람, 병들어 죽은 사람, 강을 건너다가 물에 빠져 죽은 사람, 이럭저럭 다 줄어 버리고 서울서 함께 떠난 천명 군사 중에 노돌나루를 건넌 이는 륙백명이 다 차지 못하엿다.
가실과 가치 온 군사가 노돌을 건너는 날은 삼각산으로서 하늬바람이 냅다 불고 좁살 갓흔 싸락눈이 펄펄 날렷다. 본래 한양에 잇던 군사들은 모다 노닥노닥한 옷에 얼굴 빗긔 하나 업다. 그네들은 집에서＜199＞ 올 때에 가지고 온 옷도 다 닙어 해어지고 깜아케 때무든 군복을 닙고 덜덜 떨고 섯다. 새로 가실과 가티 온 군사들은 이 광경을 보고 모다 소름이 끼첫다.
「웨 다들 저 꼴이야. 해골만 남앗스니?」
「죽지 안아야 저 꼴이라도 되지.」
이런 말들을 하며 모다 풀이 죽어서 섬거적편 영문에 들어갓다.
이 날은 서울 군사들이 20여 일이나 먼 길에 새로 왓다하야 소를 여러 마리 잡고 술을 만히 내어 큰 잔치를 베풀엇다. 각금 고구려 마병이 기우기웃 모악재로 엿보고 서울서 구원병은 오지 아니하고 그래서 이곳서 수자리 사는 군사들은 하로도 맘을 놋치 못하고 밤잠도 잘 자지 못하다가 이번에 새 군사오는 것을 보고 다들 깃빼하얏다. 그 판에 오래 굼주렷던 창자에 소고기를 실컨 먹고 술을 마시니 치운 것과 고향 그리운 것도 니저버리고 모도 신이 나서 떠들고 논다. 가실도 술이 취하엿다. 자긔와 한 방에 잇게 된 늙은 군사가 자긔를 퍽 귀애해서 술도 만히 어더주고 고기도 만히 어더주엇다. 그 늘근 군사는 20년이나 병정으로 잇섯고 서울도 오래 잇섯슴으로 영문 일도 잘 알고 퉁수도 불고 소리도 하고 춤도 촐 줄 알며 또 여러 번 전장에 나갓슴으로 싸홈도 우습게 녀긴다. 한참 떠들다가 이 늙은 군사가 무릅 장단을 치며 소리 한 마듸를 부른다. 그 사설은 이러하다.
「에헤야- 산도 설고 물도 선데 누구를 따라 예 왓는가.」 이 선소리고 끗이 나니 그 중에 한 오륙인 늙은 군사가 역시 무릅 장단을 치며 「에헤야-요- 님딸아 온 것도 아니로세. 구경온 것도 아니로세. 룡천검 드는 칼로 고구려ㅅ놈 사양을 온길 일세. 에헤야-요-」하고 화답을 한다.
늙은 군사는 더 신이 나서 얼신얼신 억개춤을 추어가며 「에해야-요- 새로 온 군사야 말 물어 보자. 고향 산천은 엇지 된고. 부모 양친은 엇지 된고. 두고 온 처자도 잘 잇드냐. 에헤야요」하면 다른 늙은 군사들도 또 억개춤을 얼신얼신 추며 「님따라 온 것도 아니로세.」하고 앗까 하든 후렴을 부른다.
다른 방에서 얼굴 불근 군사들이 소리를 듯고 모혀든다. 방이 터지게 모히고도 남아 싸락눈을 마즌면서 문 밧게 섯다. 소리하던 군사들은 더욱 흥이 나서 닐어나 춤을 추는 이도 잇고 손으로 부르거든＜200＞ 다리를 처서 장단을 마초는 이도 잇다. 늙은 군사 한 마듸를 먹일 때마다 밧는 사람이 늘어간다. 가실도 가만가만히 숭내를 내다가 나종에 곡조를 배화 후렴하는 패에 참예하게 되엇다.
늙은 군사는 일단 소리를 놉혀 「에헤야요. 사냥을 가쟈. 사냥을 가. 날이 새거든 사냥을 가자. 모악재 넘어 림진강 건너 고구려군사 산냥을 가자.」
「에헤야요- 님따라 온 것도 아니로세. 구경 온 것도 아니로세. 룡천검 드는 칼로 고구려왕의 머리를 버혀 대왕께 밧치러 온 길일세.」
「에헤야요. 인생 백년이 꿈이로다. 어듸서 와서 어듸로 가. 온르은 살아서 놀더라도 내일 일은 누라 아나. 아마도 북한산 석비레판에 살마자 죽은 혼이로고나.」
「에헤야요」하고 모다 슬픈 듯한 목소리로 후렴을 부른다. 후렴이 끗나면 일동은 깜작 아니하고 늙은 군사의 임만 바라본다. 늙은 군사의 주름잡힌 얼굴에 흐트러진 백발이 천줄기 만줄기 함부로 늘어 젓다. 여전히 얼신얼신 춤을 추며 「에헤야요. 북한산 석비레 판지를 말아. 흐터진 백골을 건들일라. 어즈버 우리네도 한 번 아차 죽어지면 흐터진 백골이 되리로고나.」할 때에 볏헤 걸은 늙은 군사의 눈에서는 눈물이 번적번적한다. 후렴밧던 군사들은 후렴을 부르랴다가 모도 목이 메어 울엇다. 가실은 복바처 오르는 울음을 참다 못하야 목을 노아 울엇다.
이 때에 갑자기 영문 마당으로서 취군 라팔소리가 울여온다. 군사들은 모다 깜작 놀낫다. 그러나 누구나 다 알앗다. 고구려 군사가 밤을 타서 한양성으로 처들어 오는 것이다.
가실도 남들이 하는 모양으로 활과 살통을 메고 칼 하나를 들고 나섯다. 영문 마당에는 수천 명 군사가 길게 길게 렬을 지어 늘어섯는데 압헤는 엇던 말타고 긔든 장수가 긔를 둘러가며 군사들에게 호령을 한다. 「지금 고구려 군사가 무악재로 처넘어 오니 너히는 마조 나가 싸호되 만일 고구려 군사가 쪼기거든 북한산 끗까지 따라가라.」고 한다. 이 때에 난데업는 화살 하나이 그 장수의 탄 말 귀를 스치고 날아온다. 수천명 군사는 일제히 고함을 치고 인왕산 모통이를 돌아 무악재를 향하고 달려갓다.
