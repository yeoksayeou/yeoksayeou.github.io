朝鮮民族의 全盛時代
						申采浩

To study the ancient history of the East, one cannot possibly establish the basis, true essence, system, and unity of history without first exploring the origins of ancient Korean culture. Therefore, in examining ancient Korean culture, one must first delineate the scope of ancient Korea's territory. Where can we find the textual evidence from ancient documents? The *Kupon Chindan To* (九變震壇圖) of *Sinji* (神誌) has not been transmitted, and Puyŏ (扶餘), of the Tan'gun (檀君) lineage, inherited the ancestral enterprise, flourishing with a brilliant culture in the Manchurian-Mongolian continent for over a thousand years. Reaching the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods, it engaged in wars with the Han Chinese, sometimes winning, sometimes losing, with long periods where the clouds of war never ceased. By the time of Duke Huan of Qi (齊 桓公), it was a period of decline for the Korean nation (朝鮮族). Defeats increased daily, severely shaking the foundation of the Korean nation residing in southwestern Manchuria and Mongolia, shrinking the national territory eastward to Yongping Fu (永平府). Later, Qin Shi Huang's (秦始皇) southeastern wars were entirely aimed at driving the Korean nation to the northeast. The Great Wall (萬里長城) was established as the border between Korea and China, and a great construction project extending over ten thousand *li* was undertaken. Consequently, the textual evidence of ancient Korea was lost through hundreds of years of destruction by fire in warfare. Furthermore, as Korea's capital was frequently moved due to enemy calamities, the expansion and contraction of its territory<59> became even greater. Reaching the middle ages, the distorted interpretations and arbitrary judgments of foolish Confucian scholars (愚儒輩), blindly devoted to Sinocentrism (大華主義), arbitrarily shrank the territory of Tan'gun, randomly relocated the capital of Puyŏ, even mistaking Nyŏngbyŏn's Myohyangsan (寧邊妙香山) for Paektusan (白頭山), Asadal (阿斯達) for Hwanghae Province (黃海道), and burying Kija (箕子) as far as the Taedong River (大同江). How can this not be lamented? Looking at the national histories written by so-called historians, every stroke of the brush differs, every word spoken is incorrect, like a crowd of blind men wandering through the night, unable to find their destination. Just as the Great Wall has a deep fate with our Korea, the study of the Great Wall serves as excellent evidence for finding our ancient territory. The *Huainanzi* (淮南子) states that the Great Wall "(struck the Liao River (遼水) in the north and connected to Korea (朝鮮) in the east)." It is clear that even at the time of the *Huainanzi*, the Great Wall was built as a defensive border between Korea and China. Therefore, one who knows the Great Wall must know ancient Korea.

*The section from "Previously mentioning Koguryŏ..." onwards is missing from the original text up to the part referencing the China XX Korean History Compilation Office.*

Kaesomun (蓋蘇文) of Koguryŏ (高句麗) said, "(From Puyŏ (自扶餘), a long wall was built, reaching the sea in the south, over a thousand *li* long)." (This is the longest wall in our national history). Caesar (該撒) of Rome (羅馬) said, "(Because the northern barbarians frequently pressed near, a wall was built on the Rhine River (萊因河北), its length reaching several hundred *li*)." (This is the longest wall in Western history). However, Qin Shi Huang (秦始皇) had Meng Tian (蒙恬) build the long wall to the north, starting from Lintao (臨洮) and reaching Liaodong (遼東), its length exceeding ten thousand *li*. This is what the world calls the Great Wall (萬里長城). It is not only the longest wall in Eastern and Western history but truly the progenitor and colossal example among all walls since walls began. Now, examining the records of the Great Wall, the Great Wall was not initially built by Qin Shi Huang. During Qin Shi Huang's time, the existing long wall was expanded, extended, and additionally built upon. Furthermore, those who added to the long wall were not limited to Qin Shi Huang; many repaired and built the long wall after Qin Shi Huang. However, because no one undertook a larger expansion project than Qin Shi Huang, it ultimately became Qin Shi Huang's Great Wall, standing uniquely imposing throughout the ages. The history of the long wall is extremely obscure, making it difficult to clearly distinguish the border between the Korean and Han nations at that time. Let us now consider three cases: A, B, and C. "A" refers to the long walls before Qin Shi Huang. The *Records of the Grand Historian*, "Account of the Xiongnu" (史記匈奴傳) states, "King Wuling of Zhao (趙武靈王) defeated the Linhu (林胡) and Loufan (樓煩) in the north, built a long wall from Dai (代) along the foot of the Yinshan (陰山) mountains to Gaoque (高闕), established Yunmen (雲門) and Yanzhong (鴈中) prefectures." This is the long wall of Zhao (趙). "(Yan (燕) had a worthy general, Qin Kai (秦開), who was sent as a hostage to the Hu (胡). The Hu trusted him greatly. Upon returning, he attacked and defeated the Eastern Hu (東胡), pushing the Hu back over a thousand *li*. During the reign of King Xi of Yan (燕王喜), a long wall was also built from Zaoyang (造陽) to Yangping (陽平), establishing the Shanggu (上谷), Yuyang (漁場 corrected to 漁陽), Youbeiping (右北平), Liaoxi (遼西), and Liaodong (遼東) commanderies to resist the Hu)." This is the long wall of Yan. "(During the time of King Zhaoxiang of Qin (秦昭王), he killed the king of Yiqu (義渠). Qin possessed the Longxi (隴西), Beidi (北地), and Shangjun (上郡) regions and built walls to resist the Hu)." This refers to the long walls before Qin Shi Huang. "B" refers to the long walls after Qin Shi Huang. The *Book of Northern Qi* (北齊書) states, "(In the sixth year of the Tianbao era (天保六年), Emperor Xianzu (顯祖) mobilized 1.8 million commoners to build the long wall from Youzhou (幽州) Xia Kou (夏口) west to Hengzhou (恒州), over nine hundred *li*.)"<60> "(In the seventh year, from the Xihe (西河) main garrison, a long wall was built east to the sea.)" "(The Later Ruler (後主), in the sixth year of Datong (大統六年), built a wall east from Kudui (庫堆?) to the sea, following the curves of the mountains for over two thousand *li*, cutting mountains and building fortifications.)" This is the long wall of Northern Qi (高齊). The *Book of Zhou* (周書) states, "(Emperor Wen (文帝), in the sixth year of Kaihuang (開皇六年), second month, mobilized 110,000 adult males to repair the long wall.)" "(Seventh year, second month, mobilized over 100,000 adult males to repair the long wall.)" This is the long wall of Sui (隋). The *Jehol Diary* (熱河日記) mentions "(Building the long wall with Xu Zhongshan (徐中山))." This is the long wall of Ming (明). Thus, there were also long walls after Qin Shi Huang. "C" refers to the long wall of Qin Shi Huang. The *Records of the Grand Historian*, "Basic Annals of Qin" (史記秦本記) states, "(In the 32nd year of the First Emperor (始皇 三十二年), Lu Sheng (盧生), a man of Yan, returned from a mission into the sea, bringing diagrams and registers which said, 'The one who will destroy Qin is Hu (胡).' The First Emperor then sent Meng Tian (蒙恬) across the Yellow River to seize Gaoque (高闕), Taoshan (陶山), and Beijia (北假), and build fortifications.)" "(In the 34th year, convicts and corrupt officials were sent to build the long wall.)" The "Account of the Xiongnu" (匈奴傳) states, "(After Qin destroyed the six states, the First Emperor sent Meng Tian leading a force of 100,000 [corrected from 10,000] north to attack the Hu, seizing all the land south of the Yellow River ('White Sheep River' 白羊河). He used the river as a barrier, built forty-four walled county seats along the river, and moved convicts there to garrison them. He opened a direct road from Jiuyuan (九原) to Yunyang (雲陽), utilizing the terrain of mountains along the border, cutting through valleys, repairing what could be repaired. It started from Lintao (臨洮) and reached Liaodong (遼東), extending over ten thousand *li*.)" The "Account of Meng Tian" (蒙恬傳) states, "(Starting from Lintao, reaching Liaodong, extending over ten thousand *li*.)" This is the Great Wall of Qin Shi Huang. The above is a brief history of the long walls of various Chinese dynasties. However, three further questions arise concerning this: "One": Did Qin Shi Huang's long wall build upon, continue, repair, add to, and expand the old sites of Yan, Zhao, and King Zhao? Or did he expand the territory far outwards and move the construction further north? "Two": Did the long walls after Qin Shi Huang only repair the old sites of Qin's wall? Or did they retreat inwards or advance outwards, changing the foundation of Qin's wall? "Three": Its western boundary originally started at Lintao (嘉裕關 - Jiayuguan is typically associated with the Ming wall, Lintao is further east), but its eastern boundary is only said to reach Liaodong. What area does Liaodong refer to? This is the problem. According to the *Wei Shu*, "Biography of Zhangsun Chen" (魏書長孫陳傳), "Chen (陳) was serving in the palace guard when he campaigned against Helong (和龍). The enemy came out from the west gate, intending to breach the outer defenses. Chen struck and repelled them, pursuing them to the foot of the long wall." This clearly shows that the long wall was outside Longcheng (龍城). The *Tongdian* by Du You (杜佑通典) states, "North from Suzhou (蘇州) to the dilapidated long wall pass is two hundred fifty *li*." Pak Yŏnam (朴燕岩 - Pak Chiwŏn) said, "(The long wall at Shanhaiguan (山海關) was built by Xu Da (徐達) during the Ming dynasty)." This can be considered a brilliant insight. This resolves the first problem. If the long walls after Qin Shi Huang did not follow the old sites of Qin Shi Huang's construction, then Qin Shi Huang's wall was not necessarily confined only to the old ruins of Yan and Zhao. Generally, depending on the rise and fall, strength and weakness of a state, it could expand territory outwards or shorten its defenses. Therefore, the statement in the "Account of the Xiongnu," "(Repair what can be repaired, build anew where it cannot be repaired)" serves as specific evidence of the long wall's shifts. Thus, the second problem is clarified. The third problem is difficult due to the Liaodong issue. To discuss the Liaodong issue, two problems must necessarily be raised: "One," what was the national situation and territorial extent of Korea at that time? "Two," how far did Yan's expansion reach? Looking at books like *Ginyeon Aram* (紀年兒覽 - Jinian Erlan?) and *Sŏgwak Chapnok* (西郭雜錄 - Xiguo Zalu?), it is said that (Murong Huang's (慕容皝) invasion of Puyŏ and Li Ji's (李勣)<61> attack on P'yŏngyang (平壤)) reduced ancient Korean history to ashes in one stroke. Consulting Sima Qian's History (*Ma Shi* 馬史 - referring to 史記 Sima Qian), the histories of the Six States (六國史記), "(including the history of Yan, were all burned by Qin Shi Huang)." Thus, there is no place to verify the ancient histories of Korea and Yan, making the resolution of the second problem extremely difficult. However, let us now gather fragments of ancient history from the tattered remnants. The *Wenxian Tongkao* (文獻考備 - Should be 文獻通考 Wenxian Tongkao) states, "(Guzhu (孤竹) (present-day Yongping Fu 永平府) belonged to Korea (朝鮮) during the Spring and Autumn period)." This is what Yu Huan's *Weilüe* (魚豢魏畧) says. The *Weilüe* states, "(Korea saw Yan presumptuously usurp the title of king and wanted to attack Yan with others.)" *Abridged*: "(The Yan general Qin Kai (秦開) deceitfully acted as a hostage in Korea. Korea trusted him greatly. Qin Kai returned, reported to Yan, attacked Korea, expanded territory by two thousand *li*, reaching Man P'anhan (滿潘汗) as the border. Korea consequently weakened.)" Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian* (司馬氏史記) states, "(Qin attacked the Eastern Hu (東胡), expanding territory by over a thousand *li*)." How could Yu Huan, living several hundred years later, based on what evidence, change "Eastern Hu" to "Korea" and change a thousand *li* to two thousand *li*? Furthermore, how did he know that the expanded territory reached Man P'anhan? It is said that this group is of the same kind as Mùqiū Jiǎn (毋丘儉) of Wei (魏). Comparing the *Samguk Sagi* (三國史記) and the *Sanguo Zhi* (三國志), the *Samguk Sagi* is our history, and the *Sanguo Zhi* is the history of the Three Kingdoms of China: Shu (蜀), Wu (吳), and Wei (魏). "(The Wei general Mùqiū Jiǎn invaded Koguryŏ and entered Hwando Fortress (丸都城))" etc. Because the historical records of Koguryŏ stored in Hwando Fortress largely fell into Mùqiū Jiǎn's hands and were transmitted to China, ancient Chinese who knew about Korean historical events were unsurpassed by the historians of Wei. Isn't the name of Tan'gun Wanggŏm (檀君王儉) first seen in the *Weilüe*? Because Chen Shou (陳壽), serving in Wei at the time, wrote works that were heavily cited by the *Samguk Yusa* (三國遺事), the affairs of Kibu (箕否 - King Pu?) and Kijun (箕準 - King Chun) are also seen in the *Weilüe*. The customs of Puyŏ (扶餘) and the names of the "70-80 states" of the Three Han (三韓) are first seen in the *Sanguo Zhi*. This was written by Chen Shou while he also served in Jin (晉). Although the *Hou Han Shu* (後漢書) records the names of the "70-80 states" of the Three Han, the *Hou Han Shu* was written by Fan Ye (范曄), who came after Chen Shou. The fact that Qin Kai seized Korean territory must also be based on the *Weilüe*. Earlier scholars have said that west of Yongping (永平) was Yan, and east of Yongping was Korea. If the territory expanded by Qin Kai was two thousand *li*, his footsteps must have crossed the Amnok River (鴨綠江). Yi Yulgok (李栗谷) changed two thousand *li* to one thousand *li*, deeming it too far, while Chŏng Tasan (丁茶山 - Chŏng Yagyong) deemed two thousand *li* correct but too close, falsely attesting that Man P'anhan was south of the Taedong River (大同江). Alas! How could such biased and stubborn views be transmitted? Even during the Koguryŏ period, they always occupied Fengtian (奉天 - Mukden), possessed the northern half of Zhili (直隸) and eastern Mongolia (蒙古). Therefore, historical records frequently mention invasions of Taiyuan (太原) (present-day Shanxi Province 山西省), which describes the situation of dispatching troops southward from Eastern Mongolia (東蒙古). The poem "Koguryŏ Fortress" (高句麗城詩) by Fan Shen (樊深), a Tang dynasty person (唐人), says: "Fortress gate opens in a remote land, Empty forest, long ramparts stand. Suddenly, market scenes transform, No longer pipes and strings perform." This refers to Korea coming south from Eastern Mongolia, occupying this land, building a fortress, and establishing a capital; it sings of the traces of fighting against Yan. Korea's military operations at that time extended from Yongping (永平) to north of Beijing (燕京), bordered the Xiongnu (匈奴) (present-day Mongolia 蒙古地) to the north, and<62> reached Shanggu (上谷) (present-day Xuanhua Fu 宣化府) to the south. Looking at Yan's counterattack against Korea, "(Attacked Shanggu in the north, raided Guzhu (孤竹) [present-day Yongping Fu 永平府] in the south, and reached the Liao River 遼河)." The *Old Records of the Grand Historian* (故史記 - likely just referring to 史記) states, "(The Yan general Qin Kai attacked the Eastern Hu, expanded territory, and also mentions attacking the Hu from Zaoyang (造物?) to Xiangping (襄平), building a long wall, establishing Shanggu, Yuyang, Youbeiping, Liaoxi, Liaodong, etc. commanderies)." Examining this, one can see the general outline of Korea's border with Yan. Shanggu (上谷) is present-day Xuanhua Fu (宣化府); Yuyang (漁陽) is the northern part of present-day Shuntian Fu (順天府); Youbeiping (右北平) is the northern part of present-day Yongping Fu (永平府); Liaoxi (遼西) and Liaodong (遼東) are the northern parts of the western half of present-day Fengtian (奉天). What the *Weilüe* calls Man P'anhan (滿潘汗 - should be 滿潘汗 or similar, likely referring to two places) are the two counties, Mun (汶?) and P'anhan (潘汗?), established by Emperor Wu of Han (漢武帝). Their names are clearly recorded in the *Han Shu*, "Treatise on Geography" (漢西遼東郡志 - likely referring to Hanshu Dilizhi section on Liaodong Commandery West). They correspond to present-day Gaiping (蓋平) and Haicheng (海城) areas. The statement "expanded territory by two thousand *li*" refers to the distance from Shanggu (上谷) (present-day Xuanhua Fu) to Xiangping (襄平), halved [re-calculated? 再折], reaching south to Gaiping and Haicheng, the distance being two thousand *li*. Therefore, the Liaodong of the Yan commandery and the Liaodong reached by Qin's long wall can be known. Thus, the third problem is also resolved. The *Jin Shu*, "Tai Kang Di Zhi" (晉書太康地志) states that Jieshi Mountain (碣石山) in Suicheng County (遂成縣) of Lelang Commandery (樂浪) was the starting point of the long wall. This passage will raise doubts for readers of history. Lelang (樂浪) is P'yŏngyang (平壤), and Suicheng County (遂成縣) is south of P'yŏngyang (referring to ancient P'yŏngyang in Guangning/Jinzhou 廣寧金州, not present-day P'yŏngan Province 平安道 and P'yŏngyang). Qin's long wall extended far beyond P'yŏngyang. Grammatically, the starting point is called 'qi' (起 - start) and the endpoint 'zhi' (至 - reach). Since the starting point of Qin Shi Huang's long wall was Lintao (臨洮), why is Lelang written as the place where it started (所起地)? Consulting the *Bei Shi* (北史) and *Sui Shu* (隋書), Wei (魏) and Sui (隋) once established a commandery named Lelang (樂浪) and a county named Suicheng (遂成) in Shanggu (上谷). It is clear that Yan's long wall started in Shanggu Valley (上谷). The record in the *Jin Shu* refers to this. The author of the *Jin Shu*, Emperor Taizong of Tang (唐太宗 - Note: Emperor Taizong commissioned the Jin Shu, but Fang Xuanling led the compilation), was born at the end of the Sui dynasty and lived in Jinyang (晉陽), frequently saw this, and spoke of Yan's long wall in this way. Then what about Jieshi Mountain (碣石山)? The *Yu Gong* (禹貢) chapter on Jizhou (冀州) mentions "(It borders Jieshi on the right 夾右碣石)" and below it says "(From Taihang (太行) and Wangwu (王屋) to Jieshi 至於碣石)." This is because there were two Jieshi mountains at one time. The *Xin Tang Shu* (新唐書) states "(In Shicheng (石城) of Pingzhou (平州), there is Jieshi Mountain)" and "(In Liucheng (柳城) of Yingzhou (營州), there is also Jieshi Mountain)." At that time, it seems plausible that if many stones stood like steles (碑碣), they were named "Jieshi Mountain." Why wouldn't there be a Jieshi Mountain in Shanggu? In the Japanese Historical Journal (日人歷史學雜誌), Mr./Dr. Kan's (甘氏博氏?) statement that the Lelang P'yŏngyang mentioned in the *Jin Shu* and the long wall were built by Qin Shi Huang lacks accurate verification, merely repeating the errors of predecessors, and contains many factual inaccuracies.
How Yan's long wall was related to Korea has already been discussed above. The relationship with Yan concerns the affairs of the Chinbŏn (眞蕃) and Lelang (樂浪) periods [likely referring to states/periods within Kochosŏn]. The relationship with Zhao (趙) pertains to the time of Puyŏ Korea (扶餘朝鮮). Puyŏ Korea was the largest state among the Three Koreas [likely Samchosŏn: Puyŏ, Chin, Mahan/Pyŏnhan?] and first opened contact with China. Therefore, the "Basic Annals of Shun" (舜本記 - Not a standard text name, likely referring to a section in Shiji or Shangshu about Shun) states "(North Mountain attacked the Fā xīshēn 發息愼<63>)." Fā xīshēn (發息愼) is a mispronunciation of Puyŏ Korea (扶餘朝鮮). The *Guanzi* (管子) says "(The Fā Cháoxiān 發朝鮮 of eight thousand *li*)" and also "(The patterned hides of Fā Cháoxiān 發朝鮮)." Fā Cháoxiān is also a shortened form of Puyŏ Korea. The *Records of the Grand Historian* refers to "Ye" (濊), "Maek" (貉), Korea, or sometimes just "Ye" or "Maek." "Maek" gets its name from wearing patterned hides (文皮) as clothing. Ye is a shortened form of Puyŏ. In reality, they are all the same. Zhao's relationship with Puyŏ Korea began with Duke Xiang of Zhao (趙襄子). The "Account of the Xiongnu" states "(Duke Xiang of Zhao defeated and annexed Dai (代), Lin Hu (臨胡), and Luo 狢 - probably Maek 貉)." Afterwards, the subject peoples of Puyŏ, [Wei?] Lin ([魏?]林 - perhaps 林胡?) and Loufan (樓煩), became powerful, causing much trouble on Zhao's borders. King Wuling of Zhao (趙武靈王) changed clothing styles, practiced cavalry and archery, frequently fought with Puyŏ, and built a long wall for defense. Thus, Zhao's long wall was built to defend against Puyŏ Korea. The *Records of the Grand Historian*, "Hereditary House of Zhao" (史記趙世家) calls Puyŏ "Eastern Hu" (東胡), while the "Account of the Xiongnu" calls Lelang [people associated with Lelang/Kochosŏn?] "Eastern Hu." At the end of Zhao, Li Mu (李牧), a capable general of the northern border, defeated and routed the Lin Hu (林胡) and Loufan (樓煩). However, he only defended within the long wall and could not advance beyond it. After Zhao fell, Puyŏ became even stronger. The Xiongnu (匈奴) Chanyu (單于) Modu (冒頓) frequently presented beautiful women and fine horses as tribute to Puyŏ. The *Records of the Grand Historian*, describing the vastness of Modu's territory, says "(From Shanggu eastward, it borders Ye Maek Korea 接濊貉朝鮮)." It is clear that this was after Puyŏ Korea had been defeated by Modu. Therefore, Zhao's long wall was built entirely because of Korea, and Shi Huang's long wall, up to Shanggu, was also for defending against Puyŏ Korea.
Puyŏ Korea not only harbored resentment against Yan* but also frequently attacked Yan, which is why Guan Zhong (管仲) led the assembled forces of the "Nine Alliances of Feudal Lords" (九合諸候) to defeat Guzhu (孤竹). For several hundred years, Puyŏ was the leader of the Three Koreas, invading China, until it also perished after the fall of Yan and Zhao. Alas! During that time, there must have been records like those of Sinji (神誌) and Ko Hŭng (高興之倫 - of the Ko Hŭng type?) detailing the whole story—ambitious monarchs, ruthless generals and ministers, loyal and righteous officers and soldiers, and strategists plotting in curtained headquarters—facts that could make one sing, dance, weep, or be startled. But Korea was already overturned (板蕩), and ancient books were not transmitted. Although China has Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian* (史馬遷의 史記), it exclusively adopts an exclusionary ideology of "Revere China, Expel Barbarians" (尊華攘夷), distorting historical examples. It is worse than Plutarch (布魯帶克 - P'ŭrot'aek'ŭ?), who closely observed the situations of neighboring states and recorded their full stories. Sima Qian's History (*Ma Shi*), aside from recording one or two instances of contact with neighboring states, treats them merely as objects for the self-aggrandizing curiosity of the Chinese themselves. It corrupted the name of one country to create several countries, such as corrupting the names of Suksin (肅愼), Chosŏn (朝鮮), Puyŏ (扶餘), Ye (濊), Donghu (東胡), etc. Or it recorded the affairs of country 'A' as the affairs of country 'B', like recording the affairs of the Hu (胡) and Maek (貉) all in the "Account of the Xiongnu." When recording battles, it reversed victories and defeats; when discussing customs, it misrepresented their good and bad aspects. Not only is it unusable as a source for verifying capitals and personal names,<64> but not even a fragment of record exists about international diplomatic exchanges. This is truly lamentable. Now, although it is difficult to know precisely the extent of the Korean border and the reason for the construction of the long walls by Yan, Zhao, and Qin—whether it was more for the Xiongnu or due to relations between the Korean and Han peoples before the Xiongnu—by studying the Great Wall, we can grasp half of half of how vast ancient Korea's territory was and the scope to which Korea's power had developed. We can be confident that the Great Wall is truly a great material reference in the study of East Asian history.

"Addendum: A Short Story of the Great Wall"

The Great Wall is not only the most important hidden treasure trove of East Asian history and an architectural marvel, but its historical influence on both East and West is immense. Before the construction of the long wall, observe how the Puyŏ, Xiongnu (匈奴), and other tribes, with longbows and strong arrows (長弓勁*), whipped their horses southward (鞭馬南下), captured and killed (虜殺) Han Chinese, plundered (掠奪) wealth and grain, and sometimes even carved out territory to establish their own independent states. The *Spring and Autumn Annals* (*Chunqiu*) and *Records of the Grand Historian* show the Kunyi (昆夷), Xuyi (徐夷), Laiyi (萊夷), Chidi (赤狄), Zhongshan (中山), etc., existing within China's heartland, establishing kingdoms, calling themselves kings, and acting rampantly as they pleased. After the long wall was built, Puyŏ, the great nation of the northeast, returned to its homeland and did not attempt to move south again. One branch of the Mongol Xiongnu tribe also fled westward into Europe (西歐에 竄入), causing disturbances (侵擾) with the "Attila tribes" (峨特族 - Et'ŭkchok, likely referring to the Huns/Attila), eventually triggering the great ethnic migration in Western history. How indeed did the presence or absence of the Great Wall relate to the fall of Holy Rome (聖羅馬) and the rise of the New Renaissance (新文藝), influencing the ebb and flow of international culture? Another branch of the Xiongnu also contended with Puyŏ in the east for several decades, ultimately causing the Puyŏ people to migrate (遷動) as well. They moved east (東으로 徙하야), sometimes called Okchŏ (沃沮) or Chinbŏn (眞蕃), or moved south (南으로 遷하야) to become the Six Ministries of Chinhan (辰韓六部). The details are omitted here. I lament that readers of history only know about the Great Migration of peoples in Western Europe and are unaware that an identical phenomenon occurred in the East. Whenever I look up at the towering Great Wall, I cannot suppress profound feelings.
From the China XX Korean History Compilation Office (中國XX 朝鮮史修編室에서) <67>
<59-67>

**Summary**

Sin Ch'aeho argues that ancient Korea, particularly the state of Puyŏ, was a major power that controlled vast territories in Manchuria and contested northeastern China, predating and rivaling early Chinese states. He reinterprets the history and purpose of the Great Wall(s) of China, contending they were constructed primarily as defenses against this powerful ancient Korean presence, not solely against the Xiongnu or other nomadic groups as commonly believed. He criticizes traditional Sino-centric historiography for minimizing Korea's ancient domain and power. From the perspective of Korean colonial history, this article is significant as a powerful expression of nationalist historiography by a leading independence activist. It directly challenges the historical narratives promoted by both traditional Chinese sources and contemporary Japanese colonial scholars, aiming to restore a sense of pride and historical agency by emphasizing the strength and centrality of the Korean nation in ancient East Asian history. Historians would find the article's specific interpretations of Chinese sources, its territorial claims for ancient Korea, and its explicit anti-Sinocentric and implicitly anti-colonial stance highly interesting.

朝鮮民族의 全盛時代
						申采浩
東洋의 古史를 연구하랴면 반다시 朝鮮 古文化의 源流를 탐색치 안코난 도저히 史의 根據와 史의 眞髓와 史의 體系와 統志를 작성할 수 업슬 것이다. 그러면 朝鮮 古文化를 강구함에는 先히 朝鮮 舊疆版圖範圍부터 劃定하여야 되리니 古代文獻의 考證을 어데서 차자낼 것이냐. 「神誌」의 九變震壇圖가 傳치 못하고 「檀君」系統인 扶餘가 祖業을 계승하야 滿蒙大陸에서 赫赫文化 1천여 년을 發揚하여 오다가 春秋列國時代에 及하야 漢族과 전쟁을 이르켜 或勝或敗하며 장기간에 亘한 戰雲이 息寧의 日이 無하더니 齊 桓公 때에 와서는 朝鮮族의 衰運期라 敗蹟이 날노 多하매 滿蒙西南部에 居하는 朝鮮族의 根據가 甚히 동요하야 永平府 以東까지 國域이 축소되엿스며 其後 「秦始皇」의 東南戰爭은 專혀 朝鮮族을 東北으로 驅逐함이라 「萬里長城」을 朝鮮支那에 國界로 하고 大工事를 起하야 萬餘 里를 延長한 것이다. 그런고로 古朝鮮의 文獻考證은 屢十百년 兵焚의 消失되고 또한 朝鮮의 國都가 敵禍로 因하야 자조 遷徒되엿스니 疆域의＜59＞ 變縮함이 더욱 만을지니 中古에 至하야 愚儒輩의 曲解抑斷이 大華主義에 盲狂하야 「檀君疆域」을 마음대로 주리고 「扶餘國都」를 되는대로 遷徙하여 심지어 「寧邊妙香山」을 「白頭山」이라 하고 「阿斯達」을 「黃海道」라 하며 「箕子」를 「大同江」에까지 갓다 뭇어 노왓스니 엇지 痛恨할 배 아니리요. 소위 史家덜에 적어 노은 國史를 보면 붓끗마다 다를 말끗마다 맛지 아니하야 譬컨대 羣盲 終夜길에 헤매며 目的地의 소재를 찻지 못함과 갓도다. 萬里長城이 우리 朝鮮과 宿命이 깁흐니 만콤 萬里長城考는 곳 우리 古疆을 찻는데 一證據가 훌륭하다 하노라.  「淮南子」 가로대 萬里長城은 (北擊遼水東結朝鮮)이라 하엿으니 「淮南子」 당시에도 萬里長城이 朝鮮과 支那의 國防界로 싸은 것이 明白하니 長城을 아는 자 반듯이 古朝鮮을 알 것이다.

扶餘朝鮮이 燕으로부* 嬚怨을 搆할 뿐만 아니라 扶餘가 자조 燕을 伐하얏음으로 「管仲」이 「九合諸候」의 歸衆을 督率하야 「孤竹」을 破함이 그것이다. 數百年間에 扶餘가 三朝鮮의 盟主가 되야 中國을 侵하다가 燕趙가 亡한 뒤에 또한 亡하엿스니 嗚呼라 그 間에 「神誌高興之倫」의 그 顚末를 기록한 바 有하엿나니 野心만은 君主와 辣腕한 將相이며 忠義慷慨한 將士와 帷幄密勿하던 謀臣덜의 可歌可舞可哭可驚할 만한 사실이 다 하얏스나 朝鮮이 이미 板蕩되고 古書는 一傳이 無하엿스며 中國의 「史馬遷」의 「史記」가 有하나 專히 「尊華攘夷」로 排外적 思想을 取하야 史例를 紊亂하엿스니 도로혀 「布魯帶克」의 鄰邦情形을 詳察하고 그 顚末를 具載함만 못하도다. 「馬史」는 그 一二次 隣邦과 接觸된 사실을 記한 外에 다만 自己中國人의 自尊好奇心의 玩賞物과 如할 뿐이다. 一國의 名을 訛하야 數國을 맨들엇스니 肅愼, 朝鮮, 扶餘, 濊東胡, 등의 國名을 訛함이며 혹 「甲」국의 사실를 「乙」國事로 記하니 「胡貉」의 事를 다 「匈奴」傳에 記한 등이라. 戰를 記하매 勝敗를 顚倒하고 風俗을 論한즉 그 美惡을 變亂하엿나니 國都人名의 可考 할 資料가 되지 못할 뿐아니라＜64＞ 그 國際의 交涉來往한 일을 一片의 記錄이 不存하엿스니 실로 慨嘆할 바라 이제 朝鮮國境所至와 燕趙秦의 長城을 築한 원인이 匈奴를 爲함보다 匈奴以前에 朝漢兩族의 관계되엿음을 비록 明確히 알기는 어려우나 萬里長城을 硏究함에 在하야 朝鮮古彊이 얼마나 크며 朝鮮의 强盛함이엇던 範團지 발전되엿던 것을 半의 半을 아라낼 수 잇스며 萬里長城이 東洋硏究史上에 在하야 실로 위대한 實物의 叅考됨을 確信하리라 하노라.

“附長城小話“

萬里長城이 東洋史의 最重한 秘藏의 寶庫요 建築上의 偉觀만 될뿐 아니라 東西兩洋에 역사적 影響이 至大하다 하노니 長城을 築하기 이전에 鑑하야 扶餘, 匈奴, 諸族이 長弓勁*로 鞭馬南下하야 漢族을 虜殺하며 財穀을 掠奪하야 甚하면 土地를 割據하고 自立國을 建設하는 등이라. 「春秋傳史記」의 보인 昆夷, 徐夷,  萊夷, 赤狄, 中山 등이 中國의 本土內에 在하야 建國稱王하며 橫行自姿하얏고 長城을 旣築한 후로는 東北의 大國인 扶餘로 故土에 還하야 다시 南下의 形勢를 取하지 못하고 蒙古의 匈奴族도 一枝로 西歐에 竄入하야 「峨特族」과 侵擾를 起하야 마참내 西洋史上의 民族적 大遷動을 이르켯나니 「聖羅馬」의 沒落과 「新文藝」의 發生이 萬里長城 有無에 關하야 國際文化 消長된 影響이 과연 엇더한가. 匈奴의 一枝가 한 東으로 扶餘에 爭抗한지 累十年에 마츰내 扶餘族도 遷動되야 東으로 徙하야 沃沮眞蕃이라 或稱하며 南으로 遷하야 辰韓六部가 되엿나니 其詳細는 此에 畧하고 讀史者가 다만 西歐의 民族大遷動만 알고 東西에 또한 同一한 現象이 有한 것을 아지 못함을 慨嘆하며 웃둑한 萬里長城을 바라볼 때마다 深刻한 늣김을 禁치 못하노라.
