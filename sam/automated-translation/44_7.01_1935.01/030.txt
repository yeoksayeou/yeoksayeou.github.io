Magazine Title: 삼천리 제7권 제1호
Publication Date: 1935년 01월 01일
Article Title: 三大新聞社長 「時局問題」 討議, 昭和十年 新春劈頭에 全半島에 울니는 建設의 第一聲 (Discussion on "Current Issues" by the Presidents of the Three Major Newspapers, The First Voice of Construction Resounding Throughout the Peninsula at the Dawn of the New Year, Showa 10)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Discussion/Roundtable)

Discussion on "Current Issues" by the Presidents of the Three Major Newspapers, The First Voice of Construction Resounding Throughout the Peninsula at the Dawn of the New Year, Showa 10

First Proposal: Request for the Hosting of a Joint All-Korea Newspaper Reporters' Conference by the Three Companies

(Reason) Representing the voice of the vast majority of the populace, we hereby request the presidents of the three major private newspapers to jointly host an All-Korea Newspaper Reporters' Conference. As you know, in the current state of Korean society, there are far too many social, economic, cultural, or political matters that require all of us, from every faction and individual, to gather in one place for thorough discussion and deliberation. For example, even considering just a few issues at the most basic level, there are first the problems of relief for disaster victims, the eradication of illiteracy, and the matter of spiritual upliftment. Thus, we must break through this atmosphere of long-standing stagnation. To do so, since the majority constituting the current "brains" of the peninsula are the personnel of media organizations spread throughout the 13 provinces, domestically and overseas—in short, the bureau chiefs of newspaper and magazine companies—if, on this occasion, the approximately 1,000 bureau chiefs from each company were summoned to Kyŏngsŏng, and representatives from various organizations in Kyŏngsŏng were invited to join, good proposals would arise where collective wisdom gathers. Moreover, it would allow us to be enveloped in a tense atmosphere after several years, indeed after a long time, so we believe unquestionably in its absolute effectiveness.<27> We propose that the agenda and convening costs for this meeting be discussed later, and request that, as a general principle, you host and convene this conference.
If a joint effort by the three companies is not possible, even a single company hosting it would be acceptable; please convene a bureau chiefs' conference.
We reiterate: Please recognize that this request is truly the voice of the populace, especially the intellectuals, who have long awaited such a gathering since the similar conference hosted by the Mumyŏnghoe (無名會) in 1927.

Song Chin-u, President of the Tonga Ilbo

The proposal is very good, but sadly, I cannot agree to it for two reasons concerning practical matters. Those two reasons are:
First, the issue of expenses.
Second, to uphold the essential role of the newspaper.

That is, if the Tonga Ilbo were to jointly convene all the branch bureau chiefs of Korea with the Chosŏn and Chungang companies, other companies aside, Tonga alone would have to ask about a thousand people to come. For each bureau, one bureau chief and one accompanying reporter would customarily come together, and for us, with many directly managed branches, we would have to summon the reporters from those places as well, so we must estimate no fewer than one thousand people. However, if our company were to ask the bureau chiefs to come, we cannot just tell them to come like for a Sin'ganhoe, Samch'ŏng, Ch'ŏndogyo, or Christian conference. We would inevitably have to consider not only round-trip travel expenses but also the costs during their stay in Kyŏngsŏng. Thus, even estimating just 10 won per person, at least 10,000 won in expenses would be immediately required. What comes to mind at this time is an occasion several years ago when our company held bureau chief conferences while touring the 13 provinces. Even when gathering at the central location of that province, the travel expenses for those coming from remote places with poor transportation were unexpectedly high. During the Hamgyŏng Province conference, it was said that those coming from places like Samsu, Kapsan, and Sinhŭng spent over 80 won just on transportation to and from Hamhŭng. How much more would it cost for all these people to gather in Seoul? Therefore, each company would have to bear a minimum burden of 10,000 won, which is truly difficult for our company's current situation, having barely escaped deficit financing. I believe it would be better to use that money, if available, to serve our readers through improving the content of our pages.

Second, although I understand from your explanation of the proposal's gist that there is a separate scope for what newspaper companies should do, you asked if it wouldn't be better for the newspaper companies to take the lead and initiate action, given that it is a special matter of our society where other organizations currently lack the possibility of convening a meeting. I too well understand the special circumstances of our society's situation, but a newspaper company is a media organization, not some social group or action-oriented business entity. Therefore, I believe it is difficult, and should not be done, to step beyond its original boundaries and involve itself<28> in practical, society-wide issues.
Originally, educational institutions and media organizations are fundamental existences for their nation and society, so I believe they should not be utilized for temporary situations. Whatever incidents occur outside, schools should just continue educating, and newspaper and magazine companies should diligently report and strive to express the state of social affairs.
Several decades ago, when a certain event occurred in this land, I let Chungang School go its own way as Chungang School. If, at that time, the school had been dragged into the temporary situation, Chungang School would have disappeared by today. Also, during the heyday of the Sin'ganhoe, suppose a certain newspaper company had joined forces with it and engaged in the Sin'gan Movement. Do you think that newspaper would have survived after the Sin'gan Movement eventually passed?
Therefore, since the principle is that schools and newspaper companies should endure eternally for millennia, I believe it would be better for another social organization to host an opportunity to discuss the various currently accumulated issues collectively.
With the above, I trust I have explained my reasons for disagreement. To add a little more, suppose the expense issue is resolved, and permission for the gathering is obtained from the authorities through proper legal procedures. And suppose talented people gather in Seoul. However, among the thousands gathered, not only is there a lack of training in assembly conduct, but also lacking the power to clearly perceive today's delicate atmosphere, suppose one or two unexpected remarks are thrown out during the mass gathering. Isn't it as clear as daylight that the gathering would promptly be dissolved? Let's concede one step further and suppose the conference concludes extremely orderly and smoothly. Even so, where would the power to execute the decided resolutions come from? Resolutions on paper were necessary during the past era of propaganda and sentiment, but now I believe it is more effective to retreat and teach even one schoolchild "Ka Kia Kŏ Kiŏ"<29> [basic Hangul].
However, the matter of convening a conference solely for our Tonga Ilbo to gather all its Korean bureau chiefs to handle business matters such as editorial policy or sales strategy coordination is an agenda item we too always hold as an ideal and are currently considering.

Pang Ŭng-mo, President of the Chosŏn Ilbo

Looking at examples from advanced foreign societies, when a significant pending issue arises for that nation and people, first the government acts, and then political parties and all sorts of social organizations move to discuss and handle the issue; this is the established rule.
In such cases, newspaper companies faithfully reflect, express, and report the situation on their pages. I believe the relationship between newspapers and social issues should always be like this to be correct.
Due to various reasons, this connection has not always been smooth in our society, and I understand that due to special circumstances, the active capacity of social organizations is not being fully demonstrated. I believe that is why you are now urging even the comparatively organized newspaper companies to take initiative. However, after careful reconsideration, I still believe it is right for newspapers to proceed as media organizations, while social organizations (if existing ones are insufficient, even by creating new ones) should step forward to work on issues concerning the entire nation and society. Even now, for instance, isn't there the Education Association for educational issues, the Society for the Promotion of Native Products (物産獎勵會) for industrial issues, the Society for the Popularization of Scientific Thought (科學思想普及會) for science, and the Korean Language Society (朝鮮語學會) for unifying the writing system? Even if the Sin'ganhoe has been dissolved, aren't there the previously existing organizations of Christianity, Ch'ŏndogyo, and Buddhism? If all these organizations lack strength individually, wouldn't there be ways to combine their capabilities? Therefore, even if a joint bureau chiefs' conference of the three companies were held, if it were merely to discuss the business affairs of the newspaper companies without involving political or social issues, the rationale might be different, but even then, each company has its own different circumstances.
However, we do have the completion of our new company building this coming May, so we are considering whether to perhaps gather only our company's bureau chiefs on that occasion.

Yŏ Un-hyŏng, President of the Chosŏn Chungang Ilbo

To break through the stagnant atmosphere currently pervading our company, hosting an All-Korea Newspaper Reporters' Conference as the first step in that construction effort is indeed a timely and appropriate proposal in the sense of gathering diverse talents in one place. Of course, it's good.<30> However, there are obstacles to its realization. To speak practically, setting aside a joint meeting of all bureau chiefs from the three companies, first, for some reason, even the three individuals from the three newspaper companies in Seoul do not meet often. Moreover, when traveling around the provinces, rather than cooperating, the personnel related to the three private newspapers are often red-eyed with competition to gain sales and advertising.
I understand that some preparatory work might be necessary here, but in any case, I agree with hosting a joint bureau chiefs' conference of the three newspaper companies. And issues like expenses are matters for further study.
The above is still just my personal opinion. As the company's policy is decided through the executive meeting of the company, please do not consider this the company's opinion yet. (The same applies below)

Second Proposal: Request for the Establishment of Libraries in 2,500 Locations, One per Township

(Reason) Because the Chosŏn Ilbo and Tonga Ilbo have already mobilized students for 7 or 8 years to promote literacy campaigns, approximately 200,000 people are overcoming illiteracy each year, and for this achievement, the Korean people express their deepest gratitude.
However, that is merely a preliminary movement, limited to "efforts for literacy acquisition," and does not constitute a movement that imparts "knowledge." Thinking now, while efforts for literacy acquisition are necessary and should continue as before, on the other hand, as a movement aiming one step higher,<31> we wish to establish simple public libraries, at least in the seat of every township office throughout Korea, providing convenience for the villagers there to read for free.
To achieve this, we ask the three newspaper companies to donate their daily newspapers free of charge, and also to provide a monthly subsidy of 1 won per location. That is, if you permanently subsidize approximately 30,000 won annually for 2,500 locations—an average of 10,000 won per company—influential individuals in each village could be persuaded to offer their own reception rooms (舍廊) as venues. With just that monthly expense of 1 won covering electricity fees, and arranging for donations of various magazines and books published in Korea from other sources besides the newspapers, establishing simple libraries would surely show astonishing progress in the culture of this land.
Therefore, please consent to donating 2,500 copies of newspapers daily free of charge, and at the same time, consent to donate 4 won annually per location in cash, totaling 10,000 won for 2,500 locations.
If so, methods and individuals to realize this will emerge.

Yŏ Un-hyŏng, President of the Chosŏn Chungang Ilbo

I certainly agree with the proposal to establish 2,500 public libraries. It would be wonderful if a sincere—(in other words, non-professional)—statesman (經世家) emerged to bring this movement to completion. While basic literacy promotion is still necessary now, I believe it is time to ascend one step further towards an enlightenment movement, a knowledge dissemination movement.
However, since this too has not gone through the company's important organs, it is merely a personal view, but regarding the request to donate 2,500 newspaper copies daily and subsidize about 10,000 won annually, it's not that we lack such sincerity, but in practice, the company's financial situation must also be considered, so I will reserve my answer on this for now.

Pang Ŭng-mo, President of the Chosŏn Ilbo

It is said that nearly 10 years have passed since the Chosŏn Ilbo initiated the literacy campaign to eradicate illiteracy. Since I have been with the company for only a few years, I do not know well, but according to hearsay, our company started this movement two years earlier than other companies, then it was interrupted midway due to the company's declining fortunes, and for the past few years, we have been putting forth our full effort again. Its actual effect is truly astonishing, with nearly 100,000 people entering the "world of knowing letters" each year. Therefore, our company's current thinking is that we are resolved to continue this movement permanently, regardless of any sacrifices.
However, there is also the thought, "What's the use of just knowing 'Ka Kia Kŏ Kiŏ'?"<32> so we have been wondering if there isn't some better plan beyond that. Now, the proposal for 2,500 simple libraries has emerged, and of course, it's good. Regarding the matter of donating 2,500 newspaper copies free of charge and subsidizing expenses at 33 chŏn per month, totaling 10,000 won annually—this is a significant matter, so we wish to study it further before deciding.

Song Chin-u, President of the Tonga Ilbo

1. We will donate the newspapers. If, indeed, 2,500 libraries can be uniformly established throughout all of Korea as you described, that alone amounts to 2,500 won per month, or 30,000 won annually, calculating at the newspaper price of 1 won per month.
2. The issue of disbursing 10,000 won in cash requires further sufficient consideration from the company's standpoint. Whether scattering 10,000 won would yield commensurate results is a point that needs thorough deliberation before deciding, so I will reserve my answer on this matter.

Third Proposal: The Issue of Nationality Affiliation for Immigrants to Manchukuo

(Reason) A large immigration society with semi-governmental, semi-private capital of 50 million won has been established, and through its efforts, 2 million disaster victims and others are said to be immigrating to Manchukuo over the next<33> 10 years. How should the issue of nationality affiliation for these 2 million people be properly handled? In other words, should they become citizens of Manchukuo and be incorporated into its nationality?

Pang Ŭng-mo, President of the Chosŏn Ilbo

If there are certain "special rights" granted only to "citizens of Manchukuo"—for example, if there are barriers preventing foreigners from owning land, rights to exploit forests and mines, or business rights—then since they have already migrated to Manchukuo seeking convenience in life, I believe it is right for them to enter that country's nationality and obtain the greatest privileges.
I believe it is advisable for them to acquire civil rights and the business rights held by Manchukuo citizens and live accordingly.

Song Chin-u, President of the Tonga Ilbo

Regarding the matter of continually relocating disaster victims abroad just because there are floods and droughts—
However, I believe that for compatriots who have migrated, the same affiliation issue as before will not arise. This issue was significant during the Chang Tso-lin regime era, but for compatriots migrating under the Plenipotentiary stationed in Manchuria, this affiliation issue will likely not be such a major problem. Though the future is unknown.

Yŏ Un-hyŏng, President of the Chosŏn Chungang Ilbo

I will refrain from commenting on any issues related to Manchukuo. Consequently, regarding the nationality issue that will arise after our compatriots migrate there, I will firmly refrain from expressing my opinion today. Although I heard stories when I met American journalists returning from Manchukuo the other day, in any case, I wish to avoid speaking on this issue for the time being.

Fourth Proposal: Relief Measures for Disaster-Stricken Compatriots

Pang Ŭng-mo, President of the Chosŏn Ilbo

During the floods in the three southern provinces last summer, the funds collected so enthusiastically amounted to only about 30,000 won for Tonga Ilbo, just over 10,000 won for Chungang Ilbo, and 40,000 won for our company, totaling around 80,000 won. Looking at the donors, this year there wasn't even anyone who donated 1,000 won, and indeed, not even anyone who donated 500 won. Most donations were from common people, giving 10 chŏn, 1 or 2 won, etc. Recalling this process, rescuing the over one million victims of this summer's flood and drought<34> would require hundreds of thousands, even millions of won, which is utterly beyond the capacity of newspaper companies.
Therefore, regarding permanent relief measures, we can only hope that others will devise them. That said, I disapprove of indiscriminately relocating them overseas. I hope that ways for them to survive while remaining in this land will be considered.

Song Chin-u, President of the Tonga Ilbo

Poor people, regardless of the country in the world, have historically had no recourse but to be saved by that country, that is, by its government. No matter how many days we collect donations through newspapers after obtaining permission, when would it ever reach one million won, or ten million won? It is only right that the authorities governing these people provide more active relief.
What comes to mind at this moment is when the South Hamgyŏng region suffered great damage from floods several years ago. The Tonga Ilbo collected 80,000 won in donations, provided emergency relief such as food and clothing to the disaster victims who had lost everything, and also gave 80 won in cash to each household to help them recover. Those people used that 80 won as seed money and have all become self-sufficient farmers today, having completely found their means of livelihood. Those who received aid back then still visit us to express their gratitude. Relief should be provided at least to this extent, but times have changed now, and due to various reasons that cannot be openly stated, it has become difficult even to provide aid to that extent.

Yŏ Un-hyŏng, President of the Chosŏn Chungang Ilbo

I do not agree with indiscriminately relocating disaster victims who lost their property due to floods and droughts overseas. However, I will speak on this matter after conducting further research.<35>

**Summary**

This 1935 roundtable discussion features the presidents of Korea's three major private newspapers (Tonga Ilbo, Chosŏn Ilbo, Chosŏn Chungang Ilbo) responding to proposals addressing pressing social issues: convening a national reporters' conference to discuss problems like disaster relief and illiteracy, establishing 2,500 small public libraries nationwide with newspaper support, clarifying the nationality status of Korean emigrants to Manchukuo, and finding effective relief measures for disaster victims. The presidents generally acknowledge the severity of the issues and the good intentions behind the proposals but raise significant practical obstacles, primarily financial constraints (the high cost of conferences and library subsidies) and concerns about exceeding the newspapers' proper role as media outlets rather than social action organizations. Yŏ Un-hyŏng (Chosŏn Chungang Ilbo) is somewhat more receptive to the conference and library ideas personally, while Song Chin-u (Tonga Ilbo) and Pang Ŭng-mo (Chosŏn Ilbo) are more cautious, emphasizing the need for other social organizations or the government to take the lead, particularly in large-scale relief efforts. The Manchukuo nationality issue is treated with particular caution, especially by Yŏ Un-hyŏng, reflecting its political sensitivity.

From a historical perspective, the article offers valuable insights into the socio-political climate of colonial Korea in the mid-1930s. Historians would find interest in the expressed awareness of widespread poverty, illiteracy, and the impact of natural disasters, alongside the perceived inadequacy of existing social structures and government response. The newspaper presidents' discussion reveals the financial precariousness of even major press organizations, their perceived limitations under colonial rule (hinted at by Song Chin-u regarding relief efforts and Yŏ Un-hyŏng's reticence on Manchukuo), and the ongoing debate about the press's role—whether purely informational or more actively engaged in social movements, referencing past experiences like the Sin'ganhoe. The cautious tone and practical objections highlight the constrained environment in which Korean civil society operated.

三大新聞社長 「時局問題」 討議, 昭和十年 新春劈頭에 全半島에 울니는 建設의 第一聲
第一案  三社合同 全朝鮮新聞記者大會開催의 要求
(理由) 吾人은 민중 대다수의 소리를 전하는 의미에서 민간 3大 신문사장께 이제 3社 합동으로 全朝鮮新聞記者大會의 개최를 요구합니다. 아시는 바와 가치 現下의 朝鮮사회에는 사회적, 경제적, 문화적 또는 정치적으로 우리들 各派各人이 一席예 모여서 爛商討議 하여야 할 사항이 너무도 허다함니다. 가령 최저 수준의 諸問題 數三을 곱자 하여도 爲先 罹災同胞의 善后문제, 문맹 타파문제, 精神振作문제 등 이리하야 沈滯積年의 이 공기를 깨처 노아야 하겟슴니다. 그리함에는 현재 반도의 「頭腦」를 구성한 대부분이 13道 海內海外에 배열되어 잇는 언론기관의 關係者, 속히 말하면 신문 잡지사의 지국장인 즉 此際 각 社의 지국장 1,000여 명을 京城에 소집하야 여기에 在京 각 단체의 同席을 구하여 諸문제를 토의한다면 衆智의 모히는 곳에 조흔 案이 생기겟고, 몃해만에 또 오래간만에 긴장된 공기 속에 싸여질 수 잇슬 것이니 그 효과의＜27＞ 絶對를 信하야 無疑함니다. 그 會合席上의 議題와 소집비용 등은 追後 협의하기로 하고 爲先 大體論으로 이 대회를 주최하여 소집하여 주기를 바람니다.
만일 3社 合同이 가능치 못하다면 1社 單獨式으로라도 可하오니 지국장 대회를 소집하시압소서.

第三案  滿洲國 移住民의 國籍歸屬問題案
(理由) 半官半民의 5천만원 자본 가진 大移民 사회가 설립되야 그 손으로 2백만명의 罹民其他가 滿洲로 압흐로＜33＞ 10個年 사이에 이민되리라 합니다. 이 200만명은 그 국적의 歸屬問題가 엇더케 되어야 올켓슴니까. 다시 말하면 滿洲國 人民이 되고 그의 국적에 편입되어야 올켓슴니까.

朝鮮日報社長 方應謨
「滿洲國人民」에게라야 주는 무슨 「特權」이 잇다면-例컨대 土地所有權, 森林, 鑛山의 採堀權, 營業權 등을 외국인에게는 안 준다는 그런 장벽이 잇다면 기왕 생활의 편의를 차저 滿洲로 이주한 이상 그 나라 국적에 입하여 그 최대의 특권을 가지도록 함이 올흘 줄 암니다.
市民權도 엇고 滿洲國人이 가지는 營業權도 엇어 가지고 살어가야 가할 줄 암니다.

東亞日報社長 宋鎭禹

第四案  罹災同胞의 救濟策
朝鮮日報社長 方應謨
昨夏 三南 水災 때에 그러케 열심으로 거둔 돈이 東亞日報 3만여 원, 中央日報 만여 원, 우리 社가 4만원 합하여 8만원 내외 밧게 아니되엇슴니다. 義捐 諸씨를 살펴보건대 금년에는 천원 낸 사람도 업섯고 심지어 오백원 낸 사람도 업섯서요. 모다 10전 1, 2원 등 대부분 細民의 義捐이엇슴니다. 이 경과를 回想할 때에 백여 만의 今夏 水旱害民을＜34＞ 구제하자면 수십만, 수백만금이 필요할 터인데 신문사의 힘으로는 도저히 不及하외다.
그런즉 恒久 구제책에 대하여는 다른 데서 생각하여 주기를 기대할 밧게 업슴니다. 그런다고 무턱대고 해외로 이주식히는 데는 不贊成이외다. 이 땅에 두고서 엇더케 살어나갈 도리를 생각하여 주시를 기대함니다.

東亞日報社長 宋鎭禹
가난한 백성은 세게 어느나라를 물론하고, 그 나라에서 즉 그 정부에서 구제하는 것밧게 도리가 업서 왓슴니다. 우리가 기부금 허가를 어더 가지고 신문지상으로 백날 거둔대야 어느 천년에 백만원이 되겟슴니까, 천만원이 되겟슴니까, 그저 이 백성을 다스리는 곳에서 좀더 적극적으로 구제하여 주어야 올흘 줄 암니다.
이 때에 생각나는 것은 수 년 전 咸南地方이 水害로 大慘害를 입엇슬 때 東亞日報社가 義捐金 8만원을 거두어 아조 의지가지 업시된 罹災民 每戶에 식량 의복 등 應急救濟하여 주고 또 현금 80원식 주어 구한 일이 잇섯슴니다. 그분들은 그 80원을 미천 삼고 오늘날 모다 자작농들이 되야 생활의 計를 완전히 엇고 잇습데다. 그 때 구제밧은 분들이 지금까지도 차저와서 謝意를 표합데다. 구제한다면 이 정도로는 하여야 할 터인데 지금은 세월이 달너저서 여러 가지로 表面化하여 말할 수 업스나 그 때 정도도 하기로 어렵게 되엇슴니다.
