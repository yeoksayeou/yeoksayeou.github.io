To My Bedchamber, Anthology of Masterpieces from the Peninsula's New Literary Scene of the Last Twenty Years
						Yi Sanghwa
"Madonna" To the Bedchamber (寢室) I polished myself all through last night, let us go,
To the Bedchamber—
The old moon is about to set, the footsteps my ears hear—oh, are they yours?

"Madonna" Look at the candle flame of my heart, holding tighter to the short wick, complaining without tears.
Suffocating even in the wool-like (洋털가튼) breath of wind, it is about to go out in deep blue (DIF푸른) smoke.

"Madonna" Let's climb, let's go, the cloud on the front mountain, like a goblin (독갑이), comes closer without feet.
Ah, lest anyone should see—my heart pounds, my lady, I call you.

"Madonna" The dream the night gives, the dream we weave, the dream of life that people embrace and ponder are no different, ah, let us go to my Bedchamber (寢室) that knows no time (歲月) like a child's heart, to that beautiful and old place (거긔로).

"Madonna" The stars' laughter is about to fade, and the dark night waves are about to disappear too,
Ah, before the mist vanishes, you must come, my lady, I call you."<178>

**Summary**

This poem by Yi Sanghwa is an impassioned plea to "Madonna" to join the speaker in his "Bedchamber" (寢室). The bedchamber is portrayed as a secret, intimate refuge, a timeless space immune to the outside world, where they can share dreams. The poem uses imagery of fading moonlight, a dying candle flame, approaching clouds like goblins, and vanishing mist to create a sense of urgency and secrecy. From a historical perspective, the poem, written during the Japanese colonial period, might interest historians for its intense focus on a private, almost escapist space. The yearning for a sanctuary away from the external world, coupled with the slightly Westernized address "Madonna," could reflect complex cultural currents and individual desires for intimacy and withdrawal during a time of national hardship and surveillance.
