Night Train, Selection of Masterpieces from the Peninsula's New Literary Scene of the Past Twenty Years
						Yeosu

Carrying the weary souls of the XX-ed people,
The night train runs panting and puffing.
Like a fugitive (도방군) escaping through a pine path with his bundle,
Oh, this monster running on the field path of the midnight border!

Does the sky outside the train window resemble my frustrated heart?
It feels suffocating, like my chest will burst, oh so utterly dark.
Leaning my head sideways on the luggage of wandering, I ponder.
Oh, where have the beautiful dreams of my hometown gone?

My village, cozy like a pigeon house, a pigeon coop,
What has become of it now?
Amidst the sounds harmonizing with the train wheels' noise,
A hazy, troubled dream unfolds!

The night wind of the Northern Plateau shakes the train window.
(Everyone is fast asleep, exhausted)
Oh, this desolate visitor, do not knock on the door.
Our hearts, with nowhere to lean on, are weeping now..

But the locomotive, piercing through the night darkness,
Shouts, "Charge! Charge! Charge!"

Upon the bodies of the weary people,
This tedious night weighs down heavily.
When will it ever dawn? When will it ever pass?
Ah, when will it ever awaken and raise us from this frustration?
(Written in 1927) <172>

**Summary**

The poem "Night Train" by Yeosu depicts the bleak journey of displaced Koreans on a train traveling through the dark night, likely towards the northern borderlands. The speaker laments the loss of their cozy hometown and feels suffocated by the oppressive darkness, mirroring their inner despair. While fellow passengers sleep exhaustedly, the speaker is haunted by the wind and their own sorrow. However, the relentless forward movement and sound of the locomotive offer a faint, almost desperate, sense of pushing onward, ending with a yearning question for liberation from the heavy "night." For historians, the poem vividly captures the sense of displacement, loss, and oppression experienced by Koreans under Japanese colonial rule, particularly hinting at migration (often forced by economic hardship or Japanese policies) towards Manchuria or other northern regions. The "XX-ed people" (likely censored, implying suffering or oppression) and the pervasive darkness symbolize the colonial condition, while the final lines express a longing for dawn, interpretable as national liberation.

밤車, 半島 新文壇 二十年來 名作選集
						麗水
XX되는 백성의 고달픈 魂을 실고
밤車는 헐레벅덕어리며 다러난다.
도방군이 짐싸가지고 솔밧길을 빠지듯
夜半 國境의 들길을 달리는 이 怪物이여!

車窓 밖 한울은 내 답답한 마음을 닮엇느냐
숨맥힐 듯 가슴 터질 듯 몹시도 캉캄하고나
流浪의 짐 우에 고개 비스듬이 눕히고 생각한다.
오오 고향의 아름답든 꿈이 어듸로 갓느냐.

비닭이집 비닭이장 가치 오붓하든 내동리
그것은 지금 무엇이 되엿는가.
車 박휘소리 諧調마처 들리는 중에
희미하게 벌려지는 뒤숭숭한 꿈자리여!

北方 高原의 밤바람이 車窓을 흔든다.
(사람들은 모다 피곤히 잠들엇는데)
이 적막한 訪問者여 문두드리지 마라.
의지할 곳 업는 우리의 마음이 지금 울고잇다..

그러나 긔관차는 夜暗을 뚤코 나가면서
『돌진! 돌진! 돌진!』 소리를 지른다.

피로한 백성의 몸 우에
무겁게 나려덥힌 이 지리한 밤아.
언제나 새이랴나 언제나 거치랴나
아아 언제나 이 답답함에서 깨워 이르키랴느냐.
(1927년 作)＜172＞
