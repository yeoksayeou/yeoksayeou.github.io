Publication Date: 1935년 01월 01일
Article Title: 배따라기 (Paettaragi - A type of traditional Korean sailor's song)
Author: 金東仁
Article Type: 소설 (Novel/Fiction)

Paettaragi
						Kim Tongin
It is fine weather.

Even though it's fine weather, it's not the kind of arrogant sky that looks down mockingly from on high at us tiny "people," possessing a dignity that we "people" dare not approach—a sky without a single speck of cloud. Rather, it's a sky like an understanding friend to us "people," with low, softly gathering pink clouds, extending a hand to grasp ours. A sky of love. I was rolling about on the freshly sprouted green grass on the slopes of Moranbung, facing the Taedonggang River, which ceaselessly pours its blue waters into the Yellow Sea.

This day is the third day of the third lunar month, the day for the first boating excursion on the Taedonggang. On the water, which looks dark from above, blue pleasure boats ride over the glistening waves, and from them, melodies of various kinds, drunk on the spring scent, float over, shaking the spring air, softer than velvet. And the Korean court music (雅樂) that drifts along with the songs of the kisaeng—slowly, long, fluently, softly, and also sorrowfully—as if determined to harmonize perfectly with all the tenderness of spring, makes not only the dark spring water flowing in the Taedonggang, the green grass shoots sprouting on Ch'ŏngnyubyŏk cliff, but even the fiery blood vessels leaping in the human heart tremble through the moist spring air. It is spring. Spring has come. The music made when the gentle little breeze awakens the dark Korean pines or brushes past the sprouting grass is a beautiful music unheard elsewhere. Ah, the green beauty of spring that intoxicates people! Having spent my life in Tokyo from the age of fifteen, unable to see such a spring to my heart's content,<226> I cannot help but feel moved doubly compared to those who always see it. Within P'yŏngyang city, one knows spring has arrived merely from the green color of the trees just budding through the cracked earth and the haze of the willows about to sprout; spring hasn't fully arrived yet. But in this Moranbung area and the long forest (長林) across the Taedonggang, reminiscent of fertile lands, the tenderness of spring has fully arrived. And the green hue of that long forest, decorated with quite well-grown wheat and barley. One can imagine without seeing the appearance of a farmer standing in that field, looking out with a satisfied smile. Clouds seem to be constantly drifting across the sky. The shadow of the clouds that shone upon the wheat moves away with the clouds, and there, a fresh green color, as if the world was just created, blooms forth. When the wind blows even slightly, the well-grown wheat lies down and rises like waves, dancing in shades of green and blue. And the kites, praising the leisureliness of spring, draw circles in the high sky, adding further to the fragrance of the beautiful spring. "In the warm spring affection / Descending upon us, / In the warm spring affection / Descending upon us." After reciting aloud a couple of times, I lit a cigarette. The cigarette smoke billowed up towards the sky. Spring has come to the sky too. The sky was low. It seemed low enough that one could easily touch it by climbing to the top of Moranbung. And the pink clouds, seeming even higher than the low sky, drift about here and there, tangled together. When I witness such beautiful spring scenery and listen to the whispers of spring like this, I can never help but think of Utopia. What is the purpose of our constant striving and laboring? Is it not also in the construction of Utopia? When thinking of Utopia, one cannot help but think of Emperor Qin Shi Huang, the "possessor of great character" and the one who "thoroughly enjoyed the greatness of man." Trying to find out how we might not die, he sent three hundred young boys and girls out on ships to seek the elixir of immortality; indulging in artistic luxury, he built the Epang Palace; enjoying daily banquets with thousands of officials; thus, Emperor Shi Huang, who tried to establish a Utopia here, no matter how tens of thousands of historians may curse him, was truly a hedonist in the true sense and arguably the greatest figure since the dawn of history. Only with such a purely courageous person<227> can our human history, even if it ends, be said to have had one "man." "He became a great man," I muttered, lifting my head. At this moment, I heard a strange, sad sound trembling from near Kija's Tomb (긔자묘), vibrating the spring air as it drifted over. I listened intently without thinking. It was the Yŏngyu Paettaragi. And the owner of that Paettaragi was such a skillful singer that any ordinary performer or kisaeng couldn't even touch his heels.
"I pray, I pray. / To the mountains, rivers, sun, moon, and stars, / To Heaven, I pray. / Our lives, like silken threads, / Save us, I pray. / E-ya, ŏgŭyŏjiya."
Just as it reached this point, from the water below, the sound of a janggo drum and a kisaeng's song rose up, and the Paettaragi became inaudible. I had spent one summer here two years ago. Anyone who has stayed for several months in Yŏngyu, the home of the Paettaragi, will surely realize an inescapable sorrow in that song. Yŏngyu—I don't know the name, but if you climb X Mountain and look out, the vast Yellow Sea lies before you. The scenery there at sunset is something anyone who sees it once will never forget. When the huge, fiery red sun dances as if it's about to fall back into the shimmering sea or soar back up, and from there, occasionally, only the mournful "Paettaragi" drifts from an unseen boat, I, being prone to tears, often wept. Seeing this, it's not unbelievable that the wife of some Yuan official threw away all her glory like old shoes and set off on an aimless journey on the water with a boatman. Even after returning from Yŏngyu, that "Paettaragi" was deeply etched in my heart, unforgettable try as I might, and the thought of wanting to go to Yŏngyu once more, hear that song again, and see that scenery again never left me. ... ...
The janggo sound and the kisaeng's song stopped, and only the Paettaragi mournfully drifted over. Due to the intermittent wind, it was sometimes inaudible, but matching my memory with the melody, the Paettaragi I heard was this:—<228>
"Came out to the riverside, / Saw me and then / Became utterly distraught, / Wondering if it's a dream or reality, / Reality or a dream, / Rushed over frantically, / Grasped me with delicate hands, / Crying out to the vast heavens, saying, / 'Fallen from the sky / Sprung up from the earth. / Came riding on the wind / Carried along the cloud path.' / Holding onto me like this and weeping, / Neighbors and relatives all gathered..."
Hearing up to this point, I finally couldn't bear it, stood up abruptly, took down my hat hanging on a pine branch, put it on, and climbed to the top of Moranbung to find the source. From the peak, the song could be heard a bit better. He was singing the very end of the Paettaragi, this part:—
"Even if I beg for rice / And make porridge, / Please, for goodness sake, / Don't become a boatman. / E-ya ŏgŭyŏjiya—"
Trying to find his direction by his voice, I stopped in my tracks. "Where could he be? Kija's Tomb (긔자묘), or Ŭlmiltae (을밀대)?" But I couldn't stand there long. Deciding to look for him somehow, I went to Hyŏnmumun Gate (현무문) and stepped right outside. The deep pine forest of Kija's Tomb spread out right before my eyes. "Where?" I asked myself again. Just then, he started singing the Paettaragi again from the beginning. The sound came from the left. "It's the left side," I thought, and following the sound, I wandered through the pines for a while until I finally found him, rolling about alone in a relatively open and bright spot near Kija's Tomb. His face was just as I had imagined. His face, nose, mouth, eyes, build—all squarish. The deep wrinkles on his forehead and his dark eyebrows indicated a life of hardship and a simple character.<229> Seeing a gentleman looking at him, he stopped singing and sat up. "Why stop? Please continue," I said, sitting down beside him. "Well..." was all he said, raising his eyes to look at the open sky. His eyes were good. The vastness and greatness of the sea were fully reflected in them. He was a boatman. I guessed. "Is your hometown Yŏngyu?" "Yes, well, I was born in Yŏngyu, but I haven't been back in about twenty years." "Why haven't you been back to your hometown for twenty years?" He sighed—"Why, it seems fate is the strongest." His words, that fate is the strongest, were mixed with unforgettable resentment and remorse. "Is that so?" I merely looked at him. After a moment of silence, I spoke again.— "Well, let's hear your story. If it's not something to hide, tell me about it." "Well, it's not something to hide..." "Then, let's hear it." He looked up at the sky again. But after a little while, saying "Alright," and seeing me light a cigarette, he lit one himself and began his story.— "It was the eleventh day of the eighth lunar month, nineteen years ago," he started, and the story he told was roughly like this.

The village where he lived was a small hamlet facing the sea, about twenty ri from Yŏngyu town. In that small village where he lived (about thirty houses), he was quite a well-known person. His parents had both passed away when he was fifteen, and the only relatives left were his younger brother and his wife living separately next door, and his own wife. He and his brother were the wealthiest in the village and also the best fishermen; they were relatively educated, and among the villagers, the brothers were outstanding singers of the Paettaragi. In short, the brothers were the representative figures of the village. The fifteenth of the eighth month is the Ch'usŏk holiday. On the eleventh day of the eighth month,<230> he headed to the market, partly to buy things needed for the holiday, and partly to buy a mirror his wife always envied. "Bigger than the one at the village head's house. Don't forget." His wife followed him out to the road, reminding him not to forget. "I won't forget," he said, and facing the rising crimson sunlight, he left his village. He "loved his wife, though it sounds funny to say it like this." His wife was unusually delicate and pretty for the countryside. (He told me this way.—) "Even if you go to Tenjugol in the city (P'yŏngyang), finding one like her wouldn't be easy." So, in the village, and especially at that time, their relationship as husband and wife was enviably good. The elders often advised him not to be bewitched by a woman. Although their relationship was good—or rather, because it was good—he was very jealous regarding his wife. And his wife often did things that provoked jealousy. Not that her conduct was bad, but she had a very cheerful personality, talked easily with everyone, and was naturally charming. In that village, whenever there was a holiday, using the excuse that his house was the cleanest, the young men would all gather at his house. Those young men all called his wife "Azumŏni" (auntie/madam), and his wife, in turn, called them "Azubani—" (uncle/sir), joking and enjoying themselves with them, her laughter-prone mouth always filled with smiles. Whenever that happened, he would just glare from a corner, and after the young men left, he would invariably jump on his wife without question, kicking and hitting her, taking back everything he had bought for her before. When they fought, his brother and sister-in-law from next door would always come to intervene, and when that happened, he would always end up hitting his brother and sister-in-law too.—His younger brother possessed a dignified presence rarely found in villagers, and although constantly exposed to the sea breeze, his face was fair. This alone could be cause for jealousy, but he especially couldn't stand it when his wife was particularly kind to his younger brother. About half a year before he left Yŏngyu—that is, about half a year before he went to the market to buy the mirror—it was his birthday. His family prepared food and ate well, but he had a habit of saving delicious food to eat a little later. His wife must have known this habit well, but when his younger brother came around lunchtime, she tried to give the food he had carefully saved earlier to his brother.<231> He glared fiercely, signaling "Don't give it," but whether his wife saw it or not, she gave it to his brother. He felt quite displeased inside. If only there was an excuse, this wench..., he thought. After giving the food tray to her brother-in-law, his wife stepped back and happened to slightly step on his foot. "You wench!" He forcefully lifted his foot and kicked his wife hard. His wife tumbled over the food tray and then slowly got up. "This wench, where is the woman who dares step on a man's foot!" "Did my foot break because I stepped on it a little?" His wife, her face flushed red, shouted in a tearful voice. "You wench! Talking back..." He stood up and grabbed his wife's hair. "Brother! Why are you doing this?" His brother stood up and grabbed him. "Stay still, you brat," he said, pushing his brother away, then struck his wife indiscriminately. "Kill her, this wench! Get out!" "Kill me, kill me, I won't leave this house even if I die!" "Won't leave?" "Why wouldn't I leave, whose house is it..." Just then, her determination "I won't leave" struck him deeply. He lost the desire to hit her further. Standing there dumbfounded, just glaring, he said, "Damn wench, then I'll leave," and rushed out the door. Ignoring his brother's call, "Brother, where are you going?" he went straight to the neighboring village's rice wine tavern without looking back, and sat across the table from the woman selling liquor there... ... That evening, quite drunk, he bought a dollar's worth of rice cakes for his wife and returned home... ... Thus, peace reigned for another three or four months. But this peace could not last forever. Peace was shattered again because of his younger brother. His younger brother, who had been frequently visiting Yŏngyu town since early May, started staying overnight in town for several days at a time from around the end of May. Along with this, rumors spread that he had acquired a mistress in town. After these rumors surfaced, his wife hated his brother going into town more than insects, and when he returned after staying for several days, she would immediately go to his brother's house to confront him, even arguing with her sister-in-law, his brother's wife,<232> for not stopping him from going. Around early July, his brother went into town and stayed for about ten days. This time too, like before, his wife, after fighting with his brother and sister-in-law to no avail, finally came to him, demanding to know why he was just letting his brother frequent such wicked places. He, who hadn't looked kindly upon this situation, shouted from the first word.— "What business is it of yours? I don't want to hear it." "Useless fool! Can't even stop your younger brother from going to such places!" In a fit of anger, his wife shouted this. "You wench, what?" He stood up abruptly. "Useless fool!" Before the words were fully out, his wife collapsed on the spot with a cry. "You wench! Where did you learn such insolence towards a man!" "Where did you learn to hit a woman! Useless fool!" His wife cried out tearfully. "Lowly wench, still? Get out, don't stay in my house, get out!" He shouted while striking her down. Then, he opened the door and pushed his wife out. "I won't leave!" his wife cried, running out. "Damn wench," he muttered as if spitting, and slumped down in his spot. His wife did not return even as the sun set and darkness fell. Although he had driven her out, he was waiting for her return. Even after dark, he didn't light a lamp, sitting stiffly with anger, waiting for his wife to come back. However, his wife's laughter, sounding truly joyful, echoed from his brother's house all night long. He didn't budge, sitting in that spot all night. At dawn, intending to kill his wife and brother, he went to the kitchen, got a knife, came back, and flung the door open. If his wife hadn't been standing blankly outside the door with a worried face, looking in, he would surely have killed both his wife and brother. The moment he saw his wife, realizing the love filling his heart, he threw down the knife, rushed out, grabbed her hair, yelling "You wench," dragged her inside, bit her cheek, and they rolled around together on the floor.— If I were to tell all such stories, there would be no end, but the triangular relationship between "him," "his wife," and "his younger brother" was roughly like<233> this... ... Digression ends— As luck would have it, there was a mirror at the market that suited his fancy. Compared to today's mirrors, it sometimes showed one's nose larger or mouth smaller, but at that time, and in such a village, it was a priceless treasure. After buying the mirror and finishing his shopping, thinking about how happy his wife would be when he gave her this mirror, he returned home, embracing the sea that seemed to overflow under the bright evening sunlight, without even stopping at the usual tavern. However, when he entered the room of his house, an unexpected scene unfolded before his eyes. In the middle of the room was a table with rice cakes. His younger brother stood in one corner, his head-towel fallen off hanging behind his neck, the ties of his jacket all undone. His wife, too, had her hair completely loose down her back, her skirt hanging down below her navel. Seeing him, his wife and brother seemed at a loss, standing motionless. The three of them stood there for a while, dumbfounded. But after a moment, his younger brother finally managed to speak.— "Where did that rat go?" "Hah! Rat? You caught a fine rat." He didn't finish his sentence, threw down his bundles, rushed over, and grabbed his brother by the collar. "Brother, really, a rat!" "Rat? You scoundrel! Where is the man who catches such rats with his sister-in-law?" He slapped his brother several times, then pushed him by the back and threw him out the door. After that, he rushed towards his wife, who was standing in the lower part of the room, trembling at the thought of the beating that would now come to her. "You wench! Where is the woman who catches such rats with her brother-in-law!" He knocked his wife down and struck her indiscriminately. "Really, a rat... Ouch, I'm dying!" "You wench! You too, a rat? Die!" His arms and legs landed haphazardly on his wife's body. "Ouch, I'm dying. Really, earlier, the young master came, so I put out the rice cakes for him to eat..." "I don't want to hear it, what nonsense from a wench who sticks to her brother-in-law..." "Ouch— It's true, a rat came out..." "Still a rat?" "While trying to catch the rat..." "Lowly wench! Die! Drown yourself in the water and die!" After beating her to his heart's content, he pushed her out by the back just like his brother. Then, towards her back,<234> he spat, "Make your living on a fishing boat!" Although he had vented his anger fully, his heart still felt uneasy. He went to the lower part of the room, leaned against the wall, and stood there blankly like someone who had lost consciousness, just staring at the rice cake table. Being a village facing the sea to the west, it got dark later than other places, but still, by around 9 PM (戌時 - Hour of the Dog), it was pitch black. He moved away from the wall to light a lamp and turned around to look for the matches (석냥). The matches weren't in their usual place. As he searched here and there, rummaging through things, when he lifted a bundle of old clothes, there was a squeaking sound, and something darted out. It then scurried away to the other side. "It really was a rat." He whispered in a small voice. And then, he slumped down helplessly on the spot. The scene that had happened before he arrived passed through his mind like a motion picture. His brother came home. His wife, kind to her brother-in-law, puts out a table of rice cakes for him. Just then, a rat jumps out from somewhere. The two chase after the rat. After causing quite a commotion, the rat hides in some corner. They look around, searching for the rat. That's when he entered. "Lowly wench, she'll come back soon..." He forced himself to think this and lay down on the spot. However, his wife did not return as the night passed and dawned, nor even when the sun reached mid-heaven. Gradually worried, he went out to look for her. She wasn't at his brother's house either. He searched the entire village, but no one claimed to have seen her. Then, around noon, about three or four ri down by the seashore, he finally found his wife, but she was not the same wife full of life as before; her body was swollen twice its size from the water, and her pretty mouth, which used to always hold a smile, was now filled with foam—a dead wife. He was senseless until he carried her body home. The next day, they held a simple funeral (장사). On his brother's face, following behind, there seemed to be a resentment, as if asking, "Brother, what is all this?" Starting the day after the funeral, his brother disappeared from the small village. One or two days passed normally, but even after five or six days, his brother did not return. So he inquired around.<235> Someone said that a person looking exactly like his brother, carrying a bundle like a mountain shaman's, was seen walking steadily eastward with the setting red sun at his back five or six days ago. Thus, ten days passed, twenty days passed, but his brother, once gone, never returned, and his brother's wife, left alone, spent her days sighing constantly. He couldn't just stand by and watch this either. All the blame for this misfortune lay entirely with him. He, too, finally became a boatman, staying close, albeit in a small way, to the sea that had swallowed his wife, and inquiring about his brother's whereabouts wherever he went. He got passage on a boat and set out on the waterways. Everywhere he went, he asked about his brother, giving his name and description, but he could find no news of him. Thus, ten years passed like a dream. Nine years ago, in autumn, his boat, cutting through the thick fog passing the Yŏnan (연안) coast, was wrecked by a fierce wind. Several companions died, and he lost consciousness, floating on the water. When he finally regained consciousness, it was night. And somehow, he was ashore, and saw his brother tending to him, drying him by the bright light of a fire kindled nearby. Strangely, without being surprised, he asked calmly.— "You—how did you get here?" His brother remained silent for a moment, then finally replied.— "Brother, it's all just fate." About to drift off to sleep from the warmth of the fire, he startled awake and said again.— "You've gotten awfully thin during these ten years!" "Brother, I've changed, but you've changed a lot too!" Hearing these words as if in a dream, he drifted off into a deep sleep again. After sleeping soundly for a couple of hours, sweeter than honey, he woke up to find the bright fire still burning as before, but his brother was gone, nowhere to be found. Asking people nearby, he learned that his brother had stared blankly at his face for a while, then, with the bright firelight at his back, silently vanished into the darkness without a word. The next day, no matter how much he searched, his brother's trail vanished, impossible to find. Thus, helpless, he got passage on another boat and set out on the waterways again. When his boat reached Haeju (해주), he went into the Haeju market to buy something and caught sight of someone resembling his brother in a shop across the way. He ran over, but the person was already gone. As the boat didn't stay long in Haeju,<236> he had to leave his heart behind in Haeju and set off on the sea route again. For three years after that, wandering here and there, he couldn't see his brother again. After those three years, six years ago from now, as the boat he was on passed Kanghwa Island (강화도), he heard a "Paettaragi" drifting towards the sea from a steep mountainside facing the water. Moreover, certain phrases and melodies were altered in his brother's unique style—a Paettaragi that no one but his brother could sing. The boat didn't stop at Kanghwa Island and just passed by, but since it was scheduled to stay in Inch'ŏn (인천) for about ten days, he immediately disembarked and crossed over to Kanghwa Island. Searching here and there, he asked at a small inn (客主집), and learned that a man whose name and appearance matched his brother's had indeed stayed there, but had gone back to Inch'ŏn three or four days earlier. He immediately turned back, crossed over to Inch'ŏn, and searched, but even in the small city of Inch'ŏn, there was no way to find his brother. Since then, through snow and rain, six years have passed, but he has never met his brother again, and doesn't even know if he is alive or dead.
As he finished speaking, a few tears glistened in his eyes, reflecting the evening sun. After a long pause, I finally asked.— "Sir, what about your sister-in-law?" "I don't know. I haven't been back to Yŏngyu in twenty years." "Sir, where will you go now?" "I don't know that either. Is there a fixed place? I just drift wherever the wind blows." He sang the Paettaragi once more for me. Ah, the unspoilable remorse submerged within it. The sorrowful longing for the sea. After finishing the song, he stood up, his back fully absorbing the crimson evening sun, and walked slowly towards Ŭlmiltae. I lacked the strength to stop him, sitting blankly, just watching his back until he disappeared. That night, even after returning home, the Paettaragi and his fateful story echoed persistently in my ears, preventing me from sleeping at all. Waking up the next morning, without even eating breakfast, I rushed to Kija's Tomb to look for him again. The grass where he had sat yesterday was pressed down to one side, commemorating his visit, but he was nowhere to be seen in the vicinity. But—but, the Paettaragi echoed vibrantly from somewhere,<237> seeming determined to make all the pine trees tremble as it drifted over. "It's Moranbong, he's at Moranbong," I thought, and ran towards Moranbong in one breath. There was no one on Moranbong. Nor at Pubyŏngnu Pavilion. "It must be Ŭlmiltae (乙密臺)," I thought, and went again to Ŭlmiltae. In the ravine connecting Ŭlmiltae to Pubyŏngnu, seemingly connected even to hell, densely packed pine trees, as if not letting a single drop of water seep through—all their myriad needles were singing the trembling Paettaragi, but he wasn't here either. Towards the sky of Kija's Tomb spread the countless needles of all the pine trees, and below them, the millions of blades of grass all sadly sang that Paettaragi, but he could not be found anywhere in this small Moranbong area. Going down to the riverside, I found out his boat had left early that morning. After that, summer and autumn passed, a year went by, and spring arrived again, but he, who had briefly visited P'yŏngyang, leaving behind only his fateful experience and the sad Paettaragi, did not reappear on the small Moranbong. Spring came again to Moranbong and Kija's Tomb, and the grass blades he had sat on and bent last year grew straight stalks again, about to bloom with purple flowers, but he, who lamented his endless remorse solely through the "Paettaragi," could no longer be seen in this small Moranbong and Kija's Tomb. Only the "Paettaragi" he left behind seemed to be remembered, commemorated, whispered by every leaf.

**Summary**

While enjoying a spring day in P'yŏngyang near the Taedong River, the narrator hears a profoundly sorrowful rendition of the "Paettaragi" folk song. He finds the singer, an aged boatman, who recounts his tragic past. Consumed by jealousy towards his younger brother and wife in their coastal hometown near Yŏngyu, he tragically misinterpreted a situation, beat his wife, and drove her out, leading to her suicide by drowning. His brother disappeared shortly after. For nineteen years, the man has wandered the seas as a boatman, haunted by guilt and searching for his lost brother, whose unique version of the "Paettaragi" he occasionally hears but can never locate. After sharing his story, the man vanishes, leaving only the lingering echo of his song. From a historical perspective, the story offers insight into rural Korean life, family dynamics, and traditional culture (like the Paettaragi song) during the colonial period. The themes of intense personal tragedy, inescapable fate, jealousy, wandering, and profound remorse, set against specific Korean landscapes, provide a window into the emotional and psychological experiences of individuals in early 20th-century Korea, potentially reflecting broader feelings of loss and displacement.

조흔 일긔라도, 하눌에 그름 한 점 업는-우리「사람」으로서는 감히 접근치 못할 위엄을 가지고, 노피서, 우리 조고만 「사람」을, 비웃는 듯이 나려다 보는, 그런 교만한 하눌은 아니고, 가장, 우리「사람」의 리해자인 듯이, 나추 뭉글뭉글 엉긔는 분홍빗 구름으로서, 우리와 서로 손목을 잡자는, 그런 하눌이다. 사랑의 하눌이다. 나는, 잠시도 멋지 안코 푸른 물을 황해로 부어 나리는 대동강은 향한, 모란붕 기슭, 새파라케 도다나는 풀 우에, 딩굴고 이섯다.

이 날은 三月 삼질, 대동강에 첫 배노리 하는 날이다. 깜-아케 나려다 보이는 물 우에는, 결결히 반이는 물결을, 푸른 료리배들이 타고 넘으며, 거기서는, 봄향긔에 취한 형형색색의 선률이 우단보다도 보드러운 봄공긔를 흔드르면서 나러온다. 그러고, 거기서, 기생들의 노래와 함께 나라오는 조선아악(雅樂)은, 느리게, 길-게 류창하게, 부르럽게, 그러고 또 애처럽게- 모든 봄에 정다움과 끗까지 조화하지 안코는 안두겟다는 듯이, 대동강에 흐르는 식컴은 봄물 청류벽에 도다나는, 푸르른 풀어음, 심저어 사람의 가슴 속에 봄에 뛰노는 불붓는 피줄기까지라도, 습기만은 봄공긔를 다리노코 떨리지 안코는 두지 안는다. 봄이다. 봄이 왓다. 부드럽게 부는 조고만 바람이, 식컴은 조선 솔을 께며 또는, 도다나는 풀을 슬치고 지나갈 때의 그 음악은, 다른데서는 듯지 못할 아름다운 음악이다. 아아, 사람들 취케하는 푸르른 봄의 아름다움이여. 열다섯살 부터의 동경 생활에, 마음것 이런 봄을 보지 못＜226＞하엿든 나는, 늘 이것을 보는 사람보다, 곱이상의 감명을 여기서 밧지 안을 수 업다. 평양성 내에는, 겨우, 툭툭 터진 땅을 헤치면, 파릇파릇 도다나는 나무색이와, 도다나려는 버들의 어음으로 봄이 온줄 아를 뿐, 아직, 완전히 봄이 안 니르럿지만, 이 모란봉 일대와, 대동강을 넘어 보이는 가난옥토를 련상시키는 장림(長林)에는, 마음 봄이 정다움이 니르럿다. 그러고 또, 꽤 자란 밀보리들로 새파라케 장식한 장림의, 그 푸른빗. 만족한 우슴을 고 그 벌에 서서 내다보는 농부의 모양은 보지 안어도 생각할 수가 잇다. 그름은, 작고 하눌을 나라단니는 모양이다. 그, 밀 우에 비최엇던 구름의 기름자는, 그 구름과 함께 저편으로 몰러가며, ꊱ�긴는, 세게를 아까 만드러 노은 것가튼, 새로운 록빗이 피서 나간다. 바람이나, 조곰 부는 때는, 그 잘-자란 밀들은, 물결과 가치 누엇다 니러낫다, 一綠一靑으로 춤을 춘다. 그러고, 봄의 한가함을 찬송하는 솔개들은, 노픈 하눌에서 둥그럼이를 그리면서, 더욱더 아름다운 봄에 향그러움을 더한다.  「다스한 봄정에
