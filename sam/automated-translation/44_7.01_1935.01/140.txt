Author: Not specified

Yŏŭido Flight School, What Does the Peninsula's Only School Look Like?

The Yŏngdŭngp'o road, whenever was it paved like this? A concrete road over 10 kan wide stretches all the way from the banks of the Han River. For a suburban drive, it has become good enough to likely be counted on one's fingers.

One day in mid-November, I boarded an intercity bus going from the Han River to Yŏngdŭngp'o. This was to find the Chosŏn Flight School on Yŏŭido, located between Noryangjin and Yŏngdŭngp'o.

Was it already over ten years ago? Back when I was a middle school student, I was led by a teacher to see some American airplane that had flown in, and later, when the famous An Ch'ang-nam's airplane flew in, I went once more, and since then, I had never set foot on this Yŏŭido!

Hundreds of thousands of p'yŏng of golden lawn fields and white sandy beaches stretch out. Far off in that direction, the blue waters of the Han River meander past Map'o's Chamdu, flowing leisurely.

The flight school stood starkly on the dry grass field, in the middle of the vast plain. Seen from afar, there was a large hangar shaped like a triangular yurt one might see in the Mongolian desert, and several tall buildings were arranged there. I wondered if all these were the flight school buildings<86>, and if so, I couldn't help but be astonished, thinking that this school in Chosŏn had larger buildings than Posŏng College or Yŏnhŭi College.

When the tin-roofed townscape of Yŏngdŭngp'o came into view far ahead, the bus conductress instructed me to get off here and walk in. Getting off midway, I walked across the sandy plain towards where the several buildings were. Just then, a large airplane was flying westward across the sky, its silver wings flashing, and in front of the hangar, two or three military police officers (헌병) with swords stood looking around alertly. And seven or eight gentlemen dressed in suits (세비로) stood gathered, seemingly engaged in some discussion.

I assumed that airplane was a training plane from the flight school, and that a youth in white (白衣청년) dreaming of becoming the next Lindbergh or An Ch'ang-nam was bravely making the propeller roar while performing maneuvers. And that the gentlemen in suits must be the flight school instructors (교관) who teach meteorology, mathematics, and skillful piloting techniques (逞才飛行術).

And looking at the tall hangar, three or four more airplanes stood lined up in their imposing forms. Beside them, a group of middle school students, seemingly on a field trip from some region, were listening attentively to their teacher's explanation.

I entered the office and requested a meeting with the school principal (교장). However, the clerk returned my business card, saying, "This is the Japan Air Transport Company (日本航空運輸會社)," and "The Flight School (飛行學校) is that small building over there."

I had thought the flight school and the airfield were separate. Wouldn't the flight school be where Mr. Sin Yong-uk and Mr. Kim Tong-ŏp are? And isn't the airfield the passenger airfield of the commercial company connecting Tokyo, Osaka, Keijō (Seoul), and Dairen? Realizing that everything I had seen, whose large scale had surprised me, all belonged to this passenger transport company, I felt an indescribable sense of desolation.

So, that hangar belongs to the airfield, and those several airplanes belong to the passenger company? That vast airfield also belongs to the company, and the group of students on the field trip came to see the airfield? Moreover, was the airplane flying energetically across the sky not a training plane from the flight school, but a passenger plane carrying passengers bound for Dairen?

Following the clerk's directions, I walked past the two two-story company buildings, past the large hangar, and past another warehouse-like building. After walking for a while, towards the western side near the hangar, a large, black-painted building resembling a tobacco company warehouse appeared. Looking up, I saw six characters written horizontally in large white chalk on the black planks<87>: "Chosŏn Flight School (朝鮮飛行學校)". Inside, through the wide-open glass windows, three airplanes rested quietly with their wings lowered.

I pulled open a glass door on one side. It seemed to be the office; a crude table stood in the center, with five or six chairs arranged around it. A large map was hung horizontally on the wall, and the smell of gasoline stung my nose.

"I came looking for the flight school. Is this the school office?"

"Yes, it is."

"Is Principal Sin Yong-uk here?"

"The principal comes some days and doesn't come other days."

"Is any instructor here?"

Just then, a young man in a suit, appearing to be around 30 years old, who was eating lunch, said, "I am Kim Tong-ŏp." This man was the instructor, a 2nd Class Pilot.

Since news had recently appeared in the newspapers that he had completed a tour around Chosŏn by air, visiting places like Keijō, Ch'ŏrwŏn, Hamhŭng, Nanam, Ŭiju, Ch'ŏngju, Kwangju, Pusan, and Chŏnju, I was already familiar with his name.

"Are there no classes today?"

"No, not these days."

"Where have all the students gone?"

"There are none."

"Not even one?"

"No, there used to be some before."

"Before?! Then are there graduates?"

"There are no official graduates yet either."

I found it hard to quickly grasp the idea of a school with no graduates and no current students.

"How about instructors?"

"Mr. Sin Yong-uk and I, the two of us, handle things."

"How many school airplanes are there?"

"There are three in total."

"Why has it become such a desolate school with three airplanes but not even any students? In the world, the Yŏŭido Flight School is still known as Chosŏn's only school, founded by Korean hands to train Korean youths as pilots. It's greatly anticipated for this reason, and greatly admired by white-clad youths (白衣靑年) interested in aviation. For the reality to be like this, isn't it too much of a disappointment?"

"I know that well. It's truly embarrassing. But it's all because of money. If we just had the funds, we could make it no less<88> than the flight schools in Tokyo, as we have to follow the directives of the supervisory authorities (監督官廳). Also, unlike before, there are now many Koreans whose piloting skills, both theoretical and practical, are excellent and fully qualified to be school instructors. If we could invite all of them, I know it could become a splendid school, but..."

Hearing this, in my heart, I thought of Ch'oe Ch'ang-hak, who donated 30,000 won to the Mining Department of an Industrial School, I also thought of a certain wealthy man from a certain place in Kyŏngbuk (North Kyŏngsang Province) who reportedly donated 100,000 won from selling a mine, and I also thought of Mr. Pang Eung-mo, who invested hundreds of thousands in the newspaper business.

"Regarding funding, I heard several years ago that a fundraising campaign was started with Marquis Pak Yŏng-hyo and others as promoters. Did that not go well?"

"I don't know well."

"For funding, you just need the school building and airplanes, right? How much does a good airplane cost per unit?"

"Receiving retired army planes from Tokorozawa (所澤) is not good as they are leftover worn-out machines. Even for those owned by civilian pilots, you need to pay at least 20,000 won to buy a usable one."

"How many years of study at the school are needed to get a 3rd Class License (3등 免狀)?"

"We need to teach for three years."

"Since you already have three planes, wouldn't it be okay to just buy a few more?"

"Among the three, one that I always fly is sound, but the others are damaged."

"What are your plans for the future?"

"Without losing hope, we are striving day and night to somehow complete a large flight school."

I lacked the courage to ask further. I could only pray fervently that this flight school would be completed quickly, that the air routes across all of Chosŏn would be pioneered and operated by the hands of white-clad pilots from this school, and that eventually, the day would come when spirited sons of the Peninsula (半島快男兒) are active on the air routes of all East Asia. Offering a deep bow and this prayer, I turned back.<89>

**Summary**

The author visits the Chosŏn Flight School on Yŏŭido, initially impressed by the large airfield facilities, only to discover they belong to the Japan Air Transport Company, while the actual Korean flight school is housed in a small, warehouse-like building. The school, despite having three planes (two damaged), has no students and no graduates, primarily due to lack of funding. Instructor Kim Tong-ŏp expresses embarrassment but holds onto hope for future development if funds can be secured. The author laments the situation, contrasting the school's potential and nationalistic hopes with its desolate reality, and ends with a prayer for its future success and the rise of Korean aviators.

For historians, the article starkly illustrates the economic hardships and limitations faced by Korean initiatives under Japanese colonial rule, contrasting the well-funded Japanese enterprise (Japan Air Transport) with the struggling Korean one (Chosŏn Flight School). The mention of specific Korean financiers (Ch'oe Ch'ang-hak, Pang Eung-mo, Pak Yŏng-hyo) and the discussion of funding difficulties highlight attempts at Korean self-sufficiency and the obstacles encountered. The explicit nationalistic desire for Korean pilots ("white-clad youths," "spirited sons of the Peninsula") to operate Korean air routes and eventually across East Asia provides insight into aspirations for national capability and presence even within the colonial context. The casual mention of Japanese military police (hŏnbyŏng) also subtly points to the background presence of colonial authority.

汝矣島 飛行學校, 半島 唯一의 이 學校 모양은 엇던가
永登浦 길은 언제 이러케 닥것든지 10여間 넓은 공그리드길이 漢江 江畔에서부터 一路-길게 連하여 잇다. 교외에 뜨라이브 길로는 아마 첫손까락에 곱으리 만치 조와젓다.
11월 중순, 어느날 나는 漢江서 永登浦 가는 시외뻐스에 몸을 실엇다. 鷺梁津과 永登浦 사이에 잇는 汝矣島의 朝鮮비행학교를 찻기 위함이다.
발서 십수년 전이나 될가. 그때 米國 무슨 비행기 한 대가 飛來한 것을 보기 위하야 중학생 때에 선생에게 인솔되어 가 본적이 잇고, 그 뒤 또 유명한 安昌男 비행기가 나라왓슬 때에 또 한 번 가본 뒤 영영 발길을 옴겨보지 못한 이 汝矣島!
수십만평을 헤일 金잔듸밧과 白沙場, 멀니 저쪽으로는 麻布 蠶頭를 푸른 한강물이 구비치며 시름업시 흐르고 잇다.
비행학교는 마른 잔디밧 우에, 너른 벌판 속에 덩그렁하게 소서 잇다. 먼-방에서 보매 蒙古사막서 보는 삼각형 둥주리집 모양으로 된 크다란 격납고와 놉흔 건물 여러 채가 노엿기에 이것이 모다 비행학교 건물＜86＞인가하고 만일 그러타면 朝鮮에 잇는 이 학교는 普成專門학교나, 延禧專門학교 보다도 더 큰 건물을 가젓구나 하는 驚異의 생각을 금할 길이 업섯다.
永登浦의 양철 집웅 시가가 멀니 눈 압헤 보일 때 여차장은 여기서 거러드러 가라고 지시하는 대로 중도에서 내려 백사벌을 걸어 그 여러 건물 잇는데로 간즉, 마츰 크다란 비행기 한 대가 창공에 銀翼을 날이며 西天 하늘로 날려가고 格納庫 압헤는 칼찬 헌병 2,3인이 두리번 두리번하고 경계하는 눈을 날니며 서서 잇스며  그리고 세비로 입은 신사 7,8명이 모여서서 무슨 공론인가 하고 섯다.
나는 저 비행기는 비행학교의 연습 비행기로, 지금 린드박-이나 安昌男을 꿈꾸는 白衣청년이 푸로페라 소리 욕감히 날니며 묘기를 피우는 것이라 하엿고, 세비로 입은 신사들은 畢竟 기상학도 가르치고 수학도 가르치며 逞才飛行術도 가르키는 비행학교 교관들인가 하엿다.
그리고 놉다란 격납고를 본즉 아직도 3,4대의 비행기가 그 雄姿를 나란히 하여 노여 잇는데 그 엽헤는 마츰 어느 지방에서 견학하려 온 듯한 중학생 일대가 선생의 설명에 귀를 기우리고 잇섯다.
나는 사무소에 드러가서 학교 교장에 면회를 청하엿다. 그리햇더니, 사무원은 내 명함을 도로 내어주며, 여기는
「日本航空運輸會社」
고
「飛行學校」
는 저 웃쏙 조고마한 건물 잇는 것이 바로 그것이라 한다. 나는 비행학교와 비행장이 따로 잇는 줄 알엇다. 비행학교라 하면 愼鏞頊이나 金東業씨들이 잇는 곳이요. 비행장이라 하면 東京, 大阪과 京城, 大連을 연결하는 장사회사인 여객비행장이 아니냐. 그런데 지금 내가 그 규모가 큰 것에 놀나면서 본 것은 모다 이 여객회사의 소속건물이든가 함애 일종 말할 수 업는 적막을 깨달엇다.
그러면 그 격납고도 비행장 것이고, 그 여러 채의 비행기도 여객회사 것인고. 그 너른 비행장도 회사 것이고, 견학하러 온 학생대도 비행장 구경 온 것인가. 더구나 창공에 성수잇게 날려가든 그 비행기도 비행학교의 연습기가 아니고 大連가는 여객 실은 여객기이든가.
