**Body Text Translation:**
林巨正 (Im Kŏkchŏng)
						홍명희 (Hong Myŏnghŭi)
Ch'ŏngsŏkkol (靑石골 - Blue Stone Valley)

Although O Ka, in the heartland of Ch'ŏngsŏkkol, had been implicitly treated as the leader due to the bandits' comings and goings, his seniority, and his persuasive speech, he knew better than anyone else that his character and strength were not suited for true leadership. Therefore, he had made up his mind to seize an opportunity to elevate Im Kŏkchŏng to the position of leader. On the day Kŏkchŏng returned to Ch'ŏngsŏkkol with Kil Makpong and Kwak Nŭngdo, several chieftains gathered in the *sarang* (men's quarters) of Kŏkchŏng's house. While discussing preparations for a feast, O Ka set the stage and began, clearing his throat once or twice, "Everyone, I have a word to say." He continued, "Until now, we have managed our affairs, big and small, through collective discussion. But from now on, there will be many urgent matters, and relying on discussion could easily lead to delays and failures. So, how about we now choose one among us as a commander, establish him above us, and conduct our affairs according to the commander's orders and rules?" He looked around at the various chieftains. Sŏrim, Ri Ponghak, and Hwang Ch'ŏnwangdong, being quick-witted, understood O Ka's intention. Im Kŏkchŏng, Pak Yubok, and Pae Tolsŏk, possessing discernment, also guessed O Ka's meaning. However, the clueless Kwak Oju and the less discerning Kil Makpong suspected O Ka wanted to become the commander himself and shook their heads. Oju spoke first, almost confrontationally, "Do you want to play the commander?" O Ka laughed heartily and replied, "If I wanted to, would you folks elect me?" Next, Makpong said spitefully, "No matter how urgent things get, won't there be a brief moment for discussion?" O Ka stroked his beard and answered, "Discussion is good, but done poorly, it can lead to ruin, like letting the dog carry away the ancestral tablet." <210> Sŏrim, smiling faintly, said, "We were talking about preparing a feast, and suddenly you bring up electing a commander. Does this mean we should elect the commander first and then hold a really grand feast?" O Ka clapped his hands and exclaimed with a hearty laugh, "A first-rate strategist is different, I see!" "If we are to elect a commander, how shall we do it?" "Let's gather at the assembly hall (都會廳) tomorrow morning and elect him by general consensus." "Shall we elect only a commander and not a second-in-command (中軍)?" "In my opinion, it would be fine not to elect a second-in-command separately, but let's decide that by general consensus as well." While O Ka and Sŏrim conversed, Oju bantered with Makpong. "Is he a commander or a blacksmith (대장장이)? If I become one, maybe I should ask him to forge me a new flail." "You already have a flail, why ask for another? Someone like me who has nothing might want one." "I want him to forge me a thick iron mace." Yubok, sitting next to Oju, glared at him, and Tolsŏk, seated beside Makpong, gestured at Makpong, silencing the two. The next morning, the chieftains gathered at the assembly hall, took their seats, and after settling in, O Ka looked around the assembly and began, "Well, let's discuss the election of our commander." He immediately added, "What is there to discuss? Let's serve Chieftain Im as our commander." Only Kŏkchŏng sat silently, while the rest of the chieftains unanimously shouted their approval. Oju and Makpong even nodded towards O Ka. O Ka called a minor leader and had him bring a pre-prepared vermilion-lacquered ceremonial chair (交椅) and place it in the middle of the assembly hall. O Ka and Sŏrim approached Kŏkchŏng and requested him to sit on the chair. Kŏkchŏng did not refuse or hesitate but rose directly from his seat. When O Ka and Sŏrim moved to support him on either side, Kŏkchŏng waved them away with a gesture and walked steadily to sit astride the ceremonial chair. After the new commander was seated, O Ka and Sŏrim, along with the other chieftains, lined up and bowed in military fashion. Then, the minor leaders and rank-and-file members were called in to pledge their devotion. After Kŏkchŏng received the respects, O Ka promptly appointed Sŏrim as Adjutant (從事官) and, following Sŏrim's opinion <211>, appointed Sin Pulch'ul and Kwak Nŭngdong as military officers (軍官) to attend (待衛) the commander on his left and right. Sŏ Changsa became Adjutant Sŏ, and Guards Sin and Kwak were newly created; otherwise, no one's title changed. The various chieftains were still called chieftains as before, and the minor leaders were simply called leaders. The seating order (席次) for sessions in the assembly hall was rearranged, and the procedures for the morning report were newly established. Three ceremonial chairs were placed side-by-side at the front of the assembly hall, and three chairs each were placed facing each other on the east and west sides. Only the central chair at the front was notably high; the remaining chairs were uniformly lower. It goes without saying that the high chair was for Commander Im Kŏkchŏng. To the commander's left was the seat for the elder chieftain O Ka, and to the commander's right was the seat for the new Adjutant Sŏrim. Chieftains Ri Ponghak, Pak Yubok, and Kwak Oju were seated on the east side, while Pae Tolsŏk, Hwang Ch'ŏnwangdong, and Kil Makpong were seated on the west side. This was the rearranged seating order. Every morning, early, the commander would come to the assembly hall and take his seat. After the two guards took their positions on his left and right, first, the various chieftains would approach the commander, bow deeply (鞫躬), and then take their seats. Next, the minor leaders would come up to the main hall, bow deeply, and descend. Finally, the rank-and-file members would enter the courtyard, bow deeply, and withdraw. There were even chants accompanying the bowing and movements. This was the newly established procedure; the commander's dignity (威風) was evident just from the assembly hall seating and the morning report procedure. Whenever something arose, if the commander wished to discuss it with the chieftains, he would; otherwise, he would consult only with the Adjutant. If he didn't wish to consult even with the Adjutant, he could decide based on his own thoughts and issue orders and commands to the chieftains and leaders. Thus, the commander's authority (權勢) was even greater than his dignity suggested. For three days starting from the day Kŏkchŏng became commander, there was a grand feast. After the feast ended, Tolsŏk and Makpong finally set up their new households, leaving the houses next to the assembly hall vacant. Kŏkchŏng had Pulch'ul and Nŭngdong move in separately. Since Pulch'ul was unmarried, he started living as a bachelor with a couple of subordinates, just like Tolsŏk or Makpong had done before. Kŏkchŏng worried about Pulch'ul's bachelor life and mentioned that he should find him a woman. Yubok, overhearing nearby, suggested, "How about matching him with Chieftain O's girl?" "That sounds good. How old is that girl?" "Isn't she nineteen or twenty this year?" "She's past the marrying age (過年), then." "She's strikingly large for her age." <212> "Should I call Chieftain O and discuss it?" Kŏkchŏng considered summoning O Ka but stopped and told Yubok, "It would be best to speak to both Chieftain O and his wife, and also ask the girl herself what she thinks. You go to Chieftain O's house." He sent Yubok to O Ka's house. O Ka had handed over Ch'ŏngsŏkkol to Kŏkchŏng and requested to retire immediately (閑養), so Kŏkchŏng had even excused him from the morning reports. Thus, he was staying at home, passing the time with idle chat, sometimes calling over Master Hŏ who gathered medicinal herbs or the fortune-teller Kŏkchŏng had detained, making them his conversation partners. Yubok returned and reported, "Both Chieftain O and his wife say they will follow your thoughts, elder brother." After hearing this, Kŏkchŏng immediately called Pulch'ul, who was nearby, and asked his intention. Pulch'ul clasped his hands together and replied that it was an overwhelming honor. Pulch'ul's marriage was easily settled, and they even set a date for the wedding ceremony. While Kŏkchŏng's household and O Ka's household were busy preparing, acting as the groom's and bride's (閨秀) families respectively, rumors spread that government troops (官軍) had appeared in Tŭngtchok Kip'ŭnnae to the east and Puktchok Surimi to the north. Immediately following this, urgent messages (報發) arrived from Yangjimal to the south, T'amgogae to the southwest, and Kŭmgyoyŏngmal to the west. All carried the same news: government troops were advancing. Ch'ŏngsŏkkol was now facing government troops from all directions. Pulch'ul's wedding was out of the question; there wasn't even time to discuss postponing it. The chieftains gathered in the *sarang* of Kŏkchŏng's house to discuss how to repel the government troops. Sŏrim, who should have been the first to propose a strategy (計策), kept his mouth shut, letting the other chieftains speak their minds one by one. Kŏkchŏng looked at Sŏrim and asked with a tone of reproach, "Adjutant Sŏ, why are you silent?" "I am currently thinking about one thing, and I want to think it over a bit more, so I haven't spoken yet." "If you don't speak now, when will you?" "I'll think about it more during the day and come back tonight to tell you." "In that case, isn't it better for you to go home instead of sitting here?" "Isn't it better to think quietly alone while lying down? I'll head home right now." After Sŏrim left first, Pak Yubok suggested it would be best to hear Adjutant Sŏ's strategy and then discuss further. <213> However, Kwak Oju grumbled pointlessly, and Kil Makpong and Hwang Ch'ŏnwangdong were clearly dissatisfied. On top of that, Pae Tolsŏk argued, what harm could come from discussing things even before hearing Adjutant Sŏ's strategy? Thus, the chieftains continued their discussion for a while. The discussion essentially boiled down to two options: one was for the chieftains to divide into four groups and simultaneously engage the government troops from all four directions; the other was for all chieftains to go out together and repel the government troops sequentially, moving from one direction to another. Kŏkchŏng hadn't participated in the discussion from the start, merely listening. When the chieftains started arguing loudly back and forth about the merits of the two options, he interjected, "Stop your clamoring. I will consult with Adjutant Sŏ and make a decision that avoids failure, one way or another." This silenced the chieftains' noisy debate. As evening approached, the chieftains all dispersed. Kŏkchŏng sat alone in the *sarang*, pondering various strategies to block the government troops, when Sŏrim entered. "Have you already eaten dinner?" "Yes, I have." "Dinner was early for you." "I hurried and had a few spoonfuls before coming." "Are you ready to talk about what you were thinking over earlier?" "I came quickly because I wanted to speak in a quiet moment." "What is this great strategy, anyway?" "I'd prefer to speak in a quiet place like the lower room off the inner courtyard." "Are you worried the words might leak out? Then I'll just make sure no one else enters this *sarang*." Kŏkchŏng looked outside and called out, "Pulch'ul, are you there?" Sin Pulch'ul answered "Yes" and immediately ran up, standing on the narrow wooden porch (*t'oenmaru*) outside the lower window (欞窓), hands clasped respectfully (량수거지). "Has Nŭngdong gone to eat?" "Not yet." "Tell Nŭngdong to go eat. You go stand outside and prevent anyone from entering the *sarang*. And you too, don't come in until I call you." "What should I do if the chieftains come?" "Can't you just tell them I've forbidden entry?" "Yes. Understood." Before Pulch'ul could even step outside, O Ka entered the *sarang* courtyard. O Ka <214> had missed the earlier gathering of chieftains and came to offer his apologies. Seeing O Ka, Pulch'ul quickly stepped down and blocked his path, saying, "The Commander has forbidden entry." Kŏkchŏng, hearing this from the room, came out onto the porch and commanded, "Step aside." After rebuking (ꊾ�지저 - likely 꾸짖어) Pulch'ul and making him step aside, he looked down at O Ka, who stood as if nailed to the spot, and asked, "Is there something you came for?" O Ka replied, "No. I couldn't come during the day, so I just came to pay my respects briefly before leaving." "Is there nothing else?" "Nothing." "Then please leave. I am just about to hear Adjutant Sŏ's strategy, and because he said the plan must not leak out, I have just given the order (녕) to forbid anyone from entering the *sarang*. This is the end of it (끄티요)." "Is that so? Then I shall leave immediately." O Ka turned and left. - A passage from the work serialized 1927-34 - <215>

**Summary**

This excerpt from Hong Myŏnghŭi's novel *Im Kŏkchŏng* depicts the consolidation of power within a bandit group led by the titular character in Ch'ŏngsŏkkol valley. O Ka, the nominal elder, engineers Im Kŏkchŏng's election as the supreme commander (대장), establishing a formal hierarchy with Sŏrim as adjutant and specific guards. A rigid command structure and ceremonial procedures are introduced to enhance the commander's authority. Just as normal life seems to resume, with arrangements being made for the guard Sin Pulch'ul's marriage, news arrives that government troops (관군) are converging on their stronghold from all directions, forcing an immediate shift to military strategy and interrupting personal affairs.

From a historical perspective, the serialization of *Im Kŏkchŏng* during the Japanese colonial period (1927-1934, with this excerpt dated 1935) is significant. The novel, set in the Chosŏn Dynasty, portrays a large-scale peasant rebellion led by Im Kŏkchŏng against oppressive authorities. Historians might find interest in how the depiction of organized resistance, leadership struggles, hierarchy building within the rebel group, and the conflict against state forces could reflect contemporary sentiments or serve as an allegorical commentary on resistance and governance under colonial rule, even while set in a much earlier historical period. The detailed description of the bandits' internal organization provides insight into imagined structures of counter-authority.

막봉이게 손짓하야 두 사람을 더 짓거리지 못하게 하얏다. 이튼날 아츰에 여러 두령이 도회청에 모혀서 자리를 잡고 안진 뒤에 오가가 좌중을 돌라보며 『자. 우리 대장 뽑을 공론합시다.』하고 말을 끄내고 곳 다시 『공론할 것 무엇잇소. 림두령을 우리 대장으로 뫼십시다.』하고 말하니 꺽정이만 잠자코 안젓고 그 남아지 여러 두령들이 일제히 조타고 소리치는데 오주와 막봉이는 오가를 돌아보고 고개까지 끄덕거리엇다. 오가가 적은 두목을 불러서 미리 준비한 주홍칠한 교의를 갓다가 도회청 중간에 노케하고 오가와 서림이가 꺽정이 아페 와서 교의에 가서 안기를 청하니 꺽정이는 엇새고 빗*고 하지 안코 바로 자리에서 닐어섯다. 오가와 서림이가 좌우에서 부축하랴고 하는 것을 꺽정이가 손짓하야 물리치고 뚜벅 뚜벅 걸어와서 교의에 걸터 안젓다. 새 대장이 교의에 안진 뒤에 오가와 서림이가 여러 두령과 가치 줄을 지어서서 군례로 보이고 그 다음에 적은 두목과 졸개들을 불러 드려서 헌신을 알외게 하얏다. 꺽정이가 예를 바든 뒤에 오가가 썩들어서 서림이를 종사관(從事官)으로 정하고 또 서림이의 의견을 조＜211＞차서 신불출이와 새능동이를 대장 좌우에 시위(待衛)할 군관으로 정하얏다. 서장사가 서종사로 변하고 신시위와 곽시위가 새로 생긴 외에는 칭호갈린 사람이 업스니 여러 두령은 전대로 두령이라고 닐컷고 적은 두목들은 그저 두목이라고 닐커럿다. 도회청에 전좌하는 석차(席次)를 고처 정하고 매일 아츰에 조사보는 절차를 새로 작성하얏다. 도회청 정면에 교의 셋을 느런히 노코 동편과 서편에 교의 셋식을 마주 노핫는데 정면 중간에 노힌 교의 하나만 특별히 놉고 그 남아지 교의들은 일매지게 나젓다. 놉흔 교의가 대장 림꺽정이의 자리인 것은 다시 말 할 것이 업고 대장의 좌편은 늙은 두령 오가의 자리요 대장의 우편은 새 종사 서림이의 자리요 리봉학이 박유복이 곽오주 세 두령은 동편 자리에 안게 되고 배돌석이 황천왕동이 길막봉이 세 두령은 서편 자리에 안게 되엇다. 이것은 고처 정한 석차이고 제장이아 츰닐직이도 회청에 나와서 자리에 악꼬 두 시위가 좌우에 뫼신 뒤에 먼저 여러 두령이 대장 압헤와서 국궁(鞫躬)하고 자리에 가서 안꼬 다음에 두목들이 대청에 올라와서 국궁하고 나려가고 나종에 졸개들이 마당에 들어와서 국궁하고 물러가는데 국궁진퇴에 창까지 잇섯다. 이것은 새로 작정한 절차이니 도회청 석차와 조사 절차만으로도 대장의 위풍(威風)이 보이엇다. 무슨 일이 잇슬 때 대장이 여러 두령과 공론하고 십흐면 공론하고 그러치 안흐면 종사관 하나만 다리고 의론하고 종사관과도 의론하고 십지 안흐면 혼자 생각으로 결단하야 여러 두령과  두목에게 명령하고 지휘하게 되어서 대장의 권세(權勢)는 그 위풍에서 더지 낫다. 꺽정이가 대장되든 날부터 사흘 동안 큰 잔치가 잇섯고 잔치가 끗난 뒤에 돌석이와 막봉이가 비로소 새 살림들을 차리어 도회정 좌우 엽*가 비게 되어서 꺽정이가 불출이와 능통이를 갈러들게 하얏는데 불출이는 처자업는 사람이라 전날 돌석이나 막봉이와 가티 졸개 두엇을 다리고 홀아비 살림을 시작하얏다. 불출이의 홀아비 살림을 꺽정이가 걱정하야 기집 하나를 어더 주어야 겟다고 말하는데 유복이가 엽헤서 듯고 『오두령 집 기집아이년과 짝을 마처주면 엇더까요.』하고 말하얏다. 『그거 조켓다. 그년이 나이 멧살이냐.』 『올에 열아홉살인가 스무살이지요.』 『과년 햇구나.』 『보기가 징하두룩 큼니다.』＜212＞ 『오두령을 청해다가 말해볼까.』하고 꺽정이가 오가를 청해 오려다가 고만두고 유복이 더러 『오두령 내외에게 말을 다해 보는 것이 조쿠 아주 기집아이년에게두 소견을 물어보는 것이 조흐니 네가 오두령 집에 갓다
