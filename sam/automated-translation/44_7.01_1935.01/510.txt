잡지명	삼천리 제7권 제1호
발행일	1935년 01월 01일
기사제목	檢事局待合室 (Prosecutor's Office Waiting Room)
필자	廉想涉 (Yŏm Sang-sŏp)
기사형태	소설 (Fiction/Novel)

檢事局待合室
						廉想涉
1
In the sunny spots, the damp earth had swollen up darkly, giving a soft, cozy feeling underfoot where the thawing mud yielded, but the wind still held a harsh bite, making one reluctant to take hands tucked into the sleeves of an overcoat out into the cold. I was sitting on the only bench in the waiting room of the Kyŏngsŏng District Court Prosecutor's Office when, feeling a chill, I sought the sun and stood leaning against the doorframe with my arms folded. Just then, a pale-faced young man, looking about twenty-four or twenty-five, rushed back and forth before joining a group of five or six people loitering about. Lifting his hat slightly, he politely asked, "Where is the prosecutor's office reception desk?" "Come this way," I said, gesturing towards the waiting room, which was about half the size of a storage shed. However, he only peered in curiously from behind me, then quickly turned and hurried back the way he came, returning shortly with a schoolgirl. It was clear they were siblings—from her round eyes and full lips to the freckles on her delicate face. The girl seemed uncomfortable among the men, especially in a place like the prosecutor's office, the mere mention of which made one's heart pound with a gloomy feeling. It was her first time being summoned, and perhaps overwhelmed by some unknown force, she kept her eyes narrowed and averted as she entered. She stayed far from the cluster of men, moving away towards the corridor nearby. Yet, even as she walked to that spot, face lowered and eyes averted, the way she held her head half-turned towards the men, tilting it up defiantly, struck my eye as strangely discordant. Meanwhile, the young man had entered the waiting room behind me, fumbling to take out the summons <216> notice, and stood waiting for someone at the reception window to look out. Spurred by idle curiosity—perhaps a habit unconsciously ingrained by my profession as a newspaper reporter—I turned back into the room after the man and glanced quickly at the name written there. As the name—Yi Kyŏng-ok (李景玉)—flashed past my line of sight, I knew the summoned woman's name, but before I could read the address carefully, he flipped the postcard over and placed it on the counter before the small glass window. ... ... Yi Kyŏng-ok! Yi Kyŏng-ok! What sort of woman gets summoned to the prosecutor's office? As a witness in a theft case? The wife of a suspect in the frequent communist or independence party cases these days... ... But then, would they have left her alone until the prosecutor's office leisurely sent out a summons? Musing thus, I stepped back outside the door to observe the woman's appearance again. Her attire—a willow-green skirt, a white cotton poplin *chŏgori*, and a purple winter shawl draped over her shoulders—suggested, by its simplicity, that she was past her schoolgirl days. Moreover, her slightly worn-looking yellow high-heeled shoes, still muddy at the heels, made her seem more like an elementary school teacher or a kindergarten governess than an ordinary housewife. In any case, her posture, her well-built frame with broad shoulders and wide hips, clearly indicated she was already a mature woman, I thought to myself. ... ... But why was she summoned? Looking at the man, neatly dressed in a clean white overcoat and equally trim yellow high-heeled shoes, topped off with a jaunty spring hat already ahead of the season, one could tell they were children of a decent family. Furthermore, the fact that her older brother escorted her, unwilling to let the summoned woman come alone, spoke volumes about their household... ... Could she have been called as an innocent witness?... ... Having waited here pointlessly since nine in the morning for some unknown reason, shivering for two hours, I was indulging in idle speculation to pass the time. After submitting the summons, the man came out and called, "Hey, go wait inside," upon which the girl, clutching the large shawl around her as if adjusting a traditional outer robe (*changot*), lowered her head and walked past me into the waiting room. I paused, stepping aside, and watched her movements again. Once inside, the woman took off her shawl, folded it neatly in four, held it over one arm with both hands joined, and stood facing a corner with her head bowed. From every angle, she did not seem the type <217> to be involved as a witness or participant in some sordid affair between a man and a woman, I concluded in my mind. Having drawn this line, I felt reassured, as if there was no need for further speculation. After a while, the lingering chill crept under my arms again, making me shiver. The man who had been standing beside me was, at some point, talking to another man, rough-looking like a laundry delivery agent. "Yes, I've often come to your house to take orders, I've seen you many times. But why are you here?" that man asked with a grin, looking back and forth between the siblings. "Well, I don't know either. But how long are they going to keep us standing here like this?" the young man said, stamping his feet as if they were cold. "It's considered quick if they call you before lunch. Who's the presiding prosecutor?" "It said M on the summons. Are there Korean prosecutors too?" "Well..." My ears perked up at the mention of M. I had come today because, although the summons was actually for the editor and publisher of the S Newspaper, I had unavoidably come in his stead. I had informed the prosecutor through an attendant that a representative had arrived and would wait if the matter concerned the newspaper, but would leave otherwise. They had told me to wait, and here I still was, unable to leave. Noon approached, yet there was no word. In the front yard of the prosecutor's office, overshadowed by the dark, dense archives building, the sunlight had already passed, and though the narrow space gradually filled with more people, an atmosphere of gloom, like late afternoon, prevailed. Whenever the silent, chilly wind snuck in, everyone would peer into the waiting room but hesitate to enter. Because of the shy woman—the schoolgirl with her hair in a *t'ŭremŏri* style and wearing yellow shoes—standing demurely turned away. By now my legs were stiff, and I couldn't bear standing any longer. After much hesitation, I went straight in. On the plank bench, wide enough for three, the woman sat facing away at one end, and another man sat at the far end. I plumped myself down right in the middle, closer to the man. Of course, I was still about two hand-spans away from the woman. Nevertheless, she quickly stood up and turned pointedly towards the outside. I felt apologetic. But... ... hasn't this girl ever even ridden a streetcar before? Thinking this, she seemed both pitifully naive and irritatingly prim. A little later, the man at the end also got up and briskly walked out. That the small bench was now all mine felt like a minor victory, perhaps? <218> However, I was now left alone with the woman in the narrow waiting room. I considered leaving too, but instead moved to the far end, rested my chin on my hand, and closed my eyes. The woman seemed to glance my way, then I heard the faint rustle of her sitting down again, cautiously. ... ... Why is she making such a fuss?—I thought, yet conceded that such shyness was indeed modest. Waiting for nearly four hours was quite a ridiculous affair. I considered going out to call the office and ask someone to come wait in my place, but hesitated, fearing I might be called while I was gone. As I sat there indecisively, the sound of shuffling footsteps came from outside, followed by a voice explaining in Japanese, "... ... This is related to the infamous plot to assassinate the Governor-General—the Yun Ch'i-ho incident, you see—at that time, the main building was too small, so this was built in a rush, in forty-one days and seventeen hours..." I stood up abruptly and went out to look. It seemed a group of about thirty people were being given a tour. ... ... The Yun Ch'i-ho incident! The Yun Ch'i-ho incident! I muttered, glancing up at the flat roof of the building I had just been sitting under. In the gloomy corridor, a light whirlwind gently gathered dust in a corner, then scattered scraps of paper fluttering about before vanishing somewhere, leaving everything still again. ... ... The Yun Ch'i-ho incident! The Yun Ch'i-ho incident! How many Yun Ch'i-hos have been met since the first Yun Ch'i-ho? I recalled how last spring, An Myŏng-gŭn, or someone like that, an old man released after many years due to this incident, had visited our office as a group leader. I remembered comforting the bewildered man while taking his picture. ... ... Calling people at nine and making them wait five or six hours—that's what courthouses do. Before this absolute power, even time hides its existence. Before Yama, the King of Hell, eternity and an instant might weigh the same; what are a mere ten years or so to those who hold sway over human destiny? Then, robbing a person of a day's life, a precious fragment of their existence—isn't that still a lenient disposition for those benevolent figures who hold the power of life and death? As I mulled over these thoughts, aimlessly, Y and K from C Company shuffled in. "Hey—what's this? What brings you here?" they greeted me, then looked towards the other side, "T! Brother, have you been caught too? Are we having a newspaper reporters' convention here?" Y chuckled heartily. Turning around, I saw T from D Company. "What's going on? Are they planning to do something <219> after gathering everyone? Is it the same case?" I asked. "Well—" they replied, saying they were called as witnesses and had sent in their business cards. Meanwhile, T and the woman I saw earlier, standing at a right angle to him, watched us intently, her round eyes wider than before. "But look here. There's one behind you too, you know. Does beauty come even to a place like this!" K said with a laugh, having apparently noticed the woman peering out from behind me. Feeling embarrassed, I tried to change the subject, "Who summoned you guys?" "Prosecutor M, they say?" he replied. "Me too, Prosecutor M... ... This is terrible! I've waited five hours in the cold," I grumbled pointlessly. "Five hours? But you'll probably have to wait a bit longer! Did you bring the summons notice?" K asked, his tone suggesting that since they were called by phone and had sent in their cards, their treatment might be different. "Anyway, what does it matter if you wait five hours or six? You get a good show, might even find material for a novel..." K added, glancing again at the woman and laughing. "A good show?" "Just turn around and look," Y whispered, making a pointless remark barely audible. The woman seemed to be peering out curiously too, but I pretended not to notice and retorted, laughing myself, "Instead of me, you guys can enjoy that 'show' to your heart's content, stuck here for five hours."

2
A short while later, an office boy called Y and K inside. But if they were seeing the same prosecutor, and I had to wait until after Y and K were finished, who knew when my turn would come. It seemed pitch black. Moreover, it was annoying that they, having arrived later, were called in first. Even when you go into a diner for a bowl of noodles, common courtesy dictates serving the person who entered first, even if only by a step. But perhaps the courthouse is a land where the sun rises in the West, and prosecutors are the sort who view the world from under someone's crotch. Deciding that keeping my mouth shut was the best policy, I waited. Then, they came out again, saying they were told to wait a little longer. About ten minutes later, the three reporters from the three newspapers were called in together. Figuring that even if Yama himself were calling, it paid to be a step ahead on the path one was already taking, I followed closely behind the office boy. "You're not the person named, are you? Then file a report stating the principal isn't here," he <220> said, handing back the summons. I had already fumed inwardly that courthouse people lacked even the courtesy of a soup kitchen waitress, but really, does one have to pore over the Six Codes countless times to become this enlightened? I glared at the sullen-faced Prosecutor M, wanting to retort, "Look here, sir. What did I tell you at first? I said I am so-and-so, and I will wait if you have something to say, otherwise I'll leave. And you keep a busy person waiting for half a day, only to say this? I'm not trying to argue just for the sake of it, but isn't it infuriating that you made me wait all this time?" I wanted to lash out at him like a thunderclap... ...but then thought, "No, arguing with the *sŏllŏngt'ang* house server later would be a shrewder move than having such a bright exchange here." As I turned to leave, I heard instructions being given to L from D Company to return at three in the afternoon, as the prosecutor was now going into a public trial. I didn't know what happened to Y and K. Outside, K, who was standing alone, saw me and asked, "What happened?" "Go enjoy the show thoroughly for me," I replied and hurried away. Since then, whenever I see a soup kitchen waitress, the suspicion arises, "Why didn't that person study the Six Codes?"—a suspicion I still haven't been able to resolve.

3
Back at the office, when I mentioned that reporters from three newspapers had all been summoned by the same Prosecutor M, C from the society desk remarked, "Oh, then it must be the Yi Kyŏng-ok case!" My ears perked up again. "The Yi Kyŏng-ok case?" At that time, my mind was preoccupied with other matters, leaving me no time to pay close attention to the daily newspaper editing. I mostly just skimmed the headlines, so I had no reason to know who Yi Kyŏng-ok was or what her case involved. However, recalling that the name on the summons presented by the man with the schoolgirl at the prosecutor's reception desk was Yi Kyŏng-ok, and believing that she was undoubtedly the owner of that name, I felt embarrassed. Not only was this news to me, but if it turned out I had actually gone to the prosecutor's office because of this Yi Kyŏng-ok—even though I went reluctantly as a proxy for the publisher—it would make me look rather foolish. Moreover, realizing my ignorance about a matter that had apparently become office gossip due to my responsibilities felt mortifying. <221> "What case? You mean she filed a complaint? And why didn't you tell me sooner if something was up?" Forgetting my own negligence, I questioned him, my tone bordering on reproach. "I didn't know it would go as far as a formal complaint, but why don't you know? About that story of the secret prostitute (밀매음녀) that caused such a stir, like a 'romance'!" Hearing 'secret prostitute', I was startled for a second time. "Well, I handle the main legal section too, but how would I remember every Tom, Dick, or Harry—whether it's Yi Kyŏng-ok or Kim Kyŏng-ok?" I replied, trying not to reveal my lack of attention to my own newspaper's articles. "I don't recall the details clearly. So, what happened?" I asked. "She's a wicked woman! Supposedly attending K Academy or some such place, her specialty is cozying up to any guy who looks like he has money, saying she wants to marry him, lures him to her house with a policeman backing her up, then strips him clean and kicks him out. Won't the Ponjŏng Police Station arrest her today or tomorrow?" C, who wrote the article, chuckled as if pleased. "Arrest her? But I just saw her at the prosecutor's office. She didn't seem like that kind of woman..." I said, smiling absently. "What? You saw her? What did she look like? Very demure, I suppose," C responded eagerly, stepping closer as if this was truly fascinating news. "She seemed slight of build, but her clothes and shy demeanor didn't suggest someone so depraved. And seeing her escorted by that modest-looking brother, they seemed like children from a respectable family," I stated, looking C straight in the eye, implying I couldn't easily believe the article. "That's how women like that always act, putting on airs," C retorted, adding that her brother had visited him drunk the other day, rambling incoherently. C's tone suggested that when it came to judging a woman's character, I was just a beginner. However (if the brother came, questioned him, and even filed a complaint, perhaps she really was wronged. Newspapers claim to be the "guide of the masses," the "town crier of society," the "preservative of morality (도덕상방부재?)," but reporters are human too. Being human and not divine spirits, it's naturally impossible to know the ins and outs of someone's private conduct. Moreover, who's to say some man, spurned after having feelings for her, didn't deceive the reporter into publishing such a story out of spite, just to disgrace her? No matter how authoritative the newspaper, damaging a young woman's future prospects—especially when dealing with a public at a time when the power of print could make people believe <222> that *meju* (fermented soybean blocks) is made from red beans—is a serious matter. Whether such facts exist or not, the moment the story appears in print is the moment the needle of fate shifts for that woman. No matter how precious the newspaper's authority or the reporter's standing, meddling with an individual's destiny is, above all, a great sin.) As someone holding such views, I couldn't help but feel a pang of conscience whenever faced with such issues. "But where did you get that material?" I asked after a moment's pause. "I wrote it based on what a detective from the police department said, but I'll write more today," C replied, looking triumphant. "Write more? How can you write without doing any direct, thorough investigation, just based on hearsay? Searching for independence activists or investigating activities in some field—even if there's an occasional false report (허보), it might not matter much. But articles concerning individuals or damaging someone's reputation require extreme caution. Otherwise, aside from unpleasant problems arising later, you must consider the position of the person involved," I said, my tone implying that, based on my impression of the woman, I simply couldn't believe the article. "She must have really caught your eye," A, sitting nearby, remarked with a laugh. "What's there to worry about when the facts are clear? Something happened yesterday evening, and the Ponjŏng Police Station is planning to bring her in for questioning today," C asserted, then related the following story. A young man from the countryside near Yangju sold his cow and came to Seoul intending to use the money as capital for some venture. He was taken in by an acquaintance from his hometown, a drifter type, who promised to find him a cheap and good inn. As fate would have it, he was led to Yi Kyŏng-ok's house. Yi Kyŏng-ok's place didn't have an inn sign; the large gate and the way they lived didn't suggest hardship. Furthermore, perhaps due to something the introducer said, the hospitality was lavish. They insisted he shouldn't worry about the cost of meals. The young schoolgirl inside flirting with him seemed suspicious, yet not entirely unpleasant. After about two days, the friend who introduced him said, "The young lady of the house is destined for marriage with someone born under compatible astrological signs (소년과 수로, 화에 어서?), and she's currently attending girls' school. She wants to find a suitable husband but dislikes the superficial young men of Seoul, so she's always looking. Her older brother asked me to find someone. How about you try your luck? She seems well-off, so no worries there. Give it a shot; if it works out, great <223>; if not, no harm done. She seems interested too, so she's unlikely to refuse. Besides, even if you get involved a couple of times and she dislikes it later, that's that. Doesn't cost anything... just fooling around for a while... Anyway, just do as I say later and order a platter of Chinese food, and it'll all work out." Goaded by this, partly fearful yet enticed by the prospect—having about two hundred won, spending ten won seemed trivial compared to securing lodging for months, and maybe even hitting the jackpot by marrying into the family—his mind raced with fantasies and greed. He kept fiddling with his purse, pulling out his round pocket watch (석경?) to check the time, mulling it over. Finally, that night, the four of them—the young man, his friend, and the sibling hosts—played Hwatu (화투) cards late into the night. As instructed by his friend, he ordered Chinese food (청료리). Flushed and pretending to be drunk, he allowed himself to be led wherever they took him. After three days passed in a blur, barely noticing dawn or dusk, gradually getting drawn deeper, he found that the gambling debts owed to the girl's brother amounted to nearly half the money he had brought. Only then did his friend say, "Hey, what kind of fool plays like that? Now that things are like this, do you think they'll just let you leave?" He then slipped away, claiming he was going to arrange some money. "If her mother finds out, who knows what hell will break loose..." the friend warned, thoroughly frightening him before leading him out. Still wanting to see the girl, even briefly, he consulted his friend, who told him, "Wait in front of Pagoda Park gate. I'll bring her right out!" But the friend went off and didn't show even a shadow as dusk fell. Realizing he'd been tricked, he thought of going back, but the winding streets and unclear address, coupled with the fear of facing the brother and mother, left him wandering aimlessly. He went to where his friend supposedly stayed, only to be told, "That gentleman might come by for fun sometimes, but you can't find him now." At his wit's end, furious, he went into a tavern near Changgyo, spent all his remaining money, and drunkenly declared to anyone who would listen, "Can such things happen in this world?" A policeman from Ponjŏng station overheard him and was planning to bring the girl in for questioning. C related the gist of the article he was about to write, adding, "Such incidents are commonplace (례상사), really, but the fact that she brazenly goes around threatening to sue the newspaper makes it infuriating. She needs to be taught a lesson <224> once," he concluded, laughing cheerfully. "But that story also came from the same detective you mentioned earlier, right?" I pressed once more, still skeptical. "No. After it appeared simultaneously in three newspapers, it became an issue at Ponjŏng station, and they're keeping an eye on her. Then this new incident was uncovered, and the judiciary police are apparently thrilled. Besides, once the victim's name is clear, there's no room for doubt," C replied, seeming to regard my persistent questioning as either naive or perhaps suggesting I was the sort of person easily fooled—someone who might indeed sell a cow (소 牛) only to be tricked. However, the phrase "such incidents are commonplace..." struck me as newly alarming, and I found it difficult to understand the mentality of the judiciary police who were reportedly "thrilled" at uncovering new facts. I fell silent. Afterwards, whether Yi Kyŏng-ok sat in the Ponjŏng station jail, clad in a *chima chŏgori* without *korŭm* (coat-strings), enduring prison fare (콩밥에 혈를 빨며? surviving on beans and rice?), or whether she was still enjoying the taste of Chinese food with some other fool, or perhaps sitting demurely under the blackboard in the third year at K Academy—I never found out. The lawsuit also fizzled out, and I never had the chance to ask C about it again. But even now, when I see a schoolgirl on the street with freckles (죽은개) and *t'ŭremŏri* hair, wearing a willow-green skirt—a modern young woman who quickly lowers her head when she sees a man—I find myself staring after her like a dazed person for a moment. ... ...(End)... ...<225>

**Summary**

The narrator, a journalist, waits for hours at the Kyŏngsŏng Prosecutor's Office after being summoned as a proxy. He observes a young woman, Yi Kyŏng-ok, and her brother, also summoned. Intrigued by her modest appearance, he wonders why she's there. Later, at his newspaper office, he learns Yi Kyŏng-ok was the subject of an article accusing her of being a scam artist and "secret prostitute" who lures men, cheats them via gambling, and is about to be arrested. The narrator feels conflicted, as his impression of her contradicts the story, leading him to reflect on journalistic ethics, the power of print, and the potential for false accusations to ruin lives, especially those of young women. The story ends unresolved, with the narrator still haunted by the image of women resembling Yi Kyŏng-ok.

For historians, this story offers insights into the colonial Korean judicial system's atmosphere (long waits, bureaucratic indifference), the workings of the press and its perceived power and potential for harm, and social anxieties surrounding modern women, morality, and urban scams in 1930s Seoul. The casual reference to the Yun Ch'i-ho incident and its connection to the courthouse building itself provides a tangible link to the political history of the era woven into everyday life.
