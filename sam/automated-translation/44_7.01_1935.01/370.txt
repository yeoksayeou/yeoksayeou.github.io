海曲三章, 半島 新文壇 二十年來 名作選集
						梁柱東
Three Songs of the Sea Bend

Though it's not the boat carrying my beloved,
When I see the white sail turning at the edge of the sky,
For no reason, this heart yearns.

Standing alone by the seaside,
Gazing at the sun setting over Changsan mountain,
Without knowing, the rising tide wets my feet.

2. In the morning, as the sun rises,
I go to gather oysters on the rock;
In the evening, I gather turban shells in the shallow water.

On nights without waves,
I take a fishing boat near Talnae Island;
If the fish don't bite, I just return, bathed in moonlight.

3. Oh, you,
If you wish to write Poetry, come to the sea;
If you wish to breathe breath like the sea,

Oh, my love,
If you wish to love, come to the sea;
If you wish to immerse in passion like the sea.

After Parting

I see the footprints.
I see the footprints.
Clear upon the sand,
I see the footprints.

Traces where a friend walked one day.
Traces where the friend I cannot see walked.
Perhaps my friend will follow these footprints
And return again.

Though my love is unlikely to come this way,
Even if my love were truly to return,
The wind and waves will wash the sand,
How could they find the traces of old days?
I see the footprints.
I see the footprints.
By the seaside, the tiny
Footprints I see.<169>

**Summary**

This entry presents two poems by Yang Chu-dong. "Three Songs of the Sea Bend" evokes feelings of longing triggered by the sea, depicts the simple rhythms of coastal life (gathering seafood, fishing), and invites the reader to the sea for poetic inspiration and passionate love. "After Parting" expresses sorrow and the ephemeral nature of memory, focusing on footprints left on the sand by a departed loved one, which will inevitably be erased by time and nature. For historians, these poems offer insight into the lyrical sensibilities and common themes (nature, longing, love, loss) prevalent in Korean literature during the colonial period. The depiction of simple coastal life might reflect an idealized or escapist view, while the theme of loss and fading traces in "After Parting" could subtly resonate with the broader context of cultural or national anxieties under colonial rule.
