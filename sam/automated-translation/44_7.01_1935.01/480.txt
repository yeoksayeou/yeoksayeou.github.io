鬼의 聲, 半島新文壇 二十年來 名作選集
						李人稙
The setting moon in the deep night cast the shadow of Ch'unch'ŏn's Samhak Mountain onto the west window of the detached room of Squire Kang's house in Solgae village, Namnae Township (南內面松峴).

Beneath the single-paned window, covered only by a layer of paper, a woman lay alone on one side of a wide mattress, using one end of a long pillow. She looked to be barely twenty, her face as lovely as the rising half-moon. Though her heart felt empty with the other end of the pillow vacant, she took solace in the thought that the kicking within her belly would, next month, result in the birth of a son or daughter. She lay there, taking the moonlight shining through the old west window as her companion and finding joy in the squirming child within her belly. Tossing and turning with various thoughts, unable to sleep, she struggled until the shadow of Samhak Mountain gradually covered the window, plunging the room into gloom, and the woman, forgetting her thoughts, finally drifted off to sleep.

The duration of her slumber might have seemed like only a few blinks to a lazy person, but the woman's dream stretched out as long as a clothesline.

Perhaps troubled by nightmares during her dream, she let out a sudden scream, startling the squire and his wife sleeping in the main room of the house. Squire Kang's wife, bare-chested and wearing only wide undergarments, rushed over in a single bound.

"Hey, Kilsun! Open the door. Open the door! Hey Kilsun, Kilsun!"

After calling Kilsun two or three times with no reply, she turned back towards the main room and called Squire Kang.

"Honey, Magistrate! Come over here quickly. A strange sound came from Kilsun's room, but she won't answer no matter how much I call. What's going on?"

Squire Kang, who had been sleeping naked, hastily pulled on his trousers and ran out, shaking the door of the detached room.

(Squire) "Hey Kilsun, Kilsun, Kilsun!"

As the whole household was in an uproar calling for Kilsun, Kilsun, who was inside the room, had woken up but lay there without even breathing. <182> Finally, as if compelled, she answered.

"Father, Mother, why are you worrying so much about your precious Kilsun? Just leave Kilsun be, whether she lives or dies, and go back to sleep."

Then there was no further sound, but Kilsun's heart felt like it was melting as she lay back down on the pillow. Squire Kang and his wife, as if guilty of some crime, chuckled awkwardly.

"Alright, sleep well then. We were just worried because we heard a noise."

They returned to the main room. Squire Kang's wife, still bare-chested, sat in the middle of the room, looking dazed like someone lost in thought.

By then, the moon's shadow had sunk deep, embracing the earth. Squire Kang's main room was as dark as a cave. In that darkness, Squire Kang fumbled around the room, trying to find his tobacco pipe. His hand, instead of finding the pipe, touched his wife's body.

Like a blind fortune-teller examining a woman, he groped his way down from her head. Perhaps even a middle-aged man could feel youthful desire, for instead of finding the pipe, he tried to lay his wife down. She pushed his arm away, saying,

"Hey, be still for a moment. I'm distraught, how can you act like this?"

(Squire) "Why, what's worrying you?"

(Wife) "Look here. After doing such a terrible thing to our child, you say you're not worried? When I think of our Kilsun, my bones feel like they're melting. She's our only child, raised like gold and jade, and now her life has come to this. Whose fault is it? They say 'like attracts like' (草綠은 제빗치 좃타고 - lit. green grass likes its own color). If we had to marry her off, marrying her to a fellow commoner would have been better. What kind of damned fool would want a *yangban* son-in-law?
If I had my way, I would have chosen a poor family's second son, or if not, a well-behaved boy with no parents, brought him in as a live-in son-in-law (다릴사위), and kept him with us for life. But that wish was useless, and now we're left with just a daughter without a son-in-law. Honey, Magistrate! When you were bragging about getting a *yangban* son-in-law, making others unable to even open their mouths, it was as if silver and gold would rain down from heaven just by making that match, and Kilsun would become an immortal. How much benefit have we seen from this son-in-law?"

(Squire) "Speak a little softer. <183> Kilsun will hear. Do you think we saw little benefit? When Magistrate Kim Sŭngji was the Ch'unch'ŏn county governor, the debt we collected from Ch'oe Tŏlp'ŏk – that was money obtained through sheer coercion. Would we have received such money rightfully otherwise? Was that all? Didn't we eat well thanks to his favors?"

(Wife) "Oh, horrors! What a great gain! But where is all the money earned from that great gain?"

(Squire) "Ah, what frustrating words are these? What about the debts we paid off? What about what we ate and spent during that time?
When we were on the brink of ruin (백척간두 - lit. top of a hundred-foot pole), Magistrate Kim Sŭngji came down as the Ch'unch'ŏn county governor and asked for our Kilsun as his concubine. It was truly like dreaming of a dragon. Before, I used to tremble even at the sight of a petty official (풍언), but after becoming the father-in-law of the Ch'unch'ŏn county governor, whenever I entered the town, people would call me 'Squire Kang, Squire Kang,' and wherever I went, platters of raw beef (뉵회) and cups of wine were never far away. If that magistrate hadn't left to become a secretary in the royal secretariat (비서승) but had stayed as the Ch'unch'ŏn county governor for a few more years, we would have made a fortune. Womenfolk know nothing and just make trouble (방정) in the house, preventing things that could have happened. Just keep quiet. We'll profit from that gentleman again."

Hearing this, his wife became thoroughly angry.

When uneducated commoners quarrel, they can't speak without resorting to curses and vulgarities.

(Wife) "Stop that damned talk! So, the household didn't prosper because the womanfolk made trouble, is that it? If I become mute from tomorrow, will blessings as large as straw mats fall from heaven and cover Squire Kang from head to toe? Let's just wait and see!
You think getting favors, eating raw beef platters and drinking wine when you go to town because you have a *yangban* son-in-law is such a benefit, yet you don't realize the terrible injustice done to Kilsun. It's truly frustrating.
Is Kilsun supposed to become a virtuous widow? Why doesn't she marry someone else instead of waiting for that ruined fellow, Kim Ch'unch'ŏn or Kim Sŭngji or whatever his name is? Kim Sŭngji, Kim Sŭngji, what is Kim Sŭngji? Where is such a Kim Sŭngji-like fellow (김승 갓흔 놈 - lit. fellow like Kim's hat)? Is he keeping her like this because he's afraid of his wife and can't bring his concubine home? If he was so scared of his wife, he shouldn't have taken a concubine in the first place. Having taken one, he does this unbearable thing to another person. <184> It would be good if that ruined fellow even sent a letter, but why does he send letters? Tomorrow, I'll tell Kilsun to find another husband, and if she doesn't listen, I'll beat her to death. Did she become someone's concubine to live in luxury? What kind of damned woman stays to suffer and worry?"

Hearing this, Squire Kang was furious and wanted to beat his wife to pulp, but he gulped and held back. It wasn't because he cherished his wife that he held back, but because he cherished money.

Money, what money? In Squire Kang's mind, Kilsun was seen as a lump of money.

He thought that if he caused trouble for that lump of money and she fell seriously ill, it would be a problem. Meanwhile, he had found his pipe somewhere and lit his tobacco; only the glowing ember of the pipe flickered in the room. The early morning chill brought dew turning to frost on the maple-colored peaks; the wife, whose anger had flared, felt her body grow cold. She rustled about looking for clothes, then went to the upper part of the room (웃목), curled up alone, and dozed off sitting up (등걸잠).

(Squire) "Hey there. Wife, wife. Are you trying to catch a cold, sleeping sitting up in the upper part of the room?"

The wife lay still as a dead mouse, without even breathing. Squire Kang, knowing she wasn't asleep but pretending not to, spoke to himself.

"Womenfolk are useless creatures. What kind of fuss (방정) is that? Magistrate Kim Sŭngji even sent word telling me to bring Kilsun to Seoul, but I kept quiet because if I said such things in the house, they'd likely raise a fuss, saying we shouldn't leave right away. I'm a hero. Hearing such fuss, I endured for a moment. If she catches a cold sleeping sitting up in the upper part of the room and drops dead..."

He tapped his pipe sharply and slipped under the covers. The wife felt increasingly cold and wanted to get back under the covers, but having not gone when Squire Kang called, she felt awkward going back on her own now. She waited for him to call again, but he didn't. Getting angry on her own, she stood up again and muttered to herself.

"Why doesn't this cursed night end? Is there any damned fool who would live depending on a magistrate like that, who just waits for me to catch a cold and collapse? When morning comes, I'll beat Kilsun, the daughter born from my own womb, to death, and then I'll drink poison right in front of the magistrate, roll my eyes back, and show him how I die."

(Squire) "Die or don't die. Who told you to die? <185> Making a fuss on your own for no reason and dying. Why drag innocent Kilsun into it and beat her to death? Kilsun, whom Kim Sŭngji waits for every day."

The sounds of this pointless argument clearly reached the ears of Kilsun, lying alone in the detached room across the narrow wooden porch. Squire Kang's baseless lies sounded entirely true to Kilsun's ears.

Kilsun was Squire Kang's daughter, and she had been deceived by her father many times. If she had been deceived like this by someone else, she would have heard even the truth as lies thereafter, but with the trust a child has in a parent, she was deceived again without suspicion.

From the main room, with Squire Kang's skillful lie, the wife's outburst subsided on its own; her loud voice grew quieter, and the quiet voice disappeared. Perhaps she had fallen into a deep sleep, as no sound could be heard. Kilsun's pillow became quiet again. Outside the window, a morning magpie chattered a few times from the branches of a paulownia tree, its calls seeming to drop distinctly onto Kilsun's pillow. Kilsun, lying with sleepless eyes closed, opened them wide to see that it was already bright daylight outside. "Magpie, oh magpie, welcome news! Are you bringing tidings of a palanquin coming from Magistrate Kim's house to fetch me? Oh, what are the people of that house like? I wish I could go and see soon." She turned over once, kicking the blanket with her foot so that it only covered her below the waist.

When she thought she might die without ever having seen Seoul in her life, that was her only sorrow. Now that she thought she was going up to Seoul, countless worries unknown to others arose.

Squire Kang, good-natured and diligent, had already risen and was bustling about, nagging here and there, before heading out to the village liquor house.

Squire Kang's wife, as if some joyous event had occurred, came over to Kilsun's room, her mouth already wide open, calling Kilsun.

"Hey Kilsun! With a face as lovely and well-formed as yours, how could your fate possibly be bad?"

(Kilsun) "What good fortune has occurred?"

(Mother) "Yes, don't worry. We've been worrying needlessly all this time. Today, for the first time, I heard the detailed story from your father. Kim Sŭngji apparently sent travel money to your father, telling him to bring you to Seoul, but your father must have spent the money, as he didn't tell us until early this morning <186> when he first mentioned it. He says he'll take you to Seoul tomorrow, one way or another, so start packing your things from today. After you go up, we'll gradually come up to you. Who else do we have to rely on in our old age but you?"

As she spoke, tears dropped heavily. Kilsun looked at her and shed tears too, as if they were parting ways for good right then and there.

Just then, Squire Kang returned home, having had a good amount of pre-breakfast liquor. He seemed to have completely forgotten the lies he told at dawn. Seeing his wife and daughter facing each other and crying, he shouted sharply.

"You troublesome things! Why are women crying before breakfast?"

If Kilsun and her mother had been experiencing such a thing for the first time, the women might have been shocked (경풍), but Squire Kang's yelling was commonplace in that household. Whenever Squire Kang was pressed for debts, he vented his anger on the easy targets, his wife and child, at home. Whenever he got drunk, his drunken antics were directed at his wife and child. Whenever something he planned didn't work out, he took out his frustration at home. Therefore, his wife was perhaps afraid of Squire Kang's fists, but she considered his usual nagging something to be routinely endured.

(Wife) "Ah, don't say such frustrating things. If Kilsun leaves tomorrow, who knows when we'll see her again? We say we'll follow later, but that's just words; is it an easy thing? Look here. For today only, please stop worrying and just be quiet. If we keep Kilsun at home, how many days will we get to see her that you act like this?"

As she spoke, tears streamed down.

(Kilsun) "Mother, don't cry. How many more days will I hear Father's nagging? Once I go up to Seoul, even if I wanted to hear Father's nagging, where would I find it? Whether he nags or cherishes me, who else can I rely on but my parents? I am going to Seoul, but for some reason, my heart feels uneasy. My dream last night was so bad, I wonder if it's not a bad omen (꿈땜)."

As she thought of the dream, goosebumps rose all over her, and her tears stopped abruptly.

(Mother) "Yes, tell me about that. Last night, you screamed so loudly in your sleep. I meant to ask you what happened, but I couldn't because we talked about other things. Did you have a nightmare?" <187>

Kilsun sat quietly without answering. Squire Kang glanced back and forth between his wife's and Kilsun's faces, rustling tobacco into his pipe.

Kilsun only thought about her dream, while Squire Kang plotted his next lie.

Kilsun wasn't thinking about the dream because she had forgotten it; she was thinking about how frightening and ominous it was, making her cautious about the future. Squire Kang wasn't thinking about delaying and not taking his daughter; this time, he was thinking about some trick (귀정) that might arise.

Even an incompetent doctor is like Pang T'ong (방통 - strategist from Romance of the Three Kingdoms); without saying a word, he nodded his head and smiled faintly.

Whatever plan he had hatched, the father's face showed joy.
The mother's face held teardrops. The daughter's heart held a lump of worry.

The three family members looked at each other, silent for a while. Words first came from the one with a happy heart.

(Squire) "Alright. No more talk. It's one hundred ninety *ri* from Solgae village to Seoul. If we leave early tomorrow morning, even with a fast palanquin (단패교군), we'll arrive early the evening after next. Wife, please make breakfast early. I need to go find two good palanquin bearers. No, no need for that. I don't feel like eating yet anyway, so I'll go find the bearers first right now."

He walked out the door without looking back. Kilsun and her mother, facing separation right before their eyes, seemed to have forgotten even about making and eating breakfast, just sitting there looking at each other.

(Kilsun) "Mother, listen to my dream story.
In my dream, I had given birth to a son who was two years old. He looked lovely like a peony blossom (함박꼿). He looked at me, saying 'Mama, Mama,' and toddled around playfully in front of me. Then, the woman called our 'big wife' (큰 마누라 - referring to Kim Sŭngji's main wife) came, smiling sweetly. She looked at the young child, clapped her hands gently, saying 'Come here, come here.' The innocent child giggled and reached out his small, fernbrake-like hands (고사리 갓흔). The big wife rushed forward violently, grabbed the child's shoulders firmly, lifted him halfway, and then started munching and crunching him up from the head down. Shocked and horrified, I tried to snatch the child away. The big wife threw down the half-remaining child, thrust out her blood-reddened mouth (조동이), bared her gaunt teeth, and lunged at me fiercely. I screamed in terror and woke up. How could a dream be so dreadful?" <188>

(Mother) "Child, hearing that dream story gives me the shivers. Then don't go to Seoul, stay home. You're nineteen now, a person with a vast future ahead (전정 만리). If not Kim Sŭngji, won't there be another husband? For commoners like us, what's the point of talking about chastity (수절) or fidelity (긔절)? I'll send you somewhere you won't suffer. I don't hope for any benefit from a son-in-law either. As long as the man is sincere, even a penniless beggar (乞人) would be fine."

(Kilsun) "Mother, don't say that. Good things are destined by fate, and bad things are also destined by fate. If my fate is good, I'll be fine even going to Kim Sŭngji's house. If it's bad, where can I go to escape that fate?
Also, a person's conduct shouldn't be judged by social class (반상). Even among noblewomen (사족의 부녀), there might be those with unfaithful hearts. Even among lowly women (불상한 년), there must be many with virtuous hearts (정렬). No matter how much of a common girl (상년) I am, living in a country corner, I refuse to marry a second or third time.
Whether good things or bad things await me at the marital home, I want to go the path I must take, quickly, even by a day."

The mother and daughter's discussion continued inconclusively until noon. Squire Kang had already returned home. Squire Kang, who usually made harsh remarks even over trivial matters, seemed different that day. Even hearing that breakfast hadn't been made by noon, he didn't make a fuss, only saying, "Kilsun must be hungry, hurry up and make food." He seemed to have returned after making all preparations for leaving early the next morning.

Kilsun, intending to pack her belongings, couldn't even dust off her mirror stand, and the day drew to a close.

Squire Kang's wife, bustling about, couldn't even help Kilsun pack. Early the next morning, upon hearing the sound of horses and palanquins outside the gate, she was busy making breakfast, boiling horse feed, and packing belongings all at once. She was in such a rush that she accidentally poured soybean paste (된장) into the horse feed pot while straining it and even packed a rag amidst the clothes while packing. Despite the commotion since dawn, it was already broad daylight when they finally departed. Amidst Squire Kang's fuss, Kilsun had no time to eat and got into the palanquin. Mother and daughter wept profusely at their parting, wondering if they would ever see each other again. Solgae village seemed to be full of women; old women and young women crowded the inner and outer courtyards. Who knew when they had grown so fond of Kilsun, but they all shed tears along with Kilsun and her mother. Tears in these eyes, tears in those eyes.
It might be common for tender hearts and soft eyes to be moved to tears seeing others cry, but weeping with choked sobs (흑흑 늣기며) was unusual. <189> An old woman from a neighboring house might plausibly have such affection for Kilsun if she had raised her, but the daughter-in-law of Old Man Pak from the lower village was meeting Kilsun for the first time, having come along with her mother-in-law to see Kilsun off. At first, she began to cry intermittently, but eventually, losing all sense of shame, she cried out loud, wailing "Eong-eong." She was someone who had come to a scene of weeping and was crying thinking of her own family home.

(Squire) "Hey—at this rate, we won't be able to leave today! Hey Kilsun, get in the palanquin quickly. Hey, palanquin bearers, hurry up, lift the poles and let's go. Well then—ladies of the village, everyone, stay well. We'll see you again after visiting Seoul. Hey Kŏmdung, bring the horse here."

He mounted the packhorse briskly. One palanquin and one horse headed towards Sinyŏn River (新延江). The women of Solgae village returned to their homes one by one, two by two. Squire Kang's wife entered the empty house alone and wept, letting out loud cries.

Originally, when Kim Sŭngji went up to Seoul, he had told Squire Kang that there were circumstances preventing him from taking the Ch'unch'ŏn wife (referring to Kilsun) with him, so she should remain at Kang's house and wait. He stressed not to come to Seoul unless he sent money for the journey and summoned them.
Despite these circumstances, on the morning after Kilsun talked in her sleep, Squire Kang, pressured by his wife's outburst, had lied that Kim Sŭngji in Seoul had summoned Kilsun. He also said he would take Kilsun to Seoul the next day. After waking up, he went to the tavern, drank heartily before breakfast, and returned home to find his wife and daughter crying as if parting forever, asking if they would ever meet again. Squire Kang was dumbfounded. Squire Kang was strong-willed and powerfully built; he feared neither lightning falling from the sky nor tigers descending from Samhak Mountain. But he feared *yangban* and money.
His fear of *yangban* and money didn't mean he avoided them; rather, he pursued them like a baby seeking its mother's breast.
The manner of pursuit was the same, but the mind behind it was twofold.
When he saw a *yangban*, he felt a desire to blast them with a cannon, annihilate them, and wipe out their lineage, yet he followed them outwardly. When he saw money, he felt happier than seeing his parents and cherished it more than his wife and child, following it inwardly. He once possessed such coveted money in amounts others envied, but Ch'unch'ŏn governors or magistrates—simply put, bandits who skinned people alive (인피 벳기는 불안당) <190>—came one after another. He hoped things would get better when one left, but each newcomer was the same. Squire Kang's money all went into the guts of the *yangban*. Squire Kang was left penniless (피천 대푼 업시), drinking liquor on credit and passing the time by venting his frustrations at home. Then, Kim Sŭngji, a Seoul *yangban*, came down as the Ch'unch'ŏn county governor. He was blind to local governance but had sharp ears for rumors of beautiful women. Hearing that Squire Kang's daughter in Solgae village was beautiful, he summoned Squire Kang, elevated him high like a target platform (고소대), and had his shrewd secretary (책방) manipulate Squire Kang so effectively that Kang would have willingly died if Kim Sŭngji ordered it. When Kim Sŭngji asked for Kilsun as his concubine, Squire Kang thought a great fortune had come his way and offered his daughter. But less than a year later, the governor was transferred. It wasn't because he lost power, nor was he dismissed because he was disliked.
Kim Sŭngji's wife (실내), living in Seoul, heard the rumor that her husband had taken a concubine in Ch'unch'ŏn. She flew into a rage, ready to immediately charter a palanquin and head down to Ch'unch'ŏn. Amidst the chaos, like a major crisis had hit the household, Kim Sŭngji's younger brother urgently went to the communications office and sent a telegram to Ch'unch'ŏn. The Ch'unch'ŏn county governor left for Seoul without even getting official leave (관찰부 수유) and was transferred to a secretarial post (비서승).
Kilsun and her mother didn't know all these detailed circumstances, but Squire Kang knew them well.
Given these peculiar circumstances, he worried that if he said they would leave tomorrow but then didn't, his wife might find out the truth and incite Kilsun to change her mind. A new idea struck him: disregard everything and just take Kilsun to Kim Sŭngji; surely some solution would arise. That was his plan.

A start is half the task. Three days after setting off, they entered Seoul and went straight to Kim Sŭngji's house without any prior notice. Kim Sŭngji wasn't a particularly powerful or influential official, but he was known as a wealthy man, harvesting one or two thousand *sŏk* of rice annually.
The lofty gate (소슬대문) and rows of servants' quarters (줄행낭) greatly impressed Squire Kang; it felt as if half that wealth was already his, and his mouth fell open in excitement. Dismounting from the horse in front of the mounting block (하마석), he unhesitatingly used informal address (해라체) to the palanquin bearers he had previously addressed respectfully (하게체).

(Squire) "Hey, palanquin bearers! Quickly, carry the palanquin through the inner gate."

Squire Kang entered the main guest quarters (큰 사랑).
From the servants' area (하인 COS - likely a typo/abbreviation, context suggests servants' quarters), servants with topknots three fists high emerged.

"Sir, where are you from?"

(Bearer) "Yes—we've brought someone from Solgae village in Ch'unch'ŏn." (Abbreviated below)

**Summary**

The story depicts Kilsun, a young pregnant woman living as the concubine of Kim Sŭngji, a former county governor, who has left her behind in her rural hometown of Ch'unch'ŏn. Her father, Squire Kang, a commoner obsessed with the status and financial benefits derived from his *yangban* son-in-law, decides to take Kilsun to Seoul unannounced, based on lies he told his wife to quell her anger about Kilsun's situation. Kilsun is apprehensive due to an ominous dream where Kim Sŭngji's main wife symbolically devours her child, while her mother laments Kilsun's fate and the folly of seeking a *yangban* connection. The story ends as they arrive at Kim Sŭngji's imposing Seoul residence.

From a historical perspective, the story vividly portrays the social hierarchy and class tensions between commoners (*sangmin*) and the aristocracy (*yangban*) during the late Joseon or early colonial period. Squire Kang's desperation for status reflects the commoner's ambition and simultaneous resentment towards the *yangban* class, who are depicted as exploitative (the governors "skinning people alive"). The practice of concubinage and the precarious position of concubines like Kilsun, subject to the whims of the husband and the hostility of the main wife, are central themes. The narrative also touches upon the economic realities and the corrupting influence of power and money within the context of local administration under figures like Kim Sŭngji. Historians might find the detailed depiction of social customs, class dynamics, family conflicts driven by social ambition, and the portrayal of provincial life versus the allure and perceived power of Seoul particularly interesting.

양반과 돈을 무서워하면 피하야 다라나는 거시 안이라 어린 아해 젓꼭지 따르는 듯 따른다.
따르는 모냥은 한 가지나 따르는 마음은 두 가지라.
양반을 보면 대포로 노아서 뭇질너 죽여 씨를 업새고 시푼 마음이 잇스면서 거죽으로 따르고 돈을 보면 어미 애비보다 반갑고 게집 자식보다 귀애하는 마음이 잇서서 속으로 따른다. 그러케 따르는 돈을 이전 시절에 남부럽지 아니하게 가젓더니 준천 부사인지 군수인지 쉽게 말하려면 인피 벳기는 불안당들이＜190＞ 번가라 나려오는대 이 놈이 가면 살겟다 시푸나 오는 놈마다 그 놈이라 강동지의 돈은 양반의 창자 속으로 다 드러가고 강똥지는 피천 대푼 업시 외상술이나 먹고 집에 드러와서 화푸리로 세월을 보내더니 서울 양반 김승지가 춘천 군수로 내려와서 지방 정치에는 눈이 컹컴하나 어엽뿐 게집 잇다는 소문에는 귀가 썩 밝은 사람이라 솔개 동내 강동지의 딸이 어엽뿌단 말을 듯고 강동지를 불너서 고소대갓치 치켜 세우더니 알깍쟁이가 다 된 책방을 시켜서 강동지를 엇더케 살맛던지 김승지가 죽어라 하면 죽고 시풀만 하게 된 터에 김승지가 순이를 첩으로 달나 하니 강동지의 마음에는 이제 큰 수 낫다하고 그 딸을 밧첫는대 일년이 못되야 군수가 갈닌지라. 세력이 업서서 갈닌 것도 아니오 시려서 내노흔 것도 아니라.
김승지의 실내는 서울 잇다가 그 남편이 춘천 가서 첩을 두엇다는 소문을 듯고 열 벌 스무 벌을 뛰며 당장에 교군을 차려서 춘천으로 내려가려 하는대 왼 집안이 난리를 당한 것 갓치 창황한 중에 김승지의 아우가 급히 통신국에 가서 춘천으로 전부하더니 춘천 군수가 관잘부 수유도 못 엇고 서울로 올나가서 비서쯤으로 올문 터이라.
길순이 모녀는 그러케 자세한 사정은 다 모르나 강동지는 자세히 아는 자라.
그런 괴상야릇한 사긔가 잇는대 만일 내일 떠난다 하고 또 떠나지 아니하고 잇다가 그 마누라가 그 사긔를 알고 길순이를 충동하야 마음이나 변하게 할가 의심하야 새 의사가 나서 불고전후하고 길순이를 다리고 가서 김승지의게 맷기면 무슨 도리가 잇스리라 하는 경영이러라.

시작이 반이라 떠는 지 사흘 만에 서울로 드러 갓는대 아무 통긔업시 김승지 집에로 드러가더라. 김승지가 그리 서슬잇는 세도 재상은 아니나 일년에 천 석 추수를 하느니 이천 석 추수를 하느니 그러한 부자 득명하는 터이라.
소슬대문 줄행낭이 강동지 눈에 썩들며 그 재물이 반은 제 거시 되는 듯 하야 입이 떡 빼러지며 흥이 낫더라. 하마석 압헤서 말께 내리면서 하게 하던 교군군더러 서슴지 아니하고 해라를 한다.
(동지) 이애 교군군아. 어서 안중문으로 교군 뫼서라.
하면서 강동지는 큰 사랑으로 드러가더라.
하인 COS에서 꼭두가 세 뽐식이나 되는 하인들이 나서면서
여보 어대 행차오.
