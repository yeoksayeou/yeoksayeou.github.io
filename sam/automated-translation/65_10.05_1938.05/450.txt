The Shade of the Palm Tree and the Rest of Youth
						No Ch'ŏnmyŏng

It is truly a sad thing to attach a constrained reason to an act that is not done out of enjoyment (질겨서).

The fact that, although I don't actually like teahouses (다방), I occasionally visit them with friends stems from the unavoidable reason that, while we may have come to know the taste of tea, we haven't managed to prepare a suitable room in our own homes where we can drink and enjoy it, and so we must borrow the inconvenient public parlor (公衆 팔라) out of necessity (염ㅅ가의).

Indeed, young people find the frowning face of an old parent, holding their whole day's miserliness, unpleasant to see (보기실코), and young men think they find salvation in going out rather than sitting at home (드러앉았기), sickened by their wife's (안해) drawn-out haggling (느른 괴변) with the vegetable seller and her impoverished voice.

However, it seems the teahouse cannot be a place of cultural rest either.

"Ah, I wish we could go to a teahouse-"

When I see a friend easily getting flustered and excited as if they could receive some extraordinary cultural stimulation by going to a teahouse, I feel very sorry and pity them, along with myself.

Really, what remarkable stimulation could one possibly receive from visiting all those teahouses that have recently proliferated, spending the whole day there? Just seeing the palm trees (椰子樹) and broad-leaved trees drying up, tormented by smoke in that room thick with cigarette smoke and hazy air—air quite toxic—is tiring enough. Yet, a friend sits (안즌) there as if quite the connoisseur of refined tastes, not knowing when to leave their seat, having forgotten boredom—perhaps they find their own good atmosphere in that, but it is something I cannot understand. The South Seas species (南洋種) broad-leaved trees wilting in the smoke - the jazz music (쨔즈曲) with a voice that sounds like it stayed up all night - the friend sitting blankly, easily intoxicated without even alcohol - this certainly cannot be called anything other than a scene from the third month of spring (춘삼월의 풍경).<246>

**Summary**

The author, No Ch'ŏnmyŏng, critiques the growing trend among Korean youth in 1938 of frequenting teahouses (tabang). She posits that this habit arises less from genuine pleasure or cultural interest and more from a desire to escape uncomfortable home environments—marked by parental disapproval or the stresses of poverty reflected in marital interactions. Despite this escapism, No finds the teahouses themselves to be dreary, smoky, and ultimately unfulfilling spaces, filled with wilting plants, loud jazz, and listless patrons, offering no true rest or cultural value. From a historical perspective, the article provides a valuable glimpse into the social dynamics and anxieties of urban youth during the colonial period. It reflects the adoption of new social spaces like the tabang but also reveals a sense of disillusionment and critique, suggesting these modern venues failed to provide genuine solace or cultural substance, perhaps highlighting underlying societal limitations and the psychological strains of life under colonial rule.

질겨서 하는 게 아닌 짓에 군색한 리유를 진였다는 것은 짐짓 슬픈 일이다.
실은 다방을 좋아하지 않으면서도 이따금 친구들과 함께 여기를 찾는다는 것은 우리가 제법 차ㅅ맛은 알게 쯤 되였것만 이것을 마시며 질길 수 있는 알마즌 방 하나를 우리들 집에 작만해 노치 못했다는데 부득의한 리유가 따르고 염ㅅ가의 公衆 팔라를 불편한대로 빌니는 까닭이 있다.
사실 젊운이들은 노친네의 온종일 그 궁상을 담고 있는 찌프린 낯이 보기실코 젊운 사나희들은 안해의 그 푸성귀 장수한테서 느른 괴변과 가난한 목소리가 진저리나 집에 드러앉았기 보다는 나가는 데서 구원을 받는다고 생각한다.
허나 다방또 문화적 휴식처는 될 수 없는 모양이다.
「아이 차ㅅ집에나 좀 갔으면-」
차ㅅ집엘 가면 무슨 희한한 문화적 자극이나 받을 수 있을 상 십게 수선스레 흥분하는 친구를 보면 나와 함께 그가 무척 가엽고 치근해진다.
도대체 요새 많어진 그 다방들을 온종일 돌러 나온댓자 무슨 신통한 자극을 받을게 있을가부냐. 담배 연기로 자옥하고 흐린 공기가 제법 독스러운 그 방안에서 야자수와 활엽수가 연기에 시달니며 말르는 양을 눈앞에 보는 것 만으로도 피곤한 일이거든 자못 풍류객인 양 안즌 채 자리를 뜰 줄 모르며 권태를 잊어버린 친구는 또 저대로 좋은 운치가 있는지도 모르나 나는 알아들을 수 없는 일이다. 연기에 시드러가는 南洋種 활엽수나 -밤을 새고 온 목소리의 쨔즈曲이나 -술도 없이 쉽게 취해서 멀거니 앉아 있는 친구나 -이는 확실히 춘삼월의 풍경이 아니랄 수 없다.<246>
