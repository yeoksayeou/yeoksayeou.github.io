S(1) Pyŏng-uk's House, Farm, Afternoon (Hwangju) (F·I)

Clear autumn sky, afternoon.
Several CUTS of Hwangju scenery,
- Wŏlp'a Pavilion (月波樓), Chŏkpyŏk River (赤壁江), etc.

A boat hoisting its sail goes up against the river current. The wind blows downstream. The boat, contrary to intention, goes downstream. The boatmen suddenly become busy.

Mulberry field. Currently, Pyŏng-uk and Yŏng-ch'ae are picking mulberries. Pyŏng-uk, between picking mulberries, picks Odi berries (오-두) and mimics applying rouge to her lips.
And hums a tune.
Truly a beautiful pastoral scene- <276>

Pyŏng-uk: "Yŏng-ch'ae, my lips?"
Yŏng-ch'ae: "Oh my!"
Pyŏng-uk: "You try it too, Yŏng-ch'ae."
Yŏng-ch'ae: "Hmph- (Snorts)"
Pyŏng-uk: "I told you to forget everything and cheer up."
Yŏng-ch'ae: "..."
"Pyŏng-uk, are you there?"
Her older brother's voice. The camera quickly pans to the direction of the sound; her older brother comes this way. Pyŏng-uk happily to Yŏng-ch'ae.
Pyŏng-uk: "Yes- (Answering) It's my older brother."
Yŏng-ch'ae just nods her head.
Her older brother approaches the two.
Yŏng-ch'ae gives a light greeting and, holding her basket, excuses herself from the spot.
Pyŏng-uk and her older brother walk together.
Brother: "Even though you came for vacation, I hardly get to see you."
Pyŏng-uk: "It's because I have a friend who came with me!"
Brother: "Right, what kind of student is she?"
Pyŏng-uk: "She's well-behaved, isn't she?"
Brother: "She seems quite gentle."
Pyŏng-uk: "Actually, I was just about to tell you about her, Brother. She kept saying she was shy."
Brother: "Really?"
Pyŏng-uk: "Her name is Yŏng-ch'ae. Pak Yŏng-ch'ae. Actually, she's a friend I became close with on the train."
Brother: "What? Then she's not a school friend?"
Pyŏng-uk: "She's not a student. She has quite pitiful circumstances."
Brother: "Pitiful circumstances? Ha! You now understand others' pitiful circumstances."
Pyŏng-uk: "Brother, don't just hear it like that. Yŏng-ch'ae is incredibly pitiful. Let me tell you her story, listen. Okay?"

The sound of water splashing.
Beautiful music enters along with a folk song.<277>
Young women (5-6), stripped to the waist, washing their hair. Yŏng-ch'ae is among them.

Kyonu (견우) who came seeking his love
Please comb my hair
Please comb my hair

The seventh month, Ch'il-sŏk day
Kyonu is brave and strong
Please comb my hair
Please comb my hair"
As this folk song nears its end
O·L
Two or three torches become visible.
"Caught it!"
Someone's shout is heard.
Hyŏng-sik, having caught a large carp by spearing it, - 4-5 other children run over to look.
Child A: "Hyŏng-sik is unusual today- looks like he's trying to catch all the Ch'il-sŏk fish himself!"
Instead of answering, Hyŏng-sik grins and puts it into his fishing basket (따라치). The basket already has four or five fish inside.
O·L
Scene of the young women washing hair. Still the chorus of the folk song!
Around the time the song passes its halfway point, Kop-pun appears.
Her hair is disheveled like tangled hemp; it's impossible to tell who she is. Looks extremely urgent.
Kop-pun: "Miss, Miss Yŏng-ch'ae, are you here?"
Yŏng-ch'ae lifts her face and parts her hair.
Yŏng-ch'ae: "?"
Kop-pun: (Even more anxiously and hurriedly)
"Miss, Miss, there's chaos at home right now, 저 - 저 - the policemen came and took Father away entirely -"
Yŏng-ch'ae is startled. Quickly picking up her jacket (조고리) and putting it on, she runs off with Kop-pun.

Many people are gathered chaotically, murmuring. Yŏng-ch'ae and Kop-pun run inside. Soon, Hyŏng-sik, holding the spear and fishing basket, also runs in. The camera moves through the crowd in front of the gate and directly into the inner courtyard.
An empty-looking main floor (마루). Hyŏng-sik's caught carp in the basket. A stray cat snatches a fish from the basket and darts away like an arrow. (Aftermath of an unexpected disaster; Yŏng-ch'ae's house is now endlessly desolate.)
Camera, tearfully (F·O)

S(5) Village Entrance, Hill Path, Around Noon

Endlessly clear autumn sky.
Square kites and tail kites are flying.
Camera pans down from the kites - under a solitary pine tree.
Hyŏng-sik and Yŏng-ch'ae, dressed for travel. Before them, a distinct 'Y'-shaped fork in the road is visible.
Villagers A & B passing through this area see the two children -
Villager A: "Where are you kids going?"
Hyŏng-sik: "I'm going to Seoul."
Villager A: "Seoul? Is Yŏng-ch'ae going too?"
Hyŏng-sik: "No, Yŏng-ch'ae is going to her maternal grandparents' house."
Villager A: "To her grandparents' house... Tsk tsk... How pitiful... How did things come to this... Hope you fare well wherever you go. Still, you two mustn't break apart completely. You must fulfill the marriage arranged by your parents someday..."
Villager B: "Are they siblings?"
Villager A: "Goodness, you're slow... If they were siblings, would I tell them to marry?"
Villager B: "Oh, right! How did they lose their parents?"
Villager A: "They say Yŏng-ch'ae's father was arrested for something and there's been no news<279>... He's not the type to do wrong and get arrested..."
Villager B: "Anyway, it's pitiful."
Villagers A & B disappear, talking.

Hyŏng-sik: "You go that way -"
"I'll go this way through the town..."
The two children part ways very forlornly.
The camera pans from the separating children back up - to the sky.
The tail kite and square kite, flying separately.

S(7) Kye Wŏl-hwa's House, Main Floor

A hand holding brush and ink, painting an ink wash painting.
A painted vigorous old pine tree (looks amateurish).

Beside it, "Single-hearted devotion" (일편단심) is written, and then below - "Pak" (朴) is being recorded -
Wŏl-hwa: "Hey, are you a 'Pak'? You're a 'Kye' (桂). And then you brazenly try to write Pak Yŏng-ch'ae?"
Yŏng-ch'ae: "Oh, goodness!"
Smiling forlornly, she changes the character "Pak" (朴) to "Kye" (桂) and then writes "Wŏl-hyang" (月香). At this moment, from outside the door:
Chauffeur: "Miss Wŏl-hwa, Miss Wŏl-hyang, you've been called."
Kisaeng Mother: (From inside)<280>
"Girls!"
Wŏl-hwa: "Where to?"
Rickshaw Puller (Appearing, hands over a business card)
Business Card C·U (A rickshaw sent by Kim Kwang-ho (金光鎬) is shown)
Wŏl-hwa frowns.
Wŏl-hyang: "Why does that man keep doing this, not understanding your feelings, older sister? He thinks all kisaeng can be had as he pleases just by spending money!"
Wŏl-hwa, Wŏl-hyang put away the brush and ink and sit before the dressing table.

S(8) Taedong River, Daytime

On a pleasure boat (화방선).
Greasy with amusement.
Kisaeng, kisaeng, and two or three revelers. A sorrowful song (수심가) sounds languidly. Scene of lavish entertainment, two or three CUTS.

Wŏl-hwa, Wŏl-hyang are also visible among them.

Wŏl-hwa singing. Following the melody of this song, several CUTS of the Taedong River scenery are shown.
Flowing Taedong River, two or three sailboats, Pubyŏk Pavilion (浮碧樓), etc.
Kim Kwang-ho's expression, utterly captivated by Wŏl-hwa's voice!
After finishing her song, Kim Kwang-ho follows Wŏl-hwa. Tries to embrace her.

Wŏl-hwa gracefully pulls away.

Kwang-ho again tries to kiss her cheek.
Again, Wŏl-hwa sharply pushes him away!
Kwang-ho: "Hmph, you girls these days, unless it's some guy who makes speeches or writes a bit, you don't even consider them human, right? Dogs..."

Staggering Kwang-ho, again tries to forcefully embrace Wŏl-hwa.
Wŏl-hwa: "Ouch, let go, please! Why do you keep doing this?"
Wŏl-hwa (Pushes him away as if annoyed)<281>
Kwang-ho falls to one side. Humiliated.
He abruptly gets up and slaps Wŏl-hwa's cheek.
Wŏl-hwa, about to collapse.
Wŏl-hyang quickly runs over and supports her.
Kwang-ho: (Feels triumphant, but also somewhat sheepishly blustering) "Yeah, a kisaeng bitch acting unlike a kisaeng! Famous figures, patriots? Do you think famous figures and patriots feed and support you? If you're human, you should know gratitude..."
Wŏl-hwa: "Who asked you to pay my price?"
Kwang-ho: "Your mother took the price, you ruined beggar bitch..."
Wŏl-hwa can only weep bitterly.
The sound of revelry continues as if on fire.
The pleasure boat moves away.
(F·O)

S(9) Wŏl-hwa and Wŏl-hyang's Bedroom (F·I) Three AM

Wŏl-hwa and Wŏl-hyang lying neatly side-by-side inside a mosquito net.
(Camera moves) Again, the two lying intimately like doves.
Eyes open like plaster figures, just staring at the ceiling, no words.

Wŏl-hyang: "Sister, what are you thinking about so hard?"
Wŏl-hwa: "..."
Wŏl-hyang: "Don't think about anything and let's just sleep."
Wŏl-hwa: "You go ahead and sleep. Huu -"

Speaking forlornly, she melancholically gets up and goes out to the main floor. Tunes the strings of her kayagŭm.
The pale moonlight of the waning summer night, following the sorrowful melody of the sijo (시조) poem, makes Wŏl-hwa's face look even more desolate.

Wŏl-hwa's beautiful hands plucking the kayagŭm. A pair of jade rings<282> are unusually conspicuous.
Wŏl-hyang comes out and sits beside her.

Wŏl-hyang: "Sister, why are you like this today?"
Wŏl-hwa silently takes her hands off the kayagŭm. Gazes at the moon for a while, then turns around softly as if being pricked by ice.

Wŏl-hwa: "Yŏng-ch'ae! What sins did you and I commit in a past life for our fate to be..." (Gently grasps Wŏl-hyang's hand again) "I don't know how sorry I am to you."
Wŏl-hyang: "Sister, what are you saying?"
Wŏl-hwa: "This thing called kisaeng... For me to have made you become a kisaeng..."
Wŏl-hyang: "No, I'm alright. This is so much better than enduring such humiliation at my aunt's house. Even if I'm a kisaeng, as long as I keep myself pure, that's enough, isn't it?"
Wŏl-hwa: "So, you intend to wait for that Yi Hyŏng-sik indefinitely?"
"Yes, wait as long as you want. If you can meet him with a pure body until the end, I don't know how happy I would be either."
Wŏl-hwa: "Still, Yi Hyŏng-sik is also a man... Whether men will believe your body is pure..."
Wŏl-hyang (Only tears)
Wŏl-hwa: "It seems a kisaeng, no matter how sincere, cannot attain true love... I have no choice but to fall into Kim Kwang-ho's hands like this... Money... Money..."
Wŏl-hyang: "Sister, why do you keep saying such weak things?"
Wŏl-hwa: "You should hurry and go to Seoul. You might meet someone like that if you go to Seoul. You should find that person until the end and become the victor of true love."

Night sky -
A shooting star.
Wŏl-hwa and Wŏl-hyang lying neatly inside the mosquito net.
Wŏl-hyang is asleep, but Wŏl-hwa, as if intoxicated by unknown anguish<284>, has clear eyes like the night stars, wide awake.
Then, as if having made a decision, she carefully gets up.
Takes off the jade rings from her own fingers and puts them on the sleeping Yŏng-ch'ae's hand. Then, silently gazing at Wŏl-hyang, like someone possessed by a goblin (독개비) -
"You too will have sorrows in the future -"
She presses her cheek against Wŏl-hyang's cheek.
A single, cold tear.

S(11) Wŏl-hyang's Bedroom (Three AM)

Sleeping Wŏl-hyang's face, looks deeply troubled. Talking in her sleep.
"Sister -"
With a frightening sound, an ominous wind blows, and the open window slams shut.
Wŏl-hyang wakes up startled.

"Sister -"
The space beside her is empty. Startled Wŏl-hyang.
Quickly gets up and throws open the bedroom door.
Feels the jade rings on her finger.
Even more startled, she jumps down into the yard.

S(12) Taedong Riverside (Three AM)

With a terrifying sound, the angry waves of the Taedong River flow with the force of a lion.

One of Wŏl-hwa's shoes.<284>
Floating there, as if cursing all this sorrow.

Guest A: "It's as if it holds the dew."

Guest A: "Ha - Sir Sin seems quite smitten with Wŏl-hyang..."

**Summary**

This 1938 film scenario, adapted from Yi Kwang-su's novel "Mujŏng" (Heartless), tells the tragic story of Pak Yŏng-ch'ae. Separated from her childhood fiancé Yi Hyŏng-sik after her father's arrest, she endures hardship and eventually becomes a kisaeng named Wŏl-hyang in P'yŏngyang, mentored by the despairing Wŏl-hwa. Moving to Seoul, Wŏl-hyang maintains her purity, hoping to reunite with Hyŏng-sik. She attracts the attention of reporter Sin U-sŏn and unwanted advances from Kim Hyŏn-su. She discovers Hyŏng-sik is in Seoul but about to marry Kim Sŏn-hyŏng, the daughter of his benefactor. Despite U-sŏn's attempts to facilitate a meeting, circumstances and Hyŏng-sik's impending marriage prevent it. Wŏl-hyang witnesses the wedding in despair. The story is framed by Yŏng-ch'ae recounting her past to her friend Pyŏng-uk's brother in Hwangju. After finishing her tale, she disappears and is last seen walking into a stream at sunset.

From a historical perspective, the scenario offers insights into colonial Korean society, depicting the harsh realities faced by women, particularly the life of kisaeng and the societal pressures regarding female chastity and arranged marriage. It touches upon themes of modernization (trains, city life in Seoul vs. rural life), social stratification, the influence of Western elements like Christianity (church wedding), and the deep sense of fatalism and despair prevalent in some narratives of the period. The father's unexplained arrest could also subtly allude to the political climate under Japanese rule.
