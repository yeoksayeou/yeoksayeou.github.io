China's War Poetry
Songs of the Soldier and the Soldier's Wife (征夫, 征婦詞)

There are two types of poems titled *Chŏngbusa* (征夫詞, Song of the Soldier) and *Chŏngbusa* (征婦詞, Song of the Soldier's Wife) by a man named Liu Ji (劉績) from the Ming (明) dynasty.

*Song of the Soldier* (征夫歌)

征夫語征婦 (The soldier tells his wife,)
死生不可知 (Life and death are uncertain.)
慾慰果下魂 (If you wish to console my spirit after death,)
但視褓中兒 (Just look after the child in the swaddling clothes.)

*Song of the Soldier's Wife* (征婦詞)

征婦語征夫 (The soldier's wife tells her husband,)
有身當殉國 (With this body, I ought to die for the country.)
君爲塞下土 (If you become dust beneath the border ramparts,)
妾作山頭石 (I shall become a stone atop the mountain peak.)

The husband going off to war tells his wife, "I am now setting out on the path of war, and I do not know whether I will live or die hereafter. If you wish to console my spirit after I die in battle, just raise my flesh and blood, our young child (아히), with love, so he becomes a fine adult."

To this, the wife replies:
"Quickly, throw yourself into serving the nation. If you die an honorable death and become the earth of the frontier, I will have no more hope in this world. I will become a stone on the mountaintop and guard my chastity."

Regarding this, one might feel whether this is something a mother with a child should say in response to her husband's words above, but in any case, it is a spirited piece of writing. Indeed, befitting a nation of literature, one thinks highly of their words and writings.

The "stone on the mountaintop" (山頭石) in the *Song of the Soldier's Wife* refers to one on a northern mountain in Wuch'ang (武昌) on the bank of the Yangtze River (楊子江岸). Its shape resembles a standing person. Legend has it that a faithful wife (貞婦) of old saw off her husband departing for battle, came to this mountain, and turned into stone (化石). It is a stone associated with a legend similar to the story of Sayohime (佐用姬) of Matsura Bay (松浦渴) in Kyūshū (九州), Mainland Japan (內地).

Triumphal Song (凱歌)

There is a poem titled *Kaega* (凱歌, Triumphal Song) by Shen Mingchen (沈明臣) of the Ming (明) dynasty.

*Triumphal Song* (凱歌)

銜枚夜渡五千兵 (Holding gags in their mouths, five thousand soldiers cross the river at night,)
密領軍符號令明 (Secretly receiving military tallies, orders are clear.)
狹巷短兵相接處 (Where short weapons clash in narrow alleys,)
殺人如草不聞聲 (Killing men like grass, not a sound is heard.)

This poem seems as if it were written specifically in advance regarding the actions of the Japanese army in the current China Incident (支那事變).

River crossing after river crossing (渡河), street fighting erupting in various places, bayonet fighting (白兵戰), and the "ten-man slash" (十人斬) incidents – there are so many. Just like the text describes, faced with the valiant spirit of the Japanese army where one soldier equals a thousand (一騎當千), it would be fitting to recite this famous verse as is.

Song of Following the Army (從軍行)

奏時明月嘆時關 (Playing music under the bright moon, sighing at the pass,)
萬里長征人未還 (On a ten-thousand-li campaign, men have not returned.)
但使龍城飛將在 (If only the Flying General of Longcheng were present,)
不敎胡馬度陰山 (He would not let the barbarian horsemen cross the Yin Mountains.)

This poem is by Wang Ch'ang-ling (王昌齡) of the Tang (唐) dynasty, lamenting the many casualties incurred because the army lacked valiant generals (勇將). This truly has aspects of the Chinese army. Looking at the current incident, the line "He would not let the barbarian horsemen (胡馬) cross the Yin Mountains (陰山)" is not the issue.

The Great Wall (萬里長城) has already been breached, Shanxi's (山西) provincial capital Taiyuan (太原) has fallen, and they are fleeing in defeat across the Yellow River (黃河). However, poems like this, which sound like crying while invoking the names (일흠) of the deceased, saying "If only such-and-such a general were here," can only be seen in the poetry of the Chinese. For Chiang Kai-shek (蔣介石) and the generals (將領) of that country, such sentiments must be all the more poignant now.<45>

**Summary**

The article introduces several classical Chinese war poems: "Song of the Soldier" and "Song of the Soldier's Wife" by Liu Ji (Ming Dynasty), "Triumphal Song" by Shen Mingchen (Ming Dynasty), and "Song of Following the Army" by Wang Chang-ling (Tang Dynasty). The author interprets these poems in the context of the ongoing Second Sino-Japanese War (1937-1945). Shen Mingchen's "Triumphal Song" is praised as seemingly predicting the effectiveness and stealth of the Japanese military actions, like river crossings and close-quarters combat. Conversely, Wang Chang-ling's poem lamenting the lack of good generals is used to criticize the contemporary Chinese military leadership, suggesting their current defeats echo the poem's sentiment of helplessness. The author contrasts the perceived lamentation in Chinese poetry with the portrayal of Japanese military prowess.

From a historical perspective, this article is interesting as it exemplifies Korean intellectual discourse under Japanese colonial rule, reflecting a pro-Japanese interpretation of the Sino-Japanese War. By selectively interpreting classical Chinese literature, the author aligns with the Japanese narrative, portraying Japanese military actions favorably while depicting Chinese resistance as ineffective and lamentable, characteristic of colonial-era propaganda aimed at legitimizing Japanese expansionism. Historians might find the appropriation of Chinese culture to support Japanese imperial ideology within a Korean publication particularly revealing.

支那의 戰爭詩歌
征夫, 征婦詞

明나라 劉績이라고 하는 사람의 作에 征夫詞, 征婦詞라고 하는 두 가지의 詞가 있다.

征夫歌

征夫語征婦
死生不可知
慾慰果下魂
但視褓中兒

征婦詞

征婦語征夫
有身當殉國
君爲塞下土
妾作山頭石
종군하는 남편이 그 처에게 말하기를 「자기는 지금 征途에 나아가는데 이 뒤 죽을는지 살는지 모다 모른다. 만약 전사한 뒤 나의 혼백을 위로하려 하거든 다만 나의 혈육인 어린 아히를 잘 愛育하여 훌륭히 成人 식혀달나」
이에 대한 안해의 말은
「어서 일신을 던저 나라를 위하야 애써주서요. 당신이 명예의 전사를 하여 변경의 흙이 되면 나는 이우에 더 이 세상의 희망이 없오이다. 山頭에 돌이 되며 정조를 직히겠다고 말한다. 이것으론 우에 남편의 詞에 대하여 특히 자식가진 어머니 몸으로서 할말일가 하는 감도 있으나 하여턴 긔운있는 글이다. 과연 문학의 나라인만치 말이나 글에 대하야 위대하다고 생각한다.
征婦詞에 山頭의 돌이라 함은 楊子江岸 武昌의 북산 산상에 있는대 그 모앙이 사람 선 모앙인대 옛날 어떤 貞婦가 출정하는 남편을 떠나보내고 이 산에 와서 화석이 되었다는 말인대 內地 九州 松浦渴의 佐用姬의 이야기와 비슷한 전설을 가진 돌이다.

凱歌

明나라 沈明臣 作에 凱歌라는 것이 있다.

凱歌

銜枚夜渡五千兵
密領軍符號令明
狹巷短兵相接處
殺人如草不聞聲
이것은 금번 支那事變에 일본군 행동에 관하야 특이 미리 지어둔 듯한 시이다.
渡河 또 渡河, 각처에 봉기하는 시가전, 백병전, 그리고 十人斬 등이 얼마던지 있다. 文字와 같이 一騎當千 일본군이 용맹한 기세에는 이 名吟을 그대로 읊어도 좋을 것이다.

從軍行

奏時明月嘆時關
萬里長征人未還
但使龍城飛將在
不敎胡馬度陰山
이 시는 唐나라 王昌齡 作으로 軍에 勇將없이 인명을 막 상하는 일이 많음으로 탄식한 것이다. 참으로 支那軍인 점이 있다. 이번 사변을 보면 「胡馬로 陰山을 건니지 못하게 한다」는 句는 문제가 안이다.
萬里長城은 벌서 돌파하고 山西의 省城 太原을 함락당하고 黃河를 건너 패주하고 있다. 그러나 이런 때 「아무 대장이 있어더면」하고 망자의 일흠까지 끌어내며 우는 소리같은 시는 支那인의 시에서만 볼 수 있을 것이다. 蔣介石을 비롯하여 그 나라 將領들에게는 이제야 이런 감상이 더욱 애절할 것이다.<45>
