Author: 時中會 李晶燮 (Sijunghoe I Chŏngsŏp), 辯護士 李弘鍾 (Pyŏnhosa I Hongjong), 東洋靑年聯盟 朱雲成 (Tongyang Ch'ŏngnyŏn Yŏnmaeng Chu Unsŏng), 啓明俱樂部 朴勝彬 (Kyemyŏng Kurakpu Pak Sŭngbin), 大東民友會 安俊, 車載貞, 李承元, 李覺鍾 (Taedong Minuhoe An Chun, Ch'a Chaejŏng, I Sŭngwŏn, I Kakchong)
Article Type: 소식 (News/Report)

**Record of Meeting the Governor-General**

**Offering Advice Regarding a Mass Single Party**

By Sijunghoe I Chŏngsŏp

**Foreword**

It was February 4th. The meeting was scheduled for 3 PM, but thinking it polite to arrive about 15 minutes early, I hurried to the official residence, only to find—what's this!—about ten people had already arrived and were waiting. Inwardly, I couldn't help but gasp, "Oh dear!" Why? Because if we were to meet in order of arrival, I would be last. However, since it was originally a joint meeting, considering that listening well to others could also serve as a valuable lesson (他山之石), my mind became surprisingly calm. Soon, Governor-General Minami entered, dressed in simple Japanese attire (和服) and truly unarmed (丸腰), giving an impression entirely different from the Governor-General seen at ceremonies—like a kindly old man from the countryside (田舍의 好好翁). Because of this, the threads of the attendees' conversation seemed to unravel naturally.<30>

"I am merely an inexperienced intellectual (白面書生). Since the Governor-General's rule was implemented in Korea, nearly 30 years have passed. During that time, whenever the successive Governors-General changed positions, there were sometimes changes in partial policies regarding Korean governance, and consequently, there were occasional moments of notable difference. However, from the time of the Japan-Korea Annexation (日韓合倂) until recently, although fundamental policies regarding Korean governance must have been established by the Government-General and, of course, the central government [in Tokyo], there was also distress among the intelligentsia. Accordingly, even an insignificant person like myself, having occupied a humble corner of the Korean press, has attempted critiques (愚評) regarding this lack of clear direction more than just once or twice.

Then, after Your Excellency Minami took office, an unprecedented great change occurred in the East Asian situation. As the great Japanese Empire advanced onto the continent, Korea's position as its 'foothold' (足場) took on even greater importance. You decisively abandoned (揚棄) the previous thinking and policies that treated the Korean peninsula as merely a colony. For the nation's long-term plan, you established *Naeseon Ilche* (內鮮一體 - Japan and Korea as One Body), treating Korea exactly the same as the mainland (內地), as the fundamental direction for Korean governance. This, I believe, is an unprecedented (末曾有) act of great leadership since the beginning of the Governor-General's rule. Furthermore, the fact that you boldly implemented educational system reforms and established the volunteer soldier system as concrete manifestations of *Naeseon Ilche*, essentially bringing clarity (明朗性) to Korean governance, is truly something I view with great pleasure.

So, how should *Naeseon Ilche* be realized? I believe the future problem lies here. As a method for realizing *Naeseon Ilche*, I hear that the Government-General has recently mobilized officials to thoroughly instill its spirit in the masses through assemblies and documents. Of course, this is an appropriate method, and I certainly do not intend to criticize it. However, based on past experience, it is a fact that whenever any spirit or policy was promoted with the government office at the center or in the lead, there was a strong tendency for it to easily devolve into formalism (形式主義). Take the Rural Development Movement (農村振興運動), for example. Wasn't there a major flaw in making the people think they were doing it reluctantly just because the government office told them to? Similarly, if the *Naeseon Ilche* movement is led solely by the government office, it is clear that the masses will remain trapped in their old ways of thinking, believing 'we do this because the government office tells us to.' If things reach this point, I cannot help but foolishly worry that the *Naeseon Ilche* movement, which Your Excellency initiated with such great resolve, might end up as just an empty shell without substance (깝대기만 남고 알맹이가 들지 아니하는 결과). For the spirit of *Naeseon Ilche* to truly permeate the general public, the movement must become the people's own movement through complete cooperation between officials and the people, not just the officials.<31> Furthermore, since 'national defense in the broad sense' (廣義國防) in the current situation encompasses almost all of politics, I believe a *Naeseon Ilche* movement in its complete sense is only possible when officials (官), the military (軍), and the people (民) become one entity (三位一體). To speak more concretely, my main point is this: we should organize what might be called a Mass Single Party (民衆單一黨) or perhaps a Single Association (單一會) encompassing all adult men of Korea. However, achieving the desired goal through the people's strength alone is difficult. Officials and the military, within the structure of a peninsula-wide single league—that is, as influential constituent members—should mutually encourage and cooperate, enabling the masses themselves to awaken to the spirit of *Naeseon Ilche* and put it into practice, thoroughly making it the people's own movement.

In this way, the ideal of an intermediary organization that harmonizes and unites these three—the military, officials, and the masses—lies in its potential to become the core or the fountainhead (叢源) of the *Naeseon Ilche* principle. Therefore, the role that this Single Association would play (奏할 역할) in the actual governance of Korea would become quite significant. For instance, I saw an article in this morning's paper about Governor-General Minami's six major reform policies. Instead of just relaying these from headquarters to the regional governors to inform the public, if these reform policies were also communicated to the Single Association, the Association could digest the difficult language into plain terms and convey the true meaning of the policies to all adult men across the peninsula. This method would likely have a far greater reach in dissemination than informing the general public through local government offices. Also, suppose the local residents have grievances, inconveniences, complaints, or something they strongly wish to realize for their community life. If they present (具伸) the situation to the Single Association, which stands between the officials and the people, and the Association investigates the facts and determines that correction or new measures are necessary, and then negotiates with the officials—if the officials were to accept these requests based on principle and reason, it would be difficult to find a more harmonious system for the free expression of the people's will (民意暢達). Simply put, this Single Association, uniting the peninsula as one entity (一槐), would not only be the source of the *Naeseon Ilche* principle but also, the effectiveness and smoothness of the Governor-General's administration would only be possible through what I call the Single Association. Therefore, though detractors might call this Single Association the Governor-General's Office Special Action Unit (總督府 別動隊), I am convinced that, in reality, the Governor-General's administration can only achieve perfection when it is based on and founded upon this Single Association.

Presenting such opinions to officials at the bureau or section level felt too overwhelming a matter, so despite your busy schedule, I dared to come see you and offer these words, hoping they might be of some reference for Your Excellency's governance of Korea. I sincerely apologize for troubling you so much.<32> Please forgive me."

*Postscript* - The words above are exactly what I said to the Governor-General. I believe not a single phrase has been added. Also, Secretary Kondō (近藤), sitting beside and behind me, is certainly a man of discipline. I was truly impressed that he maintained his initial sitting posture steadily for nearly two hours. This might seem like an easy thing, but anyone who has tried it even once knows how difficult it is.

**Opening the Doors of the Judiciary Heads**

By Lawyer I Hongjong (辯護士 李弘鍾)

To gain opportunities to listen to the opinions of the general public and to save busy office hours, Governor-General Minami designated every Friday as a meeting day and began allowing joint audiences from the previous week.
Creating such opportunities for meeting as one method for realizing the 'free expression of the people's will' (民意暢達), one of the six major principles proclaimed by Governor-General Minami, is indeed a remarkable feature in today's peninsula governance, which shows the progress of the times.

Last February 4th was the second meeting day. From 3 PM to 5 PM at the Governor-General's residence in Waesŏngdae (倭城臺), I had the opportunity for a joint meeting with 14 other attendees seeking an audience.
On that day, the 14 gentlemen present expressed many well-reasoned opinions, including petitions, requests, and impressions. The Governor-General also listened intently to the various petitions and other impressions, while also sharing his own thoughts. The lengthy discussion on the current situation maintained a sense of tension amidst its freedom.

Following the order, Mr. Chu Unsŏng (朱雲成) began by emphasizing the need to form the East Asian Youth League (東洋青年聯盟). After three or four Japanese and Korean individuals had each stated their opinions, it was my turn.
Considering an opportunity like this meeting with the Governor-General extremely important, the opinions I presented concerned what I believe are the most critical issues in contemporary Korean society. I raised the following three points as requests to His Excellency the Governor-General.

Item 1: Request for the implementation of an administrative litigation system.
Item 2: Request for an increase in the number of Korean judges and prosecutors.<33>
Item 3: Request to open the doors for Koreans to hold senior positions (長官) in the judiciary.

These are the main points of my requests that day. Regarding the first item, the implementation of an "administrative litigation system," Japan proper (內地) saw its implementation starting with Law No. 48 of June 30, 1890 (Meiji 23), which was later revised by Law No. 37 of 1916 (Taishō 5), and it continues to this day. Despite the fact that its necessity is keenly felt in modern governance, which respects civil rights, the reality in Korea is that since the inception of the Governor-General's rule, no such legal rights have been enjoyed on the peninsula to this day.

Generally, matters such as granting certain rights to individuals or taking certain rights away are extremely grave issues, even from the legal standpoint of people's "rights" and "duties." Nevertheless, if administrative authorities were to commit acts infringing upon civil rights, the people truly have nowhere to appeal (呼所無處) legally.
Regarding this, no legal system has yet been established in Korea. At a time when clear governance (明朗政治) is advocated for the peninsula, compared to its implementation half a century ago in Japan proper, there is undeniably a sense of it being long overdue (晩時之感). I keenly feel the necessity of implementing the "administrative litigation system" even one day sooner. Considering the progress of the times and the improvement in the people's intellectual level (民度), isn't the current situation far more advanced than that of Japan proper half a century ago? Moreover, judging from recent events, legal bills enacted in Japan proper are often implemented immediately in Korea; this is the reality of peninsula governance. Doesn't this, in fact, indicate to us that the mainland and the peninsula are treated as being on the same level administratively? In any case, there is no discernible reason why the "administrative litigation system" has not been implemented in Korea to this day. In this meeting, face-to-face with Your Excellency the Governor-General, I earnestly request its immediate implementation.

Regarding the second item, the issue of increasing Korean judges and prosecutors, one cannot help but first mention the discriminatory treatment between Japanese and Koreans (內鮮人).
The following numbers prove this. Currently, the total number of judges in Korea is 224, but Korean judges number only 45, accounting for merely about 20% [Note: calculation error in original text, 45/224 is ~20%, text says 2%] of the total personnel. Furthermore, the total number of prosecutors is 107, but Korean<34> prosecutors number only 11, amounting to just over 10% [Note: calculation error in original text, 11/107 is ~10%, text says 1%] of the total personnel. This is the reality today.

Such contradictory facts require prompt correction, whether viewed from the perspective of the prestige of the judiciary or its rationality. No matter how well Japanese judges and prosecutors from the mainland (內地人) understand and recognize the situation in Korea in all aspects, it is an undeniable fact that there will be some difference compared to Korean judges and prosecutors when it comes to referencing and considering traditional local customs, practices, human feelings (人情), language, and so on.
In such cases where consideration is needed for the prestige of the judiciary, wouldn't it be most rational to provide more positions as judges and prosecutors to Koreans, who are well-versed in the realities and circumstances of Korea, rather than assigning the majority of personnel to mainlanders?
Moreover, recently, Koreans qualified to be judges and prosecutors are being produced every year. While the issue of qualified individuals might have been a problem long ago, these days, if there is only the magnanimity to employ them, there is ample room for recruitment. From this perspective as well, breaking free from the outdated (時代遲) notion of discriminatory treatment between Japanese and Koreans (內鮮人) and striving for clarity in peninsula governance, I strongly request an increase in Korean judges and prosecutors. At the very least, I hope for the appointment of Koreans to over 50% of the positions for both judges and prosecutors. Rather than a request, this is something I truly wish to implore His Excellency the Governor-General. What is Your Excellency's opinion?

Regarding the third item, I requested that the doors to senior positions (長官職) in the judiciary be opened to Koreans as well.
Taking my own example, I spent 12 years in the courts. However, for a Korean, promotion to senior positions like presiding judge (裁判長) or chief prosecutor (檢事正) was effectively impossible. Whether one stayed for 10 or 20 years, there wasn't much of a path to demonstrate one's talents. Therefore, I decided to leave the courts. At that time, Korean judges and prosecutors were extremely rare, and the court authorities strongly urged me not to resign. But having made up my mind, I refused all entreaties and left anyway. Of course, the authorities must have felt it was quite regrettable. For my part, I couldn't help but feel even more disappointed.

Since then, I have been practicing as a lawyer for 12 or 13 years now. However, the path ahead does not seem brightly open. This situation is certainly not unique to me; it is merely an example, but from the perspective of the state, it represents<35> a significant loss.
Looking at other administrative authorities, senior positions like bureau director (局長), governor (知事), or section chief (課長) are appropriately granted to Koreans. However, it seems unreasonable that only the judicial authorities do not give Koreans the positions of presiding judge or chief prosecutor. Why is it that not even a single Korean senior official is appointed, even in judicial institutions in remote mountain areas? I implore His Excellency the Governor-General to give this deep consideration.

In response to these requests and pleas, His Excellency the Governor-General seemed to listen deeply with a serious demeanor. He also expressed his intention to consider the matter carefully in the future.
In the past, no matter what requests or petitions one had, it was only possible to meet bureau directors or section chiefs at key posts and express opinions indirectly. Gaining a direct opportunity like this time is something I consider extremely fortunate.

By the way, the time spent on the above conversation was 15 minutes. Originally, the time allotted to me was only about 8 minutes, but since I spoke for nearly 15 minutes, I felt somewhat sorry for the others and cut my remarks short.
However, my initial plan was to make one more request.
It concerns the fact that there is not a single Korean judge in the High Court (高等法院). This is problematic both for the prestige of the judiciary and for the effectiveness of judgments.
The reason is that mainland judges (內地人) who are ignorant of Korean customs sometimes end up creating new Korean customs (朝鮮慣習). What is the reason (奈邊) for appointing only mainland judges despite enduring such disadvantages? I wanted to ask His Excellency the Governor-General's opinion. However, due to time constraints, I stopped and have no choice but to wait for a future opportunity.

**Aiming for the Unification of Asian Youth**

East Asian Youth League (東洋青年聯盟)
By Chu Unsŏng (朱雲成)

Suddenly receiving an express mail delivery from Samch'ŏlli-sa, requesting an account of the meeting with the Governor-General on February 4th, with a deadline of tomorrow<36>, I take up my pen amidst a busy schedule and will try to write something, though perhaps disorganized.
As I wrote in this magazine about observing the 71st Special Diet session that opened on July 25th last year, at that time, I had for several years deeply felt the need for the great unity of East Asian youth to build East Asian peace, sing the prelude to the Pacific era, and revive Yellow race civilization (黃人文明). Then, seeing the outbreak of the China Incident (支那事變), I felt even more strongly that this was the time. Carrying the "Proposal for the East Asian Youth League" (東洋青年聯盟案), I went to Tokyo (東京). After receiving endorsements from elder statesmen, ministers, and politicians, I returned home in mid-August. At that time, I visited Governor-General Minami, Chief Administrator Ōno (大野總監), and key bureau and section chiefs. However, the Governor-General was said to be extremely busy, so I arranged to meet him later. I only met with Chief Administrator Ōno, and we exchanged stories about our trips to Tokyo; he expressed goodwill towards me. After that, I established an office, published a small booklet, and remained silent, racking my brain over the progress of the work. Then, recently, Governor-General Minami established opportunities to listen to the voices of the people, holding meetings at the official residence every Friday. Secretary Kondō (近藤) repeatedly invited me to visit his home, so on February 4th, the Friday of the second week of these public hearings, I decided to go and signed my name on the register.

**The Setting Sun at the Waesŏngdae (倭城臺) Residence**

I had to arrive by 3 PM that day, so I took the tram to the front of the Bank of Chosŏn (朝鮮銀行) and then walked through Honmachi (本町) up towards Namsan-chō (南山町). Passing the old Government-General building, walking a bit along the wide asphalt road, nestled in a scenic wooded area, stood the imposing Governor-General's residence. When I arrived, I was the first one there. Guided by a secretary attached to the residence, I entered a spacious, modern reception room, which turned out to be the very chamber where public opinion would freely flow (風發). There were dozens of long benches and armchairs, a square table in the center, seating for eight around it, and on one side, a large armchair designated as the Governor-General's seat.
After me, Mr. I Hongjong (李弘鍾) entered, followed by interpreter Tanaka (田中), then Mr. Tachibana (橘圓壽), Mr. Hatae (波多江), Mr. Kim Sŭngbok (金昇福), Mr. I Chŏngsŏp (李晶爕), Mr. Miyazaki (宮崎), Mr. Pak Sŭngbin (朴勝彬), Mr. Son Hongwŏn (孫弘遠), Mr. O Yongt'aek (吳龍澤), Mr. Pak Kwanjun (朴寬俊), Mr. Cho Tongshik (趙東植), Mr. I Kakchong (李覺鍾), and Mr. Chu T'aedo (朱泰道)—14 people took their seats. Newspaper reporters carrying cameras entered one after another. Secretary Kondō entered holding the list of names, and the north door opened, revealing His Excellency Minami entering with a gentle expression (和顔). The room felt warm from the afternoon sun slanting through the west window. The Governor-General said, 「あついでしやう, ぢよつと開けて 置きませう」(Atsui deshō, chotto akete okimashō - "It must be hot, let's open [the door] a bit"), opened the north door, and took his seat. Secretary Kondō, looking at the list, announced they would proceed in order of arrival and called "Mr. Chu Unsŏng." I bowed to the Governor-General and sat down close to him.

**Conversation between the Governor-General and Myself**<37>

There's no obligation to report the content of the conversation here, but since I'm urged to write, I will outline the main points.
Me = Thank you for granting me this audience.
Governor-General = Please speak.
Me = What I am currently working on is the youth movement. Why do I focus on youth? It's stating the obvious, but isn't today truly the era of youth, the world of youth? The *Naeseon Ilche* that Your Excellency speaks of must be built upon the youth, and East Asian peace must also be built upon the genuine awakening of East Asian youth, mustn't it?
Governor-General = That's right.
Me = Therefore, deeply feeling the importance of the youth movement, I have refined my humble proposal (拙案) over several years. Believing that the new ideal of reviving New East Asian Culture by uniting young comrades with an East Asian ideology is the key to East Asian peace, I went to Tokyo last year, explained my convictions to various Excellencies, and even obtained their signatures. (Presented the proposal booklet).
Governor-General = Ah, due to time constraints, I cannot read it all, but looking at the signatories, many seem to have a deep understanding of Korea.
Me = Actually, I drafted this proposal before the China Incident (支那事變). When the incident occurred, I felt the need even more acutely, but as the situation grew more serious day by day, I suspended the full-scale movement and have only been conducting a spiritual movement, recently publishing 5,000 copies of a pamphlet (팜푸렛트). In the future, I intend to pursue this movement more vigorously, but as you know, while I have passion and ideals, I lack strength. Therefore, I earnestly hope for Your Excellency's spiritual support.
Governor-General = Mm, I understand well.
Me = Also, the fact that Your Excellency is conducting governance suited to the new era is acclaimed by us young citizens. Judging even from the youth's response to the recent implementation of the volunteer soldier system, I see it as grounds for celebrating Your Excellency's innovative governance. (Remainder omitted)

With the Governor-General's kind response, our 5-minute conversation concluded. Expressing my wish to seek his counsel whenever opportunities arise in the future, I withdrew. Sitting in a back seat, I listened to the exchanges of the other individuals and answered questions from reporters.

**New Directions for Korean Governance**

"Make them rely [on us], do not make them know" (依らしむべく, 知らしむべからず) was the governing principle for Korea over the past quarter-century. As a result, the public discourse (言論) of the Korean populace was effectively confined to private discussions (內室公論). Times have progressed, the people's sentiments have renewed, and finally, even the rulers have shed the outdated notion of 'reverence for officials, contempt for the people' (官尊民卑) and have come to recognize the importance of popular politics (民衆政治).<38>

Looking at the proposals made by various individuals that day, it was a page of free appeal, the first of its kind in the history of Korean governance. This is clearly a fragment of Governor-General Minami's distinctive governing policy. A society without public opinion cannot establish justice, and politics that does not reflect the people's will (民意) will find it difficult to ensure fairness. Where the voice of the people (民聲) is absent, desolation follows; the people's voice is surely the rooster's crow at dawn (淸晨의 鷄鳴聲), the sound of paulownia leaves indicating autumn's arrival (知秋의 梧葉聲). We cannot help but anticipate the future smoothness of governance resulting from Governor-General Minami's deep recognition of this subtlety.

**Impression of the Governor-General**

I have often observed that many people perceive Governor-General Minami as strict. However, the impression anyone gets from actually meeting him is that he is a Governor-General full of human warmth (人情總督). In reality, his compassionate side is likely greater than his stern side. His eyes, full of human feeling; his face, radiating warmth (和氣); his unassuming manner of receiving guests; his gentle voice; his ample physique—all confirm he is undoubtedly a "kindly old man" (好い爺さん). And he is intelligent. He grasps the speaker's point midway through and offers a response, clarifying the issue. Thus, he prevents redundant explanations. His intelligence is evident in his addresses; I have seen him speak fluently from memory several times without referring to a manuscript. In terms of speech delivery, I consider him the best among the successive Governors-General. What's interesting is the recent eloquence of military figures. Attending the Diet, I heard speeches by the ministers of state. In the current cabinet, excluding Communications Minister Nagai (永井遞相), the speeches of Army Minister Sugiyama (杉山陸相) and Navy Minister Yonai (米內海相) were far superior to those of Prime Minister Konoe (近衛首相) and Foreign Minister Hirota (廣田外相). Navy Minister Yonai could be called eloquent. Governor-General Minami, though a general, is also a skilled speaker. With his dignified bearing (風采), he already fully exhibits the stature for delivering a Prime Minister's policy speech in the future. Governor-General Minami also appears to have a common touch and seems like a diligent worker. The fact that he holds these unprecedented public audiences (民衆引見) is one aspect of his sincerity and diligence (誠勤). Looking at his office arrival time, he is said to be a hardworking Governor-General, consistently arriving at his office by 8:30 AM sharp.

**Further Remarks**

Although I know I am criticized for being an impudent youth, I have submitted petitions several times since Governor-General Minami took office. However, no matter how young I may be, when I am convinced something benefits the citizens, I tend to offer my opinions boldly. Friends tell me I'm precocious (早熟). I understand precocious implies contempt for my youthfulness (年淺). However, Britain's Gladstone (끌랫스톤) became a Member of Parliament at 22 and influenced national affairs, Pitt (빗트) became a minister at 25, and Napoleon (나폴네온) became Minister of War at 26, so at thirty-something, I am rather late in maturing. Lincoln (린컨) studied American politics at age 14, so how could it be presumptuous (越分) for us to take an interest in the Governor-General's administration? Let this suffice as an explanation. Looking ahead to Minami's future governance,<39> I would like to mention a few more things. The opinions expressed by individuals over the two recent meetings seem to focus mainly on abolishing discrimination between Japanese and Koreans (內鮮人). While I believe this will be implemented progressively, I hope the cliché "it's premature" (時期尚早) will not be used. We hope for decisive reforms immediately regarding inconveniences we feel. The abolition of the travel certificate requirement for going to the mainland (內地渡航證明廢止論) is an urgent demand. If the Publication Law (出版法) and Newspaper Law (新聞紙法) were applied equally to Koreans as to mainlanders, how great would the joy of peninsula intellectuals be? For the realization of the free expression of the people's will (民意暢達), it is crucial to establish a central guiding force for the populace, enabling the expression of pure public opinion and fostering autonomous strengthening of social education to elevate the people's intellectual level (民度). I believe controlling all sorts of movements under government supervision, as is current practice in Korea, delays the elevation of the people's intellectual level. For instance, the move to further unify athletic organizations, which are currently gaining momentum through private efforts, into government-established bodies requires more consideration from the authorities.

**Power and the Human Condition**

On the way back that day, strolling through Waesŏngdae (倭城臺) and looking down at the myriad houses of the capital (萬戶長安), I reflected on power and the human condition. Look! Hitler (히틀러) and Mussolini (뭇소리니), after enduring countless imprisonments and numerous setbacks (七顚八起), persisted with one hundred percent effort and inevitably reached the point of success. The fate of shepherd-less Germany converged upon Hitler's head, and the fate of impoverished Italy converged upon Mussolini's. Therefore, even if the calculated conduct and flattering words of young men and women today, who harbor ambitions for success (青雲之志) and drift here and there, possess superhuman superiority, unless one traverses the hill of self-reliant effort, the blessings of fate will not shower upon oneself. In this sense, I daily recite the phrase from Mencius (孟子): "When Heaven is about to confer a great responsibility upon a person (天將欲其任於是人), it first visits his mind and heart with suffering (必先苦其心志), toil on his sinews and bones (勞其筯骨), and frustrates his undertakings (行佛亂其所爲). Then, it entrusts him with the great responsibility (然後에야 大任을 맛기신다)." The time spent enduring hardship (苦) is, in fact, the time when the light (光) of hope brightly burns. This is because today's hardship transforms into tomorrow's joy (樂). Korean youth, grasp this truth! Muttering these thoughts, the proceedings of the February 4th meeting with the Governor-General concluded.<40>

**The Issue of Lifestyle Improvement**

Kyemyŏng Kurakbu (啓明俱樂部)
By Pak Sŭngbin (朴勝彬)

Q: Didn't you visit Governor-General Minami last Friday, sir?
A: Yes, I did.
Q: Could you please tell us about the opinions you expressed at that time?
A: No. My purpose in going that day was not to state opinions regarding any particular demands. At the recent general meeting of the Kyemyŏng Kurakbu, there was a resolution that two directors should express gratitude to the Governor-General regarding the expansion and improvement of educational institutions and the volunteer soldier system, both recently implemented. So, that day, Managing Director Cho Tongshik (趙東植) and I (as a director) visited the Governor-General and simply conveyed the content of that resolution. The two of us went that day in our capacity as directors representing the Kyemyŏng Kurakbu, not in our personal capacities. Therefore, we did not express any personal opinions outside of those related to the Kyemyŏng Kurakbu. Consequently, we merely relayed the matters resolved at the general meeting as mentioned before, and there was no other conversation. Thus, the words exchanged were extremely brief, lasting no more than three or four minutes.
Q: I imagine there must have been much more conversation besides that. How was it?
A: You are free to imagine. As for other conversation, when the Governor-General asked what kind of organization the Kyemyŏng Kurakbu was, I replied that it is an organization established with the aim of improving the lifestyle of Koreans, founded over 20 years ago, and that during that time, various resolutions had been made. I added that among those resolutions, some matters have recently been definitively implemented through the efforts of the authorities (官). For example, years ago, we submitted a proposal to the Government-General to implement market days according to the solar calendar dates to enforce the use of the solar calendar (陽曆 實行), and this year, that has been realized. Also, regarding the encouragement of colored clothing (色衣獎勵), we resolved on that 18 years ago, and I expressed gratitude that it is steadily being realized these days. I stated that the Kyemyŏng Kurakbu's mission is to contribute to lifestyle improvement and that it has absolutely no involvement in politics.
Q: What did the Governor-General say?
A: In response to our expression of gratitude, there was a simple acknowledgement. Regarding the explanation of the Kyemyŏng Kurakbu's purpose, he simply replied, "That's a good thing (조흔 일이라고)."
Q: What was your impression of Governor-General Minami?
A: Haha, he is the Governor-General of Korea, of course, he must be great (에라이 하겟지요).<41>

**Regarding National Spiritual Mobilization and Other Matters**

Taedong Minuhoe (大東民友會)
By An Chun (安俊), Ch'a Chaejŏng (車載貞), I Sŭngwŏn (李承元), I Kakchong (李覺鍾)

It was 3 PM on March 4th. We arrived at the Governor-General's residence in Waesŏngdae and met with Governor-General Minami. The main points we discussed that day and the Governor-General's responses were as follows.

We feel rather apologetic for having engaged in illegal movements for a long time and committing anti-Japanese actions. We are fortunate (欣幸) to have this opportunity today to meet Your Excellency and state our opinions regarding Korean governance. However, due to time constraints, we will avoid detailed discussion across various sectors and merely list the main points below for your reference.

Reference Items
1. On the Inevitability of Korea's Transformation (轉向)
a. The nationalist XX movement based on liberal and democratic thought was overthrown by the rise of the communist movement, which had liberalism and democracy as its cradle.
b. Subsequently, the communist movement was liquidated through the self-criticism of the Korean masses = the re-recognition of Eastern traditions and the awakening of national identity.
c. The aforementioned conditions of recognition and awakening among the Korean masses mutually align with the various opportunities for building a New Japan, thereby making the awakening as citizens of Japan inevitable.
Thus, the Korean masses are not a colonized, oppressed people, but have discovered the path to rebirth as citizens of a dignified, great world nation. Filled with the pride and hope of being citizens of a bright "Tomorrow's Japan," they strive to undertake their share of the duty in building a New Japan.
This is truly the historically inevitable direction, the epochal desire of the Korean masses, and simultaneously the spiritual basis for various patriotic movements. Therefore, future peninsula governance must entirely be based on such a main current.

2. On the Guiding Principles of *Naeseon Ilche* (內鮮一體)
a. Cultivate the Imperial Way spirit (皇道精神) among the Korean masses, enabling them to recognize and grasp the great ideal of New Japan.<42> Based on the historical fact of common ancestry (同祖同根), the ultimate goal shall be the formation of a unified great nation (一元의 대민족결성).
b. In the process of realizing this:
① The foundation for the union of the two peoples (兩民族) must prioritize spiritual conditions as an urgent task.
② Opportunities for fraternal cooperation economically, politically, militarily, and culturally should gradually be equalized.
③ The unification of Japan and Korea (內鮮一元化) must avoid causing misunderstandings, such as signifying Japan's cultural conquest of the Korean masses or the extinction of the Korean people. That is, differences in customs, habits, and other lifestyle aspects should not be forcibly unified as long as they do not impede the overall improvement and development of national life. Rather, aspects of Korea's unique culture and fine customs (醇風美俗) that can benefit national life should be encouraged and developed.

3. On National Spiritual Mobilization (國民精神總動員)
a. In the face of the current situation (這般 시국), the authorities' approach of treating the Korean masses with national status (國民的 處遇), thereby enabling opportunities for fraternal cooperation on the home front (銃後) and national service (國民的 奉公), is a most appropriate measure, proving its significant effectiveness (效績).
b. However, in some segments, it has not yet been sufficiently thorough, and there is a tendency for a lack of spontaneous initiative from the masses. This is an unavoidable flaw during the period of government-led propaganda.
c. Therefore, as one long-term measure for the current situation, we recognize the need to organize a large-scale national movement centered on civilian volunteers (民間有志). Especially concerning the promotion of *Naeseon Ilche* and the prevention and rejection of anti-state ideologies, the proactive, voluntary activities of civilian volunteers are necessary.
d. Civilian volunteers should also be included in the committee preparing countermeasures for the current situation (時局對策準備委員會) to reflect the people's will (民意) and promote the momentum of government-civilian cooperation (官民協力).
e. Those political and ideological offenders who have completely reformed (轉向) should be treated with particular leniency, enabling them to voluntarily participate actively in the national movement.
f. Efforts should be made for the thorough dissemination of adult education for Koreans and the organization of the masses.

4. On the Enrichment and Improvement of National Life
a. Establish a fundamental plan for the Korean economy. As one concrete requirement for realizing *Naeseon Ilche*, strive to enrich the economic capacity of Koreans and improve their actual living standards, gradually bringing the living standards of Japanese and Koreans (內鮮人) closer together.
b. Examples recognized as requiring urgent implementation now:
① Establishment of special funds aimed at rural relief = similar to people's banks (庶民金庫)<43>.
② Enactment of a special law for owner-farmer land (自作農地特別法) and distribution of farmland from the Oriental Development Company (東拓) and other large farms.
③ Financial protection for small and medium-sized merchants and manufacturers.
④ Protection, training, and education for Korean laborers = enactment of a Factory Act (工場法), etc.
⑤ Implementation of a special adjustment system for monetary debt (金錢債務 特制 調整制).
⑥ Strengthening of price controls.
⑦ Pioneering job opportunities and career paths for the Korean intellectual class.
⑧ Active guidance and protection for Koreans advancing into China (支那).

Governor-General Minami listened for a long time and then, with an attitude of deep emotion (感慨無量), spoke as follows:
Thank you for sharing many frank opinions. We particularly plan to include Koreans in the committee for countermeasures for the current situation. Korea's unique fine customs (醇風美俗) will be respected as much as possible, and the advancement of Koreans into China will also be actively encouraged.

Continuing, [we said] this is a minor question, but in the Governor-General's addresses, phrases like "Japanization" (日本化) appear. These are prone to causing misunderstanding among parts of the public. That is, they raise suspicions about whether it implies forced Japanization extending even to daily life aspects like clothing, housing, and food. Would it not be advisable to pay special attention to this in the future?

The Governor-General, with a surprised expression, replied: This is something I had not imagined. Interpreting things out of context (斷章摘句) fails to grasp my true intention, so I hope you will particularly understand this point. Some Koreans even say that my original position (本職) is Korean; perhaps that might even be true. In any case, I hope to fully realize *Naeseon Ilche* and achieve a successful conclusion (有終の美), and I ask for your continued guidance (鞭撻) whenever opportunities arise.

The meeting concluded after lasting about 50 minutes. (End) <44>

**Summary**

This collection of reports details meetings held in early 1938 between various Korean individuals and groups, representing diverse interests (journalism, law, youth activism, social reform, former political activists), and the Japanese Governor-General of Korea, Minami Jirō. The Koreans presented a range of opinions and requests centered on the Governor-General's policy of *Naeseon Ilche* (Japan-Korea Integration). Key demands included genuine implementation of *Naeseon Ilche* beyond mere formality, increased Korean political participation (suggesting a unified mass organization), elimination of discrimination in the judiciary (specifically demanding more Korean judges/prosecutors and access to senior posts), implementation of an administrative court system, addressing economic hardships, protecting Korean culture, and clarifying the meaning of "Japanization." The Governor-General generally listened attentively and promised consideration, emphasizing his commitment to *Naeseon Ilche* while acknowledging the need to respect Korean customs.

From a historical perspective, these accounts are valuable for several reasons. They provide a direct glimpse into the interactions between colonial authorities and segments of the Korean elite during a critical period marked by the intensification of assimilation policies and the ongoing Sino-Japanese War. The reports reveal the strategies employed by Koreans – ranging from cautious requests for reform within the system (I Hongjong, Pak Sŭngbin) to more proactive proposals for collaboration under the *Naeseon Ilche* banner (I Chŏngsŏp, Chu Unsŏng, Taedong Minuhoe) – reflecting diverse responses to colonial rule. The detailed demands, particularly regarding judicial discrimination and economic issues, highlight specific points of friction and Korean aspirations. Furthermore, the discussions around *Naeseon Ilche* and "Japanization" expose the inherent ambiguities and anxieties surrounding the policy, showing Korean attempts to negotiate its meaning and implementation. The inclusion of former anti-Japanese activists (Taedong Minuhoe) presenting their "conversion" (轉向 - chŏnhyang) and outlining conditions for cooperation provides insight into the complex political realignments occurring under wartime mobilization.

總督會見記
民衆單一黨에 對하야 進言하다

時中會 李晶燮

前言

그것은 2월 4일이엇다. 오후 3시 면회이지만 시각보다 한 15분 가량 일직이 가는 것이 예의 일상 십허서 밥비밥비 관저로 갓더니 웬걸! 나몬저 발서 10여인이 와서 기달이고 잇는데에는 속으로 가만히 『에쿠!』하지 안을 수 업섯다. 웨 그러냐하면 만일 도착순으로 면담한다면 나는 맨꼴재가 되겟스므로써이엇다. 그러나 원래 공동회견이라 남의 말을 잘 듯는 것도 또한 他山之石될 수 잇슬 것을 생각할 때 내 마음은 의외로 침착하여젓다. 이윽고 南總督은 수수한 和服에 그야말로 『丸腰』로 입래한 바, 식장 가튼데서 배면하든 총독과는 전연히 딴 田舍의 好好翁가튼 인상을 주어 이 때문에 면회인의 말의 실마리가 저절로 슬슬 풀니는 듯이 늣겨젓다.<30>
『저는 일개 白面書生이올시다. 조선에 총독정치가 실시되여서 어언간 30년이 되려하는 그 동안 역대 총독이 그 자리를 박구는 때마다 조선통치상의 부분적 정책에는 변동도 잇섯고 따라서 이채를 발한 적도 각금 잇섯습니다. 그러나 日韓合倂 이래 최근에 이르기까지 조선 통치에 관한 근본 정책이라는 것은 총독부에는 물론 중앙정부에서도 수립되여 잇기는 햇겟스나 개중에 지식층의 고민이 또한 잇섯스며 따라서 저와 가튼 변변치 못한 자도 조선 언론계의 말석을 더럽히엿더니만큼 이 무방침 상태에 대한 愚評을 試한 적도 무릇 한두번에 끄치지 아니하엿습니다.
그러다가 南閣下가 취임하신 이후 동양대세에 공전의 대변동이 야기되어 大 日本帝國의 대륙진전에 잇서서 조선이 그 『足場』이라는 지위가 更一層의 중요성을 나타내이매 종래와 가티 조선반도를 일개의 식민지로 박게 취급하지 아니하든 그 사상 그 정책을 단연 揚棄하고 국가백년대계를 위하야 朝鮮도 內地와 꼭 갓다는 內鮮一體로써 조선통치의 근본방침을 삼으신 것은 총독정치 이래 末曾有의 일대영단이라 생각합니다. 더구나 內鮮一體의 단적 구현화로 학제개혁을 단행하며 지원병제도를 신설하는 등 요컨대 조선통치에 명랑성을 던저 주섯다는 것은 참으로 흔쾌히 녁이는 바이올시다.

그러면 內鮮一體를 엇더한 방법으로써 구현화할 것인가? 금후의 문제는 여긔에 잇스리라고 밋습니다. 이 內鮮一體의 구현방법으로서 근래 총독부에서는 관원을 동원해서 집회로 또는 문서로써 그 정신을 대중에게 철저시키려는 듯이 들니는 바 물론 이것은 적절한 방법이라 저로서 감히 비난하고저 하는 배 아니올시다. 그러나 종래 경험에 의하면 무슨 정신이고 정책이고 간에 관청이 중심이 된다거나 선봉이 되여서 하는 일은 깨딱하면 形式主義에 흘너버리기 쉬운 경향이 많엇든 것이 사실이엇습니다. 農村振興運動 갓튼 것도 이것은 관청에서 허라니까 부득이한다고 백성으로 하여곰 생각하게 한 거긔에 커다란 결함이 잇지 안엇든가 생각합니다. 同樣으로 內鮮一體運動도 관청만이 주체가 되여서는 이것은 관청에서 하라니까 한다고 민중들은 依然 舊式의 생각에 사로잡히기 분명하니 만일 일이 이에 이른다고 하면 각하가 모처럼 대영단을 내려 이르키랴는 內鮮一體의 운동도 혹은 깝대기만 남고 알맹이가 들지 아니하는 결과에 이르지나 아니할가 감히 어리석은 근심을 하고 잇습니다. 內鮮一體의 정신이 일반 대중에게 진정하게 철저화하랴면 官뿐만 아니라 관과 민의 완전한 합작으로써 그 운동을 민중 스스로의 운동화하지 안어서는 안되리라고 봅니다.<31> 게다가 現下의 廣義國防이라는 것은 정치의 거이 전부를 포옹하게 되엿스므로 완전한 의미의 內鮮一體의 운동은 官, 군, 민 삼위가 일체됨으로써만 가능할 줄 밋습니다. 좀 더 구체적으로 말슴하면 조선의 전 장정을 망라하는 民衆單一黨이랄가 혹은 單一會를 조직시키되 민중 자력만으로서는 소기의 목적을 달하기 어려우며 관과 군이 반도단일연맹의 기구 내에서 즉 그 유력한 구성 분자로서 서로 독려하며 협력해서 민중 자체로 하여금 內鮮一體의 정신을 자각케하여 실천케해서 어데까지 민중자체의 운동으로 철저화시키자는 것이 제가 말슴드리려는 요지외다.
이와 가티 군, 관과 민중 사이에 삼자를 융화하야 일체로 맨드는 중간기관의 이상으로 하는 바가 內鮮一體主義의 핵심 혹은 叢源이 되려는 데 잇스니 만큼 그 單一會가 조선의 실제 통치 상에 잇서서 奏할 역할도 상당히 중대하여 질 것입니다. 가령 일례를 들어 말삼하자면 오늘 조간에서 南總督의 6대혁신정책이라는 기사를 보았습니다만은 이러한 것도 본부에서 각 지방장관에 전달하야 민중에게 알니기로만 하지 말고 그 혁신정책을 單一會에도 통달하면 單一會에서는 그 어려운 문구를 평이하게 소화해서 그 정책의 진의를 전반도 壯丁에게 알니게 한다면 이 방법이 오히려 지방관청을 통해서 일반 민중에게 알니는 것보다 그 보급력이 훨신 더 확대될 것입니다. 또 가령 지방주민으로부터 불리한 불편한 점 불평의 점 또는 지방의 공동생활상 꼭 실현하고 십흔 무엇이 잇다 할 때 그 사정을 관과 민의 중간에서 잇는 單一會에 具伸하면 單一會에서 그 진상을 심사하야 시정을 요한다든가 혹은 신설을 要로한다는 단안이 내릴 때 이를 單一會에서 관에 교섭하는 경우 관에서 原理上理의 요청을 청허하도록 한다 하면 민의창달로서 이 이상 더 원만한 제도는 달니 구하기가 어려울 줄 밋습니다. 간단히 말슴하면 반도를 一槐로 맨드는 單一會는 다만 內鮮一體主義의 연원이 될 뿐만 아니라 총독 정치의 보급성과 원활성도 제가 이른바 單一會를 통해서만 가능하다는 것이올시다. 그러므로 惡口더러 말하라면 혹은 이 單一會를 가르켜 총독부 別動隊라 할지 모르겟습니다만은 사실에 잇서서는 총독부정치가 이 單一會를 토대로 기초로 하는 원리 우에서라야 그 완전을 기하리라 확신하는 바이올시다.
대개 이러한 의견은 어느 局課의 관원에게 말슴들이기는 문제가 너무 벅찬 감이 잇서서 다망하신 중임을 불구하고 감히 차저 뵈옵고 한마듸의 말슴을 드려 각하의 조선통치 상 행여 나 무슨 참고가 되면 하는 생각으로 퍽 괴려움을 끼처 매우 죄송합니다.<32> 용서하서요.
附記 - 이 우에 말슴은 내가 총독에게 말슴한 바로 그대로이다 一言半句의 첨가도 업는 냥으로 밋는다 그리고 나의 側 背面에 안진 近藤 비서관은 확실히 수양잇는 인물로서 근 2시간 동안 그 처음 안진 자세를 그대로 꾸준히 가지고 잇슴에는 참으로 감복하엿다 이런 것은 헐한 일가트되 한번이고 하여 본 사람은 그 難을 알 것이다.

司法部 長官의 門戶開放으로

辯護士 李弘鍾

일반 민중의 여론을 청취하는 기회를 얻고 분망한 사무시간을 절약하기 위하야 南總督은 매주 금요일을 면회일로 정하고 지난 주일부터 공동 회견을 許하게 되엇다.
南總督이 부르짓는 6대정강 중에서 한아인 민의창달의 한 방법으로 이러한 회합의 기회를 짓게 됨은 실로 시운의 진전을 보게 된 반도통치의 금일에 있어서 일대 이채가 아닐 수 업다.
지난 2월 4일은 그 제2회 회견일로서 오후 3시부터 同 5시까지 倭城臺 총독관저에서 14명의 면회 시청자와 공동 회견할 기회를 얻엇다.
당일 참석한 14명의 諸氏로부터는 진정, 요망, 감상 등 조리잇는 의견이 만히 토로되엿스며 또한 총독으로부터도 여러 사람의 진정, 기타 감상을 열심히 청취하는 一方에 총독의 감상도 말하야 장시간의 時局談은 자유로운 중에서도 긴장미를 띄게 되엿섯다.
순서에 의하야 먼저 朱雲成씨로부터 東洋靑年聯盟 결성의 필요를 역설하기를 비롯해서 內鮮人 3,4인이 각기 의견을 말한 다음 본인의 順에 이르럿다.
나는 이번과 갓흔 총독 면회의 기회를 극히 중요시하느니 만치 今般 제출하는 의견도 現下 조선사회에 잇서서 가장 중대한 문제라고 사유되는 바 이하 3개 조항을 드러 총독 각하에 요망하엿든 것이다.
제 1항 행정재판제도 실시의 요망.
제 2항 조선인, 판검사의 증원 요망.<33>
제 3항 사법부 각장관의 문호 개방 요망.
이상이 당일 요망한 바 나의 의견의 要領인데 제 1항인 「행정재판제도」 실시에 대하여서는 日本 內地에 있어서는 明治 23년 6월 30일 법률 제사십팔호로써 이의 실시를 보기 비롯해서 大正 5년 법률 제 37호로써 이의 개정을 본 이래 금일에 至하였는 바 민권을 존중하는 현대 통치 상 절대 그의 필요성을 통감하고 있음이 사실임에도 불구하고 조선 내에 있어서는 총독정치 施政 이래 尙今까지 此에 준할 하등의 법적 권리를 享受치 못하고 있음이 半島 금일의 현상이다.
무릇 개인에게 엇더한 권리를 준다든가 혹은 엇더한 권리를 빼았는다든가 하는 등의 사실은 인민의 「권리」, 「의무」라는 법적 거점에 입각해 보드라도 심히 중차대한 문제인 줄로 사유한다. 그럼에도 불구하고 여긔에 만일 행정당국으로부터 민권을 혹은 그릇하는 행위가 있었다고 할 때 인민된 자 여기 대하여 법적 反議란 진실로 呼所無處한 바가 있다.
여긔에 대하야 조선에 있어서는 아직까지도 하등의 법적 제도가 설정되여 있지 않다. 반도 통치에 있어서 明朗정치를 표방하는 금일에 際하야 日本 內地에 있어서 반세기전 실시를 보게 된 데 비해서는 너무나 晩時之感이 不無하다. 일일이라도 신속히 「행정재판제도」 실시의 필요를 절감하는 所以이다 시운의 진전과 민도의 향상된 점으로 보더라도 日本 內地의 반세기전 보다는 훨신 진보된 금일의 현상이 아닌가. 더구나 최근의 사실로 미루워 보면 日本 內地에서 법적 제정을 보게 되는 법안은 조선에 있어서도 이의 직시 실시를 감행하고 있는 것이 반도통치의 현실이다. 도리혀 이런 점으로 볼 때에는 內地와 半島는 통치상 동일한 수준에 처해 있다는 것을 우리들에게 인식식혀 왓섯음을 말함이 아닌가 한다. 엇지 되엿든 「행정재판제도」가 오늘날까지 조선 내에 있어서 이의 실시를 보지 못하고 있음은 그의 이유를 어데서도 차즐 수 업다. 총독각하을 직접 면대하게 되는 이 좌석에서 이의 직시 실시를 요망하야 마지 안는다.
제 2항인 조선인 판검사의 증원 문제에 대해서는 內鮮人의 차별 대우를 爲先 말하지 않을 수 없다.
그것은 이하의 數字가 이를 증명하고 있다. 즉 현재 조선 내에 있어서 판사의 총수는 224명인데 조선인 판사는 겨우 45명으로 전 인원의 약 2%에 불과한 것이 사실이며 또는 검사의 총수는 107명인데 조선인<34> 검사는 겨우 11명으로 전 인원의 약 1%에 지나지 않는 것이 금일의 현상이다.
이와 같이 모순된 사실은 재판의 위신 상으로 보던지 또는 재판의 합리적인 방면으로 보드라도 속히 시정을 요하는 바이다. 그것은 日本 內地人 판검사는 모든 점에 있어서 아모리 조선의 사정을 잘 이해하고 인식한다 하드라도 조선 전래의 지방풍속이나 관습이며 또는 인정, 언어 등의 참고, 참작에 있어서 조선인 판검사보다는 좀 차이가 잇슬 것은 숨길 수 없는 사실이다.
이러케 재판상 위신으로 보아 좀 생각하여야 할 경우에 있는 內地人의게 다대수의 인원을 두기보다는 조선의 현실과 사정에 능통한 조선인에게 판검사의 자리를 보담 많이 제공함이 가장 합리적이 아닌가 한다.
더구나 최근에는 조선인 중에서도 판검사의 자격을 가진 자가 매년 배출되고 있는 현상이다. 그러면 오래 전에는 자격자 문제도 문제이엿슬 것이나 근자에는 채용할 아량만 있다면 얼마든지 채용할 여지가 있는 줄로 안다. 이런 점으로 보아 內鮮人 차별대우라는 時代遲한 관념에서 버서나서 반도통치의 명랑화를 기하기 위해서도 단연 조선인 판검사의 증원을 요망하는 바이다. 적어도 판검사를 통해서 각기 5할 이상의 조선인 채용을 바라는 바이다. 이는 요망이라는 것 보다도 총독 각하에게 실로 경원하고 십흔 바이다. 각하의 의견은 여하한가?
제 3항 사법부에 있어서 조선인에게도 장관직에의 문호를 개방하기를 요망하엿다.
나 개인의 예만 보드라도 재판소에 12년을 잇섯다. 그러나 조선 사람으로서는 도저히 재판장이나 檢事正과 같은 장관으로의 승진은 어럽게 되여있다. 10년을 있고 20년을 있엇대야 별로히 재능을 발휘식힐 진로란 것이 그리 만치 못하였다. 그래서 재판소에서 물너나기로 나는 생각햇다. 그 당시 조선인으로서 판검사란 극히 희귀하였든 것으로 나의 퇴직을 재판소 당국자들은 구지 만류하였다. 그러나 생각한 바가 있는 지라 나는 모든 것을 물니치고 그여히 나오고 마럿다. 물론 당국자로서도 퍽 유감으로 녁엿슬 것이다. 나로서는 더 한층 섭섭히 녁이지 아니치 못하였다.
그 뒤 변호사로서 개업해서 내려온 지도 지금 12,3년이 되였다. 그러나 앞길이란 그의 환-하게 열여 잇는 듯 하지 안타. 이러한 사실은 비단 나뿐만 아닐 것은 물론이요 한갓 예를 말한데 불과하나 국가로서 볼 때에는<35> 적지 않은 손실이다.
다른 행정당국을 보면 적당히 조선인의게도 무슨 국장이니 지사니 과장이니 하는 장관의 직을 許與하고 있음에도 불구하고 사법당국에 있어서만이 조선인의게 재판장이나 혹은 검사정의 직을 주지 않음은 가당치 않은 줄로 안다. 심지어 산간벽지의 사법기관에까지 조선인 장관이란 단 한 사람도 임명치 안음은 何故인가? 총독 각하로부터 심심한 고려가 잇기를 애원하는 바이다.
이러한 요망, 애원에 대하야 총독 각하로부터는 신중한 태도로 깁게 듯는 듯한 감이 잇섯다. 또한 금후 잘 고려할 의사까지도 표해 주엇다.
과거에는 어떠한 요망, 진정이 잇섯다 하드라도 要路의 각 국장이나 과장급의 인물을 맛나 간접적인 의사 표시에 불과하엿든 것이나 今回와 같이 직접적인 기회를 얻게 된 것은 심히 다행으로 녁이는 바이다.
그런데 이상의 면담에 소용된 시간을 따저보면 15분간이였다. 원체 내게 비례된 시간으로 보면 약 8분박게는 안 되는데 15분 갓가히 말하고 보니 다른 사람을 위해서 다소 未安스러운 생각이 드러서 그만 끝을 막어 버리고 말엇다.
그러나 나의 최초의 예정은 한 가지를 더 요망할야는 것이였다.
그것은 고등 법원에 조선인 판사가 한사람도 없는데 이것은 재판의 위신상으로 보던지 또는 재판의 효과로 보더라도 부당한 점이 많다.
그것은 조선 관습을 모르는 內地人 판사는 往往히 조선 관습을 새로히 맨들어 내는 일이 잇는 까닭이다. 이러한 불리를 무릅스면서까지 內地人 판사만을 둠은 그 이유가 奈邊에 잇을가? 총독 각하의 의견을 뭇고 십헛다. 그러나 시간상 관계로 이를 중지하였으며 후일의 기회를 보는 수 박게는 없었다.

亞細亞 靑年의 紏合을 目的하야

東洋靑年聯盟
朱雲成

돌연히 삼천리사의 속달 우편을 받아본 즉 2월 4일 총독 회견기를 써달나는 부탁인대 투고를 명일까지 기한부 명령을<36> 받고 다망한 중에 붓을 들게 되니 순서 없는 글로 좀 써보려 한다.
작년 7월 25일부터 열니던 제71특별의회 방청기를 본지에 쓴 바 잇거니와 그때에 사실인 즉 余가 수년 내의 宿案인 東亞 청년의 대동단결에 의하야 동양 평화의 건설과 아울너 태평양 시대의 서곡을 영창하며 黃人 문명의 부흥을 통감한 바 잇더니 마츰내 支那事變이 이러남을 보고 일층 이 시기인 줄 알고 「東洋靑年聯盟案」을 휴대하고 東京에 갓든 것이다. 가서 원로 중신과 밋 대신들과 정치가들의 찬성을 받어 가지고 8월 중순에 귀향하야 그때에 南總督과 大野 총감과 要路局課長을 방문하였으나 총독은 대단히 분망 중이라 하므로 後 기회에 맛나기로 하고 大野 총감만 회견하고 총감도 東京 갓던 이야기를 서로 교환하엿고 나에게 호의를 표하엿다. 그후에 사무소를 신설하고 소책자도 발행하며 사업 진전에 머리를 알노라고 침묵을 지키고 잇엇다. 그러다가 최근 南總督은 민중의 소리를 듣는 기회를 設하야 매주 금요마다 관저에서 회견케 되엿는 바 近藤 비서관으로부터 나더러 자기 집에 놀러 오라고 누누 부탁이 잇어 민의청취 제2주 금요인 2월 4일에 나도 가기로 하고 등록부에 기명하엿다.

倭城臺 官邸의 斜陽

그날 오후 3시 까지에 도착하여야 하므로 朝鮮銀行 앞까지 전차를 타고 다음은 도보로 本町을 거처 南山町으로 올나갓다. 舊총독부청사 앞을 도라 넓은 「아스팔트」 길을 좀 것노라면 풍경이 絶勝한 수림 속에 웅자를 뵈이는 것이 총독관저이다. 당도하고 보니 余가 제일착이다. 관저 직속 비서의 안내로 넓다란 「모던」 응접실을 들어서니 이 방이 즉 민론이 風發하는 議場인 것이엇다. 장의자, 안락의자가 수십좌가 놓엿고 중앙부에는 사각탁자가 잇고 8인석이 둘러 잇는 4편에 큰 안락의자가 총독좌석이다.
余의 다음에 李弘鍾씨가 드러오고 田中통역관이 드러오고 뒤이어 橘圓壽씨, 波多江씨, 金昇福씨, 李晶爕씨, 宮崎씨, 朴勝彬씨, 孫弘遠씨, 吳龍澤씨, 朴寬俊씨, 趙東植씨, 李覺鍾씨, 朱泰道씨 등 14인이 列席하고 신문 기자단이 사진기를 들고 陸續드러왓다. 近藤 비서관이 명부를 들고 드러서니 북편 문이 열리면서 和顔으로 드러오는 이가 南閣下엿다. 서창으로 직사하는 사양의 볕에 방안은 훈훈함을 느끼게 되자 총독은 「あついでしやう, ぢよつと開けて 置きませう」하면서 북문을 개방하여놓고 착석하엿다. 近藤비서관은 명부를 보면서 도착순으로 면담케 한다고 하며 「朱雲成군」을 부르기에 나는 총독 앞에 인사하고 가까히 안젓다.

總督과 余의 談話<37>

그날 담화 내용까지 여기에 보고할 의무가 없는데 기어코 쓰라니 대략 골자를 述하련다.
나 = 배견을 許하심에 대하야 감사함니다.
총독 = 자-말습하시요.
나 = 제가 작금하고 잇는 것은 청년운동인데 웨 저는 청년에의 관점을 두는 이유는 새삼스러운 말로 금일이야말로 청년시대요 청년의 세계가 아님니까? 각하가 말슴하시는 內鮮一體化도 청년의 우에 건설할 것이고 동양평화도 진정한 東亞청년의 자각 우에다 건설하여야 할 것이 아님니까?
총독 = 그럿치요.
나 = 고로 저는 청년운동을 통감하고 수년 내 拙案을 練하야 동양「이데오로기」를 가진 청년동지를 규합하야 新東洋文化를 부흥시키는 새로운 이상만이 동양평화의 요체로 알고 작년 東京에 가서 각하들께도 소신을 피력하고 「싸인」까지 받었읍니다 하고 案冊을 제출하엿다.
총독 = 하- 시간 관게로 다 읽을 수는 없으나 「싸인」한 여러분들을 보니 모다 조선에 대한 이해가 깊은 이들이 많군요.
나 = 사실은 이 案을 제가 작성하기는 支那事變 전이엿읍니다. 사변이 이러나고 보니 더욱 필요를 느껴스나 시국은 나날로 중대화함으로 본격적 운동은 중지하고 아직 정신운동만으로 지난 번 「팜푸렛트」를 5,000부 발행하였음니다. 금후에 저는 이 운동을 일층 힘잇게 하려 하나 아시는 대로 제게는 열과 이념은 잇으나 힘이 부족함니다. 고로 각하의 정신적 원조를 더욱 절망하고 잇음니다.
총독 = 음 잘 알엇소.
나 = 그리고 각하께서는 특히 신시대적 정치를 하심이 우리 청년국민의 환호하는 바이며 금반 지원병제도의 실시에 반한 청년들의 반향에 鑑할지라도 각하의 혁신정치를 讚賀는 所以라고 봄니다.(이하 생략)
친절한 총독의 응답으로 5분 간 담화를 맛치고 從此도 기회 잇는대로 간청하겟다는 것으로 호의를 뵈임에 余는 물러나왓다. 후좌에 안저 다른 인사들의 교담을 방청하엿고 기자들의 問에도 답하엿다.

朝鮮施政의 新示唆

「依 らしむべく, 知らしむべからず」라는 施政이 4반세기 간의 조선시정의 방침이엿다. 그리해서 조선민중의 언론은 실로 內室公論에 불과하여 왓다. 시운은 진전되고 민정은 일신하야 드디여 위정자들도 官尊民卑의 時代遲的 관념에서 해탈하야 민중정치의 중요성을 의식함에 이르럿다.<38>
그날 여러 인사들의 제의를 보더라도 조선통치사 이래 처음되는 자유호소의 한 페지엿다. 이것은 분명히 南總督의 이채적 시정방침의 한 조각이다. 여론이 없는 사회는 정의를 수립할 수 없고 민의가 반영되지 안는 정치는 공정을 기하기 어려울 것이다. 民聲이 없는 곳에 적막이 오나니 백성의 소리야 말로 淸晨의 鷄鳴聲일지며 知秋의 梧葉聲일 것이다. 이 묘미를 南總督은 깊히 인식함에서 되는 금후 시정의 원활성을 吾人은 기대하야 마지 안는다.

總督의 印象

南總督은 엄격한 이라고 인식하는 이가 퍽 많음을 余는 잘 보았다. 그러나 정말 회견자의 인상은 누구나 人情總督이라는 감상을 얻게 된다. 실상 그는 엄정한 편보다 인자한 편이 더 크다할 것이다. 인정 잇는 그의 눈, 和氣가 浮游하는 얼골, 평범스러운 접객술, 유화한 음성, 풍만한 체구, 모다가 「好い爺さん」임에 틀림없다. 그리고 총명한이다. 상대방의 말머리를 듣고 벌서 중도 응답하야 闡命한다. 그러므로 새삼스러운 진술을 하지 못하게 만든다. 그가 총명하다는 것은 그가 훈시하는 것을 여러번 보았는데 늘 원고를 들지 않고 암송적으로 유창하게 演述함을 보았다. 연설에 잇어서도 역대 총독 중에 가장 어세가 좋다고 사유한다. 이상한 것은 輓近軍人 출신들의 변술이 능함이다. 의회에 가서 국무대신들의 연설을 들엇는데 현내각에도 永井 遞相을 除之하고는 杉山 陸相과 米內 海相의 연설이 近衛 首相, 廣田 外相보다도 퍽 우수하엿다. 米內海相은 웅변이라 할 수 잇엇다. 南總督도 장군이면서도 능변가인 것이다. 그이의 당당한 風釆로 후일 수상의 시정방침연설에의 관록을 지금도 십분 뵈여주고 잇다. 南總督은 또한 평민기질을 가진 이고 부지런한 일군으로 뵈인다. 이렇게 역대에 없던 民衆引見을 하게 됨도 그이의 誠勤의 일면상이며 등청시간을 보더라도 아츰 8시반에 꼭꼭 출근하는 근면총독이라 한다.

進言餘錄
