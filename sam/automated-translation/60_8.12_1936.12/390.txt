**Body Text Translation:**

Do You Wish to Be Young Again? The Mindset of Writers
(a) Among the twenty million people of Chosŏn, approximately what rank do you think you hold as a happy person?
(b) Roughly how many readers do you estimate read your works? Also, by approximately how many do you think this number increases each year?
(c) Don't you ever wish to become young once more and experience passionate love again?

Ch'unwŏn
1. If all the people of Chosŏn were divided into two groups, the "happy category" and the "unhappy category," I know I belong to the happy side. While many suffer from lack of clothing and food, I have never lacked the necessities of life until now, and I currently have a peaceful place to live. How could this not be called happiness?
2. I have never thought about how many people read my works. However, since I took up the pen (뭇) over 20 years ago, I believe there must be some who have sincerely read my works during that time. And I know well from my experience that these readers range from fifteen or sixteen years old to elders in their sixties. I imagine that those who read my first work, "Mujŏng," have now all reached their forties or fifties.
3. My body is not yet 50, so I have never sighed, "Alas, I am old!" (鳴呼老矣).<209> Therefore, I have never longed for "youth," nor have I ever thought about trying to fall in love once again. Of course, I believe I still have passion in my body, so I cannot know what the future holds...

Sangsa
4. (a) I have never thought about happiness, so I do not know. However, happiness is subjective, and human greed is hard to limit, so I think there probably isn't anyone foolish enough to think they are the first, second, or nth happiest person on earth.
(b) I don't know. How about your company [the magazine] compiles statistics?
(c) The hypothetical "if I could become young again" is laughable. Even at 70, I might want to have a passionate love affair. But that's something to boast about after I actually turn 70.

Ansŏ
4. (1) As long as one doesn't end one's life and continues to exist, there might be some self-deception. But even beggars and those with incurable diseases have this.
(2) I don't think there are particularly many [readers]. If there are, they are, like me, beings of a certain curious uselessness.
(3) On top of this, you ask if I want to be young and vibrant again to experience love? Please, don't make me think again of the bitter experiences I've been through, I want to plead with my own illusory memories. Didn't they say even an ox won't step where it was once frightened (魂難)?

Pak Yŏnghŭi
1. Even the last seat would be fine if I could just be a happy person. But how can a person truly possess a happy life?
2. I cannot know the number of readers yet; rather than a large number, even a small number would be good if they are readers who can understand the writer's true intention (지의).
3. If I were to become young again, rather than a passionate love affair, I would want to live a life and pursue work without errors. Love is not only passionate when young; it can be experienced in middle age too, but since such thoughts and times have passed for me, speaking of it is just empty fantasy. <210>

Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi
(a) It seems like maybe no one reads my work, and yet it also feels like all 20 million might read it.
(b) Similarly, it feels like the readership might increase a lot, but there's also the anxiety that not a single person more will be added.
5. If a person's value lies only in youth, then they should never grow old.

Yang Chudong
(a) Some days first, some days last (꼴치).
(b) I don't know (不知道).
(c) Why become young again? I'm still young. I do have a desire to try it just once, but it's not the so-called 'lust' (色). If it's truly that passionate "love," then the partner is the problem. Her face must be the opposite of mine, ultra-modern (초근대형); regarding education and tastes, she must have qualifications quasi-equal (準同等) to mine—generally understanding European texts, Chinese classics, and "especially" our writing deeply; knowing a little bit of Set Sûr [?], Hartmann, the Book of Changes (周易), the Sutra of Perfect Enlightenment (圓覺經), and Hyangga (!); furthermore, she must fully appreciate hobbies like calligraphy and painting (書畵), antiques (骨董) (especially being able to appreciate with me the simple and refined (枯淡) brushwork of the納入 [submitted work?] I treasure (珍藏)), bonsai, janggi, Shepherd [dog?] (쎄파－트) (though I don't actually have one), etc., etc. Isn't this truly a difficult situation?
You ask why there are so many conditions attached to love? Well (若夫), if the object [of affection] were a beauty capable of overthrowing a country (傾國佳人), I would bow down (부복하언이리오) saying the previous words were just a joke (戱耳). Haha (呵呵).

Chŏn Sŏnggil
(a) Shall I say I am the 20 millionth unhappiest person among 20 million?
(b) Judging from occasionally receiving letters from readers, it's certain that my works have readers, but I cannot estimate the number. I myself am my most sincere reader.
(c) As a result of having experienced love, aside from wasting time, material resources, and energy (*費), although one doesn't realize it at the time, looking back after becoming somewhat calm, there is no gain, and it ultimately leads to a sense of disillusionment. I remember it only as a part of human activity, without any special pleasure. If I were to become young again, I know I wouldn't be able to have a "passionate" love affair.

Yi Hŏn'gu
(a) Saying first would be a lie, saying last would invite criticism, so as for that X in "Xth place among 20 million," I'd rather ask someone else.
(b) If there were just one devoted reader who knows me, believes in me, sends me encouragement, rejoices with me <211>, and shares indignation (奮慨) with me, that would be enough (고만일것입니다). However, I have yet to discover a reader who has this kind of relationship with me. Shall I call this my sole regret (遺憾)?
(c) Rather than becoming young once more, even if I were to live for another 30 years, I think I want to always possess progressive and intense feelings of love while aging. Perhaps in this respect, Goethe could be said to have spent his entire life in the joy of youth.

Yi Hyosŏk
4. (a) There might be times I rank first among 20 million, and times I rank dead last.
(b) How can I know? I can only use the circulation numbers of the publications where my works appear as a standard.
(c) It's not that the thought is absent, but I don't think a "passionate" love affair is likely (될상싶지) anymore. Though, it depends on the characters involved, I suppose.

Pang In'gŭn
4. (a) I have rarely thought of myself as a happy person. However, since there must be many unhappier than me, I suppose I might be around the 19 millionth among 20 million.
(b) Calculating the number of readers of my works is utterly difficult. It seems like there could be many, or few. I would be grateful if your company could investigate. Therefore, I also don't know by how many it increases or decreases each year (식).
(c) You ask if I'd want to have a passionate love affair if I became young again? Isn't that obvious even without asking (뭇지않어도 다이를말슴입니까)? Just teach me the method to become young again (가르처 주소서).

Hong Hyomin
Fourth, (a) The question is, "Among the twenty million people of Chosŏn, approximately what rank do you think you hold as a happy person?" To this, I think I would either be first or last. Why (웨냐하면)? Firstly, because it's impossible to intricately determine who is the happiest, who is second happiest, and so on, is one reason, and also because this too resides in one's own mind, being extremely subjective. I have no intention whatsoever of measuring others' happiness. Therefore, I am either first or last ("꼴지").
(b) The question is, "Roughly how many readers do you estimate read your works? Also, by approximately how many do you think this number increases each year?" This is also an estimation, so my only answer, according to my desire, is either the most read or not read at all.
(c) The question is, "Don't you ever wish to become young once more and experience passionate love again?" To this, I think I could always have a passionate love affair. It's not only young people who can have passionate love affairs. Love knows no age, old or young. If one does it, do it passionately; if not, be indifferent, that's all. However (허나), my current state of mind is, whether it works out or not, I have a wife who suits my station, so I have no such thoughts at all.

Kim Hwang-sŏp
4. (a) To others, I might seem relatively happy, but from my perspective, I also see myself as unhappy. I suppose I'm not the most unhappy person. Or perhaps I am.
(b) My readers? I have several dozen close friends (親友), so it must be at least that many ().
(c) I'm still young now, so how can I become younger? Perhaps if I became a child, I don't know. Passionate love? In purely great literary works, it's just like the heroine. One can love, perhaps. In that sense, "Tess" seems to be my eternal lover.

Ham Taehun
1. I have never thought about it that way.
2. I have never thought about how many readers read my works. I simply write a work whenever I have an artistic impulse.
3. Although I am still young, I think I'll stop with love affairs.

Yi Muyŏng
2. There is no way to know that.

Sŏ Kwangje
4. (a) When escaping the heat in summer, drinking beer when thirsty, and especially when meeting that woman I had longed to see, I feel like the happiest person in the world. When the opposite happens, I also feel like the unhappiest person.
(b) I haven't thought about such things, but friends I grew up with in the same neighborhood as a child, schoolmates from elementary, middle, and professional school, and friends I am personally close with would probably read them first.
(c) I am still in my youth, so I don't know what kind of passionate love affair I might have under what circumstances.

Yi Sŏkhun
4. (a) My national happiness ranking is No. 17,500,000.
(b) I'd be happy if it were even around fifty or sixty, but in reality, it seems unlikely to be even that many. Overall, it probably increases each year. By about 10% perhaps.
(c) My age hasn't passed the 30-year mark, so love affairs are really something for now onwards. However, I have no desire to actively pursue them. I believe I can do it until the day I grow old and die.

Yi Il
4. a. I consider myself the 20 millionth happiest person among the 20 million people of Chosŏn.
b. I know for certain that not a single person reads my works.
c. I do not want to perform in a love drama again, nor do I want to become young; I await the finish line like in a marathon.

Ch'ae Mansik
4. (a) Not just among the 20 million people of Chosŏn, I think there is no one in the whole world as unhappy as I am.
(b) The most accurate number of readers is myself, one typesetter (文選職工), one proofreader (校正보는 이), and one censor (檢閱官), totaling four people. Other than that, I cannot know how many there are, or whether the number decreases or increases.
(c) My body is young but my mind is prematurely old, so things like love (愛 따우) are now trivial (시시-함니다).

Yi Pungmyŏng
4. (a) If there are twenty million people in Chosŏn, perhaps I am number 19,999,999? Why is that? Because among my family of six, I am the happiest one.
(b) Alright then, let's solve for my number of readers using mathematics. 20 Million People - Non-readers = Pungmyŏng's Readers.
However (伹), the number of non-readers is top secret.
You ask how many readers increase per year? Well, let's see. I receive letters of encouragement from readers almost daily (每日 한 장식은).
(c) It's still an unknown variable (未知數). It hasn't been many days <214> since I got married out of love, and giving such an answer carelessly feels like it might earn me a scolding (加味 벼락탕), so I put down my pen. Ha!

Han Int'aek
4. a. The most unhappy. b. I cannot know.
c. I am not yet old, so I have never thought about such things. But even if I were old, I don't think I would want to become young just to have a love affair.

Yi Kapki
4. (a) There was a moment when I was intoxicated with the thought that I was the happiest person not only in Chosŏn but in the world, and there were also times when I ground my teeth (니를 부둑부둑 가라 볼 때도), thinking I was the unhappiest fellow in the world...
(b) I don't know.
(c) Occasionally, when I meet a young maiden (處女) somewhere, I sometimes reflect upon myself with an extremely innocent (無邪) feeling. However, when I think that I am already too old compared to that maiden, I sometimes feel a pang of sorrow (一誅의 悲哀), and simultaneously, as a reaction, the thought of "...being young once more" does arise. But Goethe (퀘테) was able to love an 18-year-old maiden even at the age of 70 and even had a son, so is such authority limited only to the Sage of Poetry? Who was Shun (舜何人)?...

Song Yŏng
4. (a) I don't know how others might judge the extent of my happiness, but subjectively, when I think about myself, I consider myself the happiest person not just among 20 million people, but among all people in the world, and because of this, I am living well.
(b) It's absolutely impossible to estimate (推想). And I also don't know by how many it increases each year (名式). In my heart, I wish it would increase day by day and month by month (日增月加), but when I review (檢討) my own creative activities, I think such ambitions are a big delusion (誤詡).
(c) "Becoming young again" is unnecessary for me. What does being old matter? Didn't old man Goethe (꾀테翁) (in his seventies) go around with (단겻슴니까) a 16-year-old girl (二八少女)? I always have the sorrowful (哀切) wish (所望) <215> that I could find just one person in mind, elegant (*雅) and detached, perhaps someone whose voice I hear but whose face I cannot see. In other words, I'd like to try having an Eastern-style love affair (東洋的 戀훼) once.

Yu Wanhŭi
4. (a) Judging from what was said in the three points above, if there comes a time when any of my desires are satisfied, at that moment, I will be the happiest person.
(b) That belongs to the unknown variables that do not permit estimation by me.
(c) Love! I still have the age, so there's no lamenting that it's too late (晩時之歎), but considering my past experiences and all other points, in order to prevent the misfortune of the other party (彼方) choosing me as their object (對象), I now only have the thought (生覺) of wanting to give up.

An Hamgwang
4. (a) (b) I am an existence far removed from happiness, and I have never once tried to estimate (推想) the number of my readers (讀者), but the editors (編輯人諸氏) and the typesetting team (植字班諸氏) are certainly (틀님없이) within the readership circle (讀者圈內), wouldn't you say...
(c) If I carelessly (자칫) answer incorrectly, it seems I might have to become the leader (黨首) of the starvation party (餓死黨) due to my wife's (家妻) cooking (炊事) sabotage (사보타-쥬), and if I were to become young again and try to revive (復活) a failed first love, it seems my wife might demand (强要) I affix my seal (捺印) to a divorce agreement letter (離婚承諾書). Please, Chief Editor (編輯局長), take responsibility for this difficult situation (難處한 責任)!

Min Pyŏnghwi
4. (a) The question is, "Among the twenty million people of Chosŏn, approximately what rank do you think you hold as a happy person?"
A very difficult question.
Perhaps calling myself the 2,000 millionth first [happiest] would make me seem too pitiful, so I shall console myself by saying I possess the 19,999,999th level of happiness.
(b) The question is, "Roughly how many readers do you estimate read your works? Also, by approximately how many do you think this number increases each year?"
- What should I answer? Perhaps the only one who reads my works is myself. And that one person (me) will increase each year.
(c) The question is, "Don't you wish to become young once more and experience passionate love again?"
What strange (괴악스런) mockery (嘲弄)! You've asked an impossible fantasy. I am the only son for three generations, born as the sole male without daughters. I long for like-minded (뜻 맛는) friends, and sometimes entertain the feeble fantasy (열없는 空想) of befriending (사귀였으면) just one like-minded female comrade (女子동*) - but I cannot become young again, nor do I have the thought (새각) of wanting to experience love again if I were young! If anything, perhaps when I turn 80, I might try having one last (마주막) love affair before I die! That's all. <216>

Ŏm Hŭngsŏp (?) [Original has 嚴興*]
(a) While I wouldn't go so far as to call myself "happy," I dislike despairing (自暴自棄) and placing myself in the unhappy contingent when compared to (빛우어) the overall national standard.
(b) Well. How can I state that definitively in numbers?
However, I sometimes (더러) think it would be nice if there were just "ten thousand" (萬名).
(c) Yes, sometimes (각-금).
However, rather than engaging in such uselessly (*부*없이) pink fantasies (粉紅色 空想), going out to the suburbs (郊外) with a close friend (親友), drinking a cup of wine (日盃酒) with loach soup (추湯), getting slightly tipsy (얼근이), exchanging idle chat (雜談), and enjoying (享樂) the day's sunset (夕陽) is a realizable (實現性) pleasure, and I believe (믿음니다) it is also my happiness.

Ch'oe Yŏngsu
4. (a) - Something we both (彼此) want to know. But it's fortunate that I don't know "what number" happiness child I am.
(b) - It's fortunate that I don't know this either.
(c) - I am a young person, so I want to quickly grow old and try having a dependable love affair with an older person once. Where (어듸) can you find a trustworthy young woman (색시) these days? <217>

**Summary**

This 1936 survey published in *Samch'ŏlli* magazine asks prominent Korean writers (文士, munsa) three questions: their perceived rank of happiness among Koreans, their estimated readership size and growth, and whether they wish to be young again for passionate love. The responses vary widely: some, like Yi Kwangsu (Ch'unwŏn), express contentment, while others, like Ch'ae Mansik, claim extreme unhappiness. Estimations of readership range from self-deprecatingly low (Yi Il, Ch'ae Mansik) to hopeful or uncertain. Views on reliving youth and love are diverse; some embrace the idea (Pang In'gŭn), some are cynical or weary (Ansŏ, Chŏn Sŏnggil, Ch'ae Mansik), some feel they are still young or find love possible at any age (Yang Chudong, Yi Hŏn'gu, Hong Hyomin, Song Yŏng), and others prioritize different life goals (Pak Yŏnghŭi).

From a historical perspective, this article offers valuable insights into the self-perception and mentality of Korean intellectuals during the mid-colonial period. Their reflections on happiness reveal personal struggles and contentment levels amidst the broader context of colonial subjugation. Discussions about readership hint at the challenges and uncertainties of literary careers at the time, including concerns about censorship (mentioned by Ch'ae Mansik). The diverse views on youth and love provide a glimpse into personal values, disillusionment, romantic ideals (sometimes referencing Western figures like Goethe or literary characters like Tess), and traditional constraints (like An Hamgwang's humorous fear of his wife's reaction). Historians can find rich material here on the inner lives, professional anxieties, and cultural attitudes of Korean writers navigating modernity under colonial rule.
