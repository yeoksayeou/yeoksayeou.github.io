The Revival of Liberalism
						Hong Yangmyŏng (洪陽明)

Recently, the term liberalism has been quite appealingly bandied about (膾灸) among a segment of the intellectual class. It is not without a sense of strangeness that the ideology of the citizen class, which arose in opposition to the feudal system after the 18th century, is being re-evaluated so highly today when capitalism is reaching a saturation point and class conflict has intensified. However, this is entirely a kind of reactive attitude of life among some intellectuals, prompted by various recent illegitimate social conditions. Yet, it neither holds a strong initiative regarding the progression of modern society nor is it a decisive factor that could motivate the transcendence (止揚) of the current state where the direction of thought seems compelled by some unknown influence or social situation. Despite this, there has perhaps never been a time when attachment to liberal tendencies has permeated (慘透) the intellectual class as broadly as today. Various events that have occurred within Japan proper in recent years certainly exemplify this. The intense interest of the intelligentsia in the Takigawa Professorship Incident (瀧川敎授事件), which has occurred successively since the year before last, and the Minobe Theory (美濃部學說) issue, the embers (灰燼) of which are still being debated as right or wrong (是耶非耶), putting aside all political events proceeding around the parliament and political parties and various political noises related to emergency warnings at home and abroad, was the sharpest (尖銳). This is sufficiently illustrated by recalling that the large-scale student strike related to the former Takigawa Incident, and later the parliamentary questions and answers concerning the Minobe Theory issue, were reported with greater prominence than any other article even in the major daily newspapers of Japan proper, which had become colorblind, and its repercussions are still being used as material for overthrowing the cabinet by the Seiyūkai (政友會), Japan's largest political party, under the guise of the issue of clarifying the national polity (國體明徵). Both incidents concluded with the defeat <74> of liberalism, leading some to view Japan's liberal tendencies as completely collapsed (顚落) because of this. However, one cannot deny that there are also those who, despite the weakness and fragile defeat due to the vacillation of liberals, point out the historical significance of how it implicitly evoked deep-seated (鬱然) sympathy for liberal tendencies and created an opportunity for re-examination aimed at the true substance (實身) of liberalism, thus evaluating it highly.

The psychological state in which a part of the intellectual class is recently re-evaluating liberalism has, in short, manifested as an antithetical (對蹠的) consciousness towards the fascist current, and of course, its class basis is extremely weak.
Liberalism, as is well known, was the ideological weapon of the bourgeoisie, which emerged based on free competition in industry and rationalism in ideology as its stronghold (土豪), targeting the feudal system, the absolute form of rule. However, when the proletariat came to assert its existence as a newly rising class in modern times, the bourgeoisie either colluded with parts of the feudal forces (封建勢力) and transformed (轉化) towards economic monopoly aimed at overcoming the economic crisis, thus leading to political absolutism, or after establishing the actual political absolute supremacy of the bourgeoisie through its financial power, bourgeois liberalism effectively died out. The former can be represented by the fascist politics practiced in Italy and Germany, and the latter by the constitutional or democratic states such as Britain, America, and France. Consequently, liberalism, detesting (嫌嫌) fascist absolutism, has shifted its locus to the intellectual class, whose function is, to the last, free criticism of social phenomena and rationalistic speculation, and also to a segment of the bourgeoisie opposed to monopolization. Furthermore, in essence, it has also changed its nature and altered its affiliation (所籍) to the class camp that believes the establishment of comprehensive (總體的) liberalism is possible through the transcendence (止揚) of the bourgeoisie. It should be the logical sequence for the segment of the bourgeoisie that believes parliamentarism is the political expression of liberalism to join forces with the liberal intellectual class and contend (抗争) against the absolutist fascist rule. However, in Japan proper currently, the Seiyūkai, the party with an absolute majority in parliament that should be carrying the banner of parliamentarism, is instead enacting a deformed (기형적) state, attempting to fight against liberalism by colluding with forces <75> that, at the present moment, possess a nature close to absolute. It is an even more subtle (미세한) situation that in Japan, what currently serves as a major brake (制動機) against fascist power is paradoxically a part of the feudal forces, such as the elder statesmen and senior vassals (西園寺公, 一木樞相 등), a series of figures.
Modern liberalism, in its origin, is an ideology that started based on the spirit of the famous Declaration of the Rights of Man, resolved by the National Assembly during the French Revolution of 1789 – "Men are born and remain free and equal in rights. The aim of all political association is the preservation of the natural and imprescriptible rights of man" (天が 부여하고 時効に 不關하는 사람의 모든 권리의 保持) – and the economic theory of the Physiocrats who advocated "free activity for individual interests" (개인적 이익을위한 자유로운 활약). That it was based on the social philosophy of utilitarianism by Bentham (벤담) and his school reflected the consciousness of the emerging capitalism of the time. However, although the ideal of liberalism has changed its content variously thereafter, in common terms, its main framework has not significantly differed: politically, the unhindered extension (伸長) of people's rights such as freedom of speech, publication, and assembly, and economically, free competition and free trade in industry as the basis for national prosperity. It should be called rather an attitude toward life or a direction of the mind in terms of ideals (理念士), rather than something completed as a system of thought. Therefore, in the current situation where expression is extremely suppressed and some influence from one side prevails obscurely, making it difficult to find one's bearings, the mood of a segment of the intellectual class longing (등憬) for the sweetness of liberalism is a superficial (踈薄) yet circumstantial mental tendency. Furthermore, it cannot be denied that it manifests antithetically to an absolutely uncritical state and carries a considerable degree of sentimental (傷感的) passivity. <76>

**Summary**

The article discusses the renewed interest in liberalism among some Japanese intellectuals in the mid-1930s. The author, Hong Yangmyŏng, notes this trend is a reaction against "illegitimate social conditions" and "fascist currents," likely referring to the growing militarism and suppression of dissent in Japan. Despite recent setbacks for liberalism, such as the Takigawa Incident and the Minobe Theory controversy, which resulted in defeats for liberal viewpoints, the author suggests these events paradoxically sparked sympathy and re-evaluation of liberal ideals. Hong observes that traditional bourgeois liberalism is largely defunct, squeezed between fascist absolutism and the rise of the proletariat, finding refuge among intellectuals and anti-monopoly segments of the bourgeoisie. He points out the irony that in Japan, the ostensibly pro-parliament Seiyūkai party collaborates with near-absolutist forces, while elements of the old "feudal" establishment (like Saionji and Ichiki) act as brakes on fascism. The author characterizes this resurgent interest in liberalism as somewhat superficial and sentimental, a longing for freedom of thought in an oppressive atmosphere, rather than a powerful force for change. For historians of colonial Korea, this article is interesting as it reflects contemporary intellectual discourse *within* the Japanese empire on the pressures of rising authoritarianism, written by a Korean author for a Korean audience. It shows how events in Japan proper, like the suppression of academic freedom (Takigawa, Minobe), resonated with Korean intellectuals facing similar, if not harsher, restrictions under colonial rule, framing the debate around the fate of liberalism against encroaching absolutism/fascism.
