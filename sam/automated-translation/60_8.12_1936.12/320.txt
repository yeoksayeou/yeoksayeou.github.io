Mind's Moon
						Yi Sŏ-gu

1. Vermilion Lips and Appetite

A moon in the sky,
A lover in the heart,
Trusting the moon, I walk the night path,
Trusting the stars, shall I cross the pass?

The sound of the song heard on street after street, from record shops, from cafes, from the mouths of children—Great Kyŏngsŏng seemed to live just to sing "Mind's Moon" day after day. Within a few weeks of its release, "Mind's Moon" had completely captured the hearts of the public. It crushed the new releases from minor record companies and proudly reigned supreme. Simultaneously, Munho, the lyricist of this song, managed to acquire money, spring, and love. He had refused the 10 wŏn offered as a lyricist fee, insisting on receiving a royalty of 2 chŏn per record sold. Since "Mind's Moon" was the top prize-winning work from a new poetry contest co-sponsored by a newspaper company and the broadcasting station, and was set to music and even broadcast by DK from the beginning, the record company blindly agreed to this demand. Thanks to this, by the 25th of this month, Munho had received over 200 wŏn. At the same time, the singer who sang "Mind's Moon," the songstress "Miyŏng," sent him loving glances, and God bestowed the blessing of a blossoming spring upon this fortunate young man blessed with money and love.

Spring had come.
Money had appeared.
Love was present.
His name was growing famous.

The joy of Munho, a passionate poet of 26 with a sturdy physique and cheerful disposition, was now exploding like a volcano. He envied neither Napoleon, who conquered the lands of Europe, nor Emperor Qin Shi Huang, who commanded three thousand court ladies—they seemed like mere rice cakes to him. Miyŏng was a flower-like young woman of 20 this year. Her lips were as thin and red as those of a baby. Whenever Munho saw these lips of Miyŏng, he invariably felt an appetite. He even dreamt of publishing an essay titled "A Beautiful Woman's Lips and Appetite" to great acclaim. It was true that after <170> having this dream, he spent several days at the library researching literature concerning women's lips. Miyŏng always smiled with her eyes, cheerfully. At first, Munho believed this eye-smile was directed only at him. Gradually, through careful observation, he realized she gave the same smile to the head of the literary department at the record company, to the composer, and even to the cafe boy when he brought black tea; Munho's heart grew gloomy.

Mi: "Teacher, why are you drooping like that?"
Mun: "Hm. Must be laundry that hasn't been wrung out."
Mi: "Shall I wring it out tightly for you?"
Mun: "Never mind."
Mi: "Someone is doing it, though."
Mun: "Don't worry about me, just go to an eye doctor."
Mi: "Why?"
Mun: "Get your eyes fixed."
Mi: "What's wrong with my eyes?"
Mun: "Because you eye-smile at just anyone."
Mi: "Isn't there a saying? 男は度胸 女は愛嬌! (Men need courage, women need charm!)"
Mun: "Charm needs to be rare to have value. Is it any good if it's as common as gold rings sold at a night market?"
Mi: "Ah— so you're saying my eyes are only fit for a night market?"
Mun: "Then smile a little less."
Mi: "I should smile more. I should smile disgustingly more." At that moment, a Chinese man carrying a vegetable basket passed by. Miyŏng had been on a performance tour to Manchukuo and knew a few words of Chinese.
"Ni nabian qu ya?" (Where are you going?)
— she meant. The Chinese vendor, seemingly confused by Miyŏng's eye-smile, which was like a fiery arrow, mumbled something indistinctly while shuffling his feet, but then beat a hasty retreat upon seeing the fierce expression of Munho standing behind her.
Mi: "See? Charm is the best for international friendship too, you know."
She winked her left eye. That cute expression—this was indeed a new discovery for Munho. Even Clara Bow's (구라라보) wink in motion pictures wasn't as coy as this. There was the elegance of a single flower petal fluttering down briefly from the sky. If Clara Bow's wink was a red petal, Miyŏng's wink just now, slightly crinkling against the background of her shapely nose, dark eyebrows, and rosy cheeks, was a pear blossom or a plum blossom.
Mun: "Ah— that one's good."
Munho's lunging fingers seemed about to poke Miyŏng's <171> eye.
Mi: "Oh my goodness, why are you pouncing like that?"
Mun: "That eye just now, do it one more time."
Mi: "Ho... this...?
She winked slightly again.
Mun: "Oh— that, that, that... that one is mine, right?"
Mi: "Come on— let's go to the eye doctor quickly. Let's go."
Mun: "Never mind. I said never mind."
Mi: "No. Since you suggested going, you have to take me. We should sew up your favorite left eye first."
Mun: "Stop, stop, you might as well sew up my own eyes."
Mi: "Hmph, treating a person like a clown, swinging them around." Miyŏng pouted her lips sulkily. The moment he saw those lips, Munho felt his appetite again. When things reached this point, Munho completely became Miyŏng's slave.

2. The Key of Love

"Miyŏng-ah, why, after enduring for three years without a word, are you now saying you don't want the engagement?" Miyŏng's mother held her long pipe askew, drool dripping, her face flushing and paling.
"Mother, you're drooling."
Miyŏng only gave an unrelated answer.
Mother: "Really now, you don't forget to worry about your mother drooling, but you forget the engagement that's been firmly set like sticky rice cake for three years?"
Mi: "It's a change of heart (心境)."
Mother: "A change in your nerves (神經)? What doctor told you such nonsense?"
Mi: "Why a doctor? He's a poet. A handsome man. My Mr. Munho, who loves me the most, said so."
Mother: "Oh, how shocking. How did my girl become like this? Shamelessly blathering on. You don't even seem like my daughter."
The mother wiped her tears. She wiped her tears with the ties of the jacket she wore when making red pepper paste, and the pepper stung her eyes, making the tears flow more than expected.
Mother: "You wench, can't you change your mind even when your mother cries like this?"
Mi: "If you don't want to cry, wash your eyes with cold water. Don't rub your eyes with the jacket ties caked with red pepper powder."
Mother: "That girl, truly, only cunning and quick-wittedness remain."
Mi: "Ho... Whose daughter would not be so?"
Mother: "Miyŏng-ah, anyway, think it over one more time. A poet is fine, a handsome man is acceptable, and loving to death might not be harmful, but how can you abandon your fiancé who even paid for your school expenses with any face? If you do that, <172> I will not consider you my child, so know that."
Mi: "Then whose child will I become? Is there more than one mother in the world? A father might be mistaken, but how can a mother be substituted?"
"Thwack..."
This sound was the mother's pipe striking Miyŏng's shoulder blade as her anger reached the end of the pipe.
"Ouch!"
Of course, this was Miyŏng feigning pain. Two parts pain, eight parts exaggerated high-pitched screech. Miyŏng went straight from this to Munho's apartment. This was the first time she had gone to find Munho in the middle of the night. Perhaps after watching a motion picture,
"Won't you have a cup of tea I brewed?"
Even if Munho took her wrist,
"Let's go to Meiji Confectionery (명치제과) instead."
Miyŏng had consistently avoided it. Her policy was to diligently avoid dangerous opportunities before any talk reached a complete conclusion. Therefore, recently, Munho himself had decidedly stopped his efforts to take Miyŏng to his apartment at night. It was under these circumstances that Miyŏng suddenly rushed over. Did she fall from the sky? Did she sprout from the ground? He expected her to rush in, beaming, with open arms like someone starved for three generations, unleashing a deluge of kisses and shouts of joy—but though he called and called, no answer was heard.
"Forget it. I ran here relying only on you, but you too are like this."
If no one had been watching, she might have sat down and cried. However, the kind young wife of the apartment manager was breastfeeding her baby and ran out to watch.
"Mr. Yi went out a while ago and hasn't returned yet."
She had called for someone who wasn't there, so naturally, there was no answer. If an answer had been heard, it would clearly have been the mischief of a goblin (독갑이).
It was fortunate there was no answer.
It became clear that neither a person nor a goblin was in Munho's room in this apartment.
Mi: "Um, I am Mr. Yi's student, and he told me to be sure to come tonight, so I came."
Miyŏng's characteristic eye-smile, one instance—
"Is that so? What to do, he isn't here."
The room of the absent owner, the room of the person she loved, the base of his life was now empty. Now was the time to discover the true nature of his private life.
Mi: "Um, anyway, Teacher said he might be out, <173> and if so, to ask you to open the door so I could sit inside and wait."
It was a request to open the door quickly. Perhaps judging that she didn't look like someone who would steal things, the woman brought the room key while still holding the baby. Miyŏng took the key and began searching for the key of love among the twenty or so keys.
"Um— it's number 18."
She found the key with the number (18) engraved on its head and opened the door.
"Number 18. That's a really good number, isn't it?"
Miyŏng smiled with her eyes again as if something delightful had happened.
"Teacher insisted and got this room."
The baby's mother peered in from the hallway with curious eyes. Inside the room, the carnations Miyŏng had bought and placed were gone, and peach branches were stuck in abundance instead.
"Well— I'll wait here then."
Saying this, Miyŏng shut the door with a click. Outside the door, the sound of a baby crying—perhaps startled by the closing door. There was no need to look around at the desk, wardrobe, or bed. What Miyŏng wanted to find were letters or photos from women, or something like Munho's own writings about himself. First, she thoroughly rummaged through the desk drawers. The bottom drawer was locked. But fortunately, the drawer right above it was open. When she took out the upper drawer, she could reach her hand into the drawer below. It was full of envelopes. Taking them out, she saw they were all letters from women. Fire seemed to flare in Miyŏng's eyes. How could those lovely eyes become so fierce depending on the circumstances? It was astonishing. The hand holding the letter envelopes trembled slightly. Her lips, ripe like cherries, turned pale blue. Even Munho, with his vigorous appetite, would not dare to eat these lips. She began examining the contents, one letter, two letters. Fortunately, most were dreamlike messages from literary girls thrilled by Munho's fiction and poetry. Among them, one woman's letter described her feelings from when they had tea together, and another woman's letter described her feelings from the day they walked to Ch'ŏngnyangni. Miyŏng began to harbor a suspicion she had never considered until today.
"Isn't Munho playing with two or three women besides me? In this situation where this frivolous man is at the center of popularity, and young women swarm around him like bees to a honey pot, <174> can I really make Munho entirely my own lover?"
Just as jealousy and doubt were surging in her heart, Munho came in. Miyŏng hid behind the curtain draped beside the bed. Munho came in quite drunk. Whistling, he took off his clothes, went to the gramophone, and put on "Mind's Moon." Following that, he put on "Springless Youth," then sat down at the desk and lit a cigarette.
"Ah— Miyŏng-ah, you cruel thing. Because of you— to protect only you, I am fighting against the temptations of all women, sweating— what are you doing right about now? Huh?"
His pathetic tone had the realism of a famous actor reciting lines on stage. Miyŏng, holding her breath behind him, was so moved that she dashed out distractedly.
"Teacher..."
She threw herself onto Munho's lap.
"Ah!"
This was the final sound Munho uttered as he fell backward, startled, mistaking Miyŏng for a goblin. Of course, Miyŏng probably didn't know that Munho had heard the report from the baby's mother that Miyŏng was waiting when he entered the room and had come in feigning ignorance.

3. Love's Test Tube

Cough! Cough! Cough!
Haggard cheeks, lips devoid of healthy color, lifeless eyes, a cough that seemed to tear at the chest—undoubtedly a tuberculosis patient.
Cough! Cough! Cough!
This was Miyŏng's coughing sound.
"Your voice is too loud."
Oksŏn, sitting as the test administrator, laughed.
Cough! Cough! Cough!
Miyŏng coughed again, lowering her voice.
"The cough sounds more or less okay, but your eyes need to look more sunken."
This was a rather unreasonable demand. However, since Miyŏng was eagerly receiving instruction, there was no objection.
"Then shall I starve for a few days?"
A grim proposal. At these words, even Oksŏn, who had been responding only with laughter, straightened her expression.
"Do you really want to know Mr. Munho's true feelings that badly?"
When Miyŏng first came and talked about Munho, Oksŏn, knowing Miyŏng liked to joke, had responded nonchalantly and jokingly. But now that Miyŏng expressed a desire to know her lover's true feelings even if it meant starving for several days, it had to be seen as a serious life matter. Oksŏn was Miyŏng's <175> cousin, one year older, currently making a name for herself as an exclusive actress at the Dongseong Theater (동성극장). Miyŏng, at her wit's end, had gone to Oksŏn to explain the situation. Oksŏn casually suggested that the quickest way was to pretend she had contracted tuberculosis and observe the man's reaction. Miyŏng's ears perked up, and she pleaded with Oksŏn to teach her the expressions needed to mimic a TB patient. Oksŏn reluctantly began teaching, ultimately concluding that the only way to achieve the desired haggard look with sunken eyes was to starve for a few days.
"But he'll want to get a doctor's diagnosis, so we need to bribe a doctor."
Oksŏn was as meticulous in her caution as she was cool-headed.
"Well, who is there for a doctor?"
Miyŏng was worried. Oksŏn, feeling sorry for her, said,
"Don't worry. Tomorrow morning on my way to the theater, I'll ask Mr. Pak, the chief technician in the X-ray room at Samsung Clinic (삼성의원), whom I know well. I'll make a heartfelt request, so just go to Samsung Clinic in 2 or 3 days."
Miyŏng went back delighted. Without washing her face or eating, Miyŏng suffered intensely for three days, finally managing to acquire the appearance of a sick person. Her unsuspecting mother, thinking Miyŏng was distressed over the marriage issue, just stared blankly with a troubled heart. On the morning of the third day, Oksŏn came to visit.
"Sister, will this be enough to fool him?"
The face she presented was quite wretched.
"Yes, that should do it."
Oksŏn, being sentimental, almost looked like she would cry.
Mi: "Then please make a phone call for me."
Ok: "Where is he?"
Mi: "Rakyang Apartment (락양 아파―트), room 18."
Ok: "Shall I ask him to come here?"
Mi: "Yes."
On her way out, Oksŏn sought out Munho and informed him of Miyŏng's illness. Munho rushed over, and upon hearing she was sick and seeing her complexion, there was no room for doubt.
"Anyway, let's get an X-ray (렌도겐)."
Munho practically pushed Miyŏng into a car.
Mun: "Should we go to the University Hospital?"
He was saying something potentially disastrous. Anywhere other than Samsung Clinic would not diagnose Miyŏng as a TB patient.
Mi: "No. Big hospitals are unfriendly."
Mun: "Then where is good?"
Mi: "Let's go to Samsung Clinic. Firstly, it's close." Entering Samsung Clinic, Miyŏng was <176> immediately pulled into the X-ray room. Munho, like the lover of a condemned prisoner awaiting sentencing while barred from the courtroom, paced anxiously outside the X-ray room. Inside the X-ray room:
Mi: "Mr. Pak, I'm so sorry to make such an unreasonable request."
Pak: "My lungs are perfectly fine, so I can hardly bring myself to say they're rotten."
Mi: "That's why it's a special request, isn't it?"
Miyŏng used all the charm she possessed in her life to persuade Mr. Pak. Mr. Pak, excited by the scent of Miyŏng's charm filling the sealed darkroom, resolved to tell the lie unbecoming of a doctor. Presently, the door opened; Miyŏng emerged coughing, and Mr. Pak followed with his head bowed.
"How is it? Yes! How is it?"
Munho asked impatiently.
"Yes... I'm sorry. The left lung is completely... completely— rotten."
The words ended. Miyŏng, stunned, gently kicked Mr. Pak's shin. Mr. Pak, as if jolted back to reality by the kick,
"Well—well, since the tuberculosis bacteria are spreading vigorously, it's best if family and close friends avoid contact as much as possible."
Munho, as if transporting a living corpse, carried Miyŏng out of the clinic, shedding tears.

4. Love and Life

This night deepened as quietly as the one before. Mr. Pak, buried in his blankets!
"Ah! Ah! I did wrong. Forgive me! The culprit who drove you to suicide is me."
Clutching his chest, tormented by a nightmare, his virtuous wife shook him awake.
"Honey, honey, why are you like this in your sleep? Wake up."
Awakened from the dream, Mr. Pak recalled the scene of Munho's suicide he experienced in his sleep. Because he had said Miyŏng had TB, Munho treated her coldly, Miyŏng promptly married the man she was previously engaged to, and Munho, cast out from the test tube, lost heart and hanged himself from a beam on the hospital operating table. As he was apologizing, holding up Munho's feet, he was shaken awake. Cold sweat streamed down his back, and his whole body felt bruised and battered.
It's not too late even now. I must go find Munho and explain the situation.
The pangs of conscience—the responsibility of a doctor—the tragedy that might arise from the false diagnosis—he could bear it no longer.
"Honey, my outdoor clothes! Clothes!"
He wandered around the room like a madman. His wife, feeling as if some emergency had occurred, <177> trembled as she brought out his clothes.
A car!
At 2 o'clock in the morning, a car was not easily found. Mr. Pak overturned his bookshelf, found Munho's poetry collection, and looked up his address. Holding the poetry book, he ran all the way to the end of Samch'ŏn-dong. Spring rain, a spring rain hastening the fall of blossoms, fell softly. He ran holding the umbrella his wife had given him, without even opening it. Of course, the apartment door was firmly locked. He pounded on the door as if to break it. An old man over fifty, the apartment caretaker, came out. Who are you looking for? Everyone's asleep, his tone was blunt, just short of saying it was bothersome.
Pak: "Uh, uh, is Mr. Yi Mun-ho here?"
Caretaker: "Yes... he is."
Pak: "Which room is it?"
Caretaker: "It's number 18, but what business do you have at this hour?"
Pak: "Yes, yes, there's something urgent I need to tell him."
Mr. Pak bounded up to the second floor like an air ball.
Thump, thump, thump.
Knocking, each moment feeling like three autumns. Suicide! Fight! Escape! A punch to the cheek! Oh well, it's okay to be hit on the cheek. It's natural to receive punishment for the sins one has committed.
Thump! Thump! Thump!
The sound of a key turning in the lock. Munho, wearing nightclothes, opened the door halfway and looked out.
Pak: "Mr. Yi, I have committed a great sin against you."
He expected a fist to fly towards his cheek at any moment, but unexpectedly, Munho just grinned.
Pak: "Are you so stunned that you've lost your mind? Yes, yes?"
Mr. Pak looked like he was about to cry.
Mun: "Please rest assured. I was planning to visit you tomorrow morning anyway to offer my thanks."
It grew increasingly strange. Offering thanks to the doctor who told a lie and sowed the seeds of misfortune—an unthinkable thing.
Pak: "Mr. Yi, please collect yourself. I am Pak Ch'unghyŏn, the wicked doctor who lied to you."
Mun: "Yes, I know well."
He laughed again. Mr. Pak, having no other choice, grabbed Yi Mun-ho's wrist and forcefully took his pulse. He didn't seem like a healthy person. Just then, a white, delicate hand emerged from behind Munho, blocked Mr. Pak's hand, and took Munho's hand instead.
Mi: "Teacher, please rest assured."
It was clearly Miyŏng's voice. Indeed, Miyŏng was hiding behind Munho, wearing nightclothes.
Pak: "Wha— Ms. Miyŏng?" This time, it was Mr. Pak's turn to be examined.
Mun: "Yes. Thanks to the lie you told us, Teacher, <178> Miyŏng was able to quickly learn my true feelings. Thank you very much."
Mi: "Ah— even after hearing that my lungs were completely— rotten, you would still give me a kiss. Is that all? When I had TB, you struggled, saying let's quickly share the illness and die. So I had no choice but to confess. It's all thanks to you, Teacher."
Miyŏng still smiled with her eyes. Mr. Pak felt dizzy.
"Ah—"
He cried out and ran outside. Laughter was heard from the room, then suddenly, even the sound of breathing ceased abruptly. It seemed Munho had felt an appetite for Miyŏng's lips again. <179>

**Summary**

The story follows Munho, a young poet whose song "Mind's Moon" becomes a massive hit, bringing him fame and the affection of the singer, Miyŏng. Jealous and possessive, Munho struggles with Miyŏng's naturally charming demeanor. Facing pressure from her mother about a prior engagement, Miyŏng decides to test Munho's love by faking terminal tuberculosis with the help of her actress cousin and a bribed doctor. When Munho reacts by wanting to share her fate and die with her, Miyŏng reveals the truth. The guilt-ridden doctor confesses later, only to find the couple happy, grateful that the deception proved the depth of Munho's love.

From a historical perspective, the story offers glimpses into the modernizing urban culture of 1930s colonial Seoul (Keijō), showcasing the rise of popular music, record companies, radio, cafes, and the entertainment industry (actresses, singers). It reflects contemporary social issues like the tension between arranged marriages and love matches, modern dating anxieties, and the adoption of Western cultural elements (Clara Bow, Napoleon, X-rays) alongside Japanese influences (Meiji Confectionery) and awareness of the regional political situation (Manchukuo).

						李瑞求
1. 朱唇과 食慾
하늘에 달이하나
가슴에 님이하나
달믿고 밤길가지
별믿고 재넘을가
거리거리 들리는 노래소리 레코―드 가가에서 카페에서 아해들의 입에서 대경성은 맛치 자고 새면 마음의 달을 노래하기 위하야 사는 듯도 하였다. 「마음의 달」은 발매 수주일에 완전히 대중의 마음을 사로잡았다. 군소 레코―드회사의 신작품을 까라누르고 당당히 왕좌에 군림하고 마렀다. 이와 동시에 이 노래의 작시자 문호는 돈과 봄과 사랑을 획득하고 마랐다. 작시료 10원 주는 것을 그는 거절하고 한 장 팔리는데 2전식 인세를 달나고 버틔었다. 이 마음의 달은 모신문사에서 방송국과 협동하야 현상모집한 신시 중의 수석 당선된 작품으로 처음부터 곡조를 부처서 DK에서 방송까지 하게 된 작품이라 레코―드회사에서는 눈을 감고 이 요구에 응하고 마렀다. 덕택에 문호에게는 이 달 25일까지의 게산으로 200여 원의 돈이 드러왔다. 동시에 「마음의 달」을 노래한 가희 「미영」이가 사랑의 추파를 보내오고 동시에 하느님은 이 돈과 사랑의 축복을 받은 행운아를 위하야 꽃피는 봄을 보내 주시었다.
봄이 왔다
돈이 생겻다
사랑이 있다
일홈이 높아간다
26세의 튼튼한 육체와 명랑한 성격을 가진 열정시인 문호의 기뿜은 지금 분화같이 폭발되고 있다. 구라파 천지를 정복한 나포레온도 부럽지 않고 삼천궁녀를 거나리든 진시황도 개떡 갔다. 미영이는 금년 20세의 꽃같은 아가씨 입살이 마치 어린 애기의 그것갓치 얄고 붉었다. 문호는 미영의 이 입살만 보면 반다시 식욕을 느낀다. 그는 미인의 입살과 식욕이라는 글을 발표하야 절찬을 받은 꿈까지 꾸었다. 그가＜170＞ 이 꿈을 꾸고 난 뒤 도서관에 가서 몇 일식 두고 녀자의 입살에 관한 문헌을 조사한 것만은 사실이다. 미영이는 언제든지 생글생글 눈으로 웃는다. 처음에 문호는 자긔만에게만 대한 눈우슴이라 믿었다. 차차 세밀한 조사에 의한 결과 레코―드회사 문예부장에게도 그같은 우슴을 주고 작곡가에게도 그같은 우슴을 주며 심지어 끽다점 뽀―이가 홍차를 가저올 때에까지 가튼 우슴을 주는 줄 알 때 문호의 가슴은 우울하야젓다.
美 「선생님, 왜 그렇게 축 느러지섯소.」
文 「응. 물짜지 안은 세탁물인게지」
美 「내가 빠짝 짜들일가요.」
文 「고만 두어요.」
美 「누가 하기는 하는데」
文 「내 걱정말고 안과 병원에나 가지.」
美 「왜」
文 「눈 좀 곧처요.」
美 「내 눈이 엇대서」
文 「아모나 보고 눈우슴을 치니가 그렇지」
美 「왜 그런 말 있지 않아요. 男は度胸 女は愛嬌!」
文 「애교도 귀해야 값이 나가지 야시에서 파는 금반지 모양으로 너머 흔해서야 쓰나」
美 「아― 그럼 내 눈이 야시장에나 나갈 눈이란 말슴이요.」
文 「그럼 좀 덜 웃지」
美 「좀 더 우슬 걸 내 지긋지긋이 더 좀 우슬 걸」 이 때 마츰 중국사람이 야채광우리를 메  고 지나갔다. 미영이는 만주국으로 연주려행을 같다 온 뒤이라 몃마듸 중국어를 안다.
「늬 나변 취야」
너 어듸로 가느냐―이렀다. 중국행상은 불길을 시른 화살같은 미영의 눈우슴에 정신이 혼란된 듯이 비슬비쓸하며 무엇이라고 중얼중얼 대답을 하다가 등 뒤에 버틔고 슨 문호의 험악한 표정에 꽁문이를 빼고 마렀다.
美 「엇대요. 국제친선에도 애교가 제일이라나요.」
하며 외인편 눈을 찌끗하였다. 고 귀여운 표정 이것이야말노 문호에게는 새로운 발견이었다. 활농사진에 보「구라라보」의 「윙크」도 이갓치 새침스럽지는 못하였다. 하늘에 꽃닢 하나가 잠간 나붓겨 떠러지는 운치가 있었다. 크라라보의 윙크가 붉은 꽃닢이라면 지금의 미영의 옷득한 코 검은 눈섭 볼그레한 두 뻄을 배경으로 살작 짜긋하는 윙크는 배꽃이나 매화꽃이었다.
文 「자― 고놈 좋다.」
덤벼드는 문호의 손꼬락은 미영의＜171＞ 눈을 찌삼 듯도 하였다.
美 「아이고머니나 왜 이리 덤벼요.」
文 「지금 그 눈 그것 한번 더」
美 「호... 요것...」
또 살작 찡겻다.
文 「오― 그것 그것 그것...만은 내 것이지.」
美 「자― 어서 안과로 갑시다. 어서 가요.」
文 「고만 두어요 고만 두재도」
美 「싫여요. 가자고 하신 이상 다리고 가시지요. 당신 좋아하시는 왼편 눈부터 꾀매 버려야  지」
文 「아서 아서 그것은 숫제 내 눈을 꾀매지」
美 「흥 사람을 가지고 광대로 휘두르드라」 미영은 실눅해가지고 입살을 쭝긋하였다. 이 입살을 본 순간 문호는 또 식욕을 느꼇다. 이렇케만 되면 문호는 완전히 미영의 노예가 되고 만다.

2. 사랑의 열쇠
「미영아 엇저자고 상년째 두고 참닷것 아모 말 없든  약혼을 지금 와서 실타느냐」 미영의 모친은 긴 장죽을 빗두로 물고 침을 흘려 가며 얼골이 푸르락 누르락했다.
「어머니 침이 흘러요.」
미영이는 딴 대답만 했다.
母 「글세, 어미 침을 흘리는 걱정은 안 이저바리고 삼년재 찰떡같이 매저놓은 약혼은 이저바  리느냐.」
美 「심경(心境)의 변화지요.」
母 「신경(神經)에 변화가 생겻서 어떤 의사가 그따위 수작을 하듸」
美 「의사는 왜 의사이야요. 시인이요. 호남아이요. 나를 가장 사랑하는 우리 문호씨가 그러  시었어요.」
母 「아이고 망측해라. 계집애가 엇저면 저모양이 되였나. 뻔뻔스럽게 이죽어릴가. 내 딸같지  도 않다.」
어머니는 눈물을 씨섯다. 고초장 담글 때 입든 저고리 옷고름으로 눈물을 씻다가 눈이 매워서 눈물은 예상이상으로 흘러나왔다.
母 「이년아, 에미가 이렇게 우러도 마음을 돌리지 못하겠느냐.」
美 「우시기가 싫으시거든 냉수로 눈을 씨스서요. 고초가루가 덕지덕지 무든 저고리 고름으로 눈을 부비시지 말고」
母 「고년 참 꾀하고 눈치만 남앗고나」
美 「호... 누구 딸이라고 안 그렇겠어요.」
母 「미영아, 엇잿든 한번 더 생각해 보와라. 시인도 좋고 호남아도 가하고 죽도록 사랑도 해  롭지 않겠으나 네 학비까지 대주든 약혼자를 무슨 낫으로 버리랴느냐. 만일 그런다면 나    는＜172＞ 너를 자식으로 알지 않을 터이니 그런 줄 아러라.」
美 「그럼 나는 뉘 자식이 됨니가. 어머니가 세상에 둘도 있나요. 아버지는 헷갈니는 수가 있  을지 모르나 어머니 만이야 어떻게 변통이 됨니가.」
「팍...」
이 소리는 화가 담배 끗까지 올나서 미영의 어깨쪽지를 나려갈기는 어머니의 담배통 소리이다.
「아야!」
물론 미영의 엄살이다. 아픔 이분 엄살 팔분의 고음 발악이다. 이 길노 미영이는 문호의 아파트로 찾어갔다. 밤중에 문호를 찾어가기는 이것이 처음이다. 혹시 활동사진 구경 끝에
「내가 끄리는 차 한잔 마시지 안으랴오.」
문호가 손목을 꺼러도
「명치제과로 갑시다 그려」
미영이는 번번히 피해 왔다. 무슨 이약이가 완전히 매듭저지기 전에는 위험한 긔회만은 애써 피하랴는 방침이었다. 그럼으로 요사히에 와서는 문호 편에서도 밤에 미영이를 아파트로 다리고 가려는 공작만은 단연 중지를 해바린 상태이었다. 그러든 차에 돌연히 달녀든 미영이다. 하늘에서 떠러젓느냐 땅에서 소삿느냐. 싱글벙글 두손을 버리고 삼대나 굶어 사든 사람같이 덤벼드러 키―쓰의 비 환호의 난리가 날 줄 아랏드니― 불러도 불러도 대답은 들리지 안이하였다.
「고만두어라. 너 하나 바라고 뚸어왔드니 너마저 그러는구나.」
보는 이만 없었드면 주저앉어 울기라도 하였으리라. 그러나 인픔 좋은 아파트관리인의 젊은 며느리는 아기밥 젓을 먹이다가 뛰어나와서 바라보고 있었다.
「리선생님은 앗가 나가시어서 그저 도라오지 안으시었어요.」
없는 사람을 불넜으니 대답이 있을 리는 없다. 대답이 들녓드면 그것은 분명히 독갑이의 작란이었을 것이다.
대답이 없기가 다행이다.
이 아파트 문호의 방에는 분명히 사람도 독갑이도 있지를 안는 게 분명해 젓다.
美 「저 저는 리선생의 제자인데 오늘밤에 꼭 오라고 하신 말슴이 있어서 왔담니다」
미영의 독특한 눈우슴 한 개―
「그러서요. 어떻게 하나 안게시어서」
주인 없는 방 사랑하는 사람의 방 그의 생활의 근거지가 지금부여 있다. 그의 사생활의 정체를 알 때는 지금이다.
美 「저 그러지안어도 선생님 말슴이 출입을 하실 지도 모르니＜173＞ 그러거든 문을 여러달  내서 드러앉어 기다리라고 그러시었담니다.」
어서 문을 여러달나는 말이다. 보와하니 도적질해 갈 사람 같지는 않었는지 아기를 안은 채 방문열쇠를 같다 주었다. 미영이는 열쇠를 받아 들고 이십여개나 되는 열쇠 중에서 사랑의 열쇠를 찾기 시작했다.
「저― 18번이람니다.」
열쇠자두에 (18)이라 번호 삭인 열쇠를 차자 문을 열었다.
「18호. 참 좋은 번호로구면요.」
미영이는 기뿐 일이나 되는드시 또 눈우슴을 첫다.
「선생님이 떼를 쓰시어서 이방을 차지하시었담니다.」
애기엄마는 호긔의 눈을 뜨고 랑하에서 기웃기웃한다. 방에는 미영이가 사다 꼬잣든 카네숀은 어듸로 가고 복사가지가 잔득 꼬처 있었다.
「저― 그럼 여긔서 기다리겠읍니다.」
하며 미영이는 문을 탁 다다 바렷다. 문밖에서는 아기의 우는 소리―아마 문닫는 소리에 놀냇나 보다. 책상 양복장 침대 그런 것은 도라볼 필요도 없다. 미영이가 찾고자 하는 것은 녀자의 편지나 사진이나 또는 문호가 긔록한 자긔의 이약이같은 것이었다. 위선 책상설합을 므조리 뒤젓다. 맨 아래층 설합은 잠겨 있었다. 그러나 다행히 바로 그 우에 달린 설합이 열려 있었다. 우에 놓인 설합을 꺼내니 아래에 놓인 설합속에 속으로 손이 드러갈 수 있었다. 봉투만 가득히 드러있다. 꺼내 보니 모도가 녀자들의 편지 미영의 눈에서는 불이 날 뜻 하였다. 그렇게 곱든 눈이 경우에 따라서는 이렇게도 험악해지는가 놀낼 지경이었다. 편지봉투를 든 손은 가비야히 떨녓다. 같익은 앵도같은 입살은 새파랗게 질녓다. 이 입살만은 아모리 식욕 왕성한 문호라도 참아 먹을 엄두를 내지 못하리라. 한 장 두장 내용을 검토하기 시작했다. 그러나 다행히 문호의 소설시에 감격한 문학소녀들의 꿈같은 사연이 대부분이었다. 그 중에 어느 녀자의 편지에는 가치 차를 마시었을 때의 감상이 있고 또 어느 녀성의 편지에는 청량리까지 산책을 하든 날 감상을 적어 보낸 것도 있었다. 미영이는 오늘까지 한번도 생각지 않는 의심이라는 것을 품게 되였다.
「문호가 자긔 이외의 녀성을 둘식 셋식 가지고 노지나 안는가. 이런 경박한 사나희 인긔    의 초점에 놓여 있어 젊은 녀성들이 꿀동에 벌 꼬이듯 하는＜174＞ 이 판에 문호를 완전히   자긔만의 애인을 삼을 수가 있을가」
가슴 속에서는 질투와 의혹이 용소슴을 치는 판에 문호가 드러왔다. 미영은 침대 옆에 둘너있는 카덴 뒤에 숨었다. 문호는 술이 거나하게 취해 가지고 드러왔다. 휘파람을 불며 옷을 벗드니 축음긔 앞으로 가서 「마음의 달」을 거렀다. 뒤를 이워 봄없는 청춘을 걸드니 책상 앞에 가 거러앉어 권년을 피워 문다.
「아― 미영아 이 몺을 것아. 나는 너 때문에― 너 하나 지키기 위하야 온갔 녀성의 유혹    과 땀을 흘려 가며 싸호고 있는데 지금쯤 너는 어떻게나 하고 있느냐 응」
청승스러운 억양은 맛치 명배우가 무대에서 부르싯는 만한 실감을 주었다. 등 뒤에서 숨을 죽이고 섯든 미영이는 고만 감격한 나머지 정신 없이 뛰어 내다르며
「선생님...」
문호의 무릅에 업드러젓다.
「아!」
