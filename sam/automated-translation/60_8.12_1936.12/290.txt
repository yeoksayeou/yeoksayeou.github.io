Magazine Title: 三千리 제8권 제12호
Publication Date: 1936년 12월 01일
Article Title: 名唱과 名歌曲, 沈淸歌와 「宋萬甲」 (Master Singer and Masterful Song, Simch'ŏngga and "Song Man'gap")
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Discussion)

Reporter - Is the *Simch'ŏngga* (沈淸歌) a song passed down in the land of Chosŏn since ancient times?
Song - No, it's not. Since Sim Ch'ŏng's birthplace is called Hwangju Tohwadong (黃州桃花洞), I would guess it's probably near present-day Suzhou or Hangzhou (蘇州抗州) in China, and since the place is called Imdangsu (臨唐水), I wonder if it might be the Yangtze River. In any case, it is a fact that it is a famous legend (傳說) passed down from ancient China.

Reporter - Then it was the "Sim Ch'ŏng" of ancient China, not the "Sim Ch'ŏng" of Chosŏn?
Song - Haha! That's right. However, as it was passed down over a long period afterward, along with all of China's civilization and culture (文物), Sim Ch'ŏng long ago became the Sim Ch'ŏng of this land (江山). Setting aside the debate about whether a filial daughter (孝女) like "Sim Ch'ŏng" actually existed in ancient China, today, the filial daughter "Sim Ch'ŏng" has become the archetypal filial daughter of this land, this country.

Reporter - How do you view the literary artistic value (文藝的價値) of the *Simch'ŏngga* performance (沈淸歌劇)?
Song - Like the firm fidelity of "Ch'unhyang," I believe the fervent filial piety (孝誠) of "Sim Ch'ŏng" will leave a great lesson (敎訓) for the women of Chosŏn for countless generations. The heart of the young "Sim Ch'ŏng," thinking of her blind father even to the point of offering her own delicate, girlish life, will shine eternally.

Reporter - When performing the *Simch'ŏngga* performance, which passage do you put the most effort into singing?
Song - From the scene where Sim Ch'ŏng, having deceived her blind father, is sold for three hundred sŏk (三百石) of rice for offering (供養米) and is dragged away by the merciless sailors (船員) into the deep waters of "Imdangsu," bidding farewell—from that point on, I exert even more effort and strain my voice.

Reporter - When performing the *Simch'ŏngga* performance, at which passage do the listeners <149> soak their collars and shed tears?
Song - Although I have specialized in singing "Yukchabaegi" (六자백이) and "Simch'ŏngga" from the start, in "Simch'ŏngga," when the banquet for the blind (盲人宴) is held in the palace (宮中), and Sim Pongsa (沈奉事), quite unexpectedly, learns that his daughter "Sim Ch'ŏng," whom he thought dead, has become the queen residing deep within the palace (九重王妣), and upon regaining his sight, doesn't know what to do, shedding tears overflowing with joy as they cling to each other—at that scene, I often see tender-hearted women especially shedding tears and sobbing (흐늑겨 우는).

Reporter - Have you ever crossed over to China and seen the places called "Hwangju Tohwadong" and "Imdangsu"?
Song - Although I have been to Peking (北京) some time ago, I have not yet managed to go as far as Hangzhou. <150>

**Summary**

This 1936 interview features master *p'ansori* singer Song Man'gap discussing *Simch'ŏngga*. Song acknowledges the story's origins as a Chinese legend, likely set near Suzhou/Hangzhou and the Yangtze River, but emphasizes that Sim Ch'ŏng has become thoroughly adopted as Chosŏn's archetypal filial daughter. He highlights the enduring moral lesson of Sim Ch'ŏng's filial piety, comparing it to Ch'unhyang's fidelity. Song identifies the emotional peak for the performer as Sim Ch'ŏng's departure to Imdangsu and for the audience as the reunion where Sim Pongsa regains his sight upon recognizing his daughter as queen. For historians, the article is interesting as it shows a prominent cultural figure navigating the origins and Korean adoption of a famous narrative during the colonial period. It reflects the emphasis on traditional Confucian values like filial piety, even while acknowledging foreign roots, potentially as a subtle assertion of distinct Korean cultural identity and adaptation amidst Japanese assimilation pressures. Song's mention of traveling to Peking but not yet Hangzhou adds a personal touch to his discussion of the story's geographical setting.

記 - 심청가(沈淸歌)는 자고로 조선 땅에 전해오는 노래인가요.
宋 - 아니지요. 심청이 난 데를 황주 도화동 (黃州桃花洞)이라 했으니 아마 짐작컨대 지금  중국 소주 항주(蘇州抗州) 근방인 듯 싶고 임당수(臨唐水)라 했으니 양자강이나 아닌       가 생각되지요. 어찌 되엇건 고대 중국에서 전하여 내려오든 유명한 전설 (傳說)인 것       만은 사실이외다.
記 - 그러면 옛날 중국에 있든 「심청」이지 조선의 「심청」이는 아니군요?
宋 - 하하! 그렇지요 그러나 그 뒤 오랜 동안을 두고 나려오는 사이에 중국의 모든 문물(文  物)과 함께 일즉부터 심청은 이 강산(江山)의 심청이가 되여 버렸지요. 옛날 중국에 「      심청」이 같은 효녀(孝女)가 사실 있었는지 없었는지는 논외로 하고라도 오늘에 와서       효녀 「심청」은 이 땅 이 강산의 전형적 효녀로 되여 버렸옵니다.
記 - 「심청가극」(沈淸歌劇)의 문예적 가치(文藝的價値)를 어떻게 보십니까?
宋 - 「춘향」의 굳은 절개와 같이 「심청」의 열열한 효성(孝誠)은 천주만대를 두고 두고 조선 녀성들에게 위대한 교훈(敎訓)을 남겨 주리라고 믿습니다. 연연한 소녀인 자기의 목숨  을 받처서까지 눈 먼 아버지를 생각하시는 어린 「심청」의 마음은 영원토록 빛날 것이     외다.
記 - 「심청가극」을 하여 내려갈 때에 이떤 대목에 가서 그 중 많이 힘을 듸려서 부르심니까?
宋 - 심청이 눈 먼 아버님을 속여서 고양미(供養米) 삼백석(三百石)에 팔려 「임당수」 깊은 물로 사정없는 선원(船員)들의 손에 끄을리여 나가면서 리별하는 장면에서부터는 더     한층 힘들 드려서 목청 뽑아내게 됨니다.
記 - 「심청가극」을 해내려 때면 어느 대목에 이르러 듣는 사람들이＜149＞ 옷고름을 적셔가며 눈물을 흘리게 됩디까?
宋 - 나는 본래부터 「六자백이」와 「심청가」를 전문으로 불러왔지마는 「심청가」에 있어서 중(宮中)에 맹인연(盲人宴)이 열여저 심봉사(沈奉事) 천만뜻밖게 죽어간 자긔 딸 「심  청이」가 구중왕비(九重王妣)의 몸으로 되여였는 것을 알자 두 눈 마저 뜨게 되여 어쩔     줄 몰으고 깃붐에 넘처나는 눈물을 흘려가며 서로 그러는 장면에 이르러서는 대개 마       음 약한 여자들은 눈물을 흘녀가며 흐늑겨 우는 것을 자주 보게 됩니다.
記 - 중국에 건너가 「황주 도화동」과 「임당수」란 데를 구경해 본 일이 였음니까?
宋 - 얼마전 북경(北京)까지는 가 본 일이였지마는 아직 항주까지에는 채 못가보왔슴니     다.＜150＞
