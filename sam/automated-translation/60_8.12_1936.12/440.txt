더벙타리
						韓笛仙
Because it was a day without a single cloud reflected on the river's surface, the fluttering blue skirt on Mansu Tae (萬壽臺), when looked up at from below, seemed to ripple in the sky.
From the wide, clear view of the Pot’ong plain and Pot’ong River (普通 江), a sudden gust of wind blew in, carrying the scent of harvested rice stalks lying flattened.
The wind leaped over the hill and rushed into P'yŏngyang (平壤) Castle.
Whoosh! It felt like you could cage an airplane in a birdcage. The geese flying high, quite high, were distinctly visible one by one.
It was a day when even the coarse grunting of pigs didn't particularly grate on the ears, a day when one might be surprised to find such a faint, dizzying fragrance even from the body of an unadorned wife.

Even on such a day, the thatched houses lined up right below Mansu Tae—misshapen like some huge beast had vomited them out—could only emit the foul smell of rotting fish, knowing no better scent.
For the man in Western clothes standing next to the blue skirt, smelling so much of powder it was like inhaling tobacco smoke, the night that would unfold on the bed made the daylight seem slow. But for <228> the children of these thatched houses, who spent the day unable to afford even a single coin for a snack on their famished bellies, the autumn sun, though short, seemed abandoned by the heavens, shockingly refusing to move from its place.
A boy, six years old this year, uninterestedly looked up at the fluttering skirt on Mansu Tae, then finally started whining,
“Gimme sweet potato, huh! Huuh!”
He began to fiercely tug and tear at his mother's skirt—already so worn it looked ready to fall apart on its own—while she carried his younger sibling on her back.
Nine-year-old Ch'ilsŏng, who had only learned to read situations, was standing next to her older brother, fiddling with the rather large scar on her mother’s forehead. Trying to stay quiet, she couldn't help but mumble,
“P'ilsŏn’s mom bought her some!”
Before she could even register that she hadn't kept the unnecessary remark to herself, her mother’s sharp scream came, “Even the girl is nagging!” along with a slap landing on her head, disheveling her already messy hair.
Surely anticipating such an outburst, she had her hand defensively on her head, peeking up at her mother’s face. Seeing the hand raised, she burst into tears even before being hit, so the slap that might have ended with one blow rained down two, three times in succession.
At this commotion, the baby sleeping on the mother’s back woke with a start and wailed piercingly, “Waaaah!” As a new crybaby erupted on her back, the mother, utterly choked with frustration, struck out blindly at both the girl and the boy.
“Just die, all of you. Die.”
As was her habit, Ch'ilsŏng ran away.
“Not a single grain of rice for dinner right now, and these dreadful things brazenly demand sweet potatoes!”
Her pigtail-like hair flapping, the mother shouted at Ch'ilsŏng, who had run off a short distance and stood sniffling, unable even to cry her heart out. Overcome by an uncontrollable surge of blood to her head, she yelled, “Where do you think you’re running, girl? You think running away will make you stay inside? Go fetch firewood for tomorrow, now!”
If she wanted to vent her anger properly, she would have chased her down at once and beaten her into silence, but she couldn't do that every time Ch'ilsŏng cried. She could only burn with frustration, her eyes blazing with fury.
Ch'ilsŏng, fearing another blow, was relieved <229> at the command to fetch firewood. Skirting cautiously around her mother, she picked up a handcart (토수래) from beside a pillar where bundles of rice straw stood stiffly like a performer’s props, and came out. Just then, P'ilsŏn appeared before her, grinning and holding a steaming sweet potato as if to show off.
Freshly steamed, its rich aroma tantalizingly filled the mouth.
Seeing this, the six-year-old boy swallowed his tears, wishing he could just take one satisfying bite, and wiped the tear tracks running down his smudged face with his grime-caked sleeve.
“Why are you standing there when I told you to go get firewood?”
Spitting out the saliva pooled in her mouth, the mother yelled again. Ch'ilsŏng swallowed a mouthful of saliva awkwardly and turned away, shouldering the handcart.
The borrowed rubber shoes she wore slapped and dragged, kicking up dust with each step.
The handcart slowly disappeared into Kija Rim (箕子林, Kija Woods).

Only then did the door, riddled with holes and with broken frames, creak open. An old woman, whose flesh seemed already interred in a tomb, poked her face out, coughed raspingly, “Khh-llung!”
“Why all this noise?… Khh-llung… Today your father… didn't get drunk… khh-llung… That’s good. Gosh… these days… it’s gotten worse…”
She intended to say more but was stopped by another coughing fit. Mistaking the fluttering skirt on Mansu Tae for a white one, she rubbed her dull eyes a couple of times with her dry hand and then said,
“Hey, daughter-in-law! Shouldn’t you light the fire now! It’s freezing cold!”
Having blurted out this irrelevant remark, the door creaked a few more times before finally closing properly, and she instinctively shrunk back.
The mother shifted the fussing baby from her back to her hip, offered her nipple, and glanced sideways with the whites of her eyes towards the closed door.
“Old folks should just lie down, that’s all.”
Trying to ease her knotted chest, she looked out at the Pot’ong plain, but only saw yellow sheaves of rice scattered here and there.
Lifting her eyes slightly, the soaring Yongak San (龍岳山) stood blocking the view, deep blue.
The child latched onto the nipple, sucked a few times, realized no milk was coming, and started crying again before its tears had even dried.
Even when babies played contentedly and smiled sweetly, she found little endearing about them. But this child, seeming only to waste away and constantly whimper, just provoked a sudden surge of anger. The mother gave it a good <230> smack, hoisted it back onto her back, bit her parched lips, and thought fiercely of just jumping into the river and ending it all, shuddering.
Thinking about it, she couldn't find a single thing in her life that was even as refreshing as a cat’s face.
Her so-called husband came home drunk every day, caused trouble, the children constantly cried from hunger, and her mother-in-law wouldn't die! If only they had enough rice to fill their bellies at mealtimes, she could perhaps turn a blind eye to all this.
It wasn't the first or second time she had resolved to end this helpless existence. But whenever she went to the Pot'ong River on a moonless night and stared into the black water, wondering if it was flowing or not, seeing grinning faces—perhaps the reflections of the dead in a black mirror—float on the surface, she would shut her eyes tight. Just as she was about to plunge in, her children would somehow rush up, cling to her ankles, immobilizing her. Defeated, she would turn back, trembling with rage.

Some fates are meant for death, she thought. When she returned home, her mother-in-law would still be coughing, and the children, thinking she might have brought food, would stare with desperate, gleaming eyes.
She had started selling her body for a few coins to anyone who asked <231> after one such failed suicide attempt, thinking, "Ah, let things be as they will!" Though remnants of her formerly virtuous bloodline occasionally stirred within her, even that gradually died out.
Today, she had barely eaten breakfast, and her stomach only gurgled with water.
The mother tightened her trouser ties.
“Just wait until he comes home drunk today. Whether I die or live, I have to make a decision. I can’t live like this day after day.”
Wiping the tears welling up in her grime-filled eyes with her jacket tie, she looked towards the direction her husband usually staggered home from.
By the roadside, some child had defecated and then fallen over crying on top of it.
That girl, P'ilsŏn, should have eaten her sweet potato alone in her room, but instead, she was still standing right in front of the six-year-old boy, nibbling away at it bit by bit.
“Who bought you the sweet potato?”
“My mommy!”
“Must’ve latched onto someone else again!”
Taking a large bite of the saliva-coated potato, P'ilsŏn giggled to herself.

The fluttering skirt on Mansu Tae momentarily stilled.
At the weather station visible behind the fire watchtower, a flag signaling an impending strong wind was raised.
Caw, caw. A magpie perched on an acacia tree on the ridge flew into Kija Rim.

“Damn, look at that bastard.”
Her husband, whom she was waiting for, was nowhere in sight yet, but her daughter, sold off to work as a hostess in a city tavern, was walking by, being harassed by some loutish young men.
“There she is!”
“Hey, getting cozy with a new beau these days, huh?”
“That girl’s fine, hey, this is good. Hehehe! Let’s meet tonight, okay?”
Hearing this, Yŏngja retorted sharply,
“Damn, I’ve seen all kinds of bastards.”
She continued walking as her mother called out,
“What are you doing? Coming at such a leisurely pace.”
and went out to meet her.
Yŏngja <232> spat out another curse, “I oughta smash their mouths,” shook off the sound of raucous laughter erupting behind her like dirt, and approached her mother.
“Like a bunch of hooligans, can’t even walk around freely.”
She stood there, showing no inclination to enter the house.
Seeing Yŏngja arrive, the six-year-old boy shouted her childhood name,
“Sŏbunne’s here! Sŏbunne’s here!”
thinking, “Yay! Something good might happen!” He stared dazzlingly at her silk skirt and blouse.
A few threads had come loose at the armpit of the blouse with its mottled pattern. Judging by the orange skirt, she hadn't bothered with undergarments, but the way her trousers sagged loosely down to her ankles below the skirt confirmed she was wearing them. Her white rubber shoes looked overly large due to the unfitting traditional socks (pŏsŏn) she wore.
The mother scanned her daughter’s knotted hair and the style of her clothes, noting the lingering childishness in her eyes as she looked back at her. Thinking that entering the house would only dirty her clothes and the room was cold anyway, she said warmly,
“Are you alright coming out like this? You should wear thicker underclothes. You must be cold!”
Inwardly, she thought about the lack of rice.
“I sneaked out, who said I was coming home?”
“Have you been well in the meantime?”
“Yes.”
Seeing a pot covered with a gourd bowl hanging in the yard, Yŏngja asked,
“Aren’t you even lighting a fire for cooking?”
“Your grandmother was just complaining about the cold. But where would we get firewood?”
Looking at the corner littered with straw mats, tin cans, broken pottery, and a chamber pot full of urine, where only a couple of handfuls of pine needles passed for firewood, Yŏngja realized she had spoken needlessly and looked up towards the hill, but felt awkward, as if the blue skirt standing next to the man was mocking her.
“Hey, sit down here on the earthen ledge (토방) at least.”
“What for? I have to go soon anyway.”
Yŏngja pushed aside the tattered clothes hanging on a line and sat gingerly on the straw mat her mother spread out for her. <233>
Hearing the mother and daughter murmuring outside, the grandmother opened the creaking door again.
“Oh, you’re here. Are you eating well? Khh-llung! I just crave some acorn jelly (묵), but I haven’t been able to have any.”
The grandmother blinked. Her words, spoken while the granddaughter remained silent, only irritated the daughter-in-law who was about to mention the lack of rice, though it couldn't be helped that the old woman couldn't just sit in the room all day without saying anything.
Yŏngja merely nodded at her grandmother. Surveying the inside and outside of the house, which was less endearing than a mud crab (더벙타리) just crawled out of the Pot’ong River’s mudflats—finding no redeeming feature anywhere, unlike the crab which at least has serrated claws on its legs—she brushed off a thorny vine that clung to her trousers like a crab from where it lay rolling beneath the earthen ledge. She thought, 'It always looks like a filthy mud crab.'
“I should go now. I came secretly, so I need to leave quickly. Ugh, night is falling again! Wonder if those hooligans are gone.”
She looked towards where the young men had been earlier. To her six-year-old brother who was tagging along, she said,
“Here, buy yourself some candy. Okay?”
She pulled a coin from her pocket, gave it to him, and brushed down her skirt, preparing to head back into the city.
The mother, realizing this might be her only chance, quickly said,
“Don’t you have any money? We’re out of rice for dinner. Your father just knows how to drink, where would he get money?”
Once started, she added the story of the children begging for sweet potatoes.
“Where would the master give me money?”
“So you don’t have even one *nip* (닢, old coin unit)?”
“I had one *nip*, used it for the streetcar fare. Came quickly, gotta go.”
“..................”
“Mother? Next time I come, I’ll bring money. Okay?”
“Well... If only your older brother were here, things wouldn’t be like this. No news from him, maybe he was beaten to death by those guys. Really, what was he thinking running off so far away? He could have earned even a single *nip* here to help the family.”
“Yes, if only brother were here... Where did Ch'ilsŏng go? Mother? Don’t let them sell Ch'ilsŏng.”
“They’re already clamoring to sell her as soon as she’s old enough. She, <234> went out to gather firewood and hasn’t come back yet— Hey, you should go now.”
“Okay.........”
Yŏngja, finding nowhere else to look but at her brother going off to buy candy, awkwardly turned towards the city, her skirt fluttering in the breeze.
Reaching the streetcar tracks, she saw the blue skirt coming down from Mansu Tae pass by with a smirk. Thinking it bad luck, she spat out a wad of saliva. When she was sold, she couldn’t rely on her mother or father, but thinking especially of her stepfather who had instigated the sale, she felt a bone-deep resentment. Yet, failing to give even a single coin to her own flesh-and-blood mother weighed heavily on her heart, refusing to settle.
'Will that clingy Hyŏn (玄) come looking for drinks again today?' she wondered. If he came, she decided she had no choice but to yield to his demands this time and somehow wheedle some money out of him without the master knowing. But thinking of her older brother who had fled to Manchuria during the rubber factory strike two years ago filled her heart with a pang (몽글 하였다).
Thinking of her brother inevitably brought Ch'ŏru to mind. Rugged-faced Ch'ŏru… he had once held her tightly in the dark, making her heart pound.
Just as the crab living its life carrying its shell (딱지) in the mudflats (감탕) had serrated claws, her brother and Ch'ŏru must be the serrated claws emerging from their hovel.
Yŏngja had caught mud crabs (더벙타리) that spewed saliva as they crawled on the mudflats of the Pot'ong River.
Her hand unconsciously went to her swollen breasts (젔가슴). Another thread came loose at the armpit.
If only her brother had been here, whatever happened, Yŏngja wouldn't have been sold, nor would she be walking towards the tavern now.
Her brother’s eyes! As Yŏngja closed her eyes, it felt like her brother and Ch'ŏru were walking towards her. What were they doing now?

The flag at the weather station flapped wildly as if about to fly away. Was the man atop the fire watchtower telling people to watch the circus act of a horse turning in circles?
Humans spinning round and round, yet all must appear small from below. For the man turning his back to light a cigarette, the top of the watchtower aligned perfectly with the blue backside.
The kite, appearing as a dot, suddenly snapped its string in the wind and fluttered rapidly away.

Ch'ilsŏng, who had gone to gather firewood, finally returned towards dusk with her handcart sparsely filled with fallen leaves and dry twigs.
“Is dinner ready? I’m starving.”
She shivered, clad only in a single layer of patched skirt, as she entered.
“Wait until your father comes.”
The mother cut her off sternly, then paced back and forth, soothing the baby, waiting only for her husband’s return. Just then, Sŏnggu walked past, placing some radishes on his carrying frame (지게) propped against the wall.
“Auntie, have you had dinner?” he asked, blinking.
“Not yet. Her father isn’t back yet. Mister, are those radishes? Give me one, please.”
“Auntie, you always… How could I refuse Auntie’s request? Here!”
He briskly handed her one.
“Auntie, come again today.”
He patted his vest pocket meaningfully.
Though it was unlikely money actually jingled, to the mother’s ears, it sounded like it did.
“Looks like you earned a lot today.”
“If you come, Auntie will have fun, and get her share too. Will you wait?”
“Do as you please.”
Giggling and squinting his eyes, Sŏnggu walked away unsteadily. The mother watched him go, remembering how two days ago, unable to bear seeing her children starving, she had gone to Sŏnggu and returned with thirty *jŏn* clutched in her hand.
Before she came to her current husband, bringing her son who fled to Manchuria and Yŏngja, she hadn't starved like this. She had managed some modest furniture and lived contentedly, envying no one. But then her husband contracted typhoid fever, using up what little they had. After he died, she somehow managed, enduring alone until she couldn't anymore. Persuaded by others that her current husband was decent, she had come to him. Back then, he didn't drink so heavily or smash things randomly as he did now; they lived inside the city walls, albeit in a rented house. Then, her hot-tempered husband quarreled with the landlord over a trivial matter, lost his job, and they fell into debt, barely surviving until they could no longer stay. After a big fight with a debt collector <236>, they had no choice but to move out here. Ever since moving outside the city walls, her husband tried to drown all his troubles in alcohol.
Thinking back to those times and her current plight, reduced to such dealings even with a porter like Sŏnggu, made her feel dizzy as if beaten. She regretted not enduring alone till the end. But regret was useless now.
She just wanted to flare up in anger, to unleash her fury freely just once.
The mother wiped her tears, went inside, and lit the lamp (람푸).
The fizzing, crackling sound of the kerosene being drawn up the wick felt like it was scraping her bones. Shadows danced darkly on the earthen walls.
The mother told Ch'ilsŏng to bring the kitchen knife from the kitchen and carved the radish to feed the children.
“Alright, go to sleep now.” She threw the ragged bedding and grime-stained pillow, which had been resting on a wooden box, onto the reed mat floor.
The grandmother pulled the ragged quilt over herself, huddled up watching the whimpering children and her furious daughter-in-law, and mumbled to herself,
“Gosh, your father! Khh-llung! Drunk again!”
Just as the children finally drifted off to sleep, the daughter-in-law ignored her mother-in-law’s query about where she was going and went outside to find Sŏnggu.

Sure enough, late at night, the husband returned, face flushed red, muttering, “Damn dog! Bastard to kill!”
Today, the husband had made a mistake, accidentally tipping over a cartload of dirt he was pulling. He got a good smack from the foreman (십장) for it. Because his colleagues intervened, he couldn't fight back properly. On his way home, he went into a tavern, drank more than he could afford, got into a fight with the owner, and came home late with his clothes torn.
Stumbling through the dark night, he took off his straw sandals (지까다비) that had fallen into a puddle, entered the stable (모말방안?), sniffing loudly.
“Me! Me! Yeah! Letting that bastard! Look down on me! And live! Aaargh, damn bastard! Who does he think I am! He dared hit me! How outrageous! Think I have no guts (밸)? I may have no money, but should I take a beating! Me, take a beating! Hmph, me, take a beating!”
After ranting for a while, he pushed aside something his foot hit and was about to lie down when the door creaked open and his wife entered.
Only then realizing his wife hadn't been in the room, the husband saw a good excuse for a fight, his eyes rolling.
“Where have you been, you bitch?”
He sat back up.
The stench of alcohol hit the wife’s face. ‘He definitely drank,’ she thought.
“Wherever I went.”
“This bitch talks back well. Itching for a beating, are you?”
“Go ahead, beat me. Drinking day and night. You expect me to fill my belly with water? Put down some money and then talk. Think I can’t say anything—”
“This bitch, I earn my keep, what business is it of yours? Aaargh!”
Annoyed, and looking for an outlet for his anger, the husband kicked his wife. Although the kick landed, the tip of his foot striking her thigh felt oddly ticklish.
As the wife fell, the lamp (백등화) flew from her hand and smashed forcefully (?) on the floor.
“Ouch! Hitting people again! Hmph, your earnings, you say! So it’s okay if your children starve all day?”
The wife pushed herself up against the far wall, grabbed her husband, and shrieked.
“Kill me. Kill me. If I die, I won’t see this miserable life anymore.”
The children were already awake but pretended to sleep, holding their breath.
“Can’t you tell me where you went, bitch?”
“It’s my fault for living with a bastard like you. With you!”
“This bitch, what’s wrong? If there's some beaten-down guy [you met], bring him out!”
“I went to earn money! You expect me to starve the children to death?”
“This bitch, just because I let you be.”
“If you have it, where’s the knife?”
“Ah, this bitch!”
He grabbed his wife tightly by the chest, then shoved her away.
“Oof!”
The wife fell, got up, grabbed the kitchen knife.
“Here. Why bother with this? Kill me with your own hands today. If I die, I won’t see the children starve.”
She charged at him.

The husband didn’t take the offered knife. Muttering “Bitch, bitch,” he kept slapping her thigh, then reached out as if to assuredly (정창) take the knife.

Yŏngja turned her face away to avoid the man’s breath. Clutching the fifty *jŏn* silver coin Hyŏn pressed into her hand, she closed her eyes, yet goosebumps prickled her entire body as she felt her brother’s shining (빝아는) eyes watching her from somewhere.

**Summary**

This bleak short story depicts the crushing poverty and despair of a family living in a shantytown below Mansu Tae in P'yŏngyang during the colonial period. The narrative follows the mother's struggles against hunger, her abusive, alcoholic husband, and the constant needs of her children and ailing mother-in-law. Her daughter, Yŏngja, has been sold into prostitution and makes a furtive visit, unable to offer financial help. The mother resorts to exchanging sexual favors with a neighbor for basic necessities. The story culminates in a violent domestic dispute fueled by alcohol and desperation, ending ambiguously as the husband assaults the wife after she offers him a knife to kill her. The final scene shifts back to Yŏngja, accepting money from a client, haunted by the image of her absent, possibly activist brother.

From a historical perspective, the story offers a raw glimpse into the extreme hardships faced by the urban poor in 1930s colonial Korea. It highlights the social disintegration under economic pressure, the specific vulnerability of women forced into desperate measures like prostitution or transactional sex, the prevalence of domestic violence exacerbated by poverty and hopelessness, and the specter of political dissidence (the absent brother who fled during a strike). The detailed depiction of the squalid living conditions, the constant struggle for food and fuel, and the characters' raw emotional responses provide valuable social commentary for historians studying the period.
