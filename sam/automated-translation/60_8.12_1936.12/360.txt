"Art" or "Death": The State of Mind of Men of Letters
Even if parents, wife, and children starve to death, do you, Sir, have the passion and determination to dedicate your entire life to fighting against poverty (because, judging by our current situation, poverty and illness will likely be inseparable from artists in the future as well) for the sake of Art (Literature, Music, Painting)?

Ch'unwŏn
I have already pledged to dedicate this body to "Art." The path I have walked for the past 30 years has been for this "Art," and the path I will walk henceforth is solely this one path. Having already made such a solemn and firm pledge, how could the mere poverty that befalls me possibly stop my steps?
I hold one belief in my heart. It is the word from the scriptures of the genius Christ: "Look at the birds of the air; they do not sow or reap, yet heaven feeds them. Do not worry about what you will eat or wear, but seek only righteousness." I know that as long as one walks the right path, the path of righteousness, heaven will provide enough clothes to wear and food to eat for any living being. Under this belief, I only strive to devote all my sincerity and talent. I believe this desire and passion will not retreat even slightly until the day my life ends.<192>

Yŏm Sang-sŏp
I have no such passion or determination. It's not that I don't fight poverty, but I don't fight purely for art; firstly, I fight to live.

Pak Yŏng-hŭi
What art can there be when one is on the verge of starvation? Human passion is not a supernatural force. If there is no life, what creation can there be? Without passion, how can there be art?

Kim An-sŏ
It is a mind that changes from moment to moment. To try and state 'this is how it is' without pinning down this changing mind is ultimately like grasping at shadows, like scooping the moon reflected on water. Therefore, there must be considerable error in my self-diagnosis of my state of mind. Why? Because emotions are never always the same. Sometimes I want to strike out at the world, sometimes I want to live purely like a young lamb without any desire or hatred, sometimes I want to beat my own chest forcefully, and sometimes I want to forget everything as if it were a dream. Which of these can I call the truth of my feelings? Yet, since these are all true fragments of my emotions, I cannot call them false. My recent self-diagnosis of my state of mind is both a misdiagnosis and, at the same time, not my true state of mind. It merely pertains to such things having existed once.
If one has a deep conviction in anything, it is right to devote one's entire mind and body to it. However, if one is in a situation where neither this nor that can be done, it might be best to align oneself with the art one likes best, but ultimately, if parents, wife, and children are dying, art will also die. Since both disappear together, I myself cannot help but die as well. If so, what is there to fight against, and to what can passion be dedicated? Poverty is a matter of degree; if parents, wife, and children are starving to death, this question becomes irrelevant.

Yang Chu-dong
I remember a passage in the preface of François Coppée's first Bethlehem poetry collection, where he gladly accepted days without bread <193> and nights without lodging to create his immortal works. I gather that the sublime realm of the artist lies here, but I still lack such passion and determination, probably because my art, rather than being superior to my parents, wife, and children, is truly the product of talent that brings shame even to my wife and children. Therefore, I slightly alter a line from Hwang Mae-ch'ŏn's Paeg'undae poem and recite it as follows:
"Aya (牙涯) is, after all, a guest among humans. Though loving famous literature, I also love my own body."

Ch'oe Chŏng-hŭi
Both passion and determination have vanished. Still, I hold the thought that someday I will tell my whole heart through writing. But I am still very young. So I am afraid to reveal my heart without reservation.

Kim Kwang-sŏp
I have never once thought of upholding only the Way of art even if I starve to death. However, as long as the life of art lives within my heart, I intend to strive for its expression.

Chŏn Mu-gil
It should be done as an avocation while pursuing another path to save parents, wife, and children.

Ŏm Hŭng-sŏp
Yes, I have it.
Because I have it, I have fought against poverty and illness until now.
—Firstly, it seems that if I do not walk the path of art, there will be no broader, brighter, firmer path for me—

Min Pyŏng-hwi
When the beautiful dream of reminiscence spreads its young wings on a quiet, moonlit autumn night, it somehow feels as if the artistic passion of old days is cooling. There was a time for me, a young poet, when I spent my days reading and dreaming of the future, immersed in literary passion.
But that was the era of my youth as a literary aspirant!
When I discover my own desolate shadow as a young intellectual of today on Chongno Crossroads, there are more than one or two days when I let out a long sigh of unknown, lonely sorrow.
This place is the Peninsula! Oh, the shadow of the artist here, how thin it was? But that too is because one sees oneself as an artist.<194>
There is no money for tomorrow's food. That is also well known. Sitting alone on a chair in a corner of the "tea room," for whom does he wait? The magazine editor兼man of letters waiting for a friend to write a manuscript while smelling the fragrance of black tea! The artist trudging along, knocking on the doors of magazine or newspaper companies to get manuscript fees (which are paltry)! Is this not a tragedy of this place?
Their faces are "melancholy"! They have faces like widows visiting a cemetery, walking distractedly along Chongno Crossroads, still carrying something related to art in their heads, only to find themselves startled by the "Go, Stop" traffic signal! This happens often!
They are poor, but they have parents, wives, and children! But their manuscript fees, their income, gave no leeway to provide food for their elderly parents, nor could they afford to clothe the bodies of their young wives and children even once. No, have they even clothed themselves or filled their own stomachs? Some people, disillusioned here, eventually tried to seek money but couldn't find it and did not abandon art.
Even while starving, they did not abandon art, striving to fulfill their duty for the sake of humanity. This is passion. This is the determination to fight poverty and leave behind great art.
On nights when the trams have stopped, unable to even afford a taxi, walking the moonlit streets of Seoul, when sick fallen leaves drift listlessly, I find myself, a man of sentiment like poetry. In my pocket, two 5-chŏn tram tickets remain. I think of tomorrow's electricity bill and the pawn ticket whose deadline is past. The firewood must have run out too?
But I stay up this night writing the manuscript requested this morning. Thinking of the money that will come in a month later—is this not the artistic passion with which artists living here fight against poverty? And is it not the determination to fight like this for a lifetime?

An Ham-gwang
It seems that even that noble passion and determination of a bygone era cannot preserve its own purity before the barrier of daily life, and only sorrow continues. Whether that passion's purity can be achieved to any degree in the future (?) seems to be a major question. <195>

Yu Wan-hŭi
If one could manage both life and art, there would be nothing more to say, but even in the case where that is not possible, it is practically impossible to ignore life or family and devote one's entire life to art. This is because life and humanity, humanity and art stand in an inseparable series, and therefore the suffering derived from connections to life or family would more than suffice to annihilate any attachment or passion for art!

Song Yŏng
Of course, I have such passion and determination. This kind of passion is my life. Just as one cannot live without life, without this passion, I would rather not live.

Yi Kap-ki
I haven't yet encountered such a serious situation, so I don't know what I would do. However, I do know how to respect, more than anyone, those noble artists recorded in history who possessed such lofty passion!

Han In-t'aek
1. Since I have resolved to end my life as a student of literature, I think that material threats will not be able to invade my will.

Yi Pung-myŏng
Having swum through a path of thorns no less painful than starvation until today, I believe there will be no change in my passion and determination for art hereafter. Seeking the path of art while pushing aside myriad obstacles is also for the sake of construction without misfortune; if I single-mindedly follow that path and starve to death, whom would I blame?

Ch'ae Man-shik
I possess that much determination and passion (for literature).
However, firstly, if my parents, wife, and children starve, would I, even if I were a bear licking its paws to survive, escape starvation?
Next, while it's true I am currently poor, even if my parents, wife, children, and I were to starve because I insist on doing literature, that is not the fault <196> of doing literature.
Furthermore, judging by the current situation, while poverty is not necessarily an enemy to literary figures, there are various difficulties no less significant than poverty. Journalism or the red pen [censorship]...

Yi Il
If there is an art equivalent to the starvation of parents, wife, and children, I would try the adventure. However, since it is merely secondary or tertiary literary amusement—malicious play (惡戱)—I cannot squeeze out such passion no matter how hard I try.

Yi Sŏk-hun
As I have considered literature an avocation (餘技) until now, I have never even dreamed of having the nerve to support my wife and children with such an avocation!

Sŏ Kwang-je
Fortunately, I do not have the debt that most Korean youths have of supporting elderly parents. Though it can be called a child's duty and obligation, I became a rebel against my family in my youth and ran away from home. Neither then nor now are my parents in danger of starvation; both are elderly, and moreover, my nephew is earning money, so I think such a situation will not arise.
As for the person called 'wife,' I sought one who could work and earn her own living even without me, so there is no worry. However, in paving my own path, I have no desire to have my own children.
Therefore, I intend to walk the path I have trodden for over 10 years for my entire life.

Yi Hŏn-gu
The state of mind of being intoxicated with the realm of art while clearly foreseeing the imminent starvation of parents, wife, and children! It resembles the ideal of the phoenix, yet it is difficult to comprehend. Even if one understands it, can one live by that conviction? One would have to face that terrifying reality. Isn't contentment in poverty (樂貧) or struggle against poverty (鬪貧) also a matter of degree?

Yi Hyo-sŏk
Though a praiseworthy passion and determination, it is thoroughly non-modern. What exists beyond life? To think that masterpieces arise from poverty is a kind of medieval romanticism, perhaps. <197>

Yi Mu-yŏng
That is something I have already resolved. I do not even think about it anew now.

Ham Tae-hun
If I hadn't had a burning desire for art, I think I would have become a 50,000-won millionaire by now. Having missed that chance, I cannot now leave to make money again. I intend to finish my life with literature, even while fighting poverty.

Pang In-gŭn
I have fought with such passion and determination in the past and present, and I intend to fight until the very end in the future as well.

Hong Hyo-min
Surveys from Samch'ŏlli often used to be based on "journalism" (쩌낼이슴), but this time the survey truly gave me something poignant. It is
Firstly, the question of whether, Sir, you have the passion and determination to dedicate your life to fighting poverty for the sake of art, even if parents, wife, and children starve. This is something difficult to answer quickly, and at the same time, it makes one think that such passion and determination are necessary. To give a serious answer to this serious question, I say, "For the sake of my art, I have the determination to strive until death (死而後己)."

Ch'oe Yŏng-su
I placed both hands on my chest and tried meditative self-reflection. The realm of "Art" is distant, and the "State of Starvation" is still hazy, so it is difficult to say. I might endure poverty, but I cannot guarantee up to the point of starvation. <198>

**Summary**

This 1936 survey from the magazine *Samch'ŏlli* asks prominent Korean literary figures (文士) whether they possess the passion and determination to pursue art (literature) for their entire lives, even if it means fighting extreme poverty and allowing their families to starve. The responses vary widely: some (like Ch'unwŏn, Song Yŏng, Yi Pung-myŏng, Yi Mu-yŏng, Ham Tae-hun, Pang In-gŭn, Hong Hyo-min) express unwavering, almost religious devotion to art above all else, including material well-being and family. Others (like Yŏm Sang-sŏp, Pak Yŏng-hŭi, Kim An-sŏ, Yang Chu-dong, Yi Kap-ki, Yi Hŏn-gu, Yi Hyo-sŏk, Ch'oe Yŏng-su) offer more pragmatic or skeptical views, questioning the possibility or nobility of creating art under extreme deprivation, emphasizing the priority of life and family, or seeing the dilemma itself as unrealistic or romanticized. A few (like Ch'ae Man-shik) acknowledge the hardship but attribute it to broader societal issues beyond just choosing art. The responses collectively paint a stark picture of the difficult conditions faced by intellectuals and artists in colonial Korea, grappling with the demands of artistic calling versus the harsh realities of survival and familial responsibility.

From a historical perspective, this article is revealing about the socioeconomic pressures on Korean intellectuals during the Japanese colonial period. The stark choice presented—art or starvation—highlights the precarious economic situation for many engaged in cultural production. The range of responses reflects differing philosophies on art's role, individual temperament, and perhaps varying degrees of personal economic security, while implicitly critiquing a society where pursuing art often meant confronting destitution. The mention of censorship ("red pen") by Ch'ae Man-shik also subtly points to the political constraints under which these writers operated.

첫재 부모처자는 饑死할망정  선생게서는 예술을 위하야 빈곤과 싸우면서라도 일생을 바치겠다는 정열과 각오가 있는가 하는 질문이다. 이것이야말로 얼는 대답하기 힘든 그것인 동시에 또한 이러한 정열과 각오가 없어서는 아니 되겠다는 생각이 든다. 곧 심각한 질문임과 아울러 심각한 대답을 하자면 「나는 나의 예술을 위하야는 死而後己할 각오가있오」하고 말합니다.

崔永秀
