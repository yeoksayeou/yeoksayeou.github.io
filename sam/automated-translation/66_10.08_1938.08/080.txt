An Account of Visiting Chiang Kai-shek's Camp in Hankow, City of Death
						By John Gunther, New York Times Correspondent

Chiang Kai-shek in the field rarely meets with newspaper or magazine correspondents. However, for some reason, I fortunately had the chance to meet him, hear his first-rate eloquence, and glimpse into his unreserved heart.

Whether it was because he was in a particularly good mood that day or perhaps a tactic for propaganda, I don't know, but in any case, Chiang Kai-shek spoke with a very passionate tone about his intention for long-term resistance and then answered the questions I had submitted.

That day, I crossed the Yangtze River, whose waves had calmed. The water of the Yangtze past midday was slightly yellowish-brown. Currently, in China, Chiang Kai-shek is revered as the strongest hero. To be precise, Chiang Kai-shek doesn't live in Hankow but across the river in a place called Wuchang.

Hankow is called the Chicago of China. Three cities meet along the river. The Yangtze serves as the connection point between the Jinghan Railway linking Beijing and Hankow, and the <63> Yuehan Railway connecting Guangdong and Wuchang.

About 300 *li* behind the front lines, Chiang Kai-shek has set up his headquarters and commands from there. It seems there are times when, unable to bear defeats, he comes close to the front lines.

Before meeting Chiang Kai-shek, I prepared my questions in advance. My wife and I went to his headquarters, entertaining various imaginations.

The people seated were myself, my wife, Madame Song Meiling, Mr. W. H. Donald, and Shao Lizi—five of us. The famous Madame Song Meiling is even more beautiful than seen in her very splendidly decorated photographs. Mr. Donald is Chiang Kai-shek's 'éminence grise' Englishman, whom everyone knows, so there's no need for me to introduce him here. The other person, Shao Lizi, is the head of the Kuomintang government's information department and a graduate of the University of Missouri's School of Journalism. Tea was served before each person.

Soon, Chiang Kai-shek appeared before us amidst his busy schedule. He had a kind face and wore a military uniform. Three stars were attached to his collar.

The main points of my questions for Chiang Kai-shek had been translated into Chinese beforehand and were placed in his hands. After speaking for a while about China's relief and reconstruction efforts, Chiang Kai-shek gave me the written answers to my previously submitted questions.

The main points of these written questions and answers are roughly as follows. Looking at the general gist of his responses, one can glimpse his unique psychology of stubbornly pushing forward despite the defeats the Chinese army had suffered up to that point.

Me: "What is the current military and political situation in China? Are you confident in long-term resistance?"
Chiang: "More than ten months have already passed since the China Incident began. I believe Japan likely doesn't think China consists only of the weak. The Chinese people will not yield to Japan's attack. Rather, mutual understanding among the people deepens, and their unity strengthens. We intend to endure any hardship until the very end."

Me: "Is there any possibility of peace restoration currently?"
Chiang: "As long as the war continues, peace is not desired, neither now nor in the future."

Me: "Compared to the time of February 1931, what is the Chinese army's resistance capability now?"
Chiang: "In 1931, China could not resist Japan. That was because we placed our trust in the League of Nations. The League of Nations did not help China. However, since this incident, China has become somewhat different from the past. We are prepared. We can resist." <64>

Me: "How do you believe this incident will turn out?"
Chiang: "First and foremost, this incident leads the Chinese people towards unity. I believe individualism among the Chinese will be augmented by nationalism."

Me: "Until when do you intend to fight?"
Chiang: "As long as Japanese troops remain in China, the war will not cease."

Me: "Isn't there any suitable policy that might satisfy Japan?"
Chiang: "While things are in their current state, there is no room to consider satisfying Japan."

Me: "By what methods are you carrying out the spiritual mobilization of the Chinese people?"
Chiang: "Since the outbreak of the incident, the government authorities have used all possible methods to explain the determination required of the nation facing this crisis. They have had all knowledgeable classes and educators travel throughout the country to emphasize the vital importance of the people assisting the government. It seems to be quite effective."

Me: "I heard that the Chinese army needed reorganization after the fall of Nanking. Has it been completed already?"
Chiang: "Reorganization and formation of the army can always be carried out as long as the war continues. Since the retreat from Nanking, improvements to the army have been steadily progressing."

Me: "How long do you expect Britain and the United States to provide diplomatic and financial assistance to China?"
Chiang: "The instability in the Far East significantly affects British and American interests. Therefore, I think there is no reason for them to be indifferent. Since the outbreak of the war, we have received sympathy from various countries, starting with Britain and America. However, what the Chinese people currently regret is the ambiguity regarding what has become of British and American aid to China."

Me: "What is the relationship with the Eighth Route Army, the Communist army?"
Chiang: "The Eighth Route Army is also a part of the Chinese army. Therefore, like other military units, they obey our orders and act accordingly. The Communist army has changed its previous policies. The leader of the Eighth Route Army, Zhu De, and other cadres are brave, but they are no different from other Chinese military officers."

Having finished the final question, Chiang Kai-shek took a brush pen and signed the Chinese written responses to my questions. Then, with Song Meiling interpreting, he asked me about the general international situation. The main issues discussed were the question of why Japan had not declared war, the issue of Philippine independence, the issue of America's Neutrality Act, and so on.

Surviving Lifelines <65>

To speak humorously, imitating the Chinese: "Even a mere louse will squirm when stepped on."

Based on my observations during my approximately two-week stay in Hankow, I noted the following disadvantages for the Chinese side compared to the Japanese:

First: The Japanese military overwhelmingly controls air superiority over China.
Second: Despite the Chinese army, exhausted by many failures, gradually improving its equipment and organization, it still has major deficiencies in the use of modern weaponry, especially artillery.
Third: The Japanese army is superior to the Chinese army in all aspects. Training, equipment, weaponry—no matter what, the Chinese army is no match for the Japanese army.
Fourth: Despite the desperate efforts of the highest commanders in the Chinese military, they still cannot field a completely unified army. Regional sentiments remain strong, and even regional warlords still act based on their own calculations. Consequently, there are cases where troops can only be formed based on regional foundations. The biggest inconvenience in this regard is the language issue. A soldier from Yunnan does not understand the language of Zhejiang Province. Next is the difficulty with food: soldiers from the south want to eat rice, while soldiers from the north prefer millet gruel. Everything else can be imagined from this.
Fifth: A major flaw that significantly hinders the activity of the Chinese military is the corrupt behavior of the Chinese people. The bad habit of seeking personal gain (私腹) has not yet been rectified.
Sixth: Lack of military funds. The outcome of the war depends on the supply of weapons and munitions. Even if the Chinese army has spirit, it's clear they cannot fight without guns and gunpowder. The lack of funds, the complete blockade of northern and southern ports occupied by the Japanese army, etc., pose a major threat to the supply of munitions to China. Generally, China is at a disadvantage in these six points mentioned above.

However, the main artery supplying munitions to the Chinese side is currently the Hong Kong - Guangdong - Hankow line. The Japanese air force bombs this line, the Yuehan Railway, daily. Yet, they have not managed to destroy it completely. Although hundreds of tons of bombs must have been dropped, trains still cautiously move through the gaps.

Imagining a future where the Yuehan Railway is completely destroyed, China will have to rely on the following two lines for its survival. The first is the highway built parallel to the Yuehan Railway, <66> but depending on the damage to the Yuehan line, this too must be considered at risk of destruction.

In such a case, the second line planned is the French railway from Yunnanfu (Kunming) to Haiphong in French Indochina. However, rumors are circulating in Hankow that France, fearing retaliatory bombing from Japan, might prohibit the transport of weapons via its railway.

And two lines remain that the Chinese side can rely on as a last resort: one is the road currently being completed from Yunnan to Burma, and the other is the road passing through the desolate regions of Outer Mongolia and Xinjiang to Xi'an and Lanzhou. It is said that about 150,000 volunteer laborers were mobilized for the construction of the aforementioned Yunnan-Burma road. This road's completion, initially scheduled for April 1st, was delayed and is now expected perhaps this summer. The automobile route from the Soviet Union is regularly used, and recently, a convoy of about 1,000 trucks arrived.

On the first night I arrived in Hankow, a Chinese person I met said, "China fights, but ultimately, won't it have to face a painful end?" When I asked back, "What exactly do you mean by a painful end?" I clearly remember him answering, "That's when the supply of goods to China is cut off."

Street Scenes Under Air Raids

"Look! Japanese planes are coming now and will bomb exactly over there," shouted a Chinese man staying at the same hotel, pointing from the balcony towards the far bank of the Yangtze River. This man was a fortune-teller, and strangely, his prediction came true. About an hour later, indeed, the loud air raid sirens wailed, and Japanese bombers appeared over the area the fortune-teller had pointed to, namely, above the train station.

They were white planes, flying extremely high and at tremendous speed.

After a short while, along with the sound of explosions, the entire multi-story building I was in shook terrifyingly, and black smoke rose from the other side of the river. In the main streets of Hankow and Wuchang, bomb shelters (地窖) are installed everywhere, and below the window of the hotel I was in, a shelter's entrance gaped open.

"As soon as the first air raid siren sounded, everyone frantically searched for safe zones, throwing the entire city into chaos."

Seeing this was pitiable, but also amusing. <67>

People who had just been walking calmly were now running frantically, foaming at the mouth. Rickshaws turned into racing vehicles at furious speeds, and cars sped away as if their wheels barely touched the ground. Soon, the streets were devoid of people and vehicles.

Eventually, when the second siren sounded, traffic police put on their steel helmets and took cover under the shade of roadside trees. Above a shelter, a group of coolies (苦力) gathered, waiting for the planes to arrive.

Finally, when the planes appeared overhead and began bombing, these coolies in blue shirts (삿쯔 - likely 'shirts') quickly darted into the shelter like monkeys. Then, during a brief pause in the bombing, they would poke their heads out of the shelter again, looking around with fearful expressions, only to scramble back in fright at the next sound of explosions. I watched this from the hotel rooftop.

The bombing by the Japanese planes was astonishingly accurate, targeting only military structures. Hankow increasingly took on the characteristics of a city at war. Prices soared terrifyingly; a bottle of whiskey cost 30 dollars. And finding an empty hotel room was harder than imagined. Hankow was completely isolated from the outside world, with only two ways to connect externally. One was by passenger plane of the Eurasia Aviation Corporation (유차라샤윤겔 회사 - likely Eurasia) and the other was using the Yuehan Railway, though the train was said to be safer. A few weeks prior, Chinese army Martin bombers (말틴폭격기) at the airfield were bombed by Japanese planes. These Martin bombers were fully loaded with bombs. The damage was immense; 26 planes were instantly pulverized by the explosion of their own bombs.

Synthesizing foreigners' critiques, it seems the Chinese people are perhaps beyond calm and verge on being insensitive. It feels as though people in one region are completely unaware of events in another, showing no reaction. One day, while having lunch and talking with Chiang Kai-shek's advisor, Donald, strange explosive sounds came from outside. Donald looked at me, smiled, and said, "Sir, those are firecrackers. It must be a Chinese funeral."

Then, the explosions erupted fiercely from all directions. Going out immediately, I saw it was a flood of firecrackers set off by the populace, ecstatic because the Chinese army had supposedly defeated the Japanese army. For someone like me, who actually knew how the Chinese army was retreating, such a scene etched an indescribably tragic feeling into my heart. The loud noise of these firecrackers surpassed that of bombing by about 20 planes. <68>

This victory celebration (祝儀) by the oblivious citizens continued relentlessly with deafening explosions from afternoon until midnight, making it seem like heaven and earth would tear apart. A byproduct problem of this war must be the resolution of the refugee issue. Providing food and jobs for the more than 16 million refugees centered around Hankow is crucial. One idea is perhaps to start a large-scale toy manufacturing industry to give work to the refugees.

Chiang Kai-shek's Sermon

Chiang Kai-shek is a Methodist Christian. Thus, he skillfully gathers popular support using experiences gained from Christianity. This is the influence of the Song family. Every Sunday morning, there is a special worship service in Hankow, where over 600 people cross the Yangtze River to gather at a chapel near Chiang Kai-shek's main headquarters. Here, Chiang Kai-shek arrives precisely on time, bows once to the congregation (會衆), and ascends the platform. When the military band finishes the processional march, everyone removes their hats and bows three times towards the portrait of Sun Yat-sen, the founding father of modern China, hanging at the front. Then, the officials put their hats back on, and eventually, Chiang Kai-shek reads a passage from Sun Yat-sen's Three Principles of the People. When Chiang reads it once, the entire congregation follows along, reciting it in unison. It's just like reading the Gospel in a Christian service (예슈교 - Jesus Religion).

After this, there is a three-minute silent prayer (黙禱), followed by Chiang Kai-shek's speech, which sometimes lasts for an hour, or even two or three hours.

During this long sermon by Chiang Kai-shek, all attendees—representatives from various circles in Hankow, high government officials, soldiers, etc.—remain standing throughout (終始).

Finally, here is an example demonstrating Chiang Kai-shek's sharp mind. Knowing well (熟知하다) that the Chinese people love money more than life, he has made payments to the wounded monetary rather than spiritual. This method of Chiang Kai-shek's can be seen as somewhat novel in modern military history.

For wounded soldiers, ordinary soldiers receive 10 dollars for their injuries, officers have two levels at 30 and 50 dollars, and generals receive 100 dollars. The fact that quite a few try to collect by faking injuries (僞傷), requiring considerable effort (前間) to determine authenticity (眞僞), is also a very Chinese story (支那然한 이야기).

Since January of this year, the total cost of these injury payments has reached 1.7 million dollars.

From Guangdong, trains fully loaded with cargo occasionally arrive secretly via the Yuehan Railway, evading the eyes of Japanese bombers. This cargo seems likely to consist of more than just military supplies. <69>

**Summary**

This 1938 article by New York Times correspondent John Gunther recounts his interview with Chiang Kai-shek in wartime Hankow (Wuchang). Chiang expressed strong determination for long-term resistance against Japan, emphasizing growing national unity despite military setbacks. Gunther details the interview process and Chiang's answers regarding China's situation, potential for peace (none during war), comparison with 1931, expected outcomes (national unity), spiritual mobilization, army reorganization, foreign aid expectations (disappointment with ambiguity), and the integration of the Communist Eighth Route Army. Gunther then provides his own critical assessment of China's weaknesses: lack of airpower, poor artillery use, inferiority to Japanese forces in training and equipment, internal disunity and warlordism (highlighted by language and food issues), corruption, and funding shortages threatening supply lines (Yuehan Railway, Yunnan-Burma road, Soviet route). He vividly describes life in Hankow under air raids, noting civilian panic, Japanese bombing accuracy, soaring prices, isolation, refugee problems, and a disconnect between civilian morale (celebrating imagined victories with firecrackers) and the grim military reality. The article concludes with observations on Chiang's use of Christian-like Sunday addresses incorporating Sun Yat-sen's ideology and his pragmatic system of monetary compensation for wounded soldiers.

For Korean colonial history, this article is interesting as it provided Korean readers under Japanese rule with a foreign correspondent's perspective on the Sino-Japanese war. It portrays both Chinese resilience (Chiang's resolve) and significant weaknesses, contrasting with Japanese military strength. Publishing such an assessment, including critiques of China's internal issues and acknowledgment of Japanese military advantages (like air superiority and accurate bombing), within a Korean magazine during Japanese colonization is noteworthy. Historians might find it useful for understanding how the war was presented to the colonized Korean populace, the potential nuances allowed under censorship (perhaps favoring perspectives that highlighted Chinese weakness), and the role of foreign reporting in shaping perceptions during that era.

死의 漢口에서 蔣介石 陣中 訪問記
						紐育 타임스 特派員 죤·간사
진중의 蔣介石은 신문 잡지의 특파원과는 좀처럼 맛나지 안는다. 그런데 엇든 까닭이든지 내가 요행 그를 맛나게 되여 그의 일류의 辨舌을 듯고 복장이 없는 흉중까지 였볼 수 있었다.
퍽 기분이 좋았든 날이였든지 그렇지 않으면 선전하기 위해서 한 수단이였든지는 모르나 었쨋든 蔣介石은 매우 열이 가득찬 어조로 장기 항전의 뜻을 이얘기하고 그 다음 내가 제출한 질문에 대답해 주었다.

그 날 나는 물결이 고요해진 양자강을 건너갓다. 한낮을 지난 양자강 물은 약간 황토색이였다. 지금 支那에서 蔣介石은 가장 강한 영웅으로 위대를 받고 있다. 정확히 말하면 蔣介石은 漢口에 있는 것이 아니고 강 건너 武昌이라는 데 살고 있다.
漢口는 支那의 씨카고라 한다. 강을 끼고 세 개의 시가 접합되여 있다. 여기서 북경과 漢口와를 연결 식히는 京漢 철로와 廣東과 武昌을＜63＞ 연락식히는 奧漢철로와의 연락을 양자강이 하는 셈이다.
前線으로부터 약 300리 후방에, 蔣介石은 본영을 짓고 지휘하고 있다. 때로는 패전에 견딜 수 없어서 前線 갓가히까지 나오는 일이 있는 모양이다.
蔣介石과 맛나기 전 나는 미리 질문할 것을 준비했다. 나는 안해와 같이 여러가지 공상을 하면서 그의 본영에 갓든 것이다.

제5. 支那군대의 활동을 현저히 둔하게 만드는 것은 支那人의 일대 결점인 부정한 행동이다.私腹을 채울여는 惡癖을 아직 곤치지 못했다.
제6. 군자금의 결핍이다. 전쟁의 결과 여하는 병기 군수품의 보급 여하에 달였다. 支那軍이원기있다고 하드래도 鐵砲와 화약이 없으면, 전쟁할 수 없는 것은 명백한 일이다. 군자금의 결핍, 일본군이 점령한 북부 諸港과 남방 諸港의 완전봉쇄 등은 支那에 대한 군수품공급에 일대 脅威를 주고 있다. 대체로 이상과 같은 여섰 가지 점에서 支那는 불리하다.

장래 粵漢철로가 완전 파괴된 경우를 상상해 보면 그 때 支那는 다음의 두 線에 生死之鍵을 두지 않으면 안 될 것이다. 제 1은 粵漢철로와 평행하여 건설되여 있는 자동차路인데＜66＞ 이것은 粵漢線 피해 여하로 역시 파괴될 위험이 있다고 보지 않으면 아니될 것이다.
이럴 경우 제 2선으로 예정되는 것이 雲南府로부터 佛領 인도의, 海防에 至하는 불란서철도이다. 그러나 漢口에서는 佛國이 일본의 返報的 폭격을 두려워하여 자국철도에 의한 무기 수송을 금지하리라는 소문이 유포되고 있다.

외국인 비평을 종합해 보면 대체 支那인은 평정을 넘어서 감각이 둔하지 안은가 생각된다. 한 지방에 생긴 일을 타 지방인은 일절 모르는 체 아무 늣낌도 없는 것 같이 생각된다. 하로는 蔣介石 고문인 도날드와 晝食을 하며 이야기하는데 맛 밖에서 기묘한 爆裂音이 들녀왔다. 도날드는 나를 보고 우스며 「군, 그것은 폭죽이요. 支那人의 葬式임에 틀님없소」 하였다.
그리자 폭음은 격렬하게도 사방에서 이러났다. 곧 나가보니 그것은 支那軍이 일본군을 익이였다. 하여 민중이 밋칠 듯 날뛰며 터지는 폭죽의 홍수였다. 이런 광경은 나와 같이 여하히 支那軍이 패퇴하고 있음을 실제로 아는 자에 대하여 무어라 표현할 수 업는 비참한 감정을 가슴속에 색이게 하는 것이였다. 이 폭죽의 대음향은 20대 가량의 비행기에 의한 폭격 이상의 것이였다.＜68＞
이 아무것도 모르는 시민의 전승 祝儀는 오후로부터 밤중까지 쉬지않고 폭음으로 천지는 찌저질 듯 하였다. 이 전쟁이 부산물로 생긴 문제는 피난민의 해결문제이리라. 漢口를 중심으로 한 약 천6백만인 이상의 피난민에게 먹을 것을 주고 직업은 주는 것이 긴요하다. 한 가지 안으로는 대규모의 완구제조공업을 이르켜 피난민에게 일을 주는 것이 엇덜까 한다.

蔣介石의 說
蔣介石은 메소지스트파의 크리스찬이다. 그리하여 그는 기독교에서 어든 경험을 가지고 교묘하게 인심을 收纜하고 있다. 이것은 宋일족의 영향이다. 매 일요일 아츰이면 漢口에는 특별예배식이 잇는뎨 약 600인 이상이 양자강을 건너 蔣介石의 대본영부근에 있는 예배당에서 집합한다. 여기서 蔣介石은 제 시간에 꼭 나와서 會衆에게 一禮하고 단상에 올은다. 군악대가 행진곡을 맛치면 일동은 탈모하고 정면에 걸닌 支那중흥의 祖 孫逸仙의 상을 향하여 세 번 禮한 다음 관리들이 모자를 쓰면 이윽코 蔣介石은 孫逸仙의 삼민주의의 일절을 낭독하는 뎨 蔣이 일차 낭독하면 會衆일동은 따더서 소리를 합하여 復誦한다. 똑 맡이 예슈교에서 복음서를 읽는 거와 같다.
이렇게 한 후에 3분간 黙禱한 다음 蔣介石의 연설이 시작되는데 어떤 때는 한 시간 또는 두 시간, 세 시간까지 갈 때도 있다.
이 蔣介石의 長설교가 있는 동안은 漢口의 각계 대표자들, 정부의 고관들, 군인 등 출석자 일동은 終始 기립하고 있는 것이다.
끝으로 蔣介石의 예민한 두뇌를 표시하는 일례가 있다. 그는 支那人이 금전을 생명보다 더 사랑한다는 것을 숙지하는 지라 부상자에게 주는 것을 정신적인 것보다 금전으로 한 일이 있다. 蔣介石의 이런 수단은 근대 軍史上 조곰 기발한 것으로 볼 수 있으리라.
부상병은 보통병 상처한 곳에 대하여 10불, 장교는 30불組와 50불組와의 두 계급이 있고 대장급은 100불이란다. 僞傷을 제조하여 가지고 오는 패가 적지 않음으로 眞僞의 감별에 前間이 걸닌다는 것도 支那然한 이야기다.
금년 1월 이래 이 부상의 총代價가 백70만불에 달한다.
廣東에서부터 오漢철로에 의하여 일본기의 폭격의 눈을 속이면서 때때로 화물을 滿載한 열차가 비밀히 도착한다. 이 화물은 아마 군수품뿐이 아닌 모양인 듯.＜69＞
