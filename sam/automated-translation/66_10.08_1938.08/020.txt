Magazine Title: 삼천리 제10권 제8호
Publication Date: 1938년 08월 01일
Article Title: 叅政權의 要望 (The Demand for Suffrage)
Author: Interviews with Ch'oe Rin, Pak Ch'un-gŭm, Han Sang-nyong
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Roundtable Discussion)

**The Demand for Suffrage**
**The Advancement of Local Autonomy: Ch'oe Rin, President of Maeil Sinbo**

Reporter: Regarding your resignation from the Chungch'uwŏn council and even stepping down from important posts in Ch'ŏndogyo to take up the presidency of the newspaper, there is speculation that since you have always harbored political aspirations, this signifies some preliminary "move." What are your thoughts?
Ch'oe Rin: I have indeed resigned from my Ch'ŏndogyo positions. A newspaper company is a place that must maintain the fairest possible stance, and I thought there might be misunderstandings if I continued to hold Ch'ŏndogyo posts, so after careful consideration, I decided to do so. For example, concerning the relationship between Ch'ŏndogyo and Buddhism, or Ch'ŏndogyo and Christianity, when criticizing from a third-party perspective, would it be right if, due to my being a Ch'ŏndogyo follower, I only observed matters from a Ch'ŏndogyo angle? However, this does not mean I have severed my religious faith. Ch'ŏndogyo is my life, so being a Ch'ŏndogyo follower remains the same, past and present. <30>
As for resigning from the Chungch'uwŏn, although there are members of the House of Peers who also serve as newspaper presidents, the nature of this is different. It was also done to transcend all angles and solely fulfill my duties as the head of this major newspaper company.

Reporter: Yes, I understand. You recently returned from a trip to Tokyo. Was this trip related to any current affairs or incidents?
Ch'oe: As I have taken on the responsibility of the company, I went to Tokyo for about two weeks for business purposes, including meeting and coordinating with various parties.

Reporter: How do you view the current situation?
Ch'oe: I believe it is a time when the Korean people must awaken significantly. The nation is currently facing an extraordinary crisis (非常之秋), standing at a historical juncture between prosperity and decline. Young men go to the battlefield offering their lives, while the elderly and children are busy serving (奉公) on the home front (銃後). In such times, should the 23 million people of Korea sit idly by without great fervor or significant service? I know this lukewarm attitude must be corrected. Thus, I know we must become participants in the great task of reviving Greater East Asia for the Empire.

Reporter: Are you saying that Koreans lack ideals and conviction?
Ch'oe: That's right. Firstly, conviction is lacking. Look even at the youth of the enemy nation, China (支那); because they have firm conviction, they dash onto the battlefield carrying guns, knowing they might die, don't they? Isn't it because they have the firm conviction that "Doing this is right. It is good to sacrifice one's life for this righteous cause" that the youth of the mainland are so brave? However, it seems Koreans lack this conviction. Koreans probably have ideals, too. They likely have splendid ideals. Whether high or low, they must have ideals, but it seems they embrace self-righteous ideals unrelated to today's reality. For example, they might say, "By this time next year, such-and-such will be achieved," but to get to that time next year, one must eat and live starting from today—in other words, one must still be alive to greet that day next year, right? But they don't keep in mind the various conditions of this reality called "today." So, wouldn't it be more correct to call such ideals mere fantasies?

Reporter: Yes, I understand. But isn't that rather a problem of leadership? Wouldn't conviction that didn't exist before arise, and ideals that didn't exist before emerge, if true leaders of the populace set forth cultural and political goals and guided the masses in that direction, <31> training and organizing them?
Ch'oe: Right. That is correct. However, looking around Korean society, there are no leaders to pull these masses. Look. People who have money and can feed themselves dislike meddling in worldly affairs, so they build walls and live reclusive lives of self-righteousness. The rest of the masses drift aimlessly, seemingly without any will.
Even so, in the past, whether good or bad, there were various organized forces that fought, clashed, and debated fiercely. Friction arises amidst fighting, clashing, and debating. Heat is generated from this rubbing and striking friction. Wouldn't this heat be the driving force for social growth? But now, there is no friction, no heat; only the large mass of 23 million exists without energy, without conviction.
I know this is all the result of not encountering proper leaders.

Reporter: Therefore?
Ch'oe: Therefore, we must grasp the times and advance wholeheartedly along the path of our nation's state policy (國策).

Reporter: This is a different matter, but just to understand your thoughts, how do you view the "Korean Language Movement," which is currently the biggest issue?
Ch'oe: The other day, while dining with Governor-General Nam (南 총독), His Excellency asked about this, and I answered thus:
"The Korean language and Korean culture are in an inseparable (不可分離) relationship. To eliminate the Korean language now means eliminating Korea's unique culture. Not only would that be difficult to achieve, but isn't it the best policy to preserve Korean culture as it is for posterity? Accordingly, wouldn't it be right to allow the Korean language to continue its use?"

Reporter: What did the Governor-General say to that?
Ch'oe: He said he thought so too, adding, "Because we encourage the spread of the national language [Japanese], the public seems to mistakenly believe that, conversely, the Korean language is being eliminated, but there is absolutely no such intention." One might think that if the national language flourishes, the Korean language will naturally fall into disuse, but that would be a matter of degree.

**Views on the Suffrage Issue**

Reporter: Did you see that in the recent plenary session of the House of Peers, Baron Sakatani (阪谷 남작) (a top executive of the Chūō Chōsen Kyōkai) publicly stated that at this juncture of reforming the military and education systems, Korean populace should also be granted suffrage, and in response, Prime Minister Konoe (近衛 수상) <32> abandoned the attitude of past government authorities who dismissed it with a mere formal phrase (一片), expressing sincere agreement, only stating that the timing of implementation requires further consideration?
Ch'oe: Yes, I am aware.

Reporter: Are you also aware that in the House of Representatives, Representative Pak Ch'un-gŭm (朴春琴 代議士) gained the cooperation of representatives from various political parties, introduced this issue, and brought it to the point of "petition adoption"?
Ch'oe: Yes, I am aware.

Reporter: Regarding that, it seems this is precisely the time to advance the suffrage movement, which some groups have been advocating for years (年來). What is your view?
Ch'oe: The suffrage movement historically has two streams. One is the request to implement the "universal suffrage law" in Korea, and the other is for Korea to have its own unique legislative assembly. Regarding the former, the "Diet Reform Deliberation Council" (議會制度改革審議會) is currently deliberating, chaired by Mr. Mizuno (水野씨), who was formerly the Governor-General for Political Affairs in Korea. This council fundamentally aims to reform the election law of the current bicameral system to truly gather the nation's best talents. As far as I know, proposals such as extending the electoral districts to Korea and Taiwan were never even considered from the beginning.

Reporter: Depending on the movement's efforts, isn't this juncture of fundamentally reforming the parliamentary system a time when it could be reformed in a positive direction?
Ch'oe: The main focus of this reform is not to expand the scope of elections geographically, meaning it's not a political issue, but rather to reform the election system administratively and technically within the existing legislative framework. For example, basing Diet member elections on workplaces to elect occupational representatives, and so on. Therefore, discussing Korean-elected representatives (代議士) was not on the table from the start.

Reporter: Then what about the latter option, having a unique legislative assembly for Korea?
Ch'oe: Since the local autonomy system is gradually advancing step by step, we should wait and see a bit longer.

Reporter: At this time of reforming the military service system, shouldn't we take another step forward and grant suffrage, so that Koreans, truly standing on equal footing in the spirit of 'Naisen Ittai' (內鮮一體 - Japan and Korea as one body), can also advance along the path of national policy?
Ch'oe: In this historical stage of administration, doing so would be right, but the timing for the suffrage movement seems a bit different. <33>

**Let's Send Representatives to the House of Representatives: Representative Pak Ch'un-gŭm (朴春琴)**

Reporter: We are well aware through the Imperial Diet records and newspapers that you have been advocating in the House of Representatives for the past 5-6 years for suffrage to be granted to Korea. Is your assertion asking for the mainland's House of Representatives election law to be implemented in Korea? Or is it an assertion asking for a separate, unique assembly to be established in Korea? Please clarify this point.
Pak Ch'un-gŭm: Actually, I intended to strongly advocate my views on this suffrage movement during a lecture meeting held recently at the Pumin'gwan (府民舘) in Seoul, but due to various circumstances, I couldn't speak. Generally, thanks to Governor-General Nam's wise decision, the volunteer soldier system, as a precursor to compulsory military service, has been implemented in Korea, and about 400 young men have marched off to the military gate. Also, the 'Naisen Ittai' education ordinance has been implemented, and it is truly joyous that Koreans are gradually acquiring the duties and rights they should possess as citizens of the Japanese nation. Thus, the remaining major issue for all of us is solely the acquisition of this civic right. Although the local autonomy system was granted to Korea over ten years ago, I know that the current times are not such that we should remain at this stage. Therefore, the biggest issue going forward is obtaining "suffrage."

Reporter: Yes, I understand. What is the path to obtaining "suffrage"?
Pak: Anyone with common sense would judge that the goal would naturally be to take a step further from the current provincial, prefectural, and township deliberative bodies to establish a Korean Assembly. Another path is to implement the Imperial Diet, specifically the House of Representatives election law, in Korea, send a large number of Korean-elected representatives to the House of Representatives, and thereby participate <34> in national politics. Frankly speaking, I know that establishing a Korean Assembly is something that cannot be realized, no matter how much we desire it. Looking at examples from various colonies, such movements inevitably contain a high risk of leading towards ethnic self-governance, so I know the government is unlikely to permit (허하지) an autonomous assembly. Therefore, from the beginning, my goal has been to implement the House of Representatives election law in Korea so that Koreans can also gain the right to elect and be elected as members of the National Diet. A few years ago, there was a movement for a Taiwan Assembly in Taiwan; judging from the process when it was debated in the House of Representatives, I know that a movement for establishing a Korean Assembly would absolutely not succeed.

Reporter: So, if the path is through the House of Representatives, what steps should be taken?
Pak: Extending the election law to Korea would constitute a major domestic reform, so I don't think it can be implemented easily, like tomorrow or the day after (래일). Therefore, as a temporary measure, I proposed in the recent deliberation council that each province elect one mainland Japanese and one Korean, totaling 26 representatives (代議士) from all of Korea (全鮮), and send them to the House of Representatives.

Reporter: If the current mainland election law were extended to Korea, would it be the universal suffrage system as is? If so, considering the population ratio, with only 500,000-600,000 mainland Japanese compared to over 22 million Koreans, wouldn't there be a risk of imbalance between mainlanders and Koreans in elections?
Pak: If the current election law's universal suffrage system were implemented in Korea as is, the result would indeed be as you described. Also, due to differences in education levels and living standards, applying the mainland's universal suffrage system directly to Korea would likely cause problems (無理).

Reporter: In that case, do you have a viable plan that is most appropriate for the current situation?
Pak: I have investigated the eligible voters, those with suffrage rights, within Korea (鮮內) for several years. If we consider taxpayers paying 10 yen or more, the number of mainland Japanese (內地人) is greater (만고) than Koreans throughout all of Korea (全道). If we set the eligibility at taxpayers paying 5 yen or more, the number of Koreans is slightly higher, but overall, the number of mainland Japanese and Korean voters becomes roughly equal (비등비등하게 되여요). Therefore, if the House of Representatives election law were implemented in Korea, basing the election on taxpayers paying 5 yen or more seems like it would result in the most ideal election, with roughly half mainland Japanese and half Korean representatives (代議士).

Reporter: As a result (말미아마) of you holding a seat in the House of Representatives, what political effects have you brought about in the past? Please mention those related to Korea as much as possible. <35>
Pak: Some years ago, when the Rice Control Law was implemented, there was an attempt to block the import (이입) of rice from outside territories (外地米), namely Taiwanese or Korean rice, into the mainland. At that time, Japan had already established a rice production increase plan in Korea, making increased production the top priority for all Korean fields. Yet, the following year, perhaps because the mainland had a bountiful harvest and could manage without rice from outside territories, they tried to restrict the import of Korean rice. Deeply feeling the disadvantage for Korea, which relies on rice for its livelihood, I strove to have the import restrictions abolished and succeeded. I strongly argued (痛論) that it was unacceptable from a national policy perspective.

Reporter: And then?
Pak: There were various other matters, but I will refrain from listing them all. However, looking at recent events, Koreans, in the context of the Sino-Japanese Incident, have truly poured out all their fervor as subjects of the Empire—making patriotic donations, sending off soldiers heading to the front, engaging in home front service, etc. I have worked hard to widely publicize (널니 알니기에 힘써서) these facts, so that the central government, as well as (위시하아) many mainland figures, have come to recognize Korea much better and are sincerely (衷心) grateful for the devotion (赤誠) of the Korean people.

Reporter: This is a different topic, but couldn't more Koreans residing in the mainland be sent (드려보낼 수없겠어요) to the House of Representatives? There must be large numbers of Koreans concentrated in places like Osaka (大阪), Nagoya (名古屋), Shimonoseki (下關), and by now, many must have settled for years and gained economic and social standing. Couldn't several people run for office?
Pak: Currently, about 800,000 Koreans live in the mainland, but not many among them hold suffrage rights, making it difficult to get elected solely based on Korean votes. I also ran in Tokyo's 4th district, and looking at the votes I received, two-thirds were from mainland Japanese. However, in any case, as the years go by, many Korean representatives will likely emerge from places like Tokyo and Osaka.

Reporter: What if you could secure enough seats to form a negotiating bloc (교섭 단체)?
Pak: Indeed. If we could just get 25 representatives, we could become a negotiating bloc, and our Korean party could be as active as the Seiyūkai (政友會) or the Minseitō (민정당)!

Reporter: Since you are currently a lonely presence in the House of Representatives, what tactics do you employ?
Pak: I don't necessarily expect my assertions to pass. Parliamentary politics is fundamentally majority politics, so how could I, as a lone soldier with weak forces (孤軍弱勢), <36> pass the kind of bills and budget proposals I envision? However, I seize every opportunity to make the opinions of the Korean people widely known on the floor of the Diet, putting all my effort into speaking out frequently.

Reporter: What are your future hopes?
Pak: Rather than my personal political hopes, it is the hope of the Korean people that Koreans should also become cabinet ministers. I also believe the opportunity for us to become ministers and even Prime Minister will come. Wouldn't it be a joyous thing for the nation if, among the dozen or so cabinet ministers, at least one seat were occupied by a Korean, participating in shaping the Empire's politics?
Didn't Hitler, a man from the Austrian federation, become the Chancellor (宰相) of Germany? Believing that such an opportunity will come for us too, I intend to continue striving forward. <37>

**Demanding Reform of the Provincial System (道制)**
**Han Sang-nyong (韓相龍)**

Reporter: Today, with the implementation of military service and co-education (共學制), it seems that the issue capturing the attention of both policymakers and the informed public (식자치) is suffrage. What are your thoughts on this?
Han Sang-nyong: Just this evening, at a dinner hosted by Army Commander Koiso (小磯 군사령관) for Commander Katsuki (香月 사령관) returning from North China (北支), I heard from Commander Katsuki. He expressed gratitude, saying that truly, during this incident, the loyalty and patriotism (충군 애국) of us Koreans were fervent. He especially expressed deep gratitude for the efforts of Korean youth working together as one with mainland Japanese behind the lines. At this time when our consciousness as citizens of the Empire is rising, I believe it is a time for us (우러들은) to strive even harder (일층) for the prosperity of our nation's fortune.
Now, regarding the suffrage issue you mentioned, <37> this suffrage movement, specifically the Kokumin Kyōkai (국민협회) line of movement, has been ongoing for over 10 years, and petitions have been adopted in the House of Representatives several times already. Both the petitioning Koreans and the government side know that the movement is appropriate. Therefore, while our hope is that it would be granted immediately, even today, it seems the conditions for implementation are not yet ripe.

Reporter: What have you usually thought? Even within suffrage, do you mean extending the House of Representatives election law to Korea, or establishing a separate assembly for Korea? What is your inclination?
Han: I believe that establishing a separate assembly involves various difficulties. Therefore, from the standpoint of complete 'Naisen Ittai', I hope that in the future, Koreans will possess the same right to vote in House of Representatives elections as mainland Japanese living in Honshū (本州).

Reporter: Then, changing the subject, you have been involved in local autonomy for many years as a member of the Kyŏnggi Provincial Council (京畿道會議員). How do you view the current local autonomy system?
Han: The local autonomy system was implemented in Taishō 8 (1919) during Governor-General Saitō's (齊藤총독) era, and although it has been revised (그동안) and is operating well now, I wish it could be further improved and advanced.

Reporter: How, specifically?
Han: It's embarrassing (게면적으나) for me to say this as an officially appointed provincial councilor (官選道議), but generally, abolishing official appointments (官選) would be more fitting with the principle of respecting popular will and also align with the current times. Take Kyŏnggi Province (경기도) for instance; out of 47 or 48 council members, about 12 are officially appointed.

Reporter: And what else?
Han: Currently, the chairman is the Provincial Governor. This should also be revised so that the chairman is elected from among the council members through mutual recommendation. It would be good to do it like the prefectural assembly (府縣會) system in the mainland.

Reporter: In that case, if both appointed members and the chairmanship come from the private sector, wouldn't there be a concern that budget deliberations and decisions might excessively reflect the people's will?
Han: That's not right. The final executive power must remain with the Provincial Governor for governance to proceed according to the government's policy; I am not suggesting preventing that. And only after the Provincial Council (道會) becomes an institution fully reflecting the popular will can the ideal politics of government-people cooperation be achieved.

Reporter: What is the total local expenditure budget decided by the Provincial Councils across all of Korea (全조선) currently? <38>
Han: Kyŏnggi Province has 12-13 million yen annually, but smaller provinces might have around 4-5 million yen, so perhaps it doesn't quite reach 100 million yen, maybe around 80 million yen.

Reporter: What else regarding the improvement of the Provincial Council system?
Han: I wish the election method could be changed. Instead of the current indirect (two-tiered) election system, it should become a direct election system. However, since there are still places where the level of civic knowledge (民度) is low—meaning many people cannot even write their own names and are ignorant of worldly affairs—I am not suggesting immediate elections by them. But reform of the current indirect election system is necessary.
There's a story that Governor-General Nam, while inspecting somewhere in Hamgyŏng Province (함경도), asked an old woman in a farmhouse, "Our country is now fighting China (支那), won't you come out and help?" The old woman replied, "What concern is it of Koreans if Japan and China are fighting?" So the Governor-General explained again,
"Japan and Korea merged and became one country long ago."
Only then did the old woman say,
"Ah, is that so? Then Koreans must help our country."
As this story suggests, if you actually go to the countryside, in remote mountain valleys (山谷僻地), there are still ignorant people unaware even of the fact of annexation. Educating them, providing schooling, and awakening their consciousness is urgent. With the populace at this level, how could something like direct elections be implemented immediately?

Reporter: Recently, there have been reports in newspapers about an increase in Imperially appointed (勅選議員) Korean members to the House of Peers.
Han: Even if it's just one more person, having more seats in the House of Peers means that Korean public sentiment and Korean affairs will be reflected that much more (구만치) in the Diet, so it is something we greatly hope for.

Reporter: Also, people like Mr. Pak Ch'un-gŭm strongly advocate that Koreans should join the ranks of cabinet ministers, and that such a time should come quickly.
Han: These are all good hopes. The desire for this to happen as soon as possible (하로급히) is second to none.
In conclusion, we Koreans must truly become one with mainland Japanese ('Naisen Ittai') and devote all our sincerity and effort (全誠力) to the nation. By doing so, we must quickly advance to the same political and social standing as mainland Japanese. <39>

**Summary**

This article presents interviews with three prominent Korean figures in 1938 – Ch'oe Rin, Pak Ch'un-gŭm, and Han Sang-nyong – discussing the issue of suffrage (참정권) for Koreans under Japanese colonial rule. Ch'oe Rin emphasizes the need for Koreans to develop conviction and participate in the "Greater East Asia" project, while also defending the preservation of Korean language and culture. Pak Ch'un-gŭm, a member of the Japanese House of Representatives, argues for extending suffrage for the House of Representatives to Korea (initially with property qualifications to balance Japanese and Korean voters) rather than creating a separate Korean assembly, viewing the latter as unrealistic. Han Sang-nyong supports suffrage through the House of Representatives and advocates for reforms to the existing Provincial Council system, such as abolishing appointed members and introducing direct elections, albeit cautiously due to perceived low civic awareness in rural areas. All three express hope for greater Korean political participation, including cabinet positions, within the framework of the Japanese Empire and 'Naisen Ittai'.

For historians, this article is highly valuable as it captures the complex perspectives of elite Koreans navigating colonial assimilation policies and political disenfranchisement. It reveals internal debates on the strategy for gaining political rights (separate assembly vs. representation in the Diet), the rationales used to justify cooperation with Japanese imperial goals (like the war effort), and the blend of accommodationist rhetoric with assertions of Korean identity (language preservation) and demands for equality (suffrage, ministerial posts). The differing views on the readiness of the Korean populace for direct democracy and the specific proposals for suffrage implementation (like Pak's tax-based qualification) offer concrete insights into the political thought of collaborating elites during this period.

記者. 이번에 중추원 참의를 그만두시고 천도교의 중직조차 내여노코 신문 사장으로 출마한데 대하야 항간에는 선생이 과거 정치 운동에 항상 뜻을 가지고 게섯는니만치 무삼 「움지김」의 전제라고 보는데 엇더함니까?
崔麟. 천도교의 직책은 그만두었음니다. 그것은 신문사라 하면 가장 공정한 입장을 직혀 나가야 할 곳인데 천도교의 직책을 그양 가지고 있으면 세상에서 오해할 점도 있을 것 가태서 신중히 생각한 끝에 그리한 것이외다. 가령 천도교라 하면 천도교 대 불교 관계라든지 또 천도교 대 기독교 관계 라지 제3자의 입장에 서서 비판할 때에 내가 천도교도인 관계로 해서 천도교란 한 각도에서만 사물을 관찰하는 것 같은 점이 있어야 쓰겠음니까. 그러타고 종교적 신앙까지를 단절한 것은 아니외다. 천도교란 나의 생명이니까 한 천도교도로 있음은 전이나 금일이나 마천가지외다.＜30＞
그리고 중추원을 그만둔 것은 귀족원 의원이면서 신문사장된 이도 있기는 하나 그와 이것은 성질이 다르니까 亦 모든 각도를 초월하야 오직 이 큰 신문사 책임자로서의 직책을 다할가 하야 그리함이 있어요.
記者. 네. 알겠음니다. 그런데 이번에 동경 가섯다 오섯스나 그는 무삼 시국 사단과 관련된 여행이엇슴니까.
崔. 이번에 社의 책임을 맛게 되었으니까 동경 각 방면에 인사도 하고 타협도 할 용건이 있어 사무와 함께 약 2주일 작정으로 갓다 온 것이지요.
記者. 지금의 시국에 대하야 엇더케 보서요.
崔. 조선 사람으로서 많이 각성하야 할 때라고 생각하여요. 국가는 지금 非常之秋를 당하야 흥망의 역사적 계단에 처하고 있어 장정들은 전장에 나가 목숨을 내 바치고 老父와 유아는 銃後에서 奉公하기에 분주한 이러한 때에 처하야 조선 사람 2천 3백만은 그리 큰 열도 없고 그리 큰 奉公도 없이 앉어 있어야 쓰겟서요. 나는 이 不熱不冷한 이 태도를 고처야 할 줄 아러요. 그래서 大亞細亞 부흥의 제국의 이 큰 사업을 이루는 한 분자가 되여야 할 줄 아러요.
記者. 조선 사람에게 이상과 신념이 부족하다는 말슴임니까.
崔. 그러치요. 첫재 신념이 부족해요. 하다못해 적국인 支那의 청년을 보아도 그네들에게는 굳은 신념이 있으니까 죽을 줄 알면서도 총을 메고 전장으로 내닷지 안슴니까. 「이리하는 것이 올타. 이 올흔 일을 위하연 목숨을 바처도 조타」하는 그런 굳은 신념이 있으니까 내지인 청년들이 저렇듯 용감한 것이 아님니까. 그런데 조선 사람에게는 이런 신념이 부족한 드시 생각됨니다. 또 조선 사람에게도 이상이야 있겠지요. 훌륭한 이상이야 있을 것이겠지요. 높으나 나즈나깐에 이상이야 있을 것이나 그러나 그 이상은 금일의 현실과는 아모 관련이 없는 독선적 이상을 안고 있는 듯해요. 例하자면 明年 이때는 이러이러한 일이 성취되리라 하지만 明年 그때까지 가자면 오늘부터 먹고 사라가야 다시 말하면 목숨이 부터 있고야 明年 이 날 이때를 마지할 것 아니겠어요. 그런데 「오늘」이란 이 현실적 諸 조건을 염두에 두지 안슴니다 그려. 그러니까 이러한 이상은 차라리 공상이라 함이 올치 안을가요.
記者. 네. 알겠음니다. 그러나 그것은 오히려 지도자의 문제가 아니겠음니까. 민중의 잠다운 지도자가 엇던 문화적 정치적 목표를 내걸고 대중을 그 방향으로 인도하면서＜31＞ 훈련하고 단속하고 하는 속에서 없었든 신념도 새로 생기겠고 없었든 이상도 새로 생길 것이 아니겠어요?
崔. 올치요. 그러하지요. 그런데 조선 사회를 둘너볼 적에 이 대중을 끄을 지도자가 없음니다 그려. 자아 보십시요. 돈이나 있어 제 먹을 것 있는 사람들은 이러쿵저러쿵 세상에 나비치기를 애당초에 싫여하니까 담을 쌓고 저만 독선하는 은돈 생활을 하고 있지요. 그 이외의 대중들은 아모 의사도 없는 듯 이리저리 방향 없이 구을지요.
그래도 전날에는 조튼 납부든 간에 결성된 여러 세력이 있어 서로 싸우고 부드치고 격론하였지요. 싸우고 부드치고 격론하는 사이에 마찰이 생기지요. 이 부비고 따리고 하는 마찰하는 사이에 열이 생기지요. 이 열이 사회 성장의 원동력이 아니되겟슴니까. 그런데 지금은 아모 마찰도 열도 없이 오직 2천3백만이란 큰 덩어리가 기운 없이 신념 업시 있음니다.
이것은 다 올흔 지도자를 맛나지 못한 결과인 줄 아러요.
記者. 그러니까?
崔. 그러니까 우리들은 시대를 달관하고 我國의 국책의 선에 향하야 一意 전심 나아가야 하지요.
記者. 이것은 딴 문제외다만은 다만 선생의 심경을 알고저 하는 말슴임니다만은 가령 시하의 가장 큰 문제인 「조선어 운동」에 대하야 엇더케 보서요.
崔. 전일에 南 총독과 저녁을 함께 하면서 총독께서도 이 마슴을 뭇길래 나는 이러케 대답했지요.
「조선말과 조선 문화는 不可分離의 관계에 있는데 이제 만일 조선말을 없샌다는 것은 조선의 고유 문화를 없샌다는 것으로 그것이 될 리도 어럽겠지만 조선 문화를 그대로 기리 보존케 함이 상책이 아닌가고. 따라서 조선어도 그대로 사명케 함이 올치 않겠는가고 말슴했지요」
記者. 그 말슴에 총독께서는 무에라 합디까.
崔. 자기도 그러케 생각한다고 그러면서 「국어 보급을 장려한다하니까 그 반면에 조선어는 업새는 줄로 일반은 잘못 오해하고 있는 모양이나 절대로 그럴 의사는 없노라」고 말슴하더군요. 국어 全盛하면 저절로 조선어는 전폐되리라고 생각할 수는 있으나 그것은 정도 문제이겠지요.

참정권 문제에 대한 견해
記者. 지난 번 귀족원 본회의에서 阪谷 남작 (중앙조선협회 최고간부)이 兵學兩制度를 일신하는 이 마당에 조선 민중에게 참정권을 마저 주라고 공언하였고 여기 대하야 近衛 수상이＜32＞ 또한 종래의 정부 당국자가 一片외 형식상 언사로 집어치우든 그런 태도를 버리고 진정으로 매우 동감인 뜻을 표하나 다만 실시의 시기에 대하야 좀 더 고려하여야 하겠다는 답변을 한 것을 보섰음니까.
崔. 네 알었어요.
記者. 또 중의원에서 朴春琴 代議士가 여러 정당 代議士의 협력을 어더 이 문제를 상정 「청원 채택」에까지 이르게 한 사실을 아섰음니까.
崔. 네 알었어요.
記者. 거기 대해서, 年來로 일부 단체에서 주장해 오든 참정권의 운동은 이 시기야말로 전개시킬 때라고 보여지는 터인데 선생의 견해는 어떠하서요.
崔. 참정권 운동은 유래 두 갈래가 있지요. 그 하나는 「보통 선거법」을 조선에 시행하여 달나 하는 것이요 또 다른 하나는 조선은 조선만의 독특한 입법 의회를 가지자는 것인데 전자에 대하야 말슴한다면 지금 「의회제도 개혁 심의회」가 있어 예전 조선의 정무총감으로 있든 水野씨가 위원장이 되여 심리하는 중인데 이것은 원칙적으로 현재의 兩院 제도의 선거법을 개정하야 진실로 국가의 선량을 모으자는 데서 나온 것으로 선거의 지역을 조선, 대만에까지 연장하자는 등의 설은 애당초부터 나오지 못했을 것으로 아는 바이야요.
記者. 그야 운동의 여하에 딸아서는 이 의회 제도를 근본적으로 개혁하는 이 마당에 있어 얼마든지 조흔 길로 개혁할소잇슬나 시기가 아니리까.

衆議院에 代議士를 보내자 代議士 朴春琴

記者. 중의원에서 귀하가 5, 6년래로 참정권을 조선에도 부여하여 달라고 부르짓든 일은 제국 의회의 의사록이나 신문지상으로 잘 알었읍니다. 그런데 귀하의 주장은 내지의 중의원 선거법을 조선에다가 시행하여 달라는 말인지요. 또는 조선에는 조선 독특의 별개 의회를 설치하여 달란 주장인지요. 그 점을 분명히 말슴하여 주서요.
