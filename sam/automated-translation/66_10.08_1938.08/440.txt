**Body Text Translation**

Scenery of Haeinsa Temple
						Na Hyesŏk

At the recommendation of a close friend, I came in the spring and ended up spending the whole summer at Haeinsa Temple. Taking the Kyŏngbu Line and getting off at Taegu, one boards a car bound for Haeinsa at the automobile depot in front of the station. After passing through places like Koryŏng and Yaro, in about 3 hours, one gets off at the entrance to Hongnyudong Valley.

At the entrance to Hongnyudong, on the stone wall to the right, is engraved the poem "Hongnyudong" by the famous figure in our history, Teacher Ch'oe Ko-un (Ch'oe Ch'i-wŏn):
"Rushing madly, layers of roaring peaks resound,
Human voices hard to discern even close by.
Always fearing the sounds of right and wrong reach my ears,
Thus I bid the flowing water deafen the entire mountain."
And on the left side by the stream stands Teacher Ko-un's Nongsan Pavilion (聾山亭), and in front of it, a stone monument inscribed with:
"Teacher Ko-un's Place of worldly escape (孤雲 先生 遯世地)"
To the left, high up, is Teacher Ko-un's shrine.
Hongnyudong is truly a fairyland beyond the dust of the world. The majestic sound of water flowing leisurely between rocks and stones, falling upon the high stone platform before Nongsan Pavilion, the lush trees—it cools the heart and clears the head.

Having the luggage carried, I followed behind, with a walking stick as my companion, strolling leisurely along the 5-ri (approx. 2 km) long valley. As the dreary rain that had fallen for days cleared up, the spring sky was not just clear; green sprouts dotted every branch, and the scent of grass wafted fragrantly. Mountains wind, waters curve; waters curve, mountains wind. Every bend in the water reveals its beauty, and every turn of the mountain shows its brightness. Just when you think the mountains end, another appears; just when you think the water ceases, more water flows. The more water, the less tiresome it feels; the more mountain turns, the more one wants to go on. Before I knew it, I reached Ongnyu Pavilion (玉流亭). This pavilion was said to have been built by the monk Hwan'gyŏng (幻鏡). Inside and outside the pavilion <231> hang numerous plaques from famous figures, both domestic and foreign. After resting there briefly, I came out again, passed through the Saja Gate (Lion Gate), entered the forest, and followed the winding path, catching my breath as I climbed the hill. Then, a tin roof, the most unsightly thing in the mountains, appeared. This was the Hongdo Inn (紅濤旅館), the designated inn for Haeinsa. I requested a room, unpacked my belongings, and then wandered around the first and second floors of the inn. The facilities were surprisingly well-equipped, almost unseen even in cities, and it was said they could accommodate 200-300 guests when full. The meals and other services were astonishing. After spending a tiring night, I set out for a morning walk to Haeinsa Temple. Entering through the Hongnang Gate, a magnificent ancient Buddhist temple complex, hidden among the trees, came into view. This was Haeinsa.

There are four great temples in Korean Buddhism: T'ongdosa Temple, the Ilch'al (佚刹) on Nojwisan Mountain; Songgwangsa Temple, the Sŭngch'al (僧刹) on Chogyesan Mountain; Pŏmŏsa Temple, the Sŏnch'al (禪刹) on Kŭmjŏngsan Mountain; and Haeinsa Temple, the Pŏpch'al (法刹) on Kayasan Mountain. Among these, Haeinsa is not only a famous temple but also the head temple of the Dharma (法之宗家) and houses the Tripitaka Koreana woodblocks, the eighty thousand Tripitaka, a world-renowned treasure, in the Great Treasury Scripture Hall (大藏經閣).

The legend of Haeinsa's founding goes like this: The venerable Indian high monk Dhyānabhadra (提納博幸 尊者 指空선사), while spreading Buddhism and enlightening beings in Tang China, once traveled throughout Korea. When passing the site of Haeinsa on Kayasan Mountain, he prophesied that a Buddhist temple (梵刹) would be built here, named Haeinsa, becoming a great field of blessings (大福 田地) in the Dharma realm. To be used during Haeinsa's construction, he cast 3,000 iron roof tiles (鐵瓦) and buried them in a pond. Later, during the reign of King Aejang, the 40th king of Silla, about 1,200 years ago, two Silla high monks, Sunŭng and I-jŏng, dharma grandsons of Ŭisang Chosa (義湘祖師), the founder of the Hwaŏm School (華嚴宗主), traveled tens of thousands of ri over land and sea to Tang China to meet the venerable Chigong (指空). However, the master had already entered nirvana long ago, and his remains were enshrined in a stupa. Before entering nirvana, the master left a will for his disciples, saying:
"After my nirvana, two novices (沙彌, sāmaṇera in Sanskrit, translated into Chinese as 息慈, meaning 'ceasing evil and acting compassionately'; refers to young monks under 20) named Sunŭng and I-jŏng will come from Chosŏn. When they arrive, give them this testament."
As prophesied, Sunŭng and I-jŏng arrived. The disciples relayed the master's final words and handed over the testament. However, the two monks, Sunŭng and I-jŏng, were not satisfied with just the testament and said:
"The master, having transcended the state of ordinary beings (凡夫), resides in the realm of the Dharma body (法身 境上), beyond birth, death, coming, and going, eternally abiding and indestructible. With our utmost sincerity, we shall behold the master's Dharma body within the stupa."
Then, the two monks clasped their hands, knelt before the stupa, entered meditation (入定), and for seven days and seven nights <232> practiced diligently without drinking or eating. On the seventh day, the stupa door opened by itself. Adorned with bright light within the Prajñā treasury (般若藏中), neither cold nor hot, the form of the master appeared, seated cross-legged on the lion throne (獅子座), beckoning them with his hand to enter. The two monks, filled with joy, entered and paid respects according to the Dharma. The master said:
"Is your devotion truly so great?"
He praised them greatly, then showed them the previous testament again, instructing them on the founding of Haeinsa. He offered sweet dew tea (甘露茶) to the two monks. After drinking it, their bodies, withered from seven days and nights without food or drink, were instantly restored. They bowed in gratitude to the master and exited the hall. The master's disciples had prepared medicinal herbs, but the two monks, feeling refreshed and full of vitality from the sweet dew tea, said the medicine was unnecessary. The disciples were filled with even greater reverence and praised them ceaselessly. Following the testament's instructions, the two monks returned to Chosŏn, entered Kayasan Mountain, established earthen huts (土窟 - the sites of the two monks' huts still exist behind the current Kŭngnakchŏn Hall), and awaited the right time through meditative play (入定 遊戲). It is said that while the two monks were in meditation, a strange radiance emanated from the tops of their heads, stretching into the void, displaying an imposing presence (儼然). At that time, King Aejang's queen was seriously ill. Despite summoning famous physicians from all over the land, her condition showed no improvement, and gloom filled the royal court. A certain scholar advised, "This grave illness cannot be cured without borrowing the power of a Taoist master (道士). Please find a Taoist master to treat the queen's illness." The king followed this advice and ordered royal envoys (금부도사 - officials who directly summoned people by royal order) to the eight provinces to search for a Taoist master. Receiving the royal command, a royal envoy reached Wŏlgwang-ri (月光里 - this place is still called Wŏlgwang-ri today; coming to Haeinsa by car, about 10 ri from Yaro, there is a bridge marked Wŏlgwangsu (月光樹), and across that bridge in the middle of the fields is an ancient stupa, said to be where Prince Wŏlgwang built Wŏlgwangsa Temple and studied), about 20 ri below Kayasan. Suddenly, a fox appeared and led the way. The royal envoy followed the fox into the forests of Kayasan, passed through the nine bends of Hongnyudong Valley, and arrived at a mysterious fairyland of bright mountains and beautiful waters. The fox vanished, leaving the royal envoy alone to look around. (The place where the fox disappeared is called Fox Rock Street (여호 바위 거리), and traces of that old path still remain.) The royal envoy thought, "Surely a Taoist master must be here for a spirit (神明) to guide me," and rejoiced, searching the surroundings. He met a woodcutter (樵夫) and asked if there was a Taoist master nearby. The woodcutter replied:
"Up there, two Taoist masters are sitting and studying."
The envoy was delighted and went to the location. There, the two great masters, Sunŭng <233> and I-jŏng, were meditating and studying. Auspicious light (祥瑞의 光明) radiated from the tops of their heads into the air. Feeling naturally awed by their imposing presence (儼然한 기품), he bowed his head in respect, conveyed the king's order, and pleaded with them to go to the royal palace to tend to the queen's illness. The two masters said there was no need to go to the palace. They took out the five-colored thread (五色糸 - Tang silk thread) that Master Chigong had given them along with the testament when they were in Tang China, saying, "Tie one end of this thread to the queen's wrist and the other end to the old tree that should be in front of the palace. Then the queen's illness will be cured." The royal envoy returned to the palace, reported this to the king, and followed the two masters' instructions. Miraculously, the queen's sickness transferred through the five-colored thread to the large old tree in front of the palace. The tree withered and died on the spot, while the queen's grave illness was immediately cured. King Aejang was overjoyed and personally went to Kayasan Mountain. When he asked the two masters, Sunŭng and I-jŏng, for their wish, they replied, following Master Chigong's testament, "Please build a Buddhist temple (梵刹) here to become an unsurpassable great field of blessings (無上 大福 田地) in the Dharma realm." The king, greatly pleased, consented and built the temple, naming it Haeinsa.

The characters "Haein" (海印) come from the phrase "Haein sammae" (海印三昧, Ocean Seal Samādhi) in the Avatamsaka Sutra (華嚴經). Because Master Sunŭng was a dharma grandson of Ŭisang Chosa, the Silla high monk and founder of the Hwaŏm School, it became a Hwaŏm sect temple (華嚴 宗刹). At the time of its initial founding, over a thousand monks resided there. Later, Master Hŭirang (希朗祖師), the royal preceptor (王師) of King Taejo Wang Kŏn (王健) of Koryŏ, emerged from Haeinsa. When King Taejo Wang Kŏn sought to unify Koryŏ after Silla, he fought a great battle with Paekche at Sŏngju. Defeated, Wang Kŏn fled to Haeinsa, sought out Master Hŭirang, and earnestly requested his help through Dharma power to achieve the great task of unifying Koryŏ. Master Hŭirang agreed, burned incense, and prayed at the Hwaŏm Sinjung Altar (華嚴 神衆壇). The Hwaŏm divine general Yongjŏk Taesin (華嚴 神將 勇敵 大神) manifested physically from the Hwaŏm sacred assembly painting (華嚴聖 衆幀畵), brandishing a fiery sword (火劒) in the air, intimidating Paekche. It is said the Paekche army was overwhelmed by this divine radiance (威光) and retreated. Due to this connection, Wang Kŏn took refuge in Master Hŭirang, made him the Royal Preceptor, donated 500 kyŏl (結只 - similar to the modern chŏngbo 町步 unit of land area) of land to Haeinsa, and ordered the magistrates of nearby counties (郡守) to protect Haeinsa. During the Yi Dynasty (李祖), King Yi T'aejo (李太祖) moved the Koryŏ Tripitaka (高麗 藏經) from Kanghwa Island to Haeinsa, establishing it as the head temple of the Dharma (法之宗家).

Passing through the Iljumun Gate (One Pillar Gate), Wangmun Gate (King's Gate), and Haet'almun Gate (Liberation Gate), one sees the Pŏmjonggak Pavilion (梵鐘閣, Dharma Bell Pavilion). To the east and west, numerous halls and monks' quarters (寮舍) are visible.

In the courtyard stand three stupas, one of the Silla artistic masterpieces built at the time of the temple's founding. Nine golden Buddhas (金佛) are enshrined within the stupas.

Taejŏkkangjŏn (Hall of Great Tranquility and Light, 大寂光殿)
Directly ahead is the Taejŏkkangjŏn. This main hall is the principal hall of the Hwaŏm sect <234> and enshrines Vairocana Buddha (毘盧遮那佛). For about 600 years after the temple's founding, it was called Pirojŏn (毘盧殿), but it was renamed Taejŏkkangjŏn after its reconstruction by the great monk Hakcho (學組大師) during King Sŏngjong's reign. Entering the hall and bowing respectfully three times, one sees Vairocana Buddha emitting the white curl of hair light (白毫相光) from between his eyebrows, illuminating the eighteen thousand worlds in all directions (照未方 萬 八千 世界), leaving no place untouched (靡不周遍); also Śākyamuni Buddha (釋迦牟尼佛) and Avalokiteśvara Bodhisattva (觀世音菩薩); ancient, brilliantly colored Buddhist paintings (泰畵); and hundreds of ancestral tablets (位牌) listening day and night to the sound of the wooden fish (木鐸). One cannot help but bow one's head. Exiting the hall, the newly built Nojŏn (爐殿, Incense Burner Hall?) is visible, and beside it, high up, stands the Changgyŏnggak (藏經閣, Tripitaka Hall).

This consists of two large buildings, an upper and a lower one, each 30 kan (칸, traditional unit of space between pillars) long. The upper and lower buildings, totaling 60 kan, house the state-commissioned woodblocks (國刊 經版), while the adjacent two buildings (4 kan each) house the temple-commissioned woodblocks (寺刊 經版). Among the world's 30 types of Tripitaka woodblocks, the Koryŏ Tripitaka (高麗 藏版) is considered the most valuable. Its grand scale, meticulous proofreading, and completeness of volumes rank it first among the world's Tripitaka woodblocks, an unparalleled treasure. There are state-commissioned blocks (國刊版) and temple-commissioned blocks (寺刊版). Work on the state-commissioned blocks began in the 24th year of King Kojong (高宗), the 33rd ruler of Koryŏ (1237,丁酉), and they were moved to this temple in the 7th year of King Yi T'aejo (1398, 戊寅). The temple-commissioned blocks include various sutras like the Lotus Sutra (法華經) and Surangama Sutra (楞嚴經) carved in the mid-Koryŏ period, and the Four-Part Vinaya blocks (四分律板) carved during the Chosŏn Dynasty.

Number of State-commissioned blocks (國刊版): 81,258 blocks (枚)
Number of Temple-commissioned blocks (寺刊版): 4,745 blocks (枚)
Total: 86,003 blocks (枚)

Number of State-commissioned Sutra Texts (國刊 經部): 1,512 titles (部)
Number of Temple-commissioned Sutra Texts (寺刊 經部): 59 titles (部)
Total: 1,571 titles (部)

Number of State-commissioned Volumes (國刊 卷): 6,791 volumes (卷)
Number of Temple-commissioned Volumes (寺刊 卷): 355 volumes (卷)
Total: 7,146 volumes (卷)

Precious Antiquities (貴重 古物)
As Haeinsa has a long history, it naturally possessed numerous valuable antiquities. However, due to several fires, only those that fortunately survived were finally collected and stored in Taishō 8 (1919). Among them, the Elephant Stupa Incense Burner (象塔 香爐) dates back to the Silla founding period, representing the oldest history, and its artistic value astonishes specialized scholars. Additionally, artificial flowers made of jade (玉으로 맨든 造花), a folding screen painted by Kim Hong-do? (金弘그道림 병풍 - text unclear), a White Longevity Character embroidered screen (白福壽繡屛), and a Flower and Bird embroidered screen (花鳥繡屛) are said to be worth tens of thousands of won. A monk in a long robe (장삼), pointing with a long stick, solemnly explains this. The listeners' minds become momentarily unified, unable to contain their admiration. Though this treasury of artifacts (器物藏) is opened and closed several times a day, it feels as if it won't be opened again as it's locked with a large padlock, "clunk," and then the large door is shut, "clunk." Somehow, one feels a sense of desolation. Visiting the Myŏngbujŏn (冥府殿, Hall of Judgment), Ŭngjinjŏn (應眞殿, Hall of Arhats), Kusŏnjŏn (九先殿, Hall of Nine Immortals?), Sim'gŏmdang (尋劒堂, Sword-Seeking Hall), and Kung'hyŏndang (窮玄堂, Hall of Investigating the Profound), one sees elderly monks in shabby robes sitting listlessly, chanting "Namu Amit'abul Kwanseŭm Posal" (나무아비타불 관세음보살) <235> while striking gongs and beating drums. In the Sa-undang (四雲堂), the Temple Affairs Office (宗務所), staff members sit at desks on chairs, attending to business. In the shabby Myŏngwŏldang (明月堂), the lecture hall (講習所), 50-60 children chatter noisily. One teacher conducts multi-grade teaching (複式 敎授). In the T'oesŏldang (堆雪堂), the meditation hall (禪房), it happens to be meditation season. There are monks with sallow complexions, monks with bright, clear faces, monks whose faces bloom yellow, old monks, young monks, plump monks, skinny monks—some kneeling facing the wall with eyes wide open, some dozing off intermittently. Generally, the meditation hall is where the True Dharma Eye Treasury (正法 眼藏) of the founder Śākyamuni (釋迦牟尼) was transmitted to Mahākāśyapa (摩訶迦葉) and passed down through generations, reaching the 28th patriarch in the West, Bodhidharma (達摩). When Bodhidharma transmitted this True Dharma Eye Treasury in Tang China, Buddhism was called "pointing directly to the human mind to see one's nature and attain Buddhahood" (直指人心 見性成佛) through mind-to-mind transmission (以心傳心). Its essence was transmitted to the second patriarch in Tang China, Huike (惠可), and passed down through Sengcan (僧璨), Daoxin (道信), and Hongren (弘忍) to the sixth patriarch in Tang China, Huineng (惠能). From Huineng's disciples emerged countless enlightened individuals, leading to the flourishing of the five schools (五宗) of the Zen (禪) tradition: Linji (臨濟宗), Caodong (曹洞宗), Yunmen (雪門宗), Guiyang (潙仰宗), and Fayan (法眼宗). It seems these five schools of the Zen tradition are still currently transmitted in the Buddhist world within Japan proper (內地), and practitioners in meditation halls (禪房) of famous temples and small hermitages are striving to attain this True Dharma Eye Treasury, the direct lineage of Śākyamuni. The day one attains this True Dharma Eye Treasury is the day one enters the realm of sages (聖賢) while still in the form of an ordinary being (凡夫), the day one masters the wondrous principles (妙理) of Buddhism and becomes a great guide (大 導師) for humans and gods in the three realms (人天 三界). So, is merely sitting and dozing Zen meditation (禪叅)? Is sitting with eyes wide open meditation? Is it speech (語)? Is it silence (黙)? Is it movement (動)? Is it stillness (靜)? It is neither stillness nor movement, neither speech nor silence; it's not dozing, nor is it sitting wide awake with eyes wide open. It is non-movement yet simultaneously movement (非動非靜이면서 卽動卽靜이며), non-speech yet simultaneously speech (非語非黙이면서 卽語卽黙). So, if it is not movement, yet is movement; not stillness, yet is stillness; not speech, yet is speech; not silence, yet is silence—it is truly wondrous and inconceivable (不可思議). This realm, which cannot be expressed in words or depicted in form, is provisionally called Zen (禪). Zen means the cessation of the mind's wanderings (心行處 滅) and the cutting off of the path of language (言語道 斷). If one penetrates the wondrous principle of Zen, is the True Dharma Eye Treasury (正法 眼庄) then something separate? It is something experienced and tasted right there, without moving even an inch (寸步). To penetrate this wondrous principle of Zen, the ancients devised methods and skillful means (方便) for investigation (叅究). Within the gates of the patriarchs (祖庭門下), there exist the so-called 1,700 kōans (公案), which are used as focal points (話頭) to investigate the wondrous principle of Zen, cease all afflictions, delusions, and discriminations (一切 煩惱 妄想 分別), and train mental concentration (精神의 統一). There is no early or late time for investigating (叅究) this Zen, nor distinction between male or female, old or young. Anyone who practices diligently with great faith (大 信根), great doubt (大 疑團), and great courage (大 奮勇) can reach this wondrous principle in the blink of an eye (瞬目之間). Yet, with the slightest deviation (秋毫의 差違), one might not penetrate it even after innumerable kalpas (無量劫). <236> If monks had no Zen meditation, there would have been no appeal in becoming a monk.

Hermitage Sightseeing (菴子 求景)
After breakfast, we set out to see the nearby hermitages.
Kŭngnakchŏn (極樂殿, Hall of Supreme Bliss) is the Memorial Hall (影閣) reconstructed by Kyejin'gong (戒眞公) in the 19th year of King Sŏngjong, Musin year (戊申, 1488 AD), 448 years ago. The main wall enshrines the portrait of the great monk Puyu (浮休大師). To the south of the hall is a stone well (石井), said to be the royal water source (御用水) of King Aejang (哀莊王) of Silla. The dense shade to the east, dyeing collars green (濃陰川 衣襟을 綠染), is said to be the Bell Tower's Strange Peak (鍾樓 奇峰也).
Leaving there, we went slightly down a narrow path heading north, crossed a ditch, and walked for a while. Climbing, catching our breath, catching our breath and climbing again, we reached a small tile-roofed hermitage perched on a cliff. This was Hŭirangdae (希朗臺), where Master Hŭirang (希朗祖師) used to pray. Behind the platform (臺) stood a beautiful thousand-year-old pine tree, instantly evoking the style of Southern School painting (南畵). After looking around the Sansingak (山神閣, Mountain Spirit Shrine), we came out, climbed again, and sat on a rock. From there, we had a panoramic view of Haeinsa Temple. Among our group of 11, there were a famously cheerful lady and a monk (法師). Someone from behind said, "Reverend C, please recite a sijo (시조)." K chimed in, "Reverend, that one, you know, that one!" The whole group burst into laughter. Then everyone started saying, "Reverend, that one, that one." But Reverend C calmly said, "I know how to do it," yet firmly refused and didn't recite anything. During this time, we arrived at Paengnyŏnam (白蓮菴), famous as a place for Zen meditation. This hermitage was founded by the great monk Soam (昭菴大師), a disciple of Sŏsan (西山), and later monks like Song'un (松雲大師), Ilhŏn (一軒), Kongsu (功壽), Yŏch'an (如贊), Ssanghwi (雙暉), as well as Hwakwang (獲光), Tobong (道峰), and Wŏlp'a (月波) contributed significantly.
Our group ate a temple meal (不供飯) with delicious side dishes (診味) at the Chodang pavilion (祖堂 樓閣), and conversation began. As it happened, three people in the group had been to Europe and America (歐米). Taking turns, stories about European customs emerged. They were very interesting stories. During the chat, someone mentioned that the outhouse at one of Haeinsa's branch temples was three kil (길, traditional unit of length, about human height) deep. Someone else remarked that when going to that outhouse, one should tie a rope around their waist just in case (萬一), causing the whole group to roar with laughter ("와"). Besides that, there were many other amusing stories, making it quite delightful. Flowing time did not grant us more leisure, and dusk approached. Our group thanked the caretaker (監院) of Paengnyŏnam for his kindness and started descending. We rested at a couple of spots, listening to the explanations and stories about Haeinsa folk songs by the eloquent monk Hwan'ch'ong (幻銃法師). Turning back from the path we had taken, we dispersed midway. Back at the inn, we had dinner, and the pleasant feeling extended even to the food. Lying alone, I chuckled over the funny stories heard that day and passed the day.

The next day, we visited Yongjajŏn (影子殿, Portrait Hall), located northwest of the main temple complex. This hermitage, <237> also known as Hongjeam (弘濟菴), was founded 350 years ago by King Sŏnjo (宣祖大王). When the great monk Samyŏng (四溟大師), having served as the plenipotentiary envoy (講和全權大使) to Japan proper (內地), returned all his titles to the state and expressed his wish to practice austerities at Haeinsa until his death, King Sŏnjo specially bestowed upon Samyŏng Taesa the posthumous title Hongje Chonja (弘濟尊者) and built Hongjeam. Later, because the portraits (影子) of the three masters Sŏsan (西山), Samyŏng (四溟), and Kihŏ (奇虛) were enshrined there, it came to be called Yongjajŏn.
The structure of the building is said to be something that current Chosŏn carpenters (木工) can hardly conceive of, and carpenters reportedly come from various places to draw up plans. It also serves as excellent material for oil painting (油畫). After listening to the interesting talk of Reverend Hwan'ch'ong (幻銃 시님), the master of this hermitage, it was 11 o'clock, lunchtime at the temple. Unable to hear more of the fascinating stories, we returned for lunch. Afterwards, we visited Kugiram (國一菴), northeast of the inn.
The construction date is unknown, but it seemed to be a considerably old building (古 建物). It felt desolate, seemingly with few people around. In front of the main gate stood an ancient hackberry tree (괴목), also excellent material for oil painting. A little way down from there were 5 or 6 earthen huts (土窟) and a tiny hermitage; this was Yaksuam (藥水菴), the nunnery (女僧房). Yaksuam was built about 40 years ago and is named so because there is a medicinal spring (藥水) in the garden. About 30 nuns live there, each occupying a room and a kitchen, like a collective dwelling (共同 密所), leading self-sufficient lives. Being originally poor households (살님), their frugality is beyond description. When their food runs out, they go out begging (東兩), collect money bit by bit (錢錢分分), bring it back, and barely manage to survive (延命). There are two nunneries at Haeinsa: Yaksuam and Samsŏnam (三仙菴). This latter hermitage was built 45 or 46 years ago and is called Samsŏnam because three immortals (三仙) supposedly descended and played Go (바둑) on a rock in the hermitage courtyard. Located by a valley stream, the sound of water is rather peaceful. Seeing the small child monks (조고마큼 식 한 신중들? - phrasing unclear, likely refers to young nuns/novices) going in and out of various rooms, one feels, on the one hand, otherworldly (神仙), and on the other, somewhat pitiful (凄凉).

We visited Taesŏnam (大善菴). This hermitage is a new building, 2 or 3 years old, situated cleanly in a scenic, high spot. A woman who had lived in the entertainment world (花柳界) during her youth experienced a great awakening (깨달은 바 있어도 - phrasing slightly awkward) and, wanting to practice austerities and spend the rest of her life in a secluded place, built this house with her private assets, which will become temple property (寺中 建物) after her death. Through the lady's capable management, attracting beautiful and skillful women, Haeinsa blooms with flowers every summer. Following that path, we climbed up to Wŏndang (願堂), located quite high up, which was rather breathless. Here resides the renowned figure, the 96-year-old Court Lady Im (林상궁 마마). The elderly monk serving as the caretaker (監院 시님) here is someone blessed with sons and daughters (有子 生女) and all five fortunes (五福이 具存), yet despite his children tearfully clinging to him (눈물노 붓잡음에도 불구하고), he left them behind to pursue spiritual practice. <238> This hall is said to be the place where King Aejang, the 4th king of Silla (哀庄王), stayed for three years after founding Haeinsa, praying for blessings (福을 빌었다), hence it's called King Aejang's Prayer Place (哀庄王의 祈福地). Besides these, there was Ch'ŏngnyangam (淸凉菴), which we couldn't visit because it was far away.

Famous spots include Pongch'ŏndae (奉天臺), Hoesŏndae (會仙臺), Chŏpsŏktae (疊石臺), Chewŏltam (霽月潭), Ch'aegokp'ok (嘖玉瀑), Wanjaeam (完在岩), Kwangp'ungnoe (光風瀨), Ŭmp'ungnoe (吟風瀨), Ch'wijŏgam (泚肇岩), Ch'wijŏkpong (翠積峰), Ch'ilsŏngdae (七星臺), Muryŭnggyo (武陵橋), Suhwach'ŏn (遂花川), and Kyŏngmigwŏn (更覓源). Additionally, there's Haksaengdae (學生臺) with its old zelkova tree providing cool shade all summer, and the enjoyable Pulijŏng (不二亭). Chilling watermelon and melon (수박 참의) in the water, grilling chicken (닭쩜) and pork (되지 고기), bringing along various seasoned vegetables (가진 나물) for lunch, we gather with dozens of friends on the wide rock where immortals played Go. Taking off our socks and shirts, rinsing chopsticks and spoons in the water, we eat with our fingers—a kind of primitive banquet (原始 萬餐會). Then, accompanied by the songs, Yukchabaegi (육자배기), sijo, and Ch'unhyangga (春香歌) performed by experts in the field (斯界에 전문가들), the sounds of "Chotta chotta" (좃타 좃타 - Great! Great!) gently flow, followed by lively dancing (덩실덩실 춤). "Play while young, for you cannot play when old and sick! Chotta! Ŏlsiguna! Chŏlsigu!" Korean dance, Western dance (洋춤)—everyone joins together, dancing. This delightful place is Chahadong (紫霞洞).

Trip to Kayasan Summit (伽倻山 上峯行)
Our group of 10 set out early in the morning, carrying a load of lunch. We walked along narrow paths overgrown with grass, sometimes losing our way and wandering, sometimes singing songs. The place buzzed with the boisterous voice of Mrs. K.
Kayasan (伽倻山), anciently the core part of the Kara (伽羅) confederacy, had its name transcribed from Kara and altered with Buddhist connotations. It is also called Udusan (牛頭山, Ox Head Mountain), Chungwangsan (衆王山, Mountain of Many Kings), Chihangsan (只恒山), or Yŏngsan (靈山, Spirit Mountain). This mountain originates from the Tŏkdŏksan (大德山) range and towers amidst the four counties of Sŏngju, Koryŏng, Kŏch'ang, and Hapch'ŏn. It stands 4,719 feet (尺) above sea level, covers an area of 3,328 chō (町步, approx. 33 sq km), and is lush with natural five-needle pines (五葉松), red pines (赤松), maples (단풍), and deciduous trees (활엽수). Strange rocks and bizarre stones stand tall, creating a clear, strange, and beautiful (明快, 奇麗) mountain landscape. In front, Namsan Cheilbong peak (南山第一峰) provides layers of enclosure. Especially the Ubijŏng (牛鼻井, Ox Nose Well) on the summit (上峰) and the stone statue of Avalokiteśvara (觀音 石像) below Paegunsŏng (白雲城, White Cloud Fortress) are destinations for sightseers (探勝客). Chilling soda (사이다) in ice (어름), dipping it and drinking, making lettuce wraps (상치쌈) with water from Ubijŏng—the exquisite taste is indescribable. After eating, we each sat perched on rocks and sang 'mokch'im tori' songs (목침도리 창가 - perhaps simple folk songs?), laughing until our sides ached (개미허리가 되다십히 우섯다). With little summer daylight remaining, on the way back, we sought out the site of Ch'ilbulam (七佛菴, Seven Buddhas Hermitage), overgrown with grass. It is said that 1,900 years ago, among the eight princes of King Kim Suro (金首露), one became the crown prince, while the other seven came here, attained enlightenment (見性 득도), and later achieved fruition (結果) at Hyŏkkye-sa Temple (赫溪寺) in Hadong. Thus, Ch'ilbulam at Ssanggyesa Temple (雙溪寺 七佛菴) is said to be where the seven princes achieved fruition. There's a legend that when relatives came to visit, they could see the seven princes reflected in Yŏngji (影池, Reflection Pond) in front of the main temple, Haeinsa. <239> Our group members climbed high onto Ch'ilbulam; some pushed through the overgrown thicket searching for the place where they lived long ago; some looked for the well and scooped water to drink; some discussed collecting donations to rebuild the temple. As some dragged their weary legs, I gave one end of my walking stick to the teacher (선생) and was pulled along. Y, who is good at making funny remarks, said:
"You just needed to close your eyes there!"
And the whole group laughed. By the time we returned to the inn, the sun had set. Having walked 40 ri (approx. 16 km) back and forth, our legs couldn't move an inch (寸步). We barely ate dinner and suffered from body aches (몸살).

Buddhist Services (佛事)
Work for the Buddha is called Pulsa (佛事). There is the Buddha's Birthday service (탄생 佛事, April 8th), the Ullambana service in the seventh month (7월 百種 佛事 - the day Venerable Maudgalyāyana (一目蓮 尊者) transferred his mother from hell to the Pure Land), the Enlightenment Day service (成道 佛事, December 8th), and the Nirvana Day service (涅槃 佛事, February 15th - note: text says 沮盤, likely means 涅槃). Among these, the April 8th service is celebrated grandly. At Haeinsa, around the end of the third lunar month, people buy plots of land, each several p'yŏng (평) in size, about 3 majŏng (마정, approx. 300m?) from Haeinsa, and set up stalls (前方). About 20 such stalls appear, bringing all sorts of goods from various places. Moreover, from every stall, the singing voices of young women (시악씨) ring out, accompanied by the sound of janggu drums (장구). For the monks of the large and small temples and the inn guests, this provides considerable solace during evening strolls. From the first day of the fourth month, the crowd gradually increases, and on the 7th and 8th of April, not only are the inns fully booked, but every house bursts with people in rooms and on porches (마루). On April 8th, hundreds of thousands of worshippers come and go, forming a sea of people (人山人海). It's a festival (慶節) worth seeing once. Currently, there is the Changgyŏnggak Pulsa (장경각 佛事): the Governor-General of Chosŏn (조선 총독) donated 10,000 won (圓) to copy the Eighty Thousand Tripitaka (팔만 대장경) and present it to the Emperor of Manchukuo (만주국 황제). A large sign reading "Keep Out" (禁斷傍) is posted on the main gate bearing the words "Kayasan Haeinsa." Inside the Changgyŏnggak, there are 23 teams (組) copying and inspecting, supervised by technicians (技手) sent down from the Government-General (總督府) and the project director (都監). This work lasts over two months. This Pulsa is not only large in scale but also provides significant income for the monks and laypeople earning 1 to 3 won per day (日賃 1원 3원식 밧는 중 속인들). Even the monks who were idle have become busy with 8 hours of labor daily. For the monks who previously had no productive capacity, the jingle of money in their pockets (주머니 속에 돈 소리가 나게 되어) makes this, from any perspective, an event that cannot be overlooked as insignificant (大事). I also went to observe it once with several ladies, and it was quite a sight. It is said that when this Pulsa concludes, there will be a grand offering ceremony (供養) and chanting (念佛).

Earthen Hut Life (土窟 生活)
Within the Haeinsa precincts, there are about 60 households (人家). These are called t'o-gul (土窟, earthen huts), and monks distinguish them significantly [from temple buildings]. Most are the homes of monks' wives and families <240>; the rest are laypeople's homes. If they have some paddy land (논마지기) or receive a monthly salary as an office worker (事務員) at the Temple Affairs Office (宗務所), they can barely maintain their livelihood. Otherwise, through washing monks' clothes (싹바질) or sewing monks' clothes (싹빨내 - likely refers to mending/sewing), their living conditions are wretched (말이 아니다). However, the authorities (當局) are reportedly demanding these t'o-gul be cleared out (整理).

Sound of the Bell (鍾 소래)
When the sound of the evening bell, the sound of the dawn bell, drifts faintly from afar through the dense forest, one's head naturally bows, and one awakens from dawn slumber. How serene (無心). Why does that bell sound so melancholic (凄凉)? It deepens my sorrow (愁心). Unconsciously, Millet's "The Angelus" (晩鐘) comes to mind. Except for special Buddhist services (臨時로 不供 있을 때), there are regular services three times daily (每日 3次 禮佛). At these times, bells ring "tteng tteng" from the large and small temples all around. Specifically, there is the morning service (아침 禮佛) at 4 AM, the noon service (午正 禮佛) at 11 AM, and the evening service (저녁 禮佛) at 6 PM. Before the Buddha, the Pujŏn monk (temple attendant, 釜殿 시님) and the Kamwŏn monk (abbot/supervisor, 監院 시님), wearing kasa robes (가사) and jangsam robes (장삼), strike the wooden fish (목탁) and chant the Surangama Mantra (楞嚴呪) and the Great Compassion Dharani (千手 陀羅尼), followed by the offering meal (供養). The side dishes (반찬) consist only of kimchi without garlic (마눌도 안 는 김치) and green vegetables (푸른 채소). Therefore, although their faces (얼골) have a clear complexion (말근 빛), they lack strength due to nutritional deficiency (營養 不足).

Inn Life (旅舘 生活)
I became a guest at the Kido Inn (紀濤旅館), the designated inn for Haeinsa. This inn provides indispensable convenience for Haeinsa, a unique establishment with facilities rarely seen even in cities. Having once spent a week at an inn in the Yosemite mountains during my tour of Europe and America (歐米 漫遊時), I couldn't help but draw comparisons. That Yosemite inn featured entirely Indian-style architecture and furnishings (印度式 建物과 裝置). Just looking at the building and decorations was enough to prevent boredom from mountain life. Furthermore, it had various entertainment facilities (娛樂 機關), dance parties (딴스회), and horse races (競馬會), ensuring not a single moment of boredom. The horse race involved small wooden horses; beautiful women among the guests, wearing various hats, would move the horses as a middle-aged woman called out numbers. This was met with thunderous applause, and those who bet on the winning horse caused a commotion collecting their money. Not only that, beautiful young women were called out from here and there, making it quite a spectacle. Having experienced such sights in the past, coming into the mountains brought back memories, making me long for those days. I wonder when this inn will become like that one. Every day, 30-40 people arrive and depart without fail. Being in the mountains, spring and winter are naturally off-seasons, but during summer for escaping the heat (避暑) and autumn for viewing foliage (紅葉 求景), tens of thousands (멫 만명) come and go. Moreover, when there are about 10 long-term guests (月泊 손님), it's enough to make one frown (눈살이 찝흐려 질 만치): rows of medicine kettles (藥湯罐) on braziers (火爐) line the edge of the wooden floor (마루). Among the guests are young men, still seemingly full of vigor, whose faces are sallow (얼골이 노랑꽃이 피고) and whose energy is completely drained (긔운이 척 느러저), listlessly spreading out their bedding and always lying down (늘 두러눈 자). Some among them, full of youthful energy (血氣 旺盛), grow impatient with the monotonous life. Wondering how best to amuse themselves, they visit every mountain hermitage and valley daily. Once every three days, these mischief-makers (장난군) gather in the second-floor recreation room (娛樂室), drink refined rice wine (清酒) and beer (삐루), and then beat on rice bowl lids (밥주발 뚝겅) with brass chopsticks (놋 저가락) as drums (장구), clap their hands (손벽), stomp their feet (발을 굴너), and dance, eventually going down barefoot into the yard and jumping around (징둥징둥 뛴다). Myself and a few other women are spectators. Because of this, the inn is bustling, and due to the expensive meal prices (食價가 빗싼), people seeking quiet recuperation (靜養) go to the hermitages. Furthermore, young women (妙齡 女子) deliberately avoid it and go to the monks' quarters (僧房). Truly, someone always makes noise starting from dawn, preventing even people in adjacent rooms from sleeping. In any case, the Hongdo Inn (紅濤旅館 - *Note: earlier text called it Kido Inn 紀濤旅館, now Hongdo 紅濤旅館*) is indispensable for Haeinsa, a paradise (天堂) and pure land (極樂) where travelers can rest their tired legs and sleep their weary bodies.

Monastic Life (僧侶 生活)
The registered monks at Haeinsa number 398 men and 100 women. Monastic life is supposedly a life beyond the mundane world (塵世), a so-called extramundane life (物外 生活), but unity (團合) and discipline (規律) form its core. A monk (僧侶) is part of the Sangha (僧伽), and Sangha means harmony (和合). Individuals from different places (生不同) and with different surnames (姓不同), having renounced the dust (塵埃) of the secular world, cut ties with loved ones (割愛捨親), left home to become monks (出家 爲僧), and entered the mountains for spiritual practice (入山 修道)—their daily existence is monastic life.
When these diverse individuals enter the mountains for spiritual practice, aiming to transform from ordinary beings into sages (革凡 成聖) and dedicating themselves to the training ground (道場, monastery), they distance themselves from worldly desires (塵世 愛慾) and become pure disciples (淸風 衲子) nurtured in the Buddhist Dharma training ground. For the sake of the Buddha's unsurpassed Dharma (無上 道法), their lives continue with harmony as the guiding principle (以和爲主), not discriminating between right and wrong (不分是非), gathering based on the Dharma (以法爲會), and mutually supporting each other (互相扶助). This is the extramundane law of harmony (物外的 和合 法) for a pure life (淸廉 度生) and simultaneously the fundamental pillar (主幹) of monastic life.
This is the general outline of monastic life. The detailed monastic regulations (淸規法) maintaining order within this life not only far transcend those of general social group life (一般 社會 大衆 團體 生活) but their developed rules also offer many points worthy of emulation (模範) for social organizations in general. To introduce the daily routine (日課) of monks, while "monk" is a general term, there are different ranks (階級) within the monastic community based on individual qualifications (威儀의 資格).
Within Buddhism as a whole, there is a division into the Zen school (禪宗) and the Doctrinal school (敎宗). The Zen school involves so-called "abandoning doctrines to enter Zen" (捨敎 入禪), meaning after understanding Buddhist teachings through textual interpretation, one enters into actual practice to realize the truth (眞理 體得), aiming for thorough enlightenment (大悟 徹底) by experiencing the reality of True Suchness (實際 眞如之理) apart from literal text (離文字 外). The Doctrinal school encompasses anyone who specializes in studying or chanting (持誦) any of the Buddha's teachings throughout his life (一代時敎, scriptures). Lecturers (講師) or monks specializing in any scripture are generally called members of the Doctrinal school. In monasteries, practitioners of Zen and Doctrinal schools are distinguished as Rip'an (理判, principle-based) and Sap'an (事判, affair-based) practitioners, respectively; Zen is Rip'an, and Doctrine is Sap'an. Thus, across both Zen and Doctrinal schools, depending on individual qualifications, there are ranks such as Great Zen Master (大禪師), Great Dharma Teacher (大敎師), Zen Master (禪師), Great Virtue (大德), Middle Virtue (中德), Great Monk (大禪), and Novice (沙彌). Cultivating these qualifications is done according to detailed monastic regulations (世則의 淸規法).
Briefly outlining the daily life of the Zen school first, their life is entirely regulated (規律的). Throughout the year, Zen centers (禪院) in various temples have retreat (結制) and release (解制) periods, known as the summer retreat (夏安居) and winter retreat (冬安居). The summer retreat begins on the 15th day of the 4th lunar month and ends on the 15th day of the 7th lunar month. During these three months, they focus entirely on Zen meditation (參禪); this is called Haan'gŏ (夏安居). The winter retreat begins on the 15th day of the 10th lunar month and ends on the 15th day of the 1st lunar month; these three months are called Tong'an'gŏ (冬安居). During the retreat (安居), daily life involves waking without fail at 3 AM every day. Everyone, young and old, gathers together in the Dharma hall (法堂), offers incense (香火) at the altar, finishes the ceremony, and then practices Zen meditation facing the wall (面壁 觀心) until 6 AM. After the morning meal offering (朝飯, breakfast), they meditate from 8 AM to 10 AM, and again in the afternoon from 1 PM to 3 PM, and from 6 PM to 9 PM. In this way, dozens of practitioners uniformly follow the same rules for the ninety-day retreat (九旬 安居). The day this concludes is Haejeil (解制日, Release Day). For the three months after release until the next retreat begins, the Zen practitioners (禪客) who lived together, aiming to cultivate ascetic practices (苦行), each shoulder their traveling bag (걸망, 鉢囊) and move to other places. Following the path of itinerant practice (到處禪), they cultivate asceticism, sometimes passing through towns and villages, sometimes visiting famous mountains, great temples, and renowned sacred sites (聖地) to purify mind and body (心身). Even during ascetic travels, they never forget their practice, constantly investigating their kōan (話頭 參究). When the retreat day (結制日) arrives, they invariably enter a Zen center (禪院) elsewhere (i.e., register their name - 入房) and begin their Zen study (參禪 工夫) anew. The life of Doctrinal school practitioners (敎宗人) typically involves waking at 5 AM. After waking, they gather in the Dharma hall, finish the ceremony, and then recite the scriptures (經傳) they specialize in. Around 6:30 AM, they have breakfast (朝飯). After breakfast, they engage in their assigned duties (任務) for the rest of the day. Among them, students (學人) studying Mahayana scriptures (大乘 經典) of Buddhism take a short break after breakfast, then gather at the sound of a bell (打鐘 集合) to begin 논강 (論講, doctrinal debate/discussion). 論講 is a type of scripture study. If there are three students, three, or if four, four, decide on a portion of study for the day, setting an equal number of pages from the Buddhist text (佛經 冊帳) as the day's assignment. They spend the day studying according to their individual understanding (見解). The next day after breakfast, they engage in 논강. If their interpretations (見解) align, there is no issue. However, if their researched interpretations differ, they go to the head monk/teacher (朝室 和尚) for a ruling (判決). In this way, through the study of the Buddha's teachings throughout his life (一代 時敎), great masters (大講師), great dharma teachers (大法師), and missionaries (布敎師) emerge. Monastic life, nurtured as pure beings within the Dharma training ground (法道場) of the Great Sage of the Three Realms (三界 大聖), Śākyamuni, is truly a place where great vessels of the Dharma (大法器) for humans and gods (人天) are formed in sanctity, inevitably inspiring admiration. <243>

**Summary**

Na Hyesŏk recounts her summer stay at Haeinsa Temple in 1938, describing the journey, the temple's scenic surroundings in Hongnyudong Valley, its founding legends involving Silla monks Sunŭng and I-jŏng and the Indian master Chigong, and its historical significance as the repository of the Tripitaka Koreana. She details the temple layout, major halls like Taejŏkkangjŏn, the priceless woodblocks in the Changgyŏnggak, and various hermitages (Kŭngnakchŏn, Hŭirangdae, Paengnyŏnam, Yongjajŏn, Yaksuam, etc.). The narrative includes anecdotes about temple life, interactions with monks and fellow visitors, descriptions of Zen meditation practices, Buddhist services (especially the Buddha's Birthday festival and a contemporary project copying the Tripitaka for Manchukuo), the difficult lives of lay families in nearby 'earthen huts' (t'o-gul), the atmosphere of the designated inn, and an excursion to the summit of Kayasan Mountain.

From a colonial history perspective, the article is interesting for its casual mention of the Governor-General donating funds and overseeing the copying of the Tripitaka Koreana for presentation to the Emperor of Manchukuo, highlighting the entanglement of cultural heritage projects with Japanese imperial politics. The description of the somewhat modernised inn contrasting with traditional temple life, the mention of visitors who had travelled to "Europe and America" (歐米), and the economic hardship faced by lay families dependent on the temple offer glimpses into the social and economic conditions of the time. The existence of designated inns and the mention of Japanese administrative terms like 'chō' (町步) also reflect the colonial context.

海印寺의 風光
						羅惠錫
나는 어느 親友의 권유로 봄에 와서 한여름을 海印寺에서 나게 되였다. 경부선을 타고 대구에서 나려 역전에 있는 자동차部에서 海印寺 행 자동차를 타면 高靈 冶爐 등지를 거처 약 3시간만에 紅流洞 洞口에서 나리게 된다.
紅流洞 입구 우편 석벽에는 우리 史上에 유명한 崔孤雲 선생의 紅流洞 시
狂奔疊名吼重巒
人語難分咫尺間
常恐是非聲到耳
故敎流水盡聾山
이 색여 잇고 좌편 溪邊에는 孤雲 선생의 聾山亭이 잇고 그 앞에는
「孤雲 先生 遯世地」
라 刻調한 석비가 있으며 좌편 높이 孤雲 선생의 사당이 있다.
紅紅流洞은 실노 塵外에 仙境이다. 바위와 돌 돌과 바위에 사이와 사이로 유유히 흘너 내려 聾山亭 압 높은 석대 우에 떠러지는 웅장한 물소래 무성한 나무 흉금을 서늘케 하고 머리를 개법게 한다.
가지고 온 짐을 지우고 그 뒤를 따라 집행이를 동모 삼어 5리나 되는 계곡을 끼고 어칠넝 어칠넝 거러가니 연일 지리하게 나리든 비가 개이매 봄 하눌은 맑다 뿐이랴 가지마다 푸릇푸릇 싹시 돗고 풀 냄새가 향긋이 뿜어 드러온다. 山回 水縈, 水縈 山回이다. 물 구비마다 水麗 아닌 곳이 업고 산 모롱이마다 山明 아닌 곳이 업다. 산이 盡하엿는가 하면 다시 산이오 水가 窮하엿는가 하면 다시 물이다. 물이 많을수록 실치 않으며 산 모롱이가 거듭할사록 가고 싶으다. 어느듯 玉流亭에 이르렀다. 이 亭은 幻鏡 법사가 건립한 거시란다. 亭閣 내외에는 내외국＜231＞ 명사들의 현판이 다수 걸녔다. 거긔 올나 잠간 쉬인 다음에 다시 나와 사자문을 거처 삼림 사이로 드러서 꼬부랑 꼬부랑한 길을 따라 숨을 모라쉬어 언덕을 올나서니 산 중에 제일 보기 싫은 함석 집웅 하나 나타난다. 이거시 過印寺 지정 여관 紅濤여관일다. 방 하나를 청구하야 행장을 풀고 나서 여관 1, 2 층을 도라다니며 구경하니 도회지에서도 볼 수 없을만치 설비가 되여 있으며 만원될 때는 2, 300식 수용하고 잇다 한다. 飯饌이며 其外 待海가 놀나웠다. 피곤한 一夜를 지내고 아침 산책으로 海印寺를 찾어 갔다. 홍랑문을 드러서니 魁傑한 古梵宮이 樹林 간에 隱映하고 잇다. 이거시 海印寺이다.
조선 불교계에 4대 名寺가 잇스니 露鷲山 佚刹 通度寺 曹溪山 僧刹 松廣寺 金井山 禪刹 梵魚寺 伽倻山 法刹 海印寺가 是다. 其中 海印寺는 명찰일 뿐 아니라 法之宗家요 세계적으로 자랑하는 重寶인 고려 장경판 팔만 대장경을 봉안하고 잇는 대장경閣이 잇다.
海印寺 창건된 유래의 전설은 이러하다. 서역 인도 고승 提納博幸 尊者 指空선사께서 唐土에서 佛法을 선포하야 교화 중생하시었는대 이 指空선사가 일즉 조선 전국을 徧踏 강산하실 때 伽倻山 海印寺 址를 지내시다가 장차 이곳에 梵刹이 건립되리니 海印寺로 명명되여 법계에 大福 田地가 될 것이라 하시고 海印寺 창건시 사용키 위하야 鐵瓦 3,000개를 주조하야 못에다 埋置하야 두었다 한다. 其後 즉 신라 제 40대 哀莊王 당시 距今 1,200년 전에 신라 고승 華嚴宗主 義湘祖師의 法會孫인 順應 利貞 양 대사가 唐土로 指空선사를 친견하기 위하야 수륙 수 만리를 도보로 入唐할 새 벌서 선사께서는 涅槃에 드신 지 오랜지라 그 유골을 탑 중에 뫼시엇스니 선사께서 涅槃에 드실 임시 제자께 유언을 남기어 말삼하시기를
「吾 涅槃 후 조선서 順應 利貞 양 沙彌가 (沙彌라는 거슨 인도 말이니 漢譯하면 息慈이다. 즉 息惡慈行의 뜻이니 20세 미만 된 젊은 승려를 칭함) 올 터이니 오거든 이 유서를 전하여라」
그 遺囑이 게시엇는대 과연 그 遺囑과 如히 順應 利貞이 도착하매 제자는 선사의 유언을 말하고 유서를 전하니 이에 順應 利貞 양 師는 유서만으로서 足肯치 않고
「凡夫를 버서나신 선사의 法身 境上에야 生死 去來에 따르지 않으시고 常住 不滅하시리니 우리의 지극한 정성으로서 탑 중에 게시는 선사 法身을 친견하리라 하고」

「그대들의 정성이 이와 같이 장하냐」

거긔서 나와서 북으로 뚤닌 좁은 길노 조곰 나려가 또랑을 건너 한참을 나간다. 올나 가다가 숨을 쉬고 숨을 쉬어 올나가니 낭떠러지에 조고마한 개와집 암자가 있다. 이거시 希朗祖師가 기도하든 希朗臺이다. 臺 뒤에는 천년이나 된 보기 조흔 소나무가 있어 一見에 南畵의 격을 이루고 잇다. 山神閣을 둘너보고 나와 다시 올나가 바위 우에 안저 一望하니 海印寺 전경이 보인다. 우리 일행 11인 중에는 쾌활하기로 유명한 여사와 법사가 잇다. 뒤에서 누가 C 시님 시조 하나 하십소 하니 K가 시님 그거요 그거 말이야요. 일동은 와 우섯다. 그러고 모다 한마대식 시님 그거요 그거. 한즉 C 시님은 점잔이 「내가 할 줄 아오」하고 긔어히 아니하고 마렀다. 그럴 동안에 참선처로 유명한 白蓮菴에 다다렀다. 이 암자는 서산 문인 昭菴대사가 창건한 후 松雲대사 一軒, 功壽, 如贊, 雙暉 등이며 獲光 道峰 月波 諸氏가 有功하다 한다.
우리 일행은 祖堂 누각에서 診味에 불공밥을 먹고 좌담이 이러났다. 때마침 歐米에 갓다 온 사람이 3인이다. 들거니 놓거니 歐羅巴 풍속 이야기가 난다. 매우 흥미 있는 이야기었다. 잡담 중에는 海印寺 말사 중 어느 절에는 변소가 세 길이나 되다고 하야 어느 분이 그 변소에 갈 때는 萬一을 염려하야 허리에 색기를 매고 가야겠다고 한즉 일동은 「와」하고 우섯다. 그 외에 여러 가지 우수운 이야기가 만하 자못 유쾌하였다. 흐르는 때는 우리에게 더 시간을 주지 못하고 황혼이 되어왔다. 우리 일동은 白蓮菴 監院에게 후의를 謝하고 내려오며 두어 군대 쉬이며 좌담을 잘 하시는 幻銃법사의 海印寺 민요에 대한 설명 이야기가 있었다. 가든 길을 돌처와 中路에서 散散이 논하진 후 여관에 도라와서 저녁을 먹으니 유쾌한 맛이 음식에까지 나타난다. 오날 드른 우수운 이야기를 혼자 두러누어 우수면서 하로를 지냈다.
이튼날은 큰 절 서북편에 있는 影子殿을 차갔다. 이 암자의＜237＞ 一名은 弘濟菴이라고 하는대 350 전에 선조대왕께서 창건하섯는데 四溟대사가 강화전권대사로 일본 내지를 다녀와서 일체 작위를 나라에 還上하고 海印寺에서 수도하다가 임종하겠다는 원에 의하야 선조께서 특히 四溟대사에게 弘濟尊者의 謚號를 나리시고 弘濟菴을 건설하신 것인대 其後 西山 四溟 奇虛 3 和尙의 影子를 뫼시었기 때문에 影子殿이라고 하는 거시다.
건물의 구조는 현재 조선 목공으로서는 도저히 상상하기 어려는 것이라 하야 각처에서 목공이 와서 도본을 그리어 가는 일이 많다고 한다. 유화의 재료로도 훌륭하다. 이 암자 주인공으 게신 幻銃 시님의 자미있는 좌담을 듯고 나니 11시 절 점심때라 더 듯고 싶은 이야기를 못다 듯고 도라와 점심을 먹었다. 그러고 나서 여관 동북에 있는 國一菴을 찾자갓다.
건설 연대는 모르겠으나 상당히 古 건물일다. 사람도 그리 없는 듯 하야 쓸쓸하엿다. 정문 앞에는 고목의 괴목이 있어 역시 유화 재료로 훌륭하였다. 그 앞으로 조곰 내려오면 5, 6戶의 토굴이 있고 조고마한 암자가 있으니 이것이 여승방 藥水菴이다. 藥水菴은 건설된 지 40여 년이오 정원에 약수가 있음으로 藥水菴이라고 한다. 여승이 30여 명 있어 공동 密所 모양으로다 각각 방 한 간 부엌 한 간식 차지하고 자치 생활을 한다. 원래 가난한 살님들이라 그 절용 절식이란 말할 수 없으며 양식이 떠러지면 동양을 나가서 錢錢分分이 모아 가지고 드러와 겨오 연명을 하고 산다. 海印寺에는 여승방이 둘이 있으니 藥水菴 외에 三仙菴이 있다. 이 암자 건설은 45, 6년 되였다 하며 三仙이 내려와 암자 뜰에 있는 바위 우에서 바둑을 두었다고 하야 三仙菴이라고 한다. 계곡가에 있어 물소래 적이 한가하며 조고마큼 식 한 신중들이 이 방 저 방에서 들낙 날낙하는 것을 볼 때 한편으로 생각하면 신선하고, 한편으로 생각하면 처량도 하였다.
大善菴을 찾자다. 이 암자는 2, 3년 된 새 건물 경색 좋은 높직한 곳에 청아하게 있는대 청소년 時에 화류계에서 놀든 부인이 크게 깨다른 바 있어도 한적한 곳에서 수도하며 여생을 보내려고 사유 재산으로 死後 寺中 건물노 될 이 집을 가지고 있다. 부인의 능한 수완으로 어여뿌고 한 기술을 갖인 부인들을 끄으러 여름 한 철이면 海印寺에는 꽃이 피고 만다. 그 길노 올나가기 자못 숨이 찬 높직이 있는 願堂으로 올나갓다. 여기에는 인물 명물인 96세 된 林상궁 마마가 게시고 여기 監院 시님으로 게신 노장님은 有子 生女하고 오복이 具存한 분으로 자녀들이 눈물노 붓잡음에도 불구하고 떼치고 나와 수도하는 분이있다.＜238＞ 이 堂은 신라 제 4대 哀庄王이 海印寺를 창건하고 이어 3년간 여기 게서서 복을 비럿다 하야 哀庄王의 祈福地라 한다. 그 외에 보지 못한 곳이 淸凉菴이나 머러서 가지 못하였다.
명소로는 奉天臺, 會仙臺, 疊石臺, 霽月潭, 嘖玉瀑, 完在岩, 光風瀨, 吟風瀨, 泚肇岩, 翠積峰, 七星臺, 武陵橋, 遂花川, 更覓源, 其外 여름 한 철 서늘한 그늘을 맨드러 주는 고목 느트나무 있는 學生臺 놀기 좋은 不二亭 수박 참의 물에 당거 놓고 닭쩜하고 되지 고기 굽고 가진 나물에 점심을 해다가 신선이 바둑을 두든 너른 바위 우에서 수십 인의 友人으로 더부러 발을 벗고 우통을 버서 제치고 졋가락 수가락을 치우고 물에 씨서가며 손구락으로 집어 먹는 일종의 원시 만찬회를 하고 斯界에 전문가들이 노래 가락 육자배기 시조 春香歌 「좃타 좃타」 소리에 슬슬 넘어가고 이어 덩실덩실 춤을 춘다. 노세 젊어서 노라 늙고 병들면 못 노나니 좃타 얼시구나 절시구 조선춤 洋춤 일동은 한데 어울너저 춤을 춘다. 여기가 놀기 좋은 紫霞洞이다.

伽倻山 上峯行
우리 일행 10인은 점심을 한 짐 해 지우고 아침 일즉이 나섯다. 풀이 욱어진 좁은 길노 가다가 길을 잃고 방황도 하고 노래도 부르고 한다. K여사의 떠들석하는 소리로 떠들석하다.
伽倻山은 옛적 伽羅 연방의 要部로서 伽羅를 轉音하야 불교적으로 變稱한 것이다. 혹은 牛頭山 衆王山 只恒山, 靈山이라고도 한다. 此山은 大德山,의 기맥으로서 성주 고령 거창, 합천 4郡 사이에 盤聳하야 산고 해발 4,719척이오 면적이 3,328町步로 천연의 五葉松, 赤松, 단풍, 활엽수 등이 울창하고 기암 괴석이 용립하야 명쾌, 奇麗한 산이다. 전면으로는 남산 제일봉이 重重 포위하고 있다. 특히 上峰 牛鼻井과 白雲城 下 관음 석상은 探勝客에 목적지가 되어 있다. 어름꺼다가 사이다 당거 먹고 牛鼻井 물노 상치쌈을 싸서 먹으니 그 진미 말할 수 없으며 먹고난 후 다 각기 바위에 걸터 앉어 목침도리 창가를 하니 개미허리가 되다십히 우섯다. 여름 해도 얼마 안나 남아 도라오는 길에 풀이 욱어진 七佛菴 터를 찾엇다. 이곳에는 1,900년 전에 金首露土의 8왕자 중 1왕자는 태자를 봉하고 7왕자가 여기 와서 見性 득도하고 其後 河東 赫溪寺에서 결과하섯다 하니 雙溪寺 七佛菴이 7왕자의 결과한 곳이라 한다. 친척들이 보러오면 다 큰절 즉 海印寺 앞에 影池가 있으니 7왕자가 이＜239＞ 影池에 비최어 보였다는 전설이 있다. 우리 일행은 七佛岩에 높이 올안진 자 욱어진 수풀을 헤치고 옛날 그들의 기거하든 자리를 차자 보기도 하고 혹 우물을 차자 물을 바다먹는 자 기부를 거두어 절을 짓자는 둥 의논이 자자하다. 다리를 즐즐 끌고 오는 자 나는 선생에게 집행이 한 끗을 줘이고 끌녀오니 우수운 소리 잘 하는 Y가

佛事
부처님 일을 佛事라 하나니 탄생 佛事 (4월 8일) 7월 百種 佛事 (一目蓮 尊者가 그 어머니를 지옥에서 극락 세계로 천도한 날) 成過 佛事 (12월 8일) 沮盤 佛事 (2월 12일)이니 其中 4월 8일 佛事를 성대히 거행한다. 海印寺에서는 3월 금음께 쯤 되면 海印寺에서 약 3마정 되는 대 땅을 몇 평식 사서 전방을 차리나니 이 노점은 한 20여 호 되어 각 처에서 가진 각색 물건을 가저올 뿐 아니라 노점마다 시악씨의 노래 소리가 울녀 나오고 장구 소리가 울녀 나온다. 큰 절 적은 절 중들 여관객들 저녁 후에 산책으로 적이 위안이 된다. 초하로 날부터 사람이 점점 많아저서 4월 7, 8일 간은 여관은 물론 만원이요 집집마다 방 마루 터저 나간다. 4월 8일 날은 수십 만명의 참배자가 오고 가고 인산인해를 이루운다. 한번 볼만한 慶節이다. 지금은 장경각 佛事가 있으니 조선 총독이 10,000원을 내서 팔만 대장경을 복사하야 만주국 황제에게 헌상하는 것이다. 伽倻山 海印寺라고 쓴 정문에 禁斷傍이라고 크게 써 부치고 장경각 안에는 23조로 난흐아 복사 검열이 있고 총독부에서 나려온 技手들과 都監은 이것을 감독하고 있다. 2개월 넘어 동안 하다 이 佛事는 그 규모가 클 뿐 아니라 日賃 1원 3원식 밧는 중 속인들은 큰 버리가 될 뿐 한가하든 중들도 매일 8시간 식의 노동으로 밧부지 않을 소 없고 생산 능력이 없든 중들은 주머니 속에 돈 소리가 나게 되어 어느 방면으로 생각하든지 大事하고 아니볼 수 없다. 나도 몇 부인들과 동행하야 구경을 간 일이 있는대 한 번 볼만 하였다. 이 佛事가 끝나면 성대한 공양이 있고 염불이 있으리라고 한다.

土窟 生活
海印寺 경내에는 인가가 60여 호 있는대 이것을 토굴이라고 하야 중들은 크게 구별한다. 대개는 중들의 처가속들의＜240＞ 집이오 其外에는 속인의 집이다. 논마기나 있든지 宗務所에 사무원으로 월급이나 타든지 하면 근근이 생활을 유지하나 그러치 않으면 중들의 싹바질로 싹빨내로 그 생활 상태가 말이 아니다. 그러나 당국에서는 이 토굴을 정리시키라 한다.

鍾 소래
황혼의 종 소래 새벽 종 소래 욱어진 숩 사이로 은은이 멀니 들녀올 때 자연 머리가 숙어지고 새벽잠이 깨인다. 무심하다. 저 종 소래 엇지 그리 처량한지 내 수심을 돕는도다. 부지불식 중에 밀레의 「晩鐘」 생각이 아니날 수 없다. 임시로 불공 있을 때는 예외거니와 정기로는 매일 3차 예불이 있으니 이 때마다 사방 큰 절 적은 절에서는 뗑뗑 종을 울닌다. 즉 오전 4시 아침 예불 오전 11시 오정 예불 오후 6시 저녁 예불이 있어 부처님 앞에는 가사 장삼을 입은 부전 시님 監院 시님이 목탁을 치며 楞嚴呪 천수 다라니로 염불을 하고 이어 공양을 한다. 그 반찬이란 마눌도 안 는 김치의 푸른 채소뿐이다. 그러므로 그들의 얼골은 말근 빛이 도나 영양 부족으로 힘을 못 쓴다.

旅舘 生活
나는 海印寺 지정 여관 紀濤여관에 一客이 되었다. 이 여관은 海印寺에 없지 못할 편리를 주는 유일 무이한 여관으로 도회지에서 보기 쉽지 못하게 설비가 구비하야 있다. 나는 일찌기 歐米 漫遊時 요세미데산 중 여관에서 1주일간 지내본 일이 있는지라 자연 연상치 않을 수 없다. 그 요세미데 여관은 전부 인도식 건물과 장치이었다. 집과 장식품만 보아도 산중 생활에 실증 아니날 만치 된 데다 가진 오락 기관이며 딴스회 경마회가 있어 한시라도 심심한 때가 없나니 경마회란 것은 말을 조고만 나무로 맨든 것을 각색으로 모자를 쓴 客 중 미인들이 한 중년 부인이 번호를 부르면 말을 옴겨 놋는 것이다. 그러면 박수로 야단이오 익이는 말 편 사람들은 돈을 타너라고 야단들이다. 이 뿐 아니라 어여뿌고 젋은 미인들은 여기 저기서 불너내어 볼 만하다. 이러한 구경을 옛날에 한 나는 산중에 드러오니 더욱 회상이 되고 옛날이 그리워진다. 이런 여관은 언제나 그런 여관과 같이 되나 십다. 매일 3, 40명식은 떠날 새 없고 산중이라 물론 봄과 겨울은 세월 없을 것이오 여름 피서로 가을 단풍 구장으로 멫 만명식 출입이 있다. 더욱이 10여 명의 月泊 손님이 있을 때는 보기에도 눈살이 찝흐려 질 만치 마루 끝에는 화로에 약탕관이 열을 지어 죽 느러노였고 客 중에는 아직 펄펄 뛸만한 청년의 얼골이 노랑꽃이 피고 긔운이 척 느러저 느른한 자로 자리를 펴고 늘 두러눈 자 其中에는 혈기 왕성하야 단조로운 생활에 조바심을 치는 자 어떻게 놀면 잘 놀가 하야 산중 암자마다 계곡 골자기마다 매일 다니는 자 3일에 한번식은 2층 오락실에 서 이 장난군들이 뫃여 정종을 마시고 삐루를 마신 끝에 밥주발 뚝겅을 놋 저가락으로 뚜디리며 장구를 치고  손벽을 치고 발을 굴너 춤을 추다가 맨발로 마당까지 나려가 징둥징둥 뛴다. 나 외 몇 여자는 구경군이다. 이럼으로 여관은 분잡함과 食價가 빗싼 관계상 종용이 수양하러 오는 사람들은 암자로 가고 더욱 묘령 여자들은 일부러 피하야 승방으로 간다. 실로 누구든지 새벽부터 떠드러 옆 방 사람까지 잠을 못 자게 한다. 여하간 紅濤여관은 海印寺로는 없지 못할 곳이오 여객의 피로한 다리를 쉬게 하고 곤한 몸을 잠들게 하는 천당이오 극락일다.

僧侶 생활
海印寺 재적 승려가 남 398인이오 여 100인이다. 승려 생활이란 것은 塵世를 버서난 소위 物外 생활이나 단합과 규율로써 그 주를 삼는 것이오 승려는 즉 僧伽이며, 僧伽는 곳 화합의 意다. 生不同 姓不同의 각 인 각 씨로서 세속 塵埃를 버서나서 割愛捨親하고 출가 爲僧하야 입산 수도하는 이들의 일상 생활이 즉 승려 생활이다.
차등 각인각씨가 입산 수도하야 革凡 成聖을 목표로 도량(사원)에 투신하는 날이면 塵世 애욕을 멀니하고 佛法 도량에서 길리는 청풍 衲子의 몸이 되나니 불타의 무상 도법을 위하야 그들의 생활이 지속됨에 以和爲主하야 不分是非하야 以法爲會하야 互相扶助하니 이것이 곳 청렴 度生의 物外的 화합법인 동시에 승려 생활의 근본적 주간이 되는 것이다.
승려 생활의 대강은 이러하거니와 그 생활 질서를 유지하는 세측의 청규법 내용에 있어서는 일반 사회 대중 단체 생활에 추워 훨신 초월할 뿐 그 발달된 규정은 전반적으로 사회 단체의 모범이 되는 점이 불소함이니 승려의 그날 그날의 생활 일과를 소개하랴면 승려라면 총칭이 되려니와 승려 중에도 개인 개인의 위인의 자격에 따라 각 계단이 있다.
불교 전체에 있어서는 선종, 교종으로 分하야 있으니 선종이란 것은 소위 捨敎 入禪이라 하야 불타의 교리를 문자상을 통해서 그 一覽 해석한 후에 다시금 실제에 드러가 진리를 체득하는 卽離文字 외에 실제 眞如之理를 체험하야＜242＞ 대오 철저를 목표하는 참선인을 말함이오 교종이란 것은 불타의 一代時敎 (경전) 중 어느 것이든지 다 경전이면 교종에 속하나니 강사 혹은 승려로서 어느 경전이든지 전문으로 持誦하는 이는 통칭 교종이라 한다. 사원에서는 선종과 교종인을 理判人 事判人이라 하니 즉 선종은 理判이오 교종은 事判이라 구별한다. 그리하야 이 禪敎 양 종을 통하야 각자 위인의 자격 여하에 딸아서 대선사, 대교사, 선사, 대덕, 중덕, 대선, 沙彌 등의 계단이 있으며 이러한 자격을 양성함에는 각기 세칙의 청규법에 의하야 교양하고 있다.
먼저 선종의 일상 생활을 들어 간략히 말하면 그들의 생활은 모 - 든 것이 규율적이다. 1년 12개월을 통하야 각찰 禪院에서는 결제 해제법이 있어 하안거 동안거 법이 있다. 舊 4월 15일부터 결제하야 7월 15일에 해제하니 其間 3개월 간은 전심 참선하니 이를 하안거라 하고 10월 15일부터 결제하야 1월 15일에 해제하니 其間 3개월을 동안거라 한다. 안거 중 일상 생활이란 매일 오전 3시면 필히 기침하야 일제히 노소 없이 법당에 뫃여 불전에 香火를 사루고 예식을 맛친 후 同 6시까지 면벽 관심하야 참선을 하고 아침 공양을 하고(조반) 8시부터 10시까지 오후도 역시 1시부터 3시까지 6시부터 9시까지 참선을 하야 이와 같이 대중 수 십명이 동일 규율 하에서 如一히 九旬 안거를 종료하는 날이 해제일이고 해제 후 다음 결제일가지 3개월 동안은 고행을 닦기 위하야 동거하든 禪客들이 각자 걸망(鉢囊)을 질머지고 타처로 옴기여 到處線을 딸아 고행을 닥그되 혹은 성읍 부락에도 지나며 혹은 명산 대찰과 이름난 성지를 찾아서 심신을 맑히기도 하야 고행 중에서도 항상 도 닦는 것을 있지 아니하고 화두를 參究하다가 결제일이 도래하면 실 여전히 각처의 禪院으로 입방 (즉 名目을 드리는 것)하야 다시금 참선 공부를 시작하게 되는 것이다. 교종인의 생활은 보통 기침은 5시이고 기침 후 법당에 모여서 예식을 마친 다음에 각자 持誦하는 경전을 외우고 6시 30분 경이면 조반을 먹는다. 조반 후는 각자 임무를 쫓아서 종일 일과를 하게 되는대 其中 佛法중 대승 경전을 연구하는 학인들은 조반 후 약간 휴게한 후에 타종 집합하야 論講을 시작하니 論講이라는 것은 일종의 경전을 연구하는 것인대 학인이 3인이면 3인, 4인이면 4인이 1일간 연구하기 위튕�야 과정을 정하되 불경 책장 수를 동일히 정하야 그 날 일과로서 종일 각자 견해를 좇아서 연구한 것을 其 익일 조반 후에 論講하게 되나니 각기 견해가 동일할 때에는 아모 異動이 없지만은 만일 각기 연구한 견해가 다를 경우에는 朝室 (선생) 和尙許로 가서 판결을 하는 것이다. 이리하야 一代 時敎를 연구하는 가온데서 大諸師 대법사 포교사가 생겨나는 것이다. 三界 大聖인 釋迦牟尼의 법도량에서 청정한 몸으로 길드리는 승려 생활이란 참으로 신성한 가운대서 人天의 大法器를 이루는 곳으로서 가히 부러워 아니할 수 없다.＜243＞
