**Body Text Translation**
Sŏnu Ilsŏn and Ch'oe Nam-yong - A Q&A Regarding Popular Songs

Here is one piece of good news for you, readers living in the same world. Today, Ms. Sŏnu Ilsŏn and Mr. Ch'oe Nam-yong have come together in one place. As you know better than anyone, isn't Mr. Ch'oe widely known through T'aep'yŏng Records, and Ms. Ilsŏn through Polydor Records?

Perhaps you, dear readers, have spent sleepless nights enjoying the records they recorded, delighting your ears.
- No, who can guarantee that after listening and listening to the songs, unable to overcome your affectionate heart, you haven't sent a heartfelt "letter" overflowing with admiration, saying, "I love your songs"?
- Please wait a moment.
I will now introduce these two individuals to you. How could I possibly listen to the stories of these people you cherish and love so much all by myself and keep a straight face? I feel like I need to tell everything from beginning to end, without omitting a single detail, to feel relieved myself.

The two entered the room together. Ms. Ilsŏn was wearing a fox fur scarf, some kind of long yellow silk *turumagi* (overcoat), cotton poplin *pŏsŏn* (socks), and rubber shoes<144>. Her slender face, clear eyes with double eyelids - (but eyes moist like the sea) - had deep eyelashes, her mouth and ears were quite small, her face very fair, but her hair was extremely black, and her height and build were just right.

Mr. Ch'oe Nam-yong is imposing. He is tall and has a considerable build. He wore a poplin *turumagi* and a navy blue striped scarf. He is quite handsome. It wouldn't be unreasonable for the young ladies who like his songs to whisper among themselves, "Isn't Ch'oe Nam-yong handsome?"

With a tea table laden with coffee and fresh fruit placed between them, the two sat facing each other, looked up, and asked the same question simultaneously:
"Haven't you been recording recently?" This seems inevitable. Just as lawyers gather and always talk about court cases, and butchers gather and discuss beef prices, these two also first brought up the topic closest to their own lives.

Ch'oe: I went recently. Our company has few singers, so we go six times a year.

Ilsŏn: Oh my, you go that often? Our company only goes twice a year, in spring and fall. Going so frequently, how many records do you record each time?

Ch'oe: Three or four. How many do you record at once, Ms. Ilsŏn?

Ilsŏn: Sometimes five, sometimes a bit more.

Ch'oe: In any case, since you possess the utmost popularity, the number of records probably doesn't matter.

Ilsŏn: Oh, not at all. You must be the one like that, sir. I'm now completely tired of it and don't want to do it anymore.

Ch'oe: You're already tired of it? How many years has it been?

Ilsŏn: It's already been 5 years. I started when I was seventeen.

Ch'oe: Has it really been that long? It's only been 6 years for me. So you started a year after me. What was your very first recording?

Ilsŏn: I did several, but among them, the one called "Holding Flowers" (꽃을 잡고) turned out rather well.

Ch'oe: Ah, right, it was "Holding Flowers"! I was walking down the street, and that song came out of a music store's loudspeaker gramophone. I didn't know how good it was. It was a sound I'd never heard before, so I wondered who it could be. Gradually, hearing people talk in various places,<145> the rumor was tremendous that a new rising singer had come up from P'yŏngyang, good-looking and a great singer.

Ilsŏn: Please don't say that. It's embarrassing.

Audience Member: What are your feelings (惑想) when standing before the microphone? (The audience member got curious and asked a question. Please forgive me.)

Ilsŏn: At first, maybe because I didn't know what I was doing, I didn't even realize I was trembling. But now that I keep doing it, just standing in front of the microphone makes my heart pound. Perhaps initially, people around me praised me, saying "Good job, good job," and I truly believed them, thinking I was genuinely good, so I had confidence. But when I listen to the released records, I'm not satisfied. I repeatedly discover things like, "I shouldn't have done that part that way," or "Next time, I need to be careful about this part," so the microphone seems to become increasingly frightening.

Ch'oe: It's the same for me. At first, I was full of confidence, but now I gradually find only flaws in my songs.

Audience Member: That indicates progress. How many records have you recorded so far?

Ch'oe: It must be around 100.

Ilsŏn: Oh my, that's quite a lot. Mine is merely 40. Among those 100, which one do you think turned out the best?

Ch'oe: Well, let me see. I can't say any are particularly outstanding, but "Did I Come Here to Cry?" (울니랴 왔든가) seems decent. I don't know about its sales performance, though. You must have several, Ms. Ilsŏn?

Ilsŏn: It's the same for me. I'm embarrassed to say any turned out exceptionally well, but perhaps the very first one I recorded, "Holding Flowers," was passable.

Audience Member: Haven't you had any failures?

Ilsŏn: Well. It seems there aren't any that could be called exceptionally good, nor any complete failures.

Ch'oe: Same here. When recording, if it doesn't work out, we can fix it however many times needed. Still, wouldn't there be some that are embarrassing to release to the market? But when the company releases them without regard for such things, it's truly embarrassing.

Ilsŏn: That's right. When I hear a flawed song I sang on the street or somewhere, I feel so embarrassed I wish I could hide somewhere.

Audience Member: When a song you sang drifts over a neighbor's wall, what is your feeling at that moment?<146>

Ilsŏn: There's certainly embarrassment, but more than that, there's a greater sense of pleasure. And when I think that people love the songs of someone like me, I feel so grateful I could cry.

Ch'oe: That's really true. This happened when I was returning from my last recording trip. Friends had me stay overnight in Pusan. That night, there was a fierce storm, and somewhere, someone seemed to be playing my record "Did I Come Here to Cry?". Outside, lightning struck, and rain poured down heavily. When that sound came drifting from somewhere unknown, far away, I became completely sentimental and felt like crying.

Audience Member: Have you ever gone on regional tours?

Ch'oe: I haven't. I performed a couple of times in Kyŏngsŏng, but that was already several years ago at the Public Hall when I was with Victor.

Ilsŏn: I've toured a lot. There probably isn't a place in North or South Chosŏn (南北鮮) I haven't been.

Ch'oe: Performances need to be done well, too. If done poorly or rashly, it only causes harm. Ms. Ilsŏn, tell us about some impressions you gained from your regional performances.

Ilsŏn: I don't have any particular feelings. The very first time I went to Tokyo for recording, I cried terribly because I missed home, but after traveling a lot, it's good that habit disappeared. However, standing on stage is much harder than standing before the microphone. When recording, only the musicians are behind me, and just a microphone is in front. Plus, if I make a mistake, I can fix it again, so I feel relieved. But the stage isn't like that. As soon as I go out, my heart pounds, and my legs tremble. Because I'm so timid, when I see people laughing and doing things on stage, all I can think is how brave they are.

Audience Member: Among the various places you've visited, were there any you particularly liked?

Ilsŏn: Sŏnch'ŏn in North P'yŏngan Province was nice, and besides that, Hamhŭng in South Hamgyŏng Province was good. I like places that are neither big cities nor completely rural. I'd like to go and live there someday.

Audience Member: What records have recently come out on the streets?

Ch'oe: There are ones called "When I Get the Bonus" (賞與金을 타면) and "Harbor Style" (港口의 스타일).

Ilsŏn: I don't even know what's out. They say the records came out a month ago, but I haven't gone to the company to see them, nor have I listened to them.

Ch'oe: You have a peculiar temperament. After recording,<147> I wait with bated breath for the test pressing to arrive from the Tokyo headquarters.

Audience Member: What are your favorite masterpieces?

Ch'oe: Rather than masterpieces, I always love the songs sung by Skippa Nazīr (스키-퍼 나질-리).

Ilsŏn: I don't know any masterpieces. To be honest, I still don't understand the deep principles of music. Wasn't there an opera called "Madame Butterfly" (호접夫人) by Miura Tamaki (三浦環) at the Pumin'gwan sometime ago? At that time, all my friends said it was good, but I actually found it less enjoyable than listening to popular songs (*yuhaengga*). Doesn't that prove I don't know music?

Audience Member: Have you met famous popular singers in Tokyo?

Ch'oe: Yes, I've met Fujiyama Ichirō (藤山一郞) and Katsutarō (勝太郞), among others.

Ilsŏn: I haven't met them. It seems one doesn't easily meet singers from other companies.

Audience Member: Do those people also make corrections when they record?

Ch'oe: They do it much more than we do. Sometimes ten or twenty times. Wouldn't they? Once recorded, hundreds of thousands of copies are sold. In any case, they greatly respect the singer's mood. Moreover, since popular songs are sung with feeling, when those people record upbeat things like jazz, they dance and make a fuss in front of the microphone. They do it just like on stage. And when doing sad songs, they look genuinely sad, as if about to cry any moment.

Audience Member: Do you pay attention to things like food because of your voice?

Ilsŏn: I can't pay attention at all. Because I naturally like salty and spicy food more than others, it probably has a significant effect on my voice. And I can never get enough sleep. Sleeping during the day doesn't feel like sleeping at all.

Ch'oe: That's right. The voice needs good sleep. Although I don't particularly avoid any foods, I always try to get enough sleep.

Audience Member: Neither of you are married, right? (Will you criticize me for bringing up such a topic?)

Ch'oe: I've already been married for 3 years.

Ilsŏn: Was it a love marriage?

Ch'oe: In any case, I waited fervently until she graduated from school.

Ilsŏn: I have no thoughts of wanting to marry, nor thoughts of not wanting to marry. What marriage could there be for someone like<148> me? I'll just live alone my whole life and die. However, it's true that things are different this year compared to last year, and last year compared to the year before. For no reason, I feel strangely lonely and sad. What's the point of living like this, or saving money? I often think about these things alone in my quiet room after returning late at night.

Ch'oe: Then you should get married quickly.

Audience Member: Aren't there people who send letters or come looking for you because they like listening to your records?

Ch'oe: There are people who come looking, and also people who send letters.

Ilsŏn: Oh, sending letters is less of a problem, but sending their unflattering photos and insisting I send my photo back gives me a headache. However, for letters written with utmost sincerity without a single vulgar phrase, I feel like I should send a reply. But as a woman, especially someone like me, I don't know if it's right to send letters, so although I feel grateful in my heart, I end up not replying. Once, as I was about to leave the broadcasting station after a live broadcast, a telegram arrived from Tokyo. The sender's name was given as "Ryu" (류), but how could I know who it was? I was always curious afterwards. Then, a few days later, a letter came from that person. The letter didn't say much, just that listening to my songs in a foreign land (異鄕) moved them deeply. After that, there was no further news, but just recently, someone told me this: A friend of theirs buys and listens to whatever records of mine come out. So I asked who that person was. They said he studied in Tokyo, graduated last spring, and is now working as a lawyer in Seoul. They asked me to arrange a meeting just once, but I don't know how it will turn out. I'm very curious. And another thing happened last winter. Every night after midnight, someone would come over my house wall and whistle. The tune was from one of my records. One night, even though snow was softly falling continuously, they sang for a long time. I don't know how melancholic it felt. Although I wondered who the person could be, I couldn't go out to see. Sitting inside my room, thinking about that unknown person, my heart felt both happy and troubled. (At this point, Ms. Ilsŏn's face, having told this story, was full of yearning for that unknown person.) (Continued on page 134)<149>

Ch'oe: Oh my, you should have gone out to see!

Ilsŏn: Maybe I should have. They did that for about a week and then stopped. Thinking about it, maybe it's better not to have met. If it was someone I didn't like, it would be a big problem. Having someone I truly dislike follow me around persistently is worse than death and terrifying.

Audience Member: Thank you for sharing your stories. We hope to hear more talks like this often in the future.

The audience member, having thus thanked the two, immediately ran to the editorial office and quickly wrote down the conversation they had before forgetting it. However, please understand that this is done purely for the fans of Ms. Sŏnu Ilsŏn. <134> <144-149, 134>

**Summary**

This 1938 article presents an interview between two popular Korean recording artists of the colonial era, Sŏnu Ilsŏn (Polydor) and Ch'oe Nam-yong (Taepyeong). They discuss their recording schedules, anxieties about performing and recording quality, fan interactions (including letters, gifts, and even a mysterious whistler), regional tours, favorite singers (including foreign ones like Skippa Nazīr and Japanese singers), and personal feelings about fame, music appreciation, and marriage. Sŏnu Ilsŏn expresses growing weariness with her career and loneliness, while Ch'oe Nam-yong appears more settled. The interview provides insights into the lives and thoughts of Korean popular singers operating within the Japanese-controlled record industry, highlighting their professional routines, pressures, and personal reflections during that period. Historians might find the discussion of recording practices (including multiple takes, influence of Japanese singers, reliance on Tokyo HQs), the singers' mobility within Korea and trips to Japan, fan culture, and their candid expressions about career satisfaction and personal life particularly interesting for understanding cultural production and individual experiences under colonialism.

기쁜 소식 한 가지를 가튼 세상에 살고 있는 여러분 독자께 전합니다. 그것은 오늘 어느 장소에서 鮮于一扇, 崔男鏞 양씨가 서로 한 자리에 뫃이게 되었읍니다. 여러분이 더 잘 아시다싶이 崔씨는 太平레코-드에서, 一扇씨는 포리돌레코-드로 그 일홈을 세상에 널니 알니고 있지 안습니까?
모르긴 하겠읍니다만은 필시 여러분은 씨들이 취입한 레코-드로 한 밤을 새우며 귀를 즐겁게 하는 일이 있지 않으실는지요.
- 아니 노래를 듯다가 듯다가 못해서 사라스런 마음을 못이겨 「나는 당신의 노래를 사랑합니다」하고 사모의 정이 넘치는 정성 있는 「글발」을 보내는 일이 없으리라고 누가 보증하리까.
- 가만 게십시요.
두 분을 여러분 앞에 이제 소개해 더릴테니까요. 그처럼 여러분이 애끼고 사랑하시는 이들의 이얘기를 나 혼자 듯고 시침을 딱 떼여서야 될 말슴입니까. 처음부터 끝까지 하나 빼지 않고 죄다 해버려야 나도 속시원할 것 같읍니다.

두 분이 똑같이 문 안에 드러섰읍니다. 一扇씨는 여호목도리에 무슨 노-란 비단두루매기를 길게 입고 옥양목보선에 고무신을＜144＞ 하고 잇섯는데 갸름한 얼골에 쌍가풀 안진 시원스런 눈은 - (그러나 바다와 같이 저즌 눈입니다) 속눈섭을 깊숙히 가지고 입과 귀는 무척 적고 얼골은 매우 히나 머리는 또 몹시 껌고, 키나 몸이 알마추 좃습니다.
崔南鏞씨는 거대합니다. 키도 크도 몸집도 상당해요. 뽀쁘린두르매기에 남색 줄진 목도리를 둘넜읍니다. 썩 잘난 분입니다. 그의 노래를 좋아하는 아가씨들이 「崔남용이가 미남이라지?」하고 서로들 얘기할 것도 무리는 아니겠어요.
양 씨가 고-히와 신선한 푸릇즈 담은 다과상을 가운데, 서로 마조 앉어 서로들 치여다 보고 똑같이 뭇는 말슴이
「요새 취입 안 가심니까?」하는 것입니다. 이건 할 수 없는 일인가바요. 변호사가 뫃이면 언제이나 재판소얘기가 나오고 소고기장수가 뫃이면 소시세를 논하고 하는 거나 마찬가지고 그 두 분 역시 자기 생활의 갓가운 이 얘기를 제일 먼저 하게 되였든 것입니다.
崔.   전번에 갔다 왔읍니다. 우리회사엔 가수가 적기 때문에 1년에 여섯번식 가니까요.
一扇. 아니 그렇게 많이 가서요. 우리회사에선 봄, 가을 두번 박게 안 가는데요. 그렇게 자조 가서서 한 번에 멫 장식이나 취입하심니까.
崔. 서너 장씩 하죠. 一扇씨는 한 번에 멫 장식이나 하시나요.
一扇. 다섯 장식두 하구 좀 더하는 때도 있읍니다.
崔. 어쨌든 최고의 인기를 가지고게시니까 멫 장이든 상관없을 것입니다.
一扇. 아이 천만에요. 선생님이야말로 그러실 거얘요. 전 인제 딱 실증이 나서 하기가 싫어 요.
崔. 벌서 실증이 나서야 되겠습니까. 멫 해나 되시는데요.
一扇. 벌서 5년 됏죠. 열일곱 살부터 했으니까요.
崔. 벌서 그렇게 되시든가요. 제가 6년박게 안되는데요. 그러니까 저보다 한 해 늦어진 심 이군요. 처음 취입하신 것이 저- 뭐든가요?
一扇. 여러 개를 햇지만 그 중에서 「꽃을 잡고」라는 것이 좀 낳었지요.
