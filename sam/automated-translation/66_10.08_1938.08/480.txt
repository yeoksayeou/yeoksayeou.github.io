Article Title: 靑春抄, 勇氣와 朝鮮 靑年 (Anthology of Youth, Courage and the Korean Youth)
Author: 崔南善 외 (Ch'oe Namsŏn et al.)
Article Type: 논설 (Editorial/Essay)

Anthology of Youth, Courage and the Korean Youth
						Ch'oe Namsŏn et al.

1. Courage and the Korean Youth

During my stay in Tokyo this past spring, the memory that left the strongest impression was the stunt flying (呈才 비행) of the American, Art Smith. It has only been a few decades since the airplane, that favored child (寧馨兒) of modern science, emerged, and the realm of the air (氣界) gradually entered humanity's sphere of influence. As a result of the constant innovation and competition among Westerners, who strive for improvement and are brave in adventure, the altitude of ascent, duration of flight, speed of propulsion, and practical efficiency increase day by day. News flashes of new records frequently startle the eyes and ears of the world, a fact known to all. However, in recent years, the focal point of amazement has been the so-called stunt flying. Flying through the air normally has already become commonplace, regarded as ordinary (凡視). Unless one opens a new realm of wonder and distinction and presents something extraordinary, one cannot maintain the favor and glory (寵榮) of the era. Thus, the principle of progress finally produced this strange and dangerous (奇險) new skill. There are various kinds of stunt flying, but the most common are loop-the-loops (筋斗 비행), spiral dives (나선 비행), and figure eights (象物 비행?). Needless to say, these are all performed in the air while riding in the aircraft. The performer displays their talent with a death-defying attitude, while the spectators are utterly astonished and frightened. This skill is particularly prevalent in America, known for its curiosity and love of novelty, and consequently, there are many skilled performers. Art Smith, a young man barely past (纔過) his youth, is renowned not only for being peerless in his country for his bold courage (담豪勇) and outstanding skill but also throughout the world. His daring wherever he went attracted great fame, and his skillful technique earned him substantial sums. He came all the way to the land of cherry blossoms and mulberry trees (櫻候桑域 - Japan) to stir the quiet skies of the East. Amidst a feverish welcome, he displayed his masterful skills in various places. As I happened to be staying (適留) there due to circumstances, witnessing the actual event was indeed a fortunate encounter, one might say.

2
His Tokyo performance was scheduled for three days, April 8th, 9th, and 10th. I watched on the final day. The airfield, Aoyama Heigen (靑山坪), was a parade ground where yellow dust had been pulverized by years of human feet and horse hooves. It was located at the very entrance where the famous Tsukuba Tanikaze (筑波谷風 - Tsukuba Valley Wind) rushes straight across the Musashi Kōya (武藏 광야 - Musashi Plain) and invades Tokyo. Therefore, even on ordinary days, sand clouds (沙團) flew in all directions, earning it the name "Sea of Dust" (鹿海 - possibly referring to the dusty plain, literal meaning 'deer sea'). On this day, however, a strong wind that had blown since the previous night raged (逞威) continuously. Even in the city's surrounding streets, flying stones <266> struck people's faces. The dust storm over the Aoyama area was beyond description. Even ordinary horses and people found it difficult to pass through, let alone an airplane, and stunt flying at that! The people of Tokyo, deeply impressed by his superb skills over the past few days, all thought, "No matter how great his courage or how immense his spirit, how could he possibly show off his skills today? It must surely be postponed," and resigned themselves prematurely. However, past 1 PM, unexpectedly, the sound of a cannon roared loudly in the western sky – this was the signal for the flight to proceed. How could I have been the only one startled upon hearing the cannon's sound? Everyone must have felt the same. And not just people; the Wind God (風伯), who had been threatening since morning, must have been even more dismayed. Consequently, rushing out onto the street, battling the billowing robe sleeves (袍袖) and restraining the fluttering lapels and hems (襟裾), I barely managed to reach the vicinity of Aoyama. Sand clouds enveloped the sky above, and dust fog shrouded everything below. The scene of the yellow, dusty field – phrases like "sun obscured, sky lightless, sand swirling, stones flying" fail to capture even a fraction of it. One couldn't open one's eyes or nose even for a moment. Forget the pilot's adventure; just being a spectator was already a great adventure. It was hardly surprising that many in the crowd (會衆), unable to endure (堪耐) for even ten minutes, turned back one after another. My companion also urged (促) me to return, suggesting we escape the suffering of facing the wind first, even if it meant losing the chance to see the skill forever. However, I waited patiently (堅忍 竚待), not so much to see the feat of the flight taking place, but rather mostly (居多) out of a desire to resolve (析) the doubt about whether it would happen or not. The wind force increased with the hour, reported at 32 meters (米突) per second on the ground. The consensus among all hearts was that it would be utterly impossible. Is it possible? Is it impossible? Does nature win, or does human effort fail? With the feeling of waiting for the curtain to fall on a drama of scientific doubt, I supported myself by holding onto a utility pole and wiped the tears from my eyes facing the wooden fence, just waiting for the engine's roar (嗚). Suddenly, a sound arose from the center of the field, followed by another sound. Shielding my eyes with my whole palm like eaves (簷), I forced my closed eyes open. Where a dust devil shaped like a sheep's horn (羊角) shot straight up into the sky, wings like dragon scales (龍鱗) leaped about within it! On this day, in this sky, where even flying birds folded their wings (翮) and dared not show their shadows, Smith's aircraft alone thrust forward its courageous form. While all creation bowed down where the sun wheel turned, a single blue-eyed boy boldly took a stance of resistance. The Wind God's (巽二 - perhaps referring to the trigram Xun associated with wind) angry hair must surely be pushing his cap off!

3
A cry of "Ah!" (喲) erupted from ten thousand mouths. Unable to fully open their eyes due to the incoming sand and dust, they looked up at the hazy yellow sky like people with eye ailments. A strange expression, a mixture of astonishment and fear, flooded their faces. If the ground was like this, what must the wind conditions be like in the air? If those standing were like this, what about the turbulence experienced by the one flying? His courage in finally proceeding chilled everyone's hearts and, at the same time, made me taste (嘗케 하다) the disappointment of a paradox (파라독쓰); for what I had expected to be impossible <268> turned out, unexpectedly, to be possible. Alas! (嗟홉다) Oh, that young boy who gives the living lesson that before the brave, nothing is truly impossible! I believe (信) that even ghosts and spirits yield to your courage to decide and act. While the crowd of tens of thousands (累萬), their spirits lost (褫魄) and instead suppressing rather than cheering his reckless bravery, maintained a silence of admiration that transcended applause and cheers, he received the fierce wind's assault head-on, briefly disappeared into the sand cloud, then suddenly revealed his brave form again and ascended with all his might. Only then did shouts of "Mansei!" (萬歲聲 - Long live!), which certainly did not reach his ears, erupt like ten thousand thunderclaps simultaneously. As he ascended, the wind speed increased. Like a small swallow (小燕) chased by a fierce falcon (猛隼), tossed about (翻飄) by the violent wind, he battled the wind, heading south and returning north, circling four times above the parade ground. Fearing that any mistake (差失) would cause the aircraft to be blown away, the engine roared at full power, struggling to hold its position. Though it was an aircraft with 100 horsepower, for a moment it seemed barely able to stay stationary in the air. During this time, more than 10 minutes passed. Since his fuel tank could only last for 30 minutes, unsure of what fate the aircraft might meet, the officials looked at each other aghast, some waving (揮) hats, others shaking handkerchiefs, urgently signaling him to descend. Needless to say, he did not see them. He climbed even higher, reaching 800 meters. The crowd murmured, "Just getting airborne is amazing enough. A loop-the-loop (筋斗) is too much to hope for," watching the single point, the single stroke of the drifting (標搖) aircraft's shadow with flustered (蒼黃) expressions as if facing a great crisis. The aircraft, having reached the extreme height of 800 meters, gradually descended following the wind direction. Then, at about 600 meters, with an extreme upward pull on the controls, deciding to risk death, he performed a loop-the-loop in a flash (閃忽). Even the fierce wind shaking the earth's axis (坤軸) could not obstruct this world-class birdman's marvelous skill; he performed loop-the-loops consecutively. Each time he inverted, the aircraft drifted, causing the spectators to bite their lips and clench their fists. After skillfully executing three inversions, he suddenly pointed the nose downwards, performing his specialty, the spiral dive, down to about 400 meters. There, he seemed to level the nose horizontally again, but then performed two more loop-the-loops. Finally, with a desperate descent, he landed in the southern corner of the field. His speed was so great that it was not surprising that spectators in the distance mistook it for a crash and rushed towards the spot. Having demonstrated his divine skill for 15 minutes and established the complete mastery of flight, shouts of "Mansei!" poured down on him like thunder and rain (雷灌雨注). Witnessing this grand spectacle of modern civilization before my eyes, at the peak of wonder (駕奇) and overwhelmed with emotion, I could only be frantic in heart and dumbstruck in mouth. Barely regaining composure, cries of "Indeed! Indeed!" (是哉是哉) burst from my mouth (衝口), which I could not suppress. This scene before my eyes suddenly revealed the core of great modern civilization, explained the essence of the prosperity and power of the vibrant West, and loudly proclaimed directly that the rise and fall of the present age is certainly fitting (固宜) and the fortunes and misfortunes of people today are natural. I could not help but bow deeply in gratitude (頂禮 감사) to the young Smith, who allowed me to instantly grasp and directly perceive the secret of civilizational progress more clearly than ten thousand volumes of history, more powerfully than the treatises of a hundred scholars. <268> How loud could my voice have been, yet I could not help but shout "Mansei!" three times, imbued with a sincerity more profound than anyone else's. This feat, which veterans and experts in the field all advised against (勸止) and the multitude acknowledged as impossible, was undoubtedly undertaken having resolved to die once. It transcended life and death, profit and desire, vanity, and fleeting fame. It was carried out solely through the spiritual manifestation of a tense, heightened great conviction and great courage. Truly, I could not help but receive this precious lesson with tears of emotion and reverence. While everyone praised it as the highest record of braving a fierce wind, I resolved to retain the impression of this day forever as my life's living knowledge, true knowledge.

4
The young hero of conquering the sky, expressing the true meaning of taming the fierce wind, said, "The reason I decided to undertake this flight, not even listening to everyone's advice against it, was to prove my own conviction that an airplane can fly in any weather. If I had cancelled today's flight, my usual boasts would have all amounted to lies (謊說). If one has skills one can be truly confident in, what does the weather matter? Today's event should suffice to demonstrate that the era of the airplane's absolute safety has arrived." Risking his life to express his confidence, braving life and death to prove a principle – consider how the great modern civilization was born from such noble convictions and the noble actions that manifest them in reality. Should we not view his adventure today not merely as the adventurous stunt of a daring aviator, but through it, contemplate the spiritual driving force and inner life of the broad, deep, and profound modern civilization? Does it not provide concrete proof through powerful facts for the abstract theories obtained from everyday books and contemplation? I heard that when he grips the airplane's controls, he enters a kind of meditative state (三昧地), free from any danger, difficulty, or fear, navigating the sky as if on a smooth path. And that reaching such a state involved extraordinary anguish and effort, passing through life and death several times, struggling in dire straits numerous times, but with a single-minded determination to forge ahead unyieldingly (邁往不屈), he finally escaped the clouds of despair and saw the sun of success. First is courage; second is strength (剛力); third is the cultivation of that strong courage (剛勇). These three are not only the main conditions for Smith's flying but are indeed the indispensable qualifications for pioneers of civilization throughout history, the very midwives of modern civilization.

5
What is called modern civilization, viewed from a materialistic perspective (형이하), is the development of science and its applications; discussed metaphysically (형이상), it is the overflowing expression of the progressive spirit. The Renaissance (復興) was its dawn, the Reformation its precursor, the discovery of geography its guide (嚮導), mechanical invention and the Industrial <270> Revolution its main drivers, and the rapid advance (驟進) of economic conditions and the complete transformation of society's appearance are parts of its results. All these things, needless to say, accumulated the merit of daily progress and renewal through diligent inquiry into principles and strong courage in execution, finally unfolding the brilliant situation of the 20th century. What about Dante? What about Luther? What about Columbus? What about Watt? What, indeed, is the common characteristic of those who undertook great roles and heavy responsibilities on the stage of reforming the old and establishing the new? It is diligence transcending life, death, glory, and disgrace; it is strong courage (剛勇) transcending hardship and suffering. The young boy reflected in my eyes today is by no means just an American youth named Art Smith. He is the incarnation (化身) of the author of the Divine Comedy walking and reciting (行吟) by the riverbank (澤畔); he is the manifestation (權現) of the discoverer of the New World struggling on the ocean (洋上); he is Martin himself, who roared like a lion (사자후) in the Roman Curia; he is James himself, who poured out his heart and soul in苦心 瀝血) the poor alleys of Glasgow. How precious! A single act from a hero of the sky has shown before my very eyes the true face and living model of so many past sages. How could I ever try not to remember it?
However, such brave men are not limited to Smith alone; countless technicians exist beyond him. Not just in the field of aviation, but countless strivers exist in various other fields. Not only among the young and vigorous, but also many among the old. In essence, the civilized world refers to a society where people from all fields, all classes, old and young, men and women, strive diligently (乾乾 勉勉) moment by moment with such spirit and energy – a place where unified progress and collective activity occur. Citing here the example of an old hero in the advancement of literary culture, also an American contemporary, might be very interesting. Not only does this person's life have a most significant relationship to recent civilization, but his strength (剛力) and courage growing stronger with age (老去益壯) might perhaps teach us facts even greater than Smith's. - Taishō 6 - <270>

**Summary**

The author, deeply impressed by American aviator Art Smith's daring stunt flight in Tokyo despite dangerous winds, uses the event as a powerful metaphor. He extols Smith's courage and determination as embodying the core spirit of modern Western civilization – a spirit characterized by relentless progress, scientific advancement, and the willingness to overcome any obstacle through bravery and conviction. The author explicitly contrasts this dynamism with the perceived situation of Korean youth, implicitly urging them to adopt this courageous spirit to overcome challenges. The piece connects Smith's feat to historical figures like Dante, Luther, Columbus, and Watt, portraying them all as embodiments of the same pioneering courage necessary for civilizational progress.

From a colonial history perspective, the article is fascinating. Written by a prominent Korean intellectual, Ch'oe Namsŏn, during the Japanese colonial period, it uses an American example (pointedly not Japanese) to inspire Korean youth. While praising Western dynamism, it implicitly critiques the perceived stagnation or lack of agency among Koreans under colonial rule, framing "courage" as essential for progress and perhaps subtly hinting at national self-strengthening. The focus on individual willpower and transcending limitations could be interpreted as a call for Koreans to find internal strength even within the constraints of colonialism.

勇氣와 朝鮮 靑年 1
今春 동경 체류 중 가장 강렬한 인상을 기억에 留한 자는 미국인 아트, 스미드의 呈才 비행이로다. 근세 과학의 寧馨兒로 비행기가 出來하여 氣界가 점차로 인류의 세력권 내로 入하게 됨은 불과 수 십년 이래의 事 ㅣ 라는 向上에 勤하고 모험에 勇한 列西國人의 爭新 競進하는 결과로 昇騰의 고도와 항속의 시간과 추진의 속력과 실용의 능률이 日昇 月恒하야 신기록 創開의 飛電이 분분히 세계의 이목을 驚動함은 人我의 共知하는 사실이어니와 최근 수 년간 가장 驚奇의 초점이 된 자는 소위 呈才的 비행이니 尋常 一樣으로 御風駛空함은 이미 당연 底事로 凡視하게 되매 神奇 卓異한 新境을 開하고 別軸을 出함이 아니면 시대의 寵榮을 유지치 못하게 된지라. 於是에 진보의 원칙이 드대어 此 奇險한 新技를 산출하였도다. 呈才 비행도 諸種이 有하나 그 중 보편한 자는 筋斗 비행, 나선 비행, 象物 비행 등이오 母論 다 飛器에 乘坐한 채로 공중에서 演하는 것이니 演者도 결사적 태도로 呈才함이러니와 觀者 - 도로혀 驚神 悸魂하며 此技도 好奇尙新으로 特著한 미국에 尤盛하고 隨하야 능수도 多하다더라. 아트 스미드란 자는 약관을 纔過한 一 소년이나 *담豪勇과 卓能秀技로 국내에 絶論할 뿐 아니라 전 세계에 명성이 隆隆한 자 - 라 그의 도처에 대담은 盛名을 招하고 巧技는 重幣를 獲하더니 적막한 동양의 空界를 교란할 次로 櫻候桑域에 遠來하야 狂熱的 환영 중 諸處에서 熟手段을 발휘할 새 余 - 因事하야 此地에 適留하다가 그 실황을 목격하니 또한 一 기연이라 可謂할 지로다.
