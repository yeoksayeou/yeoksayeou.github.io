Article Title: 었더케 하면 半島 藝術을 發興케 할가 (How Can We Revitalize the Art of the Peninsula?)
Author: 「映畵와 演劇」 協議會 (Film and Theater Council)
Article Type: 문예평론 (Literary and Arts Criticism)

How Can We Revitalize the Art of the Peninsula?
						Film and Theater Council
Date: January 20, Shōwa 13 (1938), 3:00 PM

Place: Seoul Chosŏn Hotel

Ch'oe Sangdŏk, Manager of Tongyang Theater

Yi Kisĕ, Supervisor at Kisin Yanghaeng

The late Yŏn Hangnyŏn, Supervisor at Chungang Mudae

Yi Ch'angyong, Koryŏ Film Distribution Center

Yi Kuyŏng, Head of Publicity Department at Tansŏngsa

Our Side (Samch'ŏlli Magazine): Kim Tonghwan
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi

In today's cultural sphere, the power that film and theater hold over general society is truly surprisingly large and wide. Indeed, the audience for film or theater is far, far stronger than for literary works produced by writers or compositions created by musicians. Thus, ordinary people have all come to hold a deep interest in film and theater.
At this time, what is the standard of film and theater in Chosŏn society? Furthermore, one cannot help but think about how we might develop these enterprises.
Now, society, taking up these few issues for the first time, has decided to gather the authorities and seniors of our film and theater worlds in one place to hear their ambitions and profound ideas. (Lastly, one sad thing is that after this roundtable discussion and before this article was published, Mr. Yŏn Hangnyŏn unfortunately passed away.)<84>

How much does it cost to produce a film?
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Thank you all for coming despite your busy schedules. Well then, I will now begin listening to your words. Please share many interesting and beneficial remarks. By the way, lately, there seem to be more people investing in film and theater, and there appears to be a trend where film and theater are regarded as much more important than literature. How is it? For the recent film "Nagŭnae" (The Wanderer), how much was the production cost, and what were the box office results?
Yi Ch'angyong: It's hard to know the exact production cost for "Nagŭnae" because only the filming was done in Chosŏn, while Sinŭng Kinema handled the developing, sound recording, and other aspects. So, compared to other pictures, only about one-third of the filming costs were incurred here. Therefore, looking at the picture as a whole, a considerable production cost was likely involved, but since the expenses incurred in Chosŏn were quite low, "Nagŭnae" had very good box office results.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: What about other Chosŏn films?
Yi Ch'angyong: Among those handled by our distribution center, there wasn't a single film that broke even. Until we expand the distribution network to the Japanese mainland, Manchuria, etc., I think it will be difficult to break even for the time being.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: How much does it cost to produce one film?
Yi Ch'angyong: It costs roughly 10,000 to 15,000 wŏn.
Yi Kuyŏng: Lately, film prices have risen sharply, so production costs are quite high. Anyway, even making a 7-8 reel film costs 4-5 times more than before.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Does it cost that much in places like Tōkyō?
Yi Kuyŏng: Over there, the facilities are all complete, so it doesn't cost that much. We are still like a newlywed couple setting up house; we have to acquire everything anew, so wouldn't it cost money?
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: How much did "Ch'unhyangjŏn" cost?
Yi Ch'angyong: It cost about 12,000 wŏn.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Mr. Yi Kisĕ, how much did "Sim Ch'ŏng" cost?
Yi Kisĕ: It wouldn't be less than "Ch'unhyangjŏn."
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: "Ch'unhyangjŏn" and "Sim Ch'ŏng" were good opportunities to revive classical literature. What were the results?
Yi Kisĕ: I also made "Sim Ch'ŏng" with that intention, but as a film, I think the contemporary piece "Nagŭnae" might actually be better.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Now, could the two masters of the theater world, Mr. Tokkyŏn (Ch'oe Sangdŏk's pen name) and Mr. Yŏn Hangnyŏn, please speak about theater?<85>
Yi Kisĕ: The two of you will probably be in opposition.
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn (Sangdŏk): Then let's be Shōchiku and Nikkatsu, ha, ha, ha.
Yi Kisĕ: Which side is Nikkatsu?
Yŏn Hangnyŏn: The side with no money must be Nikkatsu.
Yi Kuyŏng: Why, is Nikkatsu out of money now?
Yi Kisĕ: Until recently, they had an astronomical debt of over 9.8 million yen, but apparently, that's not the case anymore.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Mr. Tokkyŏn, we assume the Tongyang Theater is quite profitable, but could you tell us more concretely?
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn: The Tongyang Theater opened on November 3rd, three years ago, and looking at the numerical statistics during that time, the profit was indeed substantial.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: How much was the investment amount?
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn: I don't know if borrowing money counts as investment, but anyway, when the Tongyang Theater first opened, it was in difficult circumstances. It's no exaggeration to say that theater truly saved the Tongyang Theater. If we had done films, it might have been worse than even the Umigwan, but thanks to doing only theater during that time, the Tongyang Theater managed to survive. Looking at it another way, theater survived because of the Tongyang Theater. Conventional Chosŏn actors lacked refined cultivation, and because they readily revealed the unique temperament found only in actors, there wasn't any theater that succeeded by performing plays. When actors and theaters make contracts, if an actor gets sick or has an accident, the theater pays their salary, but if the actor quits, the theater can't collect the contract deposit. So, eventually, the theater only suffers losses and can't even stage plays; this happens often. That's why my elder colleague Kim Kijin and Mr. Pak of Tansŏngsa were quite opposed to me starting theater. But I stubbornly took the risk. Initially, I had two troupes, "Ch'ŏngch'un-jwa" (Youth Troupe) and "Hŭigŭk-jwa" (Comedy Troupe). "Ch'ŏngch'un-jwa" was supposed to do only serious dramas (正劇), and "Hŭigŭk-jwa" only comedies. However, audiences liked tragedies, but while they laughed at comedies, they didn't say they liked them. Because of this, "Hŭigŭk-jwa" was changed to "Hwahoasŏn" (Luxury Liner), and this troupe also came to perform only serious dramas.
Yi Kuyŏng: Anyway, audiences seem to prefer crying their eyes out rather than laughing heartily.
Yi Ch'angyong: It's the same with films.
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn: But even when performing the same tragedy, when "Ch'unhyangjŏn" is performed in Seoul, the audience is much larger, but when "Hwahoasŏn" performs, for some reason, it's less popular. Recently, however, it's not like that and is getting a bit better.
Yi Kuyŏng: Is it because "Ch'ŏngch'un-jwa" has many good actors?
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn: That can't necessarily be said.<86>
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: By the way, how many audience members can one program attract?
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn: Previously, when staging plays at the Chosŏn Theater, one script barely lasted three days, and the last day would be empty. Also, when we first started theater, it was common to perform for four days, making it very difficult to prepare scripts. But nowadays, there are cases where performances run for a week or even ten days and are always sold out.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: So, does that mean there has been much progress in terms of quality?
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn: It doesn't seem like that either. The patrons watching plays now are those who can't make out talkies, and yet they don't want to go to third or fourth-rate theaters with narrators (해설자). In other words, women from the entertainment world (화류계), and the playboy patrons (할양) who follow them, lack the skill to appreciate talkies, so they come to watch plays. Therefore, they are inevitably patrons with deep pockets. No matter what, theater seems to incur a deficit in numbers if it loses the entertainment world audience. There's a story that when Mr. Kobayashi Ichizō started theater, he didn't inform the kwŏnbŏn (kisaeng houses) at all and suffered a major failure, after which he went to the kwŏnbŏn to ask them to come. But I believe that if you want to make money with theater, you must target the entertainment world from the beginning. This kind of talk might greatly offend Mr. Yŏn Hangnyŏn's sensibilities, but I am a person trying to make money, whereas Mr. Yŏn is, so to speak, a technician.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Couldn't the Chungang Mudae carve out a unique perspective?
Yŏn Hangnyŏn: That's just talk; it needs to actually work out well.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Can't you attract a different audience base than the Tongyang Theater?
Yŏn Hangnyŏn: It doesn't work out that well.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: I heard it's always sold out during play performances?
Yŏn Hangnyŏn: That just happened occasionally. Rather than being eager to stage plays for others to see right now, I need to first put my own house in order. With the current performers, I can't produce good theater. The so-called theater actors who have walked this path so far have done theater without knowing theater. It's just a professional mentality; humanly speaking, well... as Tokkyŏn also mentioned earlier, there are some shortcomings. They don't even try to have the mindset of doing something for the sake of true art. They have no idea what's what, so talking is useless. Even though Chosŏn people nowadays adamantly go to see literary films, when it comes to theater, even if it's the same story, they don't watch. The reason for this lies not elsewhere but in the fact that the theater is poorly done, I believe.
Yi Kisĕ: That's also true, but in the future, people will increasingly watch films rather than theater.<87> This phenomenon is already visible in places like Tōkyō and Kyōto.
Yŏn Hangnyŏn: Still, if it's a masterpiece, it seems to be a bit better. "Heidelberg" and "Katyusha" drew quite large crowds, but the recent "Ch'onsŏnsaeng" (Village Teacher) was a complete failure. Actually, "Ch'onsŏnsaeng" is a good script. The content is excellent, but perhaps because the title was "Village Teacher" and the original author wasn't particularly famous, people thought it wasn't a big deal. So, we are currently preparing to perform it again.
Yi Kisĕ: There are plenty of examples like that. Even in places like Tōkyō, even if a scenario by an unknown writer is made into an excellent film, they still use originals by famous people like Kikuchi Kan or others.
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn: In short, publicity is the most important thing.
Yi Kisĕ: If we were to perform "Changhanmong" now, people might find it laughable, but it would probably be a bigger hit (フタル할걸요 - likely meant 'hit') than anything else.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Under what circumstances should the Kŭgyesul Yŏn'guhoe (Theater Arts Research Society) exist?
Yŏn Hangnyŏn: That doesn't fall within our sector. It's an organization for researching theater arts.
Yi Kuyŏng: Research organizations are also very necessary.
Yŏn Hangnyŏn: We need people who know theater more than people who do theater.
Yi Ch'angyong: That's exactly what I mean. There are many people who say they'll do it if they have money, but people who research technique are more necessary than money. Even if there is money now, if you ask who can be fully entrusted with the money, there isn't a single person to name. The late Na Un'gyu brought the Chosŏn film industry to today's level, but there's still no one who can create a film that surpasses that level. I believe technique must be polished before money.
Yi Kisĕ: I agree with that statement. Indeed, there's no one you can completely entrust money to. However, the technicians also have something to say. Until now, they haven't had the opportunity to polish their skills. Can we just blame them for not polishing their skills well without giving them a chance? Even in the Japanese mainland, during the pioneering days, they must have lost a lot of money and had a long history, which is why technicians like today's emerged.
Yi Ch'angyong: Well, yes. It's not that various objective conditions are absent. However, Chosŏn filmmakers lack sincerity too much. Rather than saying they will work if given money, shouldn't they study to be able to work? Yet, even with a blatant 10-year history in the past, they are still in that state, so who would say they have sincerity?
Yi Kuyŏng: Can the 10-year history of Chosŏn film really be called a complete history?<88> If anything, it's an abnormal chronology. Like setting up a new household, someone with money would appear, make one work, and then disappear, so it cannot be called complete.
Yi Kisĕ: Therefore, we have no choice but to start anew from now on. Each person must become self-aware, know themselves, and develop an earnest heart for true art. In the past, if they managed to entice someone with money and produce one picture, they would posture as if they had accomplished something truly great, acting as a director while being utterly empty-headed. And spending some time in the Japanese mainland, lighting the director's cigarettes, carrying chairs for them – after doing such errands, they come back and posture as a director; it's truly a laughable spectacle. In what godforsaken society would they ask to be made a director? People think being an assistant director is like being a vice president to the president, but the relationship between a director and an assistant director isn't like that. An assistant director doesn't necessarily become a director. An assistant director is merely the director's errand runner... As for cinematographers, there are people we should truly cherish and respect. Mr. Yang Se'ung, for instance, filmed six movies in Kyōto and has produced excellent results here as well. However, Chosŏn lacks directors.
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn: I thought directing was very easy... When Mr. Yi Kuyŏng directed my "Sŭngbang Bigok" (Tragedy of the Monk's Cell), I also had the thought of trying it once. (Laughter)
Yi Ch'angyong: And what can you do when some people say they can't direct something adapted by someone else?
Yi Kisĕ: So, unless you have hundreds of thousands of wŏn lying around limitlessly, what can be done in this situation? There's no choice but to invite directors from Tōkyō or Kyōto to produce works and polish techniques while doing so... This would also be quite advantageous for the entrepreneurs. Rather than paying a director here 100-200 wŏn, even if it costs 1,000-2,000 wŏn to bring someone over, it would be better compared to an unskilled director wasting expensive film worth 1,000-2,000 wŏn.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: By doing that, can Chosŏn's sentiment be fully brought to life?
Yi Kisĕ: I bring the sentiment to life while learning only the technique. And it won't always be like that; just 1-2 years should be sufficient.
Yi Ch'angyong: Money is not something that cannot be made even tomorrow. People<89> are the problem. This time too, I visited various studios in Tōkyō and Kyōto and listened to their stories, and in every society, there is interest in producing continental films. Since making films set in Manchuria (滿州) or China (支那) inevitably involves passing through Chosŏn, there's a strong desire to borrow the hands of Chosŏn people. However, when I came back here and talked about it with technicians, some understood my words correctly, while others worried that doing so would obliterate Chosŏn's artistic identity.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Compared to the Japanese mainland, how much does Chosŏn film lag behind? Has it reached the level before the [Great Kantō] Earthquake (震災 전)?
Yi Ch'angyong: The level of consciousness might be higher than that, but technically, it still cannot compare to that time.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: How far behind is theater? About 10 years?
Yŏn Hangnyŏn: It's difficult to say in terms of years, but anyway, they are incomparable. Chosŏn people do have talent. But because they have practiced theater incorrectly until now, they know nothing at all. When I first went to the Chungang Mudae, I was truly surprised. Even if they didn't know, how could they have done theater being so ignorant? When I asked, they replied that just memorizing the lines (세리후) and going on stage was enough, which left me no choice but to feel pessimistic.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Mr. Yi Kisĕ, how does it compare to the old days when you played Yi Suil?
Yi Kisĕ: It has certainly progressed far beyond that time. The very fact that we are sitting here now openly discussing theater shows that it has progressed compared to then. At that time, talking about theater was considered embarrassing, and people spoke ill of it, so it was common practice to lie and say you hadn't talked about it even if you had. Also, even after going to see a play at the theater, you'd lie and say you visited a friend's house. Now, the actors have also progressed compared to then, and the audience's level has become much higher.
Yŏn Hangnyŏn: Among the actors who have caught the audience's eye, they have all improved somewhat, but they are still endlessly childish. I constantly think about how we can gain a good audience with better theater than before. Chosŏn actors, when laughing, crying, being surprised, getting angry – they do it with the exact same expression regardless of the situation. Can this work? It should all differ according to knowledge, cultivation, class, living environment, age, but it doesn't. They try to do theater only with sound, without tempo or movement.
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn: Anyway, when they go on stage, they only strive to survive on their own. Regardless of how the play turns out, as long as they look good to the audience, that's all that matters. There are extreme cases where someone, disliking being hated by the audience for playing a villain, speaks kindly even when they should be acting wickedly, which ruins the play plenty of times.
Yi Kisĕ: That's also true, but in a scene where three people are talking, after one person finishes all their lines, they just sit quietly as if their part is done, not like someone acting in a play. We can often see this.
Yŏn Hangnyŏn: There are also cases like this. Even if you tell them to sit and think about something, completely ignoring the person coming up behind them,<90> they keep waiting for the person behind them, so their expression isn't natural. And in things like hitting scenes, they precisely remember where the opponent says their line before being hit, and the moment those words are finished, they strike immediately, but the action is truly awkward. Examples like this are commonplace, so what can be done?
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Do you have any thoughts on training actors?
Yŏn Hangnyŏn: I would, if only I had the money.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Do you have any thoughts on collaborating with someone like Mr. Kobayashi Ichizō and pursuing a different capital strategy?
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn: It's not that theater can't be done because there's no money; there's plenty. It can be done with small capital.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: For films, what about seeking external investment?
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn: Wouldn't it be impossible to make purely Chosŏn-style works?
Yi Kuyŏng: Even mainland Japanese films initially started with money brought in by Universal Pictures.
Yi Ch'angyong: As I mentioned earlier, there are many people willing to invest, but there are no technicians.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Can Chosŏn film digest about ten movies a year?
Yi Ch'angyong: They can be digested, but they cannot be produced. Until now, it has been difficult even to make five a year.
Yi Kuyŏng: There's no need to make many. Even if few are made, they should be good ones. Didn't Tōhō also fail by just churning them out?
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Within Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul) city, how many theater and film audience members move about in one evening?
Yi Kuyŏng: At least 10,000 people definitely move each evening.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Now, could you each please mention a few Korean and Western films that left an impression on you?
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn: I liked Chaplin's "City Lights" and "Unfinished Symphony." There might have been other good ones, but I haven't seen them at all.
Yi Kuyŏng: Among Western films, I liked "The Blue Light," "The Man I Killed," and "La Maternelle" (Mimosa Yakata). Among Chosŏn films, I liked "Sim Ch'ŏng."
Yi Kisĕ: I saw "Camille" (Tsubakihime) in Kyōto, and perhaps because I was feeling homesick (旅愁), I really liked it. After watching it on a rainy night, I thought about it late into the night.
Yŏn Hangnyŏn: I think I liked "Grand Hotel" and "The Gold Rush."
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Among actors, domestic and foreign, who do you like?
Yi Kisĕ: Gary Cooper and Sŏ Wŏryŏng looked good.
Yi Kuyŏng: Charles Boyer is good, isn't he?
Ch'oe Tokkyŏn: The woman who played the pawnbroker's daughter in "Unfinished Symphony" was good.
Yŏn Hangnyŏn: I don't know actors' names at all, so how can I say? There are many good actors, though. Still, perhaps Garbo is quite something.
Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi: Thank you for speaking for so long. We will strive to have such opportunities often in the future.<91>

**Summary**

This 1938 roundtable discussion among Korean film and theater professionals reveals deep concerns about the state of their industries under Japanese colonial rule. Participants lament the lack of financial stability, the shortage of skilled directors and technicians compared to Japan, and the poor training and artistic dedication of many actors. They debate whether classical stories like *Ch'unhyangjŏn* or contemporary ones like *Nagŭnae* are more viable, discuss the necessity of appealing to specific audiences (like the entertainment district patrons) for financial success, and argue about the best path forward—whether to import Japanese directors to learn technique or focus on cultivating local talent despite the challenges. The conversation highlights the difficulty of balancing artistic aspirations with commercial realities and national identity within the colonial context. For historians, the article offers a candid glimpse into the struggles of cultural production in colonial Korea, the perceived technical gap with Japan, the influence of Japanese capital and models, internal debates about artistic direction and professionalism, and the socio-economic factors shaping entertainment, including the significant role of the *kisaeng* and associated clientele. The discussion about needing Japanese directors versus preserving "Chosŏn sentiment" is particularly revealing of the cultural anxieties of the time.

崔貞熙

오늘날 문화 방면에 잇서 영화와 연극이 일반 사회에 가지고 잇는 힘이란 참으로 놀납게 크고 넓다. 진실로 영화나 연극의 관중은 문학자가 써 내놋는 문예 작품보다 또 음악가가 지어 내놋는 작곡보다 훨실 훨신 더 강한 것이다. 그래서 일반 사람들은 모다 영화와 연극에대하야 깁흔 관심을 가지게 되엿다.
이때에 잇서 조선 사회의 영화 연극 방면의 수준은 엇더한가. 또한 엇더케 하면 그 사업을 발전식혀 볼 수 잇슬가 함을 생각하지 안을 수 업다.
이제 사회에서는 제 1차로 이상의 약간의 문제를 취급하여 보려 우리 영화계와 연극의 권위와 선배를 한자리에 모히어 그 포부와 薀蓄을 듯기로 하다. (끗으로 한 가지 슬푼 일은 이 좌담회 지난 뒤 이 글 발표되기 전에 延鶴年씨가 그만 작고하신 것이다)＜84＞

영화 제작에 비용은 얼마나 나는가?
崔貞熙.여러분이 바쁘심에도 불구하시고 이렇게 와 주신 것을 감사합니다. 그러면 이제부터 맛슴 듯기로 하겠는데요. 재미있고 實益될 말슴을 만히 들여 주십시요. 그런데 요새 와서 영화 연극 방면에 투자하는 분들도 많어지고 또 예전보다 영화 연극이 문예보다도 훨신 더 중요시되는 경향이 보히는 것 같은데 어떳습니까. 최근 영화로서 「나그내」가 제작비는 얼마나 들었으며 흥행 성적은 어떠했는지요.
李創用. 「나그내」의 제작비는 잘 알 수가 없는 것이 조선서는 촬영만 하고 新興 키네마에서 현상 녹음 기타의 것을 다 햇으니까 다른 사진에 비하면 3분의 1 밖에 촬영비가 들지 않었죠. 그러니까. 사진 전체로 봐서는 상당한 제작비가 들었다고 하겠지만 조선에서 디린 비용이 퍽 적었으니까 「나그네」는 아주 흥행 성적이 좋은 편이였어요.
崔貞熙.그 박게 다른 조선 영화는 어떳습니까.
李創用.우리 배급소에서 인수한 것으로는 수지가 맛는 영화라곤 한 개도 없섯읍니다. 일본 내지, 만주 등지에 배급망을 넓히기 전에 당분간 수지 맛추긴 어려우리라고 생각됩니다.
崔貞熙.영화 한 개를 제작하는 데 얼마나한 비용이 듬니까.
李創用.대략 10,000원부터 15,000원 가량 들죠.
李龜永.요새 필님값이 밧삭 오르기 때문에 제작비가 꽤 많이 들죠. 었잿든 7,8권 짜리 영화 한 개를 만들재도 예전 4,5배 이상 돈이 드니까요.
崔貞熙.동경 가튼 데서도 그렇게 많이 드나요.
李龜永. 거기서야 설비가 다 완전하니까 그렇게 많이 들지 안치요. 우리들이야 아직 신접사리 격이니 뭐뭐 할 것이 없이 새로 가추워야하니 돈이 안 들겠읍니까.
崔貞熙.「春香傳」은 얼마나 들었든가요.
李創用.12,000원 가량 들었읍니다.
崔貞熙.李基世선생 「沈淸」은 얼마나 들었습니까.
李基世.「春香傳」에 못지지 안을 것입니다.
崔貞熙. 「春香傳」이나 「沈淸」은 고전 문학을 부흥식히는 좋은 기회였는데 그 결과가 어떠했읍니까.
李基世. 저두 그런 의미에서 「沈淸」을 만들게 됏는데요. 영화로서는 현대물인 「나그내」가 오히려 낫지 않을가 생각합니다.
崔貞熙. 인제 獨鵑 延鶴年 劇界의 두 거장 선생이 연극 방면의 말슴을 들여 주십시요.＜85＞
李基世.두 분이 서로 대립이 되실걸요.
崔獨鵑.그러면 松竹하고 日活이게 하, 하, 하.
李基世.어느 쪽이 日活인고?
延鶴年.돈 없는 쪽이 日活이겟지.
李龜永.웨 日活이 지금 돈 없나요.
李基世.전까지는 9백8십여 원이란 천문학적 수자의 빗을 가지고 있었으나 요새 와서는 그렇지 않태요.
崔貞熙.獨鵑선생 東洋극장은 물론 이익이 많을 줄 알지만, 구체적으로 좀 더 잘 알 수 없을가요.
崔獨鵑. 東洋극장이 3년 전 11월 3일에 개관했는데 그 동안의 수자적 통계를 본다면 이익이야 많었죠.
崔貞熙.투자액은 얼마나 되엇서요.
崔獨鵑. 빗을 끄러다가 넣은 것두 투자라고 할 지 모르나 었쨋든 東洋극장이 처음 개관될 때야 어려운 형편이였죠. 정말 연극이 東洋극장을 살였다고 해도 과언이 아니죠. 영화를 했드면 優美舘만도 못했을지 모르나, 그 동안 연극만 한 덕에 東洋극장은 살고난 셈이지요. 또 한편으로 보면 東洋극장 때문에 연극이 살고 在來의 조선 배우들이란 고상한 교양이 적은 까닭에 배우들에게만 독특하게 있는 근성을 턱하면 내놋는 통에 어느 극장에서든지 연극을 해서 성공한 데는 없어요. 배우와 극장과 게약을 서로 맺고 혹 배우측에서 병이 생긴다든가 무슨 사고가 있을 때면 극장측에서 월급을 지불하고 배우측에서 그만두게 되는 경우에는 게약금을 바더낼 수가 없단 말얘요. 그러니까 결국 극장측에서는 손해만 보고 연극도 못하게 되는 일이 만커든요. 그렇기에 金基鎭형과 團成社 朴씨 두 분이 내가 연극을 시작하는 것을 여간 반대하지 않었어요. 그러나 욱여서 모험을 해 보느라고 처음엔 「靑春座」와 「喜劇座」 두를 두고 「靑春座」는 正劇만 하고 「喜劇座」는 희극만 하기로 됏는데, 관객들이 비극은 좋아하나 희극은 웃기는 웃으면서도 좋다고 하지 안는 것 때문에 「喜劇座」를 변갱하여 「호화선」이라 하고 이것도 正劇만 하게 되였지요.
李龜永.었쨋든 관객은 실컷 웃기보다 우는 것을 좋아하드군요.
李創用.영화도 그렇지요.
崔獨鵑.그런데 가튼 비극을 하는데도 「春香傳」가 서울서 劇할 때면 관객이 훨신 더 많은데 「豪華船」이 하는 때면 웬일인지 인기가 덜했어요. 그랫는데 근래에는 그러지 안코 좀 나어가요.
李龜永.「靑春座」에 좋은 배우가 많은게죠.
崔獨鵑.그렇다고도 할 수 없는데요.＜86＞
崔貞熙.그런데 한 푸로에 관객을 멫 명까시 어들 수 있을까요?
崔獨鵑. 전에 조선극장에서 연극할 적엔 한 각본으로 3일을 겨우 해도 마지막 날은 텅텅 비여 있었고 또 우리가 처음 연극 시작할 적에도 4일씩 하는 것이 보퉁이여서 각본 대기가 아주 곤란했는데 요새는 한 주일 혹은 열흘식 하는데도 늘 만원인 예가 있단 말이죠.
崔貞熙.그러니까 질로 보하서 많이 진전된 셈인가요?
崔獨鵑.그렇지도 않은 것 같애요. 지금 연극을 보는 손님들이란게 토 - 키를 보아낼 수 없고 또 그렇다고 제 3,4류 극장 해설자 있는 상설관엔 가기 싫구한 손님들이거든요. 말하자면 화류계 있는 여성들, 또 거기 따르는 할양 손님들이 토 - 키를 보하낼 재주가 부족하니까 연극을 구경하려 오는 셈이죠. 그러니까 주머니는 다 - 들 튼튼한 손님들일 박게요. 뭐니 뭐니해야 연극은 화류계를 노치며는 수자상으로 결손을 보게 되드군요. 小林一三 씨가 연극 시작할 때 권번에는 하나 알니지 않어서 대실패를 한 후에 권번을 차저가 와 달나고 했다는 얘기도 있지만 돈을 벌여는 연극이라면 애초부터 화류계를 노리는 연극을 해야 될 줄 알어요. 이런 얘기는 延鶴年씨 비위에 대단 틀니겠지만 나는 돈을 벌자는 사람이고 延씨는 말하자면 기술자니까요.
崔貞熙.중앙 무대는 독특한 견지를 개척해 나갈 수 있지 않을가요.
延鶴年.말뿐이지 잘 돼야죠.
崔貞熙.東演劇場과는 별다른 관객층을 어들 수 있잖어요?
