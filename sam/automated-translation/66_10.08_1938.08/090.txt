Former High Officials of the Government-General Gather to Speak of the Difficulties in Maintaining Peninsular Security - Foreign Relations and Contact with Missionaries

Roundtable Participants
Member of the House of Peers, Bachelor of Laws, Former Vice Governor-General: Mizuno Rentarō
Member of the House of Peers, Former Director of Police Affairs Bureau: Akaike Nō
Ditto, Former Director of Academic Affairs Bureau: Shibata Zensaburō
Ditto, Former Director of Police Affairs Bureau: Maruyama Tsurukichi
Member of the House of Representatives, Former Secretary to the Governor-General: Moriya Hideo
Former Governor of Niigata Prefecture, Former Chief of Kyŏnggi Province Police Department: Chiba Ryō
Former Director of the Industrial Bureau, Government-General of Korea: Matsumura Shōsei

Reporter - The annexation was also an issue in Western countries, and thus Japan's rule over Korea after the annexation seems to have attracted global attention. (Original text omitted) Afterwards, the attitude of the missionaries towards the Government-General completely changed, and they seemed to rely on the Government-General. Also, criticism from foreign countries completely disappeared, and they seemed to praise Japan's facilities. This seems to be because the foreign policy of the Government-General executives was good. How was it? Please tell us about the circumstances without reservation.

Mizuno, Former Vice Governor-General - Regarding the Korea issue, our government has always considered foreign relations, and actions like the Japan-Korea annexation were carried out after obtaining the understanding of various countries. In particular, there were sufficient negotiations with American President Roosevelt. Therefore, America also understood Japan's true intentions. <70> However, later in 1919 (Taishō 8), the disturbance incident occurred, making foreign relations awkward. There were those who made various malicious criticisms about Japan's rule over Korea, and furthermore, foreign missionaries in Korea, dissatisfied with the Korean rule, often sent various kinds of malicious propaganda to their home countries. (Original text omitted) Therefore, regarding the Korea issue, it was first necessary to clear up those misunderstandings and show foreign countries Japan's attitude towards Korea. Prime Minister Hara was also concerned about this matter, and when we were newly appointed, he particularly worried about this and mentioned it. Therefore, in reforming the Korean rule, the idea that the conventional policy had to be changed was what the cabinet at the time considered most deeply.

A part of this can be seen in Prime Minister Hara's "Private Views on Korean Rule," in which it is written as follows:
"Christianity has spread surprisingly in Korea and seems likely to spread further in the future. Thus, it frequently came to the attention of the authorities (官邊) that these Christian missionaries and their converts tend to resist Japan, and some facts may indeed be so. However, much of this likely arises from some misunderstanding. To eliminate that misunderstanding, there is no other way than to seek communication of intent with them. Regarding this point, Duke Itō exerted the most effort when he was Resident-General, and efforts were also made during Governor-General Terauchi's era, but today there is almost no interaction with them. If you let them speak, they would say the Government-General oppressed Christianity, and if the Government-General side speaks, they would say Christians resisted us. It is natural that this situation arises from mutual misunderstanding. Just saying <71> Christianity and Christians is one thing, but according to what I hear, Catholic missionaries and believers had absolutely no relation to the recent disturbance. Also, even among Protestants, it is said that not a single person from churches like the Congregational Church participated in this disturbance. There must be some reason here. I believe this is a matter requiring great investigation. When I met President Roosevelt in America some years ago, he highly praised Resident-General Itō's handling of 감독 (likely Bishop Mutel or similar figure) in Korea. I perceived that they have deep connections in American government and private circles, but regarding this point, there are currently many regrettable things." "Also, education and religion should not fundamentally be confused, and there are considerable reasons why they should not be confused. The other day, I met with four or five missionaries residing in Korea, and when I explained this principle at that time, they said it was the same in their home country and had not the slightest objection to administrative measures avoiding the confusion of education and religion. However, they complained to me, citing various facts, that there were always measures that seemed like renewed oppression of Christianity. Although those facts mostly stemmed from misunderstandings, in essence, I thought the misunderstandings arose not from defining the distinction between education and religion, but from lacking communication with them or forcing things without considering their feelings. Therefore, sufficient caution was required on this point. And at the same time, what needed attention was not discriminating against their religion. Excluding religion A and aiding religion B is harmful and useless administratively, and there are not a few examples where such measures have caused various disturbances. Treating them with impartiality (一視同仁) is what I believe is most necessary administratively."

In this way, Prime Minister Hara deeply considered foreign relations regarding the Korea issue, and especially felt it necessary to seek communication of intent with the missionaries within Korea to let them understand the true state of our rule. Also, in fact, the situation when we first arrived in Korea was certainly such that there was not only estrangement between the missionary side and the Government-General side, but also a lack of communication of intent. Because of this, we received various criticisms from foreign countries, and even the true intentions of Japan's rule over Korea came under suspicion. It is said that during the Versailles Peace Conference, our country's representatives at the time also struggled greatly with this point. When we were newly appointed to the Korean authorities, Prince Saionji deeply cautioned us about these foreign relations, telling us to ensure that doubts were not cast upon or criticisms made against Japan's Korean <72> rule.

Regarding this point, we felt the need immediately upon taking office, but the actual situation at that time was that they not only held extreme ill feelings towards the Government-General but also had an attitude of resistance towards the government-general's administration, sometimes sheltering anti-Japanese Koreans, and even showing tendencies towards incitement. During the disturbance, we heard facts that places like Severance Hospital, supervised by Dr. Avison, had become dens for anti-Japanese Koreans, where various plots were hatched. There was also talk that P'yŏngyang's Sungsil School was a stronghold of anti-Japanese schools and served as a hideout for rebellious Koreans. Therefore, we first recognized the need to address this area, strictly regulate their wrongful actions, resolve misunderstandings when they existed, and make them understand the actual situation of our empire regarding Korea. Thus, we invited them to tea parties or dinner parties, opened our hearts (胸襟), and exchanged mutual opinions.

In December 1920 (Taishō 9), when I toured the Northern Korea region, I hosted a tea party in P'yŏngyang, inviting a large number of missionaries.

Understanding Foreign Relations
Regarding foreign relations, the Government-General made extreme efforts, sometimes dispatching Mr. Yamamoto Tadayoshi to America to inform them of the actual state of Korean rule, and sometimes sending Mr. Shirakami, Mr. Chiba, and interpreter Imamura abroad to foster understanding of our facilities for Korea. In addition, we devoted all our efforts to foreign relations through various other methods. However, after the independence万岁 disturbance, there were various incidents involving foreigners. I believe those who were involved at the time will speak about this.

Akaike: I would like to hear about Welch's efforts for Korea.

Mizuno: At that time, there was an American Methodist bishop in Korea named Welch. He greatly trusted the Governor-General, and the Governor-General also trusted Welch, who went to great trouble in various ways regarding the Korean missionary issue. Welch strove for harmony between the Government-General side and the missionaries. And when he heard that I had given a speech at a missionary meeting, he was very pleased and immediately telegraphed the gist of it to America. In any case, Welch's concern <73> for various matters in Korea had a considerable effect on relations with missionaries, I think. There was also a missionary named Smith. This person was pro-Japanese, and it is a fact that he worked in various ways standing between the Government-General side and the missionary side, and also regarding relations with Koreans.
Smith later returned to his country, but even now, he occasionally informs us of the situation in America through letters.

Permission for Korean Language Newspapers
Mizuno: Regarding the permission granted for the vernacular newspapers, the Tonga Ilbo and the Chosun Ilbo, we received various criticisms from many quarters at the time. Even someone like Count Terauchi was opposed. However, we thought that just the Government-General's official newspapers were not sufficient to understand the feelings of the Koreans, and to know what kind of atmosphere was flowing among Koreans, even if there were those who took a somewhat oppositional stance, as long as they did not fundamentally harm public order, it would be better to approve them. Therefore, we permitted the vernacular newspapers.

Among the vernacular newspapers, there were some discussions expressing opposition to the Korean rule to some extent, but in certain aspects, there were not a few beneficial points in understanding the intentions of the Koreans. In an era like today, not allowing Koreans to publish newspapers is something the situation (情勢) does not permit, so permitting vernacular newspapers is seen as an unavoidable matter. Regarding the Government-General permitting vernacular newspapers, Koreans rejoiced, calling it the discovery of a new policy. Currently, some people have said the following: "If you build a fire and there is no chimney, someday the kiln will rupture. If there is a chimney and smoke comes out, it prevents that rupture." The Government-General's permission for vernacular newspapers this time is exactly like installing a chimney; it is certainly a good thing for easing our atmosphere, and regarding this point, we sang its praises. Although there is one advantage and one disadvantage in permitting vernacular newspapers, recognizing freedom of speech to some extent was an unavoidable matter in the current ideological trend. Young officials of the Government-General of Korea and young Koreans opening their hearts (胸襟) and speaking without reservation certainly was effective in easing the thoughts of Koreans, I think.

After I left Korea, I met these young Koreans, and they said, "There was no time we thought as pleasantly as around 1919 (Taishō 8) when you gentlemen were in the Government-General. At that time, from our perspective <74>, we thought we were quite oppressed, but back then, we could speak frankly with the people from the Government-General, discuss things, and sometimes even debate heatedly with each other. Of course, there were differences of opinion, but since we opened our hearts and exchanged views, the atmosphere was very refreshing. Afterwards, the people from that time gradually disappeared, and today we don't have the opportunity to talk with Government-General people like back then, so we cannot suppress a somewhat gloomy feeling. We always say how nice it would be if people like those from that time came again, and a pleasant time arrived when we could talk frankly about what we see. If we talk frankly without reservation, even if there are differences of opinion, intentions are communicated, so everything can become harmonious. Recalling those days fills us with deep emotion." I have heard such words, and the young officials at that time studied very diligently, and they interacted with young Koreans (among whom were anti-Japanese Koreans), drank alcohol, discussed issues, and ultimately persuaded (說服) them.

That speech was suppressed in Korea was something young Koreans were very dissatisfied about, and regarding that point, there were many sympathizers among foreigners as well. Missionaries criticized the Japanese government's suppression of speech, citing the suppression of speech as an example of how the Japanese government was oppressing Koreans, and it was heavily criticized in American commentary circles at the time. That the Government-General, as a new policy, eased the suppression of speech and permitted the Tonga Ilbo, a group of new, energetic figures, was able to dispel domestic and international discussions about Japan's oppression of Koreans, and afterwards, American attacks in this area completely fell silent.

Regulation of Speech and Association
Chiba: I generally understood the extent to which freedom of speech was recognized from your words just now, but I would like to add a few points from my memory regarding the process of considerably recognizing freedom of assembly.
I heard that the previous Government-General administration had applied quite strict controls (取締) not only to speech but also to assembly, and especially stimulated by the disturbance, at the time we took office, assembly was in a state of being absolutely prohibited. After taking office, someone came to consult about wanting to hold a horse racing meet, and he asked me, very worriedly, if it was okay to hold an assembly like horse racing where many <75> people gather. There was also a person who came to ask me, worriedly, if permission could be granted to hold a Kisaeng performance (演藝會) at a theater. That's how much Koreans felt burdened by the idea of assembly. I believed that regulation is regulation, freedom of assembly is freedom of assembly, and that it was necessary to guide Koreans with a brighter mood. I declared that everything, whether horse racing or performances, would be permitted, and from then on, freedom of assembly gradually came to be considerably recognized. At the same time, stimulated by freedom of speech, various assemblies were held hosted by political associations, religious associations, and various private organizations. I would like to say a word about two or three incidents among them - In July 1920 (Taishō 9), Korean students residing in Tokyo, stimulated by freedom of speech, held critical speech meetings about the new administration within Korea.

About 10 of them landed in Pusan and first attempted criticism regarding Korean rule, implicitly inciting subversive thought (不穩思想), but the control (取締) over this was relatively lenient (寬大). However, emboldened by this, they engaged in quite unrestrained (放縱) speech at various places while coming up to Kyŏngsŏng, finally arriving in Kyŏngsŏng. Regarding this, not only the police but also influential private citizens caused much trouble, saying that overlooking such unrestrained speech would pose an extraordinary obstacle to Korean rule.

Amidst this, it was scheduled to be held on July 18th, so I ordered the student organizers to appear at the police station. While handling speech as freely as possible, I thoroughly discussed with them the need for the speakers themselves to take considerable responsibility for their speech, persuading (說納) them in advance. Nevertheless, on that day, they held a speech meeting at the theater called Tansŏngsa, but because they delivered radical speeches, the Chongno Police Station dispatched several dozen police officers and ordered the dispersal (解散). Afterwards, I stated the intention to absolutely prohibit their assemblies within Korea, and with this blow, their unrestrained speech came to be completely eliminated. Other examples include the Great Japan Ancient History Research Society (大日本古史硏究會), held under the intention of plotting independence in the Kando region, which was ordered to dissolve on April 20, 1920 (Taishō 9), and the Chosŏn People's Association (朝鮮民團), which seemed to implicitly incite the populace, was ordered to dissolve on July 30, 1920 (Taishō 9), and at the same time, six speakers of the time were indicted (起訴) <76> (Continued on page 79). In short, freedom of assembly was considerably eased, and it was necessary to give considerable consideration to freedom of speech as well, but properly distinguishing between leniency and severity (寬嚴) was something those responsible for public order (治安) at the time uniformly thought was necessary for Korean rule, and they agonized greatly over this handling. And regarding how so-called political associations and religious associations' freedom was handled, I hope Mr. Akaike, the Director of the Police Affairs Bureau at the time, will speak. <79>

**Summary**

This roundtable discussion features former high-ranking Japanese officials reflecting on their time governing Korea, specifically focusing on the period after the 1919 March First Movement. They discuss the challenges of managing foreign relations, particularly with American missionaries who were initially critical of Japanese rule and accused of supporting Korean resistance. The officials detail a shift in policy towards increased dialogue (tea parties, dinners) to dispel "misunderstandings" and gain foreign approval, citing figures like Prime Minister Hara and Bishop Welch. They also justify the controversial decision to permit Korean-language newspapers (Tonga Ilbo, Chosun Ilbo) and ease restrictions on public assembly, framing it as a necessary outlet ("chimney") to gauge public sentiment and prevent larger "explosions," while simultaneously detailing how they swiftly cracked down on speeches or organizations deemed too radical or subversive.

The article is historically significant as it provides a candid, albeit self-serving, perspective from the colonizers themselves on their strategies for control and legitimation in Korea. Historians might find the explicit discussion of managing foreign (especially American) opinion, the acknowledgment of missionary influence, the calculated liberalization of press and assembly (balancing appeasement with suppression), and the named examples of dealing with specific individuals and groups (Welch, Smith, student activists, Chosŏn Mindan) particularly interesting for understanding the nuances of Japanese colonial policy implementation and rationale in the 1920s.

總督府 前高官이 모여 半島治安 苦心을 말함 - 對外關係와 宣敎師와의 接觸
座談會 出席者
貴族院議員       法學士 前政務總監      水野鍊太郞

前 朝鮮總督府 殖産局長 松村松盛

기자 - 합병은 歐米諸國에서도 문제로 하고 있었는데 따라서 병합 후 일본의 조선에 대한통치는 세계에서도 주목하고 있은 듯 하였어요. (중략-원문) 그뒤 선교사들이 총독부에 대한 태도도 전연히 변하야 총독부에 倚信하는드시 보였슴니다. 또 외국에서의 비평도 전혀 없어지고 일본의 시설을 예찬하는 듯 하였어요. 이것은 총독부간부의 대외정책이 조왔든 까닭이라고 보여지는데 어떠함니까. 저간의 말슴을 꺼리끼지 않는 정도로 하야 주서요.
水野 前政務總監 - 종래 조선문제에 대하야는 我정부에서 대외관계를 항상 고려하야 日韓병합과 如한 것도 각국의 양해를 어든 뒤에 단행한 것이여요. 특히 아메리까대통령 루-즈벨드와는 충분한 협상을 한 것이었음니다. 그러기에 亞米利加에서도 일본의 진의를 양해하였어요.＜70＞ 그런데 그 뒤 大正 8년에 소요사건이 이러나서 외국관계가 시언치 않게 되어 일본의 조선통치에 대하야 여러가지로 악평을 하는 이가 있으며 더욱 조선에 있는 외국선교사들은 조선통치에 대하야 불만이 있기 까닭에 종종의 악선전을 본국에 보내었는데(중략-원문) 그러기에 조선문제에 대하야는 몬저 그 오해를 풀고 日本이 조선에 임하는 태도를 외국에 示할 필요가 있든 것이었어요. 이 일에 대하야는 原수상도 우려하여서 우리가 새로히 임명되었슬 때에 특히 이 일을 걱정하야 말슴하든 일도 있었어요. 그러기에조선 통치를 개혁하는 데 있어 종래의 방침을 고치지 않으면 안 되겠다 하는 것이 당시 내각에서 가장 깊이 고려하든 것이었음니다.
이 일은 原수상의 「조선통치私見」 중에도 그 일단히 보이고 있는데 그 속에 이렇게 씨여있어요.
「조선에서 耶穌敎는 의외에 만연하며 장차로도 더욱 만연하려 하는 듯하다. 그리하야 이耶穌敎 선교사 及 其敎徒가 걸핏하면 일본에 반항한다는 것도 頻頻히 官邊으로 드러오든일인데 다소의 사실은 혹은 그러할 것입니다. 그러나 이것이 많이는 무슨 오해로서 생긴일이리라 그 오해를 없이함에는 그네들과 의사의 소통을 圖하는 외에 없었음니다. 이 점에 대하야는 伊藤公이 統監이든 당시에 가장 힘썻고 寺內총독시대에도 또한 여기에 힘써왔지만 금일에 있어선 거지 반 그네들과 沒交涉이다. 彼等으로 말하게 한다면 총독부는 耶穌敎 압박하였다고 하고 총독부측으로 말하면 耶穌敎徒가 我에 반항하였다고 할 것입니다. 이렇게 되는 것은 彼我의 오해로부터 生함이 물론일 것입니다. 단 한 마듸로＜71＞ 耶穌敎를 及 敎徒라 하지만은 듯는 바에 의하면 카트릭선교사 及 敎徒는 今回의 소요에 조곰도 관계없다. 또 같은 신교라 하지만 조합교회와 같은 데 至하야는 1인도 이 소요에 가담치 않었다고 합니다. 여기 무엔가 원인이 있으리라. 크게 考究할 문제이라고 봅니다. 先年미국에서 루-즈벨트대통령을 회견하였을 때에도 彼는 조선에 있어의 감독 伊藤통감의 처치를 지극히 賞讚하였다고 하였읍니다. 余는 彼等이 미국 朝野에 그 관계가 깊은 것을 察知하였으나 이 점에 관하야서는 현재 심히 유감됨이 많습니다」 「또 교육과 종교와는 본질적으로 혼동할 것이 아니며 혼동하지 않는 점에 관하야서는 상당한 이유가 있읍니다. 過日 조선 在住의 선교사 4, 5인을 면회하였는데 그 때 그 취지를 말하였드니 彼等 본국에있어서도 同樣이라면 교육과 종교의 혼동을 피하는 행정상의 처치에는 少毫도 이의가 없었읍니다. 그러나 언제나 새삼슊�러히 耶穌敎를 압박하는 것과 같은 처치가 있다고 여러 가지 사실를 예거하야 余에게 호소하였는데 그 사실은 무론 오해에서 기인됨이 많었으나요컨대 교육과 종교의 구별을 정함에 의하야 오해를 生한 것이 아니고 彼等의 소통을 缺하고 또는 彼等의 심정을 고려하지 않고 강행함에 의하야 이러난 일이라고 생각하였기 때문에 이 점에 관하야서는 충분한 주의를 요하였읍니다. 그러고 동시에 주의할 것은 彼等의 종교에 대하야 차별를 設하지 않은 것이였읍니다. 갑의 종교를 排하고 을의 종교를 원조함은 행정상 유해무익한 것이며 이와 같은 처치에 의하야 종종의 소요를 으르키게 한 실례가 적지 않다. 彼等에 대하야 一視同仁함이야말노 행정상 가장 필요한 일이라고 믿습니다」
이와 같이 原수상은 조선문제에 대하야 대외관계에는 깊이 고려하시였으며 특히 조선 내에 있는 선교사의 의지의 소통을 圖하야 彼等으로 하야금 我통치의 진상을 알게 할 것을필요하였든 것입니다. 또 사실 我等이 처음으로 조선에 부임하였을 당시의 상태는 확실히선교사측과 총독부측과의 간에 疎隔이 있을 뿐 아니라 의지의 소통을 缺한 일이 있는 듯하였읍니다. 이 때문에 외국으로부터 여러가지 비난을 받고 일본의 조선통치의 진의까지도 의심받게 되였든 것입니다. 벨사이유의 평화회의 議際에도 이 점에 대하야 당시 我國의 대표도 여러가지로 고심하였다 하며 我等이 새로 조선의 당국에 임명되였을 제에 西園寺公은 이 외국관계에 대하야 깊이 주의시켯스며 일본의 조선＜72＞통치에 대하야 의혹을 품게 되거나 비난을 받는 일이 없도록 하라 하시였든 것입니다.
我等은 이 점에 대하야서는 부임하자 곧 그 필요를 感하였으나 그 당시의 실상은 총독부에 대하야 彼等은 비상한 악감을 갖고 있을 뿐 아니라 총독정치에 대하야 반항의 태도를갖고 때에는 排日조선인을 비호하며 심함은 선동하는 경향까지 있었읍니다. 소요의 때에는 에비손박사의 주관하고 있는 세부란스병원과 같은 것은 排日鮮人의 소굴로 되야 여러가지의 획책이 있었다는 사실도 드렀읍니다. 또 평양 崇實학교 같은 것도 排日학교의 巨魁으로서 不逞鮮人의 隱家로 되어 있었다는 말도 있었읍니다. 그러기 때문에 우선 이 방면 손을 대여 그 그릇된 소위를 엄중히 取締하고 오해가 있을 때에는 이것을 풀게 하고 彼等으로 하야금 我제국의 조선에 대한 실상을 양해하도록 할 필요를 인식하였읍니다. 이리하야 혹은 茶話會 혹은 만찬회에 彼等을 초대하야 흉금을 헷치고 상호의견을 교환하였읍니다.
大正 9년 12월에 余가 北鮮지방을 순시하였을 때 평양에서 선교사의 다수를 초빙하야 茶話會를 주최하였읍니다.

對外關係의 諒解
대외관계에 대하야 총독부에서는 극히 노력하야 혹은 山本忠義군을 미국에 파송하야 조선통치의 실상을 알게 하였으며 혹은 白上군, 千葉군, 今村 통역을 외국에 출장식혀 조선에대한 我시설의 진상을 양해하도록 하였읍니다. 기타 여러가지 방법으로 대외관계에 전력을 다하였읍니다. 그러나 독립만세소요 후에 외국인 관계에 있어는 여러 가지의 일이 있었읍니다. 이것은 당시 관계하였든 분들노부터 말삼이 있을 줄 믿습니다.
