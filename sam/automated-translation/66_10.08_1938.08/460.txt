**Body Text Translation:**
Song of the Stonemason
						Kim Myŏng-sun

The mountains of Sŏul are rock on every peak.
The mountains surrounding the ancient capital (古都), weathered like bellows (風箱), are granite (花崗石).
Pines grow in the cracks of the crumbling rock,
And spring water flows in the valleys where sand gathers.
When the stonemason (石工) puts his chisel to the hard, solid granite,
*Tang, tang*, the mountain rings and the rock shatters.

Among the granite piled high in the quarry,
There are many customers choosing gravestones.
One day, a young woman came, hesitant,
And requested a gravestone for her husband before leaving.
Leveling the high places, raising the low,
*Ttok-ttak, ttok-ttak*, for her husband’s gravestone. <248>

The epitaph (碑銘): “Our groom, at age 16,
Not knowing that water changes into stone,
Nor understanding the ways to measure the afterlife,
Believed that though heaven and earth change, marital love is immutable,
As sworn upon the pine and cypress trees in the rear garden.”
*Ttok-ttak, ttok-ttak*, oh, her beautiful heart.

On the granite, polished like a mirror,
Flat, flat, straight line, straight line,
Instead of carving his sad epitaph,
The lovely figure of that woman was carved.
Ah, what to do about this unordered work?
*Ttok-ttak-ttak*, the anguish of the times.

Long ago, too, the stonemason of Silla,
When building the Sŏkka T’ap (釋迦塔) of Pulguksa Temple (佛國寺),
Did not meet even his sister who came searching from afar,
But built it by a pond ten *ri* away from the temple entrance,<249>
So the tower’s shadow (影子) would reflect in the pond.
*Ttak-ttak, ttok-ttak*, tell them it's the Shadowless Pagoda (無影塔) in the Shadow Pond (影池).

2

A mere student, he said,
“That bluish stone lacks luster,
Let’s re-carve it as an old garden stone for my house—”
He pointed to a distant place with lively eyes and left.
Thus my mistake was hidden,
But truly, an old stone (古石) in the rear garden would have charm.

The imposing old mansion, with Pugaksan Mountain’s foothills as its rear garden,
Spoke of his unusual lineage (由緖).
In the shade of evergreen trees in splendid May,
On the golden rear garden path between bluish stone rocks,
He, guiding me to a secluded flowerbed,
With his measured steps (보조), could perhaps set right even this mad world.

Watching the magpie build its nest.<250>
Listening to the lark that cries out freely in the sky.
Now, on a bright May day, in this sunny garden,
I, a young stonemason, am feeling my youth.
Ah, I stand upon the rock that seems about to crumble.
He merely points to the moss-covered old stone (古石).

On the gentle slope (斜面), rock crumbles and sand rolls down.
My sigh must reach the bottom of the rock.
“It must have been like this even before I was born—”
He speaks gently.
“Please take that stone and, with red color in the middle,
Make our family grave site shine.”

The Chosŏn woman, who had practiced the ways of a wife (婦道) since age 7,
Was disciplined against evil before even growing up.
“A virtuous woman (淑女) should not serve two lords,” she was told.
In the stern family tradition, obedience (從順) alone was the way of a wife.
So as not to disgrace the family of a principled scholar-official (士夫),<251>
She held the sword of oath to her breast.

“At twelve, eyes closed,
I rode the palanquin and went to be married.
My face, adorned with rouge and powder,
I left my childhood home, wetting it with tears.
That bridal crown was truly heavy.
That praise was even more frightening.”

“At sixteen, I became a virgin widow.
Wearing sinner’s mourning clothes, holding a staff,
Walking, walking behind the funeral bier,
Far, far away to the grave, I went. And then,
My husband, merely a name, who was the counterpart to a living bride,
I buried deep, deep.”

3

Smoothly, straightly, upright,
I carved it as her husband’s gravestone.<252>
Sometimes, ha—stopping, I struck my own hand,
Sometimes, pinching my own fingers,
Ah, because of that lovely figure,
*Ttok-ttak, ttok-ttak*, it was her mother’s impatience, they said.

As I scrape the green moss (綠笞) from the old stone (古石),
I see the skillful color arrangement (色配) of red and white (紅白).
Dividing the shades (濃淡) of color, separating life and death.
Ah, the regrettable gravestone and the refreshing statue (朔像),
Life before and life after, separated.
*Ttok-ttak, ttok-ttak*, killing two birds with one stone, they say.

The woman was satisfied with the gravestone.
People praised the statue (朔像).
She became an innocent student.
And was changed by the storms of the world.
I remain, all day long, a mere stonemason.
*Ttok-ttak, ttok-ttak*, the tomb of youth.<253>

**Summary**

This poem, "Song of the Stonemason," by Kim Myŏng-sun, depicts the inner world of a stonemason working in Sŏul. He reflects on the nature of his craft while carving granite from the city's mountains. He encounters two figures: a young widow requesting a tombstone for her husband who died young, and a student who asks him to re-purpose a flawed stone. The poem juxtaposes the stonemason's artistic impulses and frustrations (carving the widow's image instead of the epitaph, hiding his 'mistake' by making a garden stone) with traditional narratives (the Silla stonemason of Sŏkka T'ap) and the harsh realities of life, particularly for women under traditional constraints (the widow's story). The poem ends on a note of resignation, the stonemason feeling trapped in his role while others change. Historically, the poem offers insights into the persistence of traditional Confucian values (like female chastity and arranged marriage) clashing with emerging modern sensibilities during the colonial period. The stonemason's internal conflict and the widow's tragic story reflect the societal tensions and individual struggles of the era, making it valuable for understanding the social and emotional landscape of 1930s Korea.
