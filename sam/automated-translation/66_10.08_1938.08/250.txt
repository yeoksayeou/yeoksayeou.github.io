Ancient Battlefield, Pyŏkt'ich'ikwan - Visiting the Great Battle Site of Japanese and Chinese Troops during the Imjin War
						By Puk'ansanin

About 40 *ri* (Korean *ri*) northwest of Seoul lies the ancient battlefield of Pyŏkt'ich'ikwan. It is the place where the grand armies of Japan and China clashed during the Imjin War, and where the renowned Ming general Yi Yŏsong (Li Rusong) suffered a great defeat with 20,000 troops. Even now, 300 years later, if you go there, the bloody, rank smell seems to pierce your nose, and a monument stands solitary there. Namely:

Inscription on the Monument Commemorating the Site of Pyŏkt'ich'ikwan
The world knows that a general's single success is built on the bleached bones of ten thousand, but knows not that the bleached bones of ten thousand secured the nation upon the peace of Mount Tai. This land was the battle zone for generals and soldiers of Japan, Korea, and Ming during the Bunroku Campaign (文祿役), and the mountains and rivers still remain. Alas! For three hundred years, memorial rites have not reached them. Now, enmity and friendship share these bleached bones; who would not sigh and choke back tears? Thus, patriotic volunteers conspired, gathered funds, and erected this stone to record the battle site and also offer sacrifices to the exposed spirits. The inscription reads:
The plains of Koyang (高陽), the village of Pyŏkt'i (碧蹄), gazing afar at Hwasan (華山), winding like a dragon's tail. The rocks of Hyeŭm Pass (惠陰嶺石), a natural fortress site. During the Imjin Campaign (壬辰之役), the confrontation took place here. The old general Hwangmun (黃門), his wisdom unparalleled. The fierce general Sijong (侍從), his courage immense*. The Ming general Yŏsong (如松), spurring his horse, wielding his whip. The Korean general Ŏnbaek (彦伯), raising the flag, rousing the soldiers. Decisive battles clashed several times, shouts arising constantly. From dawn till dusk, swords broke, arrows were spent. Generals and soldiers fell, bodies piled high, forming mounds of flesh. Blood flowed like rivers, their valor shook the heavens. Ah, alas! Loyalty and courage began here. Entrusting the nation's fate, forever supporting it. Their brilliant great deeds shine in the annals of history. Their heroic souls are not dimmed, their fragrance passed down for a thousand generations.
Governor of Kyŏnggi Province, Matsumoto Makoto (松本誠)
(*Translator's Note: The original text has 膽大呑* - the asterisk seems to indicate a missing or illegible character, perhaps meaning 'swallowing [something]' metaphorically for courage.)

Now, let us delve into the old war history and pay tribute to the ancient battlefield.
It was in the 7th month of Tenshō 18 (1590) that Hideyoshi (農臣秀吉), looking across the 400 provinces, dispatched his great army to the continent. A total force of 150,000, large and small ships, hulls rubbing together, departed from Nagoya and headed for Pusan. Thereafter, the war situation saw them chasing off Chŏng Pal, the commander of the southern region, and others. Konishi (小西), chasing the enemy, had already invaded Ch'ungju, the strategic point south of Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul), by the 27th. Here, he met with the 2nd Division's Katō Kiyomasa (加藤淸正), who had come via the Kyŏngju area,<156> and they advanced on Seoul together.

Thus, the Japanese army entered Kyŏngsŏng in succession and held a council of war regarding subsequent operations. As a result, it was decided that Konishi Yukinaga (行長) would advance into P'yŏngan Province, the route for conquering Ming; Kiyomasa (淸正) into Hamgyŏng Province; Kuroda Nagamasa (黑田長政) into Hwanghae Province; and Shimazu Yoshihiro (島津義弘) into Kangwŏn Province along the east coast. They then departed from Kyŏngsŏng.

However, Kim Myŏngwŏn (金命元), the commander of the Kyŏngsŏng army who had abandoned the capital early on, took position at the great Imjin River, not far from Kyŏngsŏng, gathered all the boats in the river to the north bank, and blocked the Japanese army.

In any case, it being a great river comparable to the Taedong River, the Japanese army could do nothing. Lacking materials for crossing, they could only gaze at the opposite bank helplessly. There, they employed a stratagem. That is, when the enemy held the advantage of a formidable obstacle like a great river, making approach impossible, they feigned retreat to lure the enemy out, then counterattacked with an ambush. Indeed, the Japanese army ruthlessly burned down the encampment (幕營) they had set up along the river, pretending to abandon further attacks and retreat towards Kyŏngsŏng. As expected, they crushed the pursuing enemy army with a large force hidden in ambush in the mountains (山伏), achieving a great victory. (Omission)

2
Around that time, Konishi Yukinaga (小西行長) reached the banks of the Taedong River. The Taedong is a famous great river, its vast waters flowing before P'yŏngyang. After occupying P'yŏngyang, Konishi would likely drive deep, pursuing the enemy to the Yalu River. However, Konishi remained in P'yŏngyang. This was due to provisions. In Joseon, where the fields were devastated, the supply lines (糧道) were not abundant. Moreover, Japan, being inferior (一籌 輸한) in naval power, found it difficult to transport provisions from the homeland. Furthermore, their rear communication lines were greatly troubled by the guerrilla (케리라 전법 - guerrilla tactics?) warfare of local militias (토민군) rising up everywhere.

As days passed during the stay in P'yŏngyang, already over a month had gone by when, on July 19th, Ming reinforcements arrived urgently. Liaodong Vice Commander Cho Sŭnghun (祖承訓), leading 5,000 cavalry,<157> rushed in, thinking the Japanese army could be swept aside easily (鎧袖一觸 - lit., "armor sleeve one touch").

However, the Japanese army was superior in numbers.
The Japanese lured them close to the city walls, then unleashed a barrage of arquebuses (鳥銃). When the enemy ranks fell into confusion, they launched a flank attack, delivering a massive blow. In any case, the enemy's large force was helpless, floundering in the marshy ground (浸潤沮澤), when fiercely attacked, suffering utterly devastating losses. The cavalry, which boasted of being the strength of the north, was completely knocked out.

Having achieved a great victory in the first battle, the morale of the Japanese army soared, but Ming, concerned about the enemy's report, was greatly alarmed. Therefore, they requested peace talks through diplomatic maneuvering (計交). Sim Yugyŏng (沈惟敬) was that envoy. Thus, Konishi Yukinaga, unaware that this was a stratagem, agreed to a 50-day truce. There are various theories about this truce agreement, but in essence, Konishi was ensnared by Sim Yugyŏng's sophistry (궤변) and mishandled the situation.

Indeed, the Ming court, acting as if the peace issue never existed, hastily organized a large relief army and sent it towards Joseon.
Its commander-in-chief was Yi Yŏsong (李如松), a native of Tieling (鐵嶺) in Manchuria, imposing in appearance, dignified, strategically brilliant, who had moreover pacified the entire western region, achieving great merit, second to none in the Ming army (明軍 中右). He led a great army of 43,000, rushing forward like a tidal wave.

Looking at the composition of his army, the majority were Yi Yŏsong's personal troops (手兵), the incomparably swift Manchurian cavalry. Besides them were infantry troops from the south, equipped with terrifying new weapons called Chinch'ŏlloe (震天雷 - Heaven-shaking Thunder), Chŏsinp'o (低神砲 - Low Spirit Cannon?), and Folangji (佛朗機 - Frankish cannon). When those cannons fired once, the heavens seemed to collapse, the earth split, mountains and fields shook, and the horror (慘) is difficult to describe fully with words (口舌). Therefore, the Japanese matchlocks (소총), which were quite advanced at the time, must have looked like mere clubs (몽등이) to them. Now, with such a formidable enemy gathering, Yukinaga (行長) should have been extremely cautious, but swayed by Sim Yugyŏng's sweet words (감언), he was merely waiting for a reply from Ming.

One thing leads to another (一事는 萬事). Yukinaga had previously established an extensive "spy network" (스파이 망) centered in Anju, between Ŭiju and P'yŏngyang, headed by a Joseon person named Kim Sunnyang (金順良). However, with Kim Sunnyang's capture, this network was completely annihilated (일망타진적), yet he remained completely unaware. Thus, the Japanese army, with its severed spy network, was like being in the pitch-dark night of the new moon.

In that pitch-dark night, the Ming army on the other side was hastening its march.
Alas, Yukinaga did not realize.

Indeed, on the 1st day of the 1st month of Bunroku 2 (1593), Yi Yŏsong reached Anju,<158> where Kim Sunnyang had been. There, he heard detailed reports about the Japanese army's situation and rejoiced, convinced that victory was assured (勝纂無違). That night, he drank heavily (痛飮沐漓 - likely 痛飲淋漓), composed poems, and toasted the future campaign.

At this time, by January 21st, the Japanese army had concentrated almost all its forces in Kyŏngsŏng, except for Katō Kiyomasa's and the Nabeshima (鍋島) forces. The Ukita (宇喜多) councilor's army of 10,000, Kobayakawa's (小早川) 10,000, etc., totaled about 60,000—their might was astounding.

On the 23rd of that month, Yi Yŏsong arrived in Kaesŏng, held a council of war, and dispatched Vice Commander Cha Taesu (査大受) as the vanguard. They advanced as far as the Ch'angnŭng Stream (昌陵川) north of Hongjewŏn (弘濟院) in Seoul, where the first clash with Japanese reconnaissance troops (척후병) occurred. Following them, Yi Yŏsong led the main army, and on the 25th, generals like Yi Nyŏng (李寧) and Cho Sŭngjo (祖承祖) led 20,000 troops towards Seoul. When they reached Pyŏkt'ich'ikwan, the great battle between the two armies began. The Ming army feared the Japanese swords and matchlocks, while the Japanese army feared the Manchurian cavalry.

Regarding the Japanese army's tactics and weaponry, the following interesting passage is written in the "Veritable Records of King Sŏnjo" (宣祖實錄):
Several tens of days after the great battle of Pyŏkt'ich'ikwan took place:
(King Sŏnjo) Your Excellencies must have seen the Japanese soldiers' warfare. Did the Japanese soldiers strike gongs or beat drums?
Yi Wŏnik (李元翼) (Minister) - They just carry many banners consecutively. Behind the commander, there is always a flag identifying that person.
King - Were there no drums?
Wŏnik - There were none.
King - Did they blow horns (호각)?
Wŏnik - They did not. Their army is always quiet (조를 하여), making no sound, merely observing the movements of the enemy camp, employing troops opportunistically (臨機), and their tactics are well-executed.
King - Isn't the armor (甲冑) of the Japanese soldiers strangely made?
Yi Tŏkhyŏng (李德馨) (Minister) - When Chinese soldiers shoot arrows at pursuing Japanese soldiers, they hit the armor well.
King - When the Japanese soldiers charge (馳突), they shoot with matchlocks. How do they do it?
Tŏkhyŏng - They fire bullets from the furthest distance, stab with spears when closer, and stab with the swords they hold when even closer.

In any case, the combined armies of 40,000-50,000 fought fiercely, finding even the wide Pyŏkt'ich'ikwan too narrow. After 7 or 8 clashes (合), suffering numerous casualties in the end, Yi Yŏsong urged Yi Yŏpaek (李如栢) and Yi Yŏmae (李如梅) to fight harder, but the tide had turned. The Ming army was routed and fled. The pitiful (徹愴한) scene of their retreat is described in detail in Volume 10 of Minister Yu Sŏngnyong's (柳成龍) "Chingbirok" (懲毖錄), under the section "Military Provisions and Civilian Food - Account of the Reasons for the Defeat at Pyŏkt'i" (軍糧 民食 狀 - 碧蹄 敗北祿由狀).

After being defeated here, Yi Yŏsong retreated to Kaesŏng, then went all the way down to P'yŏngyang. Later, he returned to his hometown (鄕里), became the commander-in-chief of Liaodong, campaigned against the Mongols, fell into an ambush, and met a tragic end.

1. Time        From 1 PM that day for about 3 hours
2. Battle Location North of Yŏsŏngnyŏng (礪石嶺), 2,000 meters east-west
3. Japanese Army        Approx. 28,000
4. Ming Army        Commander-in-Chief Yi Yŏsong, troops 20,000
5. Casualties      Japanese Army approx. 1,800, Ming Army approx. 5,000<159>

**Summary**

This 1938 travelogue by "Puk'ansanin" describes a visit to Pyŏkt'ich'ikwan, the site of a major battle during the Japanese invasions of Korea (Imjin War) in 1593. The author recounts the history, starting with Hideyoshi's invasion, the initial Japanese successes, the intervention of Ming Chinese forces led by Yi Yŏsong, and the subsequent fierce battle at Pyŏkt'ich'ikwan where the combined Ming and Korean forces under Yi Yŏsong were defeated by the Japanese. The article includes the text of a monument erected at the site, commemorating the battle and mourning the dead from Japan, Korea, and Ming China, signed by the Japanese Governor of Kyŏnggi Province, Matsumoto Makoto. It also includes a contemporary description of Japanese battle tactics from the "Veritable Records of King Sŏnjo". From a colonial history perspective, the article is interesting for its focus on a Japanese victory within the Imjin War narrative, the use of the term "日支" (Japan-China), and the prominently featured monument inscription by a Japanese colonial official, which frames the past conflict in terms of shared sacrifice and eventual reconciliation under the contemporary (1938) geopolitical reality. Historians might find the blend of historical recounting, contemporary observation, and the colonial-era framing (including the Japanese governor's inscription) particularly noteworthy.

서울서 서북으로 약 40리 (조선 里)를 가면 古 戰場 碧蹄舘이 나진다. 壬辰亂時 日支 대군이 접전하다가 명장 李如松이 병 20,000과 함께 대패하든 곳이라. 300년 후 지금에라도 게로 가 보면 피비린내 나는 腥風이 코를 찌르고 거기엔 기념비가 한가루 세워 있다. 즉

碧蹄舘蹟記念碑銘
世知一將功成萬骨枯不知萬骨枯措邦家于泰山之安也 此地文祿役日韓明三國將士之戰區而山河尙存矣 嗟呼 弔祭不至者 三百年今恩讐共枯骨誰不噓唏面鳴咽乎載於是乎憂國志士相謀蒐資樹石以記戰蹟且祭其露銘曰.
高陽之原, 碧蹄之里, 緬望華山, 蜓蜿曳尾, 惠陰嶺石, 自然城址, 壬辰之役, 對戰有以, 老將黃門, 智謀無比, 猛將侍從, 膽大呑*, 明將如松, 馳馬揮箠, 韓將彦伯, 振旗激士, 決戰數合, 吹喊時起, 自旦至暮, 折刃盡矢, 將士隕身, 積骸築肉, 流血爲河, 壯烈神涕, 鳴呼噫噓, 忠勇始此, 以託邦家, 以永毗倚, 烜爀偉勳, 照暎靑史, 英魂不昧, 傳芳千禝.

이라. 이제 옛 戰史를 둘처 古 戰場을 吊할거나.
400여 州를 바라서 農臣秀吉의 대군을 대륙에 출동케 한 것은 天正 18년 7월이었다. 總勢 15만 大小의 선함 舳艫 서로 어울너 名古屋를 출발 부산으로 向發 그 뒤 戰況은 삼남의 수장 鄭撥 이하를 쫓고 小西는 적을 쪼차 27일에는 벌서 경성 남방의 요충 충주에 침입하였다. 여기에서 경주 방면을 지내서 온 2番隊의 加藤淸正과 서로 맛나서＜156＞ 서울로 진격하였다.
그리하야 일본군은 속속 경성에 입성하야 이후의 작전을 軍議하였다. 그 결과 行長은 征明의 통로인 평안도에 淸正은 함경도에 黑田長政은 황해도에 島津義弘은 동해안의 강원도에 모다 진격키로 되야 경성을 출발하였다.
그런데 일즉 경성을 버리고 간 경성군의 대장 金命元은 경성에서 멀지 안은 임진강의 대하에 據하야 강중의 船船을 모다 북안에 모이여 일본군을 저지하였다.
어쨌든 대동강과 병칭하는 대하인으로 일본군도 어쩔 수 없었다. 그것은 도하 재료를 가리고 있지 않었기에 대안만 바라보며 어찌할 수가 없었다. 거기에서 계략을 썼다. 즉 적군이 대하와 같은 嶮要의 利를 가지고서 도저히 접근키 어려운 때에는 패주를 裝하고 적을 釣出한 뒤 다시 迎擊의 역습을 하려는 것이다. 과연 일본군은 강 안에 치고 있든 幕營을 악김없이 불살나 버리고 이제는 더 공격을 단념하야 경성방면으로 패주하는 듯이 보였더니 과연 따라오는 적군을 山伏에 매복하였든 대군으로 눌너 대승하였다. (중략)

2
그럴 지음에 小西行長은 대동강 岸에 달하였다. 대동간은 일홈 높은 대하 양양한 물결은 평양의 앞을 흘너 있다. 小西 평양 점령 후에 長驅敵을 逐하야 압록강에 나가질 것이다. 小西는 그양 평양에 머물넜다. 그것은 양식 때문이었다. 전원 황폐한 조선에는 糧道가 풍부치 못하였다. 그 뿐더러 수군에 있어 一籌 輸한 일본은 내지로부터 양식을 수송하여 준다 할지라도 그가 용이치 않었다. 더구나 후방의 연락선은 도처에 봉기하는 토민군의 케리라 전법에 몹시 괴로워하고 있었다.
이럭저럭 평양체류의 날자도 빨너 벌서 1개월을 지난 7월 19일 明軍은 急援次로 요동 부총병 祖承訓은 기병＜157＞ 5,000을 거느리고 일본군과 如한 것은 鎧袖一觸이라 하는 도시 달려왔다.
그러나 수에 있어 우세한 일본군이다.
이것을 일본군은 성벽 갓가히까지 끄으러 느린 뒤 鳥銃을 난발 적세 갈팡질팡할 때에 또 한곁으로 측면공격의 일대 철퇴였다. 어쨋든 적의 대병이 어쩔 수 없고 浸潤沮澤에서 이리저리 하는 판에 맹습한 것으로 전혀 전멸적 타격을 받어 북방의 强을 자랑하든 騎軍도 완전히 녹크 아웃되여진 것이다.
첬 싸홈에 대승을 博한 일본군은 志氣 크게 올넜으나 대적의 報에 掛할 明은 크게 놀났다. 그래서 計交로 강화을 청하여 왔다. 沈惟敬의 그 사절이다. 그리하야 小西行長은 이것이 계략인줄 모르고 50일 간의 휴전을 約하고 말었다. 이 휴전조약에 대하야는 여러가지 설이 잇스나 요컨대 沈惟敬의 궤변에 걸니어 小西가 일을 그릇친 것이다.
과연 명나라조정에서는 강화문제 같은 것은 언제 있것든 듯이 급히 서둘너 일대 구원군을 조직하야 조선을 향케 하였다.
그의 총대장은 만주 鐵嶺의 출신 용모 魁偉 위풍당당 기략 있으며 더욱 서방 일대를 평정하야 대공을 세우고 명군 中右에 出하는 자 없다는 李如松 그가 43,000의 대군을 있글고 노도와 같이 달려온 것이다.
그 군의 내용을 보면 大半은 李如松의 手兵인 駿速無比한 만주騎軍 그 박게는 남방의 보병군 게다가 震天雷低神砲佛朗機라고 하는 新銳 전율할 화기를 가리고 있었다. 그 대포가 한 번 파열하면 天은 崩하고 地는 裂하여 산야 진동 그 慘 口舌로써 다 하기 어럽다. 그러기에 그 때 상당히 발달을 보인 일본의 소총들은 그네들로 보면 몽등이 같은 게나 보였을 것이다. 지금 이러한 대적이 모여옴으로 行長된 자 要心에 要心을 가하여야 할 바이어늘 沈惟敬의 감언에 그만 녹은 그는 그저 한갓 明나라의 해답이 오기를 기대하고 있었다.
一事는 萬事, 行長은 그전부터 의주와 평양의 중간에 있는 安州의 중심에 金順良이라 하는 조선인을 頭首로 하야 종횡으로 벌이어 놓은 「스파이 망」이 金順良의 포박으로 일망타진적으로 모다 궤멸되여 있는데도 불구하고 그에는 조곰치도 깨닷지 못하였다. 이내 「스파이망」이 끓어진 일본군은 깜깜한 그믐밤과 갔했다.
그믐밤 속에 저쪽 편의 明軍은 길을 급히 하고 있다.
아, 行長은 깨닷지 못했다.
과연 李如松은 文祿 2년 1월 1일, 그 金順良이 있든 安州에＜158＞ 달하야 거기에서 일본군의 모양은 자세히 듯고 勝纂無違하다고 깁버서 그날 밤 痛飮沐漓詩를 賦하고 전도를 祝하였다.
이 때에 일본군은 1월 21일까지 加藤淸正과 鍋島軍을 제한 외 전부의 병을 경성에 집중하였다. 卽宇 喜多 참의군 1만 小早川의 1만 등 총수 약 6만 그 위풍이 놀라웠다.
그 달 23일 李如松은 개성까지 와서 軍議를 열고 副總兵 査大受를 선발대로 하야 출발식혔다. 그래서 서울 弘濟院 북방의 昌陵川까지 와서 일본 척후병과 첬 교전이 있었다. 그 뒤를 이어 李如松은 대군을 있글고 25일 李寧祖承祖의 諸將에 2만의 병을 있글고 서울로 향해왔다. 그리하야 碧蹄舘에 다달었을 제 양군의 대전이 붙었다. 明軍은 일본도와 소총을 무서워했고 日軍은 만주기병을 무서워 하였다.
일본군의 전법과 병기에 대하야 「宣祖實錄」에 다음과 같은 흥미있는 기사가 씨였다.
