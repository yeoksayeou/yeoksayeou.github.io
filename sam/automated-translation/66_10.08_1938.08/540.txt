**Body Text Translation:**
Song of a World Trip
						By Yuk Tang (Ch'oe Nam-sŏn)

Hanyang, farewell! I shall return after my journey.

The road ahead stretches far, ten myriad ri over land and sea.

Passing P'yŏngyang, the ancient capital of four thousand years,

How magnificent is the Amnokkang, with its great iron bridge!

Piercing straight through the seven hundred ri of the Liaodong plain,

I reach Fengtian, the old Shenyang Castle.

The smoke lingering in the pine forest of the Eastern Fuling Tombs,

Is a trace of dreams spanning two hundred fifty years.

Passing south through the Great Wall,

The great city on the plains is Beijing Castle.

The five-colored flag flying above the Hall of Supreme Harmony (太和殿上),

Shows the new light of the reformed government.

Inside the gardens of Wanshoushan (萬壽山), it is desolate.

Falling leaves flutter about.

Outside the ever-present Zhengyangmen (正陽門), the bustling market street,

Yellow dust obscures the sky.

Passing the Huang He (Yellow River) and Tianjin, pointing left and right
To the sites left by the great ancient empires (大徐帝國),

After seeing the bustling market conditions of Shanghai at the mouth of the Yangzi River (陽子江口),
The foremost trading port in the East,

Leaning on the ship's railing, in the Yellow Sea (黃海) and Bohai Sea (渤海),
Befriending the white gulls (白鷗), I arrive at Lüshunkou (旅順口).

The hand-to-hand bloody battle on Hill 203 (二百三高地),
I vaguely imagine in the setting sun.

Dalian Port (大連港) is the great gateway to South Manchuria (南滿洲).
Here I become a passenger on the South Manchurian Railway (南滿).

Traveling seventeen hundred ri, I reach the end at Xinjing (新京),
And connect to the same train on the Chinese Eastern Railway (東淸線).<293>

Reaching the Songhua River (松花江) brings me to Harbin (哈爾濱).
As the center of North Manchuria (北滿洲), its facilities are grand.

Briefly stopping eastward at Vladivostok (海蔘威),
Turning back, the Siberian plains stretch out darkly.

This boundless wilderness (無邊曠野) reached thus far,
Is the half-myriad-year old territory (舊彊) of the former Sushen (前肅愼) and later Balhae (後渤海).

Fields of tall sorghum stretching as far as the eye can see (一望無際),
Have needlessly buried the ancient traces.

Seeing the sun caught on the peaks of the Xing'an Range (興安嶺),
Seeing the moon submerged in the waters of Lake Baikal (바이갈),
Passing darkening days and dawning days in the fields,
How many days has it been? Suddenly, the Ural Mountains (우랄산).

The border marker (境界標) on the long ridge quickly appeared,
Crossing it, they say this is the land of Europe (유로파).

Quickly passing the Volga River (볼가江), in Moscow (모스크바),
They say I have completed the twenty-thousand-ri rail journey.

The large stone building on the Kremlin (크레믈린) hill,
Is the palace marking the eight-hundred-year-old capital.

The four hundred temples scattered within and without the city,
Show clearly that this country is called a Holy Land (聖地).

Nine hundred cannons surround the walls of the Armory (武器庫),
Tears well up in the eyes of the visitor passing by at sunset.

The world's largest bell (世界 最大 鍾) at the Uspenski Cathedral (우쓰펜쓰기寺),
Makes sightseers from other lands click their tongues in amazement.

Southward to Yasnaya Polyana (야쓰야나 폴리야나),
I seek the secluded residence (幽宅) of the great teacher Tolstoy (톨쓰토이).

Lying down for a night on the train,
Before I know it, I am in the Prefecture of Petrograd (빼드로 그라드 府).

On the low, damp land by the Neva River (네바江), Peter the Great (빼쩨르大帝),
Who established this great metropolis,

Intending to smooth the path for Western civilization (西方文明) to enter,
Inherited that great will for nearly two hundred years.<294>

The Great Triumphal Arch (大改善門), the Great Theater (大劇場), the Great Aqueduct (大水道) sites,
Traces of the Roman Empire (로마大帝國) stand imposing.

To see the source of world civilization,
I realize anew that one cannot fail to see it.

The St. Peter's Basilica (성바울회당 - lit. St Paul's Cathedral, but context suggests St. Peter's), which cost one hundred million won,
How can its splendor possibly be depicted?

As the headquarters (總本山) of two hundred million Catholics (天主敎人),
Its grandeur is not without reason.

On the Palatine Hill (파라탄 언덕), thoughts of Caesar (케사르);
Even great heroes end with the setting sun (落日) over a ruined castle (荒城).

Garibaldi's (가리발디) equestrian statue (騎馬像) stands tall,
Showing that new strength exists, which is reassuring.

Florence (프로렌쓰야), the center of art (美術) and literature (文學),
How could I pass by pretending not to know?

Michelangelo (미케로안젤로)! Dante (딴테), the sage of poetry (詩聖)!
Receive the bow of this sincere child.

Seeking Columbus's (콜롬보) old house in Genoa (께누아),
And viewing the great cathedral (大寺院) in Milan (미라노),

Singing "Excelsior," I cross the Alps (알프쓰),
To Switzerland (스위쓰) to tour its famous mountains and waters (名山水).

Two-thirds of the country are high mountains (놉흔 뫼) and large lakes,
The marvelous scenery (奇勝) of streams and mountains (溪山) is boundless.

Shall I grant it the title "Kŭmgangsan (金剛山) of the West"?
To call it the paradise (樂園) of the world seems excessive.

Even in any deep gorge (窮峽), there is a playground,
And even the highest mountains have railways (鐵道) for ascent and descent.

The convenience (利便) for sightseeing (遊覽上) is thus arranged,
It's no coincidence that people from all over the world (天下) gather here.<295>

The hills and valleys of the grand Central Park (중앙공원) - *This refers to NYC, not Paris*
Where springs, rocks (泉石), flowers, and trees (花木) compete in beauty (競奇),

Ladies on horseback, reins linked together (相聯),
Their leisurely passage (洋洋) is a sight to behold.

People resting, seeking the shade of trees,
Even the nursemaids looking after children,

All neatly holding and reading newspapers (新聞),
Seeing their level of education (敎育程度), I feel envious.

General Grant's (그랜트 將軍) tomb (墓所) on the bank of the Hudson River (헛손江邊),
Built through the voluntary contributions (義擧) of common citizens (平民 國民), is magnificent.

And spanning the East River (이스타江上) like a rainbow,
The five great bridges are spectacular.

The countless large companies (大會社) and banks (銀行) on every street,
It would be impossible (업스리로다) to see each one.

The goods piled high (처 싸힌) in the department stores (떼파트멘트 상전),
Just looking makes one's small mouth drop open (딱 버러질 뿐).

Watching the people coming and going on the street (路上),
Those walking seem to be running, those riding seem to be flying.

Because such speed (敏速) in transportation (交通) is demanded,
Above ground, underground, and underwater are all railways (鐵道).

Heading south to Philadelphia (필라델피아),
Even the passing traveler (過客) feels delight (늣거울사) at the Hall of Worthies (麒麟閣) and the Broken Bell (破鍾 - Liberty Bell).

Going down a little further is Washington (와싱톤),
Where Union Station (유니온 정거장) is the world's finest (世界 第一等).

All the sights the eye catches are solely dignified,
No matter how you look at it, the capital of a great nation (大國).

After touring (歷覽) the fully equipped (設備 完全) Library of Congress (國會圖書館),
And the people's palace (平民 大闕), the White House (白宮),<296>

Taking a small boat (扁舟) to Mount Vernon (마논山),
Solemn are the secluded residence (幽宅) and former home (故居) of the nation-founding great man (建國 偉人).

Spring winds (春風), autumn rains (秋雨), how many hundreds of years (몃 百年) has it been since then?
Will the incense offerings (香火) ever cease, even in ten thousand generations (千萬歲)?

The great monument (大記念碑) standing over the Potomac River (포트막江),
Is 556 feet of pure stone construction (純石築).

It seems it could almost touch (다켓다마는) the sky,
But can its height compare to the loftiness (놉흠이야) of his meritorious deeds (功烈)?

Returning north along the path I came,
I seek Boston (보스톤), called the origin (本源) of North American (北美 大陸) culture (文化).

Its various facilities (設備) truly show
That knowledge (知識) is revered (崇尚).

Grateful for Harvard (하바드) and Yale (에일), the two universities (兩大學),
Your achievements (功績) will be immortal (不朽) in the historical records (史記)!

May this blessed land where the nation arose,
Possess great glory (榮光) forever.

On the westward journey (西向 路次), hurrying along (한시 밧부게),
I go to see the great Niagara Falls (나야가라 大瀑布).

Falling down (나료는) a hundred-jang (百丈) cliff (絶崖), hundreds of kan wide,
The world's most magnificent (奇壯) and exhilarating (快活) sight seems to end here.

Even the pillars of heaven (天柱) would move at its force (形勢),
Even the axis of the earth (地軸) would tremble at its roar (울님에).

An unfurled bolt of silk (緋緞疋), a cluster of thunder (모닥이 우뢰),
The sight (꼴) and sound (소리) cannot be depicted even one ten-thousandth (萬分 一).

Seeing the Five Lakes (五湖) all around, I arrive in Chicago (치카코),
A great city (大市街) of three hundred square ri (三百方里), a major industrial zone (大工業地).

Though my eyes have seen the prosperity (盛大) of New York (늬유욕),
How awe-inspiring (무시무시 하고나) is the aspect of this water-world (水國)!

Leaving behind a single strand (한 올) of black smoke (黑煙)
On the horizon (水平線), our path flees onward.

Moving mountains (움즉이는 山嶽) and roaring angry waves (울니는 怒濤),
What is seen (보이고) and heard (들니는 건) is entirely the waves (물결).<297>

King of the waters, the sea (바다), and the king of seas,
I had heard (드럿거니와) it called the Pacific Ocean (太平洋).

Now that I face (臨) its reality (實地),
How awe-inspiring (무시무시 하고나) is the aspect of this water-world (水國)!

After safely crossing the eight thousand ri of the latter Pacific (後 太平洋),
I have arrived (다라랏스니) here in Honolulu (호놀룰루).

Among the twelve islands of Hawaii (하와이), the largest city (都城),
A separate world (別 乾坤) in the middle of the former Pacific (前 太平洋).

The large and small waves striking and retreating,
The lingering sorrow (幽恨 綿綿) of the brave Captain Cook (캡텐 쿡).

The wind blowing through the treetops, the rain washing away the dust,
The heroic spirit (英風) of Kamehameha I (카메하메하 一世) is vast and indistinct (漠漠).

Of the tropical plants (熱帶 植物) with beautiful flowers (꽃) and lush leaves (입새),
Piercing through (뚤코) various forests I know and recognize,
I seek out (차저 가니) the aquarium (水族館), finding many strange species (奇種).
The claim of being world number one (世界 第一) is surely not empty words (헛 말이).

The grateful hearts of the residing brethren (寄留 兄弟),
Publishing newspapers (新聞), establishing schools (學校),

A fine example (好模範) on the stage (舞臺上) of the new century (新世紀)!
I hope that their devotion (精誠) progresses daily (日進).

Within the circle where water and sky meet (맛단),
Struggling (씨름하면서) with whale-like waves (鯨波) and monstrous billows (鰲浪),
The path traveled for ten days is thirteen thousand ri (一萬 三千里).
Welcome sights of the East (東洋 風物), Yokohama Port (橫濱港)!

Briefly borrowing (잠시 비러서) the legs of a rickshaw puller ("구루마야"),
I throw (더지니) my body onto the ascending train.

A corner of the Musashino (武藏野) plain surrounding Shinagawa Bay (品川灣),
In an instant (順時間), Tokyo City (東京市) is here.

Profound is the Nijūbashi (二重橋), its moat (堀) deep,
Bustling is Ginza Street (銀座通), the market large.

On Hongō Plateau (本鄕臺), Mita Hill (三田 언덕), in the groves of Waseda (早稻田 숩에),
Numerous are the worthy scholars (濟濟 多士), the authority (權威) of learning (學問).<298>

Paying respects (吊慰) to the Forty-seven Loyal Retainers (四十七烈) at Sengakuji Temple (泉岳寺),
I ascend the Ryōunkaku (凌雲閣) for a fine view (眺望).

The nightless city view (不夜城景) of Yoshiwara (吉原) by the Sumida Riverbank (隅田川邊),
Is like the three-hundred-year dream of peace (太平 꿈) continuing from yesterday to today.

Leaving Shimbashi Station (新橋驛) after mid-day,
Passing through the Fifty-three Stations (五十三次) in sleep,

Nagoya Castle (名古屋城), Mount Fuji (富士山), how did I pass them (엇지 지난 지)?
Suddenly Kyoto Prefecture (京都府), the thousand-year ancient capital (千年 古都).

The grid-like streets (街衢) are orderly (整齊).
The surrounding mountains and waters (山水) offer extraordinary scenery (景槩 非凡).

After seeing the Kinkaku-ji and Ginkaku-ji (金銀閣), Higashi and Nishi Hongan-ji (東西 本願), and various famous temples (名刹),
I briefly visit Nara (奈良).

The many treasures (寶藏) in the Shōsōin (正倉院) and the museum (博物館),
Are precious relics (遺物) of the imperial era's culture (文化).

The Tsukigase plum blossoms (月瀨梅) and Yoshino cherry blossoms (吉野櫻), having come here,
I return without seeing them, feeling regretful (섭섭하도다).

On the busy journey, passing Kobe (神戶) by (거저 지내니),
White sands (白砂), green pines (靑松), fine scenery (경조타) at Suma (須磨) and Akashi (明石).

Whereabouts (어대쯤이냐) is the famous Kintai Bridge (錦帶橋)?
This is Shimonoseki (馬關), reminding me of the White Horse Bridge (白馬橋).

Getting off the train (차 나리자), I transfer (가라 타고서) to the ferry (連絡船),
Enduring the wind and waves (風浪) on the Genkai Sea (玄海灘) for one night.

Welcome are the Oryuk Islands (五六島) that come forward to greet me (압장 나서 맛는)!
What is the spring scenery (봄빗) of my homeland (故園) like now (지금 엇더뇨)?

The Europe-Asia Express (歐亞連絡船) going straight to Xinjing (新京), *[Note: This seems misplaced, the journey is ending in Korea]*
Carries (부처 실니고) the body of this final traveler (마조막 나그네).

While looking back on the dream-like past journey,
The broad waters of the Han River (漢江) come into view.

I miss you, Namdaemun Gate (남대문아)! Have you been well (너 잘 잇더냐)?
No matter how I look, my own homeland (제 고장) brings the most joy (깃븐).

The many feelings (만흔 늣김은) from circling the globe (坤輿),
There will be a day to speak of them, but for now (아즉은), this is all. - Taishō 5 (1916) - <299>

**Summary**

This lengthy poem by Ch'oe Nam-sŏn (pen name Yuk Tang), written in 1916 but published in 1938, chronicles an extensive round-the-world journey starting and ending in Seoul (Hanyang). The narrator travels overland through Korea, Manchuria, Siberia, Russia, then visits major cities and landmarks across Europe (Rome, Florence, Genoa, Milan, Switzerland, implicitly Paris/London though stanzas might be missing/misordered), and North America (New York, Philadelphia, Washington D.C., Boston, Niagara Falls, Chicago), before crossing the Pacific via Honolulu and returning through Japan (Yokohama, Tokyo, Kyoto, Nara, Shimonoseki) to Pusan and finally Seoul. The poem expresses awe at modern infrastructure (railways, bridges, cities), reflects on historical sites and figures (from Chinese emperors and Tolstoy to Caesar and Washington), and marvels at natural wonders, while consistently maintaining a sense of Korean identity and longing for home.

From a historical perspective, the poem is interesting as it was written in 1916 by a prominent Korean intellectual during the Japanese colonial period, yet published over two decades later in 1938. The journey itself, particularly the route through Japanese-controlled Manchuria (Manchukuo by 1938) and the detailed appreciation of Western and Japanese modernity, offers insight into the perspectives and experiences available to (or promoted among) certain segments of the colonized elite. The observations about Korean emigrants in Hawaii establishing schools and newspapers provide a glimpse into the diaspora's activities. The poem's final lines, emphasizing the joy of returning to one's "own homeland" (제 고장), subtly underscore national sentiment despite the global scope and colonial context. The mention of "Taishō 5" (1916) as the composition date is also significant, placing the journey's conception relatively early in the colonial period.
