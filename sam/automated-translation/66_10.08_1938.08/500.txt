**Body Text Translation:**
An Account of Meeting and Seeing Off the Poet Sage, Mr. Tagore
						Chin Sun-sŏng

It was noon on July 11th, Taishō 6 (1917). A group of 23 people stood waiting for the train from Tōkyō Station to Yokohama, intending to visit the Indian Poet Sage, Mr. Tagore, who was staying at Sankei-en in Honmoku, Yokohama City. Among them were men and women, students and teachers. There were, of course, people from the mainland (内地人 - Japanese), as well as Chinese people (支那人), Taiwanese people (臺灣人), and a Hawaiian lady. Among them were also two Koreans (朝鮮사람): Mr. C and myself.

Having heard beforehand that the Master (선생) was coming to Japan, I had wanted to find an opportunity to visit him. Just then, through a friend's planning, such a visiting group was formed, and becoming a member filled me with inexpressible joy. However, I couldn't help but feel ashamed as well. - It was undoubtedly an honor for someone like me, a disciple of shallow learning and meager knowledge (淺學 凉識), to meet such a world-renowned figure, but what questions could I ask, and what answers could I give! I worried secretly. Yet, amid this, a sliver of joy was: "Mr. C is here!"

As the train carrying us left Tōkyō Station and approached the Shinagawa vicinity, people began murmuring amongst themselves, discussing what would be appropriate to say. Should individuals ask their own questions, or should we present a unified topic and listen to the Master's discourse? Opinions varied, but eventually, it was decided to listen to the Master's talk on the topic of "The New Life of Modern Youth."

Amidst the discussions, what particularly caught my attention was how these people, meeting for the first time, various individuals, were chatting intimately as if they were old friends of ten years. I recalled a story I once read in a book: when a certain Western couple was about to quarrel, they would invariably cover the portrait of the Holy Mother hanging on the wall with paper before fighting. It meant that conflict does not occur beneath the venerable. Mr. Tagore's intangible virtue was indeed enough to make these strangers from different countries become friends as if they had known each other forever (一面 如舊).

Getting off the train and entering the gate of Sankei-en, on the right were lush trees,<275> and on the left stood a lofty, solitary peak. Along the path ascending towards the peak, fragrant lotus flowers filled the ponds on both sides, and at the far end, there was a sea bathing spot where naked children ran back and forth. The vastness of the grounds and the natural beauty made it hard to believe it was enclosed within gates. (Sankei-en is the villa of the Yokohama magnate, Mr. Hara Tomitarō (原富太郞), who offered a part of it for Mr. Tagore's accommodation during his stay in Japan.)

Our group, sweating profusely, climbed to the summit. Amidst the dense, green trees stood a refined Japanese-style house (和式制), and beside it were a Chinese-style pavilion (支那式 정자) and an old-style five-storied pagoda (古式 五重塔). The group quietly straightened their collars and waited in front of the Chinese-style pavilion for the Master. After a good while, the Master appeared, wearing a dark brown velvet cap and a long, white silk Indian robe (印度 도포), his grey hair and white beard fluttering freely.

"Everyone, I am terribly sorry. I was slightly delayed because I went to the doctor today for a toothache. Please forgive me," he said. His simple greeting and momentary smile evoked infinite sympathy and respect in people.

The group escorted the Master into the pavilion. He sat facing the desk, and we sat in rows facing him. After presenting a wreath expressing the group's respect, the Master received it with a joyful face, hung it around his neck, and offered thanks with folded hands in the Indian manner (印度式). Japanese traditional incense (日本 古式의 분향) was burned, and a gentle breeze spread the classic fragrance within the quiet pavilion, intoxicating everyone. Each person stood up in turn to state their name and country of origin. When my turn came, feeling the utmost solemnity, my heart pounded unnecessarily.

"(My name is Hakm00m Chin, I came fr0m S0ul)," I blurted out in a strained voice and immediately sat back down. Later, thinking how overly agitated my voice had been, I couldn't help but blush secretly. After the introductions, Professor Nakagiri Kakutarō (中桐確太郞) of Waseda University (早稻田大學) stood up and, in a few brief words on behalf of the group, expressed gratitude to the Master for meeting us despite his busy schedule. He added that the young people gathered today wished to hear the Master's discourse on "The New Life of Modern Youth." The Master closed his eyes tightly, meditated for a moment, and then calmly opened his mouth:

"This is a very large and important issue. Whether my words will bring satisfaction or dissatisfaction to you gentlemen, I do not yet<276> know. Why was man born? For what purpose was he born? This is a question that has troubled the minds of many people for a long time. But when we look at the Great World (대세계), what do we feel? When we see the solemn, lush forests concealing all mysteries, when we see the plains abundant with grains and myriad blooming flowers, when we see the vast, free, eternal ocean of freedom, when we hear the beautiful songs of birds and the lovely breathing sounds of fish, when we see this Great World, what do you gentlemen truly feel? I... I cannot help but feel infinite love, light, and joy! Love, light, and joy! People of the world are always engrossed only in material civilization, lacking spiritual sublimity. Between individuals, societies, and nations, they build walls (성곽) and focus solely on distinguishing things. But it is not the material life, nor distinguishing things, that enhances happiness. We yearn for something beyond material civilization. If we cling only to individuals and nations, I believe the taste of true life is utterly difficult to savor. True life. Why were we born? What were we born to do?... Yet, when I face the Great World, I always see the Supreme Person (神). I believe man was born to see this God (神). Born to feel the spirit of the universe."

Thus, for over an hour, with his beautiful, music-like voice, he fluently raised and lowered his tone. When passion arose, he clenched his fists, struck the desk with his palm, sometimes closed his eyes tightly as if chanting inwardly, and at other times seemed to be seeing something. After the entire group listened attentively without a single interruption, there were questions from 3 or 4 individuals. The Master answered kindly and in detail. After taking a commemorative photograph, the group bade farewell to the Master and stepped outside the gate of Sankei-en. It was 6 PM. An irrepressible, joyful smile, as if intoxicated by sweet wine, lingered on everyone's lips.

The first visit concluded joyfully like this. About a week later, I attempted a second visit. This was not just for a personal greeting but also to request some writing from him to publish in our *Ch'ŏngch'un* (靑春) magazine. Accompanied by two or three friends, I went to Sankei-en and sent in my calling card (通刺). A servant came out and invited us in. Treading carefully, softly lifting my feet, I followed where I was led into a spacious room. The view opened up to the blue sea, where the warm sunlight <277> shattered into thousands, ten thousands of pieces, sparkling dazzlingly. Across the sea, the Miura Peninsula (三浦반도), jutting out before our eyes, seemed close enough to answer if called. The room's decor was simple. A single flower arrangement in the tokonoma (도고노마) seemed to welcome the guests. On a desk by the veranda, a typewriter and Western manuscript paper were laid out. A tiger skin draped over a pair of chairs fluttered gently ('푸수수 날너') in the soft breeze coming down from the pine grove to the left, subtly evoking feelings of my homeland (故土). We knelt on the offered cushions and waited for the Master. Before long, the Master, white-haired but youthful-faced, wearing the same serene smile, came out calmly in a yukata (浴衣) with his obi (兵庫帶) hanging long in front. After sitting on a cushion, he stroked his white, abundant beard and said:

"I am very glad you gentlemen have come to visit me in this heat." His words did not sound like mere pleasantries. I lowered my voice respectfully:

"I am extremely happy and consider it a great honor to have this opportunity to see you often, Master. By the way, I hear you will soon be going to America (미국)? Around when might you be leaving?"

"Well, I am thinking of going around the end of August."

"Then, around when might you return to your home country?"

"That cannot be predicted... Is Korea (朝鮮) also this hot?"

"Yes. Because Korea's climate is continental, it gets extremely hot when it's hot."

"On this trip, I really intended to visit Korea and China (支那), but due to lack of time, I couldn't manage it, which I deeply regret."

"Master, although you are busy, it would be difficult, but would you perhaps write something, anything, for the youth of Korea who yearn for a new life? If you would write for us, Master, we couldn't express all our gratitude, and I believe the response would be many times greater than that for writings done for us by Western philosophers or literary figures."

"Yes. For what magazine would that be?"

"Yes. We intend to publish it in *Ch'ŏngch'un* (靑春), arguably the only magazine of its kind for all of Korea."

"That is surely a Korean-language (朝鮮文) magazine?"

"Yes, that is correct."

"I am very busy writing drafts for lectures I will give in America here. So, I cannot write at length, but if a short piece is acceptable, I will write it for you."

"Yes. How could length matter?"<278>

Thus, the Master readily agreed to write for our *Ch'ŏngch'un*.

Just then, one of the friends who had come with me asked:

"Master, I intend to devote myself to education from now on. Could you please tell me what method would be most appropriate?" Seeking the Master's opinion, he closed his eyes tightly for a long moment and then said:

"When I was in my home country, young people often came to ask about the path they should take in the future. Each time, I told them to go to the countryside, establish a private school (塾), and engage in education. Whether the students (塾生) number two or three, it doesn't matter. I do not think true human life is found in the cities. There are many people who drive cars or carriages triumphantly through complex city streets, but upon reflection, isn't the wealth and glory of this transient world truly empty? Rather than living that fruitless, seedless life, isn't true life found only by going to the countryside, singing of nature with simple children, praising God, gradually realizing the beauty of nature, seeing God, feeling the infinite eternal, and connecting with the spirit of the universe (宇宙의 靈)? If one person guides five or ten people, and those five or ten people each guide another five or ten, how can that be called a small thing? However, the qualification for the person who guides must be limited to those whose virtue is high, whose love is great, deep, and universal. You say you want to become an educator now, but if you truly want to become an educator, you must first become a child yourself. You must become purely a child and run and play with them. In doing so, you should instill in those purely innocent children the notion that you are a companion, not implant the idea that you are their teacher. Then, gradually, you must teach and edify them. Do you understand? It is easy for a child to hear their parents' words and repeat them, but sending them to school, setting fixed hours, and teaching them while they sweat is something they soon forget (잊어버리는 법). I also run a school in my home country, but it's true freedom. In the great forest, those who want to run run freely, those who want to climb trees climb trees, playing as they please, sometimes meditating, sometimes singing songs, learning what needs to be learned without realizing it (부지불식 중에). I hope you do not forget this meaning," he explained the essence of education earnestly (諄諄히). We stood up and asked the Master for a photograph.<279>

"A photo? Let's take one (백입시다). Do you have a good camera (조흔 카메라)? But let me change my clothes and come out. I want to be photographed (백이고 십소) wearing Indian clothes (印度옷)," he said and went inside. He reappeared having changed into an Indian robe (印度도포).

"Alright, let's take the picture (백입시다)," he said, and we went up to the rooftop terrace (옥상露臺) for a commemorative photo. Afterwards, the Master looked at me and said, "I will send the article I promised earlier within a few days."

* * *

It was one overcast evening. The low sky, looking as if it had wept, seemed ready to rain any moment. On the deck of the small ship *Canada* (카나다호) docked alongside the Yokohama pier, stood an old man with a lonely face, silently watching the cargo being loaded. This was the Indian Poet Sage, Old Man Tagore (타쿠르翁), whose ears had been deafened by the cheers of tens of thousands in the enthusiastic crowds at Tōkyō Station just a couple of months prior. When the departure bell rang (울닐 때), he received farewells (餞別) from only a group of Indian guests, the family of Mr. Hara Tomitarō where he had lodged for a long time, and barely fewer than ten other people, before setting off on the long sea journey towards America. He looked towards the desolate pier, seemingly regretful, and bowed with folded hands (합장 叩頭). What, indeed, must have been in Old Man Tagore's heart at that moment!

Ah, the ever-changing journey of the Poet Sage! I stood watching him until the ship disappeared upon the ocean waves. The Poet Sage continuously expressed his gratitude and also watched me for a long time from the deck. Amidst the lightly falling drizzle, the *Canada*, floating far off on the sea, vanished into the mist and clouds (雲霧). (End) - Taishō 6 (1917) - <280>

**Summary**

This travelogue by Chin Sun-sŏng recounts his two encounters with the Indian poet Rabindranath Tagore during Tagore's visit to Japan in the summer of 1917 (Taishō 6), although the article was published much later in 1938. The first visit involved a diverse group asking Tagore about "The New Life of Modern Youth," receiving a spiritual answer emphasizing connection with nature and the "Supreme Person" over materialism. The second visit was more personal, with Chin requesting an article for the Korean magazine *Ch'ŏngch'un* (Youth), which Tagore agreed to write. Tagore also shared his philosophy on education, advocating for rural, nature-based learning and educators acting as companions rather than instructors. The piece concludes poignantly, describing Tagore's quiet departure from Yokohama, contrasting sharply with his celebrated arrival, leaving the author reflecting on the poet's journey.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, the article is notable for several reasons. It highlights the interactions between intellectuals from colonized nations (Korea, India, Taiwan) and their colonizer (Japan) in a shared cultural space, albeit centered around a revered figure from another colonized nation (Tagore from British India). The author's expressed feeling that Tagore's message would resonate more strongly with Koreans than Western thinkers suggests a search for alternative, non-Western sources of inspiration and identity during the colonial period. Tagore's regret at being unable to visit Korea, and Chin's specific request for a message tailored to *Korean* youth via a Korean-language magazine, underscore the distinct national consciousness and aspirations of Koreans under Japanese rule, seeking guidance relevant to their specific situation. The multi-ethnic composition of the initial visiting group in Japan also reflects the complex imperial networks of the time.

나는 전부터 선생이 日本에 오신다 하는 말을 듯고 한 번 기회를 어더 방문코자 하든 차에 마침 友人의 계획으로 이러한 방문단이 성립되야 그 중의 일원으로 참여케 되니 깃븜은 이로 말하기 어렵다. 하나 한편으로 부끄러움도 금할 수 없다. - 나와 같이 淺學 凉識의 徒가 그와 같은 세계적 위인을 맛나보는 것이 광영이 아닌 바는 아니나 무슨 말을 무르며 무슨 말을 대답할꼬! 하고 남몰래 애를 썼다. 하는 중에도 한 줄기의 깃븜은 「C 군이 있으닛가!」
우리를 탠 차가 東京驛을 떠나 品川근방에 오니 각기 수군거리며 무슨 말을 해야 올을가 혹 개인으로 자기의 의문을 뭇는 것이 올을가 또는 통일한 문제를 제공하야 선생의 講話를 듯는 것이 좋을가 하고 의논이 不一하다가 결국은 「현대청년의 새 생활」이란 문제로 선생의 講話를 듯기로 결정되었다.
서로 의논하는 중에도 특히 나의 주의를 끄는 것은 처음 만나는 이들의 각종 사람이 서로 네야내야하며 친근히 이야기하는 것이 10년 舊交와 다름없이 뵈인다. 내 일즉이 어느 冊에서 본 이야기가 생각나니 그는 서양 어떠한 부부가 내외싸홈을 하랴하면 반다시 벽에 걸닌 성모의 초상을 조희로 가리고 싸홈을 한다 하는 이야기라. 거륵한 이 아래에서는 쟁투가 없다 함이니 타선생의 무형한 덕은 이 각나라 처음 만나는 사람들로 하야금 족히 써 一面 如舊의 벗을 만드는도다.
차에서 내려 三溪園 문에 드러서니 오른편에는 수목이＜275＞ 창연하고 왼편에는 兀然한 고봉이 웃둑 섰다. 그 봉을 향하야 올라가는 길에는 좌우편 못에 향기로운 연화가 가득 찻고 마즌편 끝에는 해수욕장이 있어 벌거버슨 아해들이 이리 뛰고 저리 뛰니 그 境域의 넓음과 자연의 景勝이 아모리 보아도 門戶 안이라고는 생각할 수 없다. (三溪園이라 하는 곳은 橫濱 豪客 原富太郞씨의 별장으로서 타선생이 일본에 체재하야 게실동안 그 일부를 선생의 起臥에 제공한 곳이라.)
우리의 一團이 땀을 뻘뻘 흘리고 峰上에 올라가니 蒼鬱한 수목 속에 和式制의 精灑한 집이 한 채 있고 그 곁에는 支那式의 정자와 古式 五重塔이 있다. 일동이 정숙히 옷깃을 발니하고 支那式 정자 앞에서 선생을 기다린 지 良久에 선생이 다갈색 우단모자에 흰 綢屬 印度 도포를 기름이 입고 회색머리와 흰수염을 푸르르 날니면서 올라와,
「여러분 대단 미안했오. 내가 오늘 치통으로 해 의사에게 갓다 오느라고 조금 느젔오. 용서하오」하는 간단한 인사와 순간의 미소는 사람으로 하야금 무한한 동정과 존경의 念을 生케 한다.
