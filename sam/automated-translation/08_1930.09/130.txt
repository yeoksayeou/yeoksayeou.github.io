Article Title: 人生은 死後에 엇더케 되나? (What Happens to Human Life After Death?)
Author: Hong Ki-mun, Han Yong-un, Pak Ch'ang-hun (Interviews conducted by a reporter)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Symposium)

**Body Text Translation:**
What Happens to Human Life After Death?
The Victory of Materialism and the Soul

Hong Ki-mun

Reporter=From the standpoint of Marxist philosophy, what happens to human life after death? That is, religious philosophers say that when a person dies, only the soul returns to the world of reality to live eternally or is reincarnated in the next life. What are your thoughts on this?
Hong=We deny the existence of a spirit separate from the brain, a soul separate from the body. When a person dies, they will be burned to ash or buried in the ground to become earth. Besides that, things like the immortality of the soul or reincarnation are just superstitions.
Reporter=If that's the case, isn't human life too nihilistic? If our life, which rarely reaches 70, is our only existence up to that point, isn't life too worthless and sad?
Hong=Wanting to live for 100 years, 1,000 years, or even 100 million years is one of our desires. Basing things on the conditions of desire, not on the foundation of reality, puts one far from objective truth. Isn't it better to speak of reality as it is and shatter that illusory dream, rather than deceiving people with nihilistic eternal life and reincarnation?
Reporter=If so, why have theories like the immortality of the soul or reincarnation dominated our society?
Hong=Of course, there are significant reasons. The first reason is that science was underdeveloped, and the second reason is that there were flaws in the social structure. Telling people to transcend or endure discontent and dissatisfaction with the current society, or preaching about paradise in the next life to eliminate criticism and resistance against the current society—all this arises from the fundamental flaws of the social structure. Aren't there those who, knowing this society fully, seek to support it, and those who oppose it? Won't eternal life or reincarnation ultimately become conciliatory doctrines for the faction that supports this society? That is why the modern capitalist class, which initially loudly opposed religion, later came to encourage religion.<34>
Reporter=Then, what is the Marxist view of life or perspective on existence?
Hong=There is something I must say first. Our philosophy is by no means a metaphysical philosophy. For us, philosophy is merely a method for understanding all things. If asked about our view of the universe, we would speak of a material universe based on sciences like astronomy and geology. Similarly, if asked about our view of human life, we would speak of social humans based on the life of people in real society. It is wrong to discuss the abstract creation or purpose of human life apart from society. Humans are generated through their dependence on society, and they have no purpose apart from their society.
Reporter=Then, what is the relationship between spirit and matter? Is spirit completely disregarded?
Hong=We absolutely do not disregard spirit. Therefore, we are quite different from the 18th-century materialists. We simply view spirit as a derivative of matter in the relationship between matter and spirit.
Reporter=The evidence?
Hong=There is clear evidence even in very simple examples. Did life exist after the universe, or did the universe exist after life? Also, while matter without spirit exists, does spirit without matter exist? From our perspective, spirit is a function that arises under a specific organization of matter.
Reporter=Standing alone under the vast empty sky where stars twinkle and all creation rests upon the great earth, reflecting on oneself, how can one not feel like a drop in the vast ocean? How can one say that the instinctual desire for eternal life does not exist in humans? Your words now seem unable to offer any resolution to that.
Hong=Speaking from the desire of a poor person, it is regrettable that money rain (錢雨) does not fall; speaking from the desire of a sick person, it is regrettable that a single spoonful of medicine does not bring immediate effect. However, objective truth and our desires are by no means the same thing. We cannot bend the truth to satisfy our desires. Furthermore, if we have a desire for longevity, we should scientifically research methods for it. Just because such a desire exists does not mean we can assert the authority of superstition.

All Existence Returns to Buddha Nature

Han Yong-un

Reporter=What happens to all of us after we die? What will you become, sir, and what<35> will I become? Since Buddhism highly esteems Indian philosophy, I believe you must subscribe to the theory of soul reincarnation. What is your view, sir?
Han=That's right. The theory of soul reincarnation does circulate within parts of our Buddhist community. That is, even if one was born as a human in this world, in the next world one might become mountains and rivers, or perhaps flowers or butterflies, or even be born as a pig or a donkey. It is said that if a person does many good deeds while in this world, they will be reborn as something good in the next life, and if they do many bad deeds and accumulate bad karma towards others, they will be born as something like a pig, an animal despised in the world.
Reporter=So, even if one is reborn as a pig in the next life, in the life after that, meaning after the pig's life ends, if their luck is good, they can be reborn as a human again. Is that the meaning?
Han=That would be the meaning of reincarnation (輪廻).
Reporter=Then, ultimately, there is absolutely no sense in which we humans return from "existence" (有), which is life, to "non-existence" (無), which is death. Even if reborn as cattle or horses, the soul, the life, lives on eternally and consistently. Isn't the theory of reincarnation a doctrine built upon the theory of the soul's immortality?
Han=If you put it that way, one could say so. However, what the world calls "existence" (有) and "non-existence" (無) depends on subjective human perception, so it is difficult to explain without understanding the Buddhist method of perception.
Reporter="Form is emptiness" (色卽是空) – isn't that the fundamental attitude through which Buddhism views this world?
Han=That's right. Form is emptiness. All is emptiness (空). Everything in the universe is unborn, undying, undiminishing, unincreasing. That "form" (色) is not just what we perceive through our senses. Even things invisible to the eye, like air for instance, are also called "form." Since they are form, then mountains, rivers, plants, trees, sun, moon, stars, constellations, running beasts, flying birds, river and sea fish and turtles, humans, and livestock are all form. Because they are form, they are emptiness. Because they are emptiness, they are unborn, undying, imperishable, unincreasing. Their essential nature (本體) remains eternally manifest. Only in the world of phenomena do partial, that is, individual, temporal appearances and disappearances (時間的 消現) occur, but the essential nature eternally neither dies nor lives; it just remains as the so-called Buddha Nature (佛性) in Buddhism. All existence is united in having Buddha Nature. Therefore, "form is emptiness" is truth. Its converse, "emptiness is form" (空卽是色), is also truth.
Reporter=I don't quite understand your words. Even if it is idealist philosophy, I wish you could explain it in a way that anyone could affirm, or at least understand, even if they cannot affirm it.
Han=Since religious philosophy originates entirely from idealism (一切唯心), I know it is difficult to explain before one gathers one's mind to it and attains "enlightenment" (悟).<36>
Reporter=Then, do you believe in the theory of soul reincarnation you mentioned earlier?
Han=I do not believe it. I think it seems like a doctrine that arose from trying to use Buddhism (佛敎) for utilitarian purposes, like other religions, namely as a means of encouraging good and punishing evil (勸善懲惡).
Reporter=Then what do you believe?
Han=The theory of Buddha Nature (佛性說). As I said before, all things in the universe possess Buddha Nature, and it is neither something that perishes nor something that does not perish.
Reporter=So, the essential nature is always clearly present somewhere in the heavens, and human birth and death are merely shadows of that essential nature – this observation seems to have something in common with the words of the pessimistic philosopher Schopenhauer.
Han=One could see some commonality in that aspect.
Reporter=If that's the case, isn't living meaningless? Aren't all human affairs and endeavors too much like bubbles, passive and valueless?
Han=Why? Is there a reason for that? Since we happen to live in this world, we must strive to adorn that life valuably.
Reporter=How does Buddhism view suicide? Is it considered a sin?
Han=There's no need to go as far as calling it a sin. It's just that one leaves because one dislikes the world.
Reporter=If one dislikes this life, won't they dislike the next life too?
Han=That's outside the question.
Reporter=Do you consider this present world more valuable or the next world more valuable?
Han=One cannot speak of a difference in value. When viewed from the perspective of Buddha Nature, they are all the same.
Reporter=Then what about the "previous life" (前生) before we were born?
Han=It's the same concept as that regarding the "next life" (來生).
Reporter=Do you avoid death and live, or do you not fear death?
Han=No. Since form is emptiness (色卽是空), whether living or dying, I don't particularly feel fear or the desire to avoid it.
Reporter=So, ultimately, what happens to human life after death? What becomes of us once we die?
Han=We just return to the universal Buddha Nature.

There is No Soul, Only Elemental Decomposition
Medical Doctor Pak Ch'ang-hun

Reporter=What happens to people after they die? Idealism says they become souls and are reborn in the next life,<37> while materialists say they undergo chemical changes according to the laws of natural science.
Pak=I am a medical scientist. Therefore, I am a student who believes in physiology (生理學). Thus, when the human body dies, nothing remains at the same time. That is, along with the cessation of life, the several grams of hydrogen, several grams of carbon, etc., that had composed the body until then—the body's water content, fat content, and all other components—decompose again, becoming earth, water, and air.
Reporter=Could all those decomposed elements gather again to become a dog, a horse, or even a person? Since they were elements that once constituted a human body, if they disperse throughout the universe through chemical processes, couldn't those dispersed elements gather again to become humans, cattle, horses, or other living beings? Could the Buddhist theory of soul reincarnation have originated from such a scientific basis?
Pak=I don't believe that those scattered elements would reassemble exactly as they were to become the same person as before, but I cannot immediately refute the idea that they might be reborn as other living beings. However, even if that were so, Śākyamuni likely did not advocate this knowing such principles.
Reporter=What exactly does it mean for a person to die? Even if all bodily functions stop, what happens to that life, that "thought"?
Pak=Life also built its home within the body, and thought also nested within the head. What is thought, really? It's the action of nerves. As that nerve function ceases, all the various functions possessed during life—consciousness, sensation, reflexes, etc.—disappear, and consequently, all the five-sensory functions like joy, sorrow, grief, pleasure, bitterness, sweetness, tartness, pain, itchiness, and peculiarity are cut off.
Reporter=Then the mystery of life is nothing special. There's mystery in the unknown, but why would there be mystery in something completely understood inside and out? If that's the case, a single human life is too transient.
Pak=Didn't the Chinese sage Zhang Yu (張裕) say it? Short life is 60, medium life is 70, long life is 80. Living well is only 50 or 60 years.
Reporter=But what determines the length of that lifespan? If there isn't a lifespan allocated by Heaven, is it determined purely by a person's development and living environment—that is, the quality of clothing, food, and shelter? If so, if two people, Mr. Yi and Mr. Kim, were given the same food, same clothes, and lived the exact same life in the same house, would they always die on the same day at the same time?
Pak=The determination of lifespan involves such external conditions, but medically, there are many factors related to the "primordium" (原基), that is, congenital inheritance.<38> Therefore, even if two people live exactly the same life, one cannot say they will die on the same day at the same time.
Reporter=Death being as it is, what about being born, that is, birth? Is it also based on the hormone-reproduction theory?
Pak=That's right. There's nothing mysterious about it at all.
Reporter=Let's assume everything originates from matter, as you say. Even so, aren't there things that cannot be explained by materialism alone? For example, predictions, dreams, or hypnotism (催眼術)?
Pak=That is indeed true. I experienced it years ago, too. Before the Great Kantō Earthquake in Tokyo (東京), there was a Japanese predictor named Tawara (田原) who said a great fire would soon occur in Tokyo and citizens should be vigilant, and it came true. I met him when he came to Inch'ŏn (仁川) some time ago. Things like this are truly difficult problems for natural scientists. Dreams are like that too. But science will conquer those as well.
Reporter=What do you think about religious philosophers talking about Heaven or Paradise?
Pak=Such things are likely the talk of idealists who are like sleepwalkers. One can only understand their intention. Just as one might trick a crying child by promising something, it seems such hypotheses became necessary to handle the ethical aspects of human life and the problem of good and evil.
Reporter=While you might not feel mystery or incomprehensibility when seeing death, don't you instead feel emotions like dread or terror?
Pak=Regarding my own death, I always feel calm, in a "come what may" manner. And over the past 10-plus years, I have cut open and dissected the bodies of over 300 people with my own hands. During those times, I never felt the slightest bit of fear or a dizzying sense of aversion. During that time, I indeed dissected over 300 bodies, including those executed in prison, vagrants who died on the streets, and those who died from ordinary illnesses, but only the "desire for knowledge" (求知心) stirred within me; I have never experienced any other sensation.<39>

**Summary**

This 1930 article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine presents a symposium on the afterlife, featuring perspectives from Marxist intellectual Hong Ki-mun, Buddhist monk Han Yong-un, and medical doctor Pak Ch'ang-hun. Hong argues from a materialist standpoint that consciousness ceases with death and concepts like soul immortality or reincarnation are superstitions used to maintain social order. Han Yong-un, while acknowledging the common Buddhist belief in reincarnation, personally adheres to the concept of returning to the universal Buddha Nature (佛性), viewing form as emptiness and life/death as part of an eternal essence. Pak Ch'ang-hun offers a scientific view, stating death is the cessation of biological functions and the decomposition of the body into basic elements, dismissing the soul and finding no mystery in death, only physiological processes and the potential for scientific inquiry even into phenomena like precognition.

From a historical perspective, the article is significant as it showcases the diverse intellectual currents present in colonial Korea during a period of modernization and ideological contestation. It juxtaposes traditional Buddhist philosophy (represented by a major figure like Han Yong-un) with modern Western ideologies like Marxism and empirical science, reflecting the intense debates among Korean intellectuals about tradition, modernity, spirituality, and the nature of reality under Japanese rule. The explicit discussion of Marxism's relevance to life's fundamental questions and the scientific dismissal of traditional beliefs highlight the challenges to established worldviews at the time.

人生은 死後에 엇더케 되나?
唯物論의 勝利와 靈魂

洪起文

記者=맑스哲學의 見地로서 본다면 人生은 死後에 엇떠케 된답니까? 卽 宗敎哲學者들은 사람이 죽어서 靈魂만은 實在의 世界로 돌아가 永生하거나 또는 來世에 輪廻한다고 하는대 거긔 對하야 엇더케 생각합니까?
洪=우리는 頭腦를 떠난 精神, 肉體를 떠난 靈魂을 否認합니다. 人生이 죽으면 불에 살러 재가 되고 땅에 무더 흙이 되겟지요. 그 以外에 靈魂不滅이니 輪廻再生이니 하는 것은 一種迷信이겟지요.
記者=그러타면 人生이 넘어 虛無하지 안씀니까? 70도 드물다는 우리 人生으로 고까지가 우리의 唯一한 生活이라면 人生은 넘어나 갑업고 슬프지 안씁니까.
洪=百歲 千歲 億萬歲까지 살고 십다는 것은 우리의 한 慾望입니다. 現實關係를 土臺로 아니하고 慾望條件을 土臺로 하여서는 客觀的 眞理의 距離가 멉니다. 虛無한 永生과 輪廻를 가지고 人生을 속이는 보담은 차라리 現實 그대로를 말하야 그 迷夢을 打破하는 것이 낫지 안씁니까.
記者=그러타면 靈魂不滅說이나 輪回說가튼 것이 왜 우리 社會를 支配하얏슬까요?
