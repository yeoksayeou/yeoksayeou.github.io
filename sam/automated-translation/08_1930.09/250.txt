Magazine Title	三千理 제8호
Publication Date	1930년 09월 01일
Article Title	峨眉山登山記 (Account of Climbing Mount Emei)
Author	南京學人
Article Type	기행문 (Travelogue)

"The moon over Mount Emei, a half-disc in autumn, its reflection flows into the Pingqiang River," isn't this the song Li Bai (李白) used to sing? Murmuring that song ten, twenty times, I am now on the path climbing Mount Emei (峨眉山) in Xishu (西蜀), a world-famous scenic spot, to see the mountain moon.

In early June, I arrived in Shanghai (上海) with Mr. Wang (王君), who had been at the Chinese legation in Tokyo (東京). It was precisely when absurd rumors were rampant that Chiang Kai-shek (蔣介石) had again set out on the Northern Expedition (北伐), gotten wounded on the battlefield (戰場), and so forth. In Shanghai, many people strongly advised us not to go, saying our lives (生命) would be in danger (危殆) due to the fighting, but we pushed aside their dissuasions. Since we had decided we must go sightseeing on Mount Emei (峨眉山), it felt as though this journey might well be our final farewell to this transient world (娑婆), and as we left the Shanghai train station (停車場), tears flowed for reasons unknown.

Throughout the 400-odd provinces of China (中國), the cruel warfare (戰爭) of brothers fighting brothers (骨肉相踏) repeated without cease. Thus, even in the carriages (車輛) of the train we boarded, soldiers directly under the National Government (國民政府直屬軍人), departing for the Western Front (西部戰線), climbed aboard, cracking melon seeds as if without a care.

However, I have no knowledge (智識) about Mount Emei. Apart from a vague, dreamlike memory (朦朧한 꿈가튼 記憶) from my boyhood (少年時代) over 10 years ago,<55> sitting in a village classical Chinese private school (漢文私塾) and learning from a master (訓長) wearing a traditional hat (冠), a Chinese poetry book (漢詩冊) containing things like the "Song of Emei Mountain Moon" (娥媚山月歌) before even starting the "Lesser Learning" (小學), I have nothing else to draw upon.

Therefore, all that Mr. Wang and I hold in our hands is a small English guidebook (英文案內記) published by the Commercial Press (商務印書舘) on Shanghai's Qipan Street (碁盤街) and a single map (地圖) of Sichuan Province (四川省) and the vicinity of the upper and lower reaches of the Yangtze River (揚子江). With these, we intend to pass through the famous Three Gorges (三陜) of Xishu (西蜀), pass the 12 peaks of Mount Wu (巫山), pass through Xia Province (斜州) – that far, far-off place said to be the source (根源) of the Yangtze River – pass Jiading (嘉定), and go all the way to Mount Emei (峨眉山).

Even if we go to Mount Emei, there will likely be no one to welcome us. In that place where the mountains are different and the waters unfamiliar, if a wandering youth (漂泊靑年) from a foreign land (異邦) visits, would a village girl (山村의 少女) offer a smile? Would a mountain crane (山鶴) flap its wings?

Even so, having lost everything early on, I wish to seek out wondrous mountains and nature where serene waters flow (悠悠한 水流흐르는 自然).

What can I do, as this is a path I cannot help but seek?

June 7th, from Shanghai. To be continued in the next issue. <56>

**Summary**

The author, a Korean student writing under the pseudonym "Nanjing Scholar," recounts the beginning of his journey from Shanghai to Mount Emei in Sichuan, China, in early June 1930. Accompanied by a friend, Mr. Wang, they decide to travel despite warnings about the ongoing civil war (Northern Expedition). The author reflects on his limited knowledge of Mount Emei, primarily based on childhood memories of learning a Li Bai poem, and carries only a simple English guidebook and map. He expresses a sense of melancholic determination to seek solace in nature, aware of the potential dangers and loneliness of traveling as a foreigner in a war-torn China.

From a historical perspective, the article is interesting as it provides a glimpse into the travels and experiences of a Korean intellectual in China during the turbulent Republican era and the Japanese colonial period in Korea. It highlights the awareness among Koreans of Chinese political events (Chiang Kai-shek, Northern Expedition) and shows personal connections being maintained (Mr. Wang at the Chinese legation in Tokyo). The author's reflective tone and sense of displacement as an "異邦의 一漂泊靑年" (wandering youth from a foreign land) may resonate with the broader experience of Koreans under colonial rule seeking experiences or solace abroad.

峨眉山登山記
						南京學人
娥眉山月 半輪秋에 影入平差江水流라 함은 李白이 부르든 노래가 아님니까. 그 노래를 열 번 스무 번 입 속으로 오이면시 나는 只今 天下의 名勝 西蜀의 娥眉山으로 山月보려 오르는 길이로소이다.
6月初生에 東京의 中國公使舘에 와잇든 王君과 더부러 上海에 到着하기는 바로 蔣介石이 北伐로 또다시 出陣하야 戰場에서 負傷하엿느니 엇저니 하는 荒㥘*所聞이 굉장하게 도라다니든 바로 그 때로소이다. 우리는 上海에서 여러분들이 싸홈으로 生命이 危殆하니 가지 말나고 부듸 말니는 것도 뿌리치고 期於히 娥眉山 求景으로 올으기로 한 터이니 생각건대 우리들도 이번 거름이 아조 娑婆를 下直하는 길인 듯도 하여 上海停車場을 떠날 때는 까닭 모를 눈물까지 흐르더이다.
中國 400餘州는 아직도 骨肉相踏의 殘虐한 戰爭이 칠끈 사이 업시 번복되더이다. 그래서 우리가 탄 汽車의 車輛에도 西部戰線으로 出發하는 國民政府直屬軍人들이 근심업는 드시 호박씨를 까먹으며 오르더이다.
그러데 나에게는 媚娥山에 對한 智識이라고 업슴니다. 10餘年前 少年時代에＜55＞ 시골 漢文私塾에 안저 「小學」을 시작하기 전에 娥媚山月歌 等이 잇는 무에라 하는 漢詩冊을 冠을 쓴 訓長에게서 배우든 朦朧한 꿈가튼 記憶 以外에는 아무것도 건지어 가질 것이라고 업슴니다.
그래서 내나 王君이나 손에 쥐엇다는 것은 上海 碁盤街의 商務印書舘에서 發行한 조고마한 英文案內記와 四川省과 揚子江 上下流 附近의 地圖 한 장 뿐이로소이다. 이것을 가지고 우리는 西蜀의 有名한 三陜을 지나고 또 巫山 12峯을 지나고 揚子江의 根源이라고도 할 저-먼 저 -득하게 먼 斜州지나 嘉定지나 娥眉山까지 가려 하나이다.
娥眉山으로 간대도 마저 줄 이도 업슬 것이외다. 山이 다르고 물이 생소한 그곳에 異邦의 一漂泊靑年이 차저간들 山村의 少女가 우슴이나 우서주리까 山鶴이나 날개를 처주리까.
그러터래도 모든 것을 일즉이 일은 나는 奇한 山과 悠悠한 水流흐르는 自然이나 차저 가고 십소이다.
차저가지 아니치 못하여 차저가는 길인 것을 엇지하릿까?
6月 7日 上海에서 以下 來號屬 ＜56＞
