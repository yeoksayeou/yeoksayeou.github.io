Everyone, Do You Know? Research Room on the "Reality of Chosŏn"

We often think we know the reality of this Chosŏn where we live, yet there are many things we pass by without knowing about each other. That is why we have specially established this column. Now, starting from this month, let us study together this reality, which becomes the basis for calculating all our movements.

National Treasury Funds Thrown into Chosŏn
Investigating the money thrown into Chosŏn from the Japanese national treasury since the era of the Residency-General (統監府) in Meiji 40 (1907) until recently, for stationing troops or establishing administrative offices in Chosŏn (temporarily disregarding the reverse flow), we find:
1. Military Expenses: 292,050,000 won
2. Administrative Expenses: 85,988,000 won
3. Chosŏn Financial Supplement Funds: 206,185,000 won
Total: 584,223,000 won (From Meiji 40 [1907] to Shōwa 3 [1928])<34>

Police Stations and Number of Police Officers
Is there anywhere you go where there isn't a police station? And is there anywhere without a patrolman (巡査) who cleverly identifies persons under surveillance (要視察人) and follows them? So, how many of them are there, really, for the police network (警察網) to be spread out like a spiderweb?

Police Stations in All Chosŏn = 250; Police Substations (駐在所) = 2,301; Police Boxes (派出所) = 171; Dispatch Offices (出張所) = 179 = The area covered by one police office is 4 square ri and 93 pyŏng.

Total Police Force in Chosŏn = Police Superintendents (警視) 52; Police Inspectors (警部) 428; Assistant Police Inspectors (警部補) 794; Patrolmen (巡査) 17,383 = Population per patrolman is 1,103 people.

Railway Mileage
The railway network, including lines like the Kyŏngŭi Line and Kyŏngbu Line, continues to expand year by year for economic, political, and military reasons. Thus:
Current Total Mileage = 2,100 miles (哩)
Planned Mileage After 10 Years = 3,500 miles (哩)

Terrorists
Listing the names of "terrorists" (터로리스트) with political significance:
1. Yi Chaemyŏng = Attempted assassination of Yi Wanyong (miswritten as Yi Wŏnyong 李元用) in Kyŏngsŏng's Myŏngdong in Yungŭi 3 (1909).
2. An Chunggŭn = Assassinated Itō Hirobumi in Harbin in the autumn of Meiji 42 (1909), was arrested, and executed.
3. O Sŏngnyun = Attempted assassination of General Tanaka (Tanaka Taishō 田中大將) heading to the Philippines at the Shanghai pier in the summer of Taishō 10 (1921).<35>
4. Kim Iksang = Threw a bomb at the Government-General (총독부) in Taishō 10 (1921) and also attempted assassination of General Tanaka in Shanghai.
5. Pak Sŏkchu = Threw bombs at the Oriental Development Company (東拓) and the Chōsen Industrial Bank (殖銀) in Kyŏngsŏng in 1926, shot several people dead, and committed suicide.

8. Kim Sangok = Appeared in the streets of Kyŏngsŏng city some years ago with a pistol, exchanged fire with Japanese police, shot one dead, then committed suicide (*彈*自殺 - likely 탄알 떨어져 자살 'suicide after running out of bullets').
9. Kang Ugyu = Immediately after the Kimi year (己未, 1919), threw a bomb at Governor-General Saitō (齋등總督*) at Nandaemun Station (南大門驛頭), later arrested and executed.
10. Yang Kŭnhwan = Assassinated Min Wŏnsik at the Tokyo Station Hotel in Taishō 10 (1921).
11. Pak Yŏl = (Abbreviated - original text)

National Taxes
They collect a lot of taxes (稅金) like land tax (地稅) and income tax (所得稅) at the prefecture, county, and township offices (府郡面廳), don't they? How much national tax (國稅) is collected from Chosŏn in one year?
Total Amount: 44,770,000 Won
Per Capita: 2 Won 33 Chŏn
Per Household: 11 Won 16 Chŏn

Number of Births in One Year
"Poor families have many children" (貧家多兒), as they say, and Chosŏn people certainly give birth well, don't they? The number of babies born last year was over 733,000, and among them, 3,500 were stillbirths (死産).<38>

**Summary**

This 1930 article from "Samch'ŏlli" magazine presents statistics on various aspects of colonial Chosŏn (Korea) under Japanese rule. It covers Japanese government spending in Korea (military, administrative, financial supplements), the extensive police network and personnel numbers, railway expansion plans, a list of prominent Korean independence activists labelled "terrorists," comparative profit data between Japanese-backed and Korean-run banks, statistics on border clashes between armed Korean groups and Japanese police, prison and inmate numbers, national tax figures, and annual birth rates. The article juxtaposes official data highlighting Japanese control and infrastructure development with figures reflecting Korean resistance (activists, border clashes) and economic disparities (bank profits), while maintaining a seemingly neutral, factual tone.

Historians might find this article particularly interesting for its compilation of diverse statistics reflecting the colonial state's apparatus (police, prisons, taxes, infrastructure investment attributed to Japan) alongside data indicating Korean resistance and socio-economic conditions (activist activities, border fighting, disparities in bank profits, birth/death rates). The labelling of independence fighters as "terrorists" and the framing of Japanese expenditure without mentioning resource extraction provide insight into the information environment and potential underlying critique within the constraints of censorship. The comparison between Japanese and Korean banks offers a glimpse into the economic stratification of the time.
