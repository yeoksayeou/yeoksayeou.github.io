**Recent Three Major Incidents and Scenes from the Field**
Kando Communist Party Attack and the City... Hong Chongin
Immediately After the Sinhŭng Coal Mine Incident... Hanyang Hak'in
Scene of the Tanch'ŏn Two Thousand Mass Disturbance... Pak Yunsŏk

**The Communist Party Attack and Kando**
C Newspaper Special Correspondent Hong Chongin

The Kando Incident. Seen from any perspective, it is a historic, major incident.
From the night of May 30th, 1930, to the dawn of the 31st, in what could be called a twinkling of an eye, this incident, where over ten cities and villages within a region 150-160 *ri* north-south and 180-190 *ri* east-west were attacked like a sudden gust of wind, is indeed, in terms of its controlled, highly planned military methods and the wide scope of mobilization, the first major incident of its kind in Kando. What is especially noteworthy is that it must also be seen as possessing great significance in that it marked an epochal transformation in the OO-ist OO movement (Communist movement) that formed the force behind the incident. Not only that, but the problems left behind by this major incident are causing subtle ripples in Japan-China relations in Kando, and at the same time, the significant impact on the lives of the 400,000 compatriots residing in East Manchuria, both directly and indirectly, is expected to unfold most seriously in the future, presenting numerous issues (results) that demand profound monitoring and research. To reiterate, critiques of this historic major incident will also vary greatly depending on one's worldview, social perspective, national perspective, etc. The "special circumstances" of Kando, where all movements could not help but show deformed progress, do not make reflection and critique of this major incident simple. And since I cannot write about several points that raise serious "suspicions," the concrete judgment of the incident will be somewhat explained by the results in the future, and in this article, as requested by Brother P'ain, I will attempt to write only about the aftermath of the major incident.

It was the morning of June 2nd when I crossed the Tumangang River under the title of a so-called special correspondent sent to investigate the truth of the incident. Tumangang River, climbing along the narrow valley <2>, I stopped my feet on the Tumŏn Railway Bridge on the border—what a desolate and lonely scene it was. Between the two shabby, desolate villages on both banks, only the modern railway bridge connecting the two countries lay imposingly, and only the excessively loud whistle of the "iron donkey" echoing in the mountain gorge, which surrounded it like a folding screen, made the traveler's sentimentality on the long road sentimental.
"From now on, it's a foreign country—" Thinking this, I boarded the matchbox-like light rail car. Truly, just one step across, and it felt like a foreign country. Unlike the rugged mountains of our land, the terrain here changed completely, with desolate plains and overlapping hills resembling the waves of the ocean. <3>
The villages, looking like piles of crab shells from afar, were all compatriots' villages. As it was the Tano Festival and the weather was mild, wherever I went, villages had one or two swings set up, and men and women, young and old, gathered in yards, peacefully celebrating the festive day. Somewhere, while running through a field, I saw four or five young wives, around 20 years old, wearing shiny new artificial silk jackets, take off their shoes and run breathlessly towards the train, then stand there shouting and laughing. I leaned against the car window and watched intently their playfulness, their copper-brown healthy faces smiling broadly enough to burst. As their shadows disappeared over the distant, desolate horizon, I unknowingly shed tears for their innocence and their inescapable, eternal ignorance.

The light rail car, moving no faster than a city tram, and the view outside the window, lacking any color or change with no forests or rivers, endlessly tired my fatigued body. Thus, with five hours remaining until Yongjŏng, I was overcome by rambling thoughts. Just before Hoegyŏngga Station, several sleepers on the railway bridge were burnt black. Also, a little past the station, a lone house below the railway line was completely burned, leaving only the foundation stones. It had a small yard, looking like a school. I heard it was the Huch'ŏnga branch of the common school. It was undoubtedly the work of the OO PArty (Communist Party). However, the small remaining traces could not convey the critical situation at that time. Moreover, in the vast wilderness, such a fire seemed no more significant than a bonfire in a corner of a yard. It can be seen that the mobilization at that time was on a large and organized scale, given that incidents occurred simultaneously even in places 100 *ri* away from Yongjŏng.

I entered Yongjŏng Village around 2:30 PM that day. Looking from afar, Western-style houses were visible, and residences were quite densely packed (櫛比). Although the place name Yongjŏng Village includes the character for "village" (村), it is in fact the central city of Yŏnbyŏn (延邊) with a population close to 20,000. On the Chinese side, there are the Public Security Bureau (公安局), Army Regiment (陸軍聯隊), etc., and on the Japanese side, there is the Kando Consulate General (間島總領事舘), with nearly 100 police officers (警官), making it a place that cannot be taken lightly. However, despite such complete security preparedness, it is astonishing that a group of merely 50-odd men could突入 (break in), destroy the power plant (發電所) to create a darkened city, simultaneously set fires, cut telegraph lines (電信線), destroy the railway bridge, throw bombs (爆彈) at the Oriental Development Company branch (東拓支店) in the city, and carry out other military attack methods (攻擊手段). As I got off at the station and thought about these things, the blood of excitement (興奮), like being electrocuted by a telegram in Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul), flowed through my whole body again. The urgency (促迫) of the time to send the first report (打電) also made me tense. I hurried first to the Consulate General. Japanese and Chinese police officers, some carrying pistols, others shouldering long guns, stood at every street corner, showing the extreme state of alert. I met the Chief of Police, who seemed as nonchalant (天然하다) as if the major incident had only happened in a dream. "Yes, yes," he said.
"Of course, it will be difficult to eradicate (根滅) the Communist Party. It won't be easy. Such incidents won't end with just this one time; they will surely continue to happen in the future. Bombs? American-made, like those things over there! Tiny little things!" he said. Behind me, inside a glass cabinet, I saw dozens of bombs, large and small, along with damaged long guns, pistols, swords, etc., packed inside. Without asking (Text omitted in original - 中略-원문), he responded again to my question:
"All the places attacked belonged solely to Japanese institutions and affiliates. Responsibility for public order (治安)? Of course, isn't this Chinese territory? The number of Chinese police (巡警) and army personnel might be said to be sufficient. But what about their actual capability (實力) and sincerity (誠意)?"
He shook his head. Of course, I hadn't intended to ask about the responsibility for public order. But from this one statement, I could sense how the consequences of this incident would unfold in the second stage. China, which calls for the recovery of the South Manchuria Railway, opposes the completion of the Kilhoe Line, and is most wary of Japanese influence in Manchuria, would surely try not to leave any pretext for intervention—this is as clear as day (明若觀火). In such circumstances, what fate (境遇) would await the 400,000 immigrant compatriots in East Manchuria, namely Kando, the site of the incident? The consequences <4> could be said to be greater than the incident itself. Indeed, recent newspapers are eloquently (雄辯) explaining this.

Let's hear about the situation on the night the incident occurred. Before that, around sunset on the afternoon of the 30th, there was a large demonstration (示威運動) by about 40 male and female students and youths in a corner of Yongjŏng city. Leaflets (비라) were also distributed (撒布). This was the prelude to the major incident. The demonstration was dispersed by Chinese police before the Japanese Consulate police could intervene, and only one person named Ch'oe was arrested. Then, around 9 PM that night, large posters (檄文), about the size of advertising posters, were plastered here and there on the walls of major streets. Daringly, the aforementioned leaflets clearly stated "Korean Communist Party (朝鮮共産黨), Koryŏ Communist Youth Association (高麗共産靑年會), Manchuria General Bureau Reconstruction Department (滿洲總局再說部)" printed in white, inciting a riot. The posters put up on the streets that night also clearly called for a riot. As the Consulate police in the special zone (特殊地帶) of Kando, they must have sensed (豫探) some atmosphere (氣脈) even before this demonstration. Coupled with the demonstration and the bold posters that evening, it seems they were on high alert that night. Regarding the situation that night, the Chief of the Consulate Police Station, who was the commander on the front line of vigilance, says:
"Of course, we had anticipated it beforehand, but we couldn't know the date and time. Moreover, with the demonstration and posters that evening, we knew better, but posters are quite common, so we didn't expect the incident to break out so suddenly that night and were just maintaining our guard. Was it around 2 AM on the 31st? As I was sitting here (Chief's office), the electric lights flickered. Since power outages (停電) were frequent here, I thought it was another power outage, but feeling that something was unusual tonight, I was about to light a candle (燭불) as the lights went out, when 'Bang! Bang!' a loud noise was heard. At that moment, I instinctively felt (直感), 'Ah - something has happened.' Then, a subordinate informed me it was a bomb... But it was dark, and with the electricity out, what could armed police do if dispatched into the city streets? Without knowing who the other side was, firing even once in the dark streets would be a truly big problem..." The stout man, in his fifties, spoke slowly, adding emphasis and expression to each phrase.

Their attack plan was extremely military-like. First, they set fire to a Chinese kiln shop (釜뎜) and a Japanese store in Ujang-ni, relatively isolated from the city center. It wasn't due to any particular grudge, but likely they predicted (豫測) that setting a fire would draw the security forces to the fire scene. Then, one unit attacked the power plant (outside the city), another unit destroyed the Hoeryŏng River Railway Bridge (海蘭江鐵橋), and yet another unit entered the city and threw two bombs at the Oriental Development Company branch. People there say that after thus disabling the hands, feet, eyes, and ears of the city of Yongjŏng, they might have intended to inflict some kind of violence (暴力) on the entire city. After about an hour of commotion, they vanished like clouds into the dark night. It was eventually estimated (推測) that there were about 50 of them. No one counted them, nor did anyone see their faces (容貌) clearly. In the leaflets left behind where they retreated, there were phrases like "5.30 Incident (慘案)..." etc., and again at the end, it was written "Korean Communist Party, Koryŏ Communist Youth Association, Manchuria General Bureau Reconstruction Department."

The night of chaos passed. However, while some people knew about the incident that night, many apparently knew nothing until the next morning when they saw the mimeographed extra edition (騰寫版號外) of the local newspaper. Even if an incident occurs, people are not in a position to follow along like going to see a fire in the Korean mainland. Previously, there was an incident where a wealthy person (富豪) living in a village refused to contribute military funds (軍資金) and fled (避亂) into Yongjŏng city, only to be followed and his entire family annihilated (歿케) in the city center. Also, the interrogations ("Who goes there?" - 誰何) by ignorant Chinese police are not only annoying but also frightening.
By the time I arrived, most things had been restored (復舊). The power plant was severely damaged (浪籍), but they said they repaired and were using the old 60-kilowatt generator they had previously installed (設備), so the electric lights were like kerosene lamps. Briefly <5> touring the damaged sites, there wasn't much left. The places set on fire weren't severely burned, and of the bombs at the Oriental Development Company, one failed to detonate, and the other only shattered window panes. Only on the Hoeryŏng River Railway Bridge, sleepers were burned over a width of about one bay, evoking the scene at the time. The weapons they carried included pistols (拳銃), bombs, knives (칼), axes (독기), alcohol (酒精), distilled spirits (火酒), cotton (솜), etc.

The incident was most severe in Tudogu, about 40-50 *ri* from Yongjŏng. (Other affected areas where incidents occurred simultaneously were Idogu, Samdogu, Sadogu, Chidogu, Kŏlmandong, Namyangp'yŏng, Huch'ŏnga, etc.) In Tudogu, the common school, which housed several hundred students, was completely burned down. Also, about 100 attackers first came to the mountain behind the town around midnight (子正頃), made loud gunshot noises, then descended into the town, threw bombs at the Consulate, set fire to the school, caused a commotion at will (?) for a long time, and then disappeared with the sound of a military bugle (聚軍*叭). Later, it was found that the initial sounds were mostly firecrackers, not gunshots.
However, Yongjŏng could be called a Korean town. Both the Consulate police and local friends strongly advised that if I wanted to see more of Kando and learn more about the traces of the incident, I absolutely must visit Tudogu and Kukchaga (局子街). But due to time constraints (促迫) and not knowing the situation well, I wasn't inclined to wander around recklessly. Moreover, even in Yongjŏng, there was an order from the Public Security Bureau prohibiting outings after 8 PM, and the streets were filled with armed Chinese officials standing guard, clearly showing a state of martial law, which made me unwilling to venture further into dangerous territory. A friend said,
"Are you that scared? Something like what happened to Mr. Chang Tŏkchun won't happen, so don't worry. Since you've come all this way!"
and laughed. Even hearing Mr. Chang Tŏkchun's name mentioned in jest made my face flush red with shame, and a chill ran down my spine as if doused with cold water. I heard from someone who explained in detail that he went missing (行方不明?) near Mo'ŏrsan (帽兒山) on the way to Kukchaga, and I was deeply saddened.

Then, the day before I left Yongjŏng, leaflets arrived by mail from the Changch'un area via Kirim and Tonhwa. Looking at them, they also called for riots. "Commemoration of the 5.30 Incident!" etc., and signed by the Communist Party Reconstruction Department. I could tell where the epicenter was. On the return trip (回路), I shared the train car with the Chief of Police. During the journey, he talked about the nearby terrain (近方) and pointed to a cloud-shrouded mountain to the north, saying, "That—is Mo'ŏrsan." Mo'ŏrsan—Military service (從軍)! Chang Tŏkchun! My head became hot like this. Passing another spot, he said, "Last time, near here, someone with a pistol killed a person on the train and jumped off!" Kando, Yŏnbyŏn, where there is no peace—on the day I left, it rained quite hard. Having eaten poorly due to bad provisions (餘料) and being exhausted, I suffered numerous nightmares on the train before crossing the Tumangang River* (End) <6>

**The Scene at Sinhŭng Coal Mine**
S Newspaper Special Correspondent Pak Yunsŏk
An incident occurred at the coal mine that digs out the Hamhŭng Coal!, widely advertised with large characters on the streets of Seoul and used as fuel in many households. At that time, as a special correspondent for S Newspaper Company, I visited the site to see it. Now, I will write down a few scenes from the site.

Awakened from a sweet summer night's dream, the train carrying us passed Wŏnsan at six AM on June 23rd, 1930. After chatting about the current situation with Lawyer Han Kukchong, who was traveling with me, I arrived at Hamhŭng Station at 9:30 AM, changed to the Chosŏn Railway Hamnam Line (朝鐵咸南線), and headed alone towards Changp'ung-ni. This was my first time traveling in this direction, so I didn't know which way Changp'ung Station, said to be 17 miles and 7 *pun* from Hamhŭng, was, nor the terrain or communication facilities. The gasoline car (까소린車) running on the light railway was surprisingly fast. The narrow car, shaped like a Seoul tram, was already packed from Hamhŭng with plainclothes and uniformed police carrying batons and pistols (拳銃), as well as police officers dressed as laborers. I sat alone holding my bag, like a single red dot in a sea of green, and they also stared at me with very peculiar eyes.

Arriving at the actual site, the area around the coal mine, spanning about 100 *ri*, was virtually under martial law (戒嚴狀態). Thus, from Oro-ri, waiting for the train going up to Sangp'ung via Changp'ung to investigate the tragic scene (慘景) at the site, I boarded it. Unexpectedly, the young Korean detective (刑事) in worker's clothes who had been tailing me stepped back, and a Japanese police officer waved his hand. (Text omitted in original - 略) If anything happens, come and investigate, I said, but he replied, "No, I have something brief to ask. Let's go into the train car for a moment." Accepting his kind request, I went inside. "Excuse me, but due to my profession, I must ask you to show me the bag you are carrying," he requested. "Must you see it?" "Yes, please. I apologize for the intrusion (恐入ります)." So, I showed him everything in the bag, manuscript paper scraps, pajamas, etc. "I'm very sorry, excuse me (相済みません失禮しました)," he said, bowing repeatedly. I felt quite sorry for not being able to provide any material to the Japanese policeman. However, having only traveled around the Seoul region before, this was the first time I had my luggage searched.

My duties for that day (June 22nd) were partially completed, so I decided to visit the site of the explosion to write a detailed report for the next day. Although there was a train line from Sangp'ung Station to Changp'ung, about 10 *ri* away, the last train had already departed.
I decided to walk the 10 *ri* with a photographer, prepared to be stopped and searched. However, at Changp'ung Station, dozens of police officers blocked (阻止) my path. At that time, I asked to see the head of the search team by presenting my business card (名剌). Mr. Watanabe (渡邊), the Chief of Higher Affairs (高等主任) at the Sinhŭng Police Station (新興署), came out and, unexpectedly, guided me with utmost sincerity. Despite having prohibited all newspaper reporters (including Japanese ones) from entering or leaving until then, and despite the prosecutor's order absolutely forbidding interviews with newspaper reporters, he even introduced me to Mine Chief Saga (左賀), who had given the order, and explained and showed me each part of the explosion damage. I was grateful for the unusual courtesy (厚待).
The root of the problem began on May 3rd last year. The 150-odd employees (從業員) of the coal mine operated by the Chosŏn Coal Mining Co., Ltd. (朝鮮炭業株式會社), which occupies the coal deposit <7> on the southern slope of Namsan Mountain in Changp'ung-ni, Kapy'ŏng-myŏn, Sinhŭng-gun, presented the company with conditions addressing their long-standing grievances:
I. Improvement of Treatment (待遇改善)
a. Opposition to unreasonable beatings (無理敺打)
b. Elimination of disrespectful language (不敬言語)
II. Wage Increase (賃金引上) (20% or more)
Note: Increase wages for women and minors by 30% or more, and for pit prop setters (支柱10取) by 40% or more.
III. Reduction of Working Hours (勞働時間縮少)
Implement an 8-hour workday.
IV. Opposition to dangerous excavation work inside the mine (坑內危險堀出役)
V. Adequate installation of sanitation facilities (衛生機關)
VI. Payment of severance pay (解雇手當). Note: Calculated at 20% or more of daily wage.
VII. Death consolation money (死亡慰藉料) equivalent to one year's daily wage.
VIII. Injured persons (負傷者) to receive treatment as requested by the individual, and payment of injury allowance (負傷手當) at the current wage rate until full recovery (全治).
IX. Non-interference (干涉) in labor union (勞働組合) organization activities.
X. Absolute opposition to unconditional dismissal (無條件解雇).
XI. Expulsion (逐出) of 3 mine police personnel (警務係員).
XII. Provision of distributed goods (配給品) at actual cost (實費).
Presenting these 12 conditions to the company, the 150-odd male and female workers further solidified their unity to achieve their demands, and also attempted various demonstrations, confronting the company. The situation became quite perilous for a time, even drawing public attention.
However, through the intervention and mediation of executives from the General Federation of Labor (勞働總聯合會) in Hamhŭng at the time, some of the 12 conditions were accepted by the company, and with concessions from the strikers' side, they returned to work unconditionally, bringing the dispute to a temporary conclusion.
We do not intend to meticulously debate the rights and wrongs of the past now. However, given that the dispute between the miners and the company, which had grown large enough to attract public attention <8>, simply saw a glimmer of resolution (曙光), it is undoubtedly true that numerous twists and turns (曲折) must lie hidden beneath the surface of the incident that subsequently caused such a major problem. Rather than tracing back the distant causes (遠因), summarizing the recent course (코-스) roughly, could the immediate fuse (導火線) that precipitated the sudden turn of events not be the following? Soon after the new mine chief, Mr. Saga, took office (赴任) on June 11th, with his new aspirations (抱負) and plans, and driven by the preconceived notion (先入注見) of eradicating (絶滅) the root cause (禍根) of the previous collective strike, he established and posted 11 new mine regulations (坑則), unprecedented before, starting with Article 1: "Absolute obedience to company orders." Furthermore, demanding the submission of a written pledge (誓約書) within a week to firmly abide by these mine regulations henceforth—didn't this lead directly (急轉直下) to the incident (招致齊來)?

In short, who could have imagined that interference such as demanding obedience to mine regulations and controlling intentions, done with colored glasses for the sake of tightening control (團束) for the future, would indeed result in such an unfortunate incident (不祥事)? This was no small setback (狼敗) for the company as well, eventually leading to the involvement of judicial power (司法權). What, indeed, do the circumstances at that time and the current situation say?

On June 21st, the resentment (反感) of the 150-odd miners towards the company reached its peak. The previous agreement and the amicable resolution method regarding the unconditional return to work were, beneath the surface, nominal at best (有名無實). The company, without reason, subjected the miners identified as key figures (分子) in the previous strike (스트라익) to inexplicable attention and surveillance (監視)! Thus, if even the slightest fault was found, they were unforgivingly dismissed, or faults were deliberately fabricated to carry out dismissals (馘首)! and purges (淘汰)! arbitrarily (恣行), causing them no small amount of anxiety and fear, which they implicitly concealed. Reaching the boiling point (템프 - tempo) of emotion, their dissatisfaction! and resentment ultimately seemed unable to reject terrorism (테로리즘)? With the demand (催告) to submit the pledge swearing absolute obedience to the 11 mine regulations, and the dismissal of Yi In-sŏp, who had acted as the main leader (리다-) of the previous strike group, on June 21st being the final straw, the situation grew quite unstable. This day passed, and around 11 AM on the 22nd, the dissatisfied 150-odd individuals? smashed over 10 barrels of *soju* (燒酒) in the dark pine forest (松林), braced themselves for the immediate sacrifice for the sake of the future, raised a toast of hardship, and then attacked and bombed the place where they had worked for 7-8 years with affection, solving their daily bread problem! The engine (엔진)! They cut railway and telephone lines, burned documents, etc., embarking on a well-planned, systematic act of terrorism, shaking the slumbering Changp'ung-ni area, causing the mine owner and office supervisors, men and women, young and old, to flee (避亂) temporarily, thus triggering the first major incident since the mine's development (開拓). In other words, the cause was simple, and the result was grave. Various rumors (流言飛語) circulated about it. The black smokestack (煙突)! How would the black smoke emitted by the laborers, working like black-skinned people with pickaxes (곡갱이) in the dark rock pit (石坑), not sparing sacrifice, indeed flow? **The Tanch'ŏn Incident and the Scene**
H Newspaper Special Correspondent Hanyang Hak'in

One
As a newspaper reporter, on the morning of July 21st, I heard the announcement from the Government-General Police Affairs Bureau (總督府警務局) about the "Tanch'ŏn Incident." The content was: Around 4 PM on the 20th, about two thousand residents (面民) of Hatamyŏn, Tanch'ŏn County, Hamnam Province (咸南端川郡何多面), due to a dispute centered around the Forestry Cooperative (森林組合) that had been ongoing for several days, swarmed (쇄도하야) the county office (郡廳) in Tanch'ŏn-ŭp. They sought a meeting (面會) with the county magistrate (郡守) demanding the dissolution of the Forestry Cooperative and the release of fifty-four residents who had been arrested (檢擧) earlier in connection with this incident. However, their demands were refused. Consequently, the crowd became agitated (激昂) and smashed the windows and glass panes of the county office with clubs (곤봉) and stones. At this, the county magistrate and county office staff all fled. The crowd then swarmed the nearby Police Station (警察署), demanding the release of the arrested individuals. As the situation turned perilous (험악하게), about thirty police officers attempted to disperse and pacify (진무) the crowd, initially firing seventy-five blank shots (空砲). However, as the situation only grew more chaotic, they eventually fired live ammunition (實彈), resulting in four immediate deaths (卽死者) and twenty-six injuries among the crowd. On the police side, Police Inspector Inoue (井上警部補) and nine others were injured. This unprecedented incident had broken out.
(July 21st AM, Police Affairs Bureau Announcement)

In other words, the Forestry Cooperative was the direct, proximate cause that led to a clash (衡突) between a crowd of over 2,000 and the police force, resulting in 4 immediate deaths and 26 injuries on the civilian side, and 10 injuries on the official side. Looking into the sequence of events leading up to this:
On the 18th, two hundred residents of Hatamyŏn swarmed the township office regarding the Forestry Cooperative issue, then turned towards the Tanch'ŏn County Office. When they reached the vicinity of Namdaech'ŏn stream, the local police came and said, "This issue won't be resolved just because you go to the county office <10>. Disperse quickly. If you disperse*, we will handle it favorably." So, they all returned. However, that night, about twenty leaders were arrested. The next day, the 19th, about four hundred residents of Hatamyŏn gathered again and marched to the Tanch'ŏn County Office, staying from morning until 4 PM, only to be dispersed again. The crowd then dispersed into the eight townships within Tanch'ŏn County, agreeing to regroup. On the following day, the 20th, about two thousand people gathered from various townships in Tanch'ŏn County. It is said that only the five hundred residents from Suhamyŏn (水下面), being too far away, could not arrive in time that day. Thus, the representatives of the crowd met County Magistrate Im Ch'angjae (任昶宰) and demanded the dissolution of the Forestry Cooperative, stating it caused more harm than benefit to them, and the release of the arrested individuals. As their demands were not met, finally, Yi Sŏngmo (李性模), chairman of the Hatamyŏn committee of the Youth Alliance (靑年同盟), told the magistrate that if he refused the representatives' demands, they had no choice but to have everyone come in and demand it. When this message was relayed to the crowd, they became agitated, stormed into the county office carrying shovels, and ended up breaking about five hundred eighty window panes. They looked for the county magistrate, who had already hidden in the attic, but couldn't find him as he was newly appointed and they didn't recognize his face (낫). They then moved towards the police station. When the demand for the release of the arrested was refused, the crowd decided to break the windows and force their release. This led to a direct confrontation with the police force, ultimately producing the reported incident.
The incident is clear and significant. Thus, on the night of the 22nd, I hurried to the scene in Tanch'ŏn as a special correspondent for my company.

Two
Come to think of it, too many major incidents have occurred recently in the northern part of Korea. Whether it's the Kando Communist Party member attack incident or the Sinhŭng Coal Mine incident, they all shocked the world. When I arrived in Tanch'ŏn, judicial officials including the Chief Prosecutor from the Hamhŭng District Court (咸興地方法院), a Police Affairs Official (警務官) from the Police Affairs Bureau, and the Provincial Police Chief (道警察部長) from the Provincial Police Department (道警察部) had arrived and were conducting an on-site inspection (臨檢). **Summary**

This article compiles three separate reports by different correspondents covering major incidents in Japanese-occupied Korea and Manchuria during the summer of 1930. The first details the "Kando Incident," a coordinated series of attacks attributed to communists across a wide area in Manchuria, highlighting the planning involved, the targeting of Japanese facilities, and the resulting Sino-Japanese tensions affecting Korean residents. The second report covers the "Sinhŭng Coal Mine Incident," stemming from a labor dispute over harsh conditions and the company's subsequent crackdown, which escalated into sabotage by miners. The third describes the "Tanch'ŏn Incident," where a large peasant protest against a disliked Forestry Cooperative led to a violent clash with police at the county office and police station, resulting in numerous civilian casualties from police gunfire.

From a colonial history perspective, these reports are significant. They vividly illustrate different forms of resistance and social unrest: organized political violence (Kando), labor struggle turning violent (Sinhŭng), and mass peasant protest against colonial economic/administrative policies (Tanch'ŏn). The articles reveal the tense atmosphere, the heavy police/military presence, the methods of state control and repression (including surveillance, arrests, and lethal force), and the precarious position of Koreans, whether as immigrants in Manchuria caught between Chinese and Japanese authorities or as laborers and peasants facing exploitation and hardship within Korea. The journalists' own experiences (being tailed, luggage searched, reliance on official sources balanced with attempts to gather local accounts) also reflect the conditions of reporting under colonial rule.

1930年 5月 30日밤부터 31日 새벽까지에 말하자면 霋時間에 南北150,60里, 東西180,90十里의 地域內에 잇는 10餘個 都市와 部落을 突風的으로 襲擊한 이 事變이야말로 또한 그 統制잇는 가장 計劃的인 武力的 手段方法과 動員의 範圍가 너른 点에 잇서서 間島初有의 大事變이다. 더욱 特記할 것은 事變裹面의 勢力을 지은OO主義的OO運動의 劃期的轉變을 지은 点에서 또한 重大性을 가졋다고 보지 안을 수 업다. 그 뿐 안이라 이 大事變이 던지고 간 問題는 間島의 日中關係에 微妙한 波汶을 現出식히는 同時에 東滿40餘萬 同胞에게 直接間接으로 밋는 生活上問題의 重大한 影響은 今後 가장 深刻히 展開될 것으로 深甚한 監視와 硏究를 要하는 幾多의 問題(結果)를 준다. 다시 말하거니와 이 歷史的 大事變은 世界觀, 社會觀, 民族觀 等 그 立場에 딸아서 批評이 또한 區區할 것이다. 모든 運動이 崎形的 進展을 보지 안을 수 업시된 間島의 「特殊情勢」 그것이 이 大事變에 對한 省察과 批評을 簡單케 하지 안는다. 그리고 重大한 「疑心」을 갓게 하는 몃 가지 点을 쓰지 못하는 以上 事變의 俱体的 論斷은 后日의 結果로써 多少說明케 하고 이 글에는 巴人兄의 注文대로 大事變의 뒷자최만 써 보기로 하자.

事件의 眞相을 探査하여 오라는 所謂 特派의 일홈으로 豆滿江을 건느기는 6月2日 午前이다. 豆滿江, 峽流를＜2＞ 끼고 올나가 國境의 圖們鐵橋에 발을 멈추니 얼마나 蕭篠落莫한 風景이랴. 兩岸의 보잘 것 업는 쓸쓸한 두 部落사이에는 兩國間을 連結한 近代式 鐵橋만이 덩거렁하게 걸쳐잇고 屛*가치 둘러맥힌 山峽에 울리는 「쇠당나구」의 分에 넘치는 汽笛소리만이 遠路의 旅情을 센티멘탈하게 한다.
「이제부터 異國이거니-」이러케 생각하면서 셩냥匣가튼 輕便車에 몸을 실엇다. 참말 한거름 건너만서도 異國갓다. 우리땅의 山岳이 모다 險峻하든 것과는 一變하야 이 땅의 地勢는 荒凉한 原野와 丘嶺이 重疊함이 洋海의 물결갓다.＜3＞
멀리 계(蟹)딱지를 모아 노은듯한 部落은 모다 同胞의 村落인데 때는 마츰 端午節에 天候는 暖和한지라 가는 곳마다 部落에는 한 두개의 「건네」가 매이고 男女老幼가 한 뜰에 모여 平和로히 佳節을 行樂하고 잇섯다. 어듼가 한 田圃로 달릴 때 *들번들한 人造絹 새져고리를 닙은 20內外의 젊은 안악네 너덧명이 신발을 버서들고 汽車를 向하야 精神업시 달려오다가 소래를 치며 웃고셧는 것을 보앗다. 銅褐色의 健康스러운 얼골에 입이 터질뜻한 우슴을 웃는 그들의 戱弄을 나는 車窓에 依支하여 유심히 바라보고 잇섯다. 머-ㄹ리 荒凉한 *平線우에 뛰는 그들의 그림자가 사라질 때 나는 그들의 천진과 구해 낼 수 업는 永遠한 無智에 알지 못하게 눈물을 흘렷다.

市內電車速力에서 더하지 안은 經便車 그러고 樹林도 河川도 아무란 色彩와 變化도 업는 窓外風景으로 疲勞한 몸을 끗업시 倦怠케 하엿다. 그리하여 龍井까지 5時間의 餘裕를 압두고 支離한 생각에 못익이게 한다. 그 때 懷慶街站(驛)바룻前 鐵橋의 枕木이 몃 개 검어케 탄 것이 잇섯다. 또 驛을 조금 지나 沿線밋헤 외딸은 집 한 채가 礎石만 남기고 全燒된 것이 잇섯다. 젹은 뜰도 잇는 것이 學校갓햇다. 들으니 普通學校湖泉街分校이라고 한다. 亦是 OO隊의 行爲임에 틀림업섯다. 그러나 타고남은 조그마한 자최만으로는 그 때의 危急한 事情을 想像할 바도 못되엿다. 더구나 曠漠한 原野이라 고만한 火災는 마당귀의 복닥불밧게 더 생각되지 안는다. 다 龍井과 100里相距되는 곳에도 그갓치 同時日에 事件을 이르켯든 것 만으로 當時의 動員이 規模잇는 廣範圍엇든 것이엇슴을 알 수 잇겟다.

龍井村에 들어가기는 그날 午后 2時 半頃이엿다. 멀리서 바라보니 洋屋도 비이고 人家가 자못 櫛比하다. 事實 地名이 龍井村이라고 「村」字는 부터잇지만은 延邊의 中心都市로 人口가 近 2萬, 中國側에는 公安局陸軍聯隊 等이 잇고 日本側은 間島總領事舘이 잇고 警官이 100名갓가히 잇는 곳으로 조금도 粗忽히 볼 수 업다. 그런데 이만큼 警備狀態가 完備하엿슴에 不拘하고 不過 50餘名의 一隊가 突入하여 發電所를 破壞하여 暗黑의 市街를 만들고 一方에 放火를 하고 電信線을 切斷하고 鐵橋를 破壞하고 市內東拓支店에 爆彈을 던지고 等等의 軍事的 攻擊手段을 하엿다고는 놀라지 안을 수 업다. 驛頭에 내리며 이런 것을 생각할 때에 京城서 한 張電報에 感電되든 듯한 興奮의 피가 다시 全身에 돌앗다. 第一報의 打電할 時間의 促迫이 또한 緊張케 하엿다. 干先 領事舘으로 달리어 갓다. 短銃을 찬 것 長銃은 메인 것 等의 日中警官이 거리 모통이마다 서 잇는 것이 얼마다 警戒의 非常함을 말하고 잇섯다. 警察部長을 만낫더니 大事變이란 꿈에나 치른 듯이 天然하다. 「이에-예하면서 그는
「勿論 共産黨을 根滅식히기는 힘들겟지요. 잘 안되겟지요. 이러한 사건은 이번 한 번에 끗칠 것이 안이고 후에도 또 뎡녕 繼續해 일어날 것입니다. 爆彈이요? 米國製인데 져긔 저런 것! 조고만 것입니다!」고 한다. 내의 등 뒤 유리欌속으로 보니 大小數十個의 爆彈과 長銃, 短銃, 劍 等의 破損된 것이 가득히 둘너잇다. 뭇지 안아도(中略-원문) 그는 다시 뭇는 말에
「襲擊 當한 곳은 全部 日本 외 機關과 所屬인 것 뿐입니다. 治安의 責任? 勿論 中國땅이 안이요? 中國 巡警과 陸軍의 人員은 充分하다고도 하겟지요. 그러나 實力과 誠意 如何가?
하며 머리를 흔들다. 勿論 나는 治安의 責任을 뭇고져 한 것은 안이엿다. 그러나 이 한 말에 이 事件의 結果가 第二段的으로 如何히 展開될 것인가를 感得할 수 잇섯다. 滿鐵回收까지 부르짓고 吉會線의 完成을 反對하며 滿洲에 잇서 日本의 勢力을 가장 꺼리는 中國이 엇떤 点으로던지 口實을 남기지 안으려고 할 것은 明若觀火한 것이니 그러한 事變의 地인 東滿 卽間島의 40萬 移住同胞들은 엇던 境遇에 이를 것인가 하는 問題이다. 事件그것보다 結果가＜4＞ 크다 할 것이다. 果然 最近의 新聞紙가 雄辯으로 說明서고 잇는 것이다.

却說 事變이 이러나든 그 밤의 情況 듯기로 하자. 그 前에 30日 午后 해질녁에 龍井市 一角에서는 40餘名 男女學生과 靑年의 一大示威運動이 잇섯다. 「비라」도 撒布되엿다. 이것이 大事變의 前奏曲이엇든 것이다. 示威運動은 日本領事舘 警察이 出動키 前에 中國巡警이 모다 解散식히고 崔某 한 名만 잡히엇섯다. *러고 밤 9時頃 大路壁上에는 「포스터」만큼식 큰 檄文이 *기져기 붓헛다. 大膽히도 前記 「비라」에는 朝鮮共産黨高麗共産靑年會滿洲總局再說部라고 뚜럿이 백인 것으로 暴動을 이르키자고 씨워잇섯다. 그 밤에 거리에 부친 檄文에도 暴動을 이르키자고 뚜럿이 써 잇섯다 한다. 間島란 特殊地帶의 領事舘警察이라 이 示威前에도 엇떤 氣脈을 豫探치 못햇슬埋도 업섯슬 것이고 兼하여 그 져녁에 그와 갓튼 示威運動과 大膽한 檄文도 잇섯슴으로 그 밤은 非常한 警戒를 하엿든 模樣이다. 그 밤의 情況에 對하야 警戒의 第一線의 指揮者이든 領事舘警察署長은 말한다.
「勿論 미리 짐작을 하엿섯스나 時日은 알 수 업섯슴니다. 더구나 그 날 져녁의 示威와 檄文으로 더 잘 알엇스나 檄文이란 것도 하 번번히 잇는 것이니까 그 밤에 그러케까지 事件이 突發하리라고 생각을 못하고 警戒만 하고 잇든 中입이다. 31日 새벽 두時頃이 될런지요. 내가 여기(署長室)에 안저엇슬때에 電燈이 검잇검잇하기에 元來 故障이 자든 곳이라 또 停電인가하고 생각하면서도 오늘밤은 異常타고 疑心을 품으며 電燈이 꺼지기에 燭불을 켜려고 할 즈음에 「탕」 「탕」 요란한 소래가 들리지 안엇습니까. 그 때 「아하- 일이 낫나보다」고 直感되엿습니다. 그 때 部下가 爆彈이라고 닐러읍니다... 그러나 밤은 *둡은데 電燈까지 업스니 武裝한 警官을 出動식힌단들 市街에서 엇떠케 합니까. 뎌便의 엇떤것까지 몰으고 밤 市街에하 한번 放銃한다면 그야말로 큰일입니다...」軆軀가 壯大한 半百의 그는 말 마듸말다 高低와 表情을 부쳐가면서 徐徐히 이러케 니야기 한다.

그들의 襲擊計劃은 極히 軍事的이엇다. 첫제 市街와는 比較的 새떠저진 牛場里의 中國人 가마(釜)뎜과 日本人 商店에 放火하엿다. 그것은 何等怨含이 잇섯든 것도 안이디 放火하면 警備가 火災處로 몰릴 것을 豫測하엿든 모양이다. 그려고 一隊는 發電所(市外)를 襲擊하고 또 一隊는 海蘭江鐵橋를 破壞하고 또 一隊는 市內로 들어와 東拓支店에 爆彈 두 개를 던지엇다. 이가치 하여 龍井이란 都市의 手足과 耳目을 빼내인 後에 全部 市街에 엇떤 暴力을 加하려든 것이 안인가 하고 그 곳 사람들은 말한다. 約 한 時間동안 騷動이 잇슨 후 그들은 구름갓치 闇夜에 자최를 감추엇다. 그들이 몃 명이엇는지 乃終의 推測이 50名 가량이라고 한다. 그들을 헤인 사람도 容貌를 자세히 본 사람도 업섯다 한다. 그들이 물려간 자리에 남은 「비라」에 「530慘案...」云云의 글발이 잇고 亦是 끗헤는 朝鮮共産黨 高麗共産靑年會 滿洲總局再建設部이라고 씨워잇섯다.

騷亂의 밤은 새이엇다. 그런데 그 밤에 事變을 알은 사람은 알엇지만 젼혀 몰으고 잇다가 이튼날 아츰에야 그곳 新聞의 騰寫版號外를 보고 알은 사람도 만엇다 한다. 事件이 잇대야 朝鮮內地에서 火災求景 단니듯 딸아나갈 形便도 못된다. 前者에도 村落에 살든 富豪가 軍資金 調達에 應치 안코 龍井市內로 避하여 들어 온 것을 딸어 들어와 市街中心地의서 一家를 참歿케 한 事件도 잇슨 곳이라 또 無智한 中國警官 「쓔갸」(誰何)가 귀치안키도 하고 무섭지 안은것도 안니다.
내가 갓슬 때에는 大槪復舊되엿섯다. 發電所는 浪籍하게 되엿섯스나 녯날 設備하엿든 60「키로」의 發電機를 修理하여 쓴다고 하여 電燈은 람푸燈 가헛다. 暫間＜5＞ 被害處를 돌보니 남은 것은 업다. 放火하엿든 곳도 그리 크게 불탄것도 업섯고 東拓의 爆彈도 한 個는 不發되고 한 個만이 터지어 유리창을 개트렷다고 할 뿐 海蘭江鐵橋에만 約一間幅으로 枕木이 탄 것이 當時의 光景을 방불케 하엿다. 그러고 그들이 가졋든 武器로는 拳銃, 爆彈, 칼, 독기, 酒精, 火酒, 솜 等이엇다.

事件이 가장 甚하기는 龍井서 4,5十里許되는 頭道溝이엇다.(그外의 被害地는 2道溝, 3道溝, 4道溝, 志道溝, 傑滿洞, 南陽坪, 湖泉街等地로 同時日에 이러낫든 것이다.)頭道溝에서는 數百名을 收容하는 普通學校가 全燒되엿다. 그러고 그곳에는 約 100名이 첫 번에는 子正頃에 뒷山에 와서 雄壯히 銃소리를 내이고는 市街地로 내려가 領事舘에 爆彈을 던지고 學校에 放火를 하고 長時間 마음것(?) 騷動을 이르키고는 聚軍*叭 소래와 가치 자최를 감추엇다고 한다. 내종에 알고보니 初最는 放銃이 안이라 爆竹을 노은 것이 만엇다고 한다.
그런데 龍井은 朝鮮人 市街라고 하여도 조타. 間島만을 좀더 보고 事件의 자최를 좀더 알려거든 頭道溝와 局子街를 꼭 보고 가라고 領事舘署에서도 勸하고 그 곳 親故들도 만히 권한다. 그러나 間時의 促迫한 關係도 잇고 事情도 잘 몰으는 곳에 덥젹거리며 딘니기도 마음에 끌리치 안엇다. 더구나 龍井만 해도 밤 8時 以後에는 外出을 禁하라는 公安局의 令이 잇고 市街에는 擔銃한 中官憲이 거리마다 서잇서 宛然戒嚴狀態임을 볼 때 險地?에 더 들어가고 십지 안엇다. 엇떤 親故는
