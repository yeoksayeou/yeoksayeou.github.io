Prominent Women and Birth Control
Dear Madam=
1. Do you practice birth control? What is the method?
2. What is the most precious thing in this world to you?
3. How many sons and daughters would you like to have?
4. What newspapers and magazines do you read?

Kyŏngsŏng, Sung 3-dong 127
Hŏ Yŏngsuk
1. Currently, I have no need for birth control, so I don't practice it. From past experience, I know well that I do not get pregnant while breastfeeding a young child. And I am currently breastfeeding my 9-month-old baby.
2. My husband and children are the most precious. If asked which is more precious, the husband or the children, I cannot answer. Although the forms of affection are different, they are exactly the same in their preciousness.
3. I am raising two sons.
4. For newspapers, I used to read the Tonga Ilbo the most. Since its suspension, there's nothing I read with particular enjoyment. For magazines, I read Pyŏlgŏn'gon and Samch'ŏlli from beginning to end every issue.

Sung 4-dong 120
Yun Sŏngsang
1. I have practiced birth control before.<50> As for the methodology, I'll leave that for another opportunity later.
2. Nothing particularly precious comes to mind. Perhaps it's because ultimately, everything is precious.
3. I think having one son and one daughter would be best, but I don't feel any urgent need for it yet.
4. Among Korean publications, for newspapers, I read the Chosŏn [Ilbo] and Chungoe [Ilbo]. For magazines, I read Samch'ŏlli, Taejung Kongnon, etc., and whatever else comes my way.

Tongnae-gun, Pokch'ŏn-dong
Na Hyesŏk
1. Rather than calling it birth control, I am careful not to have children. The method is unnatural, so I dislike talking about it.
2. My own body is the most precious.
3. I have 3 sons and one daughter.
4. For newspapers, I read both the Chosŏn [Ilbo] and Chungoe [Ilbo]. I'd like to read magazines continuously, but it's characteristic of Korean-language magazines that they start publication and then stop shortly after, so there's nothing I read regularly; I just read whatever comes my way.

Hongp'a-dong 2-ŭi 4 Ch'oe Sŏnbok - Wife of Mr. Chu Yohan -
1. I do not practice it.
2. My own body.
3. I have two daughters.
4. Tonga [Ilbo], Chosŏn [Ilbo] both papers, Samch'ŏlli, Pyŏlgŏn'gon, Yŏsŏng chi U, Haksaeng, Chosŏn chi Kwang, Sinsaeng, Sin Sosŏl, and others.

Tongdaemun Bakkat Sinsŏl-li 159
Hwang Sindŏk
1. For me, birth control is still premature. Hahaha.
2. Human life is more precious than anything, isn't it?
3. Two sons and two daughters would be nice, but since I'm poor, even one of each wouldn't be out of place.
4. There's nothing I read regularly. I read whatever comes my way, glance at the table of contents, and if there's something I want to read, I read it.

P'yŏngyang, Sinyang-ri 44 Yŏ Sunok - Wife of Mr. Yang Chudong -
1. I don't particularly welcome the theory of birth control. I'll omit the theoretical basis.<51>
2. The tone of your question is a bit strange, seemingly looking down on women somewhat, making it very uncomfortable to give a serious answer. Lately, we have many rats in our house, so should I say the cat is the most precious?
3. As our family fortunes are utterly depleted, having many children, whether male or female, is a worry, and having many who cannot fulfill their roles is also a concern. However, ideally, 2 sons and 1 daughter would seem good.
4. I always intend to read a lot but lament the lack of time. For daily publications, I generally skim through the three types: Tonga [Ilbo], Chosŏn [Ilbo], and Chungoe [Ilbo]. I don't particularly read any Korean magazines.

Outside the city, Sŏngbuk-tong 40 Pak Yŏngsŏn
1. Am I practicing birth control? If I knew how, I would want to practice it, but because I don't know, I can't. Haha.
2. For me, the most precious thing in the world is Love (namely, Universal Love).
3. I would like to have 1 son and 2 daughters.
4. The newspapers I read are Tonga [Ilbo], Chosŏn [Ilbo], Chungoe [Ilbo], and the Japanese Ōsaka Asahi. For magazines, I read the Japanese Shufu no Tomo, Taejung Kongnon, and Samch'ŏlli.

City center, Wŏnnam-dong 113 Yu Yŏngsuk - Wife of Mr. Ch'oe Tokkyŏn -
1. I do not practice birth control.
2. This land! Chosŏn (Korea).
3. I have none [children].
4. Our newspaper is the Chungoe Ilbo. Magazines are Samch'ŏlli and Sin Sosŏl published by Kŏnsŏlsa. From Japan, the Yomiuri Shimbun, and the magazine Fujin Kōron.

Sŏngbuk-ri 225 Kim Ilyŏp
1. I do not practice birth control, but I don't strive to give birth either.
2. To be honest, it would be my husband. But giving a serious answer to such a playful question is funny, so I can only say everything bestowed by heaven is precious. Husband, comrades, work, flowers, money.
3. For me, having children is fine, and not having them is also fine, but having about one smart and handsome child <52> would be nice. Of course, having one girl and one boy each would be good, but then that would be two, which is too many...
4. For newspapers, I read Chungoe [Ilbo] and Chosŏn [Ilbo]. For magazines, I continuously read Samch'ŏlli, Taejung Kongnon, Pulgyo, Sin Ch'wimi, etc., every month.

City center, Kwan-dong 69-ŭi 42 Chŏn Yudŏk - Wife of Pang In'gŭn -
1. I cannot practice it yet.
2. It is my children.
3. I would like to have two sons and one daughter.
4. I read almost all of them.

Chungoe Ilbo Sa Kim Malbong
1. I intend to practice it when the opportunity arises.
2. My three-year-old daughter Mae-mae is the most precious.
3. If possible, I would like to have two sons and two daughters.
4. Due to my connection with the newspaper company, I get to read almost all newspapers and magazines published in Korean.

Kyŏngun-dong 88 Kaebyŏk Sa Kim Wŏnju
1. I am not yet qualified to answer this question.
2. Even for the same person, what they consider precious differs according to age, the specific time, and the environment at that time. Currently, I value books as much as life itself.
3. I don't know.
4. As the opportunity is good, I read almost all Korean newspapers and magazines.

City center, Kyŏnji-dong 80 Son Ch'oak - Taejung Kongnon Sa -
1. Since I am unmarried, I cannot know.
2. When I reflect upon myself, I always feel that I am not outstanding in any single aspect. Consequently, I cannot hope for something that could be called the most precious thing in this world.
However, if I were to pinpoint what I constantly <53> desire and hold as my creed, it would be comrades. Like Michelangelo, who made art his lover, or Cavour, the founder of Italy, who said, "Italy is my lover, my wife." While I lack the strength to advance with such tragic determination and fervor, considering comrades as my husband (or lover) and representing everything else seems to be what is most precious to me.
3. I haven't thought about it yet, so I don't know.
4. There isn't anything I could say I read particularly; however, if I were to mention what I read even a little, for newspapers, it would be the Chosŏn Ilbo, and for magazines, perhaps your esteemed publication [Samch'ŏlli], Chosŏn chi Kwang, and Sinsaeng.

Sajik-tong 78 Chin Tŏksun - Wife of Mr. Kim Yŏngp'al -
I will answer your questions briefly.
1. I have not yet thought about birth control.
2. It's not flattery, but my husband is the most precious.
3. I would like to have two sons (brothers) and one daughter.
4. I read the Chosŏn Ilbo, and I read almost all the magazines that come to the house. <54>

**Summary**

This 1930 survey in the magazine *Samch'ŏlli* captures the views of prominent Korean women (including famous figures like Na Hyesŏk and Kim Ilyŏp, as well as wives of notable men) on birth control, their most valued possessions/ideals, desired family size, and reading habits. Responses vary widely: some practice or consider birth control, often citing knowledge passed down or lack of information as reasons for their choice, while others reject it or deem it premature. Views on what is most precious range from husband and children, self, Korea, love, comrades, to books, reflecting diverse personal priorities. Desired family sizes often hover around two or three children, sometimes expressing a preference for sons, though practicality and financial concerns are mentioned. Reading habits show a reliance on major Korean newspapers (Tonga, Chosŏn, Chungoe) and various contemporary magazines, alongside some Japanese publications.

From a historical perspective, the article offers a fascinating glimpse into the lives and thoughts of educated, urban Korean women during the colonial period. Their awareness of and attitudes towards modern concepts like birth control, their varying degrees of traditional versus modern values (valuing family vs. self/ideals), their engagement with contemporary media (both Korean and Japanese), and the candidness (or sometimes guardedness) of their responses provide valuable insights into female consciousness, social change, and the intellectual climate of 1930s Korea. The mention of specific publications, personal circumstances (like Hŏ Yŏngsuk's knowledge about lactation amenorrhea or Hwang Sindŏk's financial constraints), and the differing opinions among these "New Women" are particularly revealing for historians studying gender, modernity, and colonial society.

기사형태	설문

名流婦人과 産兒制限
先生은=
1. 産兒制限을 實施함니까. 그 方法은?
2. 이 世上에 第一所重한 것이 무엇임니까?
3. 아드님 몃, 따님 몃을 가지고 십슴니까?
4. 무슨 新聞과 무슨 雜誌를 보심니까?

京城 崇3洞 127
許英肅
1. 現在 나는 産兒制限할 必要가 업슴으로 아니합니다. 그것은 前例를 보아서 나는 어린 것에게 젓을 먹이는 동안에 姙娠이 되지 아니하는 것을 잘 아는 까닭입니다. 그러데 나는 지금 9個月된 아희에게 젓을 주고 잇슴니다.
2. 男便과 子息이 第一 所重함니다. 男便과 子息 그 어느 것이 더 所重하냐 무르면 그것은 대답해지지 안슴니다. 形容이 다른 愛情이지마는 그 所重한 품에 잇서서 똑갓흠니다.
3. 아들 두 개를 길느고 잇슴니다.
4. 新聞으로는 東亞日報를 第一 잘 보앗습니다. 그것이 停刊된 이후로는 별로 자미부처 읽는 것이 업슴니다. 雜誌는 別乾坤 三千里를 每號 처음서부터 끗가지 읽슴니다.

崇4洞 120
尹聖相
1. 産兒制限은 한 적이 잇슴니다.＜50＞, 그 方法論은 後日 다른 機會로 미루지요.
2. 所重한 무엇이 생각나지 안슴니다. 結局 모-든 것이 다 所重한 까닭인진 몰나도.
3. 아들 하나 딸 하나만이 제일 조흘 것 갓슴니다만은 아직은 그리 급한 생각이 나지 안슴니다.
4. 朝鮮 것으로는 新聞으로 朝鮮 中外 雜誌로 三千里 大衆公論 等 其外에 닥치는 대로 보고 잇슴니다.

東萊郡 福泉洞
羅蕙錫
1. 구타여 産兒制限이라 이름질 거시 아니라 아이 아니들만치 조심합니다. 그 方法은 不自然해서 말하기 실습니다.
2. 내 몸이 第一 所重함니다.
3. 아들 3兄弟 딸 하나입니다.
4. 新聞은 朝鮮 中外 兩 新聞입니다. 雜誌를 게속해 보고 십흐나 조곰 나다가 고만 두난 것이 朝鮮文雜誌의 特性임으로 늘 보난거슨 업고 닥치는대로 봄니다.

平壤 新陽里 44 呂順玉 -梁柱東氏 令夫人-
