Sijo Selection
Yi Mu-sin
On a moonlit night on Hansan isle,
Sitting alone in the military tower,
With a great sword girded at my side,
As I stroke my long beard,
From somewhere, the sound of a barbarian flute
Cuts into a man's heart.

Chŏng Mong-ju
Should this body die and die,
A hundred times over again die,
My white bones become dust and dirt,
Whether my spirit remains or not,
This single heart loyal to my lord,
How could it ever change?

Kim Chong-sŏ
The north wind blows on the treetops,
The bright moon is cold amid the snow,<66>
At the ten thousand li frontier fortress,
Standing grasping a long sword,
In a long whistle and a great shout,
There is nothing to stop me!

Sŏng Sam-mun
When this body dies,
What shall I become?
On the highest peak of Pongnae Mountain,
I shall become a tall, sparse pine.
When white snow fills heaven and earth,
I shall stand alone and green.

Pak P'aeng-nyŏn
A crow, struck by snow and rain,
Seems white but remains black.
The bright moon of night,
Even at night, would it be dark?
This single heart loyal to my lord,
How could it ever change?

Chŏng Ch'ŏl
Tearing out this heart of mine,
I wish I could make that moon.
In the ninety-thousand li vast sky,
Hanging clearly,
I would shine upon
The place where my beloved lord resides. <67>

P'ain
Wind of August
The soft, soft wind of August stirs my coat ribbon. Does it stir my clothes, telling me to go to the riverside? When the riverside water birds flap their wings, should I too clap my hands loudly and jump about playfully? I truly want to, but—

In the August wind, faintly heard, that sound of the people's suffering, the sound of "Aigoo!" from being beaten; when it pierces this body like a knife blade, though this body is insignificant, clenching my teeth, I cannot leave the streets, I must guard my workplace.

The August wind blows so much from Hamgyŏng Province, from Hyŏnjŏ-dong, from India, from the Philippines, from factories, from fisheries; the hot breaths of comrades like a storm, gathered together.

Comrades, let us open our chests wide to the August wind and receive it gladly<66>, South Gate, East Gate, you too spread your arms wide, receive this wind, and guide it gently into the capital.

Ah, the Flood Disaster
Must these people build houses on stone peaks? Must they scoop dirt to eat? Even if you cannot add, you take away daily, oh that heaven!

If that floating cloud is another cloud that will become rain, I want to shoot it down. When I think that wretched cloud swallowed thousands of my brethren,

Because these lives are so worthless, even the clouds hold them in contempt. No, perhaps that cloud has something to say to these people, showing itself as a great flood? That must be it, that must be it. Only we sit here, not having come to our senses. Only we sit here, not having come to our senses.

Yi Hang-bok
Oh cloud, crossing the high pass of Ch'ŏllyŏng, resting as you go,
Take the tears of grievance of this lonely subject, make them rain,
And scatter them over the Ninefold Palace where my lord resides.

Han Ho
Don't put out the straw mat, can't I sit on fallen leaves?
Don't light the pine torch, the moon that set yesterday rises again.
Hey boy, though it's just poor liquor and mountain greens, don't say there's none, bring them out. <67>
<66-67>

**Summary**

This article presents a collection of *sijo* (traditional Korean poems) followed by two modern prose poems. The *sijo*, attributed to famous historical figures like Chŏng Mong-ju and Yi Sun-sin (under the pen name Yi Mu-sin), primarily express themes of loyalty to the king, patriotic duty, integrity, and resilience in the face of adversity. The modern poems, one by "P'ain" and another untitled piece lamenting a flood, shift dramatically in tone, reflecting the harsh realities of contemporary life, likely under colonial rule. They speak of suffering, social consciousness, solidarity among workers ("comrades"), and despair mixed with a critique of indifference (even from nature or heaven) towards the plight of the common people. For historians, the juxtaposition of classical patriotic verses with modern poems expressing contemporary suffering and nascent class/social consciousness is particularly interesting. It highlights how traditional forms and themes were invoked alongside new poetic expressions grappling with the difficulties and social dynamics of the colonial period, including awareness of global connections ("India," "Philippines") and the hardships faced by laborers and disaster victims.

파인
八月의 바람
팔월의 누군누군한 바람이 내 옷고롬 글느네, 옷 글느고 강변으로 가란 말인가 강변의 물새 날개칠 때 나도 손퍽 짝짝치며 엄벙덤벙 뛰여놀난 말인가, 정말 그러고도 십건만은―

팔월으 바람결에 은은히 들니는 저 민곳맷 소리 매애맛는 애고고 소리 칼날가치 이 몸을 어일 때 보잘 것 업는 한몸이나마 이를 악물고 거리를 못떠나옵네, 일터를 직히압네

팔월의 바람은 함경도서도 현저동서도 인도서도 비율빈서도 공장서도 어장서도 너무도 만히 불어오네, 폭풍우가치 뜨거운 동무들의 입낌이 한데 몽치어서요

팔월의 바람을 동무들아, 가슴 활활 열어 헤치고 즐겁게 바더드리자＜66＞, 남대문아 동대문아 너도 팔을 널니 버리고 이 바람바더 장안에 고히 인도하게나
