The Aroma of Fine Writing

Will, Mindfulness, and Diligence
Yi Kwangsu

Young men and women must surely set forth a lifelong wish. They must firmly establish a wish, thinking, "I will accomplish this kind of work in my life." They must establish a wish that if they cannot achieve it in this life, they will achieve it even if it takes reincarnating through three, four, hundred, or thousand lives, and they will not close their eyes in death before achieving it. The great figures and patriots of old were all people who established such wishes.
"I will surely make Chosŏn a Chosŏn of knowledge, wisdom, harmony, and peace in my life."
Although there may be countless wishes depending on the individual, isn't this a fitting wish for the youth of Chosŏn today? If those who establish this wish increase, wouldn't the land of Chosŏn be stirred (掀動) by that willpower? The first thing Chosŏn youth must do today is to establish a wish. "Even if heaven and earth collapse, my wish will not change!"
However, even if one establishes a wish, what good is it if forgotten? Just as a hen incubates her eggs day and night, keeping them warm, one who has established a great wish (大願) must be mindful of it in the morning, mindful in the evening, mindful when sitting, and mindful when walking, without ever forgetting it. Only then will the wish gain more fervor and strength.
Chosŏn people have forgetfulness (健忘性), and often forget things they swore while tying their jacket strings. It's a mouse's mentality. Seeing instantly, forgetting instantly, crying and fussing instantly, forgetting instantly. This is why it is necessary to be mindful of the established wish from time to time. One must be mindful without pause to renew the willpower.
However, no matter how great a wish is established and how often one is mindfully awake to it, what use is it without action? One must set up a plan to achieve that wish and, like an ant digging the earth or a bee gathering honey, tirelessly and steadily, even if little by little, labor according to that plan to achieve the wish. This is called diligence (勤). A wish without diligence is like a boat without water.
A person without a wish is a person without a soul; a person with a great wish is a person with a great soul. Isn't youth the very time to set forth a great wish? Isn't it the time to establish a great wish that, if unattainable in one lifetime, will be achieved in three, four, nine, or ten lifetimes? O brothers and sisters (姊妹) who establish great wishes and practice great mindfulness, rise up by the thousands, rise up by the tens of thousands!

Account of Seeing Tagore Off in India
Chin Sunsŏng

It was noon on July 11, 1916. A group of 23 people stood waiting for the train from Tōkyō Station to Yokohama, intending to visit the Indian poet sage, Mr. T'a[gore], who was staying at the Sankei-en garden in Honmoku, Yokohama City.<62> Among them were men and women. There were students and teachers, Japanese people of course, but also Chinese, Taiwanese, and a Hawaiian lady. Among them were two Koreans: C kun and myself.
Having heard that the Master was coming to Japan, I had been hoping for an opportunity to visit him. By chance, through a friend's planning, this visiting group was formed, and I became a member, my joy beyond words. Yet, on the other hand, I couldn't suppress my embarrassment. -- It wasn't that meeting such a world-renowned great man wasn't an honor for a novice like me with shallow knowledge, but I secretly worried, what would I say if asked something! Amidst this, one ray of hope was, "C kun is here!"
As the train carrying us left Tōkyō Station and approached Shinagawa, people began whispering amongst themselves, wondering what would be appropriate to say. Should each person ask their own questions individually? Or would it be better to present a unified topic and listen to the Master's talk? Opinions varied, but eventually, it was decided to ask for the Master's talk on the topic of "The New Life of Modern Youth."
While discussing, what particularly caught my attention was how these people, meeting for the first time, interacted intimately, calling each other 'you' and 'I' ('네야 내야'), seeming like friends of ten years. I recalled a story I had read in a book somewhere: when a certain Western couple was about to have a domestic quarrel, they would invariably cover the portrait of the Holy Mother (聖母) hanging on the wall with paper before fighting. It means there is no strife beneath the venerable. Master T'a[gore]'s intangible virtue was enough to make these people from various countries, meeting for the first time, into friends as if they had known each other forever ('一面如舊').
Getting off the train and entering the gate of Sankei-en, on the right were verdant trees, and on the left stood a solitary peak. Along the path climbing towards the peak, ponds on both sides were filled with fragrant lotus flowers, and at the far end opposite was a sea bathing area where naked children darted here and there. Seeing the vastness of the domain and the natural scenery, one could hardly think it was within private gates. (The place called Sankei-en is the villa of the Yokohama magnate Hara Tomitarō, who offered a part of it for Master T'a[gore]'s lodging during his stay in Japan.)
Our group, sweating profusely, climbed to the top of the peak. Amidst the lush trees stood an exquisite Japanese-style house, and beside it were a Chinese-style pavilion (支那式 亭子) and an ancient five-storied pagoda. We all respectfully adjusted our collars and waited in front of the Chinese-style pavilion. After a good while (良久), the Master emerged, wearing a tea-brown (荼褐色) velvet hat and a white silk Indian robe (綢屬印度道袍) long, his gray hair and white beard fluttering.
"Everyone, I am terribly sorry (未安햇소). I was delayed a bit today because I went to the doctor due to a toothache. Please forgive me (容恕하오)." His simple greeting and momentary smile evoked infinite sympathy and respect from people.
We accompanied the Master into the pavilion. He sat facing a desk (冊床), and we sat in rows facing him. After presenting a floral wreath expressing everyone's respect, the Master received it with a happy face, hung it around his neck, and performed the Indian gesture of joined palms in thanks (合掌謝禮). Japanese ancient-style incense (焚香) was burned, and a gentle breeze spread the classical fragrance throughout the quiet pavilion, intoxicating people. Each person stood up in turn to state their name and country of origin. When my turn (次例) came, feeling the utmost solemnity (崇嚴), my heart pounded for no reason.
"(My name is Hakmoon Chin, I came from Corea)" I blurted out in a strained voice (燥音) and sat right back down. Later, thinking my voice had been too frantic, I couldn't help but blush secretly. After the introductions, Waseda University Professor Nak Tong'ak T'aro (中桐確太郞) stood up and, in simple words, represented the group, expressing gratitude to the Master for meeting us despite his busy schedule (忽忙하심을 不拘하고), and also conveyed that the youth gathered today wished (願한다) for the Master's talk on "The New Life of Modern Youth." The Master closed his eyes tightly, meditated silently (暫時 黙想하다가) for a moment, then calmly (從容히) opened his mouth:
"This issue is a very large and heavy one. I don't know if my words will satisfy you or not, but the question of why man is born (엇지하야 낫느냐)? For what purpose is he born (엇지하야 무엇을 하랴 낫느냐)? has troubled many minds for a long time. However, when we look at the Great world (大世界), what do we feel (늣기오)? When we see the solemn, dense forests hiding all mysteries, when we see the plains where the five grains (五穀) flourish (茂盛) and the hundred flowers (百花) are in full bloom (滿發), when we see the vast (廣闊), free, eternal ocean of freedom, when we hear the beautiful songs of birds and the lovely breathing sounds of fish, when we see this Great world, what indeed do you feel? I, I cannot help but feel infinite love, light, and joy! Love, light, and joy! People of the world are always absorbed only in material civilization, lacking spiritual sublimity. Between individuals, societies, and nations, they build fences and are anxious only to distinguish things. But material life is not true life, and distinguishing things does not increase happiness. We<63> yearn for something beyond material civilization. If we cling only to individuals and nations, I think the taste of true life is ultimately difficult to savor. True life... why were we born? For what purpose were we born?... But when I face the Great world, I always see the Supreme Person (Supremederson, 神). I believe man was born to see this God. Born to feel the spirit of the universe."
Thus, for over an hour, with his beautiful, music-like voice, he spoke fluently, raising and lowering his tone. When passion arose, he clenched his fist, struck the desk with his palm. Sometimes he closed his eyes tightly, seeming to recite inwardly (吟誦하는 듯), and other times, seemed to see something. The entire group listened attentively in silence without a single cough (마듸). Afterwards, there were questions from 3 or 4 individuals, and the Master answered kindly and in detail. After taking a commemorative photograph, we bid farewell to the Master and stepped outside the Sankei-en gate. It was 6 PM. An irrepressible, joyful smile, as if drunk on sweet wine, lingered on everyone's lips. (Remainder omitted - original text)

Golden Lawn
Kim Sowŏl

Lawn,
Lawn,
Golden lawn,
The grass sprouting (붓는 불은 - likely 돋는 풀은) in the deep deep mountains
Is the golden lawn by the departed lover's grave.
Spring has come, the colors of spring have come.
To the tips of the willow branches too,
The colors of spring have come, spring days have come.
Even in the deep deep mountains, to the golden lawn.

The Sorrow of Kim Satkat
Kim Tongin

When Kim Satkat, one of the most beloved figures born in Chosŏn, went sightseeing at Moranbong in P'yŏngyang, several people there who supposedly recited poetry were gathered, humming poems.
Seeing them humming verses about the surrounding scenery with the rhyme character "洲" (chu/ju - isle/continent), the mischievous (작란군) Kim Satkat stepped forward and requested to recite a verse himself, saying he felt inspired. The group, unaware he was the famous Kim Satkat, condescendingly granted his request. Satkat immediately took brush and paper and wrote:

三天半落靑天外 (Three mountains half fall beyond the blue sky)
二水中分白駕洲 (Two waters divide the White Egret Isle)

Seeing this, the assembled poets immediately questioned Satkat, asking if this wasn't the famous poem by Li T'aibaek (Li Bai). Satkat then took the brush again and wrote another couplet:

古來文章奮五句 (From ancient times, writings have flourished in five-character lines)
夕陽投筆下楊洲 (In the evening sun, I throw down my brush and descend to Yangzhou)

We wish to view this anecdote (逸話) not so much to praise his quick wit (頓智), but rather with a different meaning.
If one were to compose a Chinese poem about the scenery viewed from Moranbong using the rhyme character "洲," one probably couldn't surpass the famous Tang dynasty lines of "Three mountains..." No, if Kim Satkat had only been born a few years earlier than Li T'aibaek, perhaps the "Three mountains..." poem would remain today as Kim Satkat's poem, who knows? Faced with such a situation, because he was the optimistic Kim Satkat, he laughed it off with the lighthearted phrase "From ancient times, writings have flourished in five-character lines." Had it been Li T'aibaek instead, he might have cried his heart out, wondering why he wasn't born in his ancestors' time.

Indeed, we often encounter this phenomenon. Tolstoy, Hugo, Goethe—these people have preemptively stolen and written countless things I intend to write. Alexander the Great worried that his father might conquer all nations before he grew up, thus impoverishing his "future," but for us, it has gone beyond worry and already become reality. It is truly frustrating and infuriating. We sometimes can't help but think, if only they had been a bit more foolish.

But there is something even more disastrous than that. It's nothing else but when I, alone, thinking "This alone must be my original creation," write and publish it full of pride, only for some friend to poke me hard in the ribs sometime later. Then, smiling annoyingly, they say, "Well, well, you've plagiarized so-and-so's writing."
At first, I don't believe it, but when they present evidence, I myself blush so much I want to die. There's nothing to say in defense, and truly, there's no situation more awkward. However, I can't take back what's already published, so I have no choice but to just purse my lips a couple of times and stop. It's at times like these I feel like crawling into a hole if there were one.
Truly, there are many times we resent not being born in our ancestors' era. (Remainder omitted - original text)

Ascent
Hyŏn Sosŏng

On a small mountain, grew a single pine tree (잣나무,海松) about one ja (approx. 30cm) tall.
Even at this size, considering itself a tree, it rustled its leaves (닙흔) quite properly in the breeze and once looked down below its feet (발아레를).―
Ants and bugs clinging (붓흔) to the ground crawled about (암을암을), and nameless weeds (일홈모를 雜풀들) an inch or two tall all bowed their heads low.
At this, the pine tree became conceited (숙냉통이 나왓다).―
"I've grown quite big too!" it thought.
One day, seeing a grasshopper (멧둑이가) fly over its head with a rustle (시르륵), it thought, "It seems there are grasses or trees taller than me above me. Ah, how strange (異常하여)!" and looked up (올녀다 보앗다) above its head.―
Unexpectedly, a eulalia grass (으액이풀 - likely 억새풀) stood there, waving its broad leaves, looking down at it.
"Ah, this won't do... I must grow taller too."
And so the pine tree began to grow.
Having grown to about one gil (approx. 180cm), the pine tree, its nose high in the air again, looked down below.―
Indeed, it had grown―the eulalia grass that looked down on it before was now seen below its shoulders (억개), and besides that, all the grasses, shrubs, and rocks unseen in the past (녯날에 안이보이던) were now spread out (버려져서) before its eyes...
"Ah, having grown this much, I've reached my full height," the pine tree believed (밋고 잇섯다) of itself.
But one day, again, it heard a sound above its head. It saw an oriole, one bird (한 머리), sitting atop a tall willow tree, singing "Kkwaegolnok kkwaegolnok."
At this, the pine tree was greatly disheartened. "Ah, this is no good!" it thought, and started growing again. Having grown quite a bit, this time it became a medium-sized (中도레 가는) tree, about 10 gil (approx. 18m) tall.―
The willow tree that stood hatefully (즛밉게) above its head yesterday was now vaguely visible (너슬너슬 하고 잇게 되고) right around the pine tree's navel level (뱃곱바루서), and trees it couldn't even see before were all looked down upon, either below its chin or below its nose. Seeing this, the pine tree, just like before, once again became puffed up with conceit (숭냉통이 불녹하게 나와서). "What, now there's no one to look down on me!" it boasted greatly (自漫을 하얏다).
But then one day, another pine tree of the same kind (同屬되는), standing below its shoulder (억개 아래), came looking (차자와서) as if something strange had happened (變이나 난 듯이), saying, "Ah, elder brother, you said there was no one taller than you (當身밧게는) in the world, but that was a flat lie (거즛말입듸다)... Look up there. See the white crane (힌 두룸이 한 머리), sleeping leisurely on the pine tree that has grown much taller (할신 놉히) than your head," and went away after pointing it out (注意식히고).
Looking up, it was indeed so―a crane dozing on a far-outreaching (빼난) branch looked very beautiful―
"Huh, how ridiculous. I was just idling," it thought, its head bowed low, and continued to grow again.
This time, it grew much taller, so that every single tree on this mountain was looked down upon from below this pine tree. From now on, all the flocks of cranes that came to sleep came (오*되고 - 오게 되고) to this tree's branches, and fields and the sea, which it couldn't see before even if it tried, now vied (다토아) to send picturesque scenery before its eyes far away.
At this, the pine tree's belly swelled again (배가 불너져서). "Eh, this is it now. Could anyone possibly be born into the world who can look down on me?"―its arrogance heightened (트림이 놉하졋다).
At this time, the pine tree didn't even think (념도 안이하고) of looking up, only looking down (아레만).
Amidst this, one night, the cloudy (흐렷던) sky suddenly broke open (툭 터지면서), and clear moonlight flowing through the clouds high above suddenly flashed (가분작이 번적하고) and poured down (내리부엇다) onto this pine tree.―
The pine tree, deeply asleep (깁히 잠드럿든) in a dream of arrogance and foolishness (傲憒의 꿈), woke up startled (화다닥 놀나 깨엿다) by this light ray.―
Lifting its head and looking up at the sky, clouds were drifting by, stars twinkled (반작반작하는데), and the round moon like a king's large tray (王소반가튼)―the sky, blue far, far away (멀니멀니 푸르러 잇는), seemed to look down, peeking (비죽비죽), as if scolding it.
At this, the pine tree became ashamed (붓그러워졋다). It trembled (떨니어젓다). It became frightened (무섬어졋다).―
"No, no, my height is not height," it thought.
"Oh, how frightening. The space above my head is limitless! Right (올타). For me, there is only growth (자람)! Only becoming bigger (큼)!" it thought.
From this time on, the pine tree, without any word (아모말이 업시), just grew, inch by inch.
―1915. 5. 3―<65>

**Summary**

This collection features essays and poems reflecting on aspiration, encounters, literary anxieties, nature, and personal growth. Yi Kwangsu exhorts Korean youth to harbor grand, persistent national ambitions fueled by mindfulness and diligence. Chin Sunsŏng recounts a memorable meeting with Rabindranath Tagore in Japan, highlighting the poet's charisma and spiritual message transcending nationality. Kim Tongin laments the perceived belatedness of Korean writers, feeling overshadowed by established Western and historical figures, expressing anxiety about originality. Kim Sowŏl offers a short, evocative poem linking a golden lawn to nature's cycle and memory. Hyŏn Sosŏng presents an allegory of a pine tree constantly striving for greater height, realizing true growth lies in recognizing the infinite space beyond oneself, abandoning arrogance for continuous self-improvement.

From a historical perspective, the articles offer insights into the intellectual and cultural currents of 1930s colonial Korea. Yi Kwangsu's piece reflects the nationalist discourse emphasizing self-strengthening and long-term vision for Chosŏn's future. Chin's account shows Korean intellectuals engaging with international figures like Tagore, seeking inspiration and connection beyond the confines of the colony. Kim Tongin's essay reveals the complex psychology of colonial artists grappling with cultural influence and the burden of history, feeling constrained by both Western literary canons and East Asian traditions (represented by Li Bai). Hyŏn Sosŏng's fable, written earlier but published here, could be interpreted as an allegory for national or individual striving under limitation, where true progress comes from humility and persistent effort rather than fleeting pride. Historians might find the blend of nationalist fervor, international awareness, artistic anxiety, and allegorical reflection valuable for understanding the era's mindset.

願과 念과 勤
李光洙

男女 靑年은 반듯이 一生의 願을 發할 것이다. 『나는 一生에 이러한 일을 하리라』하는 願을 굿게 셰울 것이다. 今生에 이 願을 못 일우면 三生 四生 乃至 百生 千生을 輪廻하더라도 이 願을 일우고야 말리라. 일우기 전에는 暝目하지 아니하리라 하는 願을 세울 것이다. 古來의 偉人 志士들은 다 이 願을 세운 이들이다.
『나는 一生에 朝鮮을 知와 智와 和合과 安樂의 朝鮮을 만들고야 말리라』
個人을 딸아 無數한 願이 잇으려니와 오늘날 朝鮮 靑年으로 이것이 맛당한 願이 아닐가. 이 願을 세우는 이가 만아진다 하면 그 願力으로 朝鮮 天地가 掀動되지 아니할까. 오늘 朝鮮 靑年이 할 일은 첫째로 願을 세우는 것이다. 『天地가 문어지더라도 내 願은 變함이 업스리라!』
그러나 한 번 願을 세우더라도 이저버리면 무엇이 되랴. 닭이 밤낫으로 알을 품어 그 알을 식지 안케 하는 모양으로 한번 大願을 세운 자는 아침에 念하고 저녁에 念하고 안으매 念하고 걸으매 念하여 일즉 이저버림이 업서야 비롯오 그 願은 더욱 熱을 엇고 더욱 힘을 엇을 것이다.
朝鮮人은 健忘性이 잇서 옷고름 맷고 맹세한 것도 어느 새에 이저버리는 일이 만타. 쥐 精神이다. 금방 보고 금방 잇고 금방 울고불고 작난하고 금방 잇는다. 時時로 한번 세운 願을 念할 必要가 이래서 잇는 것이다. 잇을 새가 업시 念하여 願力을 새롭게 할 것이다.
그러나 아무리 大願을 세우고 또 時時로 念하여 졸지 아니한다 하더라도 함이 업으면 무엇하랴. 그 願을 達할 計劃을 세우고 땅을 파는 개미 모양으로 꿀을 모우는 벌 모양으로 쉬지 안코 꾸준히 조곰식이라도 그 計劃을 딸아 願을 達하기 爲하여 勞役함이 잇어야 할 것이다. 이것을 勤이라고 한다. 勤업는 願은 물업는 배와 갓다.
願이 업는 사람는 魂이 업는 사람이요 願이 큰 사람은 큰 魂을 가진 사람이다. 靑年아이 때가 바야흐로 大願을 發할 때가 아닌가. 一生에 못 達할 願이어든 三生 四生으로 九生 十生에 達할 大願을 세울 때가 아닌가. 큰 願을 세우고 크게 念하는 兄弟와 姊妹야 千으로 일어나고 萬으로 일어날찌어다.

印度 타고아 送迎記
秦瞬星

1916年 7月 11日 正午라. 橫濱市本牧3溪園에 滯留하야 잇는 印度 詩聖 타先生을 訪問코자 東京驛에서 橫濱가는 車를 가다리고＜62＞ 섯는 23人의 1團이 잇스니 그 中에는 男子도 잇고 安子도 잇다. 學生도 잇고 敎師도 잇스며 日本사람은 勿論이어니와 中國人도 잇고 臺灣人과 하와이 婦人도 잇다. 그 中에 朝鮮사람도 두 사람이 잇스니 C君과 나라.
나는 前부터 先生이 日本에 오신다 하는 말을 듯고 한번 機會를 어더 訪問코자 하든 次에 마침 友人의 計劃으로 이러한 訪問團이 成立되야 그 中에 一員으로 參與케 되니 깃븜은 이로 말하기 어렵다. 하나 한편으로 부끄러움도 禁할 수 업다. --나와 가치 淺學凉識의 徒가 그와 갓흔 世界的 偉人을 맛나보는 것이 光榮이 아닌 바는 아니나 무슨 말을 무르면 무슨 말을 대답할고! 하고 남몰리 애를 썻다. 하는 中에도 한 줄기의 밋븜은 『C君이 잇스닛가!』
우리를 탠 車가 東京驛을 떠나 品川近傍에 오니 各其 수군거리며 무슨 말을 해야 올을가. 或 個人으로 自己의 疑問으 뭇는 것이 올을가. 또는 一統한 問題를 提供하야 先生의 講話를 듯는 것이 조흘가 하고 議論이 不一하다가 結局은 『現代靑年의 새 生活』이란 問題로 先生의 講話를 듯기로 決定되엿다.
서로 議論하는 中에도 特히 나의 注意를 끄는 것은 처음 만나는 이들의 各種 사람이 서로 네야 내야 하며 親近히 이야기하는 것이 10年 舊交와 다름 업시 뵈인다. 내 일즉이 어느 冊에서 본 이야기가 생각나니 그는 西洋 엇더한 夫婦가 內外싸홈을 하랴 하면 반다지 壁에 걸닌 聖母의 肖像을 조희로 가리고 싸홈을 한다 하는 이야기라. 거륵하신 아래에서는 爭鬪가 업다함이니 타先生의 無形한 德은 이 各나라 처음 만나는 사람들로 하야금 足히 써 一面如舊의 벗을 만도는도다.
車에서 나러 三溪園門에 드러서니 오른편에는 樹木이 蒼然하고 왼편에는 兀然한 孤峯이 옷둑 섯다. 그 峯을 向하야 올나가는 길에는 左右편 못에 香氣로운 蓮花가 가득찻고 마즌편 끗헤는 海水浴場이 잇서 벌거 버슨 아해들이 이리 뛰고 뛰리 뛰니 그 境域의 넓음와 自然의 景勝이 아모리 보아도 門戶안니라고는 생각할 수 업다.(三溪園이라 하는 곳은 橫濱豪客 原富太郞氏의 別莊으로서 타先生이 日本에 체주하야 게실 동안 그 一部를 先生의 起臥에 提供한 곳이라)
우리의 一團이 땀을 뻘뻘 흘리고 峰上에 올나가니 蒼鬱한 樹木 속에 日本制의 精灑한 집이 한 채 잇고 그 겻 혜는 中國式의 亭子와 古式 五重塔이 잇다. 一同히 靜肅히 옷깃을 발니하고 支那式 亭子 압헤서 先生을 기다린지 良久에 先生이 荼褐色 우단 帽子에 흰 綢屬印度道袍를 기름이 입고 灰色 머리와 흰 수염을 푸르르 날리면서 올나와
『여러분 大段 未安햇소. 내가 오늘 齒痛으로 해 醫師에게 갔다오느라고 조곰 느젓소. 容恕하오.』하는 簡單한 人事와 瞬間의 微笑는 사람으로 하야금 無限한 同情과 尊敬의 念을 生케 한다.
