人氣歌手의 藝術·私生活·戀愛
						如山
Kim Yong-hwan, Giving Private Music Lessons
Seoul in mid-summer is like a boiling cauldron. With 400,000 people packed between Pukak and Namsan, emitting warm breaths under the hot sun, it feels like anyone would suffocate.
Leaving this Seoul street behind, I caught a train for Wŏnsan one day in July. Gazing at the cool beauty of Sambangyugok and the superb scenery of Sŏgwangsa Temple, upon arriving in Wŏnsan, I immediately sought out Kwangsŏk-tong 2-bŏnji. It was to hear about the private life and artistic views of the popular singer, Mr. Kim Yong-hwan. (Photo is of Mr. Kim Yong-hwan)

Yŏsan: Mr. Kim Yong-hwan.
In a corner of Mr. Kim's neatly arranged study,
"Kaiser Complete Works" (카이랄 전집)
"World Literature Complete Works" (세계문학전집)
"Modern Drama Complete Works" (근대희곡전집)＜121＞
such famous works were piled up almost covering the wall, and on one side, portraits of great musicians like "Wagner" (와그나-) and "Beethoven" (베-도펜) were hanging. It is said that one can know the knowledge and taste of the owner by looking at the books in their study, and now one can sufficiently grasp the attitude with which Mr. Kim Yong-hwan is cultivating himself for art.
"I hear you give private lessons. How many students are you teaching?"
"Yes. I teach singing in the morning, and seven very diligent and talented young people come every day. So I have them practice vocal exercises and sing Western masterpieces. Then, in the afternoon, I teach the violin (빠요링), and currently, ten people are attending. They are all very enthusiastic."
"Spending most of your time for others like that, when do you find time to practice for records?"
"That's why my own time is only before breakfast in the morning. I wake up around six in the morning, do some exercise, and then immediately practice music."
"Some people say that eating eggs or something similar when singing makes the voice come out well. Do you do anything like that?"
"Well. I like bananas and tomatoes and always eat them. Eating such fresh fruit is not only good for the body, but especially thinking as a musician, it certainly seems to help make the vocal cords soft and clear."
"So, from whom did you learn music?"
"The motivation to learn music was like this. I finished Wŏnsan Paesŏng School at twelve and then Pogwang Middle School. Since my student days, my interest always leaned in that direction. At eight, I was praised for singing well at a school arts festival, and in middle school, I was a trumpet player and violinist (빠요링선수) in the school band. After that, the person I really learned some music from was teacher Chŏng To-in (鄭土仁), who is now a professor at Songdo Higher Common School. I received private lessons from him when he was formerly the conductor of the Yi Wangjik Royal Music Department (李王職 雅樂部)."
Thus speaks this twenty-six-year-old young artist straightforwardly. Not only is his demeanor very proper, but his speech, reflecting his refined appearance, is also modest, without any exaggeration, spoken softly. The reporter's first impression is that he is a very gentle person.
"I heard you were involved in theater?"
"Yes. I did a bit. I learned something about theater from Mr. Pak Yŏng-ho (朴英鎬), who is now the head of the literary department at T'aep'yŏng Records. After that, I was the musical director at Wŏnsan Hall (元山舘) and learned violin (빠요링) from a German, Mr. Na Wi-el (나위엘). My involvement in the theater world (劇界) began right then. I first directed 'T'aeyang Street' (太陽街), which was a great success. Setting foot in this, I left with Sin Pul-ch'ul's (申不出) 'Arirang Counter-Piece' (아리랑 反對篇), but things fell apart in Hamhŭng. So, disheartened, I returned home, and then Mr. Yi Kyŏng-sŏl (李景雪), who has now passed away, asked me to teach him singing. Through Mr. Kyŏng-sŏl's introduction, I met Mr. Wang P'yŏng (王平), and eventually joined Polydor (포리돌-). ＜122＞"
"Among the records you've recorded to date, which one do you feel most confident about?"
"It's 'Border Night' (국경의 밤). As my own composition, nothing has received such acclaim, but due to unavoidable circumstances, its release has been halted midway."
"How many records have you recorded so far? Also, you compose; how many pieces have you done?"
"Well, if you count all the recordings, it reaches about 350 sides, and the compositions alone probably exceed five hundred."
"You've done quite a lot. Judging by sales volume, which ones were most welcomed?"
"None received particularly bad reviews, but the ones that sold well were 'Tuman River Boatman' (두만강 뱃사공), 'Chosŏn March' (조선행진곡), and 'Song of the Century's End' (세기말의 노래), which I composed and Mr. Yi Kyŏng-sŏl recorded, was a success."
"It might be awkward to say, but who among the currently popular singers do you consider the most outstanding?"
"Everyone has their own strengths (一長一能), so I cannot presume to say anything definitive. However, in my opinion, among male singers, Mr. Kang Hong-sik (姜弘植) sings the weightiest and most resounding masculine songs. I admire him as someone with vocal power that will develop even further in the future. And among female singers, no matter who is mentioned, Miss Sŏnu Il-sŏn (鮮于一扇) possesses the Korean flavor, soft yet seemingly 끊는 듯 (cutting off), seemingly 잇는 듯 (connected), with a delicate and graceful quality (硏硏嫋嫋함), making her the foremost of our time, I believe. We would like to proudly present the voices of these two individuals anywhere."
"I understand well. What is the scope of your friendships, Mr. Yong-hwan?"
"My circle of friends is not wide. And with the friends I have, I like to maintain a close relationship. One of my friends is Mr. Wang P'yŏng; he is a trustworthy person who doesn't spare his own wealth or effort to help when he sees others in distress."
"It's customary to ask artists about their 'view on love,' so let me ask you as well."
"Rather than a view on love, let me tell you an amusing story. I have received dozens of letters from the opposite sex so far, and the most spectacular among them are from widows with two or three children asking to meet somewhere or sending gifts. However, I never reply to any of them. As an unmarried man, before meeting the person I will entrust my life to, how can I carelessly become close to this person and that person? My conscience simply does not allow it."
"Then, what are the conditions for your ideal partner?"
"Someone with beautiful features."
"That's a basic condition, of course. And then?"
"Someone who knows how to greet guests kindly, knows etiquette in dealing with people (待人接客), and can endure hardship no matter how severe."
"Yes. Then, what about artistic refinement?"
"It would be even better if she understands music somewhat." ＜123＞
"What was the happiest moment for you so far? That is, when did you feel pride as an artist?"
"Last year, there was a national famous singers' music festival at the Tokyo Hibiya Public Hall. At that time, I was the only Korean participant, and the enthusiastic welcome I received from the audience, that was it."
After finishing his words, I took a taxi with this young artist and drove to the white sandy beach of Myŏngsasimni, searching for the sweetbrier (Haedanghwa) field. Even the sweetbrier, though abundant in old times, is disappearing because Westerners complained about thorns pricking their feet and had them pulled out. Then, intending to go to Kalma Peninsula (葛麻半島), we set sail on the bright sea in a small boat (一葉片舟) with a white sail hoisted, heading towards Kalma Island with a fair wind. Before my eyes, accustomed only to mountains, the clear blue waves of the East Sea (東海蒼浪) were so wonderful; the fine ripples of the bright sea turned into gold and silver, widening and narrowing, white waves crashed against the rocks, splashing towards the sky, and white gulls (白鷗) circled above. Far across the sea, where the sky and sea met, clouds veiled the mystery from human eyes with five-colored clouds. I gained an impression like that of a deserted island on Kalma Peninsula and sang a song there dedicated to a single flower. After sightseeing all these scenes of Wŏnsan, I returned via Sŏgwangsa Temple and Sambang Yaksu (三防藥水), becoming a person of Seoul city again after 5 days.＜124＞

Yi Nan-yŏng, A Flower Amidst Wind and Rain (花發風多雨)
Miss Yi Nan-yŏng
Leaving behind her beloved hometown, family, and parents, yesterday singing and dancing under a nettle tree in the Arabian desert to pass the day, today watching forsythia petals fall like foam on the flowing water by the Volga river, passing that fleeting night—people point fingers and call such a person a "gypsy" (집씨-). Yearning for the romantic and free life of a gypsy following water and grass (水草), there was a song melody that drifted through the streets of the capital last mid-summer. That song was O.K. Records' "Gypsy's Song" (집시의 노래), and the one who conveyed through this song an undeniable thread of sorrow (哀愁) into the empty chests of the youth here is Miss Yi Nan-yŏng (李蘭影), whom we are about to discuss.

From the bamboo groves thick with bamboo fields in the Yŏngnam mountains and rivers, like a lost lone wild goose crying plaintively on an autumn moonlit night in Odongdong (오동동 秋夜月), with a voice thin yet delicate (가늘고도 연연하고), delicate yet soft (연연하고도 부드러운), Miss Yi Nan-yŏng, the princess of our folk songs (民謠의 公主), how she cast an unforgettable sad melody into our hearts.
When her single body cried, tens of thousands of youths across the wide land cried; when her single body wandered east and west following the gypsy life, the youth here who followed her also flowed east and west, following water and grass. Truly, was not Miss Yi Nan-yŏng the beloved street artist (街頭의 藝術家)?
Wanting to meet the longed-for Miss Nan-yŏng, fanning myself with a wood-shaving hat, I sought her home in Ch'ungsindong, inside the East Gate, on the third day of July as the mid-summer heat bore down. A cool breeze flowing over the ridge of Nak'tasan swept through the capital Seoul, but being an exceptionally hot day, getting off the tram and climbing towards the Nak'tasan ridge via Yangsagol made me sweat considerably.
Miss Yi is nineteen this year.
Medium height.
Her nose bridge is defined, and her eyes sparkle with intelligence (英彩).
She is wrapped top to bottom in light pink silk gauze (나사).
She gives the impression of a plump, clean Miss.

Reporter: How long have you been walking this rough path in the much-talked-about field of "art" (예술), especially without reward? You must have suffered much hardship during that time. Please tell us about the motivation for first appearing on stage.
Yi: My hometown is Mokp'o (木浦) in Chŏlla Province. My father was always ill since I was young, and the family was poor—so with my uncle's help, I finished Mokp'o Public Common School there at the age of fifteen.
Reporter: And then?
Yi: The motivation that determines one's career path can seem amusing if you think about it. I have an older brother named Yi Pong-gap (李鳳甲), who is twenty-two. My brother loved art and ran a musical instrument store in Mokp'o. So naturally, the impulse to sing beautiful songs arose in the young girl's heart. Around that time, the T'aeyang Troupe (태양극단) came to Mokp'o. Thinking I could become a famous actress if I joined them, I consulted with my brother and mother, and at midnight, I set out following the troupe. Did I have proper clothes? Did I have proper shoes? Dragging worn-out rubber shoes, wearing a kitchen apron (행주치마)—hohoho, it was quite a sight. So, having actually followed them, did they let me play Katyusha (갓주샤) or Ch'unhyang (춘향) from the start? At first, they made me cook rice and light the fire in the kitchen—I wasn't an actress but practically a housemaid (계집종년). Anyway, somehow, after following that troupe for about 8 months, I ended up drifting all the way to Osaka (大阪). Yes.
Reporter: You performed there.
Yi: Performance, but what a performance it was. Truly a performance of tears; I returned carrying sad memories of Osaka of tears that will not be forgotten as long as I live. ＜125＞ Yes. Well, upon arriving in Osaka, a letter came from my mother back home, saying, "Father's illness is critical, please send some money so I can at least buy herbal medicine to serve him." But where would I get money? So at that time, I recorded a song called "Doraji Doraji" (도라지 도라지), the Doraji Taryŏng, for Taep'yŏng Records (大平레코-드). Because they promised to give me money, I recorded the ballad using all the talent I didn't have. Yes. So that song became quite popular in Osaka for a while. In other words, the sales results were quite good, but the company wouldn't give me the money. They played dumb. Where could such an outrageous thing happen? Even when I asked them to lend me just 3 or 4 won to help my sick father, they refused. If I had even 3 or 4 won in my possession then and sent it home, how happy my bedridden father would have been, thinking, "This is the money my daughter earned."
Reporter: The company is wicked indeed. Did his illness get better?
Yi: What do you mean, better? After thinking hard, I considered selling my body as a cafe waitress (女給), but then I thought that even if I succeeded by ruining myself like that, it wouldn't be the way to be filial to my parents, so I continued to focus solely on acting. Then one evening, wearing stage costume, with rouge and powder applied, just as I was preparing to go to the dressing room (樂屋 - gakuya) to sing a solo during intermission,
"チチ ナクナツタ スグカヘレ ハハ" (FATHER PASSED AWAY RETURN IMMEDIATELY MOTHER - Chichi nakunatta sugu kaere haha)
an utterly unexpected telegram was delivered. Yes. Feeling as if the world had collapsed, filled with sorrow and the regretful thought of not being able to send even one dose of medicine, my heart felt constricted, so utterly dumbfounded and grief-stricken that even tears wouldn't come. Yet, from the stage, they were shouting "Nan-yŏng, come out!" "Nan-yŏng, come out!" at the top of their lungs. Having no choice, I dragged my unwilling feet and mournfully, mournfully, as if lamenting my own fate, as if calling to the soul of my pitiful departed father, I sang the "Gypsy's Song" (집시의 노래), and then, unable even to finish the end, I collapsed right there on the stage and wept. Truly, it's heartbreaking to think about. Having children and siblings, yet both unable to be there at the end, and when he passed away, not even being able to brew him a single dose of medicine—

Reporter: And then?
Yi: Gradually, I came to understand the world. That there are many sad things in the world-- After that, I returned to Chosŏn, and fortunately, the owner of Asahi Trading Company (朝日商會) recognized the song I had recorded for T'aep'yŏng, which led me to join O.K. Records (오-케-회사).
Reporter: What was the first one you released?
Yi: It was "Phoenix" (不死鳥), followed continuously by "Nostalgia" (고적) and "Hometown Longing" (鄕愁). That was when I was seventeen.
Reporter: How many records have you put out to date?
Yi: Altogether, it must be about thirty sides. ＜126＞
Yi: "Welcoming Spring" (봄마지), "Phoenix" (不死鳥), "Song of the Five Great Rivers" (五大江打鈴), and so on.
Reporter: Yes. They were all sung on the streets. By the way, how is it? You must receive many love letters from fans piling up.
Yi: They come occasionally. Some gentlemen critique the songs like music critics (音樂評家), and some contain love letters (戀文) saying they'll 'live or die' for me. But I don't reply to any of them. Because I haven't yet received a single letter that suits my heart.
Reporter: Still, wasn't there anything with a bit of romance mixed in among them?
Yi: There was this one incident. A young man, claiming to work at the OO Bureau, sent an earnest courtship letter with his photo and even a certified copy of his family register (民籍謄本) attached. But the last phrase was a masterpiece. He wrote, "If it is not your intention, please return this family register copy and photo." In other words, it was like casting a line while passing by. So I sent them back. His age was twenty-something, and his name was XXX, but please don't write that.
Reporter: What kind of man do you seek as a future partner (配人)?
Yi: Oh, how should I put it? Anyway, someone whose facial features (耳目口鼻) are all complete, with a large, healthy, manly physique—a sportsman (스포-츠 맨) would be nice.
And given the current situation of Korean people, can one expect high living expenses? I believe a satisfactory household can be established with someone earning around 50 won. As for education, since I myself couldn't study sufficiently, someone who finished middle school would be fine too. And if you ask for one fantasy, I find myself drawn to poets or novelists.
Reporter: Which artists do you admire?
Yi: How can I comment on Korean figures? Among women of the same sex, I like Greta Garbo (구레타 칼보) and Irie Takako (入江たか子).
Reporter: What are you reading now?
Reporter: I am reading Mr. Ch'unwŏn's (春園先生) "That Woman's Life" (그 女子의 一生) with tears and emotion. For magazines, I read "Samch'ŏlli" (삼천리) and "Chungang" (중앙). I also read "Shufu no Tomo" (主婦之友) and "King" (ギンク).

The reporter, sincerely wishing for the art of this affectionate and sensitive young woman to grow ever more radiant, took his leave. The sun had already passed the tip of the Chonghyŏn Catholic Church. ＜127＞

**Summary**

This 1935 article features interviews with two popular Korean singers, Kim Yong-hwan and Yi Nan-yŏng, conducted by Yŏsan. Kim Yong-hwan, a 26-year-old singer, composer, and music teacher from Wŏnsan, discusses his musical training, influences (Western classical music, Chŏng To-in), brief theater career, entry into the recording industry (Polydor), successful songs like "Tuman River Boatman," and his disciplined lifestyle and views on ideal partners. Yi Nan-yŏng, a 19-year-old singer from Mokp'o known for "Gypsy's Song" (O.K. Records), recounts her difficult path from poverty, joining a traveling troupe, hardships faced in Osaka (including being cheated by a record company and her father's death), and her eventual success. She shares her criteria for an ideal husband and her current reading interests.

From a historical perspective, the article offers insights into the burgeoning popular music scene in colonial Korea, the blend of traditional and Western influences (classical music, violin, records), and the aspirations of young artists. It reveals the economic hardships faced by performers, exploitation by companies, the role of Japanese cities like Osaka in Korean entertainment circuits, and the cultural consumption patterns (reading materials including Japanese magazines like *Shufu no Tomo* and *King*). The singers' discussions about ideal partners reflect contemporary social values and aspirations for stability and understanding amidst the challenging colonial environment.

기사제목	人氣歌手의 藝術·私生活·戀愛

기사형태	대담·좌담

人氣歌手의 藝術·私生活·戀愛
						如山
音樂個人敎授하는 金龍煥氏
삼복의 서울은 끌는 가마속 갓다. 40만 인구가 北岳과 남산 사이에 빽빽히 드러업듸어 내뿜는 훈훈한 입김과 뜨거운 햇빗에 웬만한 사람은 氣塞할 것 갓다.
나는 이 서울거리를 뒤에 두고 元山가는 차를 잡어타기는 7월 어느 날 三防幽谷의 凉味며 釋王寺의 절경을 바라보면서 元山에 다은 길로 곳 廣石洞 2번지를 찻다. 인기가수 金龍煥씨의 그 사생활과 예술관을 드르려 함이라. (사진은 金龍煥씨)

如山 金龍煥씨
깨긋하게 차린 씨의 서제 한 모통이에는
「카이랄 전집」
「세계문학전집」
「근대희곡전집」＜121＞
등 명저가 벽을 가리다십히 堆積되어 잇고, 한편에는 「와그나-」와 「베-도펜」 가튼 악성들의 畵額이 걸니어 잇다. 누구든지 그 서재의 책을 보면 그 주인된 이의 지식과 취미를 알 수 잇다 하거니와 이제 金龍煥씨가 예술을 위하야 수련하고 잇는 태도를 넉넉히 알 수 잇겟다.
「개인 교수를 한다니 몃 분이나 가르키고 잇서요」
「네. 아츰에 성악을 가르키고 잇는데 가장 열심스럽고 천재적인 젊은 분 일곱 사람이 매일가치 차저줌니다. 그래서 발성법이나 泰西 명곡의 복창도 식히고 잇서요. 그리고 또 오후에는 빠요링을 가르키고 잇는데 현재 열 사람이 다니고 잇슴니다. 모다 열심스러운 분들얘요」
「그러케 다른 사람을 위하여 시간의 대부분을 허비하고 어느 결에 레코-드 수련을 함니까」
「그러기에 내 시간이라고는 아츰 조반전 박게 업서요. 아츰이면 여섯시쯤 이러나서 운동을 좀 하고는 곳 음악연습을 한답니다」
「노래 부를 때에 계란이나 무엇이나 자시면 목소리가 잘 나온다고 엇든 분은 그리하는 분이 잇다든데요」
「글세요. 저는 빠나나와 도마도를 조와하기 그를 항상 먹는데 그러한 신선한 과일을 먹으면 몸에도 조흘뿐더러 더구나 음악하는 사람으로서 생각할 때에 확실히 성대를 부드럽게 맑게 하는 도음이 되는 것 갓더군요.」
「그런데 누구에게서 음악을 배웟서요」
「음악배운 동긔는 이러함니다. 열두 살에 元山培誠學校를 마치고 이어 保光中學校를 마추엇는데 학생때부터 늘 취미가 그 방면에 쓸니어집데다. 여덜살 때 학예회에 가서 음악 잘한다고 칭찬드럿고 중학생 때에는 학교악대에서 라팔선수, 빠요링선수로 잇섯지요. 그런 뒤 정작 좀 음악이라고 배워보기는 지금은 松都高等普通學校 교수로 잇는 鄭土仁 선생이엇지요. 그분이 예전 李王職 아악부지휘관으로 잇슬 때 개인교수을 바덧지요」
하고 순순히 이약이하는 스물여섯나는 이 젊은 예술가는 몸가짐이 몹시 단정할뿐더러 말씨도 수려한 용모을 반영하듯 조곰도 과장이 업시 나즉나즉하게 이약이하여 준다. 기자의 첫 인상에 퍽 얌전한 분이구나 하는 생각이 든다.
「드른즉 연극에 投足하섯더라구요」
「네. 좀 햇지요. 지금 태평레코-드 문예부장으로 게신 朴英鎬씨에게서 연극에 대하야 좀 배웟습니다. 그리고 나서 元山舘에서 악장으로 잇다가 독일인 나위엘씨에게서 빠요링을 배웟는데 劇界에 投足한 것이 바로 그때이지요. 「太陽街」를 처음 연출하엿는데 크게 성공햇지요. 여기에 발을 드리어 申不出씨의 「아리랑 反對篇」을 가지고 떠낫다가 함흥가서 일이 모다 깨이지고 말엇서요. 그래서 긔운이 업시 집에 도라와 잇는데 지금은 저세상에 간 李景雪씨가 날더러 노래를 가라처달나고 하겟지요. 景雪씨 소개로 王平씨를 알게 되어, 결국 포리돌-에＜122＞ 입사한 것임니다.」
「오늘까지 취입한 레코-드중 제일 자신잇게 생각하는 것이 무에임니까」
「국경의 밤」이올시다. 내 작품으로서 이만치 환영밧은 것이 업는데 중도에 그만 부득이한 사정으로 발매되지 안코 잇습니다.」
「지금까지 몃 판이나 취입하섯서요. 또 작곡도 하신다니 몃 편이나 하섯서요.」
「글세요, 취입한 것을 모다 헨다면 350여판에 달하고 작곡한 것만 해도 오백편은 넘을걸요」
「퍽 만히 하섯구만. 발매 수량으로 보아 제일 환영밧은 것은요?」
「그러케 악평을 밧은 것은 업섯지만, 잘 팔닌 것은 두만강 뱃사공, 조선행진곡, 그리고 내가 작곡하고 李景雪씨가, 취입한 「세기말의 노래」가 성공하엇슴니다」
「말슴하기 거북하겟지만 지금 류행 가수로 누가 가장 훌융한 분으로 보십니까」
「모다 一長一能이 잇스니까 무에라고 외람되게 말슴할 수 업슴니다만은 내 생각 갓해서는 남성가수 중으로는 姜弘植씨가 가장 무게잇고 우렁찬 男壯的 노래를 불너줘요. 압흐로도 더욱더욱 발전될 성량을 가진 분이라고 탄복하고 십슴니다. 그리고 여성으로는 누구누구하여도 조선맛이 나고 부드럽고 끈는 듯, 잇는 듯, 硏硏嫋嫋함이 鮮于一扇양이 당대 一인줄 아옵니다. 이 두 분의 목소리는 우리는 어듸에라도 내어노코 자랑하고 십슴니다」
「잘 알겟슴니다. 龍煥씨 교우의 범위는 엇더함니까」
