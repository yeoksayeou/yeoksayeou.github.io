기사제목	佳人獨宿空房記 (A Record of a Beautiful Woman Sleeping Alone in an Empty Room)
기사형태	대담·좌담 (Interview/Discussion)

A Record of a Beautiful Woman Sleeping Alone in an Empty Room
Ms. Pak Indŏk, Who Doesn't Know Loneliness and Sorrow and Chops Firewood
"Can a young woman live alone?"
But this is a foolish question. Currently, there are many young women who, though appearing to others as lonely as "a solitary wild goose in the sky," spend their days singing, dancing, and living life most joyfully and pleasantly. Necessarily<82>
If asked, "What is 'youth'?"
It should not be
"Youth is the lament of a finite being heading towards the infinite."
But rather, perhaps,
"It is the beautiful song of an infinite being heading towards the finite."
Alighting from the streetcar at the Saemunbat stop in Seoul and heading towards Sajik Park, after walking for a while through a neighborhood with modern houses (문화주택), one finds an elegant house nestled amidst a flower garden (화초밧) that could truly be called a riot of color (千紫萬紅). This is the solitary companion, the "Sweet Home," of Ms. Pak Indŏk, the foremost woman of her time, who returned from America years ago, is a skilled orator, and about whom various rumors concerning divorce issues circulated. Perhaps calling it just "Sweet Home" without adding 'solitary companion' (짝, 片) would be rude?
"Are you planning to live completely single?"
"Yes, indeed. I've firmly decided in my heart to live completely single from now on. Being single is good; the principle of single life is the best."
She stops watering the flower garden, wipes the sweat trickling down her forehead with the ribbon of her crimson silk jacket (주항라적삼), and laughs cheerfully.
"Isn't it lonely?"
"What?"
"Single life."
"No-, look at these flowers. My garden is full of these beautiful flowers (꼿), and spending the morning gazing at them always keeps me refreshed, laughing to myself. I don't know what loneliness in life is. If I feel lonely, I play the piano. Or I pluck my favorite Hawaiian guitar, on a moonlit night (月明夜)-."
"You deliberately try to deceive 'loneliness,' don't you? The 'loneliness' that springs up from the heart of a person, especially a young person, surely cannot be extinguished merely by a piano or a rose (장미꼿). A piano may please the ear for a moment, and a rose may dazzle the eye for a moment, but don't you feel the 'loneliness of the soul,' which is beyond such sensory matters? It is said that since ancient times (녜로부터), one can only escape that loneliness through the opposite sex (異性)."
"Your attack is fierce indeed. But regarding that, this is what I think."
And she pours out her first-rate philosophy of single life like flowing water (청산유수식).
"Generally, loneliness flows from having something lacking. For instance, suppose one is lacking in health and always lying sick. Then one would yearn for a gentle hand to visit and comfort that lonely sickbed. But look here. I've never experienced being sick.<83> I hike in the morning, skate in winter, and swim in summer—perhaps because of that, my body is always healthy. Therefore, I cannot understand the psychology of a 'sick woman waiting for a lover'."
"I see, then the second reason is-"
I lit a cigarette.
"Secondly, if one lacks money, one might also feel lonely. One might think, 'I must find a good husband quickly so I don't starve my whole life,' and rush into marriage. But look here, I was born into a poor family and experienced working my way through school, so I am confident in my ability to feed and clothe myself. I can do any kind of work. And earn money. I still have the courage I had when I was a seven-year-old melon seller (참외장수) in Chinanp'o (鎭南浦). Even if, let's say, I became unemployed now. I wouldn't have the slightest thought of entering someone's house as a silkworm raiser (잠재기) or a kitchen maid (행남어멈); instead, I would go out to the Chongno intersection (종로 네거리) and pull a rickshaw (人力車). My personality is inherently masculine, so being a rickshaw puller, running here and there, suits my temperament better than fiddling with needlework."
"I see, that makes sense. Then the third reason?"
I chimed in, looking up at a crimson flower shaped like a rooster's comb in the flower garden, which Ms. Kŭm Mirisa (金美理士) had supposedly brought specifically.
"Next is the most fundamental issue, the person's character. Romantics (로-맨티시트) who like fantasies or the sentimentalist (센티멘탈이스트) types, perhaps called overly affectionate and sensitive (多情多限式), whose emotions of joy, anger, sorrow, and pleasure (喜怒哀樂) change frequently like summer clouds, might cry looking at flowers and sigh looking at clouds. But whether it's my mother's heredity, I was born with the character of an androgynous person (非男非女), and I absolutely detest (大忌) fantasy and sentimentality. I am a realist and a pragmatist. Action comes first before thought. I dislike mulling over things. I just laugh loudly and talk loudly under the blue sky and white sun (靑天白日), working actively and cheerfully."
"Therefore?"
"I can live quite sufficiently alone!"
"Hahaha. It must be the influence of American education, James's pragmatism (푸락마티즘)-."
"Well, there might be American influence, but my personality has been like a boy's since I was young. That personality must have grown and matured."
Hearing the lady's words, I had no choice but to give "unconditional approval," as every word and phrase sounded plausible-.
"So, how do you spend your days?"<84>
"I eat, drink water..."
"You intend to continue the explanation with washing your face and getting dressed, I suppose. Besides those things."
"Well, I chop firewood (장잭이)."
"A woman chopping firewood?"
"Yes, indeed. We have an axe (독끼) at home. Holding this thing, putting strength into my whole body, shouting 'Shhi-sha-', I chop firewood every day. How delightful it is."
"Fierce, indeed. Is there perhaps one more unique 'talent you display' of that kind?"
"Why wouldn't there be? Plenty! When I return from the office, I take a hammer (마치) and fix the fallen door of the outhouse (뒤깐), nailing it together, and I repair fallen planks (널판장) all by myself. How good I am at carpentry (목수 노릇)-."
"What about hiking?"
"I don't do it in the morning. I tried walking to Paegundae or Sajik Park (社稷공원) for a while, but I dislike meeting men in the quiet woods."
"Is it scary?"
"No, not scary, just dislike it. Also, people seem to think it's strange for a woman to walk alone, so I stopped. When I really want to go, I take a brisk walk early in the dawn when there are no other strollers."
I lit another cigarette, contemplated the issue of single life, and parted ways with the lady.

Miss Kim Maria Standing Beneath a Portrait of the Virgin Mary
The fragrant sea breeze, heavily laden with the scent of rugosa roses (해당화), brushes idly against my face as I open the train window.
Is that place over there, beyond the clouds where two or three white gulls (白鷗) appear and disappear, already Myŏngsa Simni (明沙십리)?
Two young Western female students, who boarded with me from Seoul and said they were from Shanghai (上海), gazed at the cloud-covered Kalma Peninsula (葛麻半島), then abruptly threw away their cigarettes, picked up their baskets (빠스겟), and skipped off like butterflies.
Perhaps, having started their vacation early, they gathered here at this villa village (別莊村) across the distant Yellow Sea (황해바다) to spend the whole summer running, rolling, singing, and swimming on the golden sand, indulging in all sorts of antics (온갓 가닥질).
All these "Katyushas" (갓쥬샤) will weave all sorts of "romances" (로-만스) here this summer,<85> only to scatter one by one like disappearing morning stars when the autumn wind blows. As I too am a wanderer who has lost my hometown, I naturally became immersed in this romantic and sentimental atmosphere and wanted to follow those female students, but my journey ahead was urgent, so I proceeded directly past Wŏnsan Station (元山驛) and set foot in the two-story Western-style building of the Women's Bible Institute (女子聖經學院) at the foot of Sirubong peak (시루봉밋).
Here resides the famous "Kim Maria" (김마리아씨), who, like a "canary" that forgot its song after returning from America, never utters a word about society or the nation, but has quietly hidden herself within the Dharma Gate (法門), neither crying nor singing (不鳴不泣). My purpose is to inquire from her crimson lips (朱唇) why she remains single as an old maid.

A Clean Bedroom
Ms. Kim Maria was in the dormitory of the Bible institute. Sirubong is a mountain ridge shaped like an hourglass drum (장구통) that wraps around Wŏnsan city. To reach this dormitory, one must pass the flank of Sirubong, embrace a 500-year-old zelkova tree, and enter deep into a pine forest where the bell of Hwanghak Pavilion (黃鶴樓) might ring. Following this narrow mountain path (山狹길) for a while, passing under three or four neatly lined pine trees, stands the lilac (라이락 「四ツ葉」꼿), said to have let General Napoleon (나포레옹 장군) taste the joy of victory, almost blocking the path. Below that path, a small, white, cement-paved new road (신작로) was laid out, and on both sides, so many kinds of flowers bloomed that with every step, flower petals scattered like snowflakes (六花날니듯) brushed by my feet.
Neat and stylish female students came and went through the garden. A little later,
I finally sat facing Miss Kim Maria (金馬利亞孃) in the reception room. Even if I asked her age, she wouldn't answer; thinking about it, she seemed past 30 but not yet 40. In her slightly wrinkled face, the fierce expression (慓悍한 표정) from her past involvement in political movements was nowhere to be found; instead, the gentle expression of an educator, like a sheep (羊), flowed.
Sitting opposite the young lady (孃), I sniffed quietly like a hound (산양개) a couple of times to see if the body odor often associated with "old misses" (올드, 미쓰) would hit my nose, but perhaps dissipated by the lilac scent, I couldn't sense any particular smell. However, looking through the glass window into her bedroom right next door, I saw the white bed, the freshly washed and ironed pajamas (잠옷) draped over it, the long stockings (스탁킹) hanging there, the portrait of the Virgin Mary (童貞女마리아) hanging on the wall, and several other religious paintings. I got the feeling that the sentiment (情趣) and fragrance (香臭) of this woman's single life flowed solely within that room.<86>

Is Being Single Lonely?
Me: "People like Schopenhauer (쇼-펜,하웰) were men, so they hated women and lived single their whole lives. Nietzsche (니-체) and Dante (단떼) also experienced heartbreak and world-weariness (厭世), growing old alone. But why have you, Teacher (선생), never married and lived single until now? Do you have some profound philosophy of single life?"
I tossed out a word as if annoyed. People often confess their true feelings honestly when they are angry, so to make the other person angry, one must feign anger first.
Kim: I have been single up to now. But I say this: even if I were to grow old as a virgin (숫처녀) my whole life, I will not boast to others, "I will grow old as a maiden my whole life," or "I will never marry as long as I live."
Me: Why is that, again?
Kim: Well, I see it this way. It's good for a woman to marry after being born, and it's also good to grow old and die alone without marrying. I don't think that "a married life" is good and "an unmarried life" is unhappy. Therefore, living a single life like me is good, and not living a single life is also good.
Me: It sounds like Lao Tzu's (老子) "drifting clouds and flowing water" (行雲流水) tune, I don't quite understand.
Kim: Why can't you understand? Who decides to live a single life just because they want to? Depending on circumstances and environment, one person marries and has children (有子生女), while another doesn't marry and grows old alone. Also, if you think carefully, why must life necessarily be married life (何必曰 부부생활)? Married life is fine, and not having it is also fine, isn't it? In the Bible (聖經), doesn't the Apostle Paul say in 1 Corinthians, that living a single life is good and not doing so is bad?
Me: Then do you intend to live a lonely life like Anna in the Bible?
Kim: Hohoho, I'm not saying I specifically want to live a lonely life. If my heart is moved, I might enter married life even tomorrow.
Repeating the "same mouse, same tail" (그 쥐 그 꼬리 그 쥐 그 꼬리) style of flowing water Q&A (行雲流水式問答) seemed unlikely to elicit the young lady's unique philosophy of single life, so I subtly changed the format and asked.
Me: Do you ever feel lonely when you are alone like this?
Kim: Loneliness is unavoidable in human life. I surely have lonely moments too, but perhaps because I'm used to it, I don't particularly feel the loneliness and sorrow (비애) that come from being a single person. Therefore, I believe that loneliness (孤寂), sorrow (悲哀), and melancholy (우울) do not depend on whether one is married or not.<87> Also, loneliness is something felt by people who have leisure time.
Me: Are you that busy?
Kim: I am busy. From morning till evening, I am always busy with this and that, so I don't feel like I myself am suffering from any physical distress. If I were that terribly lonely, how could I have lived single for so long like this?
Me: Then do you also advise your students, "You should live single. Single life is free from ten thousand worries and brings peace under heaven (천하태평)"?
Kim: No. I exhort them until my throat is sore, "Become a wise mother and good wife (賢母良妻), become one quickly." But I add, "If you judge in your heart that living alone is good, then live a single life."
Me: Then have you ever told students to "be like me"?
Kim: Never. What good would it do to be like me? One more disabled person (不具者).
Me: Are you disabled because you didn't marry-- hahaha--.

Are You Planning to Marry in the Future?
Me: How do you view the women's liberation movement (녀성해방운동), Teacher?
Kim: Who tied women up? Just as men must go outside (밧갓) and do men's work, women must uphold their duties (본분), manage the household well, and focus their minds on educating their sons and daughters. If there is time left after that, then it would be good to engage in social work or national affairs. I know that liberation doesn't mean abandoning a woman's natural duty (천분). How can one handle larger societal or national matters if one cannot even manage one's own affairs? Some people seem to misunderstand liberation, thinking it means men cooking and sewing in the kitchen while women go out (밧게) socializing and working. Equality doesn't come this way, nor does freedom. Therefore, I just cry out (웨침니다) for women to do women's work first.
Me: Yes, I understand. But how long do you intend to remain buried in such a remote place, so far from Seoul?
Kim: Well, I don't know. For the time being, I will quietly focus on education here where the climate and environment (水土) are good (조흔).
Me: On this occasion, I'd like to ask how you studied when you were in America.
Kim: When I was in America, I studied extremely hard while working (고학). Others received help with tuition, but since I was a fugitive (쪼겨간 사람), people thought I couldn't go back, and perhaps seeing me as someone without hope (히망업는 사람), no one helped me.
Me: Didn't you face much hardship upon your return?<88>
Kim: I thought the statute of limitations (時効) had passed, so I came out feeling relieved, but when I disembarked the ship in Kobe (神戶), various things happened.

The Lady's Philosophy of Single Life
Me: How do you usually spend your days nowadays (요지간)?
Kim: I always wake up at 5:30 AM. Then I quietly pray (긔도) to God, read the Bible (聖經) for an hour, wash up, clean the house thoroughly, attend morning worship (아츰레배), finish breakfast (조반) at seven, go to school at 8:30 to teach English and Bible, finish school at 3:30 PM, and return here to the dormitory.
Me: What about your afternoon life?
Kim: Although I'm not the housemother (舍監), I still supervise the students, and we discuss students' difficult problems or all matters of personal life, so I don't get much studying done. After the students are all asleep (취침), I study some English literature and then go to bed around one or two in the morning."
Thus, I ultimately had to retreat without fully hearing the unique "Philosophy of a Woman's Single Life" from the lips of this gentle female educator. Looking out from Sirubong on my way out, the view of the blue sea of Wŏnsan Harbor (元山海) was truly indescribably beautiful (조왓다).

Ms. Kim Illyŏp, Forgetting Youth Through Zen Meditation (叅禪) in the Dharma Hall (法堂)
Just as many women run to Hokkaido (北海道) convents chanting "Ave Maria," many also run to deep mountain temples (深山절깐) chanting "Namu Amitabul" (나미아비타-불). Both are undoubtedly women adhering to the principle of single life. However, it is said that women who run to Amitabha temples (彌陀寺) or knock on convent doors are often those who have suffered heartbreak (실연). What might have been the state of mind (心境) of Ms. Kim Illyŏp (金一葉), whom we are about to discuss?
With such fleeting idle thoughts (空想), I entered the gate of the Women's Zen Academy (女子禪學院) in Anguk-tong (安國洞). In various corners of the large Dharma Hall enshrining Buddha, young seekers (求道者) sat meditating, their bodies held rigidly straight like candlesticks (촛대). Ms. Illyŏp was not there. Wondering if she was practicing asceticism (修道) in another lecture hall (講室), I looked around when a Buddhist nun (女僧) in men's clothing (男服), with 108 prayer beads (百八念珠) around her neck and a kasaya (袈裟) draped diagonally over her upper body, quietly greeted me with palms together (合掌迎接). This was Ms. Illyŏp.
"Don't you want to go back out into the world (人世)?"
"It's good here (예가 조와요). If you don't know the Dharma joy (法悅) of an ascetic practitioner,<89> you might say that--"
"Then will you live single in this solitary temple forever in the future?"
"Yes, I have already decided to do so."
"Isn't being single lonely?"
Alas (앗불사), I realized I had asked another foolish question (愚問). Then, a definite reaction appeared on the lady's calm facial muscles.
"It used to be lonely before (녜전에는), but now it is not lonely. Because the compassionate Buddha is here, and the head monk (스님) is here too-."
"Before means?"
"Before practicing the Buddhist path (佛道)."
At this point, I recalled how the lady's first and second marriages had both ended unhappily, and lamented, wondering if those unhappy marriages had isolated such a talented person (才人) from the present world.
"So, because all your married lives were unhappy, you couldn't bear the loneliness (寂寞) and decided to follow the Buddhist path?"
"Thinking deeply, a burning desire to know the depth of life kept arising. Leaving behind small matters like men and women, love affairs (연애), wealth and status (부귀), my third family life wasn't even that unhappy, yet I suppose I broke it off simply to pursue the Way (도)."
"Can't you practice asceticism if you have a family?"
"Asceticism is austerity (고행). Since I have to make pilgrimages (순례) from temple to temple for 3 or 4 months at a time, I naturally cannot maintain a family."
I could find a solemn beauty, as if she were genuinely speaking the voice of her conscience, in every word and phrase (言言句句).
"Ms. Illyŏp, you are just past 30. There's a saying that life begins at 30, meaning the body truly matures from 30. Isn't there a corresponding sense of loneliness? It's a bit awkward to explain, but things like physiological condition (生理上 켠듸슌)?"
"Single-minded focus (一念) is a formidable thing. If you concentrate all your mind spiritually solely on that single focus, such things are not even worth thinking about, and indeed, they don't occur to the mind."<90>
"Let's say your self-control (自制力) finds peace (安神) through that. Even so, when you see the obvious affectionate married life (愛情濃厚한 부부생활) of contemporary men and women, aren't you shocked (충격밧는 바가 업*니까)?" (*transcriber's note: likely typo for 없습니까)
"No (업슴니다). In that respect, I have already become plain and simple (枯淡), like people of old felt. Besides, all that is temporary. Young people grow old, beautiful things become ugly, existing things disappear--. That's how the world is--."
"How do you spend your days?"
"From dawn until late at night (밤 늣게까지), it begins and ends solely with practice (수행)."
"Do you practice Zen meditation (叅禪)?"
"Yes. At six in the morning, I enter the main Dharma hall (대법당) and practice Zen meditation with a koan (話頭). I meditate three times a day. I wake up at three in the morning (새벽 세시지요)."
Her plain and tasteless (枯淡 無味) philosophy of single life does not particularly capture the interest (感興) of us worldly folk (俗衆). The main point is just the remaining question of whether a dead tree can bloom again (枯木生花) for Ms. Illyŏp.<91>

**Summary**

This 1935 article from "Samch'ŏlli" magazine presents interviews with three prominent Korean women living single lives: Pak Indŏk, Kim Maria, and Kim Illyŏp. Pak Indŏk, known for her education in America and outspokenness, champions single life through self-sufficiency, physical health, and a pragmatic, action-oriented personality, rejecting traditional notions of female dependence and loneliness. Kim Maria, a respected educator living quietly at a Bible institute after returning from the US, presents a more ambivalent view, accepting single life as one valid path alongside marriage, emphasizing busy work and religious faith over personal feelings, and advocating for traditional women's roles first. Kim Illyŏp, a former writer who became a Buddhist nun after unhappy marriages, finds solace and purpose in rigorous Zen practice, viewing worldly attachments like marriage as transient and ultimately insignificant compared to spiritual pursuit. The article explores different motivations and philosophies behind choosing single life, contrasting modern independence, religious devotion, and disillusionment with worldly affairs.

From a historical perspective, the article is fascinating for its portrayal of "New Women" in colonial Korea grappling with modernity, independence, education, and traditional gender roles. It showcases diverse responses to social change: Pak Indŏk's embrace of Western pragmatism and physical self-reliance, Kim Maria's attempt to reconcile Christian faith and education with a modified traditional view of women's duties, and Kim Illyŏp's retreat into Buddhist monasticism as an escape from perceived worldly suffering and societal expectations. The discussions on self-sufficiency (even pulling a rickshaw), the role of education (especially American), and the tension between personal fulfillment and societal/familial duty provide valuable insights into the complex lives and ideologies of educated Korean women during this period. The frank discussion of loneliness, physiological needs, and the reasons for choosing single life offers a rare glimpse into personal female perspectives often absent in official histories.

佳人獨宿空房記
孤獨과 哀傷을 모르고 장잭이 패는 朴仁德氏
「젊은 녀성이 혼자 독신으로 지낼 수 잇슬가?」
그러나 이것은 愚問이다. 현재 묘령 여성으로서 남이 보기에는 「하늘에 뜬 외기럭이」가치 더할 수 업게 외롭게 지내고 잇건만은 저는 날마다 노래부르고 춤추며 인생을 가장 즐겁게 유쾌하게 지내는 이가 만타. 필연＜82＞
「청춘」이란 무엇인고? 하면
「청춘이란 無限을 가는 有限者의 탄식이라」
할 것이 아니고
「有限을 가는 無限者의 아름다운 노래소리」
라고나 할가.
서울 새문밧 정류장에서 전차를 던지고 社稷공원을 향하야, 문화주택 잇는 동리를 한참 가노라면 그야말로 千紫萬紅이라 할 화초밧히 욱어진 속에 아담한 집 한 채가 노여 잇스니 이것이 年前 미국서 도라왓고, 연설 잘 하고, 또 이혼문제로 세상에 여러 가지 소문을 전하든 당대 여류 제일인자 朴仁德 여사의 짝, 『수윗트,홈』이다. 그저 「쓰윗트홈」이라 아니하고 짝(片)이라 부치는 것이 실례일가.
「아조 독신으로 지내시겟서요?」
「그럼요. 나는 인제 아조 독신으로 지내기로 마음에 딱 작정하여 노앗담니다. 독신이 조와요, 독신주의가 제일 조와요」
하고 화초밧헤 물을 주든 손을 멈추고 이마에 흐르는 땀을 주항라적삼 고름으로 씨스며 쾌활하게 웃는다.
「외롭지 안어요」
「무에?」
「독신생활이」
「노-, 이 꼿을 보시오. 이 아름다운 꼿들이 우리집 정원에는 가득차 잇는데 이것을 아츰나절 바라보고 지내노라면 늘 상쾌하여 혼자 우스며 지내게 되어요. 저는 인생에 적막이 잇다는 것을 모르겟서요. 적막하면 피아노 치지요. 내가 조와하는 「하와이안, 끼-타」나 뜻지요, 月明夜에-.」
「일부러 「적막」을 속이려 드시지요. 사람의, 더구나 청춘이 心中으로 소사오르는 「외로움」이란 결단코, 피아노 정도로, 장미꼿 정도로 꺼버릴 수 잇는 것이 아니지요. 피아노도 일시는 귀를 질겁게 해주고 장미꼿도 일시는 눈을 황홀하게 해 줄 수 잇겟지만 그러한 감각 문제보다 「영혼의 적막」을 늣겨지지 안슴니까. 그것은 녜로부터 모다 異性을 통해서만 그 외로움을 버서날 수 잇다는데요.」
「공격이 맹렬하심니다 그려. 그런데 거기 대하여 나는 이러케 생각해요.」
하면서 一流의 독신철학을 청산유수식으로 쏘다 놋는다.
「대체로 적막이란 부족한 것이 잇는데서 흘너오는 것이야요. 가령 건강이 부족하여 늘 눕어 알는다 합시다. 그러하면 쓸쓸한 그 병상을 차저 위로하여 주는 부드러운 손이 잇섯스면 하고 열망할 것이외다. 그런데 이것 보세요. 저는 지금까지 알어본 일이라고 업서요.＜83＞ 아츰이면 등산하고 겨을이면 스케-틩하고 여름이면 수영하고- 그런 까닭인지 늘 몸이 건강하담니다. 그러기에 「애인을 기다리는 病床여성」의 심리를 저는 이해할 수 업서요.」
「올치요 그러면 둘재는-」
하고 나는 담배를 피어 물엇다.
「둘재는 돈이 부족하면 또한 적막을 늣겨지겟지요. 조흔 남편을 어더서 어서 일생에 굶지말고 지내가야 하겟다 하고 불이야불이야 결혼이라도 하게 되겟지요. 그런데 이보세요, 저는 어릴 때 가난한 집안에 태어낫고 학생시대에는 고학한 경험이 잇는지라 제 먹고 제 입을 일에는 자신이 낫담니다. 저는 무슨 일이든지 해요. 해서 돈을 버어러요. 일곱살 때 鎭南浦에서 참외장수하든 용긔는 아직도 잇담니다, 그러타고 가령 지금 내가 실업자가 된다고 합시다. 그러타고 나는 누구 집안 잠재기나 행남어멈으로 드러갈 생각은 터럭끗만치라도 업고, 그때는 종로 네거리에 나와 人力車을 끄을겟서요. 저는 원래 성격이 남성적이 되어서 꼼지럭 꼼지럭 하는 바느질품 팔기보다 이리저리 뛰어다니는 人力車꾼이 성미에 마저요.」
「올치요, 그러켓구먼요, 그러면 셋재는?」
하고 나는 화초밧에 金美理士 여사가 일부러 갓다주더란 무슨 꼿이라든가 닭의 벳 모양으로 된 진홍빗 꼿을 처다보면서 맛장구를 첫다.
「그 다음은 가장 근본 문제인 그 사람의 성격 문제얘요, 공상을 조와하는 로-맨티시트나 多情多限식이라 할는지 喜怒哀樂의 감정이 여름 하늘의 구름짱가치 몹시 자즌 그런 센티멘탈이스트들이면 꼿도 보고 울고 구름도 보고 한숨짓겟지만 저는 저의 어머니의 유전인지요. 非男非女의 중성인의 성격을 가지고 태나서 공상과 감상을 아조 大忌함니다. 현실주의자요 실행주의자지요. 생각보다 실행 제일이지요. 무엇을 곰곰 생각하기를 실혀해요. 그저 靑天白日 아래 크게 웃고 크게 떠들고, 그러면서 활발하게 유쾌하게 일하지요.」
「그러니깐?」
「혼자 넉넉히 살아가지요!」
「하하하. 亞米利加式 교육의 영향이로구만, 제임쓰의 푸락마티즘-.」
「그야 미국의 영향도 잇겟지만 원체 저는 어릴 때부터 성격이 꼭 사내들 모양이 되엇스니까. 그 성격이 더 자라서 커진 것이겟지요.」
여사의 말슴을 듯고 보매 「무조건 승인」할박게 도리가 업다, 言言句句 모다 그럴듯하게 들니거든요-.
「그래 날마다 엇더케 지내세요.」＜84＞
「밥 먹고 물 마시고...」
「세수하고 옷입고 하고 설명을 계속할 작정임니다그려 그런 것 말고.」
「그럼 장잭이 패지요.」
「여자가 장잭이?」
「그럼요. 우리집에 독끼 하나가 잇담니다, 이놈을 들고 전신에 긔운을 주어 쉬-사- 하고 소리치며 날마다 내가 장잭이 패요. 엇더케 유쾌하관대.」
「맹열하군, 그러고 또 더 한가지쯤 독특하게 「발휘하는 그 천재가 업슴니까. 그런 종류로-」
「웨 업서요, 수두룩하지요. 사무실서 도라나와서는 못허고 마치를 들고 뒤깐 문짝이 떠러진 것도 뚝덕뚝덕 박어 만들고 널판장이 떠러진 것도 모다 내 손으로 곳처 노치요. 내가 목수 노릇을 엇더케 잘하기에-.」
「등산은?」
「아츰에는 안해요, 白雲臺나 社稷공원으로 얼마를 단녀도 보앗지만 고요한 수풀 속에서 사내들을 만나는 것이 실혀요.」
「무섭겟지요?」
「아니 무섭지는 아너도 그저 실혀요, 또 녀자 혼자 산보하는 것을 이상하게 생각할 듯 해서 그만두엇서요. 꼭 가고 십흔 때라면 새벽에 아모개도 산보객이 업슬 때에 한박휘- 휘 돌아와요.」
나는 담배불을 다시 한 대 부처들고 독신문제를 생각하면서 여사와 갈나저 나왓다.

童貞女 마리아 肖像 아래 선 金마리아孃
해당화 꼿 내음새를 가득 실은 향그러운 바닷바람이 차창을 여는 내 얼골을 시름업시 만지고 지나간다.
저리 구름박게 白鷗 두 서너 마리 떳다 잠겻다 하는 곳이 벌서 明沙십리이런가.
서울서부터 한 자리에 가치 탓든 上海서 온다는 젊은 서양인 여학생 둘은 구름낀 葛麻半島를 바라보더니 빨든 담배를 휙-집어뿌리며 「빠스겟」을 들고 나비가치 뛰어나린다.
아마 일즉부터 방학을 하고나서 한여름동안 금모래 우에서 뛰고, 딩글고 노래 부르고, 헤염치고 온갓 가닥질을 다하려 멀니 황해바다를 건너 이곳 別莊村으로 모아들임이라,
