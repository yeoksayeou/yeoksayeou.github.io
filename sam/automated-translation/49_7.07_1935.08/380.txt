**Rural Village Sketch**
Wŏlt'an

In the fine east wind rain, the field banks have turned green,
Long days, idle countryside, the mid-morning snack has just ended.
How would even spring rain be on a beautiful woman's head?
Lest the fresh raindrops soak her black hair—
The young wife, frowning, covering her head with a towel, dashes away on her heels,
When she came, she held the blue waves of hardship (파란세파) and briefly smiled.

The cowshed is quiet, two or three calls of the calf,
Waking late from a nap, even in the rain, it's the morning star's call.
Mountain greens and barley rice come out, "Guest, time for lunch," she says.

The front stream's water has risen, the children gather,
The Mandarin fish (쏘가리) caught in the fish trap (금울) have grown fat.
Mix chili paste (고초장) in the pot, let's eat fisherman's stew (천렵국).

Rainfall comes and goes, the smell of manure (두엄) is also savory,
The manure pile in the outhouse (뒤ㅅ간) smells even less.
One realizes the subtle fit of the ways established by the elders of old.＜282＞

**Noon (午)**
Im Hwa

The hill over there, which I watched through the car window,
Has already shed its pink blouse,
And its broad green leaves, showing their white undersides like fish bellies,
Flutter as if saying, "We're quite alive."
Summer must have deepened already.

That's why, when I came to this temple,
The houses where they were just laying the foundation and trimming the timber
Already have pillars standing and roofs covered,
And the worker laying the ridge beam (용마름) wears a straw hat.

At the edge of the remarkably well-grown mustard field (장다리 밧머리),
Dressed smartly in a crisply ironed yellow ramie jacket (황나적삼),
The dragonfly (잠자리), that fellow with a red tail (꽁지), flies majestically.

The unimpressive poplar tree (포푸라 나무) standing at the field's edge
Embraces its shabby shadow folded roundly.＜283＞

The choked whistle of the spinning company (紡績會社) beyond the mountain
Calls the country girls of Sŏul (서울) to the dining hall (食堂).
Even the dragonfly fellows,
Who sometimes drifted like a kite (소리개),
Or dashed and leaped about like a water-skimming swallow,
Now curl their tails, drag their wings,
And settle down as if dozing
In the middle of the field where the mustard stalks lie scattered like white powder (싀로가루).
Truly, the scorching sun (또약볏) these days could melt even stones.

A stuffy (훗군한) wind carries the strong smell of fertilizer,
Touches the treetops, and passes over the rocks (바우으).
A swarm of bees, clinging to the faded purple flower stamens, few remaining,
Bites and releases, releases and bites,
Buzzing their wings, confused (헤갈).
The tiger swallowtail butterfly flits about (아롱대인다), heavily laden with poisonous powder said to blind if entered.

Touching its tail, stroking its head,
That dragonfly fellow doesn't get up again.
Is it dozing, or is it already dead?

The mischievous boys, farmhands' sons (선모슴 작난군 아희들), with spiderweb nets in hand,＜284＞
Taking off their shoes, stepping softly, muffling their footsteps,
One step, two steps, their hands about to reach it—
Oh, look at the malicious eyes of those wretched fellows.

What might happen.........
To those upright and beautiful wings,
That sleek body whose red color shines like oiled?
How can it not know the heart of the chili dragonfly (곳추짱아), like early chili peppers (당초갓흔)?
How long has it been since the cherry blossoms fell? In this mere scorching sun,
Dragonfly, have your eyes, once clear as pumpkin ("호박"), already dimmed?

The deep waves of green foliage surge into and ebb from the fields.
The hill is silent like an adult.
Perhaps the beautiful belly of the tender spring is already gone.
Truly, in this downpour of scorching sun (또약볏의 소내기통),
Have the sturdy wings and thick purslane leaves all wilted helplessly?

From the lazy thicket sleeping its midday nap in the valley,
A young oriole flutters, kicks aside tree leaves,
Tears the net of quiet silence, and flies up to the sky.＜285＞

Oh, how thankful, how truly thankful.
Suddenly waking from this small, troubled dream,
I leaned on the railing, straightened my back, and looked at the back mountain.

Among the woods, a clump of daylilies (원추리), like a full moon over the pass,
Holds its head up gracefully (어음전히),
Listening, is it not, to the lingering echo (여음) of the oriole's song?

Dragging my heavy legs up the mountain slope (산 빗탈),
I saw the waving hands of the plants saying, "Don't dream, come into the midst of the era."
"You are not yet dead,"
I saw the daylily's face smiling affectionately.
I blushed (얼골을 불키고) briefly and lowered my head,
Then raised it again, thinking of the precarious life (겨우살이) of the beautiful butterflies and lush plants.

At that moment, I felt happiness, still alive, welling up (복밧침) in my chest like a wave.
May, July ＜286＞

**Feelings Sent from a Tavern (Old Poem Style)**
Yang Kŭnhwan

In Chang'an (長安), myriad houses, yet no place to stay. Wandering the night road, drenched in dew and rain.
A woman at the tavern, most sympathetic (孀情). Knocking on the door, seeking affection (尋情), what words are these?
The serving woman (酌婦) mistakenly thinks I came for drinks (飮酒). My beloved, knowing my sentiment (情緖),
Smiles faintly (笑微), asks about evening meal (夕飯). Offers a hand, urges wine, watches with compassion (恤然).
Holding that warm wine, tears fall on their own. Swallowing saliva (呑涎), watching silently (黙見), my reply is hesitant (憷).
Alas (鳴呼)! Do not say heaven and earth are vast. For this five-foot body (五尺一身), there is no place.
Do not think humanity numbers in the hundreds of millions. My one heart relies (賴依) on a single woman (唯一女).
Philosophy, do not teach that gold (黃金) is heavy. How (何由) can humans become drunk and captured (醉此擄) by this?
The meaning of life is indeed hard to know (固難知). But I grieve that worldly affairs (世事) obstruct intentions (相阻).＜287＞
Kings, lords, generals, ministers (王候將相)—who are they all? Heroes and great men (英雄豪傑) die and return to earth (死歸土).
What thing in the universe has a permanent form (常住形)? Moment by moment (時時刻刻), all changes and passes away (盡變去).
Who determines fate and imposes (施) it upon us (吾人)? However one strives, success and failure are established (成敗竪).
Riches and poverty, honor and lowliness (富貴貧賤) are like a water wheel (水車). Fortune and misfortune (吉凶) are perceived (窺達) according to each one's destiny (各身數).
The wren (鷦鷯) nesting in the forest needs but one branch. The tapir (堰獵) drinking from the river fills only its belly.
Life and death, suffering and joy are viewed dispassionately (度外視). My lifelong regret is having no companion of shared will (志侶).
Love, right and wrong (是非), joy, anger, sorrow (喜怒情)—all pushed aside with one cup (一杯), forgetting the world's hardships.
The rosy face (紅顔) about to leave, reddened with wine. White hair (白髮) approaching, rejected with song.
How can sorrow and grief (悲愁) invade before the power of wine (酒氣)? Its fragrance (香聞) is smelled, and they flee ten thousand li (萬里).
Exchanging cups (交杯) several times, becoming fully drunk (滿醉). In one grasp, the world (天下) can be shattered with excitement.
I implore you, sir, if you have sorrow (有愁), do not cease the cup (不斷杯). Do not wish to not know this pillar of spirit (氣柱).＜288＞

**Indian Tagore Poem Collection**
Translated by Ansŏ

1.
The night is dark, and in my quiet existence, your sleep was deep.
Painful love, wake up, I stand outside the door, not knowing how to open it.
Time waits, the stars open their eyes, the wind is silent. In this quietness, my heart is heavy.
Love, wake up, wake and fill my empty cup to the brim (체와놋코), and shake this night with the breath of song.

2.
My night was spent in a lonely (설은) bed. Thus my eyes are tired. My heavy heart is not yet ready to greet the morning with leaping joy.
Cover this naked (발가숭이) light. Gather the dazzling brilliance (광휘) and dancing life onto one leaf (녑에다 치어 노하).
Wrap this body many times in your soft, dark (새캄한) upper garment. And for a while, let me escape my suffering in the joys (열락) of this world (피케해 주소서).

3.
Woman, as you once brought it to my house, bring beauty carefully to my abandoned life.
Sweep away all the dust-covered fragments of time and fill this empty jar (동의을). And set right everything scattered.
Then open the inner door of the sanctuary (聖壇) and light the lamp. And let us meet quietly before God (압헤서).＜289＞

**Summary**

This collection features four distinct poetic works from the August 1935 issue of *Samch'ŏlli*. Wŏlt'an's "Rural Village Sketch" offers idyllic vignettes of countryside life. Im Hwa's "Noon" contrasts the encroaching modernity (cars, spinning factories) with intense natural imagery under a scorching summer sun, reflecting on life, decay, and resilience. Yang Kŭnhwan's "Feelings Sent from a Tavern" adopts a classical Chinese style (Koshich'e) to express existential angst, the fleeting nature of life and fortune, and solace found in wine and companionship. Finally, Ansŏ provides translations of poems by the Indian poet Rabindranath Tagore, focusing on themes of love, spiritual longing, weariness, and seeking refuge. For historians, this collection showcases the diverse literary landscape of colonial Korea: the persistence of traditional pastoral themes alongside modernist explorations of urban change and alienation (Im Hwa), the use of classical forms for contemporary feeling (Yang Kŭnhwan), and the significant engagement with international literature like Tagore's work (Ansŏ), reflecting attempts to navigate cultural identity and artistic expression under Japanese rule.

文藝-시
雨村點描
月灘

東風 간은비에 밭뚝이 푸르럿소,
해 길다 일없은 田園, 졈시참이 겨우것다.
고운이 머리엔들 봄비 좀 엇더하리
행여나 산뜻한 빗방울 검은 머리 젹실세라-
수건써 찡그리며 뒤발으로 닷든 새댁
오실 때 파란세파 담뿍안고 잠간웃데.

외양깐 한가하다 영각소리 두세마듸
낮잠을 느직깨니 雨中에도 계명성일세
산나물 보리밥 나오며 손님, 졈심요하더라.

압개울 물이불다 아해들 몰려가네
금울에 걸닌 尺魚 쏘가리가 살졋고야
고초장 솥에 풀어라 천렵국 먹으리.

비ㅅ발이 오락가락 두엄내도 구수하다,
뒤ㅅ간에 재두엄이 냄새더욱 않이나네
옛어른 낏이신 규모 묘한 맞을 알괘라.＜282＞

낫(午)
林和

내가 自動車에 실녀 유리창으로 내다보든 저건너 동산도
벌서 분홍빗 저고리를 버서던지고
넓다란 푸른 닙파리가 물고기처럼 흰 뱃바듸를 보히면서
제법 살앗소하는 듯이 너울거린다.
어늬새 여름도 짓헛는가 보다.

그러기에 내가 이 절에 올때엔
겨우 터를 닥고 材木을 깍든 집들이
발서 기둥이 스고 집웅이 덥히어
영을 깔고 용마름을 펴는 일꾼이 밀집모자를 썻지

두드러지게 잘된 장다리 밧머리를
고옵게 다린 황나적삼을 떨처 입고
꽁지가 빠알간 잠자리란 놈이 으젓이 날고 잇다

밧머리에 서잇는 승거운 포푸라 나무가
헙수룩한 제 그림자를 동그란히 접어 안고＜283＞

山넘어 紡績會社의 묵메인 고동이
서울은 村애기들을 食堂으로 부를 때
아주 소리개 모양으로 떠돌아도 보고
물을 차는 제비나 된 듯 내달으며 넘노라도 보든
짬자리녀석들도 꼬리를 오구리고 쭉지를 끄을며
장다리가 싀로가루 쓰러저 잇는 밧가운데로
조을닌 듯 내려 안는다
정말 요세 또약볏이란 돌도 녹일가보다.

훗군한 바람이 진한 거름내를 풍기며
나무끗을 건드리고 바우으를 지내간다.
벌떼가 몃개 안남은 무색한 보라빗 꼿수염을
물엇다놋코 노앗다물며
왕 왕 날개를 울니면서 헤갈을 한다.

酒家寄情(古詩體)
梁槿煥

長安萬戶無宿所. 彷徨夜路蒙露雨.
酒家有女最孀„情. 叩門尋情是何語.
酌婦誤認飮酒來. 愛人旣知有情緖.
笑微逐問夕飯何. 引手勸酒恤然賭.
握彼熱酒淚自垂. 呑涎黙見所答憷.
鳴呼莫言天地廣. 五尺一身無容處.
人類莫思數億多. 一心賴依唯一女.
哲學莫敎黃金重. 人間何由醉此擄.
人生意義固難知. 但悲世事意相阻.＜287＞
王候將相都何人. 英雄豪傑死歸土.
宇宙何物常住形. 時時刻刻盡變去.
誰定運命施吾人. 努力如何成敗竪.
富貴貧賤如水車. 吉凶窺達各身數.
鷦鷯林巢不過枝. 堰獵飮河唯滿肚.
生死苦樂度外視. 平生唯恨無志侶.
愛情是非喜怒情. 都推一杯忘世苦.
紅顔欲去以酒紅. 白髮將來以歌拒.
悲愁何侵酒氣前. 香聞逃去萬里跙.
交杯數疊滿醉成. 一握天下能碎興.
請君有愁不斷杯. 不欲不知此氣柱.＜288＞

印度 타고아 詩篇
岸曙 譯

1.
밤은 어둡고 나의 고요한 존재속에 그대에 잠은 깁헛읍니다.
괴로운 사랑이어, 잠을 깨라, 나는 문 열길도 몰으고 문 밧게 섯읍니다.
세월을 기달이고 별들은 눈을 뜨고 바람은 잠잠합니다. 이리 고요한속에 나의 맘은 무겁습니다.
사랑이어, 잠을 깨라, 잠을 깨서 나의 비인 잔을 가득이 체와놋코 노래의 숨길로 이 밤을 흔들어 주소서.

2.
나의 밤은 설은 잠자리속에서 다하엿읍니다. 이리하야 나의 눈은 고단합니다. 무겁은 나의 맘을, 뛰노는 깃븜으로 아츰을 마지할 차비를 아직 하지 못하엿읍니다.
발가숭이 이 광명을 덥허 주소서. 눈부신 광휘와 춤추는 생명을 한 녑에다 치어 노하 주소서.
부드라운 그대의 새캄한 웃옷으로 이 몸을 여러겹 감싸주소서. 그러고 이 세상의 열락에서 한동안 나의 괴롭음을 피케해 주소서.

3.
여성이어, 그대가 옛날 내 집으로 가저오듯이, 나의 버리운 생명에다 美를 고이고이 가저다 주소서.
띄끌 무든 세월의 조각들을 모다 쓸어 바리고 비인 이 동의을 채와주소서. 그러고 흐트러진 모든 것을 바로 잡아주소서.
그런뒤에 聖壇의 안 문을 열고 등잔에 불을 밝혀 주소서. 그러고 우리들을 하느님의 압헤서 고요이 만나게 하여주소서.＜289＞
