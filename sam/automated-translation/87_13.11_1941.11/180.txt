我が愛讀書 (My Favorite Books)
In these critical times, what are cultural figures reading and contemplating? Here, we inquired about the recent favorite books of over seventy members of the Cultural Department of the People's Total Mobilization Korean League (民總力朝鮮聯盟文化部), starting with Cultural Department Head Mr. Yanabe Eizaburō.

Lotus Sutra (法華經)
Kojiki (古事記)

□
Kuroki Gōichi (Navy Captain, Keijō Naval Attaché Office)
I read many books daily related to the current situation, spiritual matters, technical fields, etc., so there is no particular book I would call a favorite.

□
Kurashima Itaru (Government-General of Korea Educational Affairs Section Chief)
○ *Shina no Hitobito* (支那の人々 - The People of China) by Shimizu Yasuzō (淸水安三)
This book helps one understand well what kind of people the Chinese are. Rather than a favorite book, it is one of the books I read, was impressed by, and would like to recommend.

Karashima Takeshi (Keijō Imperial University Faculty of Law and Letters Professor)
Honestly, I do not have the leisure time to calmly peruse a single book slowly and be deeply moved. It is about reading necessary various books and magazines at top speed, folding them into my head, and organizing them. For that, I spare even moments of free time. However, I do pick up poems by Meiji Restoration pioneers or short pieces by Chinese literati as an aid to self-cultivation. This is my reply.

Honda Hideo (Colonial Bank Research Department Head)

However, the favorite books I read at least a little every morning without fail are one of several books related to Zen that I have on hand.

**Summary**

This 1941 survey from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine asks prominent Japanese and Korean cultural figures, officials, and professionals residing in colonial Korea about their favorite books during the "critical times" of war. The responses reveal a wide range of reading habits: some focus on Japanese nationalist texts (*Shinmin no Michi*, *Kokutai no Hongi*, *Nihon Shoki*), historical works (Japanese, Chinese, European), professional literature (medicine, economics, arts), and philosophical/religious texts (Zen, Sutras, Bible, Confucian classics). Others mention contemporary works related to the war effort or global affairs, while some, like Yu Chin-o and Yi Nŭng-hwa, express difficulty concentrating on reading or explicitly state they haven't been reading much. For historians, this article offers valuable insights into the intellectual climate of late colonial Korea, showing the pressures of wartime mobilization and assimilation policies reflected in reading choices (heavy emphasis on Japanese imperial ideology and history), alongside continued engagement with diverse fields and classics. The mix of Japanese and Korean respondents and their affiliations provides a snapshot of the colonial elite structure and their varied responses to the heightened political tension.
