The Great Lion's Roar of Wartime Patriots!!, This is Tōkyō, Ōsaka
						By Sin T'aeyŏk

Today, the moderator asked me to speak to you all about my trip to Tōkyō and Ōsaka, which is why I stand here today. Koreans in Tōkyō and Ōsaka are not people I should speak of as separate; they are all related to us, and they only have significance as a part of the entire Korean populace. If we speak of the entire Korean populace, we can broadly divide them into the contingent overseas and the contingent within the country. Even within the country, we can divide them into Koreans in the Mainland [Japan] and Koreans within Korea. Regarding Koreans overseas, especially those residing in Manchukuo, since the founding of Manchukuo, they have not only played an important role in the "Five Races Under One Union Movement" (五族協和運動) through the Concordia Association (協和會), but the Imperial Subjectification Movement (皇國臣民化運動) is also proceeding with extraordinary enthusiasm. Furthermore, Manchukuo, due to its geographical relationship with the Soviet Union, is entirely fulfilling a frontline role. As for our activities within Korea, I believe you all know them better, so I will not elaborate further, but in a word, it seems there are many shortcomings in all aspects. So, what is the situation of Koreans residing in the Mainland? This needs to be discussed separately, divided into the past, present, and future.

The Past Relationship between the Mainland and Korea
Even speaking of the past, the situations of ancient, medieval, and recent antiquity differ slightly, but generally speaking, it is a fact that the exchange of people (blood) between the Mainland and Korea has flourished since ancient times.<30> To give an example, the tomb of Wani Hakase (王仁博士), said to be the progenitor of Japanese culture, is located in Ōsaka Prefecture. It is clear that this elder went from Korea bringing the Thousand Character Classic and the Analects. Hōryūji Temple (法隆寺), said to be the pinnacle of ancient Japanese architectural art, is located between Kyōto and Nara; it is said this ancient temple was built by the hands of ancient Koryŏ people. Furthermore, the murals within it, currently designated as national treasures as Japan's finest works of art, were done by the hand of a Koryŏ monk named Tonch'yo (돈쬬). Shitennōji Temple (四天王寺) in Ōsaka's Tennōji Ward was constructed by the hands of high monks from Silla, and the Tennō pillar enshrined within its main hall faces west; it is said this was done from that time to have it face Silla, that is, Korea. In this way, Confucianism, Buddhism, and all else are deeply related to our Korea. Therefore, Korean compatriots in the Mainland not only visit these historical sites and think, "These ancestors came to the Mainland before us, and their descendants have flourished to this day centering on the Imperial Household, so we too, like them, must enjoy prosperity on this land for generations to come," but they are also convinced that this is absolutely possible.

The Character of Mainland People
Originally, the people of the Mainland today consist of continental and oceanic lineages, which already completed ethnic fusion in ancient times, forming the Descendants of Heaven Race (天孫民族), that is, the Yamato Race (大和民族), with the Imperial Household as the head family (宗家). Because they are a people completed through the fusion of various tribes like this, meaning their blood is fusion-oriented, their culture is also fusion-oriented, and their character is fusion-oriented. Therefore, Japan's original national character, when dealing with other peoples and other cultures, was extremely liberal and tolerant. This national character became the driving force behind the rapid development seen today. Some might rebut, asking if they are truly that liberal and tolerant, perhaps misunderstanding my words, but rather than listening to me, please go to the Mainland yourself, especially to the countryside, and interact with the farmers. They are truly as gentle and simple as their mountains and rivers. This might be a slightly excessive example, but last year when I went to Ōsaka, I met a certain Mr. X, an old friend of mine who held anarchistic leanings and caused the police considerable worry. During our conversation, he asked, "Chinese history has sages like Confucius (孔子) and Mencius (孟子), and sage kings like Yao (堯) and Shun (舜). But in Japanese history, not a single such sage has appeared. Do you know the reason why?" <31> Thinking he was bringing up this topic just to badmouth Mainland people as usual, I said, "I don't know," intending to hear him out. He replied, "It's because in China, people like Confucius, Mencius, Yao, and Shun appeared only once in an era, making it a special event worthy of historical record. But in Japan, people like Confucius, Mencius, Yao, and Shun number in the thousands, tens of thousands. Trying to record them all in history would require tens of thousands of history books, which still wouldn't be enough. As proof, leave the Ōsaka suburbs, go to the countryside, and get close to the farmers." I recall laughing upon hearing this. Of course, among city dwellers, it's no exaggeration to say many have almost lost their original character, becoming pseudo-humans. However, even looking at the example of Ōsaka as I saw it, currently, in the regular meetings (常會) under the neighborhood associations (町內會), Mainland people and Koreans are becoming one body, and everything is proceeding harmoniously.

The Current Situation of Compatriots Residing in Ōsaka
As you may know, in the Mainland, there is the Concordia Association (協和會), and all meetings or activities related to Koreans are handled by this Concordia Association. In Tōkyō, there is the incorporated foundation, the Central Concordia Association (中央協和會), which handles all Concordia Association affairs related to Koreans. Within Korea, there seems to be a strong tendency to think that all our Koreans in Ōsaka are ignorant laborers, but when I went to Ōsaka, I realized that the compatriots residing there were entirely different from what we had imagined. Firstly, in Ōsaka, there are 200 university-graduate intellectual youths. Capitalists with assets in the hundreds of thousands or millions of yen have emerged, and furthermore, these capitalists, unlike the landowners seen here, are almost entirely respectable factory owners employing hundreds or thousands of workers. Also, the majority of the rest are industrial warriors (産業戰士), striving and actively engaged in increasing their livelihood to secure the home front (銃後).

Concordia Association Activities and Beautiful Tales of Korean Patriotism
Furthermore, to cultivate the Japanese spirit among these Koreans, various activities are centered around the Concordia Association, among which the activities of the youth division are particularly noticeable. Under the Ōsaka Prefectural Concordia Association, there are 57 branches organized by police station units. Each branch currently provides military training to its youth members, holding intense drills from time to time. Also, to strengthen workplace service (職域奉公), they conduct heat resistance (耐熱) training in summer and cold resistance (耐寒) training in winter.<32> They have also organized labor service corps (勤勞奉仕隊) for industrial service to the nation (産業報國), and the activities of the women's labor service corps are especially remarkable. On the other hand, they occasionally hold consolation visits for wounded soldiers (傷病兵慰問), and singers frequently console the wounded soldiers by singing songs like "Arirang" (아리랑) and "Toraji T'aryŏng" (도라지打令). Moreover, among the high-spirited young men and women, there are quite a few instances of volunteering for blood transfusions (輸血) to hasten the recovery of wounded soldiers suffering in sickbeds, even by a single day. Not only that, last year, a movement to donate military aircraft (軍用機奉納運動) arose centered on the Concordia Association, collecting over one million yen. A ceremony was held in Tōkyō to present five fighter planes to the Army and Navy. As soon as the movement to construct official residences for Army and Navy ministers arose, voluntary donations (捐) flooded in, and while the initial goal was to collect 500,000 yen, it actually reached 600,000 yen. These various noble deeds occur one after another, resulting in countless beautiful acts and stories. Through all these movements, they gradually embody the Japanese spirit. It is easy to say "Japanese spirit," but explaining it takes a very long time. To summarize it in one word, it is "to die without a word when it is time to die." This is the essence of the Japanese spirit. One's own property, of course, and even one's life, are all possessions of the Emperor. If the Emperor commands it, one goes to the place of death without a word, unconditionally offering one's life to the nation through death – that is the Japanese spirit. Korean compatriots in the Mainland are experiencing and realizing this while sharing joys and sorrows with the Mainland people. We within Korea must also learn this spirit.

The World Situation and Our Resolve
Especially today, as the international situation becomes increasingly tense with the changes in the global political situation, as Britain and America gradually consolidate the so-called ABCD encirclement line, and as the Soviet Union, receiving aid from America, adopts a watchful stance towards the East, we Koreans must achieve a great awakening. Think about it, everyone. When a large fire breaks out, has already reduced the entire neighboring village to ashes, and is clearly seen spreading to one's own neighborhood, if one says, "Extinguishing the fire is the job of the fire brigade members, it's none of our business," what will the result truly be? When the fire approaches, one shouldn't just leave the work to the firefighters. Everyone in the village, regardless of age or gender, must mobilize fully. Each person, according to their strength – those carrying water, those digging earth, <33> those with clubs, those with hoes – must exert their total effort in this defense, shouldn't they? However, if these many people act arbitrarily according to their own whims, they will only hinder each other, making it impossible to achieve the goal of extinguishing the fire. Therefore, the villagers must be gathered, units organized with chiefs for each unit, and above them, a position overseeing everything must be established. Under such unified command, at least until the fire is completely extinguished, the villagers must unconditionally obey the orders from above absolutely; otherwise, the objective cannot be achieved. Just like this, in a wartime system, even if the general public finds some government actions somewhat incomprehensible, they must trust the government, obey its guidance and commands, and single-mindedly strive to overcome this urgent national crisis. To consolidate such a wartime footing, Ōsaka recently established something like a Wartime Guidance Countermeasures Committee (臨戰指導對策委員會) – a tripartite body comprising the police, the Concordia Association, and civilian representatives, separate from the peacetime Concordia Association structure – to strive for strengthened guidance. On the civilian side, the trend towards spontaneously organizing and activating something like a Wartime Countermeasures Cooperation Association (臨戰對策協力會) is growing strong. The activities of compatriots overseas and those residing in the Mainland are like this; how can the activities within Korea, the main home, be inferior to this without losing face? Lately, there is talk of Japan-Korea unification (內鮮一體), leading only to demands for equal rights, but before asserting rights, let us fulfill our duties. I am convinced that the time for us to rise up for the Empire and for Koreans themselves is right now. (Applause)<34>

**Summary**

This 1941 article by Sin T'aeyŏk describes his observations of Koreans living in Tōkyō and Ōsaka, Japan ("the Mainland"). He contrasts their perceived enthusiastic participation in the Japanese war effort and assimilation (Imperial Subjectification) through organizations like the Kyōwakai (Concordia Association) with the situation in Korea, which he deems lacking. He highlights historical ties between Korea and Japan, praises the perceived tolerance of the Japanese character (especially rural folk), notes the economic success of some Koreans in Japan, and details patriotic activities like military drills, labor service, donations for warplanes, and blood drives for soldiers. Ultimately, he exhorts Koreans in Korea to emulate the perceived loyalty and self-sacrifice of Koreans in Japan, embrace the "Japanese spirit" (dying unquestioningly for the Emperor), and fully support Japan's war effort under a unified command structure, prioritizing duty over rights within the framework of *Naisen Ittai* (Japan and Korea as One Body).

For historians, this article is a stark example of pro-Japanese propaganda authored by a Korean intellectual during the height of wartime mobilization. It reveals the intense pressure and rhetoric used to compel Korean assimilation and support for the Japanese empire, framing collaboration not just as necessary but as patriotic and historically rooted. The specific examples of Kyōwakai activities, the emphasis on the "Japanese spirit," and the call for unquestioning obedience offer insights into the mechanisms and ideology of colonial control and collaboration during this period.

臨戰愛國者의 大獅子吼!!, 東京, 大阪은 이렇다
						辛泰嶽
오늘 사회자로부터 東京, 大阪갔다가온 이야기를 여러분앞에서 말씀하여 달라는 말씀이 있어서 오늘 이 자리에 섰습니다. 東京, 大阪에 있는 朝鮮人이라야 특별히 떼여놓고 말씀 올닐 바가 아니고 모다 관련을 가지고 있으며 또한 朝鮮人전체의 일부분으로의 의의밖게 없는줄 아는데 朝鮮人 전체라고 할 것 같으면 대별하여 海外에 있는 부대 海內에 있는 부대로 난홀수 있고 해내에 있는 부대만 해도 內地에 있는 朝鮮人 鮮內에 있는 朝鮮人 이렇게 나놀 수 있는데 해외의 朝鮮人 특히 滿洲國에 在佳하는 朝鮮人으로 말하면 滿洲건국이래 協和會를 통하여 그 國是인「五族協和運動」에 중요역할을 할 뿐 아니라 皇國臣民化運動도 비상한 열의를 가지고 진행되고 있습니다. 더욱 滿洲國은 蘇聯에 대한 지리적 관계도 있어서 전혀 일선적 역할를 다하고 있는 중이외다. 그리고 朝鮮內 우리의 활동에 대하여는 여러분이 더 잘아실 줄 아니 더 말슴치 않겠거니와 一言으로 말하면 모든 점에 있어서 부족한 점이 많다고 보여집니다. 그러면 內地에 재재하는 朝鮮人의 상황은 어떠한가? 이는 다시 대별하여 과거와 현재와 장래에 분하여 논할 필요가 있습니다.

過去의 內地와 朝鮮關係

內地人의 性格
원래 현재의 내지인이라는 것은 大陸系, 海洋系가 있어서 이것이 太古時代에 이미 민족융화를 완수하여 황실을 宗家한 天孫民族, 즉, 大和民族을 형성하였는데 이와같이 여러족의 융합에 의하여 완성된 민족이기 때문에 즉 피가 융합적이기 때문에 문화도 융합적이고 성격도 융합적이외다. 따라서 日本의 본래의 국민적 성격이라는 것은 다른 민족, 다른 문화에 대할때 그 태도는 극히 자유럽고 관용성이 많하엿슴니다. 국민적 성격이 오늘날 이렇게 급속도의 발전을 보게된 원동력이 된 것이외다. 그리 자유관용하냐고 반박하고 나 의말에 대하여 혹 오해할 분이 있을는지도 모르나, 그것은 내 말을 듣기보다도 여러분 자신이 내지에 가서 더욱 농촌같은 데 가서 농민과 접촉하여 보십시오. 참으로 그 산천과 같이 온화하고 순박합니다. 이 말은 예로는 조금 과한 말이나 제가 작년 大阪가서 나의 舊友로 정에는 無政府主義的色彩를 가지고 警察方面에도 대단히 걱정을 기치던 某氏라는 이를 만났는데 氏가 대화중 『支那의 역사에는 孔子나 孟子같은 성인이 있고 역사상에도 堯나 舜같은 성군이 있는데 日本에는 역사상 그러한 성인이 하나도 나타나지 안었으니 그 원인이 어데 있는가를 아는가? 』＜31＞하고 묻기에 나는 氏가 여전히 내지인에 대한 險口나 할려고 이러한 과제를 내여 놓는가 생각하고 그의 말을 들을려고 「나는 모른다.」고 하였더니『그것은 支那에는 孔子나 孟子나 堯나 舜같은 이가 한시대에 한사람밖에 없으니 그것이 특별한 일이라고 해서 역사에 오를 인물이 되지마는 日本에는 孔孟, 堯, 舜같은 사람이 수천 수만을 헤이게 되니 그것을 전부 역사에 올리려다가는 역사책 여러 1만권을 매여도 부족하게 되겠기 때문이 다. 그 증명으로는 大阪근교를 떠난 농촌에 가서 농민과 근접하여 보라.』는 말을 듣고 우슨 일이 있습니다. 물론 도시에 사는 사람가운대는 본래의 성격을 거의 잃어버렸다고 하여도 과언이 아닐만치 한 사이비의 인간이 많습니다. 그러나 내가 본 大阪의 예로 본다고 할지라도 현재 町內會아래 있는 常會같은데서 내선인이 일체가 되어 모든 것이 원만히 진행되고 있습니다.

大阪在佳 同胞의 現狀
여러분도 아시겠지마는 내지에는 協和會라는 것이 朝鮮人관계의 회합, 又는 활동은 전부 이 協和會에서 하고 있습니다. 都京에 재단법인 中央協和會라는 것이 있어서 朝鮮人관계의모든 協和會業은 이곳에서 합니다. 조선내에서는 大阪있는 우리 朝鮮人은 전부 무식한 노동자들인가고 생각하는 경향이 상당히 농후하지마는 제가 大阪가서 본 大阪在佳同胞는 우리가 상상하던 바와는 딴판인 것을 알게 되었습니다. 첫째 大阪에는 2백명의 대학졸업한 인테리청년이 있고, 수십만 又는 백만대의 자본가가 생겼고 더욱 그 자본가라는 것이 여기서 보는 지주와 달라서 거의 전부 훌륭한 공장주로서 수백수천명의 직공을 쓰고 있는 이들 뿐이 뢰다. 또 其外의 대부분은 산업전사로서 銃後를 수확하기 위하여 생활증식에 挺身活躍하고 있습니다.
