How to Treat Hyperacidity, the Most Common Stomach Ailment – Fundamentally Treating it Through Cell Protoplasm Revitalization
						By Okabe Taku, Medical Doctor

Among stomach illnesses, the most common is hyperacidity syndrome (胃酸過多症). It is caused by carelessly overeating and overdrinking as appetite dictates, consuming large amounts of meat, or ingesting foods with high irritant properties (剌戟性), and it is a disease particularly prevalent among middle-aged people. Sour liquid comes up the throat, the chest hurts, and the solar plexus hurts two to three hours after eating.

However, a peculiar characteristic of this illness is that the symptoms subside upon eating, only to reappear when the stomach becomes empty.

The heartburn, which is the representative symptom of this illness, occurs because when food is eaten, the stomach secretes acidic gastric acid (胃酸) to digest it. If an abnormality occurs in the stomach fluid secretion function and the amount of hydrochloric acid in the gastric fluid increases, the stomach wall is stimulated by this acidity, causing heartburn. Now, what dangers arise if this heartburn is left untreated? Due to the high acidity of the gastric fluid, the stomach wall is eroded by its own fluid, eventually leading to vomiting blood (吐血),

causing gastric ulcers (胃潰瘍),
and in many cases, ultimately progressing to stomach cancer (胃癌). Therefore, seeking prompt treatment when heartburn symptoms arise is naturally also highly effective for the prevention of gastric ulcers and stomach cancer.

When heartburn starts, you might immediately take a baking soda preparation (重曹劑), or if you consult a pharmacy, they too will recognize heartburn as hyperacidity syndrome and likely give you baking soda (重炭酸ソ-ダ) or another alkali agent (アルカリ劑), thinking an alkali agent would be good for neutralizing acid. Indeed, baking soda has the effect of neutralizing stomach acid, and taking it provides temporary relief. However,

when baking soda decomposes,
it produces carbon dioxide gas. Then this <118> carbon dioxide gas immediately irritates the stomach wall, promoting the secretion of hydrochloric acid. Therefore, using it gradually increases stomach acid, making it actually harmful. However, there is a medicine called "Wakamoto" (와까모도), a compound medicinal yeast agent (複合藥用酵母菌劑), whose nature is entirely different from temporary sedatives like baking soda. It possesses the action of smoothing the activity of stomach wall cells and returning the abnormally hyperactive stomach function to a correct state. The distinctive feature of this medicine, as mentioned above, is that it imparts vitality to weakened stomach tissue cells,

returning the altered stomach tissue cells
to normal. Thus, its effect on gastrointestinal diseases is extremely broad (廣汎). For example, if someone suffering from hyperacidity syndrome uses it, the gastric fluid secretion function is regulated, the excessive acid naturally returns to normal, and heartburn simultaneously ceases. Also, for those who have left hyperacidity pain untreated for a long time, resulting in gastric ulcers, taking this causes the cells of the ulcerated surface to gradually regenerate (新生), and the stomach pain also subsides. On the other hand, it also has the effect of promoting digestion. Especially distinct from conventional chemical drugs (在來化學藥劑), it has no side effects (副作用), and even with long-term use, it does not become habit-forming (習慣性), which is highly praised by patients.

"Wakamoto" costs one yen and sixty sen for a twenty-five-day supply (price may vary by region according to agreed prices) and is available at pharmacies throughout all of Korea (全鮮).<119>

**Summary**

This 1941 article by Dr. Okabe Taku addresses hyperacidity (excess stomach acid), explaining its causes (overeating, irritating foods), symptoms (heartburn, pain relieved by eating but returning on an empty stomach), and serious risks if untreated (ulcers, cancer). It criticizes the common remedy of baking soda, arguing that while it offers temporary relief, the resulting carbon dioxide gas ultimately stimulates more acid production. The article strongly promotes a Japanese product, "Wakamoto," a medicinal yeast agent claimed to fundamentally treat hyperacidity by revitalizing stomach cells, regulating acid secretion, healing ulcers, aiding digestion, and having no side effects or habit-forming properties. It provides the price and availability throughout colonial Korea. From a historical perspective, the article is interesting as it reflects Japanese medical/commercial influence in colonial Korea, promoting a specific Japanese pharmaceutical product using scientific-sounding language ("cell protoplasm revitalization"). It also shows contemporary health concerns and remedies, contrasting traditional or simple fixes (baking soda) with modern, marketed solutions, highlighting the blend of medical advice and advertising common in popular magazines of the era.
