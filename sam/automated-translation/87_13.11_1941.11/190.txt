A Thousand Soldiers' Forest
						Hakusan Seiju

East of the capital, from the forest of Yangju,
Morning and evening, joyous voices are heard.
A thousand young volunteer warriors
Each singing of the Emperor's eternal reign.

Listen, you! Were you yesterday
Scooping fishing nets (漁綱) on the reed-deep banks of the Tuman River?
Or perhaps chasing horses on a pasture in southern Cheju?
How fitting! Children of the north and south, today dreaming together in one barracks (梀).

In the four hundred-plus provinces where the wheat harvest ripens richly,
A million men of Yamato have already hurried to the field of battle.
Young men, you too must quickly complete your training,
Go forth and beat the war drums, go forth and brave the rain of bullets (彈雨).

Ah, today too, the sound of wooden swords (木劍), the echo of military boots (軍靴) can be heard<116>
That manly singing voice, from the forest of Yangju to the east.
Preparing for tomorrow, to become shields for the Emperor,
The cheerful singing voices of young stalwarts can be heard.

—At the Volunteer Soldier Training Center—

At Goransa Temple

Standing in the garden where a hundred monks performed a thousand readings, even now I hear the voice of Kudara's (Paekche's) sacred scriptures (御經).

Perhaps on a spring night, like butterflies, Yamato maidens danced in this garden.

On days of flowers, did the island woman (島女) on her journey pick Goran flowers (皐蘭) and long for Kyōto?
Oh, stone woman (石女), pouring clear water (淸水) for the artisans carrying bronze pillars (靑銅の柱) and hurrying along the mountain path (山地).

Is it the rich woman (豊女), saying "It is spring" (春聯はるとて), standing still at the Suzaku Gate (朱雀門) on the capital's main street (都大路) at dusk?

Standing alone in the garden the Yamato maidens departed, I listen to the hammers of the honorable construction (御造營) and recall a thousand years.

—Visiting for Labor Service for the Honorable Construction of Puyŏ Shrine—<117>

**Summary**

The document presents two poems by Hakusan Seiju (likely a Korean author using a Japanese pen name). The first poem, "A Thousand Soldiers' Forest," glorifies young Korean men from different regions who have volunteered for the Japanese army, training at a center in Yangju to become "shields for the Emperor" and fight in the war. The second poem, "At Goransa Temple," is set during labor service for the construction of the Puyŏ Shrine. It evokes the ancient history of the Paekche kingdom (Kudara), imagining Japanese (Yamato) maidens there in the past, and reflects on the thousand-year connection while observing the shrine's construction, blending historical reflection with contemporary imperial projects. For historians, these poems are significant as they exemplify Japanese wartime propaganda aimed at Koreans, promoting participation in the military ('volunteer' soldiers) and reinforcing the imperial narrative of shared history (Japan-Paekche connection) through projects like the Puyŏ Shrine, built using mobilized Korean labor (勤勞奉仕 - kinrō hōshi). The poems starkly illustrate the cultural and political assimilation policies during the late colonial period.
