『Most Satisfying Work and Debut Work』

Samch'ŏlli-sa: Among the dozens, hundreds of poems, novels, and other works you have all produced to this day, what do you consider your most satisfying work (會心의 作)? Of course, evaluations of your representative works are mostly determined through literary critics and reader impressions, but please tell us your own thoughts as authors, setting public opinion aside.

Sŏnghae: I can't clearly point to a "most satisfying work" among my past pieces, but the one I personally feel a natural attachment to is "The Mastless Sailing Ship (키일흔 帆船)." This was published as a serialized novel in the Tong-A Ilbo over about 180 installments, and I believe I poured a great deal of sincerity and effort into it.

Tokkyŏn: It's the same for me. Among the dozens of works I've written in the past, there isn't one I'd call an absolutely "most satisfying work," but if I had to choose, maybe "P'uro's Memoir (푸로手記)" was better? It was a short story published in either "Sinmin (新民)" or "Chosŏn Mundan (朝鮮文壇)."

Samch'ŏlli-sa: Mr. Kim Tongin, perhaps it's "Potato (감자)"?

Kim Tongin: I don't have one. If I were to point to a work that relatively draws my interest, "Potato" might be included.

Samch'ŏlli-sa: How about others?... For Pinghŏ, it must be the controversial "Fire (불)."

Pinghŏ: Not really. I do desperately want to write one truly satisfying work, but that will probably be something for the future.

Samch'ŏlli-sa: It seems the phrase "most satisfying work" carries some awkwardness, leading to much modesty. In that case, please tell us about your debut work (處女作). At what age did you first release which work to the world?

Sŏhae: That's something I can answer. What I published as my "debut work" was the short story "Escape Log (脫出記)" in "Chosŏn Mundan (朝鮮文壇)," sometime 8 or 9 years ago from now, when I was twenty-two or three. I believe it was that one. I still remember the feeling then was quite pleasant.

Ansŏ: For me too, it was quite a long time ago. After returning from Tokyo, I published a poem titled "Girl of the North (北方의 少女)" in the "T'aesŏ Munye Sinbo (泰西文藝新報)." I believe that was in the spring of the year I turned twenty-three.

Pinghŏ: For me, it was probably the short story "Sacrificial Flower (犧牲花)" published in "Kaebyŏk (開闢)." That was when I was nineteen.

Tongin: It was also when I was nineteen. I first published the work "Sorrows of the Weak (弱한 자의 술품)" in the magazine "Ch'angjo (創造)." I translated it from a Japanese text I had written while at Meiji Gakuin in Tokyo.

Il Yŏp: If I have a debut work, it would be a short piece titled "Self-Awakening (自覺)" published in the magazine "Pulgyo (佛敎)." That was 7 years ago from now. Before that, I had various minor essays and miscellaneous works turned into print (活字化) in publications like "Yŏjage (女子界)," but this was probably what could be considered my debut work.

Sŏnghae: When was that in "Sin Saenghwal (新生活)" magazine? Anyway, it was when I was studying in Tokyo, so I wrote a piece titled "Half-Length Portrait of Room Three (三號室의 半身像)," based on visiting an unconvicted prisoner at Ichigaya Prison (市ケ谷刑務所). That was the first work of mine turned into print (活字化).

Tokkyŏn: I published a short story titled "Trampled Down (蹂爛)" in Japanese in a Japanese newspaper, but it might be difficult to call this a debut work. Later, when I turned twenty-five, I published a short story called "Daughter of a Tenant Farmer (小作人의 딸)" in "Sinmin (新民)"; that was probably my debut work.

Ch'unhae: For me, it would be "Snowy Night (눈오는 밤)." It was published in "Ch'angjo (創造)," which was the only literary magazine at the time, when I was eighteen.

『Manuscript Fees Received』

Samch'ŏlli-sa: What is the total amount of remuneration you all have received to date as compensation for your works? That is, the total sum received from magazine companies, newspaper companies, and publishing houses. I heard Ch'unwŏn once said that adding it all up, it came to about ten thousand won.

Tongin: If Ch'unwŏn received ten thousand won, then perhaps the next person who received the most is Ansŏ. The dozens of volumes from the old Hansŏng Toso Hoesa (漢城圖書會社) were mostly translated by Ansŏ.

Ansŏ: Those were good times. They would give 200 or 300 won even for translating things like the "Life of Joan of Arc (짠다-크傳)" or the "Life of Hannibal (한니발)." Back then, I handled a considerable amount of money called manuscript fees. After that, there was money received from newspaper and magazine companies, but how much could that be? Anyway, the total manuscript fees received over the past 10-plus years seem to be around 4,000 won.

Tokkyŏn: I think I'm around that level too. If you combine the money received for serialized novels in newspapers and the copyright fees received from publications as standalone books, it comes to about 4,000 won.

Pinghŏ: Right. Since the publishing industry isn't robust in Chosŏn (朝鮮), writers inevitably have to turn to writing serialized novels for newspapers to make money. Have I made about 2,000 won in total so far?

Il Yŏp: Everyone has so much. I wonder if I've even made 100 won. I've written manuscripts for free, but I've hardly ever been commissioned to write for money. I once received 20 won for writing a short piece for "Pulgyo (佛敎)," but I've never received several tens of won at once.

Ch'unhae: If I include the money received for writing for Christian magazines, I think it comes to about 2,000 won. If that 2,000 won had come into my hands all at once, I might see it as a large sum, but since it came in bits and pieces... On another note, because I managed "Chosŏn Mundan (朝鮮文壇)" for many years, the manuscript fees I paid out with my own hands back then amounted to a considerable sum.

Sŏhae: That's right. During the "Chosŏn Mundan (朝鮮文壇)" era, thanks to Ch'unhae's sacrificial efforts, manuscript fees were cleanly paid to all the writers. The magazine managers then and now are completely different. A society that pays manuscript fees needs to come soon.
Anyway, the manuscript fees I've received here and there... well, perhaps around 1,500 won?

Tongin: I also seem to have poor luck with manuscript fees. Tallying it all up until now, it might barely reach 1,500 won, if at all.

Sŏnghae: For me too, adding it all up, it would likely be difficult to exceed 3,000 won. Receiving royalties for the standalone book "Behind You All (汝等의 背後에서)" and manuscript fees for newspaper serial novels constituted almost the entire sum.

Samch'ŏlli-sa: Among those not present here today, if we were to count the giants who received a lot of manuscript fees, Sangsŏp would likely be mentioned. His career is long, and he has dozens of works, so he's probably second only to Ch'unwŏn. Also, Na Tohyang, who is now deceased, is thought to have earned a considerable amount. He had several publications through the old Chosŏn Toso Hoesa (朝鮮圖書會社) and wrote many serialized novels for the Sidae Ilbo (時代日報) and Tong-A Ilbo (東亞日報).<28>

『Writing Time and Posture』

Samch'ŏlli-sa: When do you all generally write? For instance, during the day, morning or evening? And do you write lying down or sitting? How fast do you write? Please tell us frankly. People are very curious about the private lives of writers or what might be called "backstage stories (樂屋話シ)."

Tongin: I don't know about others, but I write lying down. I roll around the room, completely uninhibited, keeping my body free and writing while lying prone. Perhaps it's because it became a habit, but it's very freeing. If I were told to write sitting upright at a desk with proper attire like some Confucian scholar (道學者), I probably couldn't write a single line.
And my writing speed is fast. It rarely takes me more than 30 minutes to write one installment of a newspaper serial novel. It's difficult to write the first few lines, but once the pen starts moving, it's very fast.
The short story "Red Mountain (붉은 산)," published in "Samch'ŏlli (三千里)" in the issue before last, I received the manuscript request at eleven that day, conceived the idea, and finished it by one in the afternoon, so I think my speed is generally fast.

Samch'ŏlli-sa: When do you write?

Tongin: Previously, I often wrote after breakfast when my head was clear, but nowadays I mostly write late at night. In a private room...

Tokkyŏn: Mr. Tongin, you're fast. It takes me about 50 minutes to write one installment of a newspaper novel. And, as everyone likely experiences, starting to write (着筆) is difficult. When the concept is fully matured (爛熟), the pen flies quickly, though.

Samch'ŏlli-sa: Do you write at night?

Tokkyŏn: I write newspaper serials in bed in the morning, and short stories, I stay up all night, writing when everything is quiet. Perhaps because it's become a habit, I always write lying down, propped up by a pillow, in a solitary room.

Sŏhae: I'm not like that. I always write sitting down, but I do have this particular habit (性癖). If I have writing to do, I deliberately skip dinner that day, clean the room thoroughly, forbid my wife or child from entering the room at all, prevent loud talking, laughing, or chatter from the next room, and of course, prohibit things like fabric smoothing (다듬이질). Then I take up the pen, and I struggle quite a bit until my mind settles down. Also, my speed is on the slow side. It's especially slow when I first start writing (着筆). I write, then discard, write, then discard; if I aim for 100 manuscript pages, 60 or 70 end up as waste paper (수지). And if it's a short story, once I put pen to paper, I usually finish it by staying up all night and skipping breakfast.

Il Yŏp: You really go through a lot of trouble. I write during the day. After my husband leaves for school or work during the day, I tidy up the room neatly and then sit facing the desk like a schoolgirl. That's when the pen moves well.

Ch'unhae: Me too. I simply cannot write lying down. So I write facing a desk, but rather than having the room tidied up neatly, I actually prefer writing amidst a messy, cluttered environment. My mind doesn't get scattered, and for writing, the deep night when all sounds of the world are silent (萬籟가 俱寂하는 深夜) is indeed best.

Sŏnghae: I'm not like that either. In the past, when I didn't have a regular job, it was my habit to write late at night, but now that I have to go to work at a fixed time every morning, if I stay up all night, I don't have the energy (才操) to do my professional work the next day. Nowadays, I mostly write before breakfast. As for writing speed, it's difficult at first. I usually start by doodling (雜書).
I write things like ABC or random characters without any system, and while doing so, the concept fully forms, and from then on, it flows out all at once.

Ansŏ: I start in the morning and usually write without eating. If I don't write lying down, the pen doesn't move well. Also, I have a habit of rewriting if the handwriting is bad. So, in the end, I tend to have a huge amount of discarded manuscript paper.

Sŏhae: Pinghŏ is the slow writer (遲筆).

Pinghŏ: That's right. My pen is on the slow side. Especially the first three or four pages are very slow. And I feel like I can write down all my thoughts only when I write lying down.

Samch'ŏlli-sa: What do you eat while writing? Listening to Japanese writers, they say they keep snacks or fruit beside them and eat while writing.

Tokkyŏn: Hey man. Even if we wanted to eat, Chosŏn writers need to have something to eat to keep beside them! (Everyone laughs)

Sŏhae: I smoke cigarettes. I smoke about a pack of cigarettes while writing four or five manuscript pages.

Samch'ŏlli-sa: Does having your wife nearby help you write better?

Tongin: It's a hindrance.

Sŏhae, Tokkyŏn: That's right. A huge hindrance! (Everyone laughs)

Samch'ŏlli-sa: Ms. Il Yŏp, do you write better when your husband (밧갓어른) is nearby?

Il Yŏp: Oh my. How embarrassing. What do you mean, write better? (Laughs)

Samch'ŏlli-sa: Do you all review your work once it's written?

Ch'unhae: I don't like looking at it before it's printed, so I don't.

Tongin: I don't look at it either. I just send it off as written.

Pinghŏ: I take out a manuscript I've written after some time has passed and look at it. I don't like looking at it immediately on the same day.

Ansŏ: I reread it. I feel reassured only after reviewing it.

Samch'ŏlli-sa: Are your written manuscripts generally neat? Or messy?

Sŏhae: They are neat when first written, but after reviewing, correcting, and adding more, they eventually become messy.

Tokkyŏn: My manuscripts aren't that messy.

Tongin: Ours aren't either. We don't do much correcting and adding.

Pinghŏ: Since I tend to revise my manuscripts, they are probably on the messy side.

『Hopes for Publishers』

Samch'ŏlli-sa: We imagine you writers have many demands or hopes regarding current Chosŏn publishers. Please share them with us.

Sŏhae: Generally, Chosŏn publishers lack the noble intention of leading the culture of a nation. They focus on making a bit of money, resorting to selling old tales like "The Tale of Yu Ch'ungnyŏl (劉忠烈傳)," or else, trying to attract vulgar curiosity, they chase after "ero-guro (에로,그로)." This deplorable trend (弊風) is widespread, which is truly lamentable.

Ansŏ: Is that all? They try to exploit (濫用) writers and use them for their own commercial policies.

Ch'unhae: It's true that the publishing world, including both magazines and book publishers, has largely degenerated, but this isn't just limited to the direction of publishing culture. It might be the trend of the times (時勢), something unavoidable (不可奈何).

Pinghŏ: Comparing newspapers and magazines to those of 5 or 6 years ago, we cannot deny that their format and content have progressed astonishingly. Also, the increase in readers of newspapers and magazines is truly remarkable (刮目). So, one cannot say the publishing world has regressed, but I just wish they would make an effort to lead the readership in a healthy direction.

Tongin: Also, in the bookstore sector (冊肆方面), it would be nice if someone with publishing capital, who understands the times and has considerable wealth (富力), would emerge and make an effort.

Samch'ŏlli-sa: Understood. One more thing. What do you think of the recent Arts and Culture sections (學藝欄) in newspapers? I even heard talk that the Tong-A Ilbo is abolishing its Arts and Culture section.

Pinghŏ: They aren't abolishing it. I heard they are merging it with the Women's section (婦人欄). In reality, even if you keep the Arts and Culture section, censorship by the authorities is difficult, there aren't many special people to write for it, and if you publish scholarly or specialized articles, there's no readership!

Tongin: Also, if it's an Arts and Culture section manuscript, you have to pay for it... Anyway, it's quite a problematic issue. Even if the company authorities feel that way, since there's no other way to publish literary works besides newspapers and magazines, we hope that newspapers and magazines will allocate significant space (紙面) to publish our works. **Summary**

This roundtable discussion features prominent Korean writers of the early 1930s reflecting on their careers. They discuss their most personally satisfying works (often modestly denying having one yet), their debut publications and ages, and their income from writing, revealing starkly low earnings for most compared to Yi Kwangsu (Ch'unwŏn). They share diverse writing habits regarding time, posture (lying down vs. sitting), environment, and speed. The writers express frustration with publishers prioritizing profit over cultural value, exploiting writers, and chasing sensationalism ("ero-guro"). They lament the poor financial compensation and hope for a more supportive publishing environment, while also acknowledging the progress in magazine/newspaper reach and discussing the challenges facing newspaper arts sections under censorship and commercial pressures. For historians, this article offers valuable insights into the working conditions, economic realities, creative processes, and critical perspectives of major literary figures in colonial Korea. It highlights the precarious financial situation of writers, their relationship with the burgeoning print media, anxieties about censorship, and critiques of the commercialization and perceived decline in the quality of the publishing industry.

『會心의 作과 處女作』

本社側 여러분이 오날까지 수십 수백편의 시가 소설 등 만흔 작품을 제작하여 내노흔 중에서 가장 잘되엇다고 생각하는 회심의 作은 무엇임니까. 물론 문예비평가와 독자의 감상을 통하야 여러분의 대표작 평가는 대개 결정되여 잇지만은 世論은 제외하고 작자 자신으로서의 생각을 말슴하여 주서요.
星海 「會心의 作」이라고 볼 것은 과거의 작품 중에서 뚜렷하게 지적이 되지 안치만은 다만 비교적 작자 자신의 애착이 저절노 가는 작품은 내게 잇서서는 「키일흔 帆船」이외다. 이것은 東亞日報에 180회 가량 연재소설노써 발표한 것인데 내 딴은 정성과 노력을 몹시 붓느라고 한 것이외다.
獨鵑-나도·그러치요, 여러 10편을 써낸 과거의 작품 중에서 그러케 절대성을 가진 會心의 작품이라고 업섯지만 그 중에서 골느라면 「푸로手記」가 나섯다고 할가요. 「新民」엔가 「朝鮮文壇」엔가 발표한 단편소설이엿슴니다.
本社側 金東仁씨는 「감자」일걸요.
金東仁-내게는 업서요. 비교적 마음을 끄으는 작품을 가르친다면 「감자」도 드러 갈는지 모르겟지만.
本社側 또다른 분으로?... 憑虛는 문제의 「불」일걸요.
憑虛-그러치도 안어요. 會心의 作을 꼭 하나 쓰고 십기는 하지만 그것은 아마 장래의 일일걸요.
本社側-「會心의 作」이라면 語弊잇는 듯하야 퍽들 謙讓하심니다 그려. 그러거든 處女作을 말슴하여 주세요. 몃살 나든 때에 엇던 작품을 맨 처음에 세상에 내노앗는지요.
曙海-그런 것은 대답할 수 잇지요. 내가 「處女作」이라고 발표한 것은 지금부러 이럭저럭 8, 9년 전일 수물둘인가 셋 때에 「朝鮮文壇」에 단편소설 「脫出記」을 써낸 것이 잇지요. 아마 그것인줄 암니다. 그때 감상은 퍽으나 유쾌하엿든 기억이 지금도 가지고 잇슴니다.
岸曙-나도 꽤, 오래된 일이지요. 東京서 도라나와서 「泰西文藝新報」에 「北方의 少女」라는 詩를 하나 발표하엿지요. 그것은 스물세살 나든 해 봄인줄 암니다.
憑虛-나는 開闢에 실린 「犧牲花」라는 단편소설일걸요. 그것이 열아홉 나는 때 일이지요.
東仁-나도 열아홉 나든 해 일이지요. 「創造」잡지에「弱한 자의 술품」이란 작품을 처음 발표하엿지요. 이것은 東京 明治學院에 잇슬 때 日文으로 써 둔 것을 번역하여 내엇지요.
一葉-내게도 處女作이 잇다면 「佛敎」잡지에 「自覺」이란 단편을 하나 실은 것이 잇지요. 그것이 지금부터 7년 전임니다. 그 전에도 女子界 등에 活字化식힌 小品隨筆 기타 잡다한 작품이 잇섯지만 아마 處女作이라고 볼 것은 이것이엇슴니다.
星海-「新生活」잡지의 그것이 어느 때든가 좌우간 내가 東京學窓에 잇슬 때 일이닉가 그때 「三號室의 半身像」이라 하여 市ケ谷刑務所에 未決囚을 방문하든 것을 題材로 하고 쓴 작품이 잇섯지요. 그것이 내게는 처음 活字化식힌 작품이엇지요.
獨鵑-나는 日文新聞에다가 日文으로 「蹂爛」이란 단편을 발표한 일이 잇는데 이것을 處女作이라고 하기는 어렵겟지. 그 뒤 스물다섯되든 때 「新民」에다가 「小作人의 딸」이란 단편을 내엇는데 아마 이것이 處女作일걸요.
