Publication Date: 1932년 06월 15일
Article Title: 우리 운동을 一團體 독재로 한다면 (If Our Movement Were Run by One Group's Dictatorship)
Author: 許憲, 金璟載 (Hŏ Hŏn, Kim Kyŏngjae)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Dialogue/Roundtable Discussion)

If Our Movement Were Run by One Group's Dictatorship
						許憲, 金璟載 (Hŏ Hŏn, Kim Kyŏngjae)

Without a Strong New Organization

許憲 (Hŏ Hŏn)

Magazine Staff (hereafter Staff): To clarify the nature and limits of the issue being presented now, allow me to speak at some length as an introduction.
That is, looking at past examples, when some kind of movement is first proposed in our society, the method always involves focusing eagerly on gathering all sorts of forces—large and small, gems and stones (大小玉石)—under one banner to establish a cooperative front (協同線). Looking at the resulting lineup, religious groups like Ch'ŏndogyo, Christianity, and Buddhism, and social groups like the General Federation of Labor (勞總), General Federation of Farmers (農總), and General Federation of Youth (靑總) are inevitably arranged as essential cornerstones. Besides these, dozens of groups with differing principles and assertions, such as the Kŭn'uhoe, the three newspaper companies, various magazine publishers, the Society for the Promotion of Local Products (物産獎勵會), various clubs, and even the Sin'ganhoe, were gathered together. This seems to have been the method for all movements aiming for totality in Korea over the past ten-odd years. It was an unchanging, eternal formula. As a result, was there any single aspect that was clearly broken through? The reason is that a gathering of motley colors already proves a deterioration in quality. While it might have satisfied the majoritarians who cry "A Million Strong!" (號曰百萬), in reality, the capacity for actual action was insignificant. That was the truth.
Therefore, what if, from now on, we abandon this cooperativism and entrust our movement to the dictatorship of a single organization? Truly considering it, our society is unique, and due to various indescribable circumstances, ideological divisions between organizations have become unavoidable. It is also an inescapable reality, regardless of whether it's good or bad, that conflict, slander, and friction among leaders are incessant. Thus, since the formation of a great force has ended up being nothing but formalism day and night, wouldn't it be better, from now on, to instead entrust the work to the autocracy (專制) of one group, make that group operate under a stricter centralized system (中央集權制) than before, and then install a dictator to attempt a powerful development of the movement?
Looking at recent world trends, isn't Italian politics the autocracy of the Fascist party with Mussolini as the dictator? Whether it's Hitler's faction in Germany or the trend flowing through the political scene in Tokyo right now, the whole world is being tossed about by the Fascist (팟소) current. However, please do not misunderstand that our central point is related to this. It is by no means about directly importing Fascist ideas. It is merely about trying to base the method of the movement not on cooperation, but on dictatorship. It means relying on the singular, not the plural.

Hŏ Hŏn: Whether it's Italy's Fascist party, Germany's Hitler, the example of Russia, or the recent trend in the Tokyo military establishment, it is indeed true that the form of politics, regardless of whether it's state socialism, national socialism, or ultranationalism, is gradually moving away from parliamentarianism towards the dictatorship of a single-party autocracy. The reason is likely that the various capitalist nations of the world have now reached a stage where they require intense [-unclear word-] and thus have various factors compelling them towards Fascism (팟소화) to endure. However, I will refrain from stating my personal views on Fascist ideology here and now. I will just briefly talk about the question of what if we were to restart the course of our movement in the style of a one-group autocracy. A one-group autocracy might sound like entrusting things to Ch'ŏndogyo if it's Ch'ŏndogyo, or the General Federation of Labor if it's the General Federation of Labor, but the Sin'ganhoe was originally formed for such a purpose. It's just that because its history was short (年淺), it couldn't achieve completion and was dissolved. That is, the idea was that Korean issues must be led by the Sin'ganhoe, so in the sense that it didn't recognize other opposing groups, it was no different from a one-group dictatorship. Doesn't the slogan "Under a single party banner" (單一黨旗幟下에) say exactly that? <3>

Staff: However, only Sin'ganhoe members evaluated the Sin'ganhoe that way. Anti-Sin'ganhoe members or non-Sin'ganhoe members certainly did not value the Sin'ganhoe so highly. Also, isn't the proof that the Sin'ganhoe itself did not pride itself as the absolute sole party the fact that it participated so often as one member of consultative bodies among various organizations? That's why third parties viewed the Sin'ganhoe as nothing more than an institution responsible for one function among various groups. Doesn't the fact that, first of all, Ch'ŏndogyo or Christianity did not come under its leadership (率下) demonstrate this?

Hŏ Hŏn: They did come in. Of course, they did. Although perhaps not as organizations, many influential members (敎人) did unite together under 'Sin'gan-ism' (『新幹이즘』).
And evaluating the Sin'ganhoe as just one group among various others is clearly a cognitive error.

Staff: Yes, I understand. Nevertheless, a hastily formed organization inevitably has unnatural aspects somewhere, making it difficult to see it as 'a force capable of action.' Therefore, if, from now on, autocracy were granted to an organization with a historical record and tradition of good control and training, wouldn't there be more vigorous activity?

Hŏ Hŏn: To do that, one inevitably has to examine the existing various organizations, but ultimately, upon consideration, an organization trustworthy enough for the masses to entrust with large, holistic issues is not readily apparent.

Staff: In that case, are you saying there's nothing to expect other than the formation of a second Sin'ganhoe?

Hŏ Hŏn: I will not state my conclusion yet. In short, while one-group autocracy is certainly one method, the big question is whether such a powerful organization capable of dictating the movement's line can be found.

Staff: A problem that follows the issue of one-group dictatorship is the point that the organizational method cannot follow past practices. In the past method, two or three leaders would first step forward to create an association (會) and then establish and expand branches in various places. Then they would be busy going around with a membership roster, urging 'you join too,' 'so-and-so join too,' to get names written down. In no time, they would reach, say, 20,000 or 30,000 members. Then, after gathering them, they would supposedly start training. Instead of this, how about reversing the order and building it like a pyramid? First, allow small groups to form in villages, factories, and schools, and only then, finally, undertake the organization of the upper structure. Wouldn't the structure then become substantial, so that even if the top leadership changes numerous times, the foundation (根盤) itself would remain perpetually strong? Like a bamboo shoot that keeps emerging even when cut.

Hŏ Hŏn: That is ideal. However, the reality of society simply does not allow for that.

Staff: Since you said you won't state a conclusion, I won't try to elicit your assertion. However, just based on knowledge, what requirements do you think a candidate for the dictator of a single group should possess to be suitable?

Hŏ Hŏn: First, they must have outstanding insight; second, strong tenacity (점착력) (the power to stick persistently to the end); third, inclusiveness (포용력) and decisive courage (勇斷力); and fourth, health robust enough to run around day and night even amidst flying arrows and stones (矢石間). With outstanding insight, they can correctly guide the path of the numerous party members without mistaking the general trend. And with decisiveness, perseverance that doesn't easily despair, and the inclusiveness to embrace many people, they can lead a large organization.

Staff: How many individuals equipped with such points do you think there are? Who are they in our current society?

Hŏ Hŏn: It's difficult to name names. In any case, they must be people extraordinary in intellect, courage, and everything else.

One Group and Dictatorship for Education and Industry

金璟載 (Kim Kyŏngjae)

Staff: If we were to entrust our movement to a specific single organization—be it Ch'ŏndogyo, Buddhism, Tong'uhoe (同友會), the General Federation of Labor (勞總), etc.—in other words, if we abandoned the past method of talking about cooperative fronts and moved forward with a one-group autocracy, wouldn't the overall situation become more dynamic?

Kim Kyŏngjae: That's something that can only be considered once there is a powerful organization capable of dominating (壓頭) smaller groups, whether viewed from the standpoint of power or from the standpoint of control and training.

Staff: What constitutes a powerful force capable of dominating?

Kim Kyŏngjae: First is money. Only with a large amount of money, for instance, if launching an education movement, wouldn't schools be built in every nook and cranny (坊坊谷谷)? If launching an industrial movement, wouldn't industrial institutions be established everywhere? The more money there is, the more freely and vigorously that organization can move. However, in my view, it's not easy to find an organization with sufficient funds. Second is human talent. While the members of the organization should be educated and trained to some extent, it's even more crucial to find good leaders (수뇌자) capable of commanding and guiding all those people. But in my view, such individuals are also not easily found.

Staff: Still, aren't there relatively better organizations among them? Couldn't the one-group dictatorship be entrusted to that better organization?

Kim Kyŏngjae: In principle, there would be an XX Party that would provide unified leadership. However, Korea is different; such a thing doesn't exist, and even if we assume it does, I cannot imagine it would be allowed to come above ground and legally guide various movements. Therefore, ultimately, there must be a legal organization, and guidance <4> would be received from that organization.

Staff: Then, what is the nature of this legal organization? Please outline its characteristics within the limits of what we can discuss here today. Of course, Mr. Kim, you don't need to offer a subjective critique of whether such an organization is good or bad. Just based on the general trend, what kind of elements should an organization possess—

Kim Kyŏngjae: This is what I think. Ultimately, I believe a legal political movement organization must emerge, but since political issues likely won't pass censorship, I'll avoid discussing such things here. However, regarding the nature of that organization, whether it comes from the hands of nationalists or socialists, it must be an organization grounded in the interests of the majority of Koreans—the farmers and laborers. And that organization must, from the first day of its emergence, present a clear 'action program' (『행동강령』).

Staff: Could you give one or two examples of what might be in that 'action program'?

Kim Kyŏngjae: Education and industrial movements could be included. Since the majority needs education and also needs food and clothing, education dissemination, industrial movements, etc., that can be initiated within the possible scope, should also be undertaken.

Staff: If so, doesn't that movement absolutely require a considerable amount of 'time'?

Kim Kyŏngjae: That's right. From now on, movements must absolutely consider "time" as important. Often, looking at past examples, activists talk as if some situation will be resolved tomorrow or the day after. Considering their psychological state and circumstances, the position of the leader speaking like that is perhaps understandable, but on the other hand, the workers and farmers wait for that tomorrow or the day after, only to find nothing special happens, so they just say they are constantly being deceived. And ultimately, they have been deceived. Therefore, from now on, instead of doing that, one must, as a principle, calculate a long 'time'—be it 5 years, 3 years, 10 years, or 20 years—as an essential element of the group movement and then commence the work.
Looking at historical facts from foreign countries, even someone like Danton (『딴 톤』), who was in such a rapidly breathing situation, while teaching moment-to-moment strategies, also desperately called for the necessity of 'education' and 'industry' as fundamental and eternal tasks, and actually proceeded step-by-step with education and industrial movements even amidst that turmoil.
Therefore, I don't know what kind of organization will emerge in the future, but I believe that organization must be one that incorporates fundamental movements like education or industry as an important keynote (一基調) of its activities.

Staff: If that's the case, couldn't it be said like this? In the past, the Sin'ganhoe, while spending its 40,000 members, that much expense, and 4-5 years, ended up leaving almost nothing for posterity except a scrap of historical record. Instead, if the Sin'ganhoe had, while engaging in moment-to-moment activities, also initiated education and industrial movements fundamentally, wouldn't it have left an immortal achievement? Isn't it truly a matter of eternal regret (千載의 恨事) that it lost all those workers, that much money, and the responsive public sentiment?

Kim Kyŏngjae: I won't go so far as to say whether it's a matter of eternal regret (千載의 恨事), but one does feel regret... if only the Sin'ganhoe had seriously taken "time" into consideration and also put effort into foundational work.

Staff: Yes, I understand well. Then, getting back to the main point, do you think a one-group dictatorial movement is likely to be realized under the current circumstances?

Kim Kyŏngjae: As mentioned earlier, the emergence of an organization possessing the real power for dictatorship becomes the prerequisite condition.

The Human Elements of a Dictator

Staff: Then, changing the subject, what qualifications should the leader of a dictatorial organization possess?

Kim Kyŏngjae: First, they must have outstanding political insight.

Staff: And then?

Kim Kyŏngjae: Second, if they don't have it themselves, it's fine even if it's behind them, but they must be backed by big capital (재벌).

Staff: And also?

Kim Kyŏngjae: And then, their body must be endlessly robust. Everyone thinks about the first and second points, but some tend not to consider physical health (肉體의 건전). Humans cannot escape physiological governance. For someone who is physically weak and always clutching a medicine bottle, spirit and courage cannot well up, no matter what. Grand ambitions (雄圖) arise from the possession of a sturdy physique overflowing with vitality (정력).

Staff: Then please critique the current leaders of various organizations based on that standard.

Kim Kyŏngjae: I will refrain from going that far. (Remainder omitted - original text)<5>

**Summary**

This 1932 dialogue between Hŏ Hŏn and Kim Kyŏngjae, published in Samch'ŏlli magazine, critiques the perceived ineffectiveness of past Korean nationalist movements based on broad "cooperative fronts" involving diverse groups (religious, social, political like Sin'ganhoe). The central proposal is to consider a new model: entrusting the movement to a single, powerful, centrally controlled organization led by a strong figure, akin to a "one-group dictatorship," though explicitly distancing this from a direct import of European Fascism. Both speakers emphasize the need for such a group to possess real power (financial backing, talented and trained members, capable leadership) and discipline. Kim Kyŏngjae stresses the importance of a long-term perspective ("time") and practical action programs focusing on fundamental issues like mass education and industrial development, alongside political goals. The Sin'ganhoe is critiqued for its perceived lack of tangible, lasting achievements despite its resources and duration. Leadership qualities discussed include exceptional insight, tenacity, decisiveness, charisma, robust health, and financial backing.

From a historical perspective, this article is significant for its frank discussion of authoritarian organizational models as a potential path for the Korean movement under colonial rule, reflecting disillusionment with previous strategies and an awareness of global political trends (Fascism, single-party states). It reveals internal debates about strategy, leadership, and the very definition of "effective action" within the nationalist camp. The emphasis on long-term, practical nation-building efforts (education, industry) alongside political struggle highlights a pragmatic, developmentalist strand within Korean thought, even amidst calls for radical organizational change. The critique of Sin'ganhoe offers contemporary insight into the perceived failings of the most prominent united front organization of the late 1920s.

강력한 새 단체 업시는

許憲

본사측-지금 제출하는 이 문제의 성질과 한계를 분명히 하기 위하야 서두를 조곰 길게 말슴하겟슴니다.
