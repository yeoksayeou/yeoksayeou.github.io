Confusion of Chastity at Summer Resorts
One night at Myŏngsa Simni, the sweetbrier roses still bloom beautifully

Yu Sunja

I read Samch'ŏlli every month. And I read the true stories with even more interest.
I always thought I was the only unhappy person in the world, and that I alone suffered intense agony and pain. Therefore, I have never yet appealed to anyone about my hidden pain, my hidden agony.
Perhaps because if I did appeal, I would become a woman abandoned by the world.
It wasn't that long ago - last summer. Since I was born with a very frail constitution, there probably haven't been many summers I've passed without illness. Three years ago, after being diagnosed with lung disease and dropping out of the school I attended, I began going to my aunt's house in Wŏnsan every summer to recuperate.

Last summer, I went to my aunt's house again. Perhaps my current unhappy situation is because my parents loved me more than others. But if my parents hadn't sent me, even if I wanted to go to my aunt's house so much, I would have no agony or pain now. When I think about it, I feel resentful and angry.
I went out to the beach at Myŏngsa every morning around four o'clock.

That man I met every morning - his darkly tanned, thick arms, broad chest, the abundant lines revealed above his black swimsuit, and his handsome face on a manly physique - all of it is deeply etched in my mind.
That man was swimming in the blue, wave-tossed sea that morning too. Although I saw him every day, that morning he especially made my heart flutter. My face turned red, probably because of the affection blooming in a maiden's heart.
He ran out of the sea. And then, touching the dew-soaked buds of the sweetbrier roses, he approached me wordlessly as I stood there with a burning heart.
All around was quiet. In the brightening dawn sky, the twinkling stars were disappearing one by one, and small waves gently rolled onto the faintly visible sea. The sweetbrier roses, looking as if dyed red amidst the blue-green leaves, were drenched in the morning dew and looked even more beautiful. "Forgive me," were the words spoken by that man. Although he was the one who had set my innocent heart aflame, now that he was actually beside me, for some reason, I felt terribly frightened. So I gave no answer. That young man certainly looked radiant.

Perhaps unexpected opportunity is what always plunges life into a bewildering state. After that, he and I formed quite a close relationship. Who knew that this would be the opportunity that would ruin my destiny?
Every morning at four - it became my habit to quietly open the gate of my aunt's house and run out to the beach at Myŏngsa. He was always sitting and waiting in the shade of a sand dune.
"Good morning!" his cheerful voice, accompanied by a smile, vibrated the crisp air of the dawn beach.
To my words, spoken in a thin, trembling voice, "Yes. Good morning. You're here already," he always liked to tease, saying,
"Why shouldn't I be?"
As the days passed, the flames of passion only burned hotter. Thus, whether evening or morning, he and I preferred whispering sweet nothings—shall we call them rosy dreams?—amidst the dense green pine forest rather than strolling along the beach. The pine forest was so quiet, it felt like a place prepared just for him and me.

One evening it was. Perhaps noticing my restless state, I received orders not to go out in the evening and to go out only after daybreak in the morning. But I felt like I would go mad if I couldn't meet him even for one night. I sat waiting for my aunt to fall asleep. Finally, around ten o'clock at night, my aunt lay down. Thinking that he would be waiting, I was hastily putting on makeup when I heard a knock on the back window of my room. When I opened the door, it was him, the young man I loved.
"What is it? I'm about to go out now," I said, lowering my voice, but without answering, he said,
"I am going home tonight. My mother's illness has become critical."
Hearing this, I was stunned. How must my heart have felt, knowing that he, from whom I felt I would die if separated even for a moment, was leaving?
"Tonight?"
"Yes—anyway, couldn't you come out quickly?"
At the end of his words, he let out a deep sigh. Following his words, I hurried outside.
Our feet moved towards that familiar place filled with pine trees.
"I'm sorry for making you come out so late," his words showed a much more subdued manner than before. And his demeanor also seemed different than before.
I loved him. But until that moment, I was pure and innocent. I knew how to love a man, but I knew nothing at all of a man's XX.
He pressed his burning lips to my face. I screamed loudly. But his forceful actions only grew more intense, and my body collapsed onto the grassy ground beneath the pine trees.
When I barely opened my eyes, the bright moon was setting behind the western hills, and the night dew had soaked the grass and my clothes damply.
That terrible young man—his beloved form from before had vanished somewhere, and I alone lay collapsed on the grassy ground.
The wind blowing from the sea passed through the pine forest, making my chilled body feel even colder.
The sound of the waves! The sound of insects! Everything seemed to sympathize with my misfortune.
When the summer resort visitors had all left and autumn arrived, I too left my aunt's house and returned to my hometown. But I was not the Sunja of old.
I was not my parents' innocent daughter.
As the days go by, one day, two days, my flesh wastes away, and my blood diminishes. Knowing that I am a body that will soon leave this world, yet as this summer approaches, the memories of this time last year stir in my head, and I have taken up my pen.
May 15th <32>

Tearful A Beach, Summer Has Come, But He Shows No Sign of Coming

Paek Yŏnsil

The first time I headed to A Beach was in the spring when I turned twenty. In the village, the rapeseed flowers were yellow, the barley fields had turned green, and the larks soared high, twittering. And the sea was blue. Thus, A village was a peaceful place, enough to be called a natural paradise.
Amidst this peace, I spent my days picking flowers, gathering greens, and strolling on the beach, recuperating after my illness.
Before I knew it, April passed, May went by, and the shadows of summer visitors seeking cool places away from the smoke and dust of the city began to appear one by two at A Beach. During that time, my health also recovered considerably compared to before.
It was a bright morning. The sea was blue, and the sky was azure.
On either side of the sand dunes, tents of white, yellow, and purple were stretched out in a line.
The way the tents fluttered in the wind and the way the sea waves created white crests—everything seemed to bless the vibrant youth.

T and I laughed. Breathing deeply the delicious air of the bright morning, we were so cheerful and happy that we laughed with all our might.

I came to know T one day not long after arriving at A Beach.
He was a friend of my friend's older brother. That's how we were first introduced. He used to go fishing every day in the stream that flowed in front of the village.
I had been taking my dog for walks along that riverbank before. As two young people who already knew each other met frequently in a quiet place, the flower of love bloomed there.
The two of us walked along the riverbank. And also amidst the pine forest... We would sometimes gaze blankly at the rippling flow of the river. And then we would let out long sighs for no reason.
In the meantime, June arrived, when the green foliage ripened.
It was a Sunday. My friend M, whom I hadn't met for a long time, came to visit from afar.
Since M was the friend I understood and loved the most, I told her everything about the relationship between T and me without hesitation.
Thus, the walks that the two of us used to take became walks for three, and when we went to the beach, the three of us went together.
M is a cheerful woman. She has a nature of saying whatever she thinks to anyone without hesitation. Tall with long legs, the physical beauty of her in a purple swimsuit was enough to tempt any man. T, too, was perhaps drawn to M's personality, the opposite of mine, and the charm of her abundant, voluptuous body.
In just one week, the relationship between those two surpassed the record set between T and me.
T and I had naturally even thought about marriage. Morning or evening, the two of us would talk about our plans after marriage. But the most untrustworthy thing in the world is a man's heart. I had a personality where I couldn't frankly speak my mind. Even though I clearly knew about their relationship, I couldn't say a single word to M or T, and I hid myself away from A Beach.
Until the spring I turned twenty, I was foolishly innocent about all things in life, but those without experience cannot know my soul, which plummeted headlong into the devil's den.
I was someone who shed tears, moved to sadness even by the sound of falling leaves in autumn, but now I am someone without even a single drop of tears. My once soft heart has become as cold and hard as ice.
L was the first man I knew since coming into this world. But since he has gone far away from me, how could I not be tormented?
I cannot bear not to glare with cursed eyes at the devil's mischief that tries to eradicate the joy of human existence in the universe.
The spring I turned twenty—from that time when I received a terrible wound in my heart until now, turning twenty-six, I have become a completely wicked woman.
The only ones who did not abandon me as I cried, tormented by unhappy fate, were my parents.
If there was even a thread of comfort for my wounded heart, it was the sincere—not false—sympathy of my parents.

I visited A Beach again. The A village I had visited five years ago! The blue sea and the sand dunes had not changed. And neither had the flowing river. Spring was the same in A village—A Beach—but my heart was not the heart of old. And the figures of T and M had also vanished. My heart, walking along the beach, felt as desolate as a traveler wandering in a vast desert.
My parents suggested I try learning painting while recuperating. So I too tried to liquidate the past and find a path to a new life. Spending autumn and welcoming spring with the sound of the waves, I merely struggled in vain to cultivate the path of art. But, but I still could not escape from the devil's den.
Art is life's joy—no, a momentary joy. It could not bring me eternal pleasure.
I longed for the springs of old once more, and I sought out all the places I used to go with him—the beach, the riverbank, the pine forest.
But it all merely creates futile records. I sometimes pictured T and M before my eyes.
And the son and daughter born between them—they have already become a father and a mother.
No matter how I try to forget, this feeling is always the same, what should I do?
I intend to spend my life cultivating the path of art, which holds no meaning for me, while both resenting and blessing T and M. <33>

That Friend's Whereabouts?

Im Kŭmok

That summer is already coming again. That summer that hurt my heart so terribly!!
It was the summer vacation when I was in the 4th grade. A letter arrived from a girl living near the beach, saying, "Since it's our last summer vacation, let's play to our heart's content." Was there any reason my strict parents would permit me to go to a bathing resort? My parents wouldn't let me go anywhere except school.
But Yŏngja's letter hadn't come just to me; it had also come to Suk-hŭi and Ŭn-hŭi.
I barely ate and pestered my father and mother. Saying I would paint the assignment due for the summer holidays—oil painting (油畵)—rather than go swimming.
Even my father, who had been so strict, finally gave his permission. Thus, one Sunday, Suk-hŭi, Ŭn-hŭi, and I, the three of us, went to Yŏngja's house.
As soon as we entered Yŏngja's house, the four of us wandered around the department stores in the streets and bought purple swimsuits and bathing caps. We jumped, laughed, and chattered as if some great happiness was about to befall us.
We didn't stay at Yŏngja's house but pitched a tent together by the beach and started playing house. Thus, the young women of marriageable age suppressed various dreams along with the sweet melancholy of youth beneath their consciousness and spent days that were far too bright, just like celestial maidens from heaven.
The four of us had the exact same color hats and swimsuits. When the four of us held hands together and headed towards the sea, we felt as though everyone was vacantly staring at our backs.
Only Yŏngja, living near the beach, swam well, but the other three of us couldn't even float when we entered the water. Still, since Ŭn-hŭi and Suk-hŭi had been athletic, they learned to swim in just a few days, but even after two weeks, I only knew how to splash around.
Therefore, finding no interest in swimming, I thought I'd rather try painting the drawing assigned for the summer vacation than go into the sea.
Thus, having obtained the understanding of my three friends, I started sketching them swimming from the beach. Drawing for an hour each day, after about three days, I was more than halfway done.
As it happened, many people passing by on the beach would stop to look, or some would come and stand there just to watch.
When people watched me, I felt embarrassed on the one hand, yet proud on the other. Why wouldn't I, an 18-year-old high school student, feel embarrassed being watched by many people?
It wasn't such a bright day. As the weather wasn't hot, there were fewer people on the beach.
Intending to finish the picture I was drawing, I persuaded my three friends and went out to the beach.
I continued wielding my brush, while my friends played like fish in the blue sea.
"You draw really well," a single compliment brushed past my ear, and I jerked my head up and looked behind me. A young man stood there. It wasn't the first time I'd been praised for drawing well, but for some reason, his single compliment made me incredibly happy. And I was so embarrassed I didn't know what to say, just hesitated, my face flushing, while the brush in my hand wouldn't move and only trembled.
The young man, rather feeling awkward himself, asked, "Why are you drawing?" and moved slightly away. Though I felt so embarrassed and my heart pounded, I felt disappointed when he moved away. When I started moving the brush again, he came closer once more.
He asked me where I lived, which school I attended, if I had much interest in painting, and so on, eventually asking everything from my age to when I was going home.
I just answered his questions with my head bowed, fiddling with the brush in my hand.
This became the opportunity for him and me to get to know each other, and I secretly met him alone several times in the morning or evening, without my three friends knowing.
That young man's name was KST. A well-proportioned physique with defined muscles, and a cheerful personality—he was in every way a modern man.
My three friends, noticing the connection between K and me, always called K a "modern man" in a teasing (히야까시) manner. And they pestered me to introduce K to them too. Among them, the tall and good-looking Yŏngja pestered the most.
I had a premonition that if I introduced K to them, he might leave me, so I tried my best not to create opportunities for them to meet.
However, my efforts were in vain, and one warm day, those three friends and K got to know each other.—Ŭn-hŭi, Suk-hŭi, Yŏngja—all three friends had personalities opposite to mine.
The cheerful K seemed even more cheerful and let out happier laughter when interacting with them compared to when he was with quiet me.
K, whom I used to meet alone, now met all four friends equally. When those cheerful girls chattered, talked, and laughed, I would just crouch in a corner, only looking at their faces. And I would try to read K's expression.
I tried hard to discern whether K loved me or Yŏngja, Suk-hŭi, or Ŭn-hŭi, but there was no way to know. Because K treated all four of us friends equally!! However, I also felt that perhaps he liked me less. Because I didn't talk or laugh like them, K sometimes said things like this:
"I dislike gloomy people. I dislike people who act prim," he would say sarcastically, as if directing the words at me.
It must have been the second day after I finished the drawing. Around four in the afternoon, Yŏngja put on her swimsuit and said,
"Since Kŭmok doesn't know how to swim, maybe she should just sleep in the tent," glancing at me.
Following her lead, Ŭn-hŭi also said,
"Yeah, do that. What's the point of going out?" The three looked at each other and laughed. Then, wearing the exact same swimsuits, they went out of the tent. Peeking through the tent flap at their identical figures from behind, I shed hot tears, drop by drop.
The purple swimsuit placed alongside the three friends' swimsuits—only mine lay thrown in a corner. <34>

**Summary**

This article compiles three personal accounts from young Korean women (Yu Sunja, Paek Yŏnsil, Im Kŭmok) detailing their romantic encounters and subsequent heartbreaks experienced at summer resorts (Wŏnsan's Myŏngsa Simni beach and an anonymized 'A Beach') during the early 1930s. Yu Sunja recounts being seduced and abandoned by a man she met while recuperating from illness, leading to deep emotional scars. Paek Yŏnsil describes how her beloved T was drawn away by her more vivacious friend M, leaving her embittered and cynical. Im Kŭmok relates feeling excluded and ultimately rejected when the young man she met while painting was introduced to her three cheerful friends, who seemed to capture his attention more effectively. All three narratives explore themes of burgeoning female desire, the excitement and dangers of new social interactions at holiday spots, betrayal, lost innocence, and the lasting emotional impact of these experiences within the societal constraints of the time.

From a historical perspective, these accounts are valuable for illustrating the changing social dynamics and anxieties in colonial Korea. The emergence of summer resorts like Wŏnsan beach as spaces for leisure and interaction between unmarried young men and women was a relatively new phenomenon. The stories reveal the personal struggles accompanying these changes, particularly concerning female chastity ("貞操混線" - confusion of chastity), vulnerability, and the emotional consequences of relationships that transgressed or tested traditional norms. The mention of tuberculosis (폐병) also reflects a significant public health issue of the era. Historians can gain insight into the personal lives, emotional worlds, and social pressures faced by young, educated or middle-class Korean women during this period.

明沙十里의 하로밤, 해당화는 아직도 곱게 피건만

柳順子

나는 三千里를 매월 읽슴니다. 그리고 實話를 더욱 흥미잇게 읽는담니다.
나는 언제나 세상에 나 혼자만 불행한 줄 알엇스며 나 혼자만 번민 고통이 심한 줄 알엇나이다. 그럼으로 나는 아직껏 누구에게나 나의 숨은 고통 숨은 번민을 호소한 적이 업섯슴니다.
호소한다면 그 때엔 세상에서 버림 밧은 녀성이 될가바서도.
그리 오라지도 안은 때-작년 여름-임니다. 나는 선천적으로 몸이 몹시 쇠약하게 생겻슴으로 열여름해 동안을 병업시 지난 적이 별노 업슬 것임니다. 삼년전에 페병이라는 선고를 밧고 단이든 학교에도 중도 퇴학한 후 해마당 여름이면 元山게신 아즈머님집에 가서 정양을 하게 되엿드람니다.

작년 여름에도 또 아즈머님 집에 가섯지요. 내가 지금 이럿케 불행한 처지에 잇게됨은 우리 부모가 남달니 나를 사랑하엿기 때문인가도 십슴니다. 그러나 아모리 아즈머니집에 가고 십허도 부모가 보내주시지를 안엇드면 지금의 나는 아모 번민도 고통도 업슬 것이겟지요. 생각하면 원통하고 분함니다.
나는 매일 아츰 네시쯤 되여서 명사에 해변으로 나갓슴니다.

아츰마당 만나는 그 남자-식컴엇케 탄 굵직한 팔, 넓게 생긴 가슴, 검은 해수복 우에 낫타난 풍해 보이는 선, 그리고 남자답은 체격에 미남형의 얼골-어느 것이나 나의 머리에 깁피 백여젓담니다.
그 남자는 그날 아참에도 물결뛰는 푸른 바다에서 헤염치고 잇섯슴니다. 언제나 만나는 그엿스나 그날 아츰의 그는 더구나 내 가삼을 울넝거리게 만드럿슴니다. 내 얼골이 붉어젓슴은 처녀의 가슴에 피여오르는 언정때문이엿나바요.
그는 바다에서 뛰여 나오더니다. 그래서 이슬에 저즌 해당화 봉오리를 만지면서 가삼을 태우는 내겻흐로 아모말 업시 차저오더니다.
사방은 고요햇슴니다. 밝기 시작한 예명의 하늘엔 빤짝이는 별들이 하나 둘 살어지고 보얏케  보이는 바다에 가는 파도가 살낭살낭 밀여 들어왓슴니다. 파-란 입사귀 속에 빨갓케 물타는 듯 십흔 해당화는 새벽이슬에 폭 저저서 한결 더 곱게 보혓슴니다. 「용서하십시요」의 한마듸 말은 그 남자의 말이엿슴니다. 순진한 내 가삼을 태워주든 그엿스나 정작 겻헤 오게되니 원일인지 몸시 무서웟슴니다. 그래서 나는 아모 대답도 업섯지요. 그 청년의 확실히 빗나 보헛슴니다.

의외에 긔희가 언제나 인생으 미경에 빳트린 그것인가바요. 그 후로 그와 나는 퍽으나 친밀한 관게를 맷게 되엿슴니다. 그것이 나의 운명을 저해하는 긔희임을 누가 알엇슴닛가.
매일 아참 네시-나는 아즈머니집 대문을 살작이 열고 명사의 해변으로 달여나가는 것이 습관이 되엿슴니다. 모래언덕 그늘에는 언제나 그가 안저서 기다리고 잇섯슴니다.
「안령하심닛가」하고 우슴과 함끠 나오는 쾌활한 그의 목소리는 새벽의 바닷가의 매운 공긔를 진동식혓슴니다.
나는 가늘고 떨니는 목소리로 「네. 안령하심닛가. 벌서 오섯슴니다」하는 내 말에 그는
「웨 안됏슴니가?」하고 언제나 놀니기를 조와햇담니다.
날이 갈사록 정열의 불길이 타오를 뿐이엿슴니다. 그리하야 저녁이나 아츰이나 그와 나는 해변가를 산보하기 보다 욱어진 푸를 송림 사이에서 붉은 꿈이라고 할까요. 하여간 둘이 속삭이는 것을 퍽도 즐기엿담니다. 송림 사이는 너무나 조용하여서 그와 나에게 베프러준 곳갓치도 생각되엿슴니다.

어느날 전녁이엇슴니다. 정신업시 뛰여단이는 나의 모양을 눈치채섯든지 저녁엔 나가지 말고 아참에도 날이 새거든 나가라는 선고를 밧엇담니다. 그러나 하로밤이라도 그를 못만나면 밋칠 듯 십헛서요. 아즈머니가 주무시기만 기다리고 안저 잇섯슴니다. 겨우 밤 열시가 되니 아즈머님은 자리에 누엇더이다. 그가 기다릴 것을 생각하고 불이낫케 화장을 하고 잇는데 나 잇는 방 뒷창을 「녹크」하는 소리가 들엿슴니다. 문을 열고 보니 그는 내가 사랑하는 그 청년이엿슴니다.
「웬일이심닛가? 지금 나갈여고 함니다」라고 목소리를 죽여가면서 하는 내 말에 대답도 업시
「나는 오늘 전녁에 집에 감니다. 어머님의 병환이 급하서서요」
드르니 기가 막혓슴니다. 잠시라도 떠러저 잇스면 죽을 것 갓흔 그가 떠난다니 나의 가삼이 엇더하엿겟슴닛가.
「오늘 전녁에요?」
「네- 하여간 속히 나오실 수 업슬까요?」
하는 말끗헤 그는 한숨을 휘-내쉬여 버렷슴니다. 나는 그의 말대로 밧부게 문박게 나갓슴니다.
둘의 발은 날마당 가는 송림이 가득찬 그 곳으로 옴겨젓습니다.
「이럿케 늣게 나오시게 하여서 미안함니다.」라고 하는 그의 말은 전보담 엇더케도 점잣은 양을 보혀주는지요. 그리고 그의 태도도 전보담 달느게 보혓슴니다.
나는 그를 사랑하엿슴니다. 그러나 그 때까지도 순결무구한 나엿슴니다. 남자를 사랑할 줄은 알엇스나 남자의 XX는 조곰도 몰낫슴니다.
그는 불타는 듯한 입술을 나의 얼골에 대엿담니다. 나는 소리를 놉히 질넛슴니다. 그러나 그의 강폭한 행동은 점점 더 심해갈 뿐이엿스니 나의 몸은 송림 아래 풀밧 우에 쓰러지고 말엇슬 뿐임니다.
내가 눈을 겨우 떠슬 적엔 밝은 달님이 서산에 기우러지고 밤이슬이 풀밧과 내 옷을 축축하게 적시게한 때엿슴니다.
그 몹슬 청년-녯날의 사랑하든 그의 자태는 어듸인지 사러저 버리고 혼자만이 풀밧 우에 쓰러젓섯슴니다.
바다로부터 부러오는 바람은 송림 사이를 통해서 싸늘하게 식은 내 몸덩이를 더 한층 차게 만들엇슴니다.
파도소래! 벌네소래! 어느 것이나 나의 비운을 동정하는 듯 십헛슴니다.
피서지의 나그내도 다-가버리고 가을철이 차저왓을 때 나도 아즈머님집을 따나서 고향으로 도라왓섯슴니다. 그러나 나는 녯날의 순자가 안니엿슴니다.
부모님의 순진한 딸이 안니엿슴니다.
하로 잇흘 시일이 갈사록 내 살점은 말너가고 내 피는 적어감니다. 얼마안되면 세상을 버릴 몸인줄 알면서도 이 여름이 닥처오니 작년의 이때가 머리에서 요동하여서 붓을 들엇나이다.

눈물의 A海邊, 여름은 왓것만 그이만은 올 줄 몰나

白蓮實

내가 처음으로 A해변을 향하게 된 때는 스므살되는 봄이엿슴니다. 마을엔 뱃추꼿이 누르고 보리밧치 푸르러젓스며  종달새는 놉히 떠 재글거럿슴니다. 그리고 바다는 푸르럿슴니다. 그리하여 A마을은 자연의 락원이라고 할 만큼 평화한 곳이엿슴니다.

명랑한 아참이엿슴니다. 바다는 푸르고 하늘은 파-랫슴니다.
모래언덕 이쪽저쪽엔 힌색 누른색 자쥬빗 등의 천막이 쭉 느려서 잇섯슴니다.
천막들이 바람에 날니는 양과 바다의 물결이 힌 파도를 짓는 양이 모-도가 젊은 청춘을 위하야 축복하야 주는 듯 십헛나이다.

T와 나는 우섯슴니다. 명랑한 아츰에 맛잇는 공긔를 흠벅 드레마시면서 너무나 유쾌하고 줄거워서 힘끗 우섯슴니다.
