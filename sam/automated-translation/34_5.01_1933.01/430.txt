**Body Text Translation:**
University Students' Views on Ideology, Observations of American University Students

It is quite interesting, and indeed significant, to consider what thoughts young university students have about the social phenomena of their time. A professor at North Carolina State University compiled and published the results of a survey on the thoughts of 258 students at that university. This is fascinating both for understanding the general psychology of young people and for seeing the United States (米國) from a different angle.

A indicates affirmation of the presented question.
B indicates negation of the question.
C indicates unable to answer. (The numbers indicate the percentage out of the 258 students.)

Was Christ truly resurrected from the dead?
(A 69.7 B 8.6 C 21.7)
It seems even among Christians in present-day Korea (朝鮮), there are probably few who think Christ was resurrected, yet American university students think this way.
That only 8.6 percent say he was not resurrected from the dead is something we cannot help but see as quite astonishing.

Was humankind created by a special act of God?
(A 38.8 B 36 C 25.2 )
It is quite peculiar that the numbers for A and B are nearly identical. And it is also exceedingly strange that 25.2 percent were unable to answer.
One becomes curious about what has happened to Darwin's (따윈) theory of evolution in America (米國).

When there is a contradiction between religion and modern science, does religion take <100> precedence?
(A 25.2 B 37.6 C 37.2)
Similar to the above, it is quite unexpected that so many people could not give a definite answer.

Is it acceptable for young unmarried men and women to go driving every night without a chaperone (隨付者)?
(A 51.9 B 39.1 C 8.9)
What would happen if this question were posed to students at specialized schools in Korea (朝鮮)?

Does war only serve the interests (益權) of a few certain groups (OO家) and certain groups (OO家)?
(A 64 B 28.2 C 7.8)
This is the clearest question reflecting contemporary thought among these young students.

Is the current popular clothing system better for social tranquility than the clothing of 50 years ago?
(A 64.4 B 27.1 C 8.5)
Clothing from 50 years ago refers to those long, flowing clothes from the era of Napoleon III (라포레온 3세). However, these university students believe that current clothing does not disrupt order when considering social tranquility.

Did the United States (米國) prosper more under the rule of the Republican Party (共和黨)?
(A 24.8 B 45.4 C 29.8)
Very few people affirm the rule of the Republican Party (共和黨). Also, few people consider this a question. Might the election of Mr. Roosevelt of the Democratic Party (民主黨) this time be related to this?

Our government is based on financial interests, not on morality (道徵的).
(A 65.5 B 27.1 C 7.4)
If, indeed, there are no governments or politicians in America (米國) acting morally, this is perhaps something for the rulers (위정자) of America (米國) to reflect upon.<101>

Are movies and dancing a good trend for everyone?
(A 80.2 B 12.8 C 7.0)
American university students say dancing and movies are good for everyone. How many young people in Korea (朝鮮), then, truly understand dancing properly?

If a marriage is unhappy, is it acceptable to divorce even if there is no infidelity by the wife?
(A 84.9 B 11.3 C 3.9)
American men speak out so boldly and frankly. How does this answer compare to the responses on other religious items? <102>

**Summary**

This 1933 article reports on a survey of 258 North Carolina State University students regarding their views on religion (Christ's resurrection, creationism, science vs. religion), social customs (dating without chaperones, modern clothing), politics (war profiteering, Republican vs. Democrat prosperity, government morality), and personal life (movies, dancing, divorce). The author expresses surprise at the high level of traditional religious belief among American students, contrasts their liberal views on dating and especially divorce with implied Korean norms, and notes the students' critical stance on war profiteering and the perceived lack of morality in government, linking political dissatisfaction to the recent election of Roosevelt. For historians, the article is interesting as it shows a Korean intellectual engaging with Western (specifically American) social and political thought during the colonial period. It uses American student opinions as a benchmark, implicitly inviting comparison with Korean society and revealing contemporary Korean curiosity about modernity, social change, and political critique, albeit viewed through a foreign lens. The commentary highlights perceived contradictions in American society (e.g., religious belief vs. liberal divorce views) and subtly reflects on Korean social norms.
