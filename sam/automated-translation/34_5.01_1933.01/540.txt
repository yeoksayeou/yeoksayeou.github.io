Sea Nocturne, Modern English Poetry Scene
						Edŭga P'oksol-

You did not come... ...
Oh, I did not know the night could be so quiet
And the large lone star grew with hope.
The wave-signed sea cried out beyond the boiling sandbar (沙洲)
And the powerless boat light glittered aimlessly, pitifully.

You... ...but you did not come... ...
The wind stopped, and the seagull that had lost its calm cried toward the sea.
A sweet, desolate fragrance flowed... ...a long procession of human voices... ...
A person wearing a chang'ot (장옷)
Laughed childishly. Darkness was
Returning the past joyful ecstasy... ...but you did not come. -End- <107>

**Summary**

The poem expresses a mood of melancholic longing and disappointment. The speaker waits by the sea at night for someone who ultimately does not arrive. The imagery contrasts the quiet night and a hopeful star with the restless sea, a pitiful boat light, crying seagulls, and distant human sounds, culminating in the return of past memories brought by the darkness, yet overshadowed by the absence of the awaited person. From a historical perspective, the publication of a piece titled "Modern English Poetry Scene" and attributed to a Western-sounding name (Edgar Foxall?) like "Edŭga P'oksol-" in a prominent Korean magazine in 1933 is significant. It demonstrates the interest among Korean intellectuals and the reading public in contemporary Western literature, even under Japanese colonial rule. It reflects efforts to engage with global cultural trends and introduce foreign artistic expressions, potentially serving as both inspiration and a subtle assertion of cultural identity beyond the immediate colonial sphere.
