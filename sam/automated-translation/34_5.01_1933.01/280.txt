Conversation Room

A Police Officer Who Scolded the President

In the newly emerged Spanish Republic (西班牙共和國), there is a world-renowned, brave traffic policeman, a famous attraction of San Sebastián City (산·세바스치안 市) in Spain (西班牙). This is a recent story.

Formerly a man of prison (囹圄), now currently President Alcalá-Zamora (알카라·사모라), was riding in a car together with Foreign Minister Durueta (듀르에타 씨). While driving through the streets of San Sebastián (산세바스치안 街), he looked left and right incorrectly and was about to proceed when suddenly there was an officer who called out "Stop!"

"Stop!"

"It's the President and the Minister."

"Regardless of who it is, one must not violate traffic regulations."

And the following exchange is truly witty.

"Officer, what is your name?"
This was Foreign Minister Durueta's question.

"Pan Cordero." (Cordero means 'lamb'.)

"No, you are not a Cordero (lamb), but a tiger." <82>

**Summary**

This short anecdote relates a story from the relatively new Spanish Republic where a traffic policeman in San Sebastián stopped and admonished President Alcalá-Zamora and Foreign Minister Durueta for a traffic violation, strictly enforcing the law regardless of their high status. The Foreign Minister humorously remarked that the officer, whose name meant 'lamb', was actually a 'tiger'. For historians studying colonial Korea, this piece is interesting as it presents an example of the rule of law and an ordinary official's courage in a foreign (Western) context. This could implicitly contrast with the realities of power dynamics and law enforcement under Japanese colonial rule in Korea, potentially serving as a subtle commentary or aspirational example for readers.
