**Body Text Translation:**
Fifty Jeon Silver Coin
						Kim Il-yŏp

It was by the stream in Mugyo-chō.
Na-hŭi met Man'gap, who occasionally smoothed out the unasked-for wrinkles that life’s hardships had deeply etched onto Sumyŏng's face.

Today, Sumyŏng had carried his A-frame carrier (jige) all day at the Paeogae marketplace, wandering from corner to corner, shivering with cold and hunger, his efforts proving futile. Finally, in the evening, he happened to get a job carrying the bag of a customer heading to the countryside, earning 50 jeon for the trip to Namdaemun Station. He proudly showed off the 50-jeon silver coin.

"You've made a tidy sum just as the sun's setting. Treat me to a drink for your good luck—buy me a drink!"
Just as Man'gap, intending to offer a friendly smile, slapped the hand holding the 50-jeon silver coin—clang!—the coin struck the bridge railing and fell into the stream.

"Oops!"
"Aack!"
As if by agreement, both men leaped down into the stream.

Instead of much water flowing, the stream was piled high with heaps of filthy trash, murky and black, making it impossible to see where the silver coin had fallen. Moreover, with dusk approaching, the two men, still wearing their soggy shoes, stepped into the stream and began to grope around under the bridge, downstream, and upstream. Each time their fingertips touched a shiny piece of broken pottery or a round, smooth pebble, they startled with hope, but ultimately, the silver coin was not found.

"Oh dear, oh dear," Man'gap lamented. How must Sumyŏng's heart have felt as he sighed softly, almost inaudibly to Man'gap?
Holding the endlessly precious silver coin in his hand, he had imagined, "If only I could earn like this every other day, my wife could shed her worn-out rubber shoes, I wouldn't have to bum tobacco off Man'gap, and my son—who cried and threw a tantrum, refusing to return the hat he snatched from a neighborhood child—could get his own hat..." All these thoughts had turned to froth.

"It can't be helped! Let's just go—" As Sumyŏng finally uttered words of despair and climbed back onto the bridge, Man'gap said,
"You go on ahead! I'll look a little longer—"

As Sumyŏng straightened his bent back and crossed the Paksŏk pass, he heard Man'gap's footsteps silently following behind. Sumyŏng's hope, "Perhaps he found it!" was dashed.

"Why are you so late!"
Hearing his wife's voice buzzing in his ear, Sumyŏng finally realized he had reached his own shack in Sŏngbuk-tong.
He plopped down in the warmer part of the room (araenmok) and leaned against the wall. The image of the single, gleaming silver coin stuck somewhere in the stream reappeared vividly in Sumyŏng's eyes. Just as he reached out to grab it, he saw it was only the torn floor paper.

His wife brought in the dinner table: steamed *agwi* sprouts (아기아순-나물) she had picked, boiled, and dried herself, soybean paste stew (doenjang jjigae), and a heaping bowl of yellowish rice.
The wife had expected her husband to be so hungry he wouldn't leave a single grain, but seeing the bowl come back still full of rice, her worry for her husband who couldn't even eat outweighed any relief about leftover food.
"Are you unwell somewhere?"
"... ..."
Seeing no reaction from her husband, whose expressionless face was gloomier than the color of dirt, as if she were talking to an earthen wall, she couldn't possibly ask about his earnings for the day.<111>
After eating all the leftover rice for the sake of her nursing child, the wife cleared the dishes and came back in to find her husband turned away, seemingly asleep.

"Fifty jeon—where is it—fifty jeon..."
Hearing her husband mutter this for the third time, the wife shook his body with her hand.
"Why are you talking so much in your sleep?"
"Ugh—"
Groaning, her husband simply turned back over. As his foot brushed against his wife's calf, it felt cold.
The wife had only managed to boil a bowl of rice on the brazier using fallen leaves she had gathered herself. She couldn't remember the last time there had been any warmth in the room, so the cold was real. But if it was already this cold, how would the three of them, including the baby, survive the winter solstice month? Worried, the wife lay with her eyes wide open. The morning star, shining through a crack in the wall, seemed to threaten, "Forget that worry, the time to cook breakfast is near! What will you do?"

"Fifty jeon, where is it—really, fifty jeon, where is it!"
Her husband's anguished cry was heard once more. (End) <112>

**Summary**

The story depicts a day in the life of Sumyŏng, a poor laborer in colonial Seoul. After a fruitless day, he finally earns a 50-jeon silver coin, a significant sum that sparks dreams of minor comforts for his family. However, the coin is accidentally knocked into a filthy stream and lost despite a desperate search with his friend Man'gap. Sumyŏng returns home dejected, unable to eat or sleep, haunted by the loss, epitomized by his sleep-talking cries for the lost money, highlighting the crushing weight of poverty.

This story is revealing of Korean colonial history as it vividly portrays the extreme poverty and daily struggles of the urban lower class under Japanese rule. The 50-jeon coin, while small, represents a chance for slight relief (new shoes, a hat for his son) that is tragically lost, emphasizing the precariousness of life and the lack of economic opportunity for many Koreans. Historians might find the depiction of specific locations (Mugyo-chō, Paeogae Market, Namdaemun Station, Sŏngbuk-tong), the description of labor (jige carrier), living conditions (unheated room, simple food), and the psychological impact of poverty particularly interesting for understanding the social realities of the era.

						金一葉
武橋町 개천가엿다.
나희가 주지안은 주름쌀을 고생사리에서 흠신 밧아가진 壽命의 얼골도 각금 펴질 기회를 주는 萬甲이를 만낫다.
壽命이는 오날 배오개장터에서 지게를 지고 종일 이 구석, 저 구석으로 치위와 주림에 떨며 헛거름을 치다가 저녁때에 맛참 시골가는 손의 가방을 맛하 가지게 되여 南大門역까지에 50전 삭을 밧게 되엿다고 자랑삼아 50전 은화를 내여 드럿다.
「해질녁에 톡톡한 버리를 하엿네 그려. 신수턱으로 한 잔 내게 ―한 잔 내!」
50전 은화를 든-손을 탁-치며 萬甲이도 다정한 우슴을 띄우려고 할 차에- 은화는 쨍그렁-다리 란간에 부딋고 갯천으로 떠러저 버렷다.
「앗차!」
「엑케!」
두 사람은 약속이나 한 듯이 함께 개천으로 나리뛰엿다.
물이 만히 흐르지 안는 대신에 개천은 지저분한 쓰러기가 무덱이 무덱이 싸히고 걸따랏코 씩껌해서 은전은 어느 곳에 떠러젓는지 보이지를 안엿다. 더구나 땅검이가 지랴는 해질 녁이랴 두 사람은 질벅질벅 신을 신은 채 개천으로 드러가 다리 밋흐로 아래로 우로 주물느기 시작하엿다. 두 사람은 빤지르한 사금파리 동글남작한 돌짜개에 손끗이 다을 때마다 깜작깜작 놀나며 반가워햇으나 맛참내 은화는 안 집혓다.
「거-참 거-참」하고 탄식하는 萬甲이에게 들니지 안으리만치 가늘게 한숨을 쉬는 壽命이의 가슴은 엇떠하랴.
대견하기 끗이 업는 은전을 손에 들자 오날갓흔 버리가 하루 걸너만 잇서도 안해에 뚜러진 고무신을 벗기게 되고 萬甲이의 남배 신세도-동리ㅅ집 아해의 모자를 빼아사 쓰고 안 준다고 악쓰고 울든 아들의 모자도」하고 궁리하든 것의 모다 물겁품으로 도라갓슴이랴.
「하는 수 업네! 그만 가세―」 맛참내 절망의 소리를 치고 다리로 올나오는 壽命이에게 萬甲이는.
「몬저 가게! 내 좀더-차저볼께」

壽命이가 꼬부라지는 허리를 펴가며 박석고개를 넘어 올 때 말업시 뒤를 따르는 萬甲의 발갓최 소리를 듯고 「행여나!」하고 바라든 壽命이의 바람은 끈어지고 말엇다!
「왜 이럿케 느젓수!」
壽命이는 잉-하고 귓가에 부드치는 안해의 목소리가 들니자 비로소 城北洞 자기 오막사리 집에 까저 니르른 것을 알엇다.
아랜목에 털석 안저 몸을 벽에 기대이자 개천 어느 구석엔지 박히여 잇는 반작반작 빗나는 은전 한 푼이 壽命의 눈에 또다시 환하게 낫타낫다. 금방 집어낼나고 보니 뚜러진 방바닥이엿다.
안해가 손수 뜨더서 삷마 말녀 두엇든 아기아순-나물에 된장찌개하고 누런 밥을 웃뚝이 담아논-밥상을 안해가 가지고 드러왓다.
안해는 남편이 허기가 저서 한술도 안 남기려니 햇드니 밥이 그득이 남아 나온 것의 다행하다는 이 보다는 밥도 못 먹는 남편의 걱정이 더하여.
「어듸 편치 안으시우?」
「... ...」
흙 빗보다도 침침한 표정업는 얼골에 흙담 처다 보고 말하는 듯이 반응이 업는 남편에게 오날의 버리에 대한 말은 더구나 무를 수가＜111＞ 업엇다.
안해는 어린것의 젓을 위하야 남편의 남긴 밥을 다 먹고 나서 설거질을 하고 드러오니 남편은 자는지 도라누어 잇섯다.

「돈 50전이-어듸야-돈 50전이」
남편의 이런 소리를 세번재나 드른 안해는 손으로 남편의 몸을 흔들며.
「웬 잠고대를 그리 하시우?」
「끙-」
알는 소리를 하고는 그냥 도라 눕는 남편의 발이 안해의 좀아리를 스치자 선뜻하엿다.
안해가 손수 글거온 가랑닙으로 화덕에 밥만 한 그릇 끄렷고 방에 불끼한 지가 언제인지 모르니 추을 것은 사실이지만 벌서 이럿케 추워오니 동지ㅅ달에는 어린것하고 세 식구가 엇떠케나 살아갈가 하고 걱정되는 안해는 눈이 반반하여저서 말둥말둥 뜨고 잇는데 벽 틈으로 빗치여 오는 샛별이 「그 걱정보다도 아츰밤 질 시간이 각가워오는데 엇지할테야!」하고 위협하는 듯 하엿다.
「돈 50전이 어듸야―글세 돈 50전이 어듸야!」
남편의 비통한 부르지즘이 또다시 들엿다.  (끗) ＜112＞
