The Sorrow of the Intellectual Woman

The Sorrow of the Female Teacher
By Song X-chu (宋X珠)

The story I write below is not my own story, but the story of Miss Yi XX, who works with me at the same school.
After reading this story, I wonder if you, the readers, will sympathize with Miss Yi, or if you will criticize her like the other teachers at our school.
One thing I want to say here is that people are not gods, and thus there are many instances where people deceive others and deceive themselves. I particularly want to criticize those who criticize Miss Yi for deceiving her own conscience.<85>

Miss Yi XX is a twenty-one-year-old maiden this year. She was newly appointed to this school this spring and came to work alongside me. She is a very good person, humanly speaking. However, as she is still young and inexperienced, she sometimes makes mistakes, which occasionally become a topic of gossip among the other teachers at the school.

The story begins here. Since you readers have likely all attended elementary school, you will probably read this story with interest.
At the end of the school term, elementary schools usually have what is called the principal's examination, where the principal sets the test questions and examines the students. At our school, there is always a principal's exam at the end of the term. Thus, around mid-June, like in previous years, the principal's exam was held. As this was the first time Miss Yi XX had experienced this, it seems that in her youthful thinking, she was very anxious about wanting the results of the class she was in charge of to be good.

Finally, the day of the exam (the principal's exam) arrived, and Mr. H, a male teacher who did not get along well with her, happened to be assigned to supervise Miss Yi XX's class. The reason Mr. H and Miss Yi did not get along was that Mr. H's behavior towards Miss Yi had been strange, and Miss Yi had confronted him about it. Since Mr. H, having been embarrassed once, was angry and just looking for an opportunity to get revenge, it was predictable that some sort of issue would arise there.

As soon as the dismissal bell rang, Mr. H rushed into the office and loudly recounted the following story:
"Miss Yi's class is the top rank! I've already graded everything, and there isn't even a score above 20.
Miss Yi was quite resourceful, you know. For some reason, she rearranged the children who were seated by height, mixing them up so that the capable children sat with the less capable ones and helped them."
"I learned about a woman's cowardly behavior for the first time," he proclaimed*, spreading the story. What must Miss Yi XX's face have looked like? I just watched Mr. H's undignified behavior blankly.
Miss Yi was so ashamed and resentful that she didn't know what to do, and just spent the whole day with her head down on her desk.

The next day, Miss Yi was called into the principal's office. The principal, who had already recognized Miss Yi's innocence, merely warned her not to cause such talk again in the future and let it go. But the world is so harsh that people do not want to cover up others' mistakes. In the office—the whispering here and the sounds of ridicule there—even I, as a third party, felt angry listening to it. Young and soft-hearted Miss Yi couldn't regain her spirits, glancing furtively at the whispering eyes of the other teachers.
Her face gradually lost its color, and her once bright and innocent personality became only gloomy.
One day during lunchtime, she told me this: "Miss Song! Coming to school in the morning is more dreadful than dying. I've thought about quitting many times, but my circumstances don't allow it, so I can't do that either. Yesterday, too, during lunch break, I came out here and was sitting blankly when I saw the children playing amongst themselves. For some reason, they ganged up on one child, saying they wouldn't play with her, and chased her out of their group. I don't know how pitiful and sorry I felt for that child. It's just like my situation these days," she said, tears rolling down her cheeks drop by drop.
Thinking of the innocent nature of the naive Miss Yi as endearing, I wanted to shout out and ask who was making this pitiful person cry.<86>

The Profile of the Cowardly S
By Female Office Worker Son Chŏngae (孫貞愛)

Ah, those splendid school days! I smoothly finished four years at a certain girls' high school in Kyŏngsŏng, and that spring, in April, I began working at a certain company (某會社).
Moved from the classroom to the office—like a sparrow leaving the nest, completely ignorant of the rough waves of the world—I found myself working mixed among people whose faces I didn't know. My heart, thrown into real society still holding the innocent heart of a schoolgirl, dreamt dream-like fantasies, yet sometimes, in moments of loneliness, I felt like crying.
Since I was the only woman at the company where I worked, my heart felt even more gloomy.
The talk I heard every day was filled only with an "erotic" (『에로』) flavor, and among the male employees, only an atmosphere of jealousy and strife seemed to be full.
However, should I say that the human heart changes? Even my gloomy heart came to experience days of joy.

There was someone who joined the company the day after I did; he was Mr. S, a handsome young man who had just finished at a certain technical college (某-專門學校) that spring.
Without saying anything, I often found myself looking at Mr. S, who was intently writing something in a ledger. He always showed a composed demeanor. One day—it was during lunchtime—I saw him pull out a Western book (洋書) from his pocket and read it, expressing a melancholic emotion.
"Mr. S! What book is that?"
"Oh, it's nothing special."
He finished his simple reply and started reading the book again, and my heart was drawn to him even more. And so, he and I somehow became friends. When I was busy, he would stay late to help me with my work and kindly and meticulously talked to me about various things. There, the two of us completely forgot the fatigue of the day, and consequently, we just waited for the morning commuting time to arrive each day. The world seemed very bright, and humans seemed happy. S and I—our passion grew day by day, and the two young people felt as if everything in the world existed for us.
When I arrived at work in the morning, a red flower—a dahlia—was placed on my desk.
With a maiden's pure heart, without a second thought, I touched my lips to the dahlia. I don't know how moved I was by Mr. S's feelings as I pressed my lips to that dahlia.
"Do you love flowers so much?" a cheerful voice came from the left as the window opened, and for some reason, I realized my face was blushing without my knowing. "Do you know the flower language of the dahlia?"
He looked at my face intently and asked quietly. Holding a blade of grass to my lips, my heart pounding secretly, I couldn't help but smile. He asked me, then brought his lips to the dahlia blossom. To the very blossom my lips had touched!
From then on, every other day, every third day, he brought me various kinds of flowers.
"In my garden, flowers like these bloom intoxicatingly. Won't you <87> come visit sometime?"
At these words from him, I couldn't keep my heart still. The following Sunday, I visited Mr. S's house for the first time.
In the garden, many nameless flowers, bathed in the July sun, were blooming. His room, just as I had imagined, was decorated in an artistic style.
The paintings he said he had drawn himself also delighted me, and his talk about literature and music stirred my heart even more.
Ah! How could I have known, unless I were a God, that this was the opportunity that would plunge my body into a trap! Completely drawn to Mr. S's masculine illusion, I became a person living for Mr. S. Whether at the company, on the street (街頭), or at home, my heart was filled only with S.

"Would you like to go on a trip next Sunday?"
"A trip, you say?"
"No, I mean just take a day off and come back. If we go to XX, there are many fun things to do. We can go on Sunday and leave there early the next morning, so we can arrive without being late for work."
Looking into his keen eyes, I simply replied with the single phrase, "I will do that."
Even thinking about it now, it was an event frustrating enough to bring tears.
That day—Sunday—we boarded a car and went to XX. Feeling happy as his warm hand rested on my shoulder (억개), I tried hard to suppress the desperately pounding emotion in my heart.
The night of ruin passed beautifully, and S and I went to work the next morning just like always.
Somehow, perhaps having already learned of our relationship, the company employees watched us with sharp eyes of jealousy. And they strongly criticized S and me. Because of this, S's attitude towards me gradually began to change. Each time, I couldn't help but resent his effeminate behavior.
One day during lunchtime, the president gave me a sum of money and ordered me to resign. Without any reply, I left the spot. I picked up my pen many times to write him letters, but there was not even a single reply. How could I have known that his heart, which had promised so firmly, would change like that?
Ah! A wounded maiden! Picturing his cowardly profile before my eyes, I wondered how the world could be so futile. As my mother said, if I hadn't become a working woman, I wouldn't be feeling this sorrow today, I regret. <88>

**Summary**

This article presents two short narratives under the theme "The Sorrow of the Intellectual Woman," depicting the hardships faced by educated women in early 1930s Korea. The first story, by Song X-chu, recounts the experience of a young, naive female teacher, Miss Yi, who is ostracized and contemplates quitting after being falsely accused by a male colleague of helping students cheat on an exam. The second story, by Son Chŏngae, is a personal account of a young woman starting her first office job after graduation, falling for a male colleague, S, who charms her but ultimately exploits her trust, leading to a brief affair, social condemnation at work, and her eventual dismissal, followed by his abandonment. Both stories highlight the vulnerability, societal judgment, and professional challenges encountered by women entering new roles in education and the workplace during this period.

Historians might find these accounts interesting for their portrayal of the specific social and professional difficulties faced by 'modern' or 'intellectual' women (인테리 여성) in colonial Korea. They offer insights into workplace dynamics, gender relations (including male resentment and exploitation), the fragility of women's positions in new professional fields, and the personal emotional toll (비애) of navigating societal expectations and prejudices. The mention of "erotic" talk in the office and the swift condemnation leading to dismissal reveals prevailing moral codes and the precariousness of female employment.

卑怯한 S의 푸로필
女事務員 孫貞愛

아! 호화롭든 학창시대여! 나는 4년이란 그동안에 경성 모 고등보통학교를 순조롭게 맛치고 그 봄 4월에 某회사에 근무하게 되엿슴니다.
교실에서 사무소에 옴겨진―세상의 파란이라고 도모지 모르는 둥이를 떠난 참새처럼 얼골모를 사람들틈에 석겨서 일보게 되엿담니다. 학창시대의 순진스런 처녀의 맘 그대로 현실사회에 던저진 내맘은 꿈갓흔 상상을 꿈꾸면서도 때때로는 쓸쓸한 생각에서 울고 십흔 쩍도 잇섯슴니다.
내가 근무하는 회사엔 녀자라고 나 한아 밧게 업섯슴으로 더구나 맘이 우울해 젓섯슴니다.
매일 들니는 이약이는 『에로』味가 가득찬 이약이 뿐이고 남자사원들 사이엔 질투와 투쟁의 공긔만의 충만함이 엿보일 뿐이엿슴니다.
그러나 변하는 것은 사람의 맘이라고 할가요. 그럿케 우울하든 내맘에도 환희의 날을 맛보게 되엿드람니다.

내가 입사하든 잇튼날 입사한 이가 잇섯스니 그는 모-전문학교를 금춘에 맛춘 미모의 청년S씨엿슴니다.
나는 아모 말도업시 장부에 무엇을 열심으로 적고 잇는 S씨를 자조 바라보게 되엿서요. 그는 언제나 침착한 태도를 보혀주엇슴니다. 나는 엇뎐날―그것은 점심시간이엇지요. 그가 洋書를 포켓트에서 끄집어내여 침울한 감정을 표현하며 읽는 것을 보앗슴니다.
「s씨! 그것이 무슨 책임닛가?」
