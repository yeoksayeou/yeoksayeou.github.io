A Memoir of a Russian Officer's Bloody Battle at Port Arthur
Bederŭk'ŭ

The Russo-Japanese War was a historical scene that decisively turned the tide of the situation in the East. This is the secret record for the ages from a Russian Navy Ensign who participated in the bloody battle of Port Arthur back then.
At the time, Russian Navy Ensign Bederŭk'ŭ

From St. Petersburg to Port Arthur

1. Japan is Not Scary

"On the night of February 10th, over ten Japanese torpedo boats launched a surprise attack. Consequently, our battleships 'Retvizan' and 'Tsesarevich,' and the cruiser 'Pallada,' were damaged by torpedoes and left the line of battle." This telegram announced by Viceroy Alekseyev was published at the top of the newspapers.

On February 10, 1904, the citizens of St. Petersburg were startled before they even rose early in the morning. How could the Japanese fleet mount such a barbaric attack, disregarding their own safety, against our superior fleet at Port Arthur? Even if this were true, they would soon be shown a crushing defeat, but why was our fleet just standing idly by? Thinking about this, day by day, the entire city was in a state where work couldn't get done.

I had known since last winter that this war was unavoidable. Hearing various explanations from the instructor about the difficult issues between Russia and Japan, I thought it would be quite fun if, when the war finally started, I could actively participate at the front line and crush Japan. However, sometimes I heard rumors from pacifists or some statesmen that the relationship between the two countries might be resolved amicably and peacefully, and I also saw articles about it in the newspapers. At those times, thinking that war might not happen, I was truly disappointed. Even at school, the lessons were not interesting at all, and my spirits gradually flagged, making it unbearable. I would idly draw lines through words like "torpedo," "gun port," or "sight scale" with my pencil. Sometimes I blotted them out completely with black ink.

However, I felt that Russia and Japan would go to war. "The country called Japan is a weak nation. It won against China, but there was various aid that allowed it to win. China lost because, firstly, it was unprepared and disunited for war; it couldn't be helped. Japan is a nation whose people have strange hair called 'chonmage' on their heads. Forty years ago, it was a small country that greatly feared Britain, America, France, and our country, and was treated with contempt in trade. Wasn't it talked about even after the Sino-Japanese War? By the time we graduate from school, become ensigns, and go to war, our military will likely reduce them to ashes, and the war will quickly reach its conclusion. If that happens, how truly regrettable it would be," I lamented, always letting even small things weigh on my mind. (If the war had to start, I wished it would be after May. In February or March, I would graduate and become an Ensign. Becoming an officer, I could go to war, so I could go to the front.)

That the war started was something that made me want to jump for joy, but that it had *already* started gave me considerable disappointment. (If Japan loses and peace is made before I can go to war, doesn't it all become nothing?)

Just as Ludovich shouted from the platform earlier—if it really happens like that, it will just be pointless.

However, the theory that several of our fleet's ships had not left the battle line and had not sunk became prevalent. The commotion within the school grew ever larger. "Gather in the auditorium, gather!" As the shouts were heard, we gathered in the corridor.

Today, His Majesty the Tsar was visiting the Winter Palace, and tomorrow he was scheduled to come to our school. Furthermore, according to the school principal, at midnight on February 10th, the Japanese fleet and our fleet exchanged fire in the Far East.

As reported in yesterday's newspaper, we learned the confirmed report that 2 or 3 ships of our fleet <64> had met a torpedo attack and left the battle line, but were not sunk. However, the fact that we suffered damage from the 'Little Japan' country deeply wounded the pride of the entire military academy.

The mood of everyone coming out of the auditorium was unpleasant. Not only was the result of the night attack disappointing, but we realized that our Pacific Fleet had been completely negligent in its war preparations.

Off-duty officers, not to mention many officers of the military academy, even our company commander, had flocked to the Winter Palace. At 2 PM, His Majesty arrived. We were waiting for our commander, Mr. Kondra, to bring back some important announcement.

About three hours later, our commander, Mr. Kondra, returned from the Winter Palace.
"War has been declared."
"Hurrah, Hurrah!" we cheered.

A rumor spread: (Japanese warships were sunk by our military, Viceroy Alekseyev himself boarded a torpedo boat to engage in the attack, and the Japanese forces were totally defeated). We shouted "Banzai" a second time.
"Just as expected, the Russo-Japanese war seems to be ending before we even become officers," I ultimately felt disappointed.

Today too, the weather is fine. The entire city, visible from the gymnasium, was bathed in reddish twilight.
"Hey—it's wrong. Japan wasn't annihilated. Look at this." As Ludovich brought the evening paper, I got down from the horizontal bar in the gymnasium and took it with trembling hands. Indeed, it was true. I realized the earlier rumor was a lie. Not only that, but the naval battle that resumed for 50 minutes seemed to have no effect whatsoever. I threw the newspaper down before my eyes and trampled it. Just then, the wind blew and carried the trampled newspaper far away. The two of us watched its path and gave a bitter smile.

The war news heard during the day had frayed our nerves, so although review started at 7 o'clock, my mind felt strange, and I just sat blankly, staring at the stars in the sky outside the window. My comrades seemed to feel the same as I did; they weren't reviewing but whispering quietly. There was absolutely no room to collect one's thoughts. My heart pounded ever stronger, and when I couldn't focus on the book, someone said, "Let's stop." At those words, everyone laughed.

I was extremely pleased about His Majesty's visit tomorrow afternoon, because I thought he would speak about the declaration of war this time.

2. His Majesty and the Ensigns

On February 11th, after barely finishing about three hours of lessons, we ate lunch. The senior students were scheduled to practice palace interior guard duty, but surprisingly, those selected that day were all junior students. Furthermore, it was quite startling that even the flag bearer was newly selected from among the junior students. There, we speculated this way and that.

At 2 PM, all students of the military academy lined up in the school hall in dress uniform. We stood there for almost an hour.
It was 4 PM. Only then did the order circulate to each company that His Majesty had arrived.
The military academy head, Major General Chukhonin, called out in a strict voice, "Attention!"

Shortly after, His Majesty entered, accompanied by the Empress, the Crown Prince, and Grand Duke Alexei. Many guests followed behind them. All eyes turned towards His Majesty.
His Majesty looked at each company, returned their salutes one by one, and then inquired about the status of each company from the principal. Then, coming to the center of the hall, he called out, "Senior students, five paces forward—"
"Now I know, we are definitely being appointed as Ensigns."

When all the senior students had moved five paces forward according to the command, His Majesty spoke in a clear voice, "Thinking of the great men of our fatherland, Nakhimov, Kornilov, and Istomin, and in this time of national crisis, despite it being slightly earlier than your graduation period, I hereby appoint you as Ensigns. I wish that you <65> always consider your noble mission and deeply engrave in your hearts the lessons left by the great men of our fatherland in war."

We were appointed as Ensigns by His Majesty. As soon as His Majesty's words finished, we simultaneously sang the national anthem loudly and shouted "Banzai." At that moment, the hearts of the 128 young new Ensigns throbbed with happiness and joy they had never experienced before.

His Majesty soon reached the barracks. The students stood rigidly in line on both sides of the corridor, and the group of young Ensigns gathered at the entrance with His Majesty. Thus, as His Majesty passed before us, with hearts full of passion, we pleaded, "Your Majesty! Please send us to the Far Eastern Front!"
"Officers are also needed in the Baltic Sea. You shall remain here for a while. However, orders for some of you to take up posts soon will be issued," he said.
The notion that "Whoever receives the first assignment to the war zone is fortunate" filled every member.

At the entrance, one Ensign helped His Majesty put on his overcoat, and another helped the Empress with hers. Both Majesties, having left the school, soon boarded their carriage, the surroundings of which were encircled by a crowd of Ensigns.
The students went out into the street without putting on overcoats, some even without hats. Together, they all proceeded to the Winter Palace.
Since we were making such a fuss trying to kiss the hands of Their Majesties as they alighted from the carriage, they entered the palace late.
We had tea and cakes at the palace and returned after five minutes.

My officer's overcoat and uniform were supposed to arrive at my older brother's house, so I went there to get them, but my brother didn't believe I had become an Ensign. Feeling quite angry, I showed him the attached officer's insignia and ran outside without even a greeting.

Busily heading to the 6 PM thanksgiving service, a pretty young lady I had never met before turned towards me and greeted me, "Congratulations!"
"How could that beauty know about my special promotion? It's truly strange. But who on earth is that beauty? I couldn't call it romance, but inwardly I was very curious. But romance is impossible..." Feeling both regretful and lonely, I attended the thanksgiving service.
I was still in my old student uniform, but only my hat had the officer's insignia; although my appearance was completely changed (변턠린의 형태엿으나 - *likely means 'completely changed'*), my spirit was that of an officer, so I was happy. That feeling was truly wonderful. Feeling the present moment as if ascending to heaven, I slept at the school.

3. Triumphant Departure amidst Cheers

The next day was the day for deciding the assignments for the senior candidates. The regulation for military academy graduates selecting their assignments was that only the top 15% of graduates, in order of academic excellence, could choose their assignment freely. The remaining classes drew lots to decide.

Fortunately, I was among the top students, so I could choose my assignment first. I chose attachment to the Kwantung Fleet in the war zone of Kwantung, which I had never even seen.
Among the 6 Chief Petty Officers and 4 top students who chose their assignments, two wished to go to Vladivostok Port. (Ludovich took the same direction as us). Anyway, since the 18 top students first chose locations with vacancies, the assignments for the others were decided by lot as follows:
Black Sea Fleet 25, Caspian Sea 3, Petersburg Fleet 9, Baltic Fleet 79. We, who had suddenly become officers ahead of schedule, were extremely happy. <66> Thinking (We are still fighting Japan. We finally became Ensigns. And in a fortnight, we will depart for the war zone), I alone felt doubly excited.

Someone among us said that there are three happy days in a lifetime: the day one becomes an officer, the day one is promoted to general/admiral, and the day one gets married!
One way or another, the day of departure arrived.

Wearing my officer's uniform, I headed to a small Russian city to get leave from my father. On the way, on the train, an officer from the Warsaw Regiment and an Engineer General were riding together. The general came to my seat and shook my hand firmly.
"In this war, let the army and navy join forces and do our utmost without regrets..." Hearing such words from an admirable general I had never seen before, I felt my face flush.
"Of course, we will," I replied.
When I happily mentioned, "I selected the Kwantung Fleet at Port Arthur (浦監 - *likely means Port Arthur/Kwantung here*), so I will be going to the war zone before long," the Captain said,
"Ah! A great honor. I wonder if I might go to Port Arthur too... Shall we not raise a toast in the dining car, anticipating Japan's annihilation first?" The general also agreed.
There, the three of us, like friends who had known each other for 10 years, forgetting rank, conversed enjoyably in the dining car.

It couldn't be helped.
The Fortress Commander, Major General Stessel, had issued a special strict order to seal all windows facing the sea and to ensure not a single point of light was visible from the streets. This was, of course, done so as not to become a target for the Japanese fleet.
And so, the approximately 20-day pleasant journey came to an end. And thus, the longed-for Port Arthur, in tasteless and desolate darkness, emitted a metallic sound, and our souls resonated with each other. It made our young blood boil to the extreme.

On the morning of March 16th, we arrived at Jinzhou. There, we learned that the Japanese fleet was currently bombarding Port Arthur harbor. The sound of cannons could be heard from somewhere. Ludovich, enraged, shouted, "Hey! It's Port Arthur! Let's fight as long as we have strength!"
As the train stopped for about an hour, we took the opportunity to observe the defense construction that had just begun. There, a seemingly agile Private First Class was supervising the work, digging trenches. That soldier appeared to be a man of intense hostility. "If the enemy seems to come here, I'll shoot them down from here. And if they come towards the harbor entrance, I can just shoot them down from inside this trench, right?" he said. The officers merely nodded, "Uh-huh! Uh-huh!"

Soon, we boarded the train again. We passed Yingchengzi without stopping and finally arrived at Port Arthur at eleven at night. On the way, from a point about five Russian versts (五露理) from Port Arthur, we were warned to seal the train windows so that no light leaked outside. Thus, upon alighting within the station grounds, there wasn't even a single light, and we couldn't distinguish east from west (東西도 分호수가에는 - *likely means 'unable to distinguish directions'*). There was a wooden house, which is the famous rest stop called Seredina (Central).
The shadow of the carriage rolled along as if swaying and flowing on the lake surface.
In the building, there was a bar, a sofa rest area, even a stable. The 16 traveling companions admired the vast forest belt and the crystals of white snow while sharing drinks.

At Dashitsiao station (大石橋の 傷車場 - *傷車場 likely typo for 停車場, station*), we were stopped for a full day and night due to accumulated snow. I, who longed for Port Arthur, wanted to arrive even a minute or second sooner, but with this much heavy snow, it couldn't be helped. The train departed, but it was so slow that I felt rather angry and anxious.

Upon arriving in Mukden (奉天), a Navy Ministry official came out to meet us and told us about the war situation, the great deeds of the 'Varyag' and 'Koreets,' the sinking of the 'Boyarin?' (독야-링 - *identity unclear, possibly Boyarin*), and other stories. Regarding the tales of glory of the 'Varyag' and others, whenever night came, we would gather in the train's smoking room, play the piano, sing that song ('thee' 그대 might be 'that song' 그 노래), and drink strong liquor. We called this the "Call to Quarters" (總員令).
We would also make noise, and when thoughts of women arose, <67> we would drink our fill and sometimes let out screams.
And from morning till night, inside the train, trying to relieve the boredom, we joked and chatted with each other, forgetting the train's motion. Even amidst this commotion, the grand spectacle of war would come to mind, and there were times I spent a while in silent contemplation.

At Zlatoust Station, I bought a Cossack sword. (This sword later became an important one.)
Before long, the pleasant train journey gradually came to an end. On the night of February 28th, we arrived in Irkutsk. The next morning, we transferred to a train going to Baikal Station. After crossing Lake Baikal, we rode in carriages.
That day, the sky was clear without a single cloud. The scenery of the mountain range's topography reflected on the surface of Lake Baikal appeared before my eyes like a dream. It was impressive (스러웟다 - *interpreted as impressive/moving*). Finally, as it became seven o'clock, we returned to the station. There, a certain young colleague and the wife of a certain Captain (Lieutenant), who had been stationed in the Port Arthur fleet for several years, came to see us off. Amidst the chaotic crowd in front of the ticket gate, the wife requested me to deliver this letter and a holy icon to her husband in Port Arthur. (However, this ultimately led to a sad outcome. On the very day we arrived in Port Arthur, that Captain met a heroic end on a torpedo boat).

To bless the health of our colleague's lovely wife, we raised our cups in a toast within the station premises. We cheered. The people gathered inside the station also cheered loudly.
(In Moscow and St. Petersburg too, they give tremendous send-offs to people heading to the war zone).
At ten o'clock, our party boarded a mysterious train. That train was fully loaded only with officers. Those officers represented each branch of service (Infantry, Artillery, Cavalry, Engineers, etc.), about 83 in total. With spirits high as a rainbow (意氣虹霓갓치), they pledged to annihilate the Japanese forces.
Wreaths were quickly thrown in through the windows from outside. We firmly shook hands with each other.

The train gradually picked up speed, leaving the crowd at the station behind. The streetlights flowed past like arrows, and the sound of "Banzai" gradually faded into the distance, becoming inaudible.
Soon, the train proceeded at full speed. For a moment, the 13 young Ensigns, standing in a state of deep emotion and sorrow, sat down in the train compartment as if tired and shared drinks.
The city was already out of sight. It was the countryside. Dim lights shone faintly from between the dark forests (숨사히에서 - *interpreted as 'between the forests'*). The train was now heading towards Moscow.

4. Remembering Lake Baikal

It took one night to get to Moscow. The train reached Moscow at 10 AM the next day.
Ludovich and I went out to buy things. Ludovich bought binoculars, and I bought a watch.
The Slavyansky Bazaar (스라완스키 勸工場) was less impressive than expected, so we went all over (온 몬에 갓는대 - *interpreted as 'went all over'*), and that place was a little fun. (몃번인지 든저주엇다 - *Sentence fragment, unclear meaning, omitted in translation*).

Which day will it be when we see this school building again? Hit by an enemy shell, we might part forever holding onto this memory. Beloved alma mater! Fun alma mater! Well then! Farewell!

At Nikolayevsky Station, many people who came to see us off had already gathered.
There were very many people who came to see us off. My brother also came, accompanied by his wife, along with people from the neighborhood.
The Chief of Naval Operations, Rear Admiral Rozhestvensky, also came to see us off. And so, he expressed his farewell wishes by giving us kisses (키스를 하를 - *interpreted as 'giving kisses'*).

The train we were to board was covered in yellow, red, white, and purple flower wreaths, leaving not even a single step's worth of room to set foot. And on the seats, fine wines, sweets, and types of tobacco were placed so densely they made the space narrow. Everything was a splendid sight. At 9 PM, a single blast of the steam whistle sounded. The send-off crowd repeatedly shouted "Banzai."
The junior students from the military academy hugged each one of us (안 갓흔 여자는 업섯다 - *interpreted as 'hugged each one'*).

We arrived at the Nikolayevsky Bridge embankment. The neighborhood people seemed to show a kind of goodwill towards the soldiers, as if moved (개인 듯이 - *interpreted as 'moved'*) by this war.<68>
However, my heart felt heavy because I couldn't meet that young lady again! (Why! Why couldn't I speak then? If only I knew where she lived, how happy today's departure would be. Going to the war zone is something I resolved to do, but I might die in battle. Perhaps I might return triumphantly by good fortune. But that pretty young lady will probably be married by then. She will belong to someone. But it cannot be helped.)

Going to the front line, at 7 PM, I turned my back on my alma mater, to which I had grown attached for a long time.
The school gate, the trees in the yard—everything transformed into things that evoked our emotions.
The dim building enveloped in evening mist stirred up our sad hearts.
Although I resolved to be nonchalant, looking back again and again, moved by indescribable sentiment (키스를 - *interpreted contextually*), the principal finally called us out to choose spots with vacancies (次員잇는 - *interpreted as 缺員있는, vacant*) in Port Arthur harbor and Vladivostok.

Regarding our decision to bravely go to the war zone without the slightest sign of hesitation, he expressed delight at this patriotic act and spoke of practical battle lessons for the sake of the homeland. He then said that if each person (名人 - *interpreted as 各人*) prepared for departure by dinner time on February 19th, he would request departure on the 20th through the Naval General Staff. And so the ceremony ended.

Morning came on the 19th. We, who were to depart for the Far East, were called by the principal and received beautiful small cloisonné (七寶砂器의 - *interpreted as cloisonné*) holy icons bestowed by Empress Alexandra Feodorovna.

The awaited departure day came.
While strolling along Morskaya Street (모-루스카야 町), I thought about the pretty young lady who had said "Congratulations" sometime ago. Every time a carriage or tram passed, I carefully watched only the women. (Surely not that young lady...) However, even the sound of footsteps... (발거름 거리조차 - *incomplete thought*)
O longed-for alma mater!
O our longed-for street! Our oath ceremony was held in the military academy's conference room. That day, 11 Ensigns, for some reason, arrived at the ceremony hall several minutes late. Attendees included relatives, close friends, and also pretty young ladies.
In front of them, the principal severely scolded the 11 Ensigns who were late for the appointed time. And despite being in front of relatives and old friends, he sentenced them to one week in the guardhouse (營倉) as punishment according to school regulations.
Soon, the adjutant (副官) took their swords from them and made them withdraw from the ceremony hall. Probably, he was taking them to the school guardhouse.
The hall grew quiet, and only the dejected figures of the young Ensigns being led away by the adjutant (副宮에게 - *interpreted as 副官에게*) remained. Regarding this harsh and strict measure, we rather sympathized with the principal. Punishing latecomers on the very important first day of duty clearly showed how strictly each person's duty should be observed. (To be continued in the next issue) <69>

**Summary**

This article is a first-person memoir by a newly commissioned Russian naval ensign, Bederŭk'ŭ, recounting the beginning of the Russo-Japanese War in February 1904. He describes the initial shock and patriotic fervor in St. Petersburg following the Japanese attack on Port Arthur, his accelerated graduation from the naval academy, the emotional farewells, and the long train journey eastward across Russia towards the front lines via the Trans-Siberian Railway. The narrative captures the mix of youthful excitement for war, nationalistic pride (including underestimation and derogatory views of Japan), anxiety about the future, and poignant moments of departure. From the perspective of Korean colonial history, the publication of this Russian account in the Korean magazine *Samch'ŏlli* in 1932 is noteworthy. It offered Korean readers under Japanese rule a perspective from Japan's former adversary in a war that sealed Japan's dominance over Korea, potentially evoking complex feelings or serving as a subtle counter-narrative, while also highlighting the fervent nationalism and underestimation of Japan that preceded Russia's defeat.

露西亞 將校의旅順血戰手記
						베데룩크
日露전쟁은 東洋의 대세를 결정적으로 廻轉하여 노은 역사적 장면이엇다. 그때 旅順혈전에 참가하엿든 露國해군 少尉의 千古의 秘記가 이것이다.
當時의 露國海軍少尉 베데룩크

聖彼得堡에서 旅順으로
1. 日本이 무섭지안타 『2월 10일 夜半 日本水艇 10數隻 기습하여 왓기때문에 우리의 전투함 「레트비잔」 「제사레우이치」 巡洋艦 「바ㄹ라-다」는 水雷때문에 파손되야 戰列을 떠낫다.』 아렉셉 총독의 발표한 電報는 신문의 돕푸에 게재되엿다.
1904년 2월 10일 페텔불그의 시민은 아츰 일즉 니러나기 전에 경악하엿다. 旅順에 잇는 우리의 우세한 艦隊에 대해서 日本艦隊가 얼마만큼 신변을 생각지안는 야만적 공격을 취햇는가. 이것이 과연 사실이라고 해도 얼마안되어 곳 慘敗를 보혀줄 것이나 웨 그러케 우리의 艦隊도 멍하니하고 잇는가 하는것을 생각하면 하로하로 全시민은 일이 손에 쥐이지안을 상태엿섯다.
나는 今番의 전쟁에 대해서는 작년 겨을부터 벌서 피하기 어려운 사정에 잇는 것을 알엇다.
露日 兩國間에 끼운 얼마간의 難문제에 관해서 강사로부터 여러 가지 설명을 듯고 끈내 전쟁이 시작된 때에는 반다시 前線에서 활약해서 日本을 참패식히면 퍽 자미잇겟다고 생각햇다. 그러나 때때는 兩國관계가 원만하게 無事해결할지 모른다는 소문을 평화론자나 爲政者 일부에서 드른일도 잇고 또한 신문에서도 그 기사를 보도하엿으나 그적에 전쟁이 되지 안케된다고 생각한 즉 참으로 실망하엿다. 학교에 가서도 학과가 조곰도 자미업슴으로 점점 氣가 죽어서 견댈 수가 업섯다. 연필로 水雷이라든가 砲門이라든가 혹은 照尺이라고 하는 語句에 자연히 선을 쭉쭉 끄엇다. 때때로는 식컴엇케 먹물로 업새 배렷다.
그러나 露日는 전쟁이 된다라고 늣겻다. 「日本이라고 하는 나라는 약한 나라다. 支那와 싸화서는 익엿다. 그러나 익일만한 여러 가지 援助가 잇섯다.
支那는 第一전쟁에 대해서 不用意하고 不統一햇기 때문에 젓다고 해도 할 수 업는 일이다.
日本은 죤마게라는 이상한 털을 머리에 가지고 잇는 국민으로서 지금으로부터 40년전은 퍽 英米佛과 우리나라를 무서워 하엿으며 通商上에 잇서서도 업수역임을 밧은 小國이엿다.
日淸전쟁 후도 우리들 입에 오르지 안든가 우리들이 학교를 졸업하고 少尉가 되여서 出征하기까지는 우리군사 때문에 骨灰微盡이 되여서 전쟁은 빨니 결과를 고하리라. 그럿케되면 엇지나 참으로 섭섭한 일이다』라고 하엿으나 언제나 적은일 까지도 맘에 올여댓다. (될수 잇는대로 전쟁이 시작된다면 5월 이후로 되어다구 2, 3월은 졸업하고 少尉가 된다. 士官이 되여서 出征할 수 잇으니 戰地에 나갈 수 잇는 것이다).
전쟁이 시작된 것은 뛰고십흘 만하게 깃분일이지만 벌서 시작된것은 얼마마한 실망을 준다. (出征하기 전에 日本이 저서 媾和가 된다면 아무것도 되지 안치안는가).
앗가 演壇에서 루트빗치놈이 부르지젓지만-실제 그럿케 된다면 不用하게 될 뿐이다.
그러나 우리들의 艦隊 數隻이 戰列을 떠나지안코 침몰되지 안엇다는 設이 유력하게 되엿섯다. 교내의 소동은 더욱더욱커젓다. 『강당에 모혀라모혀라!』라는 소리가 들니게 우리들은 廊下에 모혓섯다.
오늘은 冬宮폐하의 行幸이 잇서서 明日은 當校에 오시게 되엿다. 더욱히 교장선생의 設에 의하면 2월 10일 零時 日本艦隊와 우리 艦隊가 極東에서 砲火를 밧꾼다고.
어제 신문에 보도된 바와 갓치 우리 艦隊＜64＞ 2,3隻은 水雷습격을 맛나서 戰列을 떠난것이지 침몰된것은 안이라는 確報를 알엇다. 그러나 小日本 나라때문에 피해를 밧엇다는 말은 兵학교 전체의 자존심을 심히 상하게 햇다.
강당에서 나온 여러 사람의 맘은 불유쾌하엿다. 夜襲의 결과가 자미업슬 분 안니라 우리 태평양 艦隊는 전혀 전쟁준비를 게을니 하엿다는 것을 알게되엿다.
근무이외의 非番사관은 물론 兵학교의 다수의 사관 우리들의 隊長까지 冬宮에 몰여갓섯다. 午後 2시 폐하는 行幸하엿섯다. 우리들은 隊長으로부터 엇던 중대한 신고를 가자올것을 기다리고 잇섯다.
세시간쯤 지나서 우리들의 隊長 곤드라씨가 冬宮으로 도라왓섯다.
「선전포고가 되엿다.」
「오-라, 오-라」하고 우리들은 떠들엇다.
(우리軍때문에 日本군함은 침몰되여서 ´독 아렉셉은 스사로 水雷艇을 타고 공격에 從事하고 日本軍은 全敗에 도라갓섯다)라는 風說이 전하엿섯다. 우리들은 두 번재 만세를 불넛다.
「점점 예상대로 日露전쟁도 우리들이 사관이 되기전에 끈나는 갑다」라고 결국 실망햇다.
오늘도 조흔 日氣다. 기계체조장에서 보이는 全市는 붉으스레한 황혼이엿다.
「여-틀엿다. 日本 전멸한 것이 안이다. 이것 보라.」 루드빗치가 夕刊을 가저왓섯슴으로 기계체조의 철봉에서 나려서 떨니는 손으로 밧어본 즉 참 그럿타. 앗가의 風說은 거짓말이엿슴을 알엇섯다. 그 뿐만 안이라 50분 사이에 속행된 海戰은 아모 효과도 업는 모양이엿섯다. 신문을 눈우에 던지고 밟어바렷다.
때맛츰 바람이 불어서 밟어버린 신문을 멀니 가저갓섯다. 두 사람은 그 행방을 보면서 苦笑하엿다.

낫에 드른 전쟁의 보도는 우리들의 신경을 산란케 햇슴으로 복습은 7시부터 시작햇지만 맘이 이상해저서 멍하니 안저 창밧게 하늘의 별들만 처다보앗다.
여러 동무도 나와 갓흔 기분을 가지고 복습을 하지안코 가만가만 수근거렷다. 思想이 통일할 여유 등은 전연업섯다. 動悸가 점점 강하게 되여서 책이 보´지 안을때 누군가 그만 두자하고 말하엿다. 그 소리에 모다 우섯다.
明日 오후 폐하의 臨幸에 대해서 나는 몹시 깃거웟다. 今番 선전포고에 관한 것을 말삼해 주실이라고 생각햇기 때문이다.

2. 폐하와 少尉들 2월 11일 겨우 3시간쯤 課程을 맛치고 晝飯을 먹엇다. 상급생은 예정대로 宮殿의 내부경위를 연습할 것인데 의외에도 그날 선발된 것은 悉皆 하급생이엿고 더욱히 旗手까지가 하급생 중에서 새로 선발된 것은 좀 놀날만한 일이엿다. 거기서 우리들은 이리저리 상상해보앗다.
오후 2시에 兵학교 全生徒 全員은 예복을 입고 校堂에 정렬하엿다. 거진 한시간이나 서잇섯다.
오후 4시이다. 그때에 겨우 폐하의 御着하시엿다고 각 隊에 傳令이 돌앗다.
兵학교장 츄프닝 少將은 엄격한 소리로 「氣着」을 불넛다.
얼마안되여 폐하는 황후 황태자 아레키세이 大公을 다리시고 들어오시엿다. 來賓도 만히 그 뒤를 조차왓다. 일제히 陛下의 쪽으로 시선이 모히엿다.
폐하는 각 隊를 보시며 일일히 對禮를 하신 후 교장에게 각 隊의 상황을 下問하신 다음 校堂 중앙부에 나오시며 「상급생 5보앞-」을 불느시엿다.
「지금에야 알고보니 우리들 틀님업시 少尉로 임명되는 것이다.」
상급상 一同은 명령에 의해서 5보앞으로 나간때 폐하께서는 명랑한 음성으로 「나는 조국의 위인 나희몹, 골나릅, 이스토신이하는 것을 생각하고 국가多事의 가을 너희들이 졸업기의 조곰 일즉함에도 不抱하고 이에 少尉로 임명한다. 원컨대 너희들은 항상＜65＞ 자기의 존귀한 사명을 생각하고 전쟁에 잇서서 조국의 위인이 남겨노흔 遺訓을 맘에 깁히 색여야한다」라고 말슴하엿다.
우리들은 폐하로부터 少尉로 임명되엿다. 폐하의 말삼이 끈치자 말자 일제히 國歌를 高唱하고 만세를 불넛다. 그 순간에 128人의 젊은 新少尉 등은 아직까지 한번도 경험해 본적이 업는 행복과 歡喜로 가삼을 울넝거리엿다.
폐하는 곳 兵營에 다다러섯다. 生徒는 廊下 兩側에 일렬로 정립하고 젊은 少尉의 一團은 폐하과 입구에 모혀섯다. 그리하야 폐하가 우리들 앞을 통과할제 滿腔의 열정으로써 「폐하여! 우리들을 極東戰線에 보내주십시요」라고 원햇다.
「발칙크바다에도 사관의 필요가 잇스면 너희들은 잠시 이땅에 머믈어라. 단 일부는 近近 赴任의 명령이 잇을 것이다」라고 말삼하섯다.
「엇잿든 제일 먼저 戰地 임명을 맛는 者가 행복」이라고 하는 관념이 각 員에게 가득차고 잇섯다.
출입구에서 少尉 한사람이 폐하에게 외투를 입혀드리고 황후폐하에게 외투를 입혀드렷다. 학교에서 나가신 兩 폐하는 곳 마차를 타섯스니 그 마차 주위는 少尉들 무리로서 둘너싸혓다.
生徒들은 외투도 입지안코 中에는 모자도 쓰지안코 거리에 나섯다. 一同은 일제히 冬宮에 이르럿다.

그 압헤서 교장은 정각에 느진 少尉 11人을 峻烈하게 꾸지젓다. 그리고 친척 故舊압헤도 不抱하고 校規로써 벌칙 일주간의 營倉을 言渡하얏다.
곳 兵학교 副官은 그들로부터 劒을 빼앗고 식장에셔 退步식히엿다. 아마도 그들을 교내 營倉에 다리고 가는 것이다.
식장은 조용하야지고 悄然히 副宮에게 끌니여 나가는 少尉들의 젊운 그림자가 남을분이다. 우리들은 이 혹독하고 엄격한 처치에 대하야 도리여 교장에게 동정하얏다. 근무 第一日이라고 하는 중요한 날에 지각자를 벌한 것은 교장이 각 자의 직책을 如何히 嚴守할 것이라는 것은 如實히 표시한 것이다. (이하 次號續)＜69＞
