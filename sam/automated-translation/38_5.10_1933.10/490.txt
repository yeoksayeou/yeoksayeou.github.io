Article Title: 旁兒打鈴 (Pounding Song / Milling Song)
Article Type: 시 (Poem/Song Lyrics)

旁兒打鈴
(Panga T'aryŏng)

It's good, oh so good!
Oh, wild goose crying and flying in the yellow dusk sunset wind,
Ryongmunhak Kwanŭmsindan (龍門學觀音神壇) is distant, so who will deliver news from the north? I pray,
Please deliver one letter from me as you go.

It's good, oh so good!
The twelve high peaks of Musan (巫山十二)
Soar above the clouds,
And the boat floating among apricot trees and pines,
Is it a vermillion-painted boat on the Five Lakes (五湖)? Oh, the blue boatman's song!

It's good, oh so good!
That moon floats roundly, but
Why can my beloved not come? The moon hanging in the azure sky, my beloved must surely see it too,
For what sin in a past or present life were we two, e-heru, born like this? <81>

It's good, oh so good!
Amidst the falling leaves blown by the wind,
If the crying Kim Saeng (김생) were heard unfeelingly,
There would be no reason to mind, but
In the vast blue heavens (悠悠蒼天), it is earnest, sound by sound, it is sorrow.

It's good, oh so good!
Child, prepare the inkstone,
Let's write a letter to my beloved.
The white paper with black ink, you will see my beloved, but this longing brush, why can I not?

It's good, oh so good!
Going up the mountain, the mountain mill; coming down to the field, the water mill.
In the Namwŏn study, Yi Toryŏng (리도령), without immortal companions,
Rides a boat on the Kwanghallu Pavilion, enjoying the night in the third watch.

It's good, oh so good!
In the vibrant season of spring breezes and willow blossoms, that cuckoo breaks others' hearts.
Leaving behind so many empty mountains, why do you come to cry before my window?

It's good, oh so good!
I pray, I pray, before God (하나님) I pray.
Some people have good fortune and serve one husband for life, but
Why has my body become like seeing out the old year and welcoming the new (送舊迎新), e-heru?

It's good, oh so good!
On the wide waters of O-Ch'o Tongnam (吳楚東南), the coming and going merchant ships,
With sails hoisted in a fair wind, beating drums boomingly,
*Ŏgiŏch'a*, the sound of weighing anchor, is the scene of returning sails from a distant shore (遠浦歸帆).

It's good, oh so good!
Mr. Nam (남도령) at the foot of Namsan Mountain, even if you cut all kinds of flowers,
Do not cut the golden-threaded black bamboo (金絲烏竹).
Let it grow this year, grow next year, grow briefly for three, four, five years,
Grasp it as a fishing rod; if you catch fish, it's success, if you can't catch fish,
Tie a love knot (相思), and until that knot comes undone,
Let you and I play together day and night forever (晝夜長天). <82>

It's good, oh so good!
Looking up, myriad peaks and thousand summits; looking down and observing, a white sandy beach (白沙汀).
The bent and aged tall pine, unable to withstand the fierce wind (狂風), does a half-dance,
And pavilions with beaded curtains and painted eaves (珠簾畫閣) stretch into the blue sky (碧空), where parrots and peacocks (鸚鵡孔雀) fly flutteringly.

It's good, oh so good!
At the foot of Mount Ch'iaksan (雉岳山), at Tongnaktae Pavilion (東洛臺),
Immortals (神仙) gather and only play *paduk* (바둑), *tanggi-tanggi*.

It's good, oh so good!
Looking up, an airplane (飛行機) flies; looking down and observing, it's an automobile (自動車).
Driver (運轉手), let me ask you,
If my beloved has boarded, just slowly turn the wheels (바퀴) towards my house. Ah, isn't it good?

Oh moon, moon, bright moon! T'aebaek (太白, Li Bai) rode a whale ascending to heaven (騎鯨飛上天),
And So Tongp'a (蘇東坡, Su Dongpo) died, leaving no lingering resentment (怨客).
With whom to play, e-heru, has that moon become bright?

It's good, oh so good!
The boat floating, floating on the river, after T'aebaek rode the whale to ascend to heaven,
Is a boat carrying poetic sentiment (風月?), e-rehu, floating away.

E-he e-he e-ya ŏraugyŏra pang-a rog구나 (Chant for pounding/milling song)
The crane playing before the lotus pond (蓮塘) is flying away,
E-heri Ch'undangdae (春塘臺)!

E-he e-he e-ya ŏraugyŏra pang-a ruguna (Chant for pounding/milling song)
The sun sets in the West Mountain (西山),
And from the East Ridge (東嶺), that moon just now rises. <83>

**Summary**

This text presents the lyrics of a song titled "Panga T'aryŏng" (Milling Song), structured around the recurring refrain "It's good, oh so good!". The verses blend traditional Korean themes and imagery—natural landscapes, longing for a lover, allusions to folklore (Ch'unhyang story) and Chinese poets (Li Bai, Su Dongpo), descriptions of folk life (milling, boating)—with modern elements like airplanes and cars. The overall tone mixes appreciation for beauty and life's simple pleasures with underlying notes of sorrow, longing, and perhaps fatalism regarding love and destiny. From a historical perspective, the poem is interesting for its clear adaptation of a traditional folk song form (*minyo*) published in a mainstream magazine during the colonial period. It showcases the persistence of traditional culture while incorporating modern technology (airplane, car) into its imagery, reflecting the changing landscape of 1930s Korea. The blend of classical allusions, folk motifs, and contemporary objects provides insight into the cultural milieu of the time.
