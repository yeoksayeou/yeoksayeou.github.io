The Newspaper War Between Tonga Ilbo and Chosŏn Ilbo Amidst the Great Scramble
						By Ch'obyŏngjŏng
The Oil War between Russian Oil and American Oil
The Gramophone War among the major companies: Victor, Columbia, Taihei, Siren, Polydor, O-Keh

The Newspaper War Between Tonga Ilbo and Chosŏn Ilbo
Against the formidable fortress of the Tonga Ilbo, built like an iron wall over 16 years, the Chosŏn Ilbo, revived this spring, suddenly began firing its heavy artillery, armed with a lineup of talent seemingly gathered over ten years and vast funds amounting to hundreds of thousands of won. Six months later, this has now reached a white-hot intensity, like the battle for the Verdun fortress that decided the fate of Germany and France, or the fierce battle of Waterloo where the fate of the great Napoleon hung in the balance. A great war has commenced where both sides are staking their company's fortunes, believing that the newspaper defeated in this battle will be utterly crushed.

Will Tonga Ilbo be crushed? Or will Chosŏn Ilbo be crushed? This confrontation between the two rival newspapers, determined "not to stop until the other is brought down!" This cannot but be one of the greatest spectacles adorning the Peninsula market in the autumn of 1933.

We can observe three possible outcomes for this battle.
First. After competing fiercely for a while, they will cooperate and advance side-by-side.
Second. Their cooperation will fail, and one company, exhausted, will declare itself a second-rate newspaper and fall out of the competitive sphere. <30>
Third. The other company will go completely bankrupt.

However, the first scenario is unthinkable based on our common sense. The observation that they will advance side-by-side like the Osaka Mainichi and Osaka Asahi does not seem plausible. Considering the historical precedents in Korea and the personalities and fighting spirit of the leaders currently structuring and operating the two newspapers, this is utterly inconceivable.
The third scenario is unlikely to reach such an extreme either. Therefore, the status of the two newspapers after a few years will ultimately correspond to the second scenario. Then, between Tonga Ilbo and Chosŏn Ilbo, which one will fall to second or third rate? Which president will endure the shame and be exiled to the lonely island of Saint Helena?

I will lay out the basis for this judgment by examining three aspects:
1. The financial resources of the two newspapers
2. The personnel deployment of the two newspapers
3. The future plans of the two newspapers

1. The Future of the "Three Increases" Policy
Tonga Ilbo is operating with 350,000 won. Since it is a 700,000 won joint-stock company with half the capital paid in, it's correct to view 350,000 won as fully realized cash. Of this 350,000 won, about 100,000 won spent before becoming a joint-stock company was repaid and taken over when the company was formed, so that amount is considered pure expenditure. The remainder was used for building construction, land purchase, acquiring machinery and fixtures, and advertising and business expenses. It is said that the current liquid operating capital, not tied up in fixed assets, amounts to 140,000-150,000 won.

When asked about the assets of Tonga Ilbo, President Song [Chin-u]'s response was as follows:
"To build 'this building' (the current headquarters on Gwanghwamun Street), we made two capital calls totaling 175,000 won, but that was insufficient, so we added about 80,000 won more. Thus, the building alone cost 230,000-240,000 won. Three rotary presses, one million typesets, and other various equipment cost 100,000 won. The current land near Sipchagak totals about 1,000 p'yŏng, which, valued at 300 won per p'yŏng, amounts to 300,000 won. Adding this and that, the total capital amounts to about 750,000 won. It might sound amusing that a company with 350,000 won paid-in capital has assets more than double that amount, but this is due to the natural appreciation of land purchased over ten years ago and annual savings resulting from non-distribution of dividends." Since scrutinizing the words of a responsible person is discourteous to a person of character, we shall consider Tonga Ilbo's current assets to be 750,000 won. Furthermore, when the third and fourth capital calls are completed soon, and the remaining half, 350,000 won, comes in, it will become a newspaper company possessing at least one million won.

Then, what about Chosŏn Ilbo? If we count the money spent since its founding 16 years ago, through the Sin Sŏk-u era, the Yi Sŭng-bok era, and the Im Kyŏng-nae era, it would exceed 500,000 won. However, let's set that aside for a moment and simply consider the matters related to Mr. Pang Ŭng-mo's involvement since the establishment of the joint-stock company this spring. This is because, although the newspaper is the same Chosŏn Ilbo continuing for 16 years, the reforms this spring swept away past relationships concerning the company's structure and personnel deployment, effectively making it a newly reconstructed newspaper.
As everyone likely knows from recent events, Chosŏn Ilbo recently increased its capital by 300,000 won, and then by another 200,000 won, becoming a company with 500,000 won fully paid-in capital. Of this 500,000 won, 1,100 p'yŏng of land on Gwanghwamun Street was purchased for 100,000 won to build the company headquarters. 200,000 won was set aside separately for construction costs. Several tens of thousands of won were invested in rotary presses and fixtures, and some amount was spent on advertising, leaving about 100,000 won as operating <31> capital. However, Chosŏn Ilbo is said to be poised to carry out a third capital increase depending on the circumstances. In short, the financial strength of the two newspaper companies is currently evenly matched (伯仲之勢), and it is correct to view them as likely to remain neck-and-neck in the future.

Also, neither newspaper company has debt, which could be called the "cancer of development." Tonga Ilbo's correspondent bank (取引銀行) is Haedong Bank, and Chosŏn Ilbo's correspondent bank (取引銀行) is [blank space in original, likely a specific bank name was intended but omitted]. Even if the two companies do not have specifically established lines of credit with their respective banks, they would likely be able to receive unsecured loans of several tens of thousands of won in case of need.

Let's look at the "Three Increases" (三增) of the two newspapers. The Three Increases refer to "capital increase" (增資), "building expansion" (增築), and "page increase" (增頁).
Regarding the expansion of the company building, Tonga Ilbo is set to undertake an expansion next spring with a budget of about 200,000 won, sufficient to prevent feeling cramped for at least the next 30 years. They say this money will be raised without a capital increase, suggesting they have accumulated this much money over time. Chosŏn Ilbo plans to invest 100,000 won in land and about 200,000 won in the building for their new headquarters, with the possibility of increasing the amount and scale as needed.

And then there is the page increase (增頁). The pace of development in terms of the number of pages for private newspapers borders on astonishment (植한다 - likely meant 則한다, 'borders on'). What used to be 4-page papers just 6 or 7 years ago became 6 pages, then leaped again to 8 pages, and a third leap brought them to 10 pages. Now, a leap to 12 or 16 pages remains. In the current situation, if either newspaper increases its pages to 16 or 12, the other newspaper will inevitably be forced to follow suit immediately. However, increasing the number of newspaper pages requires a foundation. That foundation is the growth rate of advertising volume. Osaka Mainichi and Asahi have over 50 columns (段) of advertising daily. To maintain the current 10 pages, Korean newspapers fundamentally need 30 columns daily. To increase to 12 or 16 pages, 40 columns of advertising become necessary. Increasing pages without this advertising growth is reckless and deviates from sound business practice. Therefore, both companies are exerting all efforts to increase advertising from Tokyo and Osaka, even spending on promotion. Consequently, Tonga Ilbo is reportedly striving to surpass 200,000 lines of advertising annually, up from the current 180,000 lines.

Thus, the "Three Increases" – "capital increase," "building expansion," and "page increase" – are as described above. In short, superiority is not yet apparent in the Three Increases. Only time will tell.

2. How is the Personnel Deployment?
Then, what about the lineup of personnel deployment? The center of interest in evaluating the two companies truly lies here. In terms of personnel deployment, Chosŏn Ilbo shines distinctively. There is a saying, "a cabinet of prime ministers." It refers to an organization assembled by gathering the best talents. There is also the term "golden cabinet." It is a title for an organization composed solely of the most brilliant figures. The editorial department organization of Chosŏn Ilbo can be called a cabinet of prime ministers, a golden cabinet; it is the most splendid organization since the founding of the newspaper and belongs to the most luxurious lineup among the writing staffs of all private newspapers.
Specifically, Vice President and Editor-in-Chief Yi Kwang-su, Principal Writer Sŏ Ch'un, Acting Editor-in-Chief Chu Yo-han, Deputy Director Kim Hyŏng-wŏn, Foreign News Director Hong Yang-myŏng, Political Director Ham Sang-hun, Research Director Hong Ki-mun, and others are all prominent debaters (論客) and strategists (策士), whose journalistic experience is all highly valued. Furthermore, Chunwŏn [Yi Kwang-su]'s readers alone must number in the tens of thousands, and Mr. Sŏ Ch'un's respect within the commercial and industrial circles exceeds our imagination. The more than 20 reporters under these gentlemen are also sharp, spirited, and outstanding figures of the time (犀利氣銳當代逸物). Editorials are written by Yi Kwang-su, Chu Yo-han, Sŏ Ch'un, Ham Sang-hun, Hong Yang-myŏng, Hong Ki-mun, Mun Il-p'yŏng, and others. As guest contributors, there are Cho Man-sik from the Kansŏ region, Director Sin T'ae-ak, and as an organic guest, Mr. Hong Myŏng-hŭi. Looking at the lineup of fiction writers, there are Chunwŏn [Yi Kwang-su], Pyŏkch'o [Hong Myŏng-hŭi], Kim Tong-in, Yi Ki-yŏng, Ch'ae Man-sik, and others. Among the artists are Yi Kap-ki, Kim Kyu-t'aek, and others.
The president directing the overall strategy, Mr. Pang Ŭng-mo, is an unknown quantity. His scholarship and talent have yet to be tested before the public. He has not been seen by others [in this capacity]. He is not someone who became president gradually like others; Pang's appearance is truly meteoric. <32> His becoming a millionaire by suddenly acquiring a gold mine was meteoric, and his throwing that money all at once into the newspaper business was also meteoric. Also, unlike others who become president supported by those around them, he became one through his own initiative. He is a person with the decisiveness to break traditions and the magnanimity of a rustic warrior burning with a great cause. The business director is Mr. Kim Ki-bŏm, a spirited newcomer who learned his trade over many years amidst the commercial and industrial scenes of Osaka and Tokyo.

Facing this brain trust organization of Chosŏn Ilbo, how does Tonga Ilbo fare? At Tonga Ilbo, Mr. Song Chin-u serves concurrently as Editor-in-Chief, with Acting Director Sŏl Ŭi-sik, Research Director Yi Yŏ-sŏng, Political Director Kuk Ki-yŏl, Social Affairs Director Hyŏn Chin-kŏn, Economics Director Ko Chae-uk, Literary Arts Director Sŏ Hang-sŏk, and others. Editorials are written by Sŏl Ŭi-sik, Yi Yŏ-sŏng, Ko Yŏng-hwan, Chu Yo-sŏp, Hyŏn Chin-kŏn, Ko Chae-uk, and others.
Under these gentlemen, more than 30 talented reporters with rich knowledge and experience are active.

However, we must make a point here. The core of all matters ultimately comes down to the quality of personnel, and this is even more true on the Peninsula, steeped in great events and ideas. Looking at the figures in Tonga Ilbo's leadership organization mentioned above, while there are several outstanding individuals, we find some who are not well-known socially, who have never become friends of the populace, who have not operated within the understanding of society, causing us to feel a touch of sorrow upon realizing they are members of this top organization. The president is Mr. Song Chin-u. Known to the world as a hero of one faction, his steadfast and indomitable will, his spirit aspiring from capturing a tiger to seeking a dragon (得虎望龍), and his outstanding statesmanship (經世) are widely understood by society and the public. And Vice President Chang Tŏk-su, currently traveling abroad, is already established as a political figure of passion, talent, and intellect.
With these two gentlemen working together, the great figure (傑物) from Honam, Mr. Kim Sŏng-su, sits enthroned (鎭座) as the proprietor, and Kim Pyŏng-no, Kim Yŏn-su, Hyŏn Sang-ch'ung, Ch'oe Tu-sŏn, and others form a coalition to assist in the great East Asian construction. Also directly among the writers are Chŏng In-bo, Yun Paek-nam, Yi Ŭn-sang, and literary figure Chang Hyŏk-chu also graces the pages with his novels.
The business director is Mr. Yang Wŏn-mo, renowned as a financier with outstanding management skills and credited with building today's great Tonga Ilbo.

Tonga Ilbo's strength, whatever one may say, lies in the foundation of advertising and readership built consistently over 16 years. About half of the 400-plus branch offices across the country are consistent branches that have remained unchanged for 16 years. Accordingly, the readership also consists mostly of loyal subscribers. Compared to this, Chosŏn Ilbo, being younger, has a more fluid readership. Advertising is also similar. No matter how much is spent on promotion, advertising does not gather overnight. Tonga Ilbo's 16 years of pioneering efforts are now bearing fruit. Chosŏn Ilbo's advertising base still lags behind Tonga Ilbo's.

Let me add one superfluous remark. Is Tonga Ilbo not perhaps too intoxicated (취하는) with its 16-year tradition? Among the employees, the majority are long-serving staff of over 10 years, and it is said that branch managers with 16 years of continuous service number below 200. Sixteen years is one-fifth of a century and one-third of a 60-year lifespan. It is indeed a long time. While we can sense the innate quality in their perseverance and endurance, sitting there decorating the same pages and selling the same paper every day for 16 years, isn't it difficult to avoid ossification, fixation, stagnation, and stagnation? If one sits for 16 years just listening to the antenna signals coming from the outside world, isn't there a danger of becoming a fossil carved from stone with ears and eyes?
Dr. Hainan of Osaka Asahi once lamented that there is no one as ignorant of worldly affairs as a newspaper reporter. Because they are always inside the newspaper office, they lack opportunities to understand the actual atmosphere of the outside world and easily become fools. I am by no means saying this about Tonga Ilbo, but rather stating the principle that even water stagnates if left in one place for 10 years, and mountains gather moss after 10 years. If I may make an inappropriate suggestion, it is that Tonga Ilbo might benefit from adding a bit more novelty (新味) to its pages and personnel. <33>
Beyond this, social contribution activities such as relief efforts for disaster victims and promoting literacy campaigns and Danish gymnastics exercises during student vacations are common practices (通例) for both companies and not particularly noteworthy.

3. Conclusion
In Korea today, there are no representative organizations and no political stage, leaving newspaper companies as the only enterprise capturing universal attention.
And now, the newspaper business on the Peninsula is being pioneered with unprecedented vigor. This is indeed a very interesting and thankful development.
Finally, I sincerely hope that both companies prosper. And since I bear no grudge or favor towards either company, I have strived for impartiality in my writing. One more point: the reason I did not discuss Chungang Ilbo, despite it being another private paper, is that Tonga Ilbo's immediate (當面) adversary is Chosŏn Ilbo, and Chosŏn Ilbo's immediate (목전) adversary is Tonga Ilbo. Therefore, I temporarily set it aside as being outside the main competitive sphere. I hope there is no misunderstanding.

The War of the Six Major Record Companies
It's a flood of records.
It's the golden age for record artists.
For middle-class families who have no entertainment other than records, the only thing they seek is records. The time when only Columbia and Victor were battling each other has become nothing more than a tale from the distant past. Although the numerous large and small gramophone shops across Korea, numbering over 300, release nearly 50 new titles from each company every month, 1,000 or 2,000 copies of each title sell easily.

Looking at the companies producing Korean-language records and their prices:
Victor - 1 won 50 chŏn, Columbia - 1 won 50 chŏn, Polydor - 1 won 50 chŏn, Siren - 1 won, Taihei - 1 won, O-Keh - 1 won.
These six companies are currently engaging in fierce commercial strategies, spurring on their literary departments, and straining their minds to sell many interesting records.

Now, looking at the exclusive artists affiliated with each company:
Victor: Yi Ae-ri-su (currently recuperating), Kang Sŏk-yŏn, Ch'oe Nam-yong, Chŏn Ok, Kang Hong-sik
Columbia: Ch'ae Kyu-yŏp, Kim Sŏn-ch'o, Im Hŏn-ik, Kim Sŏn-yŏng, Ch'oe Myŏng-ju
Polydor: Wang Su-bok, Wang P'yŏng, Kim Yong-hwan, Sin Il-sŏn
Siren: Kim Yŏn-sil, Na Sŏn-kyo, Kim Yŏng-hwan, Ch'oe Hyang-hwa, Namgung Sŏn
Taihei: Yi Nan-yŏng
O-Keh: Sin Pul-ch'ul, Chŏn Ch'un-u, Sin Ŭn-bong, Sŏ Sang-sŏk, Paek Hwa-sŏng
Most are either popular singers or people with special talent in non-sense [comedy]. Thus, the records produced by squeezing the blood and oil, so to speak, of the twenty-five exclusive artists from these [six, though only listed 5 sets] companies become the choked cries emerging from the loudspeakers heard on the streets.

As there are many record companies and competition intensifies, battles over artists are inevitable. Wang Su-bok and Ch'oe Myŏng-ju are both P'yŏngyang kisaeng. After meeting an acquaintance who was a musician in P'yŏngyang and honing their voices, they first recorded for Columbia. Indeed, their results were astonishing. <34> However, as soon as Columbia's literary department head, Mr. Yi Wŏn-bae's position shifted, before the chaos settled, Polydor stealthily snatched Wang Su-bok away. O-Keh pursued Ch'oe Myŏng-ju relentlessly, wearing out their shoes, but she ultimately settled down with Columbia. Chŏn Ok first recorded for Polydor but quietly became exclusive to Victor. Mr. Sin Pul-ch'ul, after making noise about temporarily recording for Columbia, ended up joining O-Keh, creating humorous pieces. Mr. Sin Pul-ch'ul's breakout work was the comical "Bald Head."
A recent incident that startled competitors was Siren's "Miss Chosŏn" affair. It is clearly a pseudonym for Miss Kim Sŏn-ch'o, an exclusive singer for Columbia Records. How did Siren's literary department manage to snatch another company's exclusive artist and have her record? According to Mr. Yi Sŏ-gu, head of Siren's literary department, he pretends it was recorded before Kim Sŏn-ch'o became exclusive to Columbia and is just being released now. Whatever the case, it has become a topic of conversation that they have clearly pulled off a stunt comparable to a princess's acrobatics.

So, how much do they actually receive in recording fees?
Since every company keeps the details secret, there's no way to uncover them, but for a popular song, per side (한따불에 - likely means per recording session or per side):
Grade A: 100 won, Grade B: 60 won, Grade C: 30 won, Grade D: 20 won approximately.
For non-sense [comedy] or drama, depending on the number of people:
Grade A: 120 won, Grade B: 80 won, Grade C: 40 won approximately.
For Kisaeng singing:
Grade A: 70 won, Grade B: 50 won, Grade C: 20 won perhaps.

What needs consideration here is that none of the companies have recording studios in Kyŏngsŏng. They generally travel to Tokyo or Osaka to record. Consequently, unnecessary travel expenses are incurred. The travel cost alone, including round-trip second-class fare, lodging, and guide fees, amounts to roughly 100 to 150 won per person. Here lies the difficulty for Korean records. Recording far away under time pressure means there is little room to fully consider the artist's health and mood, and unnecessary travel costs are incurred. This makes it difficult for recording fees to rise.

Now, let's look at the lineup of the literary departments, which hold the life-or-death issue for these six companies:
Victor - Yi Ki-se; Columbia - An Ik-cho; Polydor - Wang P'yŏng; Siren - Yi Sŏ-gu; O-Keh - Kim Nŭng-in; Taihei - Min Hyo-sik
Yi Ki-se and Yi Sŏ-gu both come from the newspaper world; Kim Nŭng-in is a graduate of Yŏnchŏn [Yonhui College?]; Wang P'yŏng is from the playwriting department of Yŏn'gŭksa [Theater Research Society]; An Ik-cho is said to be an agriculture graduate. Min Hyo-sik is not a literary man but maintains his position through skill and passion.

One final word I cannot withhold concerns the birth of a new Korean sound called "Ch'ang'gŭk" (창극). The "Tragic Song of King Tanjong" (端宗哀曲), a work into which Victor's literary department poured its heart and soul (심혈을 기우려), truly gave the public a great feeling (늣김을) and shone a ray of light (一道의 光明) on the path of Korean traditional music, which had been blocked and causing frustration. In this respect, its contribution cannot be considered small. Following this, although it did not achieve great success, Siren released "Changhŭngmong" (長恨夢) sung by Nokchu (綠珠) on <35> upper and lower sides, apparently yielding considerable profit. In the future, major companies will surely compete (압흘 닷호와) to record and release this Ch'ang'gŭk. And the public, tired of hearing the same old songs day after day, having heard the new taste and new feeling of Ch'ang'gŭk, will clearly seek out only Ch'ang'gŭk. Thus, the popularity of Ch'ang'gŭk, similar to Japan's "Naniwabushi" (浪花節), will be astonishing.

Generally, how many records need to be sold to break even? It is said that 2,000 copies avoid loss (밋지지 안코), and sales beyond that yield great profit. In Korea, some records have sold 5,000-6,000 copies, and some have even sold over 10,000 copies. On the other hand, some sell only 400-500 copies and stop. These probably incur losses of several hundred or even 1,000 won per title (한 장에 - likely means per title/matrix, not per physical disc). Among the six companies listed above, which one will achieve final victory? In other words, which will monopolize the record market? Which will accumulate the most money? It is not clear now amidst the great scramble.
Incidentally, Victor's factory is in Yokohama, but its headquarters are in the United States, and its capital is American-affiliated. Recently, significant capital from Mitsubishi and Sumitomo is said to be flowing in. The aforementioned Yokohama factory alone reportedly has a site of 6,000 p'yŏng, building floor space of 3,000 p'yŏng, and including machinery and facilities, is valued at 3.5 million won. Its counterpart, Columbia, is a British-affiliated company. It acquired Japanese gramophone companies like Nitto Chikuonki and Tokyo Chikuonki in Shōwa 3 [1928] to become Nihon Columbia. It goes without saying that the majority of shareholders are British.
Besides these, Polydor is German-capital affiliated, while Siren, O-Keh, and Taihei are Japanese-affiliated.
The six companies release 600 new titles a month. If each title sells 5,000 copies, that's 3 million copies per month, totaling 36 million copies a year. Even at 1 won per copy, it's a battle for a huge market worth 36 million won. Competition is bound to reach a white heat (白熱化).
Indeed, while Korean records sell 5,000 or 10,000 copies at best, Japanese records, if they hit, are truly astonishing. Victor's "Shima no Musume" (島の娘) sold 300,000 copies, Victor's Fujiyama Ichirō's "Boku no Seishun" (僕の靑春) sold 200,000 copies. Older hits like "Tokyo Kōshinkyoku" (東京行進曲) and "Sake wa Namida ka Tameiki ka" (酒は淚か溜息か) sold 200,000 copies.
Accordingly, the recording fees paid to artists are surprisingly high. For Yoshizumi Kosaburō recording 20 Nagauta pieces for Nitto, the fee was 160,000 won. For Sadanji recording Marubashi Chūya for Nitchiku [Nihon Chikuonki], the fee for just one record was 20,000 won. Nitto paid Kiyomoto Enjudayū 100,000 won. Truly dream-like stories.
In Korea too, as gramophones become more widespread, more records will be sold. Consequently, we can anticipate the competitive struggle among the six major companies intensifying. (The Oil War section will appear in the next issue due to space constraints.) <36>

**Summary**

This 1933 article details two major commercial battles unfolding in colonial Korea: the fierce rivalry between the established Tonga Ilbo newspaper and the newly revived Chosŏn Ilbo, and the burgeoning competition among six major record companies (Victor, Columbia, Polydor, Siren, Taihei, O-Keh). The author analyzes the newspapers' financial strength, personnel, and future plans, predicting a protracted struggle where one might eventually fall to second-tier status rather than outright bankruptcy or cooperation. The piece then shifts to the "record war," highlighting the scramble for popular singers (often kisaeng), the high costs associated with recording in Japan, the emergence of Ch'ang'gŭk (a new narrative song form), and the immense potential profits illustrated by massive record sales in Japan, contrasting sharply with the smaller Korean market. For historians, this article offers a fascinating glimpse into the intense commercial and cultural competition filling the void left by suppressed political life in 1930s Korea, revealing the significant capital investment (both Korean and foreign) in media and entertainment, the dynamics of talent acquisition, and the nascent development of a Korean popular music industry under Japanese influence and competition.

						草兵丁
露西亞石油對米國石油의 石油戰
빅토아 콜넘비아 太平 시에론 포리돌 오-케이 大會社의 蓄音機戰

東亞日報 對 朝鮮日報 新聞戰
16년 동안을 金城鐵壁가치 다 지어노은 東亞日報의 아성에 돌연히 今春부터 부활된 朝鮮日報가 10년 정기를 묵근 듯한 인재의 陳容과 數十萬이란 거액의 자금으로 巨砲를 발사하기 시작하야 그것이 6개월을 지난 오늘에 이르러는 獨佛兩國의 國運을 決하든 벨탄要塞爭奪戰가치 또 大奈翁의 흥망을 그 一戰에 걸든 워-털루의 激戰가치 아조 白熱化하여 此一戰에 패하는 신문사는 영영 꺽구러지고 말니라 하게까지 피차에 社運을 賭하는 大戰이 개시되고 마럿다.
東亞日報가 꺽구러지나? 朝鮮日報가 꺽구러지나? 「つちかーつを倒して止まん」하는 相克된 두 신문사의 이 陳容! 이것은 1933년 가을의 半島市場을 장식하는 최대 景物의 하나가 아니되잘 수 업다.
우리는 이 싸홈의 결말을 세 가지로 관측할 수 잇다.
첫재. 한참 경쟁하다가 협조하여 並進하리라.
둘재. 그 협조가 不調되어 그 중의 一社는 力이 盡하여 제2류 신문으로 자처하고 競爭圈外로 ＜30＞ 逸落하리라.
셋재. 他一社는 영영 망하고 말니라.
그런데 첫재는 우리의 상식으로 생각하여 낼 수가 업다. 大阪每日과 大阪朝日이 雁行하드시 並進하리라하는 관측은 맛지 안으리라. 그것은 朝鮮의 역사적 전례와 현재 두 신문사를 構成運轉하는 首惱者의 인격 及 투지로 보아 도저히 생각해 낼 수 가 업다.
셋재도 그러케까지 극단으로는 가지 안을 터인즉 결국은 數三年 후의 두 신문사의 지위는 둘재의 案에 해당되고 말니라. 그러면 東亞日報와 朝鮮日報 둘 중에 어느 것이 2류 3류로 떠러지나. 어느 사장이 붓그럼을 무릅쓰고 센트 헤레나의 孤島로 쫏기어 가나?
나는 이 판단의 소재를
1. 두 신문사의 資力
2. 두 신문사의 人才配置
3. 두 신문사의 將來計劃
의 세 가지 방면으로 陳列하여 보리라.

1. 三增政策의 將來
東亞日報는 35만원의 돈을 가지고 쓰고 잇다. 70만원의 주식회사가 반액을 拂込하엿슴으로 35만원은 완전한 현금이 드러와섯다고 보는 것이 올켓타. 이 35만원 중에 주식회사 되기 전에 쓴 돈 10여 만원을 회사가 될 때에 물어주고 인수하여 왓스니까 그것이 純消耗된 세음이고 그 餘는 집 짓고 땅 사고 기계와 비품을 작만하엿고 선전과 사업비용으로 써서 현재 고정되어 어러붓지 안은 순전한 運轉資金이 14, 5만원에 달한다고 한다.
東亞日報社의 資産은 얼마나 됩니까 하고 뭇는 말에 宋社長의 대답은 이러하엿다.
「이 집(光化門通)네 거리의 現社屋」을 지으려고 2회 불입 175,000원을 하엿는데 그것이 부족되어 약 8만원 가량을 더 너헛스니 건물에만 23, 4만원이 드럿고 輪轉機 세대 활자100萬字 기타 諸備品에 10만원이 드럿고 현재 기지가 十字閣附近에 잇는 것까지 모다 1,000평 가량되는데 每坪300원식 잡으면 30만원에 달함니다. 이리하야 이것 저것 모다 헤면 자금이라 할 것이 75만원 가량이 됨니다. 35만원 불입 회사에 2배 이상의 재산이 잇다면 우습게 들니겟지만 10여년 전에 사 노은 땅이 자연 증가된 관계와 무배당으로 由한 年年의 저축이 이러케 된 것이지요.」한다. 책임잇는 이의 말을 詮索함은 인격자에 대한 실례이기 우리는 東亞日報의 현재 자산을 75만원이라 보고 그러고 또 不久에 제3회 제4회 불입까지 완료되어 남어지 절반인 35만원 돈이 마저 드러오게되면 적어도 100만원대의 돈을 가지고 잇는 신문사가 되어지리라고 본다.
그러면 一方 朝鮮日報는 如何한고. 16년전 창간된 이래로부터 申錫雨時代 李昇馥時代 林景來時代에 쓴 돈까지 헨다면 50만원 이상을 넘을 터이지만 그것은 잠간 별 문제로 하고 순전히 方應模氏 손으로 今春 주식회사 창립이후의 관계만을 따저 보는 것이 간편하겟다. 그것은 신문사는 一聯된 16年來의 가튼 朝鮮日報社로 되 今春의 개혁은 社의 구성과 인재의 배치 등 과거관계를 一掃하고 새로 재건한거나 다름업는 새 신문사가 되엇기 까닭이다.
최근의 사실이기 누구나 알터이지만 朝鮮日報는 최근에 30만원 그 다음에 20만원의 增資가 잇서 50만원 전액 불입의 회사가 되엇다. 이 50만원중 光化門通에 1,100평의 토지를 10만원 주고 사옥을 지으려고 삿고 건축비로 20만원을 따로 내어 노앗고 輪轉機와 비품 등으로 數3만원이 투하되어 잇고 선전비로 얼마간 入費되엇슴으로 運轉資金으로 ＜31＞ 씨울 돈이 약 10여 만원이 잇는 세음이다. 그러나 朝鮮日報는 경우에 의하야 제3차의 증자를 단행 할 기세라 하니 요컨대 두 신문사의 자력은 현재 伯仲之勢에 잇스며 압흐로 막상막하할 것으로 보는 것이 올켓다.
또 두 신문사에 모다 「발전의 癌」이라 할 부채가 업다. 그리고 東亞日報의 取引銀行은 海東銀行이요 朝鮮日報의 取引銀行은 銀行이다. 두 社가 자기의 取引銀行에 대하야 「클레짓트」의 설정이 구체적으로 되어 잇지 아니하다 할지라도 만일의 경우에는 수만원 정도의 信用貸는 밧을 수 잇슬 것이다.
그리고 두 신문사의 「三增」을 보자. 三增이라 함은 「增資」「增築」「增頁」이다.
사옥의 증축은 東亞日報社는 明春에 약 20만원 예산으로 적어도 압흐로 30개년간은 狹隘을 늣기지 안을 정도의 증축을 단행하기로 되엇다는데 이 돈은 증자에 의하지 안코 지으리라 하니 그 동안 이 만한 돈을 축적하엿든 듯 朝鮮日報社屋의 신축은 토지에 10만원 건물에 약 20만원을 투하할 터인데 필요에 응하야 증액 증축할 것이라 한다.
그리고 增頁이다. 민간신문의 頁數로서의 발전속도는 경이에 植한다. 6, 7년 전까지 4頁紙이든 것이 다시 6頁이 되고 再躍하야 8頁이 되고 3躍하야 10頁이 되엇다. 이제는 12면으로 혹은 16면으로의 飛躍이 남어 잇다. 지금 형세에는 어느 신문이든 16頁이나 12頁으로 增頁한다면 다른 신문사도 칼박고 뛰음뛰기로 그에 따라가고 말 必至의 勢에 잇다. 그런데 신문 수를 증가한다 함에는 의거하는 토대가 잇서야 한다. 그것은 광고분량의 증가율이다. 大阪每日 朝日은 매일 50段 이상의 광고량이 잇다. 朝鮮의 신문도 현재의 10면을 유지하자 하면 기본적으로 매일 30段이 잇서야 한다. 이제 12면식 혹 16면식으로 느리자면 40段의 광고가 필요하게 된다. 이 광고의 증가가 업시 增頁한단 함은 商道에 버서지는 無謀의 일이다. 그럼으로 두 社에서는 선전비를 써 가며 東京大阪의 광고를 느리기에 전력한다. 그래서 현재 東亞日報는 年18萬行의 廣告行數를 20만 돌파의 예정으로 邁進한다고 한다.
그러니 「增資」「增資」「增頁」의 3增은 이상과 갓다. 요컨대 3增에는 아직 優劣이 보이지 안는다. 時日이 오직 해결하여 줄 것이다.

최근에 동업자를 놀낸 사건은 시에론의 미스朝鮮事件이다. 콜넘비아社의 전속 가수로 잇는 金仙草 孃의 變名이 분명하다. 남의 회사 전속가수를 시에론 문예부은 무슨 재조로 가로차다가 취입을 식혓나. 시에론 문예부장 李瑞求 氏의 말을 드르면 金仙草가 콜넘비아의 전속이 되기 전에 취입햇든 것을 이제 안 판다고 시침을 뗀다. 엇잿든 분명히 공주곡예에 필적한 作亂을 해 노왓다고 이약이 거리가 되야 잇다.

마즈막 一言을 앗기지 못할 것은 唱劇이라는 새 朝鮮소리의 출생이다. 삑타의 문예부에서 그야말맛다나 심혈을 기우려 力作한 端宗哀曲은 과연 대중에게 크나큰 늣김을 주엇스며 갈 길이 막혀 가슴을 치든 朝鮮소리에 一道의 光明을 빗긴데 잇서서는 그 功이 적다고 볼 수가 잇다. 뒤를 이워 비록 큰 효과는 거두치 못하얏다 하나 「시에론」에서 長恨夢을 綠珠의 唱으로 ＜35＞ 上下編을 발매하야 상당한 수익이 잇는 모양이다. 압흐로 반다시 이 唱劇은 名社가 압흘 닷호와 취입발매할 것이요 세상에서도 밤낫 가튼 소리만 듯다가 새 맛 새 늣김나는 唱劇을 드른 이상 唱劇만 차즐 것은 명확할 것이니 이로써 日本의 「浪花節」에 흡사한 唱劇의 유행이 놀나울 것이다.

대체 레코-드는 몃 장만 팔니면 採算이 되는고 하면 2,000매면 밋지지 안코 그 이상 팔니면 大利를 남는다고 한다. 조선서도 5, 6000매 나간 것도 잇고  1만여매 나간 것도 잇다. 그 대신 한편에는 불과 4, 500枚 나가고 마는 것도 잇다. 이런 것은 아마 한 장에 수백원 또는 1,000원씩 밋질엇이다. 지금 이 우에 기록한 여섯 회사 중 어느 것이 최후의 승리를 어들가. 다시 말하면 레코-드 시장을 독점할가 가장 만히 돈을 모을가. 지금은 大亂戰中이 되어 잘 알려지지 않는다.
그런데 삑타-는 공장이 橫濱에 잇는데 본사는 米國이요 자본도 米國系이다. 이것이 최근에는 三菱과 住友의 자본도 만히 투입되어 간다고 하는데 前記 橫濱 잇는 공장만 하여도 基地가 6,000평 건물의 건평 3,000평 기계설비까지 350만원이라 한다. 여기 대칭되는 컬럼비아는 이것은 英國系의 회사다. 日東 蓄音機 東京蓄音機 등 日本人側 蓄音機會社를 昭和3년에 매수하여 日本컬넘비아 회사로 되엇다. 株主의 대부분이 英吉利人임은 물론이다.
