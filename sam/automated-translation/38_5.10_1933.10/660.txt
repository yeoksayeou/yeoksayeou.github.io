庭園의 鄕愁
						片石村

「아테네』의 거리 우헤 希臘의 한울은 고요히 綠玉의 나래를 드리우고 피곤한 『마돈나』처름 쉬고 잇섯다. 지중해의 푸른 몰결이 털어놋는 작은 한숨인 바람결에 가볍게 몸소름 치면서-
이 최초에 축복바든 땅을 헤서 스스로 싸흔 신화의 세계에 쇠사슬을 얼커논 자는 누구냐?

운명의 여신의 슬푼『엘레지』를 『아담』의 가족들이 그들의 魂의 문을 열고 불러드리기 시작한 뒤부터 유쾌하던 태양은 아직도 우리들의 지평선에서 우서본 일이 업다.

대학연구실의 直下에서는 동서남북 『콩크리트』의 철벽에 幽閉된 뜰 안의 들추어내 논 이 작은 가슴이 숨차서 헐덕인다. 행복한 어머니의 품을 떠난 대지의 한쪼각은 이러케 병드러 누어잇다.
빗 업는 뜰 안이여 가난한 너의 호주머니는 학살된 돌쪼악으로 가득하고나. 일즉이 그것들은 太平洋의 엇던 해안에서 밤을 기다려 별들의 속삭임을 엿들고 잇섯다. 그러나 지금은 『후록코-트』를 입은 교수를 시른 『시보레-』 바퀴에 깔녀 부스러저 그스러 버린 그것들의 시체의 새파란 얼골들을 나의 마음은 어르만지고 잇다.

뜰 안의 한 구석에 힌 유령과 가티 몸을 쪼그리고 서 잇는 잣나무 하나. 머-ㄴ 길을 거러온 바람의 팔에 몸을 맛겨 오졸오졸 떨고 잇는 겸손한 그 모양-
『삘딍』의 검은 집웅에 걸안저 끗업는 한울의 방랑에 지친 다리를 쉬며 낫잠 자는 태양의 얼골을 작은 잣나무는 처다본 일이 업다. 西班牙의 여자처름 갓가워 오는 황혼을 끄러안고 그는 운다. 늣겨 운다.

바다ㅅ가에 돌쪼악을 보내 주어라. 오늘도 홀몸인 거의는 바다에서 낙근 이야기들을 속삭이려 물결을 타고 도라왓건만- 구경군을 일허버린 별들의 노리도 銀河水가에서 흥이 시것다. 작은 새들은 그들의 꿈으로 짠 진주 목도리를 잣나무 억게에 거러 주려 들로부터 도라와서 기다리고 잇다. 산으로 잣나무를 보내주어라. ＜115＞

Garden's Nostalgia
						P'yŏnsŏkch'on

Above the streets of "Athens," the sky of Greece (希臘) quietly lowered its jade wings and rested like a tired "Madonna." Shivering lightly in the breeze, a small sigh released by the blue waves of the Mediterranean Sea—
Who is it that, from this first blessed land, forged chains upon the world of myths they themselves built?

Since the family of "Adam" opened the gates of their souls and began to invite in the sad "elegy" of the goddess of fate, the once-cheerful sun has yet to smile upon our horizon.

Directly below the university research lab, inside the garden confined (幽閉된) by iron walls of "concrete" to the east, west, south, and north, this small, exposed breast gasps breathlessly. A fragment of the earth, separated from its happy mother's embrace, lies sick like this.
O lightless garden, your poor pockets are full of massacred stone fragments (돌쪼악). Once, on some coast of the Pacific Ocean (太平洋), they waited for the night to eavesdrop on the whispers of the stars. But now, crushed under the wheels of a "Chevrolet" carrying a professor in a "frock coat," my heart caresses the pale blue faces of their crumbled, scorched corpses.

In a corner of the garden stands a single pine nut tree, crouching like a white ghost. Its humble form trembles timidly, yielding its body to the arms of the wind that has walked a long path—
The little pine nut tree has never looked up at the face of the sun, napping as it rests its legs, tired from endless wandering in the sky, perched on the black roof of a "building." Like a woman of Spain (西班牙), it embraces the approaching twilight and weeps. It weeps feelingly.

Send the stone fragments back to the seaside (바다ㅅ가). Today too, that solitary one returned, riding the waves to whisper stories caught from the sea—but the play of the stars, having lost their audience, has lost its excitement by the Milky Way (銀河水). Little birds have returned from the fields, waiting to hang a pearl necklace woven from their dreams on the pine nut tree's shoulder. Send the pine nut tree to the mountains. <115>

**Summary**

The poem "Garden's Nostalgia" by P'yŏnsŏkch'on uses the metaphor of a small, confined university garden to express a deep sense of loss, displacement, and yearning for nature and freedom. The garden, walled in by concrete, and its elements – broken stones originally from the Pacific coast, a lonely pine tree – are personified as suffering entities separated from their true homes and natural state. Contrasted with idealized images of ancient Greece and the vastness of the sea and mountains, the poem laments the encroachment of modernity (represented by the university, concrete, a Chevrolet) upon the natural world, portraying it as a kind of sickness or imprisonment. The poem is suffused with melancholy and a longing for reunification with a lost origin. For historians, the poem is interesting as it reflects the feelings of confinement and alienation potentially experienced under Japanese colonial rule in the 1930s. The "sick," fragmented garden yearning for its larger whole (the sea, the mountains) can be read as an allegory for Korea itself, oppressed by an external modernity and longing for its lost autonomy and natural integrity. The author's pen name, P'yŏnsŏkch'on (Village of Stone Fragments), further emphasizes this theme of fragmentation.
