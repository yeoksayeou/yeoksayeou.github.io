Magazine Title: 삼천리 제5권 제10호
Publication Date: 1933년 10월 01일
Article Title: 晩婚打開 座談會, 아아, 靑春이 아가워라! (Roundtable Discussion on Overcoming Late Marriage: Ah, Youth is a Waste!)
Participants/Speakers: Yi Kwangsu (李光洙), Na Hyesŏk (羅蕙錫), Kim Kijin (金基鎭), Kim Ansŏ (金岸曙), Kim Tonghwan (金東煥) (Listed as 本社側 出席諸氏 - Company-side Attending Gentlemen)
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Roundtable Discussion)

**Late Marriage Overcoming Roundtable Discussion: Ah, Youth is a Waste!**

Attendees from Our Company: Yi Kwangsu
Na Hyesŏk
Kim Kijin
Kim Ansŏ
Kim Tonghwan

**1. Why Aren't People Getting Married?**

Reporter: If you stand at the Chongno intersection for just thirty minutes and watch the young men and women coming and going, the majority have rough complexions, dull eyes, and seem depressed. Also, most move sluggishly, making it difficult to find any sharp, swift-swallow-like smartness.
When you look into these young people immersed in gloom and faded colors, most are unmarried men and women. These unfortunate men and women are living as singles despite being at the age when they should marry. This meeting was organized this evening hoping to find some way to rescue these individuals, even if just a little.
First, why aren't the young people of today getting married? Or why are they unable to?

Yi Kwangsu: I don't really know what women think, but speaking for men, firstly, in a world where it's hard enough to live alone, they can't see a way to manage a livelihood after getting a wife. So they generally delay, saying 'this year or next year,' and end up missing the marriageable age (혼긔).

Na Hyesŏk: That might be a point, but speaking of women in the bloom of youth, they've seen many cases where their seniors married and lived unhappily. So, they get fed up and don't even consider entering married life from the start. Really, how many men and women who received higher education and new learning enter marriage and have happy households? If you look at the statistics, aren't there more unhappy cases than happy ones?

Kim [Ōk - This name seems like a typo in the original, likely Kim Ansŏ or another participant, as Kim Ōk (김억) is not listed as attending. Assuming it's Kim Ansŏ or perhaps Kim Kijin continuing]: Whether it's due to education or not, the fault lies more in the personality differences between couples. Generally, looking at this thing called modern marriage, the conditions considered are beauty, education level, and wealth, but it seems no one gives thought to mutual personality compatibility. Because of this, they become mismatched couples, and the marriage is bound to fail after just a few days. So, to say that modern men and women avoid marriage because they fear this point <84> is perhaps misjudging the world.

Kim Kijin: That's right. The reasoning that "My seniors' marriages were bad, so I won't marry either!" doesn't make sense, I believe. Of course, there are those who failed, but on the other hand, think how many people live happily.
Rather, the reason why young men and women currently at the marriageable age (適齡期) are not marrying is that the surrounding circumstances steal the desire to marry from the individuals themselves.
It's purely an economic reason. At a time when the number of people who have lost their means of livelihood is increasing, how can one try to eliminate the evils of late marriage without guaranteeing food, clothing, and shelter? The problem always comes back to the fundamentals.

**2. Are Divorced Men Avoided?**

Reporter: I understand. Then, instead of speaking generally and vaguely about the reasons for not marrying, let's discuss each side specifically. Why do women, even after graduating from girls' schools and receiving higher education, grow old as "old misses"?

Kim Kijin: Because there's nowhere to go! Meaning, there are no potential husbands! It's because they are past their prime and in a difficult situation.
Indeed, it seems there's a shortage of grooms today. Among young men from their twenties to early thirties, is there anyone without a first wife? Whether by parental compulsion or whatever, they've all already married young (조혼). Still, there are some young men studying in middle schools, vocational schools, or universities who haven't married yet. However, these individuals, having already seen their seniors suffer from unhappy marriages since they were 16 or 17, resisting parents and resenting the system, have firmly decided not to repeat such misfortune themselves and are people of determination focused solely on their studies. Until they finish university and find a good job, they won't even entertain the thought of marriage, so female students can hardly consider these unmarried male students in school as potential partners ("하스" - husband?). And they dislike becoming concubines (첩).

Reporter: On the other hand, do you know how many divorced men there are in the marriage market?

Kim Kijin: Of course, there are. But how many men are truly divorced? Men who have completed the deregistration process on the civil register (민적상)? That's rare (이문 - likely means 'rare' or 'uncommon' here).

Reporter: Do new women welcome men who have even completed deregistration?

Kim Kijin: It seems so. After finding fault with everything, where else is there to go? Also, strictly speaking, divorced men are all victims of a unfortunate era; there's room for sympathy (동정).
Also, within my knowledge, parents with daughters used to jump up and down, saying, "Give my daughter as a concubine! Give my daughter to a divorced man!" But recently, they seem understanding, saying, "How about a divorced man's position? So-and-so's family also gave their daughter that way." Moreover, <85> there's a growing trend not only regarding divorce but also not minding if the potential husband has sons and daughters.

Na Hyesŏk: Even so, what about being divorced? While virginity (童貞) might be a virgin's ideal, it cannot be the absolute, sole condition.
If one can feel love for the person involved, that should resolve all issues.

Yi Kwangsu: That's right. It's a problem that can be solved if you ladies just change your perspective on men.

Kim Kijin: And as I mentioned earlier, for women, divorce means committing one's life to someone who provides food, clothing, and goes for walks together. So, everyone wants to get married. If you look at average families now, the daughter graduated from girls' school, the older brother is unemployed, and the household finances are worsening day by day, so they are burning inside due to reality. Therefore, I know everyone has a fervent desire to marry quickly, as soon as possible.

Reporter: Then why aren't men getting married? Is married life unhappy?

Yi Kwangsu: I recently read a Western novel titled "Free." The story goes like this: a husband nurses his sick wife devotedly, but she eventually dies. At that moment, he should feel overwhelming sadness, but the instant the doctor declares her dead, the thought "Ah, now I'm free! I'm liberated! I have freedom!" surges up, it says. It seems he was quite happy to be liberated from the pressures of his anxious wife and home life. I believe this novel skillfully captures and portrays the psychology of modern men.

Na Hyesŏk: Indeed, there are more unhappy marriages than happy ones. And the number of people advocating single life has increased significantly.

Kim Ansŏ: If things continue this way, the number of people avoiding marriage or intending to marry late will only keep increasing.

**3. How Can This Be Overcome?**

Reporter: Then, how can we overcome this trend of late marriage? I heard from a medical doctor that marrying late is not good. People become melancholic and lonely.

Kim Ansŏ: Yes, that's true. Even the most shrewish old miss becomes gentle and womanly after acknowledging the opposite sex.

Na Hyesŏk: However, modern single women show efforts to avoid all melancholy by leaning towards music or art <86>. In the West, they might even take up dancing (딴스).

Reporter: In short, although there are men and women who don't marry due to economic reasons and disillusionment seen in their seniors' marriages, isn't it that a much larger majority don't marry because they simply lack the opportunity?

Na Hyesŏk: In reality, there isn't really a marriage market.

Reporter: If there is, it's currently only department stores and schools. Among these, men cannot freely enter girls' schools, so they cannot openly become marriage arrangement places (모개소). Only department stores remain, and since anyone can see the shop girls (숍프껄 - shop girl) there, and most of those shop girls have received a considerable education, it's said that the number of men going to department stores to choose a woman they like has increased quite a bit. Sometime ago, an important executive at Mitsukoshi (三越) said that their department store's "salesgirls" (賣り子) usually get married within 2-3 months of being hired. That's how easily they supposedly get married.
Also, this is a story from Japan, but I saw in a Japanese magazine that Mr. Kobayashi Ichizō (小林一三), a businessman famous for Hankyū (阪急), newly established a coffee hall called Ginza Kissa (銀座喫茶) in Ginza (銀座), Tokyo. The waitresses working in this hall must:
1. Be entirely unmarried women (처녀).
2. Have finished higher girls' school.
3. Have beautiful features.
Thus, it is said that single men wanting to find a wife can come to this tea hall and freely socialize. I believe this could become a modern love market (戀愛市場) or marriage market (結婚市場). How about establishing such modern facilities in Chosŏn (조선) to provide ways for free socialization and free choice? What do you think?

Kim Ansŏ: I believe the opportunities for male-female socialization are not blocked. Anyway, if you get to know just one woman, you consequently get to know her various female friends, and the same goes for men.

Na Hyesŏk: The effort of a respectable "madam" (마담) is probably needed.

Reporter: What if we implement co-education right from middle school?

Na Hyesŏk: It would likely be difficult to realize.

Yi Kwangsu: Even without co-education, it would be good if the gates of educational institutions (學園의 門戶) were opened whenever there's an opportunity. Creating chances for contact (접혹) with the opposite sex through school concerts, sports days, and other <87> various gatherings would be beneficial, I believe.

Kim Kijin: Also, it would be good if society created more venues where literature enthusiasts could gather with fellow literature lovers, and music lovers with fellow music lovers, to talk freely and discuss.

Kim Ansŏ: That's right. There probably aren't any moralists (도학군자) left in this world who would feel danger in such gatherings anymore.

**3. Is Female Beauty Declining?** [Section number repeated in original]

Reporter: Isn't female beauty becoming less than before? Even visiting girls' schools, while there might be one or two devastatingly beautiful women (경국의 미인) in a class, the majority seem to lack the graceful classical beauty of the past, and many appear to have rather stark (살풍경하게) faces. Since beauty (美貌) is the life of a woman, if that "park of the human body" (人體의 公園) is not beautiful, no matter how much higher knowledge and virtue she possesses, won't she be evaluated poorly?
But doesn't it seem like the average beauty of modern women is regressing?

Kim Kijin: It absolutely cannot be seen as regression. Modern women's physiques and appearances are actually developing in harmony with the three-dimensional urban architecture built of iron (鐵) and stone (돌). It's a much more pleasant phenomenon.

Kim Ansŏ: That's right. Not only are there more outstanding beauties than in the past, but generally speaking, appearances have developed considerably from an aesthetic perspective.
It will likely develop even further in the future. Women's appearance will probably develop further along with the development of material civilization. Moreover, the clothes of modern women are truly nice. In the past in Chosŏn, clothing fabric and colors were invariably solid colors (純色), but recently, many wear fabrics with floral patterns (花草문의), and there's no color – blue, yellow, red, white – that they don't wear. It's much nicer to look at.

Kim Kijin: Besides that, the clothing system of Chosŏn women is originally beautiful enough to be ranked top in the world, and with the fabric also developing in a good direction, it's correct to view the overall beauty of women as significantly improving.

Yi Kwangsu: Physique is one thing, but modern women have good eyes. Those eyes, seemingly full of talent (재조) and wisdom (지예), always looking fresh, are endlessly nice. Not just the eyes, but the nimble, intelligent, and lovely expression floating over the entire face is nice. <88>

**4. From What Age Is It Considered Late Marriage?**

Reporter: In Chosŏn, after what age is someone already called an old bachelor or an old maid?

Na Hyesŏk: For women, twenty-three or four is the marriageable age (結婚適齡期), so anyone past that age falls into the old maid category.

Reporter: When do women feel the strongest sexual urges (성적 충동)?

Na Hyesŏk: Probably around the aforementioned age of 23 or 24, when they want to marry. When they yearn most strongly for the opposite sex.

Reporter: And men?

Kim Ansŏ: Up to 30 would be the marriageable age, I suppose. Past that, one would become an old bachelor, perhaps even a very old bachelor.

Kim Kijin: In the current situation, there are many men over 30. There are probably many women past 24 or 25 too.

Reporter: As a measure to alleviate the difficulty of marriage, if we could promote the trend of welcoming divorced men, I think many unmarried women could be resolved (해소 식힐 수). From the men's side too, if they were to accept women who had married once and returned, with the same attitude as towards a virgin bride (색시), wouldn't the marriage difficulty be significantly eased?

Kim Ansŏ: Ultimately, that's a matter of feeling ("氣持ち" - Kimochi), but somehow, there's bound to be a somewhat uncomfortable (께름한) aspect somewhere.

Kim Kijin: According to a biologist I heard, in a woman who has had relations with another man, it's unavoidable that his blood is mixed somewhere in the blood vessels of her body. Therefore, to preserve the purity of the bloodline (血統の純粹), it seems first marriage is indeed better.

Kim Ansŏ: Thinking that another fellow's (년석) blood is mixed in one's own child would be quite unpleasant. I don't know how women think about it.

Reporter: Thank you for speaking for such a long time. <89>

**Summary**

This 1933 roundtable discussion from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine features prominent intellectuals Yi Kwangsu, Na Hyesŏk, Kim Kijin, Kim Ansŏ, and Kim Tonghwan debating the causes and potential solutions for the perceived problem of late marriage (만혼) among young Koreans. Key reasons cited include economic hardship preventing men from supporting a family, women's disillusionment after seeing unhappy marriages of educated seniors, a lack of suitable partners (especially grooms without prior marriages), and insufficient opportunities for natural socialization between sexes. Proposed solutions range from changing attitudes towards divorced individuals, creating modern "marriage markets" like specialized cafes or utilizing department stores, promoting social gatherings based on common interests, and opening up school events for interaction, though co-education is deemed difficult. The discussion also touches upon changing beauty standards and the definition of marriageable age.

Historians might find this article particularly interesting for its candid reflection of social anxieties and changing mores in colonial Korea during a period of economic depression and societal transition. The participants' views reveal tensions between traditional expectations (parental roles, concern over divorce/remarriage, bloodline purity) and modern influences (economic pressures, new education for women, Western ideas like "freedom" from marriage, concepts of love matches, and evolving beauty ideals influenced by urban modernity). Na Hyesŏk's feminist perspective and the discussion around economic factors provide insight into the specific challenges faced by educated Koreans under colonial rule. The mention of Japanese examples (Mitsukoshi, Kobayashi Ichizō's cafe) also highlights the cultural influence and comparative context of the time.

1. 엇재서 결혼들을 아니하는가
記者-종로네거리에 삼십분 동안만 서서 가고 오는 청년남녀들을 보면 얼골 빗갈이 거칠고 눈에 정채가 업고 긔분이 우울하여 다니는 이가 대부분 임니다. 또 몸가짐이 느릿느릿하여 물찬 제비가튼 스마-트한 점을 발견할 수 어려운 이가 대부분임니다.
이러케 우울과 퇴색한 빗깔에 잠긴 청년들을 아라보면 대개가 결혼 아니한 남녀들임니다. 결혼하여야 할 년령에 처하여 잇스면서도 독신으로 지내는 이 불행한 남녀. 이 분들을 다소라도 건지어 줄 도리가 업슬가 하여 오늘 저녁 이 모임을 만든 것임니다.
몬저 엇재서 현대의 청년들이 결혼을 아니하는가요. 또는 못하고 잇는가요.
李光洙-녀자들 생각은 잘 모르겟스나 남자들로 말하면 첫재 저 혼자 살기도 어려운 세상에 안해까지 어더가지고는 생활을 도모지 하여 나갈 도리가 생기지 안으니 대개 금년이나 명년이나 하고 해마다 늑자추다가 그만 혼긔를 일코 마는 이들일걸요.
羅蕙錫-그러한 점도 잇겟지만 묘령의 녀성늘로 말하면 선배들이 시집가서 사는 것이 대개 행복스럽지 못한 꼴을 만히 구경하고 낫스니까 그만 진저리가 처서 애당초부터 결혼생활에 들 생각을 하지 안는 까닭이 만치요. 실상 교양이 놉흔 신학문 밧은 남녀로서 결혼에 들어 행복한 살님을 하는 이가 몃낫 되어야지요. 통게로 따저본다면 행복한 이 보다 불행하게 된 이가 더 만안가요.
金億-그야 교양의 유무보다 오히려 부부의 성격 차이에 죄가 만케지요. 대개 신식결혼 그 물건을 보건대 결혼 조건으로 도는 것이 아름다우냐와 학식이 잇고 업고와 돈이 잇고 업고를 생각하여 보지만은 누구하나 서로 성격의 조화를 염두에 두는 이가 잇는 듯 십지 안슴니다. 이러니까 맛지 안는 부부가 되어 그 결혼은 몃날 아니 가서 파탈이 생길 밧게 더 잇서요. 그러니까 이 점을 ＜84＞ 두려워서 현대 남녀들이 결혼 회피한다고 하면 그는 세상을 잘못 보는 탓이겟지요.
金基鎭-그러치요. 선배의 결혼이 납밧스니까 나도 아니하겟노라! 하는 리유는 당치 안을 줄 아러요. 그야 실패한 사람도 잇겟지만 그 반면에 행복스럽게 사는 사람도 엇더케나 만타구요.
그보다도 현재의 적령긔(適齡期)에 잇는 청년남녀들이 결혼을 하지 아니하고 잇는 까닭은 주위의 사정이 결혼할 생각을 당자에게서 빼앗는 까닭이지요.
그것은 순전히 경제적 리유지요. 생활할 길을 일허버린 사람이 늘어가는 때에 의식주의 보장을 주지 안코 엇더케 만혼의 페해를 제거하려 들겟슴니까. 문제는 근본에 늘 귀착이 되어요.

2. 리혼한 남성을 꺼리는가
