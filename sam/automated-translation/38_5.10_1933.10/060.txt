Sorrowful Circumstances
						An Sŏ (岸曙)

This body, a leaf turning with the wind,
Whirling aimlessly East and West,
Will this heart ever find a moment's peace?
Separations are so frequent, I weep.

Tears are the pillow for my elbow in shrimp-like sleep, <16>
Sighs come and go, a dream of longing.
Indifferent are these day-like sorrowful circumstances,
In the mirror, this face grows old alone.

The heart that swore, vowing never again,
Is like a sandcastle where the sea water ebbs and flows.
Though I raise a cup to resolve these feelings,
Past days reflect and swirl within the cup. <17>
<16-17>

**Summary**

The poem expresses deep sorrow and a sense of rootlessness, comparing the speaker's life to a leaf blown aimlessly by the wind. Themes include frequent partings, lonely nights filled with tears and longing sighs, the feeling of growing old alone amidst unchanging sorrow, and the futility of resolutions (likened to sandcastles) and attempts to drown sorrows in drink, as memories only resurface. From a historical perspective, the poem, written during the Japanese colonial period, resonates with a sense of personal displacement and melancholy. While ostensibly personal, historians might interpret this profound sadness and lack of agency as potentially reflecting the broader atmosphere of helplessness or quiet suffering experienced by Koreans under colonial rule, expressed through the individual voice of the poet.
