Yes, That's Right, Older Sister —Poetic Folk Song—
						Kim An-sŏ

Yes, that's right, older sister, your words are right
But still, older sister, please believe and live
The stream water flows and goes to the sea
Even the stream water flows and goes to the sea

It's unbelievable, older sister, unbelievable, isn't it
Even so, older sister, please keep believing
This is the heart that turns East and sets West
This is the heart that turns East and sets West

But still, older sister, what can be done?
Even so, older sister, please endure
The flower blossom lives trusting the wind
Even the flower blossom lives trusting the wind

Rain at the Port

Softly, softly, rain falls at the port,
The rain falling silently at the port,
Is it perhaps these tears of mine, longing for my love?
Softly, softly, it makes the grains of sand weep.

This rain, which might not come if I wait and wait,
But comes unwantedly when I am sorrowful,
Are you weeping for the tearful heart
Of my love who left without even meeting?

Though the sun sets amid the softly falling fine rain,
The seawater surges into the port
My love, who comes and goes a thousand li following the water routes,
In what place will they weep the night away?

Cosmos

Where the gentle breeze comes and goes,
A white cosmos flower blooms alone,
Its face reflected in the mirror-like stream,
Fluttering, white, it flows away.

When the autumn wind rustles and struggles,
The white flower, heart sorrowful, may sigh,
But when the stream water holds it in its embrace,
Smiling brightly with joy, it welcomes it.

On an evening when the cold wind blows in late autumn,
The cosmos flower, fluttering to and fro,
Is tormented by the frost throughout the night;
Pitifully, blossom by blossom, it ends up falling. <114>

**Summary**

This article presents three poems by Kim An-sŏ under the collective title suggesting a "poetic folk song" style. The first poem addresses an "older sister," urging belief and endurance despite doubt, using metaphors of flowing water and a wandering heart. The second, "Rain at the Port," expresses sorrow and longing for a departed lover, comparing the rain to tears. The third, "Cosmos," depicts the fragile beauty of cosmos flowers blooming in autumn, reflecting in a stream, enduring the wind, but ultimately succumbing to the frost. From a colonial history perspective, the poems are notable for their lyrical and personal focus on themes of nature, longing, and endurance, rather than overt political commentary. This inward turn and the adoption of a folk song style ("俗謠") might reflect the limited avenues for expression under Japanese rule or a conscious effort to connect with Korean traditions and sentiments during a period of cultural suppression. The gentle melancholy could subtly mirror the atmosphere of the era.

浦口의 비
보슬보슬 浦口에 비가 옵니다,
소리 업시 浦口에 내리는 비는
님 그리는 이내의 눈물이든가,
보슬보슬 모래알 울녀 줍니다.

올가 올가 기두면 아니 오다도
설은 때엔 애꾸지 오는 이 비야
만나지도 못하고 떠나신 님의
눈물겨운 심사에 네가 우느냐.

어느 곳서 이 밤을 울며 새릿가.

커스머스

나풀나풀 하얏케 흘러 갑니다.

갈 바람이 우수수 몸부림하면
심사 설어 하얀 꼿 탄식하다도
시냇물이 품 속에 안아주면은
해적 해적 깃버서 반겨듭니다.

늣 가을에 찬바람 부는 저녁을
나풀나풀 넘노는 커스머스 꼿
밤을 두고 서리에 시달니우니
송이송이 가엽다 지고 맙니다. ＜114＞
