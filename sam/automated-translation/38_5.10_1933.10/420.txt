Folk Song Collection (民謠集)

Song of Sorrow (愁心歌)
East wind, west wind blow,
Sleet and rain fall,
In times of peace and plenty,
I long for my beloved.

Cuckoo (자규야),
Do not cry.
If you must cry, cry alone.

Why have you come
To wake me,
Who was asleep?

Embracing the mountain,
Holding the water, I turn to look,
But that mountain will not change
To become my beloved.
Truly, I miss my beloved.

Banga Ballad (芳兒打鈴)
Good, it is good!
Amidst the falling leaves blown by the wind <68>
The weeping Mr. Kim (김생).
If heard without concern,
It has nothing to do with me, but

In the vast blue heavens,
Sound after sound,
Eheru, it is sorrow.

Good, it is good!
On the wide waters of Och'odongnam (오초동남),
The merchant ships coming and going,
With sails hoisted in the fair wind,
Beating the drums, *duri dungdung*,
The sound of weighing anchor, *ŏgiŏch'a*,
Returning sails from distant shores (遠浦歸帆),
Ehehe-ra, is this not it?

Nanbongga (難奉歌)
Heaven and Earth (乾坤) do not age, the Moon remains ever-present,
The lonely rivers and mountains mark a hundred years now.
Ehee eh elhwa, good, it's good!
Oh how wonderful, my love!

The magpies (길날애비) fly away,
Fluttering, fluttering.
My precious, precious love.
Ehee ehe elhwa, good, it's good!
Oh how roundly, my love! <69>
<68-69>

**Summary**

This article presents the lyrics of three traditional Korean folk songs (*minyo*): *Susimga* (Song of Sorrow), *Panga T'aryŏng* (Banga Ballad), and *Nanbongga*. *Susimga* expresses deep longing for a loved one amidst descriptions of nature. *Panga T'aryŏng* contrasts the sorrowful sound of weeping with the lively scene of merchant ships. *Nanbongga* juxtaposes the permanence of nature with the passage of time and expresses affection with energetic refrains. From a historical perspective, the publication of these folk songs in a major magazine like *Samch'ŏlli* during the Japanese colonial period is significant. It reflects an effort to preserve and disseminate Korean folk culture amidst pressures of assimilation, showcasing traditional sentiments of sorrow, longing, and appreciation for nature and life, which could offer historians insights into cultural continuity and expression during that era.
