Discussing Social Issues with Mr. Abe Isoo, Head of the Social Masses Party
						Changnanggak

Mr. Abe Isoo recently came from Tōkyō to Sŏul, stayed for two days, September 21st and 22nd, and returned. Mr. Abe was famous as a former professor of political science at Waseda University, and as we already know well, he has recently been busy working for the working class as the leader of the proletarian party. The writer visited Mr. Abe at the Chosŏn Hotel and spoke with him for a long time. The initial intention was to start by asking how Chosŏn, or rather Chosŏn and the Chosŏn people, appeared in his eyes, and then take a step further to inquire what he thought should be done about the various problems of Chosŏn. However, upon hearing his candid expression of feelings (吐情), I realized that this would not be possible due to various difficult circumstances. Therefore, I decided to discuss general (not specific) social issues instead.
One thing to add is that Mr. Abe is as renowned a Christian as Yamamuro Gumpei or Kagawa Toyohiko. Therefore, his words had a tendency to start all issues from Christianity and conclude back to Christianity. Readers reading this article should understand this point.

1. Relationship between Christian Activities and the Social Movement
Me: It is a pleasure to have you here. September in Chosŏn is said to be a truly wonderful season. The weather is fine, and even Westerners often praise and leave the clear blue autumn sky without a single cloud. Since you came at a good time, please enjoy the scenery of Chosŏn much before you leave.
Abe: Yes, thank you. Actually, the Kanfu Renrakusen (Pusan-Shimonoseki Ferry) last night was hit by such severe waves that I was quite shaken, but this morning, coming by train from Pusan, seeing the beauty of the lakes along the Naktonggang River and the beauty of the peninsula's mountains washed away all my fatigue. Sŏul is an ancient capital, so there are many sights; I intend to look around freely before returning.
Me: What is the purpose of your visit this time?
Abe: Although I am usually too busy to leave Tōkyō, the 25th anniversary of the Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul) YMCA is taking place here, and they asked me to come and give a lecture, so I barely managed to leave. I will stay for only two days and return immediately to Tōkyō. <37>
Me: Are you a religious believer, sir?
Abe: Yes, I am a Christian.
Me: If you are a Christian, how does that harmonize with the social movement? Firstly, ideologically, shouldn't one be an atheist, that is, a materialist? And from the perspective of the movement, shouldn't one move beyond humanism and the principle of mercy?
Abe: Not at all. Christianity and the social movement are by no means contradictory. Rather, they can be seen as two sides of the same entity. In the 9th year of Taishō (1920), I traveled through America (米洲) and various parts of Europe (歐羅巴) and observed the facilities for the poor in each country, finding that most were run by Christian hands. Where I couldn't visit the facilities directly, I went to libraries and looked at books. The New York Library (紐育圖書館) alone had over 1,000 reports from various social work institutions. Visiting England (英吉利), Germany (獨逸), France (佛蘭西), etc., I found about 2,000 large social institutions established and managed by Christian churches. The social problem – to put it more concretely, the problem of poverty, the problem of relieving the poor – should truly be faced sincerely from a humanitarian standpoint of love for humanity. In this regard, I believe the power of Christianity will indeed grow larger.
Me: Temporary poverty relief work (救貧事業) or last-minute charity work (臨渴堀井) can surely be done by the government of the time, and certain private individuals can also manage it adequately, can't they?
Abe: No. That might have been possible in the old days when the number of poor people was small, but in times like today when the unemployed are everywhere, who could possibly handle all of it? Isn't there an old saying that even the state cannot relieve poverty?
According to recent statistics, America (亞米利加) has 3 million unemployed, and Europe has close to 8 million unemployed. Within Japan (日本) alone, the Home Ministry's (內務省) statistics report 480,000 unemployed. This number is increasing. As the depression deepens, it will likely increase further in the future. What should be done with all these people? All these numerous unfortunate people.
Me: Then are you saying that these unemployed people can be saved by the power of the Christian religious movement?
Abe: There is a saying in the Bible. If someone asks for your lower garment, give them your upper garment as well. Also, if someone slaps your left cheek, turn your right cheek to them as well. If only all humanity could practice these words, wouldn't it work? My ideal is that if everyone just gives up the idea of 'living a luxurious life' (贅澤な生活さへしなければ), all social problems will be solved. If people just abandon the thought of luxury, I believe it will surely be resolved.

2. View on Tolstoy
Me: So, have you yourself reached the state of mind (심경) where you would give your upper garment if someone asked for your lower one?
Abe: To confess honestly, I have not yet reached that point. However, I believe I will be able to reach it before long. It has been over 53 years since I started believing in Christianity. I was baptized during my third year at Meiji Gakuin (明治學院), which was in the Meiji (明治) era. At that time, while studying economics at Meiji Gakuin, the truth I realized was this message spoken by Jesus. Later, after reading Tolstoy's 'Theory of Religion' (宗敎論), I held this thought even more firmly. Tolstoy is my teacher. In his 'Theory of Religion,' he argued for revising the Bible passage, suggesting that the words 'without reason' (故なくして) in the phrase 'Do not strike a person without reason' were likely added by his disciples and should be removed. Thinking about it, Tolstoy's words are correct. Does anyone strike others or live in a fine house and own many sets of clothes without a reason? Right or wrong, everyone has their own reasons. Therefore, unconditionally, one should not possess two sets of clothes; unconditionally, if asked for trousers, one should give their jacket; unconditionally, if told to offer the right cheek, offer the left cheek.
If only this spirit becomes thorough, our society will be reformed. The poor will decrease, and all social problems will be resolved.
I believe that 'someday' (何時かは) this ideal will be reached.
Me: But isn't it too distant (遼遠하지) for everyone to reach such a state of mind? Didn't even Tolstoy ultimately fail to renounce his title of Count and die without becoming possessionless (無一物)? <38>
Abe: Tolstoy was like that because of his wife. He wanted to distribute the vast lands of the Count's estate to the peasants and become just a plowing farmer himself, but because of his greedy wife, he couldn't achieve his goal. The valuable bed of the Count's house, the fine white horse, and other furniture – if it had been up to Tolstoy alone, he would have given them all away to the tenant farmers (소작인). Noticing this, his wife took them away and put them elsewhere, so he couldn't fulfill his wish. But this is not something to despair about.
Me: If so, if it can be achieved through religious movement alone, why did you start the proletarian movement and are now the leader of that political party?
Abe: Because it can also be promoted (촉진식힐) through political means.
Me: How can social problems be solved politically?
Abe: Let me give one example – simply put, doesn't the problem of unemployment stem from overproduction (생산과잉)? Each country wants to sell as much of its own goods as possible and buy as little as possible from other countries, leading to surplus products, accumulation of stock, and subsequently, unemployment, right? Therefore, to prevent overproduction, wouldn't it be sufficient if each country imported from other countries only as much as it exported to them? For instance, if Japan bought 500 million yen worth of goods from America (米), Britain (英), India (印), and Australia (濠), then wouldn't it work if other countries also bought 500 million yen worth of goods from Japan?
To control this, wouldn't it work if each country dispatched 5 or 10 trade officials to an organization like the League of Nations (國際聯盟) to reside there and handle mutual settlement (互相決濟) between nations?

3. Recent Activities of the Proletarian Party and Others
Me: If one becomes a true Christian, can they wash away the concept of hating others? Even between societies or ethnic groups with different scopes of life (生活範籌)?
Abe: I believe so. Some years ago, at a lecture meeting at Tōkyō Imperial University (東京帝國大學), Kagawa Toyohiko was also present, and we discussed this issue. Kagawa mentioned that when he was studying abroad in America, the memory of Americans contemptuously calling him "Jap Jap" was deeply ingrained in his bones, and if he hadn't become a Christian, he would have gnashed his teeth (切齒腐心) and thought of taking revenge on Americans. However, although I have traveled extensively in America and foreign countries, I have never once thought of foreigners as hateful. Once, while passing by the fountain in the central park of Berlin (伯林), two German women walking beside me saw me, nudged each other's waists, and mocked me, but even then, I felt nothing. In any situation, it depends on one's state of mind (心法). Humanity must love one another. The spirit of Christ can make humanity love each other.

Me: What is the current situation of the proletarian movement in Japan?
Abe: As you can see, it is in a dormant state. I believe the way to break through this lies in greatly promoting the cooperative movement (協同組合運動).
Me: How do you view Fascism (팟쇼)?
Abe: Seeing the results of Mussolini's (뭇소리니) four or five years in power in Italy (伊太利) and Hitler's (힛트러) two years since taking power in Germany, I oppose Fascism. Fascism has not yet shown a solution to economic problems, has it? (Remainder omitted - original text)
Me: Thank you for speaking with me for so long. <39>

**Summary**

This 1933 interview from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine features Korean journalist Changnanggak speaking with Abe Isoo, a prominent Japanese Christian socialist, professor, and leader of the Social Masses Party, during Abe's brief visit to Seoul. Abe discusses the harmony between his Christian faith and socialist activism, citing Christian-run social welfare institutions abroad and the teachings of Jesus and Tolstoy as foundational. He proposes international trade balance managed through a League of Nations-like body as a political solution to unemployment caused by overproduction. Abe expresses his belief in universal love overcoming ethnic animosity, contrasts his experience with Kagawa Toyohiko's, touches upon the dormant state of Japan's proletarian movement, suggests promoting cooperative movements, and voices his opposition to Fascism based on its performance in Italy and Germany. For historians, the article offers a glimpse into the thinking of a major Japanese leftist figure during the colonial period, his idealized Christian socialist perspective, his cautious avoidance of directly addressing Korean issues despite being interviewed in Seoul, and the state of socialist thought in Japan facing rising nationalism and economic depression. The interaction itself, between a Japanese political figure and a Korean journalist, is noteworthy in the context of colonial power dynamics.

社會大衆黨首 安部磯雄氏와 社會問題를 語함
						滄浪客
이번 東京으로부터 安部磯雄氏가 서울에 와서 9월 21, 22의 兩日間을 체재하고 도라갓다. 氏는 前早稻田大學 정치과 교수로도 유명하엿거니와 최근은 無産政黨의 수령으로 勤勞階級을 위하야 분주하고 잇슴은 우리들이 이미 잘 아는 바와 갓다. 필자는 氏를 『朝鮮호텔』에 차저 장시간 이약이 하엿다. 처음의 의도는 氏의 눈에 비쵠 朝鮮 다시 말하면 朝鮮과 朝鮮人을 엇더케 보는가 하는데서부터 시작하야 한 거름 더 나아가서 朝鮮의 諸問題를 엇더케 하엿스면 조켓느냐 하는 것을 質코저 하엿스나 氏의 담백한 吐情을 듯고 이것이 여러 가지 거북한 사정이 잇서 그리못할 것임을 늣기엇다. 그래서 나는 다시 일반적의 (특수적이 아니다)사회문제에 대하야 이약이를 난우기로 하엿다.
한 가지 첨가할 말은 氏는 山室軍平이나 賀川豊彦氏와 並肩하리 만치 기독교도로써 著名하다. 그러기에 氏의 말은 모든 문제를 기독교에서 출발식혀 다시 기독교에로 歸納식히는 관습이 잇섯다. 이 점은 이 글을 읽어 주시는 讀者諸氏가 이해하여 주어야 할 것이다.

1. 基督敎活動과 社會運動關係
나―이러케 오시니 반갑슴니다. 朝鮮의 9월은 참으로 조흔 시절이라고 함니다. 기후가 조와서 一點雲이 업는 새파란 가을 하늘을 서양사람들도 만히 찬미하고 가는 터인데 조흔 때에 오섯스니 朝鮮의 風光이나 만히 賞玩하시고 가십시오.
安部―네. 감사함니다. 실상은 어제 밤 關釜連絡船이 엇더케도 풍랑이 심하엿든지 퍽으나 혼이 낫는데 오늘 아츰 釜山서 차타고 오며 보니까 洛東江沿岸의 湖水美라든지 半島의 山岳美에 모든 피로를 다 씨서 버럿슴니다. 서울은 古都이니 여러 가지 풍물이 만허 마음대로 구경하고 도라가려 함니다.
나―이번 오신 뜻은.
安部―원악 분주한 몸임으로 東京을 떠날 수 업지만은 마츰 이곳 京城 基督敎靑年會의 25주년 기념이 잇게 되어 날더러 와서 강연하여 달나기에 겨우 떠난 터이외다. 이틀 동안만 滯在하고 곳 東京으로 도라 가겟슴니다. ＜37＞
나―선생은 종교신자임니까.
安部―네. 기독교도올시다.
나―기독교도라면 그와 사회운동과가 엇더케 조화됨니까. 爲先 思想上으로 무신론자 즉 유물론자이어야 올켓고 運動上으로보아 人道主義 慈悲主義를 버서나서야 올흘 터인데요.
安部―아니지요. 결코 기독교와 사회운동은 모순이 아니됨니다. 도로혀 一體의 兩面이라 볼 수 잇서요. 나는 大正9년에 米洲와 歐羅巴各地를 도라다니며 各國의 빈민에 대한 시설을 보고 왓는데 대부분이 기독교의 손으로 되여가고 잇습데다. 미처 시설을 답사하지 못한 곳은 도서관에 드러가 책을 보앗는데 紐育圖書館에 비치된 사회사업의 각 기관의 보고서만 1,000여 책이 잇고 英吉利 獨逸 佛蘭西 等地로 가 보아도 약 2,000個所의 큰 사회기관이 시설되여 잇서 기독교회에서 경영하고 잇습데다. 사회문제- 좀 더 구체적으로 말하면 가난의 문제 가난한 사람의 구제문제는 진실로 人類愛의 인도적 견지에서 진심으로 당하여야 할 터인데 이에 대하여는 기독교의 힘이 과연 커질 것으로 봄니다.
나―일시적 救貧事業 또는 臨渴堀井의 자선사업은 그야 때의 政府에서도 할 수 잇고 어느 특수한 개인들도 넉넉히 할 수 잇지 안슴니까.
安部―아니지요. 그것이 녯날가치 빈민의 수효가 적엇슬 때는 그리할 수도 잇섯지만 오늘과 가치 실업자가 간 곳마다 차 잇는 때에 누가 잇서 一非 그것을 다 할 수 잇슬가요. 녯말에도 가난은 나라에서 조차 구제하지 못한다고 하지 안어요.
최근 통계를 보면 亞米利加는 300만의 실업자가 잇고 歐羅巴에는 800만에 갓가운 실업자가 잇다 함니다. 日本國內만 하여도 內務省의 통계에 48만명의 실업자가 잇다고 함니다. 이 수는 작고 느러감니다. 불경기의 심각화에 따라 압흐로도 더 늘어 갈 것임니다. 이 사람들을 다 엇더케 함니까. 이 만흔 불상한 사람들을.
나―그러면 그 실업자를 기독교의 종교운동의 힘으로 구제하여 낼 수 잇다고 말슴함니까.
