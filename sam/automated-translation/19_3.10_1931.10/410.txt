The Rose
						Original by Turgenev, Translation by Yi Yŏngch'ŏl

The end of August... Autumn is already upon us.
Just as the sun tilts towards the western mountains... it drives forth thunder, lightning, and a downpour.
The visible surroundings are reddish in the evening glow—
She sits slumped in the parlor chair, seemingly lost in deep thought, gazing blankly out the half-open glass window towards the front of the garden.

I know well what thoughts she is lost in.
That she has been seized by a strange emotion, irrepressible even for a moment of intense anguish—
She softly rises and goes out into the garden, then disappears.

An hour... then another hour... she does not return.
I go outside and walk the path she walked.
Already, the surroundings are a pitch-black night! On the gravel path wet with rain, I see something darkish—dark, yet distinctly reddish-looking—and bending down, I pick it up: a fresh rose! The very one I saw on her breast two hours before.

I return again to the parlor and gently place the rose on her desk.
Her footsteps approach softly, softly; her face is paler and bluer, yet her relatively narrow eyes seem pleased.
Discovering the rose, she holds it in her delicate hand, brushes off the mud-stained petals (화변), and looks intently at me.

A moment! Her eyes seem submerged in sorrow, tears welling up.
"Why do you weep?" I ask her.
"Look at the rose. It's become so ugly, stained with mud like this."

Meaningfully, I say,
"Your tears will wash away all the mud."

"Will tears wash it? It seems they would burn it away!"
Finally, she throws the rose into the dying brazier fire,
"Fire burns better than tears!"

Her lovely eyes, still submerged in tears, smile faintly.
I realize. That she too was in the fire. <101>

**Summary**

This translated prose poem by Turgenev depicts a melancholic scene where a narrator observes a woman seemingly in distress. After she disappears into the garden during a storm, the narrator finds a rose she wore, now soiled, on the path. Upon her return, the woman rejects the idea that tears can cleanse the rose, choosing instead to burn it in a brazier, implying fire is a more fitting purifier for something tainted. The narrator concludes by realizing the woman herself is enduring a similar internal "fire." For historians of colonial Korea, this piece is interesting as an example of the reception and translation of Western (specifically Russian) literature during the period. It reflects the contemporary Korean literary language and sensibilities, showing how foreign emotional themes were adapted. The translation by Yi Yŏngch'ŏl into 1930s Korean, including its specific orthography and vocabulary, provides linguistic data, while the theme of sorrow, cleansing, and perhaps tainted purity might resonate with broader cultural or social anxieties of the time, albeit expressed through a foreign literary lens.

薔薇
						트르계 넵(원작), 李永哲(번역)
8월도 마즈막... 벌서 가을이 닥처오도다.
태양은 바야흐로 서산에 기울 제...천둥 번개와 함께 소낙비를 모라가네.
보이는 환경이 저녁 노을에 붉으레 한데-
그 여자는 객실 의자에 비스듬이 안저 깁픈 생각에 잠기는 듯 반쯤 열닌 유리창으로 멀거니 뜰 압플 바라다보네.
나는 그 여자가 무슨 생각에 잠기는지 잘 아노라.
격렬한 고민 순간이나마 억제할 수 업는 이상한 감정에 사로 잡힌 바 됨을-
그 여자는 살몃이 니러나 뜰노 나가더니 살아저 바렷노라
한 시간... 또 한 시간... 돌아오지 안커니.
나는 박그로 나와 그 여자가 거러간 길을 거닐엇도다.
임이 주위는 어둠으로 캄캄한 밤! 비에 저진 자각돌 길 우에 나는 검읏한 것을-어두엇으나 확실히 불그레한 듯한-을 보고 허리를 굽혀 집어보니 생생한 장미화! 두 시간전 그 여자의 가슴에서 보든 것이엿만
나는 다시 객실로 도라와 그 여자의 책상에 그 장미를 살작 노핫나니.
삽븐삽븐 거러오는 그 여자의 발자최 얼골은 더 햇슥하고 파랏컷만 비교적 가느스름한 그의 실눈은 깃븐 듯.
장미를 발견한 그 여자 간얄핀 손에 불녀 놋코 진흙 무든 화변을 털면서 유심히 나를 바라다 보네.
찰나! 그 여자의 눈은 비애에 잠긴 듯 눈물이 핑그르르 도나니.
「왜 우시우.」 하고 나는 그 여자에게 물엇노라.
「장미화를 보서요. 고만 진흙이 무더 이럿케 숭해젓스니.」
나는 의미 잇게
「그대 눈물이 진흙을 다 씻츠리니.」
하고 말하매
「눈물노 씻기우릿가? 태워바릴 듯!」
필경 그 여자는 장미를 꺼저가는 화로불에 던지며
「불은 눈물보다 더 잘 태우리니!」
그 여자의 사랑스러운 눈은 여전히 눈물에 잠겨 잇으면서 살작 우섯도다.
나는 깨달엇노라. 그 여자도 역시 불 속에 잇섯다는 것을.＜101＞
