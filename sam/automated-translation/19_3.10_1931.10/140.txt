The Numbers of Chosŏn Laborers
						By Yi Yŏsŏng

Total Number of Laborers
How many laborers might there be in Chosŏn?
The sharp increase in farmers leaving villages due to rural overpopulation (mainly referring to artificial overpopulation), the growth of urban unemployed groups, the development of modern industry within Chosŏn and the rapid increase in the number of factories, and the development of civil engineering projects—these facts have inevitably produced a large number of laborers. However, because Chosŏn's capitalism did not grow from within the womb of the agricultural economy but rather flooded in like a rapid current from the outside, the agricultural economy suffered only destruction without construction. Consequently, the potential recruits for the new economic industries were suddenly pushed out onto the streets, and naturally, industrial institutions capable of absorbing them were not yet prepared. Thus, countless unemployed proletarian groups have mostly become outdoor casual laborers fighting unemployment, with only a small portion becoming factory laborers. The remainder have become completely unemployed, facing starvation and wandering aimlessly.

According to the above, the total number of Korean laborers is 1,136,017, accounting for 93.8% of the total 1,184,004 laborers in Chosŏn. Japanese laborers number 22,491, representing 1.8% of the total, and Chinese laborers number 26,496, not exceeding 2.2%.
Now, comparing the total number of Korean laborers, 1,136,017, to the total Korean population of 18,667,334 (from the survey at the end of Shōwa 3 [1928]), it accounts for only 6.1%. However, if we add the lowest stratum of the rural population, the tenant farmers (pure tenant farmers), representing 1,255,954 households with one person per household, and combine this with the number of laborers, we get a mass of 2,391,971 people, representing 12.8% of the total (Korean) population. Furthermore, if we add the total number of part-farmer/part-laborers and the unemployed, 1,085,110 people, the sum reaches 3,478,001, accounting for 28.6% of the total population. However, since this figure of 3,478,001 represents individuals who each head a family averaging 4 to 5 members, it actually signifies between 13.9 million and 17 million people. How astonishing is this number!

Number of Factory Laborers
Next, let us look at the number of factory laborers. Factory laborers are indeed the core of modern industry, and their social position is more significant than that of the bourgeoisie in the 18th century. Although it has only been a few decades since Chosŏn's agricultural laborers began to proletarianize, their numbers still constitute only a very small portion among the working class itself. However, compared to the year of annexation, their numbers have also increased at an astonishing tempo. Citing figures for factory laborers obtained from the recent Government-General Resource Survey:

Number of Government-Run Factories: 14 locations

Total Number of Factories: 4,033 locations

Total Number of Personnel: 100,604 persons

(Note) In Meiji 45 (1912), there were 15 factories with 8,330 employees.
(Based on the Shōwa 6 [1931] Government-General Resource Survey.)

According to the table above, the total number of factory personnel in Chosŏn reached 100,604. (We find this is over 50,000 more than what was surveyed by the Chosŏn Railway Association Railway Affairs Investigation Committee. Although the survey years differ by about two years, such a large difference is unlikely. The major reason is that the Chosŏn Railway Association survey and the Government-General survey used different definitions for a factory unit.)
That is, the Government-General survey recognized establishments employing 5 or more workers as factories, whereas the Railway Association likely calculated using a much higher unit threshold. The usefulness of these two statistical surveys lies in the former showing a figure that provides some basis for knowing the total number of Chosŏn laborers, and the latter being somewhat more specific in revealing the total number of factory laborers.
Now, the figure 100,604 does not represent the number of laborers themselves, but the total number of factory personnel, including staff. Therefore, it seems correct to subtract about 10,000 from this number. Why<41> is this? Because in the Shōwa 4 (1929) Government-General factory personnel survey statistics, we find that among the total personnel of 92,566, 9,138 were factory staff. Considering that the total personnel increased by 8,038 by the Shōwa 6 survey, it is appropriate to estimate the staff number at around 10,000 and subtract it from the total personnel figure, thus counting about 90,000 as factory laborers. (It should be noted that magazines, newspapers, etc., have often mistakenly reported the number of 'personnel' as if it were the number of workers; this is an error).

The fact that laborers engaged in the foodstuffs industry sector account for 34% of the total laborers clearly indicates that Chosŏn is a stereotypical agricultural country. That the textile industry holds the next position is likely because the cheap and abundant labor and raw materials in Chosŏn strongly attracted Japanese textile industry capital. Furthermore, the advanced industries (metal, machinery, refining, etc.) that are most developed in leading nations are still in a very rudimentary state in Chosŏn, thus occupying the lowest ranks even when viewed by the number of employees.

**Summary**

This 1931 article by Yi Yŏsŏng analyzes the number and distribution of laborers in colonial Chosŏn (Korea). It presents statistics showing over 1.1 million Korean laborers, comprising nearly 94% of the total workforce, but only 6.1% of the Korean population. The author argues that including tenant farmers and the unemployed reveals a much larger proletarianized mass (potentially 28.6% of the population, representing 14-17 million people when families are considered). Factory labor, though growing rapidly since annexation, numbered around 90,000 workers (after adjusting personnel figures), concentrated in food processing and textiles, reflecting Chosŏn's role as an agricultural supplier and source of cheap labor for Japanese capital, while heavy industry remained underdeveloped. The author highlights the destructive impact of externally imposed capitalism on agriculture and the resulting large pool of underemployed or unemployed labor. For historians, this article offers valuable contemporary statistical data (despite noted discrepancies between surveys), a critical perspective on the socio-economic transformations under colonialism – specifically the nature of early industrialization, the proletarianization process driven by agricultural distress, and the structure of the colonial labor market. The author's attempt to quantify the broader working population beyond registered laborers is particularly revealing of the era's social pressures.

조선에 노동자 수효는 엄마나 될가?
農村人口過剩(주로 人爲的 인구과잉을 가르침)으로 農民離村者의 激增, 都市失業群의 증대, 조선內 근대적 산업의 발달과 공장수의 激增 밋 토목사업의 발전, 이가튼 사실은 필연적으로 多數한 노동자를 製出하게 되엿다. 그러나 조선의 자본주의는 농업경제의 胎內에서 성장된 그것이 안이오 외부의 자본주의가 급류 가티 물녀든 그것이기 때문에 농업경제는 건설 업는 파괴만을 닙게 되여 新經濟産業候補軍은 갑작이 街道로 쏘다저 나오게 되엿스니 그들을 흡수할 만한 산업기관이 미처 준비되지 못하엿슬 것은 물론이다. 따라서 무수한 失業無産者群은 겨오 그 일부만이 공장노동자化 하엿슬뿐 대부분은 屋外 자유노동자가 되여 실업과 싸호고 잇는 현상이며 其餘는 全然 失業群이 되여 饑饉에 당면하야 정처업시 流離하고 잇는 形便이다.
