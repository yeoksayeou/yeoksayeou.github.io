Jealousy and Rice
						Yŏm Sang-sŏp

Just as dawn broke, S rushed over.
As I was about to fall back into my morning sleep, I waited for S, who had come right up to the porch, then got dressed haphazardly and went out onto the wooden floor.
"Sorry to bother you. Let's step out for a moment."
S wanted to pull me away so my younger brother and wife wouldn't hear, fearing embarrassment.
Guessing what it was about, I wasn't entirely without curiosity, but finding it bothersome, I followed him out towards the mountain, partly to get some morning air.
"Didn't sleep a wink last night. She charged in last night, you see."
He poured out his agitated feelings as if a major incident had occurred.
"Hmph! That's why they say if you want your feet comfortable, wear oversized socks, and if you want your body comfortable, live with one woman!"
It wasn't that I was angry about losing my morning sleep, but I felt annoyed at his utter lack of tact in foolishly bringing them into one house.
"What could I do? She chased me down in a rickshaw in the middle of the night."
"Lucky she didn't just load you onto the rickshaw!"
"Well, what was I supposed to do? If I didn't tell her the address, I'd be held captive for days. She snatched my Western suit jacket and demanded it."
"She didn't snatch your trousers?"
"Surely not my trousers."
"Lucky you—" I chuckled aloud.
Speaking from the perspective of siding with the first wife might be morally wrong by principle (道理), but I thought, regardless of the first wife or principles, one must first eat—survive—doesn't one?

I knew S’s wife from his main family (본댁) had come up when S’s younger brother came looking for me yesterday morning, pestering me to tell him his brother's address.
"I don't know. I know it's around here, but what can I do if he's hiding it even from me?"
Inwardly, I wanted to tell him, but why would I betray S's confidence when he had pleaded so earnestly, trembling with fear? I managed to restrain the gesture that almost pointed the way. If the first wife had come up to start housekeeping again, I might have told him, even at the risk of hearing complaints later from S or S’s second wife (작은집).
But what good would it do to provide for S now, when he depends solely on the second wife’s disposal even for the smoke rising from the chimney morning and evening, let alone the smoke exhaled from his two nostrils? It wouldn't matter if his first wife, or even his first wife's grandmother, came.
"What should we do? Should we somehow grab hold of her and provide for our sister-in-law?" Even when the younger brother consulted me like this, I advised against it.
"That won't do. If you care for your sister-in-law, let her go back down as she is. If a fight breaks out now, the affection between your brother and his wife will be shattered forever. A first wife's jealousy (투긔) is commonplace, but it stems from a lack of shrewdness. In the end, she’ll cling to him. In today's world, would a smart woman with money feed and support her husband? It won't last long anyway, so just leave <105> it alone. Whenever they eventually separate, won't he run back to his first wife, whether he likes her or not? Is she worried he'll find another woman because he has money? She has nothing to worry about."
"But they say he's settled down now, so it's even more frustrating that he's acting like this again because of that woman."
"That's just how it is. Reaching forty and just beginning to understand her husband's affection, only to lose it again, must be tantalizing, but it won't work that way. Just do as I say. Haha..." I found my own words amusing.
"But does she have anywhere to go?"
"Her family in the countryside or her parents are said to be rich, so wouldn't there be somewhere, like her parents' house, where she can stay?"
"Who would ask her to come and stay?"
"Living with one's parents at forty would be embarrassing, and it hasn't even been two years since she came up after selling the house due to family ruin. If she were to go to her brother-in-law's house looking for food, no one would welcome her, but why did she let the household fall apart just because he wanted it to? Now, even if she dislikes it, there's nothing she can do. Even if the concubine is well-off, she wouldn't feed the first wife's family too, right? And since your brother is sitting there with his hands clasped, unable to move, isn't he stuck? What's the pleasure in just sneering, thinking 'Because I resent my husband or brother, should you alone live comfortably?'"
"Who knows? Given my sister-in-law's temper, she won't go back down without meeting him."
"Don't do that. You should hurry up and get married, then take care of your sister-in-law. You still have your share, right?"
"Mine? It's been ages since my brother completely snatched away even the flea's blood worth I had."
I realized this was the main reason he had absolutely no sympathy for his brother. I had heard that S's younger brother and my younger brother weren't schoolmates but were close friends from the neighborhood (마장 동모). When they talked about S amongst themselves, they apparently didn't call him 'hyungnim' (older brother) but used his full name. Calling him 'hyung' (brother) when speaking to me was, despite everything, an attempt to maintain appearances.
Sometime after the younger brother left, S came over agitatedly (as I had already guessed) and asked, almost resentfully, if I had revealed his address. "What if I had? Was it only your promise? Did I ever agree to keep it a secret?" I laughed it off.
"It's ruin! Ruin!" S became increasingly furious.
Later, I heard that the younger brother had frantically searched the neighborhood, peering into any house without a nameplate, and finally caught his brother sitting on the edge of the porch. As a result, I ended up receiving complaints from both brothers. Of course, he would also tell his sister-in-law that I hadn't revealed the address, so she would surely complain and badmouth me. My ears didn't itch, but engaging in such thankless tasks when I had no stake in the matter was pointless.
"What kind of situation is this? Without anything gained, I just become the odd one out, inside and out!" I complained back, but in reality, justifying myself was more urgent than complaining. Lately, all I seemed to do while sitting around was listen to baseless complaints and make excuses.
S seemed to have changed his mind instantly.
"So, what would be best?" he asked again, seemingly embarrassed, seeking advice.
"Do as you see fit. How could I possibly know who you find prettier or have more affection for, even if they were my own children? Though I know the first wife is generally decent..."
I tried to brush it off with a joke.
"As a third party, looking at it coolly, what would be the right thing to do?"
"They say the wife of one's youth shouldn't be discarded; abandoning her brings punishment."
However, the words I offered with effort didn't seem to register.
"I'd like to prepare some travel money, send her off quietly, and bless you to live happily ever after with your young, pretty, and wealthy second wife (제이부인), your heads growing white as scallion roots together like Su Wu and Gui Da, but it seems your fate isn't written that way. Also, that would be too one-sided, so try living, flanked by your left and right madams, even if it's physically taxing, for as long as you can."
I had nothing left but jokes.
It was obvious: he had ditched one household because he wanted a new one, so now, despite the criticism, his only option was to cling to the second wife. But this clear path seemed unclear to S, which was frustrating and pitiful. <106>
From days before, he had been saying that he expected the second wife to bring about a thousand won, buy a house outright, install him as the husband, and provide well for him, feeding and clothing him as he wished. But contrary to his expectations, she brought only a few hundred won, pinched every ten-jeon coin, and narrowed her eyes calculatingly, so all that built up was annoyance.
"I'll tell her to go back down again! If I'd known it would be like this, I regret not leaving the first household as it was!" he lamented, but each time, rather than laughing at or disliking S's unrealistic thoughts, I felt pity for him. He spoke with integrity, but lacked self-awareness that this integrity was, in truth, about nothing.
"If you were in my shoes, what would you do?"
"I'm not 'Takebayashi Musōan,' so it's somewhat beyond my imagination..."
S had been calling himself 'Takebayashi Musōan' self-mockingly ever since he started living with the second wife (referring to Takebayashi Musōan, who reportedly lived off his wife's earnings in Paris at the time).
"Damn fool!"
Muttering his usual curse, he left. I knew he was heading straight to his first wife's place. This morning at dawn was the first time we met since then.

S wanted me to go across to his house with him, but I refused, saying what business did others have in such matters. I would listen if he brought up stories I hadn't asked about, and occasionally offer advice or counsel, but I wasn't in a position to jump into a fight between co-wives (시앗 싸홈), nor did I want to do something foolish from which I couldn't even recover my investment.
"She says she's leaving right now. Please come and stop her. She won't listen at all. If I lose her at this point, what will I do? Maybe I should just head to the Han River."
S's sleep-deprived eyes welled up.
"She's not just a virtuous woman, but a virtuous husband, it seems."
I cracked this joke, but it didn't feel like a situation for just joking around. Then, was there any sharp solution? If it were a matter of knife-fighting or immediate danger of murder, I would go and intervene, but this was someone else's private affair. Still, one side was a modern woman who understood the world, while the other, though I hadn't met her, was a traditional woman about whom I could only guess based on common sense. From the sound of it, last night, right after S left, she had chased him in a rickshaw, stood in the middle of the yard with her hands behind her back asking, "Is this S's house—Ch'oe Chŏng-ok's house—," refused to come up when asked, haggled stubbornly for a while, and only came up after the second wife paid the rickshaw fare. Then the three of them stayed up all night without a wink of sleep, quarreling fiercely. If that was the usual course of events, it seemed plausible, but she appeared quite spirited and stubborn. If someone like me ineptly intervened there and got slapped with a "Who the hell are you?", where would I recover my investment!
"I'm bowing out."
"Hey! I know it's an imposition, but I saw her packing her things just now. What should I do?"
Throwing away all dignity and pride, he pleaded like someone trying to grab a fish floating in water. It was a completely different demeanor from the day he moved into this house, when he had stormed out after arguing with me over money matters at my place, shouting angrily for her to go back down, then calling me out and bustling about as if ready to head straight to a pleasure quarter (요정).
After thinking hard, I sent my housemaid with a message from my wife: "Hearing you are leaving today, she felt sad and prepared breakfast, asking if you could drop by for a visit." The idea was to separate the occupants of the main room (안방 식구) and the side room (건넌방 식구), appease the main room occupant accordingly, and manage the side room occupant accordingly, because having them in the same house prevented any clear conversation and would only stir up more conflict.
The second wife didn't come. Left with no choice, I had to make the trip myself.
Except for the baby's sling, all her belongings were hastily packed into a large wicker trunk, standing prominently in the middle of the room.
"What's all this? A bit large for a fugitive's bundle... Did you receive <107> urgent news from home? Is the house collapsing?"
To ease the woman's tense heart, I had no choice but to make idle talk.
"Now that the real homemaker has arrived, I should step aside," the second wife said with a smile, lightly scolding her husband for making a fuss since morning, but she didn't seem angry.
She gave the old woman cooking rice 20 jeon and told her to buy some meat.
"A big guest has arrived, so we can't treat her ordinarily."
She smiled brightly. It seemed like a farewell treat since she was leaving. Seeing her distinguish side dishes, supposedly to treat the first wife, even with sarcasm, I thought she wasn't narrow-minded or twisted.
"Why don't you try living together in one house for now? If you do, maybe some solution will emerge! If the owner just pulls out like this, who will live here and manage the household, like a kite snatching a magpie's nest? Also, if it's a household that can't work out anyway, shouldn't you be the one to finish it off with your own hands since you created it, and see it to the end before leaving?"
As S requested, I was trying to hold her back for the time being.
"What's there to it? Even if I abandon this so-called household, there's nothing I'd regret. And I wanted to leave for the child's sake, so I must go quickly."
She had initially come up for the child's health, seeking a change of air. While staying at the inn, the child had improved steadily, but after moving into this house, he fell severely ill again. For nearly two weeks, the mother had been frantic day and night, and just as the child seemed to be turning a corner and she was regaining her composure, this new storm broke. Since I saw her almost daily at the market and knew her situation well, it was truly pitiful to watch her leave like this after spending money and suffering so much far from home.
"Why don't you leave after the child has fully recovered? It's probably not the house's fault anyway. Is there any reason to use the child as an excuse and leave as if someone is grabbing you by the scruff of the neck?"
I had witnessed this woman's absolute—sometimes seemingly blind—maternal love firsthand, and I was moved by it, thinking gratefully in my heart that she wasn't just some modern woman roaming around as another man's concubine. Of course, superstition is superstition, but she had rushed up here because a fortune-teller (못구리) back home said the south was auspicious. When the child got sick again here, she spent 5 or 6 won, then 2 or 3 won at a time on things like bear gallbladder (웅담) and ox-bezoar calming pills (우황청심환). Even so, as the child withered, she seemed to secretly visit supposedly efficacious fortune-tellers (못구리쟁이) from time to time. That's why I idly said it probably wasn't the house's fault. Anyway, while the child's father couldn't even buy a single mesh undershirt (맥고자) that everyone else bought and wore—he wouldn't even say 'go buy one' for 60 or 70 jeon—he seemed unwilling to spare any expense for the child's medicine, be it 5 or 6 won, or even 50 or 60 won, if a doctor recommended it.
"Let's separate. But I won't give up the child."
She had apparently made this declaration to S the previous evening. It wasn't something he could take even if she gave him up, nor was he in a position to take him, unable to manage even himself.

(Seems like she might commit suicide. Please come over. She went out earlier saying she was paying respects, maybe she left then. I never knew she could be this attached...)
I was waiting for the second wife, who said she would come to my place after breakfast, when around noon, the old woman's son brought this note. Misreading '떠나나보이' (maybe she's leaving) as '떠낫나보이' (maybe she left), I was also somewhat alarmed. Did he want me to come and persuade the first wife since the second one had gone out? I hesitated, feeling it was too meddlesome, but then the child came again. Broadcasting S.O.S. signals continuously.
When I went over, the couple's fight had just ended, and there was a temporary truce. One family unit in the main room (안방), one in the side room (건넌방), and one on the porch (마루)—the one on the porch was the first wife's three-year-old son. The little boy seemed pitiful, his existence completely ignored due to the adults' love quarrels and food fights. I gave him 5 jeon to buy snacks. S, having had his Western jacket and shirt confiscated again by the side room occupant, couldn't come out and had nothing to wear, so he sat sweating in a thick winter shirt. It was a tactic to keep him confined, fearing he might abscond with the main room wife. <108> Unaware of his inner thoughts, the main room wife apparently claimed she was going back to her hometown, but it seemed like a lie. It looked like she was maneuvering, perhaps planning to move somewhere else within Seoul, hoping her husband would slip away with her. Therefore, the first wife insisted that the second wife couldn't leave either. She persistently demanded they live in the same house. If the main room mother and son were sent away, she would lose her husband; if the second wife's mother and child set up a separate household, the husband would likely stay put in the concubine's house and rarely visit her properly. So, she insisted on living together. The second wife, seeing it as a practical matter—a problem of livelihood and calculation—wanted to slip away cleanly like a pulled tooth, but the first wife wanted to argue it as an erotic (에로틕) matter, a matter of affection. One side had money, youth, knowledge, and sexual freedom, while the other had none of these but possessed the anguish of a middle-aged woman of forty. I thought neither side was unreasonable.
As I was about to leave after comforting S, the old cooking woman, who was standing at the gate holding the child the second wife had left in her care, stopped me to gossip about the first wife. "Maybe she wants another child; in the middle of the night, she ran into the main room and yanked the blanket off the sleeping person..." she giggled.
"So what happened then?"
I also laughed quietly.
"The child's mother told him to go sleep with her quickly, but does the child's father listen? They stayed up all night like that. Maybe they enjoy it or find it amusing, but I couldn't sleep a wink because of them."
"Why? Did you start thinking about your old man?"
"Old man or dried persimmon, I wish I were born a man to experience such luxury."
"That's what I'm saying."
Back home, as I lay down to catch up on the sleep I missed in the morning, S followed me again.
"Being alone, ropes and razor blades catch my eye, making me feel the danger and suddenly scared by my own thoughts," S said, smiling bleakly.
He seemed to feel the temptation of suicide when alone, so he needed someone nearby or sought someone out to forget the temptation. S spent an hour recounting the story of how he met the second wife and their subsequent honey-sweet (?) love life, ruining my sleep completely. Feeling bad for listening for free, I bought 10 jeon worth of alcohol as payment and sent him off.
As I lay down again, trying to sleep, about an hour later, S creaked the door open again, repeating "Sorry to bother you." He said he came because the second wife hadn't arrived, feeling anxious and wondering if she had come straight here.
"Well then, darn it. Whether she leaves or not depends on you."
He pressed his request again and left.
"He's completely hooked! Pitiful, but too unmanly."
Hearing this from home, I laughed.
"He expects me to clean up the mess after he enjoyed the sweet honey alone?" I laughed too.
The second wife arrived carrying the child. She had stopped by her place, given the old woman rice to cook, and then brought the child here.
"So, are you really leaving?"
"Yes, I'm leaving. Why should I stay and watch that spectacle? It's obvious they plotted together, tried to leech off me, and when that failed, they set up this household and sneakily called her up."
I wondered how a woman could think like that.
"S-gun isn't that kind of person. You must be misunderstanding because you're angry. In any case, you should think about that child."
I pointed to the child sleeping on her lap.
"What does that matter? In this world, even people who've shared pillows break up, leaving behind several children each..."
Her resolve was firm. Even from the start of this household, it seemed precarious and unlikely to last long. Although the money for the house was insufficient, S had pestered me into playing the role of an unwanted broker. I spent days looking at houses, finding one place after another, but she would find some fault to derail the purchase, seeming more worried about selling it later than enthusiastic about buying.
To S, she apparently pretended she planned to expand when her mother came up, and S seemed to believe her, but it didn't look that way to me. It seemed she regretted <109> the money crumbling away and was afraid, wanting to buy something small but worried it would be hard to sell if they suddenly separated.
Anyway, since she was determinedly saying, "What does it matter if we separate?", it was difficult to bring up the subject again.
"Even if you separate, you can't separate on bad terms. S-gun can't make up his mind right now and is in that state; how can you be so heartless as to just brush him off and leave? You should at least calm his mind before you go..."
Gently persuading her, I read her S's note sent to me earlier, exaggerating that if left like this, he really might commit suicide. Though her lips curled saying,
"Is it out of affection? He's just doing that because he's desperate right now," her eyes welled up with tears. Gaining confidence that I could hold her back for now if I gradually appeased her, I gave her some pocket money and told her to buy ingredients for side dishes to treat her. Seeing that, she didn't stand up saying she was leaving, indicating she intended to eat dinner here and stay.
"Anyway, it's best to postpone for a few days, slowly think about the future, and then make a decision so you won't have regrets. Acting on impulse often leads to later regret, thinking one went too far."
Her silent listening suggested she was taking my words to heart. Just then, S slipped in. The conversation naturally broke off. The couple sat averted, like fighting chickens.
"Is the talk all done?"
S abruptly said this, looking up at me. I frowned, signaling him to leave quickly. If it seemed like I was persuading her in collusion with S, my words wouldn't have even five percent effect. I couldn't understand S's psychology, unable to wait even that long and following her here. If he weren't anxious about leaving the second wife entirely in my hands, he wouldn't need to come and watch over her at that very moment. No matter how urgent, he could have come to me secretly through the back door later to ask. I knew he was very suspicious, having only been deceived by others. Even when house hunting, if I introduced a house belonging to someone I knew, S would immediately suspect some collusion between that person and me. Annoyed, I would withdraw, but finding it hard to just watch, I would offer help again.
"What talk are you talking about?"
My wife scolded him. S looked embarrassed, then, perhaps thinking his male pride was at stake,
"Go if you want to go! Who told you not to go!" he blurted out loudly.
"Hmph - who said I wasn't going!"
The second wife's face immediately flushed red. She stood up, grabbed the child, and stormed out. No matter how much my wife and I tried to hold her back, she wouldn't listen and left.
"Why did you come already? Did someone say they were going to tear her apart?"
I was annoyed that he had overturned the situation just as I had managed to change her mind.
"Did I say something unnecessary? Could she have really left in anger?"
S, having clearly seen her leave with his own eyes, asked my wife as if seeking an analysis of female psychology, whether she had truly left in anger.
"Of course. You spoke disagreeably, telling someone already simmering with anger to leave, wouldn't that provoke defiance?"
"Just as you might bluff, maybe she's using some tactic too. If she dislikes going back down, she might pretend to be angry."
I chimed in from the side.
"Then please call her back again and talk to her."
Finding it awkward to approach me again, this time he pleaded with my wife.
"Alright, you go wait there. I'll either send someone or go myself and bring her back."
After S left, we sent the old woman, but she didn't come back. It wouldn't do to have prepared dinner and not be able to treat her, so I went over again. Feeling embarrassed and weary, I wanted to wash my hands of it now, but having started to look after someone else's affair, big or small, I thought I should see it through completely.
"You're quite stubborn, aren't you? Does it take three or four invitations, plus me coming over myself?" <110>
At this one remark, perhaps feeling apologetic, she came along without another word. It seemed she also disliked sitting cooped up in that house filled with low pressure.
"S-gun might talk big, but he's anxious inside, so please don't act like this and change your mind. And if the first wife moves out, even to a rented room, wouldn't things go back to normal as if nothing happened? If you leave now, anyone would think you were determined to leave anyway and are just glad an excuse came up to slip away, wouldn't they?"
"There's no way we can live together. She says she won't leave unless I find her a house as good as the one she's in now. Besides that, how can we manage two households? We have to pay the children's school fees. The son has supposedly become a leftist (주의자). He pesters his father for money every other day, complaining how his father can live so comfortably like this. The kid, not even twenty, bosses his father around like a servant. How can anyone live under that pressure? That's the least of it. Even if provisions are well supplied, if it's not just for a day or two, she'll chase him down daily and raise hell. Who can endure that torment? There needs to be at least one way out of ten... It's pitiful. I really didn't know his situation was like that."
"In the world today, are there only one or two people like S-gun? If you buy them a Western suit pretending it's for their parents, and take them to a Kisaeng house (긔생집) a few times—though I've never seen S-gun go to a Kisaeng house—anyway, after doing this and that, even a rice-bearing tree chooses its owner, isn't that how it naturally happens? But since there's no law that says a person must die, somehow a path will open up. Since the first wife's household is unmanageable anyway, she'll go back down too, and if he gets a job, he can manage to live, right?"
This was all I could say.
"One lives by eating and dressing, but if there's affection, one can manage somehow. Just think back quietly on the past 6 or 7 years. Don't just look ahead or calculate gains and losses..."
I tried saying this too, but unless there was at least one point in a hundred, or even one in ten, favorable to this side, my words lacked force. While trying to persuade her, I found myself almost being persuaded instead.
"It's obvious why he asked me to come up this time and ditched the first wife's household. It was just to leech off me, but even if we were to use it together later, who would be fooled like that?"
That statement was also indefensible. This side did it out of sheer inability, not out of malice to leech, but thinking that way is common and not something to be criticized. If S had spent generously when he had some money and put in effort one way or another, it would have been better. But being stingy even to the point of not giving back a single won to a struggling friend or buying a single piece of clothing for the woman involved or the child born, while being taken advantage of by unrelated people, and then trying to get cozy when he needs something—who would appreciate that?
"Even if he says he has absolutely nothing now, back when things weren't so difficult, was there ever a time he didn't spend my money, even just ten won?"
Back then, things were good, so it wasn't a flaw, but now she was bringing it up. Whether male or female, if you want to get money or benefit from someone, you have to feed them as much as they need and spend as much as necessary, showing sincerity through your actions. But S tried to catch a dog without even bait, which is why he failed, I thought.
After eating dinner together and chatting, the second wife went across. Having someone to talk to when lonely is a great comfort. Her mood improved, and she promised my wife and me that she would stay a few more days.
My wife and I were relieved, but we talked about how they must be tired tonight and hoped they would sleep peacefully without causing a neighborhood disturbance by shouting.
"But even though she agreed here, she'll probably rush to leave as soon as she gets back. If she quietly settles down, it would be awkward and the act wouldn't work, so she'll bluff about leaving. S will tremble and plead sincerely to stop her. The side room wife (건넌방 집네 - referring to the first wife here) must be upset starting from us inviting her over for dinner today, so she won't stay quiet either. The three points will stick out independently, causing a brief commotion."
"I wish I could go watch! I wish I could see what the first wife looks like," my wife laughed. The old woman at our house described the first wife's appearance, and they all burst into laughter.
"Putting that aside, I wonder if S, with his temper, won't assert his 'manly pride' and tell her to leave out of bravado, just like earlier?" <111>
"If he tells her to go, she'll go. Let him suffer a bit and see how desperate he gets. And then, immediately shedding tears and fretting around, does that uphold manly pride? Men just have that useless stubborn streak."
My wife was presenting a united female front, intending for me to hear.
"If she leaves when told, fine, but aren't we wasting all the effort we put in?"
As we were talking like this, S's old woman rushed in frantically. She said a finger was broken and asked for any medicine, while also asking me to come over again. Apparently, the first wife started it, grabbing the second wife's hair and slamming her down, breaking her finger in the process. Everything seemed to be proceeding according to the program we had just discussed.
The womenfolk seemed ready to go support the second wife, saying "Oh dear, how pitiful. We should go check," but I shouted to stop them.
"What caused another fight?"
"This morning, while eating, she pleaded earnestly with the second wife not to leave and to live together. But then, perhaps due to another whim, this evening she started picking a fight, asking why she hadn't left yet. So, this side wouldn't stay quiet either, right? She retorted, 'It's my house, what business is it of yours what I do?' and told her to take her husband and get out herself. Then they grabbed each other's hair... It was quite a spectacle. What's wrong with the child's father? He just fretted in the middle. The weaker second one couldn't possibly withstand that brute force."
The old woman recounted what she saw and heard vividly, clearly excited. The old woman was a staunch supporter of the second wife. It seemed the first wife, nearing childbirth, had abandoned all pretense of being high-class; the husband was just someone to clear away meals; only the child's mother was gentle, considerate, sensible, and understood propriety, she praised endlessly.
While underlings naturally flock to those who spend money, I thought if only the first wife (큰댁) had behaved with a little more dignity, her husband wouldn't have lost face! I handed over a bottle of Okto-jŏnggi (옥도정긔 - likely a topical antiseptic/pain reliever) and sent back the message: "The affairs of the three of them are for the three of them to discuss and decide among themselves. I am a useless person in this matter, so I will not go."
Thinking about it, it seemed the first wife had tried asking the second wife to live together because she was currently spending money, but then seeing the husband cling only to the second wife and converse secretly in Japanese, she got jealous. Her plan must have been to confiscate the husband's only Western suit to immobilize him, and then tell the second wife to leave.
The next day, it was quiet until noon, so on my way out, I casually dropped by S's place. It was very calm, like after a storm has passed. The second wife, massaging her swollen, frog-like finger, spoke loudly enough for the first wife to hear, saying she now planned to raise capital to start a business or find a teaching position at a school and live in Seoul. I replied absently that it sounded good, thinking to myself, "So, as revenge for yesterday's fight, she plans to drive out the first wife before going back down!" The more they fought, the more detrimental it was only to the first wife, yet I couldn't understand why all first wives in the world were so foolish. Perhaps they received such neglect (소박) precisely because they lacked such prudence and acted recklessly with only stubbornness.
As I was about to leave, S followed me to the gate.
He seemed about to bring up the "So, what would be best now?" question again.
"Why do you all act so disgracefully (효상)?"
I tried to preemptively dismiss yesterday's topic.
"After she came back from your place yesterday, she immediately started packing the luggage I had unpacked, making a fuss about leaving. I barely managed to hold her back, and then the big one (큰 것 - first wife) started picking a fight, asking why I was holding her back."
It wasn't particularly surprising news, but S, emphasizing the "I" in "I barely managed to hold her back," showed a slightly pleased expression for some reason. His tone hinted he wanted to make it clear she didn't stay because I held her back, but because *he* did.
(So, someone who was fighting just moments ago about leaving would suddenly sit down willingly? Shouldn't there be the formality of holding her back and stopping her...) - I thought about retorting like this, but stopped, thinking it might sound like I was boasting about my contribution. From dawn yesterday, waking a sleeping person, ordering them here and there, and keeping them agitated all day—if the purpose was to change the second wife's mind and make her stay, then even if he himself held her back, <112> wouldn't it be natural to say at least one word like, "You went through too much trouble yesterday, sorry about that"? Once it's past the throat, one forgets how hot it was.
Even for the second wife, she settled down because her own mind changed, not because she was scared by my words, of course. But my persuasion likely played some role in changing her mind. And she must have made that promise to my wife and me because she had decided not to leave; if she intended to leave no matter what, why would she deceive us? S's face looked up at me again.
"So, did I hold back the first wife too?"
I tried a slight sarcasm, but he seemed unable to grasp my meaning.
"What's there to hold back or not? Now she's trying hard to drive out the little one (작은 것 - second wife)."
"She has quite the nerve. To live in this world, you need that much guts. You're not much worse yourself, though..."
S looked at me indignantly, then, perhaps venting frustration from hearing something unpleasant, reproached me, "Yesterday, when I asked you to come over, how could you cut me off so coldly (매정스럽게)?" What kind of person acts like this? I just stared blankly at S's face. There's a proverb, "Every tradesman minds his own gain," but it seemed he wanted everyone to look after only his affairs, move only according to his words, and comfort only him. And then, once my own business suffered, he'd wipe his mouth clean and turn away sharply as if nothing had happened.
"Instead of doing that, next time, come fight in our yard. If I have to get involved like licorice in medicine every time you fight, this old man really can't be running around fields and paddies aimlessly until late at night trying to stop his son's love quarrels and daughter-in-law's co-wife fights, can he?"
I retorted with nonsensical words, like scolding a child. As I turned to leave, he called out, "Hey! Hey!" and followed me.
I was caught again in the middle of the field.
"Alright—so now, what would be best?"
"Am I a fortune teller who can read palms? Am I a shaman who can perform exorcisms? If I were a famous doctor, I'd brew you some deer antler (록용) or concentrated ginseng soup (독삼탕) to boost your yang energy... hahaha—"
"Do ginseng and deer antler drain yang energy? Ha..."
"If it's impossible to boost it anyway, I mean I'd feed you alone so well that neither the main room nor the side room could make a peep, making you a bit more capable. Why, does that prick up your ears? Haha..."
"Damn fool! Always talking nonsense... Try to understand someone else's situation."
Another scolding, another plea.
"What can I do? If you can't handle it, why don't you just hand over both the first wife and the second wife to me? I'll sort them out decisively."
I turned abruptly to leave, but feeling bad for having joked too much, I added,
"Instead of that, do whatever it takes to raise just 10 won and negotiate again. Whether you make her go back down or get her a rented room, those are the only two options. And if you ask the second one to provide even that money, would she?"
—I advised him thus.
"Where am I supposed to get money—" S scratched his head.
"I'd like to tell you where the money could come from, but..."
"What?"
His ears seemed to perk up at important matters.
"Well, I'd like to tell you, but I can't because I'd end up in jail."
"What is it, what is it?"
"Think about it. Are there any special means? Forge promissory notes or tunnel through walls..."
"Damn fool!"
I felt pity for myself, wandering around engaging in idle chatter while being called a damn fool.

The next evening, S came in energetically, wearing no jacket but swinging a cane (단장).
"Why have you been so scarce?"
"I was running around trying to raise some money... Got 50 won, but 20 <113> was immediately snatched away by a creditor I ran into right there, couldn't help it. With 30 won, get her a rented room (세ㅅ°© - likely 세ㅅ방), arrange some provisions, pay the child's school fees..."
He calculated aloud, this and that.
"What kind of debt is this at a time like this? Forget paying, I'd slap them and take it back."
Although he wasn't usually that foolish about money, since he said it was snatched, I figured it must have been.
"Anyway, that's fortunate. So, did the first wife leave?"
"She left. Now I can breathe easy."
He seemed relieved, like a troublesome tooth had been pulled. But I inwardly doubted whether the second wife would want to live with him again after he let one worry go.
"So, what did the second one say?"
"What did she say...?" he asked, glancing at me instead. It was pitiful that S was trying to get news about the second wife, who slept in the same place, from me.
"If she says she's leaving again, 'I can hold her back'..." I started to laugh, but S jumped in surprise.
"Me hold her back? Do you think she trusts me that much? For some reason, she seems to misunderstand."
"Why would she misunderstand me? Did she say I persuaded her with sweet talk, held her back, and then let her finger get broken?"
I felt a bit displeased. But it was pointless to ask; he wouldn't answer.
"Or perhaps she thinks I only look after your situation and collude with you (자낀¤와 부동 - likely 자기와 부동 meaning 'colluding with you') to make her spend money?"
"Who knows. She doesn't say clearly."
I fell silent. Whether it was a tactic to create a defensive line (방어선) out of fear that the second wife and I might become close, or if someone had really slandered me, what did it matter? I didn't think deeply about it.
"By the way, you told the second wife about getting that 50 won, right?"
"Why? Yes, I did!"
Even without asking, he would have told her. Not necessarily out of honesty, but more likely to boast, even if it was just 50 won, that he had the resourcefulness and ability to circulate funds when needed.
"But, unaware of your circumstances, wouldn't the second wife dislike it? That you didn't manage to procure a single penny when setting up house with her, even bumming cigarette money off her, but as soon as the first wife arrives, you promptly arrange 50 won to spend. She'll surely resent it. Besides, if you don't contribute a penny to the household, who would appreciate that?"
"Huh?" S exclaimed in surprise, then became awkward.
"Let her misunderstand if she wants. As long as I have no ill intentions..."
"That may be so, but principled arguments (정도론) are one thing, and the pesky emotional issues in actual life are another, aren't they? Even setting aside a wife's bias, wouldn't anyone feel resentful (야속하게) in such a situation?"
S offered to treat me with the leftover money and suggested going to his place, but I refused.
After dinner, the second wife came over to visit.
"The house must be quiet now, right?"
"Quiet is an understatement. The whole neighborhood feels desolate."
The women exchanged such remarks.
"Still, you're quite resourceful. At our house, we haven't received salaries (월급) for several months, and at most, it's 10 won or 5 won, but you manage to swing 50 won here and there."
When my wife praised him, the second wife pursed her lips.
"It's different because she's the first wife. It's been over a month (달포) since I came up, has there ever been a time he spent even a single jeon of his own?" she said, the resentment she had hidden surfacing in her eyes.
I made numerous excuses, arguing it wasn't like that, but she shook her head.
A week later, the second wife left. That week was also spent waiting for her reportedly broken finger to heal somewhat. When leaving, she apparently told S she would arrange and send him 100 won. I laughed inwardly. S returned from the station, shed tears, and immediately wrote and sent a letter after her. While he recounted memories of their time together for over an hour, he again expressed resentment that I hadn't come over on the evening that big fight broke out. I wanted to reprimand him, but seeing him cry was pitiful, so I just listened.
The person who went back down, indeed, perhaps due to some misunderstanding towards my wife and me, never sent even a single postcard (엽서) saying she arrived safely. My wife and I occasionally talked about how she must be seriously sulking about something, but after a month or two passed, we've forgotten about it lately. (End - Original Text) <114>

**Summary**

This short story by Yŏm Sang-sŏp depicts the turmoil faced by a man, S, caught between his traditional first wife and his younger, more modern mistress (referred to as 'second wife'). The arrival of the first wife throws S's precarious living arrangement, financially dependent on the mistress, into chaos. S proves indecisive and weak, constantly seeking advice from the narrator, his friend, who is reluctantly drawn into the domestic drama. Despite the narrator's attempts at mediation, the conflict escalates, leading to arguments, jealousy, and even physical altercations. Ultimately, S manages to send his first wife away with borrowed money, but the mistress, disillusioned by S's behavior and financial dependence, also leaves shortly after.

From a historical perspective, the story vividly portrays the social complexities and personal struggles arising from changing family structures (polygamy/concubinage persistence alongside modern ideas), economic hardship, shifting gender roles, and the clash between traditional obligations and modern relationships in 1930s colonial Korea. Historians might find interest in the detailed depiction of financial dependency shaping relationships, the narrator's reluctant role as mediator reflecting community involvement (or burden) in private affairs, the explicit mention of social anxieties (like a son becoming a leftist), and the psychological portrayal of characters navigating societal transitions and personal failings.

嫉妬와 밥
						廉想涉
막 밝자 S가 달겨드럿다.
아츰잠이 다시 들랴든 나는 마루 압까지 와서 섯는 S를 기다리게 하고 옷을 주섬주섬 입고 마루로 나왓다.
「미안할세. 잠간 나가세.」
S는 내 아우나 안해가 들으면 창피하니까 끌고 나가려는 것이다.
무슨 일인줄 짐작한 나는 다소 호긔심도 업지는 안흐나 귀치 안흔 것을 아츰 바람도 쏘일겸 산으로 따라 나갓다.
「간밤엔 한잠 못잣네. 어제밤에 달겨드럿네 그려.」
일대 사건이나 돌발한 듯이 자긔의 흥분된 감정을 그대로 쏘다 놋는다.
「흥! 그러기에 발이 편하려만 버선을 크게 해 신고 몸이 편하려면 한 계집 데리고 살라지 안나!」
아츰잠 못잔 것이 분한 것은 아니나 얼뜨게 한 집으로 모라 노토록 넘우나 수단 업는 것이 도로어 미웟다.
「인력거를 타고 밤중에 쪼차온 것을 엇덕하나.」
「인력거에 올려 안처주지 안흔 것만 다행하이!」
「그럼 엇덕하나. 번지를 안 가르처주면 멧칠이고 내가 감금을 당할 형편이니. 양복 저고리를 빼앗고 내노하야지.」
「바지는 안 빼앗겻든가?」
「바지야 설마 빼앗겻겟나.」
「다행하이-」하고 나는 실소를 하얏다.
큰마누라 편을 처서 말하는 자긔가 도리(道理)로는 잘못일지 모르지만 큰마누라고 도리고 간에 위선 먹어야―살아야 하지 안는가―하는 생각을 하얏다.

어제 아츰에 S의 동생이 내게 차저와서 형의 집을 가르처 달라고 졸을 때 S의 본댁이 올러온 줄은 알엇다.
「난 모르겟는데. 이 근처인 줄은 아나 내게까지 속이는 걸 엇저나.」
속으로는 가르처주고 십지만 S가 그러케 겁을 벌벌 내면서 신신당부를 한 것을 무엇하자고 실신하랴 십허서 요긔라는 손짓이 나올듯 나올듯한 것을 참아버리고 말앗섯다. 큰댁네가 올라와서 살림을 다시 할 것가트면 나종에 S에게나 S 작은집에게 청원을 듯는 한이 잇드라도 가르처 주엇슬지 모른다.
아츰 저녁으로 굴둑에서 나오는 연긔는 말할 것도 업고 두 코구멍에서 내뿜는 연긔까지도 작은집의 처분만 바라고 지내는 지금의 S를 본댁네 아니라 본댁네 할미가 왓다기로 대어주면 무얼하랴 십헛든 것이다.
「엇덕할까요? 엇덕하든지 붓드러서 아즈먼네를 대어줄까요?」 아우가 이러케 의론을 할 때도 나는 말렷다.
「안될 말일세. 형수씨를 위하거든 그대로 내려가시게 하게. 지금 쌈질이 이러나면 형님 내외분은 정이 영영 버스러지는 것일세. 큰마누라의 투긔란 의례 잇는 것이지만 그게 약지 못해서 그러는 것일세. 나종에는 안고 잡바지는 걸세. 요새 세상에 돈 잇고 똑똑한 계집이 서방 멕여가며 살겟나. 오래 갈 것도 아니니 가만＜105＞ 내버려 두시라 하게. 언제든지 헤어지면 미우나 고흐나 본마누라에게로 달겨들 것 아닌가. 돈이 잇스니 또 다른 계집을 어들까봐 걱정인가 아무 염려 업스실 것일세.」
「인제는 마음을 잡앗댓는데 그 녀자 때문에 또 저러니까 더 안탁가와 하는 거지요.」
「의례 그럴거지. 나이 사십이나 되어서 인제 겨오 남편의 정을 알게 되다가 또 그나마 노치게 되니까 더 감질이 나겟지만 그런다고 되는 것이 아니야. 내 말대로만 하시라게. 허허...」 하고 나는 자긔말이 웃으웟다.
「하지만 갈데가 잇나요.」
「시골집에나 친정이 부자라니 친정에나 어듸든지 몸 부칠데야 업슬라구.」
「누가 와 잇스라나요.」
「나이 사십에 친정사리도 창피할 것이요 패가 하고 집 파라가지고 올러온지 잇해가 못 되어서, 밥 어더 먹으려 싀아재비 집으로 드러가면 반가월 할 사람도 업겟지만 그럴 것을 웨 살림을 헤치자는 대로 헤처 버리드란 말인가. 지금 와서는 실혀도 하는 수 업지. 첩이 넉넉하기로 본마누라 식구까지 멕이지는 안흘께지. 그러타고 형님은 두 손길 맛붓잡고 안젓스니 꼼작 수 업지 안흔가. 남편이나 형님이 미워서 너 혼자만 편히 지내란? 하고 희살만 노흐면 쾌할건 무에 잇나.」
「누가 압니까. 아즈먼네 성미에 만나지 안코는 안나려 갈거니까요.」 「그러지 말고 자네가 어서 장가를 들어서 형수씨를 모시고 살게 그려. 자네 목슨 제대로 잇게지?」

나는 잠잣고 말앗다. 작은집과 나와 친해질까 보아 방어선(防禦線)으로 그러는 수작인지 정말 누가 중상을 햇는지 아무러면 엇더랴 하고 깁히 생각지도 안햇다.
「그런데 자네 그 돈 50원 되엇다는 말은 작은 집에게 말햇지.」
「웨? 햇는데!」
뭇지 안해도 하얏슬 것이다. 고지식해서 그러타느니 보다도 50원 돈이라도 급하면 순환할 재조와 능력이 잇다는 자랑 삼아서라도 풍을 처노핫슬 것이다.
「그러나 자네 사정은 몰으고 작은집이 실혀하지 안켓나. 자긔와 살림 차리는데는 단돈 한푼 변통해 오지 안코 담배갑까지 씨우다가 큰댁네가 오니까 50원 탁이 금시로 주선해다가 쓴다고 실혀할 걸세. 게다가 살림에는 한푼 보태지 안흐면 누구는 조화하겟나.」
「응?」 하고 S는 놀라는 소리를 하다가
「오해하라면 하라지. 나는 조금도 악의가 업는 다음에야...」 하고 멋슥해진다.
「그는 그러치만 정도론(正道論)은 정도론이요 실제 사는데 잣단 감정 문제가 귀치 안치 안흔가. 녀편네의 편성이 아니라도 누구나 그런 경우에 야속하게 생각지 안나.」
S는 돈푼 남은 것으로 한턱 낸다고 자긔게로 가자는 것을 거절해 버렷다.
저녁밥 후에 작은집이 놀라왓다.
「인제는 집안이 조용해젓겟슴니다 그려?」
「조용만 해요. 동리가 다 쓸쓸해진 것 갓태요.」
녀편네끼리 이런 수작을 한다.
「그래도 재주가 조세요. 우리집에서는 월급도 멧멧달을 못 타고 고작 된다하야 10원 아니면 5원인데 50원씩 휙휙 돌리시구」
집사람이 칭찬을 하니까 작은집은 입을 빗즉하며
「큰마누라니까 다르지요. 내가 올러온지 달포가 되어야 단 일전을 내서 쓰는 일이 잇서요.」 하고 속에 숨겻든 노긔가 눈에 발려 나온다.
나는 그러치 안타는 변명을 무수히 하야 주엇스나 고개를 내둘은다.
그런지 일주일 후에 작은집은 떠낫다. 그 일주일도 부러젓다는 손가락이 좀 나키를 기다리는 것이엇다. 나려갈 때 S에게는 돈을 백원을 변통해서 부처주마고 하얏다 한다. 나는 속으로 웃엇다. S는 정거장에 다녀 드러와서 눈물을 흘리며 편지를 뒤미처 써 부첫다. 그동안 지낸 추억담을 한 시간 이상이나 들어주는 동안에는 내가 그 큰 쌈이 이러나든 날 저녁에 안 가준 것을 야속하다고 또 원망이엇다. 핀잔을 주고 십헛스나 우는 것이 가엽서서 듯고만 넘겻다.
나려간 사람은 그야말로 우리 부부에게 무슨 오해가 잇는지 잘 갓다는 엽서 한 장 업섯다. 무엇에 단단히 토라젓나보다고 우리 부부가 가끔 이야기를 하든 것도 한달 두달 지나니까 요새는 이저버렷다.(끗-원문)＜114＞
