Article Title: 動亂의 上海, 一音樂家가 愛兒屍體를 안고 울든 記錄 (Record of Shanghai in Turmoil, a Musician Weeping While Holding Her Beloved Child's Corpse)

動亂의 上海, 一音樂家가 愛兒屍體를 안고 울든 記錄
						朴慶姬
From Tokyo to Shanghai

It was the spring of 1927 when I, who had been studying voice at the Tokyo Music School, decided to go to China, driven by a desire to study far away in Italy and also to follow my husband (Reporter's Note = Mr. Yi Yŏsŏng) who was then taking refuge abroad, and finally took my first deeply moving steps onto the Shanghai docks. From then on, after finding a small rented room in an "apartment" (아바-트, 멘트) on Chap'uk Posan-ro (閘北寶山路), the two years we spent living in Shanghai were truly a continuous, most tense sequence of fear, wonder, and hardship from the first day to the last.
Now, as I gaze at the swallows returning south at the beginning of spring, I recall with a wistful feeling, and already four years have passed since then. Sitting in a sunny spot, drowsy in the spring light, weaving together this piece of reminiscence—is this too not a meaningful task?

The Activities of the Women Warriors

At that time, in the French Concession (佛蘭西租界), the famous director of the Russian Imperial Music School, Mr. "Ma-shin" (마-신), had fled the "Revolution" (革命) and hastily arrived in Shanghai, where he was teaching music.
There were about 40 young vocalists receiving private lessons from this foreign old musician, who had the fierce appearance of a Northern European (北歐人); among them, young British and American men and women were the most numerous. I too, upon arriving in Shanghai, immediately knocked on his door and arranged to receive lessons.
That day too, after stopping by the hospital, I was passing through the British Concession (英租界) intending to knock on Mr. Ma-shin's door as usual when, ah! Suddenly, the large crowd walking briskly along the pavement (푸-브, 멘트) in front and behind me surged like rushing water, like a sweeping storm, shouting "Waa-" and following frantically behind me. Startled, I turned around; the smell of gunpowder hit my nose, and directly across from me, the sound of gunfire erupted like popping beans.
It was the scene of the Chinese army being routed and fleeing from the Kuomintang's (國民黨) plainclothes units (便衣隊). Along the roadside, corpses of soldiers soaked in dark blood lay scattered here and there, and many citizens who couldn't find a way to escape in time were also falling down, killed instantly by stray bullets. <35>
As the situation became so desperate, wasn't it natural for the thousands of Chinese men (王大人) and young women (小姐) walking the streets to weep and wail as they ran towards the safety of the Concession? Shops instantly closed their shutters entirely, the rickshaws and cars that had seemed to cover the whole city abruptly vanished, and the sounds of advertising bands from department stores and fabric shops fell silent.
The fear of death flowed into every corner of the city, robbing all the citizens of their courage. Caught in this midst, while feeling my mind gradually grow faint, I suddenly thought of my husband who had gone towards Pukcham (北站 - Shanghai North Station) earlier. What had happened to him?
The staccato sound of machine guns grew louder. The footsteps of the scattering crowd became more urgent. Just then, someone grabbed my arm. How could I have known? It was my husband. He had turned back from his way to the station and had run searching for me.
"Come on! This way, this way!"
Only these two fragmented phrases escaped our lips; there were no other words. Afterwards, we took refuge in the basement of a certain Chinese primary school nearby.
Outside, military trucks filled with British Army (英國陸軍) soldiers and armed Indian soldiers (印度兵) were deploying forward, forward. War! War! War! Turmoil!
It was then that I saw the female plainclothes units (女子 便衣隊) of the Chinese Kuomintang. Like the male units, they wore ordinary clothes, but carried guns. They had pistols, long spears, and clubs. I have no way of knowing how skilled their shooting was, but wherever the muzzles of their guns pointed, the soldiers of the warlord Sun Ch'uan-fang (軍閥孫傳芳) collapsed. Although female, their figures possessed a fierce spirit. They all ran well and were, above all, brave. Ah, even the women of this foreign land were so courageous.

Performing at the Carlton Cafe

One evening at the Carlton Cafe (칼톤, 캅페), considered first-rate in Shanghai, a grand festival called "Festival Ball" (패스트뺄니), held once a year, took place, and on that day, I found myself going onto the stage, led by my respected teacher, Mr. Ma-shin. The "Festival Ball" was the biggest annual event eagerly anticipated by the foreigners here in Shanghai, and despite the admission ticket for this day being a minimum of 6 yuan, about 2,000 people gathered. Probably British and American people were the most numerous. The Koreans present were me, my husband, and my relative Mr. Kim Seyong (金世鎔).
Here, I wore for the first time in my life a strange costume. As you may have occasionally seen in medieval scenes in motion pictures, the waist was tied very narrow and cinched, and the skirt was puffed out roundly like a hot air balloon filled with wind, and I wore a large crown on my head – it was like the clothing of an 18th-century French (什›蘭西) woman. Then, as instructed, I opened the main door of the stage, took one step, two steps, walking like a person in utmost despair, and sang a song. The title of the song that night was "Lost Love" (失戀), requiring the expression of a young person's disappointment, gloom, and anguish after losing love, accompanied by a subdued, trembling, and sorrowful voice.
Since this piece was something I had regularly learned from Mr. Ma-shin, I could sing it with all my might, but portraying the actions of sorrow was truly difficult. I hadn't even practiced this performance once and went straight onto the stage due to lack of time.
On my left and right, like the two wings of a bird, beautiful British and American women in splendid attire stood watching, and as soon as my song ended, they immediately began to dance.
That one night, I was truly immersed in a foreign atmosphere.
After that, I went on stage whenever I had the chance. Once, I happened to perform on a stage where Chinese people had gathered, and at their special request to sing a "Korean song" (까우리노래), I sang "Half Moon" which goes "Blue sky, Milky Way..." (푸른하늘 銀河水) and one other song.

Holding My Beloved Child's Corpse

I gave birth for the first time in Shanghai. Whether to call it a miscarriage or a premature birth, I delivered at eight months, and perhaps because it was my first time, the labor pains lasted for four days, truly making me walk the line between life and death. <36>
Perhaps the reason for such a premature birth was because the house where we lodged was on the third floor, and leading my frail body, I had to go down to the dining room on the lower floor every morning and evening, getting exhausted from climbing up and down those stairs, and also because, at that time in Shanghai, my tuition fees didn't arrive regularly, so even in the cold winter, we often couldn't light the stove well, and ate simple food (粗食).
My husband brought a doctor from the first day. Firewood was prepared in the room, and the fire began to burn brightly.
The duty of childbirth, imposed upon women with no escape except perhaps for nuns (女石人) – this is a pain truly unimaginable for women without experience of childbirth. The pain of labor. Could there be any physical suffering greater than this?
Day and night, for eight cycles, that is, on the evening of the fourth day, I finally gave birth to the baby in that distant foreign land after enduring the pain. Lying there, my whole body in a daze, I vaguely heard the midwife telling my husband it was a girl.
Then I heard the baby cry "kyae-kyae" five or six times. At this sound, I smiled faintly for no reason and fell into a deep sleep.
I learned later that the newborn baby died in less than five minutes. Even though it was just a lump of flesh, didn't it have properly formed eyes, nose, mouth, and ears, and unlike a plaster doll (石膏人形), wasn't it a living being possessing a fluttering life? It disappeared merely five minutes after coming into the world. Since I never saw its smiling face, it's unlikely that deep affection would have attached itself. Since I never heard it call "Mommy, Daddy," motherly love likely hadn't formed either. Yet, somehow, it felt desolate.
In Shanghai, when a young child dies, complicated procedures are required for burial. One must go to the Municipal Council (工部局) – now the Public Security Bureau (公安局) – to obtain a burial permit, and also make prior arrangements with the Crematorium (火葬場). But on that day, it was already getting dark.
My husband went outside and came back having bought a blanket. He tightly wrapped the little corpse in it, laid it under the bed (寢台), and we, the couple, slept on that bed.
Somehow, even though the child's breath had stopped, because it came from my body, I felt a sense of loss and pity, and wanted to hold it close to my body and sleep with it for just one night. If my husband hadn't strongly dissuaded me, saying it wasn't good for my postpartum body, wouldn't I have stayed up all night weeping, holding that dead body?
The next day, having thought about it, we donated this body to the university hospital there. The university hospital decided to dissolve it in alcohol and keep it in a glass jar.
We wept quietly in Shanghai, with the joy of having made a contribution to human culture. (End) <37>

**Summary**

Pak Kyŏnghŭi, a Korean vocalist studying in Tokyo, recounts her move to Shanghai in 1927 to join her husband, Yi Yŏsŏng, and continue her music studies with a Russian émigré teacher. She describes the chaos and fear during the arrival of the Kuomintang forces, witnessing street fighting, civilian casualties, and notably, armed female KMT plainclothes soldiers displaying bravery. Amidst this turmoil, she also recalls participating in Shanghai's expatriate social life, performing at the prestigious Carlton Cafe. The memoir culminates in the painful memory of giving premature birth after a difficult four-day labor, only for the infant daughter to die within minutes. Due to bureaucratic hurdles and the late hour, she and her husband kept the baby's corpse under their bed overnight before deciding to donate it to a university hospital for medical study, finding a bittersweet sense of contributing to "human culture."

From a historical perspective, the article offers a rare female Korean voice describing life in Shanghai during the turbulent late 1920s. It provides a vivid ground-level view of the Northern Expedition's impact on the city, including the often-overlooked role of women in the conflict (KMT plainclothes units). The juxtaposition of political violence with the expatriate cultural scene (Carlton Cafe, Russian émigré teacher) highlights the complex layers of colonial-era Shanghai. Furthermore, the personal tragedy of childbirth and infant death, coupled with the decision to donate the body, offers insights into personal coping mechanisms, modernizing attitudes towards medicine/science among some Koreans abroad, and the specific difficulties faced by Koreans living outside the peninsula during this period.

動亂의 上海, 一音樂家가 愛兒屍體를 안고 울든 記錄
						朴慶姬
東京에서 上海로
내가 東京音樂學校에서 성악을 배우고 잇다가 멀니 伊太利로 공부가고 십픈 생각과 또한 그 당시 몸을 해외에 피하고 게시든 남편(記者註=李如星氏)의 뒤를 따르고저 中國行을 결심하고 마츰내 上海埠頭에 감명 깁푼 첫발자곡을 옴기어논 것은 1927년 봄이엇슴니다. 그로부터 閘北寶山路의 「아바-트, 멘트」에 조고마한 세방을 어더가지고 우리들이 2년동안이나 지내든 그 上海의 생활은 첫날부터 끗날까지 실로 공포와 경이와 고난의 가장 긴장된 연속이엇슴니다.
이제 첨아끗헤 날려드는 강남제비를 바라봄애 撫然한 생각에 回憶하니 벌서 4년의 日月이 그 사이를 흘럿슴니다그려. 陽地짝에 안저 봄빗에 끄을니어서 一片의 이 회상을 역금이 또한 뜻업는 일이릿가.

娘子軍의 活躍

그때 佛蘭西租界에는 露西亞의 유명한 帝室音樂學校長 「마-신」氏가 「革命」에 쫏기어서 倉慌히 上海에 이르러 음악교수를 하고 잇섯슴니다.
北歐人的으로 慄悍하게 생긴 이 異邦의 老음악가를 차저서 개인교수를 밧고잇는 젊은 성악가로 말하면 약40명이나 되어서 그중에 英美國 남녀청년들이 가장 만하엿는데 나도 上海에 도착하자 곳 氏의 문을 두다려 교수를 밧기로 하엿슴니다.
