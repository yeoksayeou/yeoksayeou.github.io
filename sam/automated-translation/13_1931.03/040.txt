Movement to Expand Freedom of Speech, Assembly, and Association
						An Chaehong, Yi Chuyŏn

The Necessity of the Movement and Its Strategy
An Chaehong

The issue of expanding freedoms such as assembly, association, and speech seems easy, yet is very difficult. Brother Pa'in (巴人兄) insisted that I write this, but I do not think I am necessarily the right person for it. Currently, the regulation applied in this area in Chosŏn (朝鮮) is the Security Law (保安法) announced in July of the 11th year of Kwangmu (光武) during the late Korean Empire, under the Residency-General's rule. Article 1 states that the 'Minister of Internal Affairs' can order the dissolution of an association when necessary to preserve peace and order. Furthermore, police officers, when necessary to preserve peace and order, can restrict, prohibit, or dissolve assemblies, mass movements, or crowds (Article 2), and can prohibit the posting and distribution of documents or pictures, public readings, or other verbal expressions in streets or other public places, with corresponding standards for applicable punishments.

Additionally, the 'Assembly Control Ordinance' (集會取締令), promulgated as Police Order No. 2 in August of Meiji (明治) 43 [1910], with the main text stating, "For the time being, political assemblies or mass gatherings outdoors are prohibited," is still applied today.

All other laws and ordinances related to this matter are surely known to those concerned with this issue, even without my listing them all.

Regarding publishing, the Newspaper Law (新聞紙法), enacted in May of Meiji 43 [1910] or February of Yunghŭi (隆熙) 3 [1909], and concerning newspapers, the law from July of Kwangmu (光武) 11 [1907] (revised in Yunghŭi 2 [1908]), are still enforced as they were. These regulations are already considered unsuitable for the times, but more importantly, their application varies greatly in severity. Regarding the various matters mentioned above, voices demanding their revision and, consequently, demanding freedom of assembly, association, and speech, are rising.

However, regarding the most urgent issue of expanding freedom, you have requested "concrete strategies possible through the actual movement in Chosŏn (朝鮮) to the extent feasible," but unless I myself have the determination and preparation to disregard all obstacles and take the lead, there is no need to just offer empty words.

For several years now, the acquisition of the three major freedoms has been publicized in print through resolutions and demands by newspaper reporters' groups and other social movement organizations. I myself, as a reporter, have expressed this intention and have advocated for making such claims through movement groups, but it was just talk; no one had any plan to materialize it concretely. There have been instances over several years where this slogan was attached to covertly initiated incidents, but otherwise, it was merely propaganda in print.

Why is that? It is because there are daunting obstacles. Firstly, there are two paths to achieving this expansion of freedom, both very difficult. One path requires the people's mass growth to manifest as a powerful force, making it seem [censored] to expand freedom up to a certain limit rather than impossible not to expand it. This cannot be achieved simply through demands. However, such a manifestation of power through the people's mass growth <6> cannot easily occur.

Another path requires the state of the people's consciousness and emotions to be very submissive and loyal towards one side, to the extent that it is believed that even if freedom of assembly, association, and speech were expanded, there would be no actions arising from their autonomous function that would harm so-called peace and order. Us and Assembly, Association, and Speech
Yi Chuyŏn

I
Freedom of speech, publication, assembly, and association cannot be denied as long as human social life itself cannot be denied.

How can humans, who cannot live without labor and cannot maintain isolated labor relations, possibly survive socially without the freedom to speak, the freedom to write, the freedom to gather, and the freedom to organize? Especially in today's society, characterized by capitalist commodity production, without a high degree of freedom guaranteed for these, humans cannot labor, and life itself is inevitably negated. Freedom of individuality and equal human rights are the greatest content of bourgeois democracy, and bourgeois democracy is the theoretical foundation for the existence and development of today's capitalist society. Capitalist society could not have emerged without emphasizing and guaranteeing these freedoms. Therefore, there is no bourgeois state today that, in principle, denies its people the freedom of speech, publication, assembly, and association. Nevertheless, the bourgeoisie, having established its [censored] ruling power, has completely lost its former historical [censored] nature and become a [censored] [censored] entity, unsatisfied unless it [censored] the freedom of speech, publication, assembly, and association. Today's social phenomena (omitted - original text) show this is by no means a matter of preaching to the ruling class, nor is it the kind of issue that can be resolved through pleas and diplomacy; one must understand it is solely a matter of the power relations of class rule. Furthermore, in a society with [censored] relations, there is no genuine (omitted - original text) for us. Therefore, the [censored] to [censored] freedom of speech, publication, assembly, and association becomes the most crucial immediate aspect [censored] of the class struggle, and at the same time, it will continue until the final stage of the class struggle. **Summary**

An Chaehong and Yi Chuyŏn discuss the critical need to expand freedoms of speech, assembly, and association in colonial Chosŏn, acknowledging the difficulty under restrictive laws like the Security Law and Assembly Control Ordinance. An expresses skepticism about past methods like petitions and resolutions, suggesting true expansion requires either overwhelming mass power or convincing authorities that expanded freedoms pose no threat—both seemingly distant prospects. Yi Chuyŏn adopts a more radical, class-based perspective, framing the issue as an integral part of the class struggle against bourgeois (and implicitly, colonial) power. He dismisses past efforts as "firing blanks" and argues that genuine progress requires mobilizing the masses (workers and peasants) through agitprop, fostering class consciousness, and linking the demand for these freedoms to their immediate interests and broader struggles, including strikes and demonstrations, ultimately driven by strong political and union organizations.

From a historical perspective, the article reveals the intense frustration with the lack of basic civil liberties under Japanese rule in 1931. The explicit mention and critique of specific laws (Security Law, Assembly Control Ordinance, Newspaper Law) highlight the legal framework of repression. The contrast between An's cautious realism and Yi's call for mass mobilization rooted in class struggle reflects differing strategic viewpoints within the Korean nationalist and socialist movements. The heavy censorship (marked by 'XX') in Yi's more radical text vividly illustrates the very restrictions being discussed, making the article itself evidence of the conditions it describes. Historians would find the discussion on strategy (mass power vs. negotiation/persuasion, linking civil liberties to class struggle), the mention of specific organizations (Samchong), and the use of terms like 'Agitprop' indicative of the ideological currents and practical challenges facing Korean activists during this period.
