**Article Title:** 異國에 사랑 (Love in a Foreign Land)

**Article Type:** 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

Love in a Foreign Land
						Choe Sŭng-il

A. Nanjing Edition
(Distant Chinese music drifts from a restaurant-)
Woman: "I dislike it."
Man: "What is it you dislike?"
Woman: "I mean I dislike men like you."<162>
Man: "Why do you dislike me?"
Woman: "Why dislike? Because I dislike you, that's why."
Man: "If you dislike me, shouldn't there be a reason?"
Woman: "Reason—reason, yes there is. I mean I dislike people like you who lack self-reflection (自己反省)."
Man: "Self-reflection = 'Look back upon myself.' No matter how much I look back, I've done nothing wrong."
Woman: "What did you and I promise? When I left Sŏul first, what did you and I promise at Kyŏngsŏng Station? You said you'd follow me after getting a red line drawn through your family register (民籍謄本)."
Man: "And so?"
Woman: "Did you come having done that?"
Man: "A sin—a sin—yes, quite so. What else could it be but a sin? But I am thinking of only one woman now; I am certainly not keeping two women. Look, I got married because they said they'd make me a *mobondan* durumagi coat when I was twelve, but when I saw the woman standing in the wedding hall (初禮廳), she looked like nothing more than an electric pole (電氣). Should I call her my wife forever? (Bitterly) Brothers of the world! Sisters! I cry out. 'We are the sons and daughters of our parents; we are certainly not slaves (奴隷)!' Sending sons off to marry—sending daughters off to be married—out of a desire for honor (名譽慾) claiming they married into a rich family's influence or a noble house—<162> should we bear responsibility for these sins of our parents?" (Composing his voice) Look here, Miss Chŏngsun, should we have to bear responsibility for such sins?"
Woman: "I'm not talking about responsibility for those sins. I'm telling you to take responsibility towards me. Responsibility towards me—let me say it again. Drawing the red line on the family register—that's what I mean."
Man: "Ah—ah—the path of love is thorny brambles—a dark cave—especially a love trying to be bound in this foreign land (異國) where the mountains are unfamiliar and the waters strange—Here is the bustling street of Huapailou (花牌樓)— Look here, Miss Chŏngsun, can't our feelings remain solemn like the existence of yonder Purple Mountain (紫金山), and can't our path of love charge straight forward like the wide, paved avenue (坦坦大路) of yonder Zhongshan Road (中山路)?"
Woman: "Perhaps."
Man: "What do you mean, 'perhaps'? Now—everyone, I will now remove this woman's mask (假面). Sometimes this woman says the red line absolutely must be drawn on the family register, and at other times she says, 'What does that family register matter? As long as we love, that's enough.' So how on earth should one handle (調理) this woman's feelings?" (End)<163>

B. Tokyo Edition
(Distant Shamisen music drifts from a traditional restaurant)
Woman: "Go on, say something."
Man: "What should I say and how?"
Woman: "So you—" (-*sob*-*sob*, she chokes up crying).<161>
Man: "Why are you crying?"
Woman: "Hey, why ask why I'm crying? I never cried in Chosŏn or in Nanjing. Love is this— 'Don't cry. Won't passersby think it strange?—(Bluntly) If you're going to cry, let's go to my place.'"
Woman: "So what if passersby think it's strange? No, so what if the whole world thinks it's strange? Ah—ah—Ueno Park (上野公園) Shinobazu Pond (不忍地) love—tears—If you intend to return to Chosŏn, take me with you. After dragging me from Chosŏn to Nanjing—to Shanghai—to Tokyo, making me into this state, you plan to just sneak off back to Chosŏn following Hyesuk's tail— No way. No way (cries)."
Man: "No. No. Why Hyesuk? What relationship is there between Hyesuk and me?"
Woman: "What do you mean, what? No relationship with Hyesuk? Then what are those letters you exchanged? Why, because I'm young, lack eloquence, and have no talent for romance, you find yourself drawn to that older, sharp-featured, seductive Hyesuk, don't you? With the same skill you used when you said (muttering to herself: Oh—my beloved! I call you an angel (天使)), what did you say you call Hyesuk? A high-class angel (高級天使)!<162> Did you?"
Man: "No. Hey, I only got to know Hyesuk because of you. Calling each other older sister and younger sister, talking about same-sex love (同性戀愛) and opposite-sex love (異性戀愛), that's how I know her. No. The letter was just to ask to borrow some books. (To himself) This is really trouble."
Woman: "Ah—ah—Our love, which moved from the harbor (港) of pleasure (歡樂) surrounding the Confucius Temple (孔子廟) in Nanjing to the wide fields of Musashino (武藏野) in Tokyo, is now taking a train on the Tōkaidō Line (東海導線) with Hyesuk, returning to an *ondol* room (溫突) in Chosŏn. Go if you must. If you try to hold onto departing love, only sighs and tears remain; there's no way it will return, so go."
Man: "No. Hey, are you crazy?"
Woman: "Everyone, I am now letting this man go. This young man, who begged for love and pleaded at my knees behind Purple Mountain in Nanjing—before the grave of Li Bai (李太白)—came to Tokyo and is now singing a song of great victory (勝利) over me from within the arms of another woman. He repudiated the marriage (結婚) his parents arranged. And he went around shouting that love is nature (自然) and emotion (感情) and romance (浪漫). And so he met me. Next, he met Hyesuk. Who will he meet after that? And who will I meet from now on? Female comrades (女性同志) of the whole wide world (滿天下)! Is this the way to find the truth (眞實) of love?"<163>

**Summary**

This two-act play/scenario depicts the relationship turmoil of a Korean man and woman (likely the same woman, Chŏngsun, in both acts) living abroad in the 1930s. In Nanjing, the woman confronts the man for not legally dissolving his arranged marriage in Korea as promised before they left. He defends himself by decrying parental tyranny in marriage but struggles with commitment. In Tokyo, the same conflict resurfaces, intensified by the man's apparent involvement with another Korean woman, Hyesuk. The woman laments being dragged across East Asia only to be potentially abandoned, questioning the nature of modern love and fidelity as the man seems poised to return to Korea with Hyesuk.

The play is interesting for historians as it portrays the mobility of educated/urban Koreans within the Japanese empire (Korea, Nanjing, Shanghai, Tokyo) during the colonial period. It highlights the tension between modern ideals of romantic love and traditional obligations (arranged marriage, family registry), reflecting the social changes and anxieties of the era. The explicit references to specific locations in Nanjing and Tokyo ground the narrative in the lived experience of Koreans abroad, while the themes of betrayal, emotional conflict, and the search for authentic relationships speak to the complexities of modern identity formation under colonial rule.
