Hero Theory
						Wŏn Sehun

According to transmitted sayings, it is said that only a sage can know a sage. If so, then only a hero can know a hero. However, just as the opposite of the poor man is the rich man, and the opposite of the commoner is the *yangban* (兩班), if the opposite of the mediocre man (*cholchangbu*, 拙丈夫) is the hero, then even a mediocre man can know a hero. When Cho Cho (曹操), addressing Yu Pi (劉備), discussed the heroes of the realm, saying, "The heroes of the realm are only you, my lord (使君, Yu Pi), and myself (Cho)," it was a hero discussing heroes. However, I am a mediocre man discussing heroes. Just as a mediocre man discusses heroes, the theory is also mediocre and brief. Wherein lies the distinction between a hero and a mediocre man? I wish to answer with a single phrase. That phrase is: "Those who are capable of anything (無所不爲) are generally all heroes, and those who have things they will not do (有所不爲) are generally mediocre men." The term 'hero' can be said to mean 'a capable person,' and the term 'mediocre man' can be said to mean 'an incapable person.' Not just recently, but according to what is often seen and heard, capable people seem to be incapable of nothing, while incapable people have things they do not do. To act as a communist, become a believer in Christianity, become a comrade to A who is the exact opposite, and also a comrade to B, gaining trust everywhere—this is truly a capable person, a hero who does not refrain from doing anything. However, the type known as the mediocre man, in order to do one thing, refrains from doing another, and ends up becoming an incapable person. <52>

Seeing what such heroes do, some have argued that politicians inevitably cannot avoid changing their allegiance (*pyŏnjŏl*, 變節). However, that argument seems to have missed the mark. While it may seem like changing allegiance to the observer, in reality, it is not a change of allegiance, but rather because their original true nature (*chinmyŏnmok*, 眞面目) is dazzlingly brilliant. In other words, it is not a change of allegiance but the true colors of a hero capable of anything. If a widow who had lived alone in an empty room for several decades after being widowed were to remarry, one might call it abandoning her fidelity (*hwejŏl*, 毁節) and remarrying. But if a widow in name only consorted with various men morning, noon, and night, enjoying pleasures, and then finally declared she would formally begin married life with a certain specific man, that woman is not abandoning fidelity and remarrying; she is beginning a formal married life after living like a secret prostitute (*amch'ang*, 暗娼).

In the old Chosŏn (朝鮮) era, there was a certain person who served as magistrate in many different counties and was extremely upright and clean-handed (*ch'ŏngbaek*, 淸白), not taking even a single penny of the people's money. Consequently, his reputation as a "renowned official who loves the people and governs well" (*aemin sŏnch'i ŭi myŏnggwan*, 愛民善治의 名官) and an "incorruptible official" (*ch'ŏngbaengni*, 淸白吏) spread throughout the court and the country. As a result, he became the governor (*kamsa*, 監司) of a certain province. From the time he became governor, his oppression of the people and greed for wealth (*t'amjae*, 貪財) knew no bounds (*musobulwi*, 無所不爲). At that time, the governor's beloved concubine (*aech'ŏp*, 愛妾) saw the governor and wept. When the governor asked the reason for his concubine's weeping, the concubine said, "Your Excellency served as magistrate in many counties but did not covet even a penny of the people's wealth. Now, to covet wealth and oppress the people like this must mean Your Excellency's original heart (*ponsim*, 本心) has changed. If your original heart has changed, how can you live long? I weep because I fear I may not be able to serve Your Excellency for long." Hearing this, the governor laughed and said, "That is because you still do not know my original heart. When I served as magistrate in various counties, the reason I did not covet the people's wealth was not because my heart truly did not covet it, but because if word got out back then that I was greedy, I wouldn't have gotten much money anyway and wouldn't have been promoted. But now I have become a governor, my position is high, and I can take a lot of money, so taking it starting now is precisely 'my original heart'." Just as the concubine lamented, thinking it was a change of heart because she truly did not know the governor's original heart, so too people argue it is a change of allegiance (*pyŏnjŏl*, 變節) because they do not know the true nature of politicians—or rather, capable people, in other words, the heroes of the age.

Heroes capable of anything and mediocre men who have things they will not do! Do you deem my mediocre theory acceptable? <53>
<52-53>

**Summary**

Wŏn Sehun argues that the defining characteristic of a "hero" is their willingness and ability to do absolutely anything (無所不爲, *musobulwi*), regardless of principle or consistency, contrasting them with the "mediocre man" (拙丈夫, *cholchangbu*) who possesses scruples or things they will not do (有所不爲, *yusobulwi*). He refutes the idea that such "heroes," particularly in politics, undergo a change of allegiance (*pyŏnjŏl*, 變節), asserting instead that their seemingly contradictory actions reveal their true, multifaceted, and opportunistic nature, illustrated by an anecdote about a Chosŏn-era governor. For historians, this article offers a contemporary critique from 1934 Korea, potentially reflecting disillusionment with political figures or collaborators during the colonial period. The framing of opportunism not as betrayal but as the "true nature" of "capable" individuals provides insight into cynical perspectives on survival, success, and morality under Japanese rule.

英雄論
						元世勳
傳하는 말에 依하면 聖人이라야 聖人을 안다고 하엿다. 그러면 英雄이라 能히 英雄을 알 것이다. 그러나 貧者의 反面이 富者이고 常漢의 反面이 兩班임과 갓치 拙丈夫의 反面이 英雄이라면 拙丈夫도 英雄을 能히 알 수가 잇다. 曹操가 劉備에게 對하야 天下英雄을 論하다가 『天下의 英雄은 오즉 使君(劉備)과 操라』함은 英雄으로 英雄을 論한 것이다. 그러나 나는 拙丈夫로 英雄을 論하는 것이다. 拙丈夫가 英雄을 論하는 것만치 論도 拙論이며 簡短하다. 英雄과 拙丈夫의 區別이 어데 잇는가? 하면 나는 한마듸로써 答하고저 한다. 『無所不爲하는 사람은 大槪가 모다 英雄이오 有所不爲하는 사람은 大槪가 拙丈夫이다』하는 한마듸이다. 英雄이라는 말은 『잘난 사람이라고 할 수가 잇고 拙丈夫라는 말은 못난 사람이라고 할 수가 잇다』 近來뿐만 아니라 往往 보고들이는 바에 依하면 잘난 사람들은 못하는 즛이 업고 못난 사람은 하지 아니하는 바가 잇다. 共産主義者노릇도 하고 耶穌敎의 信者로 되며 正反對가 되는 甲에게도 同志가 되고 乙에게도 同志가 되야 到處에 信任을 받는다면 이것은 참으로 잘난 사람이며 무엇이든지 하지 아니하는 것이 업는 英雄이다. 그러나 拙丈夫의 種類는 한가지를 하기 爲하야 달은 한가지는 하지 아니하다가 못난 사람이 되고마는 것이다.＜52＞
어떤 분은 그러한 英雄들이 하는 바를 보고 政治家는 반듯이 變節하지 아니치 못한다고 論한 이가 잇다. 그러나 그 論은 正鵠을 得하지 곳한듯하다. 論者가 보기에 變節하는 듯하지만은 其實은 變節이 아니라 本來의 眞面目이 况惚燦爛한 故이다. 다시 말하면 變節이 아니라 無所不爲하는 英雄의 本色이다. 寡婦된지 數十년에 獨守空房하든 寡婦가 再嫁를 갓다면 毁節再嫁라 하겟지만 일홈만이 寡婦로 朝三暮四로 뭇男子와 交接하며 享樂하다가 終末에 그 어떤 一定한 男子와 正式으로 夫婦生活을 하노라고 宣言한다면 그 女子는 毁節 再嫁가 아니라. 暗娼的 生活에서 正式夫妻의 生活을 비로소 始作하는 것이다.
녯날 朝鮮時代에서 어떤 분이 여러 고을 원님노릇을 많이 하는데 아조 淸白하야 民間의 돈이라고는 한푼도 먹지 아니하엿다. 그리하야 『愛民善治의 名官이라』 『淸白吏』라는 名望이 朝野에 宣傳되얏다. 그 結果로 그는 어느 道의 監司가 되엿다. 監司가 된 때로부터는 虐民貪財하기가 無所不爲이엇다. 그때에 그 監司의 愛妾이 監司를 보고 울엇다. 監司가 그 愛妾의 우는 曲節을 물은즉 그 妾이 말하되 大監께서 여러 고을 원노릇을 하되 民財를 한푼도 貪하시지 아니 하드니 이제 이와같이 貪財虐民하시는 것은 必是 大監의 本心이 變하신 것이라. 本心이 變하시고 어찌 오래 사시릿가? 내가 大監을 오래 모시지 못할가 하여서 우나이다. 監司가 그 말을 듣고 우스며 말하되 그것은 네가 아즉도 나의 本心을 알지 못한 것이다. 여로 고을 원노릇을 할 때에 民財를 貪하지 아니한 것은 本心이 民財를 貪하지 아니한 것이 아니라 그때부터 貪財한다는 所聞이 나면 돈도 멧푼 먹지 곳하고 베슬도 올너가지 곳할터임으로 그러하엿거니와 오늘은 監司가 되엿으니 베슬 높고 돈도 많이 먹을 수 잇으니 이제부터 먹는 것이 곳 『나의 本心이라』하엿다. 참으로 그 妾이 그 監司의 本心을 알지 못한 데서 變心인가 하여 哀嘆함과 갓치 政治家라기보다 잘난 사람 다시 말하면 時代的 英雄의 眞面目을 알지 안한는 데서 變節이라고 論한 것이다.
無所不爲의 英雄들과 有所不爲의 拙丈夫들아! 나의 拙論을 可하다 하는가?＜53＞
