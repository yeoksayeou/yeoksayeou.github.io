To the New Land
						Ch'unwŏn (春園)
To the new land
Eŏyadŭya eŏeŏhyŏri
Ŏgwiŏch'a weigh the anchor
Old land, farewell
Ŏgwiŏch'a to the new land

Eŏyadŭya eŏeŏhyŏri
Ŏgwiŏch'a weigh the anchor
On the vast blue waves, a ten-thousand *ri* journey
I am going, to the new land

Eŏyadŭya eŏeŏhyŏri
Ŏgwiŏch'a weigh the anchor
If I go now, when shall I return?
To the new land without promise

Eŏyadŭya eŏeŏhyŏri
Ŏgwiŏch'a hoist the sail
Farewell, farewell
I am going, to the new land

Birthday Clothes
The birthday clothes big last year, this year reach the knees
"It's small, what to do?- I'll hand it down to my younger sibling" Seeing the adults laugh, the child laughs along too <46>

**Summary**

This entry features two short poems by Ch'unwŏn (Yi Kwangsu). The first, "To the New Land," uses the rhythm of a sea shanty to depict a departure from the "old land" towards a "new land." The journey is long and the return uncertain, carrying a tone of both hope and apprehension. The second poem, "Birthday Clothes," is a brief, gentle observation of a child outgrowing clothes and innocently planning to pass them down. From a historical perspective, "To the New Land" is particularly resonant. Written in 1929 during Japanese colonial rule, the vague yet evocative "new land" could symbolize a desire for escape, change, or a different future, reflecting the complex sentiments of Koreans navigating their uncertain national situation, while the ambiguity leaves its exact meaning open to interpretation.
