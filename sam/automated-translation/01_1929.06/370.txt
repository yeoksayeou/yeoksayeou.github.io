Article Title: 頌三千里 (Ode to the Three Thousand Ri [Korea])
Author: 沈熏 (Sim Hun)
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

頌三千里
						沈熏

Climbing to a high place and looking down upon this land, how dignified are the large peaks and small mountain spurs! Where the line of sight (視線) melts into the haze, they soar up jaggedly, then curve and run in slopes (勾配)—how beautiful you are, making one wish to embrace and roll in your bosom.

The dense pine ridge of Mongmyŏk is like the waist of a mother who cherished and caressed, and Samgak Mountain is like the tip of a warrior's sword drawn before others; as if one thrust would pour down dark clouds, your spirit (氣象) is still so imposing (凜凜).

Though surrounded by the sea, the waves are not angered; though finely embroidered (繡) with springs and streams, the water is clear and the sea so blue, one sip feels as if it would grant a lifespan (壽) of 100 years—bubbling up, it overflows and flows on.

On that mountainside where our ancestor sleeps, the cuckoo cries, keeping watch through the long night. My beloved, when you too return, I will lay you gently to rest upon that hill, and lie myself at your feet to forget deep sorrow.

Brother leaving with a gourd dipper slung on your back, Sister gathering sorghum (高梁) ears in the rough fields—may even your white bones (白骨) return to be buried in this soil. Oh, the more I look, the more beautiful you are, my Rivers and Mountains! <47>

**Summary**

This poem by Sim Hun is a lyrical ode to the beauty and spirit of the Korean landscape (referred to as "Samch'ŏlli," the Three Thousand Ri). The poet expresses deep affection for the mountains, seas, and streams, comparing geographical features like Mongmyŏk (Namsan) and Samgak Mountain to a mother's embrace and a warrior's sword, respectively. It touches upon themes of ancestral connection, love, loss, and the enduring hope for return to the homeland, even in death. From a historical perspective, the poem, written during the Japanese colonial period, is significant for its strong expression of national identity and love for Korea, coded in the praise of its natural beauty and the resilience of its people. Its focus on the land ("Rivers and Mountains") and the longing for return subtly conveys patriotic sentiment and resistance against assimilation efforts during a time of suppressed national expression.
