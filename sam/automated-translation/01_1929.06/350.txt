**Body Text Translation**

Flower-Selling Child (賣花童)
Yi Ŭn-sang (李殷相)

Flower-Selling Child
The flower-selling child,
The flower-selling child,
The cry "Buy flowers!" floats through the street.

Buy spring, buy the spring!
The mountain cuckoo on the high mountain, buy the mountain spring!
The blue willows by the riverbank, buy the river spring! <46>

The mountain spring, the river spring,
The mountain spring, the river spring,
Comes loaded on the child's flower cart.

O flower-selling child,
O flower-selling child,
Please carry away this heart's fine end too.

Taking those flowers,
I will show them to those who come and go.
I will make the hearts of those who see them burn too.

Burning heart, red flower,
Burning heart, red flower,
Carried across this land of three thousand *ri*.

Flower of Chosŏn (조선의 꼿)

In the rugged mountain valleys and such places,
Seeking the spring light, the sprout (용리) comes forth.
Ah, the budding flower of Chosŏn.

Today decorating this eastern mountain,
Tomorrow spreading fragrance across this land,
Ah, the budding flower of Chosŏn.

Though wind and rain blow across the fields,
Though trees upon the mountains fall,
Ah, the blooming flower of Chosŏn. <47>
<46-47>

**Summary**

The document presents two poems by Yi Ŭn-sang. The first, "Flower-Selling Child," depicts a child selling spring flowers, symbolizing hope and vitality. The speaker wishes the child could also take away a piece of their heart, spreading passion ('burning heart') across the land ('three thousand ri,' referring to Korea) like the flowers. The second poem, "Flower of Chosŏn," uses the metaphor of a resilient flower budding and blooming despite harsh conditions (wind, rain, falling trees) to represent the enduring spirit and future potential of Korea (Chosŏn). For historians, these poems, published in 1929 under Japanese colonial rule, are significant expressions of subtle Korean nationalism. The imagery of enduring hardship and burgeoning life (the flower, the spring) likely resonated with contemporary readers as symbols of hope for Korea's future and survival despite the colonial environment. The use of "Chosŏn" and "Samch'ŏlli" explicitly grounds this sentiment in a national context.
