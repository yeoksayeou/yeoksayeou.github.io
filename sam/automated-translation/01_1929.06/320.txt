**Body Text Translation:**
A Pilgrimage to Famous Literary Places
						Ch'ogong

Although I am not Yi Hyŏngsik, who was engaged to Elder Kim’s daughter Sŏnhyŏng and was going to study abroad in America, I boarded the Kyŏngbu Line train in Sŏul bound for Pusan. And though it wasn't because the train had stopped due to major floods in the Yŏngnam region, I nonetheless got off alone unexpectedly at Samnangjin Station.

Furthermore, while Yi Hyŏngsik, leading the lovely young women Yŏngch'ae, Sŏnhyŏng, and Pyŏng'uk, entered a Japanese inn hearing a maid's voice call out "Irasshai-," I, as if calling a deer a horse (指鹿謂馬格), went into a thatched hut behind the police substation and sat down alone, drinking bean-paste soup boiled with beef broth.

While drinking, I looked around for the inn where Hyŏngsik might have stayed. Right beside the station stood two or three cherry blossom trees (櫻花나무) in full bloom, and adjacent to them, on one side of a shop selling the famous Samnangjin pears packed in large baskets, there was a two-story wooden inn with a sign reading "Morita" (森田) that catered to customers.

Judging by the rotten planks, its age seemed to be 20 or 30 years, making it plausible that it had experienced the flood from the novel over ten years ago. Also, considering that Hyŏngsik had sought lodging without even a raincoat, this inn close to the station seemed likely to be *that* inn. Thinking this, I suddenly stood up and went to the front of the building. Just then, a Japanese maid with plump legs came out carrying a pear basket, smiled brightly, and urged me to buy fruit as a gift. If I were to buy now, I'd be buying pears, buying sentiment, and buying the four memories of *Mujŏng* (The Heartless). I immediately paid 20 *chŏn* and received six large, fist-sized pears.

The great floodwaters described then – where after many days of rain, waters from ten valleys merged into one, sweeping away houses and crops – given the topography of Samnangjin, must have ultimately flowed through the railway tunnel and into the Naktong River; there could have been no other way. Looking at the base of that mountain, where the gouges from the water are still visible, an elementary school with a tin roof had since been built, and in the wide schoolyard, students like ant heads (개아미 대가리가튼 학생들) were scattered about thick and fast. There were perhaps only 40 or 50 houses. Still, with fan-shaped mountains surrounding it tightly like a bride's chamber screen, and the wide plain leading to Tongnae and Pusan opening up far away, it was a place fit for human habitation.

The iron fence by the rice paddy ridge where Hyŏngsik's group leaned against the pear tree watching the water; the roadside so vividly described, where the old woman embraced her daughter-in-law in labor and wept; the thatched cottage kitchen where the two irreconcilable young women, Yŏngch'ae and Sŏnhyŏng, vied with each other, saying "I'll light the fire!", rushing down – I can almost hear the old woman's voice, moved to gratitude, chanting like a prayer, "May you live long, be wealthy, have many sons, and live well," but the actual house cannot be found. Perhaps not being able to find it is what preserves the essence of *Mujŏng*? (To page 22) <45>

(From page 45)
While station waiting rooms elsewhere are usually cramped with low ceilings, feeling oppressive, the waiting room here was significantly longer, wider, and higher. If chairs were lined up, it could instantly become a concert hall capable of seating about a hundred people.

Inside here, Pak Yŏngch'ae, the former kisaeng, with her beautiful voice, sang:
"The little baby frets. / It frets for lack of milk. / A bowl of warm soup, / Let's mix it with rice and feed it."
And if Pyŏng'uk, the most progressive character in the novel and whose personality is well-defined, the tomboy from the Tokyo Music School, had played the violin, not only the travelers bored by the flood but anyone would have been entranced. If Kye Wŏrhyang, the passionate woman like Sappho of Greece, who committed suicide on Nŭngna Island in P'yŏngyang, had also been present, how much more tense the scene would have been?

Leaning back on a bench, I thought of those women who cried over various loves and desires. Listening intently, it felt as if I could almost see Yŏngch'ae, Sŏnhyŏng, and Pyŏng'uk, and even hear their voices. Finally, I could almost see the audience's applause, the police chief receiving the proceeds from the charity concert and bowing to the three young women, and even the unconventional newspaper reporter Sin Usŏn entering, adjusting his shapeless felt hat askew.

My memory of *Mujŏng*, which I read once over ten years ago, is quite faint. However, luckily having the chance to wait 20 or 30 minutes at Samnangjin to transfer to the Kyŏngnam Line, I got off the train, and thoughts of *Mujŏng* naturally arose, leading me to jot down these rambling impressions. *Mujŏng* is an unforgettable work for its excellent descriptions and for showing a group of young men and women during Korea's period of enlightenment. Samnangjin is even more unforgettable for being the setting of the work's climax. But more than that, the name Samnangjin itself evokes affection. Names like Samnangjin or Sariwŏn melt easily in the mouth like eating a pear, don't they? They have fragrance and pleasant pronunciation. <26>

**Summary**

The author, Ch'ogong, visits Samnangjin station, a key location in Yi Kwangsu's famous novel *Mujŏng* (The Heartless). He reflects on the novel's events and characters—Yi Hyŏngsik, Pak Yŏngch'ae, Kim Sŏnhyŏng, Pyŏng'uk—imagining them in the real setting: the station, a nearby Japanese inn, the site of the flood, and the station waiting room where a charity concert took place in the story. While searching for specific spots mentioned in the novel proves difficult, the author finds the atmosphere evocative and appreciates the literary significance and the pleasing sound of the place name "Samnangjin." For historians, the article offers a glimpse into literary tourism in colonial Korea, blending appreciation for modern Korean literature with observations of the contemporary landscape under Japanese influence (Japanese inns, infrastructure, school). The casual mention of the police chief and the Japanese maid alongside Korean characters reflects the societal mix of the time.

文學上 著名地巡禮
						草公
나는 김장로의 따님 善馨이와 約婚하여 가지고 아메리카로 유학을 가는 李享植이도 아니것만은 京釜線을 서울서 집어 타고 釜山을 향하엿고 또 嶺南에 큰물이 저서 긔차가 멈춘 것도 아니것만은 三浪津 停車장에서 슴겁게도 혼자 내렷다.
더구나 어엽분 영채, 선형, 병욱의 세 처녀를 다린 李享植이가 「이랏샤이-」하는 하녀의 소리를 드러가며 일본려관으로 드러가드라는데 나는 指鹿謂馬格으로 派出所 뒤 草幕에 드러 안저 소내포로 끄린 장국물을 혼자 마시고 잇섯다.
마이면서도 형식이가 드러잇섯스리란 그 려관을 두루 살펴보니 바로 뎡거장 엽헤 滿開한 櫻花나무가 두서너 그루 서고 그와 맛다이어 有名한 三浪津배를 대광주리에 너허 파는 商店 한편에 손님을 치는 森田이란 看板부친 二層木製旅館 한 개가 잇섯다.
널판지가 썩은 것으로 보아 이미 家齡이 2,30年에 미치어 10여 년 전 그 小說 속 洪水를 첫슬것 갓기도 하고 또 享植이가 우비도 업시 려관을 차젓든 뎜으로 보아 뎡거장과 갓가운 이 려관이 그 旅館 갓기도 하엿다. 이러케 생각하고 나는 불연듯 이러나 그 집 압흐로 가니 마츰 다리가 투실투실 익은 일본하녀 하나가 배광주리를 들고 나오다가 빙그레 우스며 과일을 션물로 사 가라고 권한다. 이제 산다면 배 사고 情 사고 그러고 無情의 넷 記億을 살 판이라 나는 즉시 20錢을 내주고 주먹만한 큰 배 여섯 개를 바더 들엇다.

그 때 여러 날 비 끗헤 열에 열골 물이 한데 合水하여 집깐과 곡식을 마구 밀어 나가더란 큰물줄기는 三浪津의 地勢로 살퍼본다면 結局 털도 길뚝을 뚤고 洛東江으로 흘너 갓슬 밧게는 별 도리가 업겟다. 아직 물에 패인 홈이 잇는 저 산 밋을 보니 거기에는 어느 새에 함석집웅의 보통학교가 섯고 너른 校庭에는 개아미 대가리가튼 학생들이 옥실옥실 허트러저 잇섯다. 人家도 不過 4, 50戶 될가. 그래도 부채모양의 連山이 新婦방 屛風가치 빈틈 업시 들너친데다가 멀니 東萊, 釜山 가는 너른 平野가 툭 터진 곳이 사럼죽한 곳이다.
享植一行이 배를 기대고 물구경을 하엿다든 논두덕의 鐵條網이나 老娑가 臨産의 며누리를 끼어 안고 우드라는 특히 描寫가 잘된 그 길가나 「제가 때일게요!」하고 不共戴天의 두 처녀가 서로 다투어 부엌으로 내려가드란 그 초가집깐이 어대인지 老娑가 感激하여 壽富多男子하고 잘 사시오 하며 念佛가치 외우드란 말소리는 들니는 듯 하것만은 집깐은 알 수 업다. 알 수 업는 것이 오히려 無情을 살니는 것이 아니될가? (第22頁에) ＜45＞
