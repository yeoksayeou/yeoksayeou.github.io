自叙傳 (Autobiography)
						洪命熹 (Hong Myŏnghŭi)
緖言 (Preface)
I am a person without the self-esteem to write an autobiography. How did I, lacking self-esteem, come to write one? As an answer, I must confess one weakness. I have the weakness of being unable to assert my stubbornness. Never mind my childish obstinacy, but how did I, who rarely yielded to others even up to my twenties, acquire this weakness? Was it the result of cultivation, realizing stubbornness as a vice? Not so. After experiencing the changes of the world (世變), my disposition changed unknowingly (不知不識間); my competitive spirit (紛競心) diminished, my self-confidence (自信力) lessened, and this weakness began to emerge. Now, I exert some conscious effort to correct this weakness, but this ingrained habit of over twenty years is not easily fixed, and I continue to suffer losses, big and small, day by day. When P'ain (巴人) cajoled me into writing an autobiography, I refused. P'ain, being a persistent person, did not take back his words and kept pestering me. Rather than letting him be annoyed, I endured his pestering, then his wheedling, and after the wheedling, his insistence, until I finally lost the courage to refuse and agreed to write something called an autobiography. Ultimately, my weakness caused me harm. What harm? Turgenev's discovered secret to navigating the world (處世秘術) is to hide one's weakness and attack others with it, but for me to confess even this weakness, isn't that a loss? This is pointless talk. Having agreed, how should I write this autobiography? I wasted energy and time agonizing over it. Should I follow Rousseau's "Confessions" and confess my past nakedly? I don't even dream of offering it as documentary evidence for the Last Judgment, but even Rousseau's nakedness is said to be incomplete. If a person lacking even Rousseau's resolve were to rashly wield the brush, they would only become a laughingstock. Should I imitate Nietzsche's "Ecce Homo" and vent the spirit (氣熖) of a philosopher? I am not one to leap from Alpine peak to peak, so I would only add another example of Mr. Hwang or Mr. Paem [common surnames, implying 'just another ordinary person']. I have seen Kropotkin's "Memoirs of a Revolutionist," Trotsky's "My Flight from Siberia," Katayama's "Autobiography," and heard there are autobiographies by figures like Sakai and Ōsugi. The more I think about what I've heard and seen, the more the thought that autobiographies and similar writings are not for ordinary people takes precedence, and my courage to write one disappears. Lacking the courage to refuse, I agreed to write; lacking the courage to write, I couldn't, and idly delayed, finding myself in a predicament. Having no choice, I preface it with the adjective "would-be" and begin to write. As my body has passed through some forty years, it has left numerous shed skins (解皮) along the way; I intend to pick up only the larger-looking ones among them. If I breathe air into these skins, childish boasts might emerge; if I add color to them, hidden shame might be revealed. But I do not wish to chase the wind of a Chosŏn Baron (초센남작), so I won't puff them up with hot air to invite ridicule. Nor do I intend to slash Dorian Gray's portrait, so I won't apply harsh colors to add to my suffering. Thus, this nominal autobiography will amount to no more than picking up and putting down shed skins, drawing them like a clumsy picture. That it will become even clumsier among the clumsy is because P'ain has grasped my wrist as I draw.

유소년시대 (Childhood)
Three years after I was born into this world, my mother passed away, and my great-grandfather passed away. When I was six, my grandmother passed away. I gained a stepmother when I was four, <10> but I was raised by my great-grandmother and great-aunt. I called my grandmother the "lower room grandmother" (아래ㅅ방 할머니) and my great-grandmother the "inner room grandmother" (안방할머니). Much later, when my great-grandmother left the inner room and moved to the main room (대방), the title "inner room grandmother" was changed to "main room grandmother" (대방할머니). However, by then my grandmother had passed away, so there was no need to distinguish the grandmothers by the rooms they occupied, and I mostly just called her "grandmother" (할머니). I called my great-aunt "Kkokka Mother" (꼬까어머니). This nickname is said to have originated when I was three or four, seeing my great-aunt wearing new clothes on her daughter-in-law's bridal visit day and saying she wore "kkokka clothes" (꼬까옷 - children's term for colorful new clothes). Thereafter, besides calling her "Kkokka Mother," I also called my great-uncle "Kkokka Father" (꼬까아버지), and even their son and daughter-in-law "Kkokka Uncle" (꼬까아저씨) and "Kkokka Auntie" (꼬까아주머니). It was only after I turned six or seven that I started playing at Kkokka Mother's place, sometimes even sleeping there; before that, I never left my great-grandmother's side. I didn't know it then, but I later heard that the effort my great-grandmother put into raising her motherless great-grandson was beyond words. Just finding someone to nurse me was no small task, and since I was frail and frequently ill as a child, she rarely had a peaceful night's sleep. Even in her later years, when my great-grandmother slept, she always tilted her head back uncomfortably, which looked awkward. This began when she slept with young me in front of her, tilting her head back lest her breath should touch my face, and it eventually became a habit. Although all of us siblings, from my father above down to us, were raised by our grandmother's hand, the immense grace I received was due to my particular circumstances.

When I was five, I began learning the Thousand Character Classic (천자), supposedly entering school. Even before that, I already knew characters like Heaven, Earth, Sun, Moon, Parents, Siblings (天地日月父母兄弟) from what my grandfather wrote for me on pieces of paper. However, being naturally prone to illness and spoiled, my studies were hardly diligent. At eight, I finally began learning the *Sohak* (소학, *Lesser Learning*) and was taught how to make phrases by putting together five characters for composition. Because I had my grandmother and Kkokka Mother, I didn't know the sadness of lacking a mother. But when someone who came with my mother from her natal home saw me alone, they would sniffle and cry, telling me how my mother, after giving birth to me, suffered from postpartum complications that became an illness, lingered for three years, and then passed away. On her deathbed, my mother nursed baby me and said, "This child won't even know his mother's face (얼골)." Hearing various stories about my mother, tears of longing for her began to fall from my eyes. Composing the five characters "蒼蠅年年生吾母何不歸" (Flies come year after year, why does my mother not return?) marked, so to speak, the beginning of my composing Chinese poetry (한시). However, it was only after I turned eleven or twelve that I learned to apply rhymes (韻) and discern tones (高低). I think it was when I was thirteen or fourteen. My father was composing poems on snow (咏雪詩) with my teacher and a few other elders. Seeing me nearby, he told me to try composing a verse too. The two couplets of the five-character regulated verse (五律) I composed then were "何如花落後 疑是月生初 飄蕩爭侵夜 虛明可映書" (How is it after the flowers fall? / Suspected to be when the moon is first born. / Drifting contends to invade the night, / Its empty brightness can illuminate a book.) My father remarked that the character 書 (book) was forced [in context?] (强□) in a snow poem, but praised the "empty brightness" (虛明) line as good. I felt as if I had received an unexpected honor. Having grown up without fearsome elders in the house, my father was the only person I feared. When I fought with my aunt, who was six or seven years older, calling each other names, my grandmother or Kkokka Mother would just laugh and let it be, even siding with me when my aunt raised her hand against me. But my father, whether I was right or wrong, would always glare at me disapprovingly. Thus, I avoided my father. Thinking back now, the reason I still remember those unremarkable poetic lines is that it was the first time in my life I received praise from my father. There are two unforgettable things from when I was eleven. One is getting into trouble for stealing. The other is developing a taste for novels. The theft occurred at the house of a person serving my grandfather<11>; I still vividly remember the scene. In the evening, I went to play at a small house behind the main men's quarters (사랑). As I was about to leave through the gate, that person, smoking a pipe, was strolling outside his own wicker gate (삽작). Seeing me, he called out, "Hey kid, won't you come visit my house?" So I briefly entered his house. He showed me various things, among them a pair of pincenez spectacles (샐쭉안경). These pincenez were like those served with *makgeolli* (막걸리) appetizers at the Seoul house, but it was the first time I had seen them, and I coveted them. He said, "Kid, try them on," and even put them on me, but he didn't give them to me. Thinking back, perhaps he didn't give them because they weren't useful, rather than because he was reluctant. He was called away by my grandfather. Left alone inside with his son, the son went out, maybe to urinate or defecate. In that interval, I stole the spectacles and fled. His son chased after me, shouting, "Why are you stealing my father's glasses and running away!" I was terrified. I threw the spectacles into the neighboring vegetable patch (채마밧) located at the corner of the room [likely meaning adjacent to the house], stood there, looked at his son, and said, "Go into that patch and find them," then ran straight home in one breath. Though I never kept secrets from my grandmother, I remember not telling her about this incident. However, this event became a great lesson for me. Afterwards, whenever I saw something I desired, the pincenez would come to mind first, and I wouldn't dare entertain the thought of stealing. As for novels, during the New Year holidays that year, I borrowed a volume of *Samgukchi* (*Romance of the Three Kingdoms*) from my great-uncle's house. I read—or rather, was taught—the first two or three volumes together with an elderly man from the household. The next ten or so volumes, I read by myself. Of course, it was like a dog eating wild grapes (개가 머루 먹듯한 것); there was more I didn't understand than I did. But I read the part about Zhao Zilong grasping his spear and mounting his horse (趙子龍綽鎗上馬) with excitement. I finished skimming through the twenty volumes after the "day of the chicken ghost" [likely referring to a specific day marking the end of New Year festivities] when I started learning characters again, and I remember my teacher telling me to stop reading them. After that, I became hooked on reading novels on the street [or perhaps meaning 'popular novels'? 길래], becoming absorbed in works like *Dong Zhou Lie Guo Zhi* (*Chronicles of the Eastern Zhou Kingdoms*, 東周列國志) and *Xihan Yanyi* (*Romance of the Western Han*, 西漢演義) even more than the *Analects* (論語) or *Mencius* (孟子). However, our house had few novels, and I had to read them secretly without the elders knowing. So, after coming up to Seoul at fourteen, I borrowed and read many from friends. How I managed to read things like *Shui Hu Zhuan* (*Water Margin*, 水滸傳), *Xi You Ji* (*Journey to the West*, 西遊記), let alone *Jin Ping Mei* (金甁梅), with my insufficient literary comprehension (文理不足) at the time—there's a limit to skipping what you don't understand (疑者闕之), but what enjoyment could there have been in skipping almost the entire text (全文闕之)? Yet, it's remarkable that I read them, forgetting even to eat. I got married at thirteen, came up to Seoul at fourteen, and at fifteen, began attending a school called Chunggyo Ŭisuk (中橋義塾). Nowadays, *not* attending school is unusual, but back then, attending school *was* unusual. So how did our conservative family come to send me to school? Externally, the headmaster (塾監) of Chunggyo Ŭisuk at the time was someone my grandfather did not dislike (상업지 안케 녀기는 사람), and he persuaded my grandfather. Internally, my father, having insight (眼識) into the trends of the times, spoke to my grandfather. Every day when I went to school, my grandfather gave me one 2 *chŏn* 5 *ri* white copper coin (白銅 한닙) without saying what it was for. This became my capital for things like cigarettes (卷烟). When I returned from school, occasionally, by grandfather's command, I would go before him and recite "A-I-U-E-O" (アイウエオ) for him to hear, and also write Arabic numerals. After listening and looking at everything, my grandfather would often laugh and ask, "What is all that?" Sometimes, if something displeased him, he would say dismissively, "Trying to catch the outside rabbit, you'll lose the house rabbit. I suppose you intend to just read those letters [implying Japanese script over classical Chinese study]?" In any case, I attended the school for three full years, graduated from the Japanese language course (日語科), and went down to the countryside that year. By then, I was already eighteen (열여덜). **Summary**

Hong Myŏnghŭi recounts the beginning of his decision to write an autobiography, prompted by the persistent encouragement of someone called "P'ain," despite his own feelings of inadequacy and weakness derived from life experiences. He reflects on his early childhood, marked by the loss of his mother and upbringing by his great-grandmother and great-aunt ("Kkokka Mother"). He details his early education in Chinese classics, his first attempts at poetry (receiving rare praise from his feared father), a memorable childhood incident of stealing pincenez glasses which taught him a lesson, and his burgeoning fascination with classical Chinese novels like *Samgukchi*. The narrative then shifts to his teenage years: marriage at 13, moving to Seoul at 14, and attending a modern school, Chunggyo Ŭisuk, at 15 to study Japanese, a decision influenced by external persuasion and his father's foresight, despite his traditional grandfather's skepticism. For historians, this memoir offers valuable insights into the personal life and intellectual formation of a prominent Korean figure during the colonial period. It highlights the transition from traditional Confucian education to modern, Japanese-influenced schooling (Chunggyo Ŭisuk, learning Japanese), the complex family dynamics shaped by loss and tradition, and the author's early engagement with both classical East Asian literature and Western/Japanese revolutionary thought (mentioned in the preface), reflecting the intellectual ferment among Korean elites under colonial rule. The casual mention of receiving money daily for school hints at his privileged background, while the reference to "Chosŏn Baron" suggests an early awareness or critique of collaborationism.

나는 자서전을 지을 만한 자부심이 업는 사람이다. 엇지하야 자부심이 업시 자서전을 짓게 되엇는가? 대답으로 한가지 약점을 자백할 것이 잇다. 나는 고집을 세우지 못하는 약점을 가젓다. 어릴 때악지는 말하지 말고라도 20시절까지 좀처럼 남에게 지지 아니하든 내가 어느틈에 이 약점을 가지게 되엿는가? 고집을 악덕으로 깨달어서 수양한 결과인가하면 그러치도 아니하다. 내가 世變을 격게 된 뒤로 不知不識間에 성질이 변화되어 紛競心이 줄어지고 自信力이 적어저서 이 약점이 생기기 시작한 것이다. 지금은 이 약점을 교정하랴고 다소 의식적 노력을 더하건만 20여년의 구든 버릇이 좀처럼 고처지지 아니하야 나날이 크고 적은 損을 보고 지내는 중이다. 巴人이 자서전을 쓰라고 꽤일 때에 나는 謝絶하얏다. 巴人은 根氣잇는 사람이라 내노흔 말을 거더치우지 아니하고 나를 성가시게 한다는 이보담 자긔가 성가시신 것을 참고  꽤인 뒤에 졸르고 졸른 뒤에 욱이어서 나는 마침내 사절할 용기를 닐코 자서전명색을 짓겟다고 승낙 하얏다. 구경 말하자면 나의 가진 약점이 나에게 損을 끼친 것이다. 損은 무슨 損인가? 『툴게니에프』의 발견한 處世秘術이 나의 약점을 숨기고 그 약점으로 남을 공격하는 것인데 이만 약점이라도 자백하게 되는 것부터 나에게 損이 아니고 무엇일가? 이것은 실업슨 말이다. 이왕 승낙한 바에 자서전을 엇더케 지을가? 고심초사 하느라고 정력과 시간을 함께 낭비하얏다. 룻소의 「懺悔錄」을 본바더 과거를 적나나하게 고백할가? 최후심판에 登據書類로 제공할 것은 꿈에도 생각지 안는 일이지만 풋소의 적나나도 철저하지 못하다거니 룻소만한 결심도 업는 위인이 섯부리 붓질하다가는 남의 우슴거리만  될 뿐이고 늬이체의 「에츠 호모」를 흉내내어 철인적 氣熖을 토할가? 알푸스 高峯에서 高峰으로 뛰엄질 할 주제가 아니니 황씨 뱀씨의 實例 하나를 더할 뿐이다. 나는 크로포트킨의 「혁명가의 지난 생각」을 보왓고 트로츠키의 「脫走記」를 보왓고 片山의 「자서전」을 보왓고 또 堺大杉 등 인물의 「자서전」이 잇다는 말을 들엇다. 듯고 본 것을 생각할수록 자서전과 밋 종류의 문자는 평범한 인물의 지을 것이 아니거니 하는 생각이 압흘서서 자서전을 지을 용기가 업서젓다. 사절할 용기가 업서 짓기로 하고 지을 용기가 업서 짓지 못하고 헛도이 시일만 延拖 하노라니 곤경이다. 할 수 업시 woud-be라고 형용사부터 부처노코 자서전을 짓기 시작한다. 오늘날 나의 몸이 40여년간 지나오는 길에 數多한 解皮를 머물럿스니 그 解皮들 중에 크게 보이는 것만을 주서 보랴고 한다. 解皮에 김을 너흐면  稚氣잇는 자랑이 나올 것이고 解皮에 채질을 더하면 엄폐한 수치가 들어 날 것이다만 초센남작의 바람을 쫏고자 하지 아니하니 헛김을 너허서 남의 우슴을 살리 업고 또리안, 그레이의 화상을 칼질하랴 아니하니 채질을 혹독히 하야 나의 고통을 더할 리 업슬 터이다. 그리한 즉 명색 자서전은 解皮를 주섯다 노핫다 하며 졸렬한 그림가티 그리는데 지나지 못할 것이니 졸렬한 중에 더욱 더 졸렬하게 될 것은 巴人에게 손목을 잡히고 그리는 까닭이다.

유소년시대
내가 세상에 난 뒤 세 살 되든 해에 어머니가 돌아가고 증조부가 돌아가고 여섯 살 되든 해에 할머니가 돌아갓다. 네 살에 새어머니가 생기엇스나＜10＞ 나는 증조모와 대고모 손에서 자라낫다. 조모를 아래ㅅ방 할머니라고 불르고 증조모를 안방할머니라고 불럿는데 훨신자란 뒤에 증조모가 안방을 내노코 대방으로 옴기게 되어서 안방할머니란 칭호를 대방할머니라고 고치엇다. 그러나 그 때는 조모가 돌아간 뒤라 거처하는 방으로 할머니를 구별할 필요가 업섯든 까닭에 그저 할머니라고 불르기를 만히 하얏다. 대고모는 꼬까어머니라고 불럿스니 이 칭호는 내가 세 살인가 네 살 때에 대고모가 그 며누리 신부례ㅅ날 새 옷 닙은 것을 보고 꼬까옷 닙엇다고 지어낸 것이라고 한다. 그 뒤로 꼬까어머니 외에 대고모부를 꼬까아버지라고 불를 뿐 아니라 그 아들내외까지 꼬까아저씨 꼬까아주머니라고 불럿섯다. 꼬까어머니에게 가서 놀고 혹 자기도 하기는 륙칠세된 뒤에 일이지만 그 전에는 안방할머니 압흘 떠나지 아니하얏다. 나는 몰랏지만 나종에 말을 들으니 그 때 증조모가 어미업는 증손자를 길르느라고 애쓴 것은 이로다 말할 수가 업섯다. 젓 어더 먹이는 것만도 여간 일이 아니엇는데 어린 내가 몸이 약하고 병이 자저서 밤잠을 편히 잔날이 드물엇다. 증조모가 만년까지도 잠이 들 때는 항상 보기에도 거북하게 고개를 뒤로 저치는데 이것이 어린 나를 압헤 누이고 잘 때 입김이 혹시 얼골에 끼칠가하야 고개를 뒤로 저치기 시작한 것이 인해 버릇이 된 것이엇다. 우의 아버지로부터 아래 우리 여러 형제가 모다 할머니 손때로 자라낫건마는 내가 편벽되게 망극한 은혜를 닙은 것은 나의 처지가 그러케한 것이다
