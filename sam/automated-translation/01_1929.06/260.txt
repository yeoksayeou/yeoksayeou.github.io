Announcement of the Newly Selected "Peninsula's Eight Scenic Views" Recommended by All Korean Writers, Its Purpose, and Our Company's Plan

First View: Kŭmgangsan (Kangwŏn Province)
Second View: Taedong River (P'yŏngyang)
Third View: Puyŏ (South Ch'ungch'ŏng Province)
Fourth View: Kyŏngju (North Kyŏngsang Province)
Fifth View: Myŏngsasimni (Wŏnsan)
Sixth View: Haeundae (Tongnae)
Seventh View: Paektusan (North Hamgyŏng Province)
Eighth View: Ch'oksŏngnu Pavilion (Chinju)

We felt the need and duty to newly select the "Peninsula's Eight Scenic Views" from the mountains and rivers of this Korea where we live, and having consulted this important task with all the writers of Korea, we take pride in the honor of publicly announcing the results as selected by vote from 37 individuals from the literary world, as shown above.

Perhaps some may ask, "What's this sudden fuss about the 'Peninsula's Eight Scenic Views'?" However, in our thinking, because we see a lamentable trend among the youth recently, due to some erroneous machinations (作爲), to praise even the mountains and rivers of others as better, we intend to greatly rectify (匡正) this. Furthermore, our famous scenic spots (名勝) are generally places with historical backgrounds that touch the hearts of even descendants a thousand generations later. Therefore, we know we must cast a new historical eye (史眼) upon them, enabling today's populace, boiling with the spirit of reform (更張), to feel <34> something and realize something. Is that all? Since ancient times, it has been said that where there are good mountains and waters, good people are born, and from eyes that see good mountains and waters, good writing and good thoughts emerge. Thus, by introducing these mountains and rivers, which are foremost in Asia for their purple mountains and clear brows (山紫明眉), we aim to encourage everyone towards the grand undertaking of traversing the mountains and rivers (山河 踏破) and also to create an opportunity (機緣) to solidify the pride of us Koreans.

What shall be done with these "Eight Views" obtained through such valuable intention and great effort? Our company will henceforth devote all its energy to introducing these meaningful mountains and waters through writing, speech, and pictures. As a first step, we have entrusted this to eight writers from the current literary scene, under the principle of one view per person. After having them personally tread upon and see the scenery, we will publish their travelogues, written with inspired mastery (入神), in successive issues of this magazine. We believe that when these various scenic views are revealed to the world through the tips of writers' pens, this too will undoubtedly be a historically significant undertaking (壯擧), and we feel self-pride (自矜).

Appendix 1: Regarding the selection of the Eight Views, there were instances where specific spots within one region were listed separately, such as one person writing Moktanbong Peak and another writing Ŭlmil Terrace. These have been grouped together under Taedong River. Puyŏ and Kyŏngju were treated similarly. Please understand.
Appendix 2: We went to great lengths regarding writers we wished to ask for recommendations but whose addresses we did not know; however, we ultimately could not find them and had to omit some. Furthermore, some responses arrived late for the editing deadline and were also excluded. We ask for your understanding.

Kim Tongin
Regarding scenery, I, who originally lack taste in it, know that the exquisite Kŭmgangsan is good, but I cannot distinguish it from the mundane Sŏngbukdong. I cannot distinguish between the grandeur of Japan's Mt. Fuji and the grandeur of Kyŏngju's Ponghwangdae Terrace. I find scenery quite similar to the world-renowned Kŭmgangsan in all sorts of mountains and streams. When I went to Sŏgwangsa Temple, I thought the scenery was nice, but I saw it become ordinary before five days had passed. When I went to Kyŏngju, I thought it was an ordinary plain, but that impression remains deeply etched in my mind still. As someone who originally likes to travel, I have set foot (in my previous affluent days) in most places where trains go. However, I have yet to see exquisite scenery or mundane scenery. The traveler's circumstances at the time can make mundane scenery exquisite and exquisite scenery mundane. The reason why mundane Kyŏngju remains as beautiful scenery in my mind like this is because I had a beautiful companion then, and the reason why the fine scenery of Sŏgwangsa Temple left no impression on me is perhaps because I was a pitiful person wandering alone under the pine trees while all the well-matched couples were chattering happily at Sŏgwangsa then.

**Summary**

This 1929 article from *Samch'ŏlli* magazine announces the results of a poll among 37 Korean writers to select the "Peninsula's Eight Scenic Views." The top eight chosen were Kŭmgangsan, Taedong River, Puyŏ, Kyŏngju, Myŏngsasimni, Haeundae, Paektusan, and Ch'oksŏngnu Pavilion. The magazine states its purpose is twofold: to counteract a perceived trend among youth of admiring foreign landscapes over Korea's own, and to highlight the historical significance of Korea's scenic spots to foster national consciousness and pride. The magazine plans to commission eight writers to visit one scenic spot each and publish their travelogues. The article includes the vote counts, the full list of nominated locations, and individual recommendations/comments from several prominent writers of the era.

From a historical perspective, the article is significant for its explicit aim to promote national identity and pride through appreciating Korea's natural beauty and history during the Japanese colonial period. The concern about youth admiring foreign (implicitly Japanese) things and the desire to "rectify" this sentiment reveals underlying cultural resistance and nation-building efforts within the constraints of colonial rule. The selection process involving prominent literary figures, and the plan to publish travelogues, highlights the role of intellectuals and cultural media in shaping national consciousness. The inclusion of comments from writers like Kim Tongin, Mun Ilp'yŏng, and Sim Hun provides insight into individual perspectives on landscape, history, personal experience, and nationalism at the time. Im Hwa's choices (Pusan Pier, Kyŏngsŏng Station, Sinŭiju Customs, Chongno Crossroads) stand out for focusing on modern, urban, or border infrastructure rather than traditional natural/historical sites, possibly reflecting a different, perhaps critical or modernist, perspective.

혹 엇던 분은 새삼스럼게 「半島八景」이 무엇이냐고 말슴하실 넌지 모르나 우리 생각에는 근래에 그릇된 엇던 作爲의 소치로 젊은이들 사이에 산천까지 남의 것을 더 낫게 찬탄하는 가통할 경향이 보임으로 그를 크게 匡正하여 노으려 함과 또 한가지는 우리의 名勝이란 대개 역사적 배경이 잇는 곳이 되어 千代後人의 가슴에조차 逼하는 바 만흔 터인즉 모름직이 새로운 史眼을 여기에 던저 更張의 意氣에 끌는 오늘 민중으로 하여곰 무엇을＜34＞ 늣기고 무엇을 깨닷게 하도록 하여야 할 것인 줄 아노라. 엇지 그 뿐이랴. 自來로 조흔 山水가 잇는 곳엔 조흔 인물이 나고 조흔 山水를 보는 눈에는 조흔 글과 조흔 생각이 우러러 나온다 하는 터인죽 山紫明眉 하기로 亞細亞에 읏듭인 이 산천을 소개함으로써 諸氏에게 山河 踏破의 壯擧를 促하며 또 우리 朝鮮人의 자부심을 굿게 하는 機緣을 짓고저 하는 바이다.
