동무를 부름
						丁七星
나의 사랑하는 동무들은 모다 어듸가 잇느냐 살엇느냐 죽엇느냐 죽엇드래도 지나가는 동남풍에 조차 소식이 잇스련만은

지금은 5월이다. 산과 들에는 죽엇든 풀들이 다시 청청하여지고 파란 하늘에도 날개를 우산발가치 버린 새들의 노래 소리가 가득찻다. 동무야 그대도 저하늘을 이고 섯다면 날과 가치 물이 오르는 저 초목과 귀에 간지러운 저 새소리를 듯고 니젓든 녯날의 터전과 동무생각에 가슴을 조이고 잇스련만은 내 목소리가 통하지 안는 것을 보면 동무야 그대는 퍽으나 머나먼 곳에 가 잇는 듯하다. 水路千里에 陸路千里라면 생렴도 내기 어려운 일이지만은 만약 한 오백리길에 그대가 잇다면 얼는 다라가 맛나고 오지 안으랴. 정말 올 봄에는 동무가 그립다. 멀니 나간 동무생각에 가슴이 쓰리다 (6頁削-원문)

◇

조선의 오월의 하늘은 정말 맑기도 하다. 저기 힌구름 한 쪽이 西으로 西으로 체조하면서 간다. 동무여 어대에서 하늘 우에 체조하며 西으로 西으로 가는 구름덩어리가 보이거든 올타꾸나 조선의 땅우에 어린 동무가 저 구름 밋헤서 멀니 온 나를 생각하고 잇거니 하고 긔억이나 하여주게 그런데 여보게들 엇더케 조선으로 도라들 못오겟는가. 일을 위하여 어서 도라왓스면 얼마나 반가울가? 일긔 조흔 첫녀름을 당함애 밧게 나간 동무생각이 정말 간절쿠나. ＜12＞

Calling Friends
						Chŏng Ch'ilsŏng

Where have all my beloved friends gone? Are you alive, or are you dead? Even if dead, there should be news carried even on the passing southeast wind.

It is now May. In the mountains and fields, the grass that had died has become green again, and the blue sky is filled with the singing voices of birds that have spread their wings like umbrella ribs. My friend, if you too stand beneath that sky, like me, you must be listening to the sprouting plants and the tickling bird songs, your heart tightening at the thought of the forgotten old grounds and friends. But seeing as my voice does not reach you, my friend, it seems you have gone to a very distant place. If it's a thousand *ri* by water and a thousand *ri* by land (水路千里에 陸路千里라면), it is hard even to imagine, but if you were within perhaps five hundred *ri*, would I not quickly run to meet you? Truly, this spring, I miss my friends. My heart aches thinking of friends who have gone far away. (Page 6 omitted - original text)

◇

The May sky of Chosŏn is truly clear. Look, a patch of white cloud goes westward, westward, doing gymnastics. My friend, wherever you are, if you see a cloud mass doing gymnastics high in the sky, going westward, westward, please think, "Ah, my young friend on the soil of Chosŏn, beneath that cloud, is thinking of me who has come from afar," and remember me. By the way, friends, how is it that you cannot return to Chosŏn? How glad I would be if you quickly returned for the sake of the work/cause (일)! Facing this fine weather of early summer, the thought of friends who have gone outside is truly earnest. <12>

**Summary**

The author, Chŏng Ch'ilsŏng, expresses a deep longing for absent friends during a beautiful May in Korea. He reflects on shared memories and the pain of separation, wondering where his friends are and why they are so far away, possibly overseas ("thousand *ri* by water and land"). He urges them to remember him and asks why they cannot return to Chosŏn, suggesting their return would be welcome "for the sake of the work/cause." This piece, written in 1929 under Japanese rule, might interest historians for its poignant expression of separation and longing, common themes reflecting the Korean diaspora or political exile during the colonial period. The mention of returning "for the sake of the work/cause" (일을 위하여) subtly hints at underlying nationalistic sentiments or a call to contribute to the homeland, despite the potential risks under colonial surveillance.
