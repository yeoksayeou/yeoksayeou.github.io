아츰해 頌歌
						片石村

The stars gather the countless threads of light that embraced the Earth and fly outside the universe.
Over the ancient graves of foreign prisoners, the morning sun sprinkles its laughter.
Even on the tip of the sharp electric memorial tower, the lighthouse for birds of the night in the middle of the field,
and below it, upon the face of the fallen bandit soaked in dew, the morning sun casts its bright laughter.

The "Canary" (카나리아) in the cage reportedly went mad last night. The soul's (령양) heart must be sad.
The bird's two eyes, like Sappho's (싸포-) who does not sing, tread on the shadow of the morning sun crushed by the bars, gazing at the flying clouds, its old friend.
In the cruel cage that tries to shrink and confine its dream, as vast as the universe—

O morning sun—
Upon the ancient ruins of Cain (카인), torn and trampled by discrimination, conquest, plunder (약취), and falsehood,
You alone do not break the promised time,
Nor abandon the old habit of spreading your wings of light each morning and flying over the wilderness.

가을의 果樹園에서 (In the Autumn Orchard)

The stars, like young acrobats, fall endlessly, tail to head, into the net of darkness.
The naked form of the "Poplar" (『포푸라』), stripped of its blue coat, is felt upon the embankment (방천).
In the orchard, the Apple trees (林檎나무) shudder violently. Leaves, leaves, leaves searching for graves.
West-Southwest (西南西)—
The wind seems to be in this direction.
It tears through the black tresses of the Jin-dung trees (진둥나무) and runs through the fleeing stars with the legs of Achilles (『아키러쓰』).
From the sea, the sound of a frenzied whistle seeking rescue rolls over all the uneven surfaces (凸凹面) of the Earth.
S. O. S—
Black sea—will you not abandon your ambition to dissolve
a bold speck of a steamship within your demonic heart?

When morning comes, O farmers, let us go out to the fruit fields to gather the countless fallen stars. Prepare the carts. Let us load these miraculous fruits and go to the market of ancient Baghdad (『바그닷드』) in the East (동방).<97>

**Summary**

The work consists of two poems by P'yŏnsŏkch'on. The first, "Ode to the Morning Sun," contrasts the darkness and despair of the world (imprisonment, madness, ruins of 'Cain') with the reliable, impartial arrival of the morning sun, a symbol of persistent hope and natural order transcending human failings like discrimination, conquest, and plunder. The second poem, "In the Autumn Orchard," depicts the decay and transition of autumn, using imagery of falling stars, stripped trees, and fleeing winds, culminating in a call for farmers to gather the fallen 'stars' (fruits) like treasures for the market, suggesting renewal and harvest even amidst decline. From a historical perspective, the themes of oppression ("foreign prisoners," "cage," "discrimination, conquest, plunder," "ruins of Cain") and the resilience of hope symbolized by the sun in the first poem could resonate with the Korean experience under Japanese colonial rule. The second poem's focus on cyclical nature and finding value (harvest) amidst decay might offer a subtle commentary on endurance and finding sustenance even in difficult times.
