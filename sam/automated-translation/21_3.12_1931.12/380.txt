**Body Text Translation:**
Past and Present
						Kangnam

It has already been forty years since Japan and Qing China made Korea their battlefield and fought a war. Although the war started from something very small, its cause and result were very large. It was not unreasonable for Japan, which was beginning to grow vigorously at the time, to feel its own country was too small and seek to expand beyond the sea. Accordingly, it was also not unreasonable for Qing China, which had prided itself as the "great power" for thousands of years, to try and restrain that. Where these two not unreasonable, yet justified (有理), positions clashed, the terrifying event called war inevitably arose.

On the night before the full moon of the eighth lunar month, the moon was bright and clear. Even while saying, "Trouble is coming, trouble is coming," in the villages, people were still pounding rice cakes, making *nochi* (a type of food), and frying *chijim* (pancakes). That year, there was an unprecedentedly good harvest, so much so that some households prepared food by the *sŏm* (a large unit of measure), whereas before they might have prepared only one or two *mal* (a smaller unit). It must have been done under the notion that trouble is trouble, and a holiday is a holiday.

However, on the day of the full moon of the eighth month, as the round, round moon smiled like a newborn baby, beaming in the eastern sky, the decisive battle determining the fate of Japanese soldiers (日兵) and Qing soldiers (淸兵) began in P'yŏngyang. The sharp, crackling sounds of small arms fire, the occasional booming of cannons (쿵-쿵-), the sight of flames streaking through the air... these shattered the peace of the joyous holiday and dimmed the brightness of the full moon.

At that time, Pongnam's grandfather was watching this terrifying scene from thirty *li* away. He felt as if the whole world was turning upside down. It was the first time in his lifetime he had witnessed war, but even hearing the stories of old elders, though there were tales of fighting with bows and stones, fighting like that with rifles and cannons was something he had never heard of or seen before (금시초문이오 금시초견). Whether X won or the barbarians 'Yi' (되 「夷」) lost was not the issue for him. Only the immediate astonishment and fear dominated his entire mind.

At dawn the next day, as soon as it became light, hundreds of defeated Qing soldiers (敗軍), fleeing empty-handed, tried to cross the lower Taedonggang River (大洞江). Some drowned, but some luckily survived. These fortunate Qing soldiers, their hair like tangled hemp (삼딴가튼) hanging down, pointed to their stomachs with their hands, utterly exhausted (긔진맥진하야), and collapsed on the ground. The food intended for visiting ancestral graves (성묘) ended up becoming sustenance that saved the lives of the Qing soldiers they had never dreamed of seeing. Thus, the war's outcome resulted in Japan's victory.

Word spread far and wide that earnings (버리) in P'yŏngyang were good. Because people heard that carrying just one load of military provisions weighing three *mal*<101> earned a horse-hoof silver ingot (마뎨은 馬蹄銀), and even carrying moving luggage (이사짐) for a rich family earned a generous silver coin each time, everyone flocked to P'yŏngyang, wanting to become carriers of military supplies or moving luggage. Pongnam's grandfather also dreamt of making a fortune overnight as a carrier and came to P'yŏngyang. At that time, Pongnam's grandfather was living hand-to-mouth (막간사리) in the countryside, working for a wealthy family. Having passed forty and entering the valley of old age, he thought and thought, concluding he couldn't see a comfortable life in his time just living hand-to-mouth. In this turmoil where the world seemed to be turning upside down, he decided to take a chance, following the "36th stratagem" (삼십륙계, i.e., fleeing or making a bold move): if it worked out, great; if not, so be it. He packed his things and came to P'yŏngyang. At that time, Pongnam's grandmother followed her husband, holding the hand of Pongnam's father, who was ten years old. Thus, they came to P'yŏngyang, rented a small single room in a rented house (세ㅅ집), and established a respectable A-frame carrier's (지게ㅅ군) family.

Time flowed on. Though the colorful spring passed over Ŭlmildae many times, their family still struggled with poverty. The bustling and lively, prosperous (북적북적하고 풍청풍청하든) times caused by the war soon passed, and the Korean economy began to dry up again. The impact reached even their family, whose existence was unknown to the world. When living hand-to-mouth in the countryside, they hadn't worried about rice and firewood, but now, morning and evening meals (끼니) were the problem. Pongnam's grandfather, who had married late due to his hand-to-mouth existence and had children late, had hair that was turning white (희뜩희뜩하엿슴니다). The strength to carry a *sŏm* of red beans (팟 한 섬) became a thing of the past.

"My back hurts."
"I'm old now."
Such sighs frequently escaped his lips.

One autumn, he fell ill with a severe, rapidly progressing illness (신병) and died in someone else's rented house, leaving behind a single A-frame carrier (지게) as an inheritance to his son, who was twenty at the time.

At that time, war broke out between Japan and Russia (로서아). The power of Japan, then vigorously expanding (뻐치는), knew no bounds and advanced, crushing whatever it encountered (처물니고). Ten years earlier, when fighting Qing China, the stage had been Korean territory; ten years later, that stage had suddenly shifted to Manchuria (만주).

此處は禦國の何百里
離れて遠き滿洲の
(Here, hundreds of *ri* from the homeland / Far away in Manchuria)

Singing such songs ubiquitously, their spirit as they trampled over the corpses of 203 Meter Hill (이백삼고지) was indeed heroic. And so, Japan instantly became a first-rate world power. And it became the master of East Asian peace.

Pongnam's father took the inherited A-frame carrier and went out onto the streets like his father. However, unlike his father's thinking, he did not set out dreaming of making a fortune overnight. He did so out of necessity, for his day-to-day survival. In the meantime, time flowed on. How many times the cherry blossoms (사구라) of Ŭlmildae bloomed and fell, one cannot know, but Pongnam's father married and had a bunch (땀) of four sons, with Pongnam being the first. Pongnam's father, lamenting that he, like his own father, had not been able to study, sent Pongnam to primary school despite his difficult life circumstances.

The intelligent (총녕한) Pongnam studied with excellent grades throughout the six years (륙년) and graduated. Now, Pongnam speaks Japanese quite well. If dressed in a *haori* (하오리), anyone would mistake him for a Japanese person; his Japanese became that fluent. However, even his fluent Japanese was of no use in freeing his father from the A-frame carrier. Had it been his grandfather's time—around the Kabo year (1894)—it might have provided a living, but now, when even eighty-year-old elders know the word *mame* (마메, Japanese for bean), how could Japanese language proficiency provide a living? Thus, letting his precious (앗가운) Japanese go to waste (썩이고), he entered a rubber factory as a worker (직공), receiving twenty won a month.<102>

Time still flowed on. In spring, automobiles kick up dust (몬지) as they speed freely along the wide road above Ch'ŏngnyubyŏk cliff (청류벽). And over the sand flats of Nŭngnado Island (능라도), airplanes instead of skylarks fly, performing tricks (재조). Pongnam is now quite grown up and diligently (부즈런히) commutes to the factory every day. He works diligently. He works diligently for twelve hours a day without a moment's rest for 66 *chŏn*, 6 *ri*, 6 *mo*, 666... (a very small amount of money). Seeing the propaganda promoting domestic products (물산장려) at the beginning of each lunar year (정초), he thoroughly bought and used only Korean products. He has never touched even a single cigarette (꼬치?) or a single drop of the tobacco and alcohol others enjoy for their taste. During the spring and autumn general cleanings, a white paper was always posted on his family's doorway (문깐) (even though it was a rented house), likely indicating compliance with sanitation rules. When told to keep to the left (좌측 통행), he always stuck closely to the left side whenever walking on the street. However, his family still struggled with poverty. No, they struggled with poverty even more severely than in his grandfather's time or his father's time. Although the nation's money had increased hundreds, even thousands of times compared to the Kabo year, their poverty only deepened with each passing generation (대(代)를 지남을 따라).

Inside the Mukden-bound (봉천행) special express train (특급렬차) that departed from Pusan, members of the Osaka Business Club's (대판 실업구락부) South Manchuria (남만주) inspection group (시찰단) were out in the observation car (전망차), smoking thick cigars (여송연). They talked, citing numbers about the remarkable development of Japanese trade after the abolition (철페) of the export tax (수출세) between the mainland (내지) and Korea. Various newspaper companies in Pusan, Taegu, and Seoul considered their passage through Korea a great honor and printed their photos and anecdotes (일활?) in large type (튼 활자). However, on that train, there was a rare guest (진객) riding who was no less distinguished than they were. He was James Kim (쩨임스.김), a respected elder returning home in glory (금의환향) after thirty years, having majored in sociology in America (미국) and received a Ph.D. (철학박사). Inside the doctor's briefcase was his doctoral dissertation (학위론문) in English, titled "The Social and Economic Development of the Korean People Over the Past Fifty Years." The doctor had written the thesis based on statistical tables (통계표) regarding Korea published by the Japanese Ministry of Foreign Affairs (일본외무생 - likely 外務省), found in an American library. Upon receiving the good news (희보) that his thesis (론문) had passed, the doctor was overjoyed and immediately packed his bags and boarded a ship in San Francisco (상항). However, upon landing in Pusan, the doctor's heart filled with despair. Along the railway line, the shacks (오막사리) from thirty years ago still stood unchanged. The doctor lamented inwardly (쟁태식을 하엿슴니다). Why haven't our Korean people awakened (깨지 못햇는고)? Why were our Korean people lazy (게을넛는고)? After thus delivering a monologue (독백) of resentment (울분) for a while in the observation car, the doctor happened to strike up a conversation with one of the inspection group members on his way back to his own car (차ㅅ간).

"What are the doctor's impressions (감상)?" asked one of the inspection group, having seen the doctor's business card (명함).
"Despair."
The doctor's reply was quite tragic. A little later, the doctor continued in a somewhat excited tone:
"Why were our Korean people so ignorant (무지하엿는지오)? Didn't the same opportunity come to Japan and Korea around 1860?"
“Yes, Same opportunity.”
"But Japan met Perry (페리) and opened its ports, while Korea burned them to death. Korea was ruined by one man, the Taewŏn'gun (대원군)."
“Yes, by only one man.”
"And why are the Korean people still so ignorant and lazy? If they learn and become diligent even now, there should still be opportunities."
“Yes, you are right.”
As the train neared Kyŏngsŏng Station, the doctor prepared to get off. The inspection group watched the doctor's retreating figure and scoffed (코우슴) to the member standing beside them.

"One line of Japanese text" (「일본문 1줄」) (End)<103>

**Summary**

This article contrasts the past and present through the lens of three generations of a poor Korean family in P'yŏngyang, starting from the Sino-Japanese War (1894). Despite major historical events like the Russo-Japanese War and supposed economic progress indicated by increased national wealth and modernization (trains, planes), the family's poverty deepens with each generation. Pongnam, the third generation, graduates primary school, speaks fluent Japanese, and follows modern rules, yet finds himself working long hours in a factory for meager pay, worse off than his grandfather who was an illiterate carrier. The narrative juxtaposes this reality with the optimistic views of Japanese businessmen celebrating trade growth and a US-educated Korean PhD who, based on official statistics, wrote about Korea's development but is shocked by the visible poverty upon his return, blaming Korean ignorance and laziness, particularly the historical figure Taewŏn'gun.

For historians, the article offers a poignant social commentary on the lived experience of poverty under Japanese colonial rule, contrasting sharply with official narratives of modernization and progress. It highlights the perceived lack of economic opportunity for Koreans despite acquiring modern skills like Japanese language proficiency, the persistence of hardship across generations, and the internal Korean discourse (represented by the PhD) that sometimes mirrored colonial criticisms regarding Korean stagnation, providing insight into the complex social and psychological landscape of the era. The final interaction with the Japanese businessmen underscores the disconnect between the rulers/elites and the ruled/impoverished.

古今
						江南
일본과 청국이 조선을 싸흠터로 하고 전쟁을 한 지가 어언간 사십년이나 되여옴니다. 전쟁의 시초는 매우 적은데서부터 시작되엿지만은 그 원인과 결과는 매우 큰 것이엇슴니다. 당시에 한창 자라나기 시작하든 일본이 자긔 나라 안을 좁다고 바다 밧그로 떨치려 하는 것도 무리는 안이엇슴니다. 그에 따라 수천년을 「대국」으로 자랑하고 잇든 청국이 그것을 제어하려고 하는 것도 무리는 안이엇슴니다. 이 두 무리안인 유리(有理)가 맛서는 곳에 필경 전쟁이라는 무서운 사건이 생기게 된 것이엇슴니다.
음력으로 팔월 보름 전날 밤, 달은 밝고 명랑하엿슴니다. 난(亂)이 난다, 난이 난다는 하면서도 촌에서는 여전히 떡거을 치고 「노치」를 하고 「지짐」을 지젓슴니다. 그 해 따라 전에 업시 풍년이 들어 전에는 한 두 말에 치나 하든 음식을 그 해에는 섬으로한 집까지 잇섯슴니다. 난은 난이고 명절은 명절이라는 관념 아래서 그리하엿든 것일 것임니다.
그러나 팔월 보름날 둥굴고 둥근 달이 갓난이 웃는 모양으로 벙글벙글 웃으며 동텬에 소슬 때, 평양에서는 일병(日兵)과 청병(淸兵)의 최후를 결하는 격전이 시작되엿슴니다. 호즉 근톡톡하는 적은 총소리, 잇다금 가다가 쿵-쿵- 하는 대포ㅅ소리, 불꼿과 불꼿이 죽- 죽 벗는 모양... 그것들은 즐거운 명절의 층을 깨트리고 보룸달의 밝음을 무광케 하엿슴니다.
때에 복남의 할아바지는 삼십리 밧게서 이 무서운 광경을 바라보고 잇섯슴니다. 세상이 모다 뒤집히는 듯한 감이 생겻슴니다. 당대에 난리 구경을 하는 것이 처음이엇지만은 녯 어룬들의 말을 들어도 활과 돌로 싸혼다는 말은 잇서도 저러케 총과 대포로 싸호는 것은 금시초문이오 금시초견의 일이엇슴니다. X가 이긔거나 되 「夷」가 지거나 그에게는 문뎨가 안이엇슴니다. 다만 목전의 놀나움과 두러움이 그의 온 정신을 지배하고 잇슬 뿐이엇슴니다.
