**Ah. The Last Face, While Hearing the Sad New Year's Eve Bell**
Kim Ok-kyun on the Eve of His Assassination - When I Was an English Teacher in Shanghai
Yun Ch'i-ho

The last time I saw Kim Ok-kyun (金玉均), the pioneer of a generation, before his death was the very day before he met his end at the hands of Hong Chong-u (洪鍾宇), bearing a thousand years of regret.
The place was Shanghai, the building was the Tonghwa Yanghaeng (東和洋行, a hotel-style inn) where he met his end, and the room was the very bedroom where he had unpacked his luggage.
At that time, I was a young exile, dragging my lonely shadow in Shanghai, teaching at the Chung-Hsi Hsüeh-yüan (中西學院, a mission school run by Americans in the American Concession, the same school where I had studied English before going to America) located within the American Concession there.
One day, I received a letter saying that Mr. Kim was coming over to Shanghai. So, I counted the days, and on the day he arrived, I rushed over to the Tonghwa Yanghaeng in the evening after finishing my school hours.
Just as I was about to enter the room, a tall, lanky, imposing man wearing a large *kat* (traditional hat) and a purple *turumagi* (overcoat) was leaving. <12> Thinking it strange, I later asked Kim Ok-kyun about him, and he said that this was the very Hong Chong-u who would aim a six-shooter at him. Whether Mr. Kim knew it then or not, when I asked why he was traveling with Hong Chong-u, he showed no hint of temptation or unease on his face and said,
"There's no particular connection, but when I said I was going to Shanghai, he just said, 'Then I'll go see Shanghai too,' and he's been accompanying me since Japan."
Listening to Mr. Kim speak at that meeting, he mentioned that his current trip was not only to meet Li Hung-chang (李鴻章) in Tientsin (天津) but also to meet other important figures in China with the intention of planning something political. He carried several letters of introduction, including one from Li Hung-chang's son, who was at the Japanese Legation in China.
On that day, seeing Mr. Kim's appearance, terribly weary and weathered from many years – truly many years spent amidst the snowstorms of Hokkaido (北海道) or the misty waves of the Ogasawara Islands (小笠原島) – I felt my heart ache with a kind of pitiful emotion.
The next day, while I was teaching as usual at the Chung-Hsi Hsüeh-yüan, someone rushed in and delivered a letter from Mr. Kim Ok-kyun. The content was a request for me to come in the afternoon as there was something he absolutely needed to see me about. I told the messenger to tell him I would come as soon as my classes ended.
In truth, Shanghai at that time was different from now; there were few fellow Koreans Mr. Kim could seek out, nor was there anyone he could visit to open his heart and discuss plans with.
Just as the last class hour was ending, another person arrived with urgent news.
"Mr. Kim Ok-kyun has just been assassinated!" I threw down my teaching pointer and ran through the chaotic streets to the Tonghwa Yanghaeng.
Ah! It was already too late. Mr. Kim Ok-kyun had been hit by a bullet and was already dead, lying on the bed ("뻬트" - bed).
Beside him, a young Japanese man was weeping, clutching the body. He was a youth who greatly admired Mr. Kim and had always followed him around, even in Japan.
I asked him what had happened. He said, "While the teacher and I were in this room, Hong Chong-u came in, sat down without saying much, and asked for a cup of tea. While I went downstairs to get the tea, Hong shot the teacher with a pistol and then immediately fled to the Chinese barracks (中國 兵營)."
I stood there stunned. How could any composure arise amidst such a sudden <13> change, such a shocking incident? Just last night, in this house, this room, this very spot, I had spoken with him – bold, courageous, and skilled in diplomacy. Only one night had passed, and already he and I were separated by life and death.
To call this the transience of life feels too slow; to simply call it the death of a regrettable person brings tears of blood.
Weeping, I returned to the school, retrieved the letter that had arrived from him earlier from the wastebasket, and as I read that final message, his last writing, I felt my limbs gradually stiffen like wood and stone.

It has been over 30 years since he passed! By now, even the flesh and bones that lay in his grave must have vanished without a trace. Even so, what remains deeply regrettable now is that I never had the chance to ask in detail why he came to Shanghai or what he intended to do there before sending him to the netherworld. It is infuriating.
At that time, his relations with China were extremely poor. So why did he come to Shanghai? It was truly a journey of eternal regret, and one can only see it as an enigmatic trip. (Reporter responsible for text) -(End)-

**Chang Tŏk-chun at Namdaemun Station**
Yu Kwang-yŏl

To write about the last face of my dear friend Chang Tŏk-chun (張德俊)! That is impossible. It has already been 12 years since Chang Tŏk-chun went alone to the Kando Incident (間島事變) area, and we lost track of whether he was dead or alive... How quickly time passes! Already 12 years... have gone by... However, whether Chang Tŏk-chun is alive or dead will remain unknown, not just for 12 years, but for billions of years.
I first met Chang Tŏk-chun in the early spring of 1920, about 20 days before the founding of the Tonga Ilbo (東亞日報). The place I first met him was the house in Hwa-dong (花洞) that currently bears the sign of the Chungoe Ilbo (中外日報社).
He was a young man of 27 or 28, dressed in a simple ink-wash-colored *turumagi*. I was a youth of 21 or 22, just entering the literary world. His face was friendly, a face that welcomed people. But somewhere, a hint of sadness enveloped his entire face. His eyes, always lost in sentimentality, constantly held tears.

Pitifully, in his growing youth, he suffered from tuberculosis. Occasionally, along with a painful cough, bloody phlegm would emerge. When I asked, "You must take care of yourself; how can you go on like this?" his eyes would fill with tears, yet a sad smile would appear on his lips as he said,
"What's there to worry about? It's fine. As long as I live, being enveloped in emotion is enough. Does human life have eternity? I'll just work hard for the Korean people while I live, and then die."

Not many days later, his illness worsened considerably, and sometimes he coughed up so much blood that he inevitably had to be admitted to Severance Hospital outside Namdaemun (南大門). I came back from working outside, busily writing an article, when the phone in front of the then-chief editor, Yi Sang-hyŏp (李相協), rang shrilly. Mr. Yi answered,
"Mr. Yu Kwang-yŏl, I think it's Chang Tŏk-chun."
I took the phone.
"Yes, this is Yu Kwang-yŏl. How is your condition?"
"Oh, it's alright. Come visit later. Yes - bring some fruit and also... ..."
"And also... ... what is it?"
"And also... ... I'll introduce you to a pretty nurse... ..."
And he hung up. Receiving the call, my face turned red.<14>
When I went to the hospital in the evening, he was in a separate ward to the west, standing blankly, gazing at the setting sun. The spring sun, having traveled a long path, hung in the western sky, quietly shining through the hospital room's glass window. Seeing me, he brightened up, asked me to sit down, took my hand, and tears streamed from his eyes. I couldn't find words of comfort. Silence continued for a long while. An indescribable sorrow seemed to permeate every corner of the room. He spoke first,
"Well, just now, looking through this window, I saw a seriously ill patient who looked like a beggar come and repeatedly ask to be admitted, but the hospital kept saying there were no rooms and wouldn't take him. If that person had money, they wouldn't have done that... Because he's a penniless beggar, they can't even save a life that's about to die, and he doesn't even have a place to lie down as he closes his eyes for the last time. So he lay down on that lawn over there, and then someone carried him off somewhere... Meanwhile, from the nurses' station over there, the nurses are singing hymns like 'Far away, brighter than the sunlight, the place where heaven is believed in can be seen.' On one side, they seek a heaven brighter than sunlight, while on the other, there's no place to lie down when dying. Even if the world talks about 'justice and humanity,' it's useless for the weak. Even if the League of Nations exists and peace conferences are held, it's useless for the weak..."
He sobbed quietly. By his bedside lay a blood-stained towel. A neatly dressed nurse came in, quietly peeled some fruit, placed it down, and left.
Autumn arrived. The newspaper was suspended (停刊). Angered, he decided to go to his hometown, and I accompanied him.
Watching the fallen leaves on the mountains and fields and the desolate autumn scenery, we got off at Sariwŏn (沙里院) and took a car to his home in Chaeryŏng (載寧). It was a vast plain with a desolate village. Chang's house was a humble thatched cottage of 4 or 5 *kan* (traditional unit of space). Then as now, having no spare set of clothes, I sat wearing Western clothes, so he put his Korean clothes on me and wore his summer shirt and trousers himself. Chang's home was in the 'Namuri Plain' (나무리벌판) of the Chaeryŏng Plain (載寧平野). The vast plain, with all the crops harvested, was endlessly desolate. Far away, beyond the dimly visible mountains, the sun set, painting the sky with the colors of twilight. The autumn wind blew. A goat tied in the yard bleated mournfully, as if lamenting the fading day. He and I stood there wordlessly.
Me: "What a desolate plain..."
He: "Born in the wilderness, returned to the wilderness..."
He seemed lost in some deep emotion.
After returning to Seoul (귀경한 후), hearing about the Kando Incident, the passionate man suddenly packed his belongings and prepared to leave. His mother, sending her son off to a dangerous place, perhaps had a premonition.
"My son, go to the church and receive the pastor's blessing (안수기도) before you go. My heart just won't be at ease."
"Mother, don't worry. I'm better than some pastor. Even without prayer, God will protect my body as I work for the Korean people..."
He left with an excited expression. When he departed from Namdaemun Station (南大門驛), autumn was already deep. After he left, all news ceased. His fate unknown, he never returned. His brother, Tŏk-chu (德冑), went looking for him but returned empty-handed (空還). Where in the vast Manchurian plains could one find his trace? If he died somewhere, who would find him, who has no grave? Perhaps only the spring skylark or the pale autumn moonlight could find him?
In the vast Namuri Plain where he was born and raised, the magnificent sunset surely still sets unchangingly even now. Where did the ill-fated youth, Chang Tŏk-chun, who was embraced by its bosom, go?

**Son Pyŏng-hŭi at Sangch'unwŏn**
Yi Ton-hwa

If we consider the state of being alive (生) as egalitarian, then the state of death (死) can be said to be discriminatory.<15> In the state of being alive, there is no difference between the great and the common person, and no distinction between the rich and the poor. We all just tumble into this world with a first cry (落地一聲哭), you and I alike. However, in the state of death, I believe there are various distinctions. First, since death carries history behind it, just as your history and mine differ in countless ways, there will be relationships tied to the actions of the great and the common. Also, in death itself, there are distinctions like unnatural death (變死), death by execution (刑死), death by illness (病死), natural death (順死), etc. If we were to count all types, including dying in war, suicide due to love, sudden death from cholera (虎列刺), dying after suffering from leprosy (문등병), the number seems endless. In any case, the thing called death, whether seen as settling the accounts of a past life or as the terror of entering future eternal silence, has a sense of tragedy amidst the strangeness, and a sense of catharsis amidst the mystery. Since none who have died have returned to explain the taste of death, our impressions, interests, or misunderstandings about death itself will differ from person to person. However, even if we cannot guess the state of the afterlife (彼岸) with our current consciousness, we must have directly experienced all the aspects of facing death through the facts of all those who have already died. Thus, I believe everyone's feelings about the moment of death (臨終) are truer and more solemn than usual. Just as the ancients said, "When a person is about to die, their words are good" (人之將死 其言也善), not only does the dying person's heart become so, but so do all those facing the deathbed. Moreover, aren't impressions of the final moments of a great man, the death of a giant, something we don't often encounter, thus holding a special kind of sentiment? It is in this sense that Samch'ŏlli-sa (三千里社) gave me the instruction to relate, even just a little, my impressions from directly experiencing his deathbed. And attached to that instruction was the command to speak of the 'last face' of the late Teacher Son Ŭi-am (孫義菴 선생).

The date Teacher Ŭi-am passed away, as the world knows, was May 19th, Year Imsul (壬戍年, 1922), three years after the Kiminyŏn (己未年, 1919), at midnight. His illness was total paralysis (全身不遂症), and the time from the onset of illness to his death was about one year plus some months.
The Teacher held a distinct view regarding the concept of death in his daily life. He had always said that death should not be faced with fear but with transcendence, not with an attitude of submission but with a spirit of overcoming. He always told his disciples (門弟), "Even in your imagination, try dying by being shot by X. Try falling from a high cliff (벨앙). Try drowning in the great sea. Try entering death while still alive."
This, of course, was heard as words emphasizing the mental and physical training of his disciples, but it also allows us to understand the Teacher's attitude towards death (臨死). Furthermore, in one of his sermons (法說), the 'View of the Afterlife' (死後觀), he advocated the theory of the wave transmission (波傳說) of life-giving, active energy (에네루기). That is, he thought that the energy of a person's vital activity is eternally imperishable, transmitted through the lives of future people.
This also allows us to glimpse a part of the view of the afterlife held by that elder.
In any case, the Teacher did not face death, the end of life, with fear and submission, but with transcendence and overcoming. (Abbreviated - original text) For the first few months, both the Teacher himself and those around him held onto a thread of hope and administered medical treatment. However, after several more months passed, seeing that there was absolutely no possibility of recovery, those around him spent their days in complete despair. But the patient, the Teacher himself, showed no sign of disappointment whatsoever. He repeated hundreds, thousands of times, "Don't worry, I just won't die." Looking at his family and disciples, he repeated, "I will not die. I will not die," and if those around him showed signs of disbelief, even in his illness, he would joke and laugh, saying, "I'm going to live to be 99, how about that?" Many times his face was full of cheerfulness (和氣滿面).<16> Because he was an elder with such a strong attitude towards death, his thoughts about work did not cease even until the day he died. At that time, the church (교회, likely referring to Ch'ŏndogyo) was just beginning to split into new and old factions. But the Teacher, lying in his sickbed, would sometimes call his senior disciples (高弟) to give direct orders, or proclaim messages like testaments to the congregation (敎中), continuing his mental efforts regarding matters large and small, just as he did in ordinary times. Truly, it was "Serve with utmost diligence and loyalty until death, and only then cease" (鞠躬盡瘁 死而後已).
On the final night, May 19th, Imsul, rain famously poured down. It seemed as if the entire world, heaven and earth, was turning into a sea of water. At that time, the Teacher's single thread of life began to fade away. But occasionally, he would open his eyes, look at the people around him, say again, "I will not die, so don't worry," and finally entered eternal silence.
The Teacher's lips would never open again, and his words vanished forever from this world, but the sound of his voice saying, "I will not die, so don't worry," continues in our ears.
There was a profound feeling that the Teacher was resting, not dead. His abundant physique (體胴), his full (豊潤) yet vibrant and cheerful (和氣潑刺) face, laid out extremely calmly, extremely solemnly, extremely majestically upon the earth, lying there composedly (從容), seemed like the quiet remainder of a cannon after firing a shell towards Mars, or hinted at the disappearance without a trace of a fragrant cannonball, filled with celestial perfume, after scattering light and scent in the heavens (天空). Then, the mind gradually enters the grand tragedy (大悲壯) of the great universe. Boundless sorrow, boundless emotion, boundless feeling enveloped this body.
Honestly speaking, in my entire life so far, I have never seen such a fine countenance (觀相) as Teacher Ŭi-am's, nor consequently have I seen such a death twice. This, of course, can be interpreted as being somewhat colored by my subjective feelings, but it is because the energy of his activities during his lifetime – connected to the Kabo Revolution (甲午革命), the Kapchin Upheaval (甲辰風雲) (Abbreviated - original text) – shone even brighter through the solemnity of his deathbed, bestowing upon us an even loftier sense of deep emotion.

**The Last Face of Minister Min Yŏng-hwan**
Yu Chin-t'ae

Now, as I attempt to recall the late Minister Min Yŏng-hwan (閔泳煥), with whom I had a particularly close relationship in the past, I feel myself filled with boundless emotion reflecting on the past and present.

The turbulent political situation (政形) of that time and the Minister's tragic end are already well known to the public, so I will avoid tedious explanations here and simply write briefly about the circumstances when I last met the Minister.
The time was November 17th, Kwangmu 9 (光武九年, 1905). When the so-called Protectorate Treaty was concluded, the already tumultuous world became even more agitated, and the capital city seethed like boiling water.
At that time, all conscientious ministers considered themselves officials of a fallen nation (亡國大夫) and firmly shut their doors, acting like people of a ruined country (亡人).
Having decided to temporarily suspend everything and go down to my country home (at that time, my home was in Ch'ŏngju (淸州)), I visited the Minister to bid farewell. This was on the morning of November 18th, the day after the Protectorate Treaty was concluded.
When I entered the Minister's residence (邸), the inner and outer gates were tightly shut, and the silence was like the dead of night. The desolate atmosphere was completely like that of a ruined household (亡家).
The Minister had just returned home not long before, having gone to Yongin (龍仁), Kyŏnggi Province (京畿), the day before the treaty signing, for the funeral of his first wife, Lady Kim (金氏). I opened the Minister's door and entered. Inside the room, the Minister sat with his head bowed. He glanced briefly at my face and then lowered his head again.<17> I too, without a word, looked at the Minister and took heavy steps to sit beside him.
The atmosphere in the room was extremely heavy; just the Minister with his pale, silent face and I sat in the dark room with the closed door.
Silence continued for a long time.
The Minister finally raised his head, looked at me briefly, and sighed, "Huuu," in a heavy, low, trembling tone. "To live... (Abbreviated - original text) I have no face to show [His Majesty?] above, and no countenance to face the people (XX) below. To die... I cannot even die... Huu-uuu..."
Each word stabbed deeply into my already heart-stricken chest. Hot tears welled up in my eyes again. After another long pause, I prepared to rise.
"I am going down to the countryside today."
The Minister raised his head.
"Go?"
"Yes."
"So, you're really going?" His voice grew louder.
"..."
In the Minister's uplifted eyes, tears glistened.

This was the final meeting between the Minister and me. How could I have known? Although it is the iron rule of life that all who are born must die, could I, now old, suppress my somber emotions when recalling the Minister's subsequent end?
When the Minister met his end, someone came urgently to Ch'ŏngju, so I immediately went up to Seoul. At that time, I was about 30 years old.

Finally, to introduce his character and a hidden anecdote: the Minister's character was one of unwavering loyalty and righteousness (忠義一貫), and his heart was extremely upright, making him deeply trustworthy.
He also had a deep affection for his juniors. He highly praised the young Yi Sŭng-man (李承晩) at the time, always commending Mr. Yi whenever he met people. After Mr. Yi went to America, the Minister sent 500 *nyang* monthly for his family's living expenses, continuing this until the month before he passed away.

After returning from his post as Minister to the United States, having felt the influence of Western culture, he established a middle-school level institution called Yŏhwa Hakkyo (與和學校, near present-day Ch'ŏngjin-dong (淸進洞)) and promoted educational endeavors. One day, carrying a cane, he walked through the Chongno streets in broad daylight to visit Han Kyu-sŏl (韓圭卨, referred to as 韓江石) in Posin-gak (보시꼿이? likely 보신각, Bosingak area) near Samgak-chŏng (三角町). This became a big issue in the Nambukch'on (南北村, noble districts of Seoul).
They said, "Walking down the main street in broad daylight, is that proper behavior? Tsk, tsk," and so on, the criticism was loud.
Because the issue became quite troublesome, he subsequently avoided walking during the day. Thinking about it now, it's just one of those laughable things.

**Recollections of the Late Madam Pak Wŏn-hŭi**
Saebyŏk (New Dawn)

On March 9, 1923, a student strike involving Kim Un-hae (金耘解), Pang Myŏng-wŏn (方明元), and others broke out at Taesŏng Middle School (大成中學) in Yongjŏng (龍井). This was the beginning (嚆矢) of the student movement in East Kando (東滿). To assert their group identity, these striking students established their own school, which was the Tongyang Hagwŏn (東洋學院). The English instructor at this academy was the late Madam Pak Wŏn-hŭi (朴元熙). On May 12th of the same year, a lecture meeting hosted by the student council of this academy led to the sacrifice of three students:
What to do about the contradictions of the modern era - Kim Un-hae
The modern era and religion - Kim Un-wŏn (金雲園)
Heaven on Earth - Chŏng Chŏk-a (鄭赤牙)
This was the beginning of the social movement in East Kando. Following this, in July of the same year, the Pang Han-min (方漢旻) incident occurred, and one of the individuals arrested (被檢者) in this case was the late Madam Pak Wŏn-hŭi.<18> The Madam was the wife of the late Kim Sa-guk (金思國) and a pioneer in the Korean women's movement. She passed away on January 5, 1928, around 10:45 PM, at the age of 30, becoming a person of another world. Her funeral, organized jointly by social organizations, is a well-known fact.
Korea, with much work to do - Few hands for the task - A worker now dead - The late Madam Pak Wŏn-hŭi! When the sky is high, the horses are fat (天高馬肥), and the fallen leaves of the North Country (北國) gather, whisper, and scatter again, thoughts of the deceased become even more profound. Would the deceased who has gone to another world not feel it? From the immortality of energy ("에네루-기" 不滅), wouldn't the differentiated substance or spiritual resonance of the deceased be felt? Especially the deceased who "loved autumn" - who had the pen name "Ch'usŏng" (秋聲, Autumn Sound) - the deceased who greeted autumn together behind prison bars - ultimately compels me, feeling the season ("씨-슨" - season), to write at least a few lines of reminiscence.
Late elder sister (누의님) Pak Wŏn-hŭi! Between the Japan ("니혼" 따) where you studied and the China ("중화" 따) where you taught, amidst the foreboding premonition that some explosion might occur at any moment, in the ruins of the Tongyang Hagwŏn, where you, elder sister, stepped onto the stage at the opening ceremony and, starting with "Sometimes to the east, sometimes to the west...", tearfully spoke – only moonlight brightly illuminates the overgrown autumn grass. On the other hand, the Yongjŏng prison, where you, pregnant, threw a soup bowl onto the unfeeling cement ("세멘") floor and, searching for out-of-season *kkakdugi* (diced radish kimchi) in the scorching heat (炎天), kept the guards busy – has expanded from one building then to four now. Yet even its attached prison, the "basement" (지하실), is bustling and full (성황-만원), clearly illustrating the reality of Koreans (XX인). Moreover, some years ago (年前), the rubber shoes (고무 X리) you used in your cell were discovered, becoming a "treasured item of the prisoners" – "Pak Wŏn-hŭi's shoes (X리)".
The theories of Ellen Key ("에렌. 케이") that you translated in prison, and the several pages of your posthumous manuscripts (遺稿) published in "Labor Movement" (노동운동) and the Chosun Ilbo (朝鮮日報), now drowse silently. But the sign for the "Kyŏngsŏng Women's Youth Association" (京城女子靑年會) that was attached to your house – where is it now?
The personality of the late Kim Sa-guk, who would pat your back when hearing your strategies (X략) but sometimes seemed annoyingly bothersome, was likely cursed more by his environment than his character. But now, both are just a cup of earth (一杯土) on the green mountain!
Pang Han-min, who wrote reminiscences of you, elder sister... in "Pyŏlgŏngon" (別乾坤), became a wanderer ("薄男"?) at the Chosŏn Exposition and ended up in prison. The driver for the East Kando Youth Federation (東滿靑總) – your young brother (오라비) – the late Kim Ch'ŏl-san (金鐵山), staged a struggle over his corpse (시체쟁탈전) in Wŏnsan (元山) before going underground. It is not just your own circumstances, elder sister, that have changed; everything has changed.
This world, carrying the eternal history of countless lives and flowing towards the eternal sea, has shifted its solemn pace amidst all contradictions and outcries (including your outcry, elder sister) – as a historical necessity!<19>

**Summary**

This article compiles five short memoirs by different authors, each recalling their last encounter with a significant historical figure: Kim Ok-kyun (by Yun Ch'i-ho), Chang Tŏk-chun (by Yu Kwang-yŏl), Son Pyŏng-hŭi (by Yi Ton-hwa), Min Yŏng-hwan (by Yu Chin-t'ae), and Pak Wŏn-hŭi (by Saebyŏk). The recollections capture poignant moments often preceding tragic deaths – assassination, disappearance during political turmoil, death from illness after imprisonment or political struggle, and suicide following national disgrace. The pieces reflect on the character, ideals, and final circumstances of these individuals against the backdrop of late Joseon Dynasty reforms, the Japanese colonial period, independence movements, and social activism.

From a historical perspective, these personal accounts offer valuable intimate glimpses into the final days and emotional states of key figures during a tumultuous period. Yun Ch'i-ho's account provides details surrounding Kim Ok-kyun's assassination in Shanghai. Yu Kwang-yŏl's piece highlights the patriotic fervor and tragic fate of journalists like Chang Tŏk-chun involved in covering events like the Kando Incident. Yi Ton-hwa's memoir offers insight into the philosophical outlook and final moments of Son Pyŏng-hŭi, a major religious and nationalist leader. Yu Chin-t'ae's recollection of Min Yŏng-hwan's despair after the Protectorate Treaty humanizes the response of the Joseon elite to colonization. Saebyŏk's tribute to Pak Wŏn-hŭi illuminates the struggles and sacrifices of early female activists and socialists in the colonial era, particularly in the Kando region. Historians might find the specific details of interactions, locations (Tonghwa Yanghaeng, Severance Hospital, Yongjŏng), dates, personal reflections on political events (Protectorate Treaty, Kando Incident), and connections between figures (Min Yŏng-hwan and Yi Sŭng-man, Pak Wŏn-hŭi and Kim Sa-guk) particularly interesting.

잡지명	삼천리 제3권 제12호
발행일	1931년 12월 01일
기사제목	아하. 마즈막 얼골, 除夜의 슬푼 종소리 드르면서
기사형태	회고·수기

아하. 마즈막 얼골, 除夜의 슬푼 종소리 드르면서
暗殺前夜의 金玉均씨-내가 上海의 英語敎師로 잇슬 때
尹致昊

내가 一代의 선구자 金玉均씨를 생전에 마즈막 맛나보기는 氏가 千載의 遺恨을 품고 洪鍾宇의 손에 너머지든 바로 전날이엇슴니다.
곳도 上海요, 집도 그가 최후를 마지하든 東和洋行(호텔식의 여관)이며 또한 방도 바로 氏가 짐을 푼 침실이엇슴니다.
그 때의 나로 말하면 어린 망명객의 몸으로 외로운 그림자를 상해에 잇글어 그곳 米國租界 안에 잇는 中西學院(米國人이 경영하고 잇는 밋숀, 스쿨인대 바로 내가 米國 가기 전에 영어공부를 하든 학교임니다)에서 교편을 잡고 잇섯슴니다.
하로는 氏가 上海로 건너온다는 편지를 밧엇슴니다. 그래서 날자를 꼽아 氏가 도착되든 날에 불이야 불이야 학교 시간을 마치고 저녁 때 東和洋行으로 차저 갓슴니다.
내가 바야흐로 방으로 드러서려 할 때에 큰 갓을 쓰고 자주빗 두루막이를 입은 키가<12> 후리후리하게 생긴 장대한 사람 하나가 나가기에 이상하구나 하고 뒤에 金玉均씨를 맛나 물엇더니 그자가 바로 氏에게 6穴砲를 겨누든 洪鍾宇엇다고 합듸다. 氏는 그 때 알고도 그러는지 모르고 그러는지 무슨 일로 洪鍾宇와 동행하느냐고 무르니까, 그는 얼골빗에 조곰도 유혹의 기색을 아니 보이고,
「아모 별 관계가 업는대 다만 내가 上海로 가겟다니까 그럼 나도 한번 上海 구경을 하겟노라」 하고는 日本서부터 동행하는 것이라고 합데다.
그 좌석에서 氏가 하는 말을 듯건대, 今番 행차는 天津에 잇는 李鴻章을 만나는 외에 또한 中國의 중요인물을 맛나 정치상으로 무엇인가 기도하는 바 잇노라 하면서 여러 사람의 소개장을 지니고 잇섯스며 특히 日本의 中國公使舘에 잇는 李鴻章의 아들의 소개장도 가지고 잇섯습데다.
그 날에 氏는 여러 해-그것은 실노 여러 해로 혹은 北海道의 풍설에 혹은 小笠原島의 물결 치는 해무 속에 몹시 지치고 거친 용색을 하고 잇섯슴을 볼 때 나는 일종 애처러운 감개에 가슴이 압품을 깨달엇슴니다.
그 이튼날 나는 여전하게 中西學院에 가서 敎授하고 잇느라니까 엇던 사람이 급히 달려와서 金玉均씨의 편지를 전한다. 내용은 기어히 맛날 일이 잇스니 오후에 좀 와달나고 함이엇다. 나는 시간이 끗나는대로 곳 차저가겟노라고 사환에게 이약이하여 보내엇다.
실상 그 때에 上海는 지금과 달너서 氏를 차질만한 동포도 별로 업섯거니와 또한 氏가 차저가서 胸襟을 터러놋코 謀事를 圖할 만한 이도 업섯슴니다.
그럴 때에 마즈막 시간을 보고 일슬 지음에 또 한사람이 급히 기별을 가지고 왓슴니다.
「金玉均씨가 지금 바로 암살을 당하엿다고」 나는 교편을 집어뿌리고 蒼慌히 분잡한 거리를 지나 東和洋行으로 달녀갓슴니다.
아하! 그 때는 이미 때가 느젓슴니다. 金玉均씨는 탄환에 마저 「뻬트」 위에 누은 채로 이미 절명된 이후이엇슴니다.
그 겻헤는 시체를 붓잡고 우는 日本人 청년이 하나 잇섯슴니다. 그는 金씨를 몹시 숭배하야 日本서도 늘 따서단이든 청년이엿슴니다.

親友 張德俊군의 최후의 얼골을! 쓰라고 그것은 안될 말이다. 張德俊군이 間島事變 때에 單身隻影으로 가서 죽엇는지 살앗는지 소식을 몰은 지가 벌서 12년... 세월도 쉽기도 하다! 벌서 12년이... 되다니... 그러나 張德俊군의 생사불명은 12년은 고만두고 억천만년을 갈지라도 역시 생사불명일 것이다.

그는 가여웁게도 자라 가는 청춘에 폐병을 알코 잇섯다. 각금 괴로웁게 하는 기침과 함께 혈담이 무더나온다. 필자가 『좀 調攝을 하여야지 저러서서 어떠케 함니까』 하고 물으면 그는 눈에는 눈물이 흘으면서도 입에는 쓸쓸한 우숨을 띄우고

저녁때 병원을 가니 西便으로 따로 떠러저 잇는 병실에서 그는 우득허니- 넘어가는 석양을 바라보고 잇섯다. 긴 길을 지나간 봄날의 태양이 서천에 걸리어 병실 유리창을 고요히 빗친다. 나를 보고 반색을 하더니 좀 안지라 하면서 손을 잡고는 눈에서 눈물이 주르를 흘른다. 나는 위로할 말을 발견치 못하엿다. 한참 동안 침묵이 게속되엿다. 방안의 구석구석에 말할 수 업는 애수가 아리인 것 갓다. 그가 먼저 입을 열어

常春園의 孫秉熙씨
李敦化

나(生)는 動態를 평등적이라고 보면 죽(死)는 動態는 차별적이라 할 수 잇슴니다.<15> 나는 動態는 위인이나 凡人이 달을 것이 업고 부자와 빈자의 구별이 업슬 것입니다. 거저 落地 一聲哭으로 너나, 가 우수수하게 이 세상에 쓸어 나온 것입니다. 그러나 死라 하는 죽엄의 動態에 잇서서는 여러 가지 차별이 잇슬 줄로 암니다. 爲先 죽엄의 배후에는 역사를 걸머지게 되는 고로 너 나의 역사가 천차만별로 달은 배- 잇는 위인과 凡人의 행위의 관계도 잇을 것이오 죽엄 其者에 잇서도 變死, 刑死, 病死, 順死 등등의 차별이 잇고 갓흔 變死나 병원에도 전쟁에서 죽는 이, 戀愛로 자살하는 이, 虎列刺로 갑작이 죽는 이, 문등병으로 고생하다 죽는 이 따위까지를 모조리 헤자고 보면 其 수가 끗이 업슬 듯 함니다. 엇재든 죽엄이라 하는 물건은 『生』 其者와 달나서 과거의 일생을 청산하는 의미로 보든지 미래의 영원한 침묵으로 드러가는 공포로 본다든지 이상한 중에도 비장하며 신비한 중에도 통쾌한 감이 잇슴니다. 죽어 본 이가 다시 살아와서 죽엄의 맛을 설명하는 것을 들어보지 못한 우리로서는 죽엄 其 者에 대한 감상이라든가 흥미라든가 오해에 대해서 각인 각색으로 달을 것이지요. 하나 그러나 우리가 비록 죽은 후의 彼岸의 사정에 대해서는 현재의 의식으로 추측치 못한다 할지라도 죽엄에 임하야서의 모든 動態라는 것은 임이 죽은 모든 이들의 사실에 의하야 직접 체험하야 보앗슬 것입니다. 그리하야 임종에 대한 모든 사람의 감정이란 것은 평상시보다 가장 진실하고 엄숙할 줄로 암니다. 고인의 말에 「人之將死에 其言也善이라」는 말과 갓치 죽는 사람의 맘도 그러하야지려니와 임종을 대한 모든 사람도 또한 그러할 것입니다. 더구나 위인의 최후 거인의 臨死에 대한 감상과 갓흔 것은 우리가 흔이 어더보지 못하는 것 만치 一層別 달은 감상에 잇을 것이 안임니까. 이 의미에서 직접 경험한 그의 임종에 대한 감상을 조고만치 이야기해 보라고 하는 것이 三千里사에서 나의게 나린 분부엿슴니다. 그리고 그것을 이약이하는 데는 故 孫義菴 선생의 『마주막 얼골』을 말하라는 분부가 첨부되엿슴니다.

義菴선생이 임종한 날자로 말하면 세상이 다 아는 바와 갓치 己未年을 지난지 3년 후 壬戍年 5월 19일 夜半이엇고 병으로 말하면 全身不遂症이구요 병난 후 임종시까지의 시간으로 말하면 약 1년 有餘이엇슴니다.
선생이 평소에 잇서 死에 대한 관념은 별달은 견해를 가젓슴니다. 죽음이라는 것은 공포로 대할 것이 안이오 초월로 대하여야 쓰고 굴복적 태도로 대할 것이 안이오 극복적 기분으로 대하여야 쓴다고 하야 왓슴니다. 선생은 門弟들의게 항상 하는 말이 『그대들은 관념으로라도 X에 마자 죽어보라. 놉흔 벨앙에서 떠러저 보라. 큰바다에 빠저 보라. 살아 잇서서 죽엄에 들어가 보라』 하엿슴니다.
이것은 물론 門弟의게 심신의 훈련을 강조한 말삼으로 들니거이와 또한 써 선생의 臨死의 태도를 알 수 잇슬만 하고 그리고 선생의 法說의 하나인 『死後觀』에 잇서는 생명적 활동적 에네루기의 波傳說을 주장하엿슴니다. 즉 사람의 생명적 활동의 에네루기라는 것은 미래의 사람의 생명을 통하야 영구히 불멸한다는 것이라고 생각한 것입니다.
이것도 그 어룬의 가진 死後觀의 일단을 엿볼 수 잇슴니다.
좌우간 선생은 死하는 것 인생의 최종이라 것을 공포와 굴복으로 대한 것이 안이오 초월과 극복으로 대하얏든 것입니다. (略-원문) 처음 멧달 동안은 선생 자신이나 주위의 사람들도 一縷의 희망을 붓치고 병치료를 하엿슴니다만 또 멧 달을 지난 후에는 도저히 회복될 가능이 업슴을 본 주위의 사람들은 아주 낙망으로 지내엿슴니다. 그러나 병인인 선생 자신은 조곰도 실망의 태도가 업섯슴니다. 『거저 나는 죽지 안으니 걱정들 말어라』 하는 말을 백 번, 천 번, 거듭하야슴니다. 가족을 보고도 門弟를 보고도 「나는 죽지 안는다. 나는 죽지 안는다」는 말을 반복하다가 주위의 사람이 밋지 안은 기색이 보히면 병중에도 농담 겸 우스면서 「나는 99세는 살터이니 엇더냐」하고 和氣滿面한 적이 만앗슴니다.<16> 死에 대한 태도가 그러틋이 강한 어른인 고로 죽는 날까지도 일에 대한 생각은 조곰도 쉬지 안앗슴니다. 그때로 말하면 교회가 한창 신구 兩派로 갈이기를 시작하는 때입니다. 한데 선생은 병석에 누은 채로 혹 高弟들을 불너 친히 명령하고 또 유서와 갓흔 글로써 敎中에 선포하기도 하야 대소사를 물론하고 平生時와 갓치 정신적 노력을 계속하엿슴니다. 眞 소위 「鞠躬盡瘁死而後乃己」 엇습니다.
최종의 날인 壬戍 5월 19일 밤인즉 유명하게 폭우가 쏘다젓슴니다. 一天四海가 왼통 물바다로 들어가는 듯 하엿슴니다. 그럴 때에 선생의 一縷의 생명은 점점 살아지기를 시작하엿슴니다. 하나 각금 눈을 드러 좌우의 사람을 살펴보고는 다시 「나는 죽지 안으니 걱정들 말나」 하는 말슴을 하고는 필경 영원의 침묵으로 들어가벌엿슴니다.
선생의 입은 다시 열닐 길 업고 선생의 말삼은 영원히 이 세상에서 살아저 벌엿스나 「나는 죽지 안으니 걱정말나」 하는 말소래만은 우리의 귀에 계속되야 잇슴니다.
선생은 쉬는 것이오 죽은 것이 안이라는 감격이 잇섯슴니다, 그 풍부한 體胴 그 豊潤하고도 和氣潑刺한 얼골을 극히 정숙히 극히 엄숙히 극히 壯히 대지의 우에 실니우고 從容히 누어잇는 상태는 맛치 화성계를 비행하는 비행대포가 대포알을 꽝- 하고 쏜 뒤에 고요히 남아 잇는 듯이 보혀젓스며 천향을 가득이 실은 화포가 천공 중에서 불빗과 향취를 만방하고 흔적 업시 살아지는 기미가 들어슴니다. 그리다가 마음은 차츰 대우주의 大悲壯으로 들어가짐니다. 무궁의 한 무궁의 감 무궁의 情이 이 몸을 싸고 들엇슴니다.
정직히 말하면 나는 지금까지의 내 평생으로는 義菴선생과 갓흔 보기조흔 관상을 다시 본 적이 업고 따라서 그러한 죽엄은 두 번 본 적이 업슴니다. 그는 물론 나의 주관적 감정이 다소 交添되야 잇다 해석할 수 잇스나 그러나 선생의 평소에 在한 활동의 에네루기인 甲午革命 甲辰風雲 (略-원문)에 통하야 잇는 그의 활동의 에네루기는 그 임종의 장엄을 통하야 一層 광채가 나고 一層 고상한 감격을 우리의게 던진 까닭임니다.

지금 余가 과거에 가장 친밀한 관계를 맷고 지나든 고인 가운데 특히 忠正公 閔泳煥씨를 회상하려 하매 스사로 今昔이 감회가 무량하여 짐을 늣기게 된다.

당시 波瀾이 중첩하든 政形과 公의 비장하게 짓든 최후난 임이 世人이 주지하난 바이니 이 자리에선 煩說하기를 피하기로 하고 다만 余가 公을 최후로 만나든 때 정경을 간단히 쓰고저 한다.
때는 光武 9년 11월 17일 이른바 保護條約이 체결되자 소란하든 세상은 더 다시 일층 그 度를 더하야 장안은 물끌듯이 뒤끌엇섯다.

余는 모든 것을 당분간 중지하고 시골집(그 때는 余의 집이 淸州에 잇섯다)으로 나려가기로 작정한 후 작별차로 公을 訪한 것이 바로 保護條約이 체결되든 이튼날인 11월 18일 아츰이엇다.
余가 公의 邸를 드러스니 안박 門이 꽉꽉 닷겻스며 寂寥하기가 심야 삼경과 갓햇고 그 쓸쓸한 품이 완연히 X家 갓헛다.
公은 그 前前日 즉 조약체결 前日 公은 初娶부인 金씨의 葬式 땜은에 京畿 龍仁으로 갓섯다가 바로 歸邸한지 얼마 안되든 때이엿다. 余는 公의 방문을 열고 드러스니 방 속에는 公이 머리를 숙이고 안젓다가 余의 얼골을 잠간 보고는 다시 머리 숙이엿스며<17> 余 亦 아모말 업시 公을 보며 묵어운 발을 띄여 公의 것헤 안젓다.
방안에 분위기난 극히 침중하엿스니 다만 문 닷긴 컴컴한 방안에 말업는 창백한 얼골의 公과 余 두 사람이 안젓슬 뿐이다.
