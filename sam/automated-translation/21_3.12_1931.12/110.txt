Publication Date: 1931년 12월 01일

Author: Song Kyewŏl (宋桂月), Kim Wŏnju (金源珠), Hwang Aesidŏk (黃愛施德), Yi Kyŏngwŏn (李敬媛), Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi (崔貞熙)

My Dear Hometown
A Sketch of Pukch'ŏng (北靑)

Song Kyewŏl (宋桂月)

Perhaps because I left my hometown at fifteen and have since spent five years (五個 星霜) in complex Seoul, I always miss the friends I played with holding hands when young, the sea where we swam in summer, and the back hill where we went to pick flowers. Indeed, that place where I ran and played so innocently for fifteen years during that time full of dreams always makes me feel longing. If I write this down, calling it so-called patriotism (愛鄕心), perhaps my heart will feel more at ease...
My hometown! It is a fishing village called Sinch'ang-ri (新昌里) in Pukch'ŏng County (北靑郡), North Hamgyŏng Province (咸北), a place sixteen hours north by the Kyŏngwŏn Line (京元線) train, but it has many convenient points as transportation by sea or land passes through. A village of about a thousand households is surrounded, with the East Sea lying on one side before it, the Namdaech'ŏn (南大川) River flowing beautifully right beside it, and behind it, the Yŏnbongsan (連峰山) and Inbong (人峰) mountains stand towering (聾立), making it a very scenic place. About 3 *ri* from here, there is a place called Sokhu (俗厚), where a mountain called Moraesan (모래山 - Sand Mountain), famous since old times, stands like a cloud beside the East Sea. Regarding this Moraesan, it is said to be where generals fought battles in ancient times. When one general was about to die after being struck by a sword in battle, he said, "If I die, please make my grave on this Moraesan! And the sign that I will be reborn for our Chosŏn (조선) will be that this Moraesan gradually grows higher, so know this!" and died. According to the village people, there is talk that the mountain is indeed gradually <29> getting higher. There must be many other places worth boasting about. As someone who has studied history for over 10 years, I trust everyone will acknowledge my ignorance despite the many famous places in my hometown, and I shall return to the story of the fishing village.
The products of the East Sea include pollock (명태), cod (대구), flounder (가재미), shad (준치), seaweed (미역), *saengch'wi* (생취 - possibly a type of edible seaweed or shellfish), clams (조개), mussels (거이), shrimp (새우), sardines (정어리), croaker (민어), cutlassfish (칼치), *hoi* (호이 - possibly whale?), *soi* (소이 - possibly whale?), and various other fish are caught here. That is, for the people of this fishing village, these fish are their mother and their life. Thus, 순진 (Sunjin - possibly meaning innocence/simplicity, or a local term/name?) and 순진 (Sunjin) alone bound together the deep hearts of these fisherfolk, and it became the common sentiment of ordinary people not to know anything more terrifying than themselves. Thus, only the marks of straw sandals (집신짝) and worn-out rubber shoes (헌- 고무신) were imprinted on the fine sand of the wide, white sandy beach. However, the muddied waters stirred by this rapidly changing (變遷) social system have flowed even into this small, remote fishing village. On the white sandy beach once trodden by straw sandals, the double lines (二字) drawn by *geta* (「게다」 - Japanese wooden clogs) and the marks of iron-soled shoes (철신작) have become crowded (雜踏). On one hand, tin roofs (양철집웅) have appeared, and in front of them, signs like "Sardine Fertilizer (鰮肥) Factory XXX Tarō (太郞) Branch Store," cod factories, and mussel factories – such new scenery has increased. Thus, as the fishermen rowed in chanting "Eogiyeocha," pushing through the blue waves, black shadows (검은 黑影) met their returning boats and ruthlessly rummaged through the boat holds (배칸). These two [fishermen and the newcomers/factory owners?] felt a new fear and melancholy they had never considered, while simultaneously, the hunger of their young wives and children (妻子) and the cries of their young children asking for food grew louder. The sighs of these fishermen were simply overcome by the sound of the incoming waves. The strength and spirit to drive away these factories have not yet come to these fisherfolk, so how will they block this force that will confront them in the future... However, there is something I especially want to proudly introduce here to my ten million female comrades: the economic independence that women of North and South Hamgyŏng Province (咸鏡南北道) cannot help but be proudest of. Some readers might think this is a lie (!) and purse their lips, but firstly, I want to state that these women have neatly cleared (淸算) their minds and possess the great ambition to conquer the entire universe alone. To speak more concretely, women with four or five, or even seven or eight young children, manage all household chores, then push carts (구루마), roll up their clothes to their thighs (넙적다리), roll up their sleeves like sleeveless Western clothes (양복모양), wear headscarves (머리수건), carry large basins (함박) on their heads, push carts, and walk around the entire market town (시가). This is likely a sight unseen and unheard of outside this place.
Furthermore, even women from the middle class and above consider such labor (로동) sacred. When two or three daughters-in-law from one household go out and return as the sun sets, each earns about one won (일원) or one won and 50 jeon (일원50전). Thus, from the labor of these women, one can sufficiently see that there is no class distinction (계급적 차별) like fights between the rich (부자 싸옴) here. Next is the market (장). While other places usually have markets once every four or three days, this place has one thirty times a month! Thus, there is a market reliably once every single day. However, while in places like Seoul or the three southern provinces (3南地方), men go out to do the market shopping, here, if a man goes out to the market, he is called a petty man (졸장부), and it's a place where he would be immediately criticized. Therefore, for the market, only women gather. Whether selling rice, firewood, or vegetables, regardless of the type of trade, it's entirely women gathering to sell and buy. Some women walk about 30 *ri* on foot just to come to the market, yet they bring only one or two eggs (鷄卵), receive 5 or 6 *jeon* for them, and take this money, collect it, and use it to pay for their studying husband's travel expenses (노자) – such women are the majority (태반).
Next, some women, in order to educate their children, also endure countless hardships, not eating what they want to eat, not wearing what they want to wear, through summer and winter, and there are numerous examples (例) of raising fine individuals. In any case, looking from any perspective, diligent, lively, and strong-minded women are Hamgyŏng Province women. No one would deny that they are progressing in all aspects, and with this, I conclude.<30>

Chinnamp'o (鎭南浦), the Dream Place of My Girlhood, Where Waves Lap

Kim Wŏnju (金源珠)

Having left my hometown long ago, I have never particularly longed for it nor praised it. Yet, when asked to write something under the title "My Hometown," my heart flutters strangely.

Chinnamp'o (鎭南浦) is where I was born, and it is where my flesh and blood (골육) have been based for over 20 years now (상금것). However, if I must specifically mention my hometown, I think of a place called Ŏkchangdŭl (억장들), about 3 *ri* (Korean *ri*) away from Chinnamp'o. This place is gently embraced from behind by the wide skirt-like Yŏndaebong Peak (燕臺峰), and in front, the Namp'o River (南浦江), which receives water flowing from the Taedong River (大洞江) and carries it to the Yellow Sea (황해), flows leisurely. It is a cozy (안윽한) fishing village. Regardless of the four seasons (四節), silvery fish flop about on the boats, and the Namp'o apples (南浦사과), a famous product (名産) of Chosŏn, are produced in Masan-ri (麻山里), just one ridge (재동) away from here.
Listening to the plaintive songs of boatmen riding the morning and evening tides, I spent the majority of my most "romantic" (로맨틱) girlhood here in Ŏkt'angt'ŭl (억탕틀 - likely a typo/variation of 억장들). When the yard felt small, I climbed the mountain; when tired (실증) of the mountain, I went down to the sea to gather shellfish, playing very freely and vigorously.

It has been exactly 10 years this year since I left my hometown. My hometown today, after 10 years! Yŏndaebong Peak, which my young feet climbed up and down so wearily, still spreads its benevolent skirt, and the flow of the Namp'o River, which endlessly received the breath of energy overflowing from my small chest, is unchanged from before. Yet, what is transient (허무한) is only the trace (자최) of humans.

Perhaps it is fitting (맛당하다) that human changes are severe in the port of Namp'o (南浦), a place of constant flux (有爲轉變), where things arrive in the morning, pause with affection (情), and then depart without regret (유감) on the evening tide. However, what is strikingly noticeable to the eyes of a traveler visiting once or twice a year is how the streets once bustling with Korean people 10 years ago have become desolate like traces after being burned down, and how the foot of the mountain, green 10 years ago, is now covered with shacks resembling crab shells (게딱지).
Everyone wishes to live well, that 마음 is surely the same for all. They too must have put in effort over the 10 years, yet why is it that one only hears sounds of ruin (망하다), and not a single person prospering (興) is seen in Namp'o? Whether hateful or lovely, just like the unavoidable affection for one's flesh and blood, though I have no particular attachment to my hometown, I cannot deny that my heart tightens (죄이다) when I think of my shabby "hometown."<31>

P'yŏngyang (平壤), the Water Capital, the "Venice" of the East

Hwang Aesidŏk (黃愛施德)

My hometown is P'yŏngyang (平壤). Before introducing my hometown, I ask for the readers' forgiveness. P'yŏngyang—if it's P'yŏngyang, even without my specific introduction here, its scenic beauty (景槪絶勝) has already been praised and lauded many times by the sharp pens of various writers (文人).
Nevertheless, as someone with absolutely no talent (才操) in writing, depicting the scenery (景色) of P'yŏngyang is too difficult a task, so I cannot help but hesitate. Therefore, although I want to boast about P'yŏngyang's scenery, I must reluctantly refrain and will try to briefly write down my feelings about my hometown.
It is surely a fact that everyone holds an attachment (애착심) to their hometown. How much more so for me, born in P'yŏngyang, which is worthy of being boasted about to the world; how could I not have an attachment to my hometown? I want to boast about my hometown, P'yŏngyang, in any aspect. Firstly, P'yŏngyang occupies a good location as a city.
Is that not why it possesses a shining history as a capital for over 4,000 years?
Because it occupies a better location than Kyŏngsŏng (京城 - Seoul), known as the capital (首府) of Chosŏn, I anticipate much development in the future. Transportation is also convenient. Although not a port, the Taedong River (大洞江) capably assists with the convenience of boats (舟船).
Perhaps because the mountains are majestic (웅장) and the river water is clear, the hearts (맘) of the hometown people are very broad and large.
I too have experienced and felt it many times; when climbing the high peak of Ŭlmildae (乙密臺), the universe appears wider, people's hearts seem to broaden further, and thoughts seem to reach a higher level.
How could I not rejoice at being born in P'yŏngyang, blessed with the artistry of magnificent nature!
Hometown people have big and broad feet, so they succeed in business. Where there is success, there might also be failure, but compared to people from other places, they tend to succeed more often.
And many prominent figures (인물) are produced. It might be my biased view (편견), but I wonder if most of those known as capable individuals (일꾼) in Chosŏn are not P'yŏngyang people.
However, one thing I consider regrettable (유감) is that many hometown people go out to work in other places (타처). Since I myself live in Seoul instead of living in my hometown, I have no face (염치) to speak—even now, when I happen to seek out my hometown, I feel a sense of desolation, like visiting an old house without its owner. Lastly, my wish is for my hometown P'yŏngyang, which lacks nothing to complain about, to have an even more shining history, just like in the old days, and with this, I conclude.<32>

Praise and Censure of Kyŏngsŏng (鏡城), Possessing Beautiful Mountains and Rivers

Yi Kyŏngwŏn (李敬媛)

1. Scenery (風物)

My hometown, Kyŏngsŏng (鏡城).
Also known as Tansŏng (短城). There is no room here to explain the historical origin of this name, but as the name itself already subtly suggests (暗示), my hometown is rich in historical fragrance. A quiet village overflowing with poetic charm (詩趣), green with the moss of history, imbued with ancient colors (古色蒼然), giving more the feeling of a tranquil village than a capital—this is Tansŏng.
Perhaps because the moss of history is green and the ancient colors are profound, the scenery is extremely serene and beautiful. Setting aside the instinctive attachment to the cradle (요람) that nurtured me for nearly 20 years, and observing coolly, I dearly love my hometown's scenery.
The mountains are fine, the water is clear, the fields are wide—and moreover, the sea is close. There is the Susŏngmun Gate (壽星門 - South Gate) from which one can overlook (俯瞰) the village in the deep morning mist; there is the perimeter of the fortress wall that tells more than enough of old history; there is Sŭngamsan Mountain (勝岩山) where the full moon (滿月) and crescent moon (弦月) alternately shine; there is Kwanhaesa Temple (觀海寺) overlooking the distant sea; there is Wŏnsudae Pavilion (元帥臺) listening to the nearby cries of the sea; and about half a *ri* (半里 許) to the east lies Tokchin Port (獨津浦口), which I imagine has scenery quite similar (恰似) to Aeran Island (愛蘭島 - possibly Aihran-do/Aeran-do).
Fearful perhaps of harming this varied splendid scenery (勝景), even a single blade of grass growing within seems to be careful of its own growth and mindful of its charm (致趣), so do not be surprised. Inside the fortress, there is a weeping willow (垂楊) that ranks first in the East. A renowned painter, after journeying (遍路巡*) through various regions of Japan and China (中國), finally arrived here, discovered this weeping willow, admired it endlessly (賞嘆不已), and named it thus; so saying it ranks first in the East is likely not too exaggerated a praise.
Why is there no poet in this town, possessing such serene and beautiful scenery, to sing and express it? Why is it that poor Chosŏn (삼천리 - lit. three thousand *ri*, a poetic name for Korea) sent forth barely one poet, and after that, news has become distant? From this place with varied beautiful scenery, it would be fitting if there were more poets contemplating the tragedy of the "people running to the sea" (바다로 달니는 사람들) evoked by the blue crescent moon hanging over the old fortress ruins singing of the eternal flow, and by the tragic roar of the sea (海吼音) heard in bed on winter dawns.

2. Apple Orchard (林檎園)

"The apple orchard (능금밧), starting on the hill, slopes down to the stream bank. When the cold snow of the north country (북국) melted and the willow branches by the stream took on moisture, sunlight flowed onto the purplish apple tree branches, east for ten *ri*, south for ten *ri*, the vast apple orchard shone fertilely patch by patch (아름아름). When the barley in the fields formed ears and the sound of flutes outside the village was quiet, apple blossoms (능금꼿) thickly enveloped the hill, and the rich fragrance of flowers <33> flowed gently (살낭살낭) over the hill, over the fields, across the stream, across the plains, reaching the village. Without dreaming of lemon fragrance from southern lands, the apple blossoms here were the dream of the village people. When the village maiden, wearing a crimson skirt (다홍초마), got married, the apple blossoms fell, and golden waves rippled in the fields; in late autumn, frost-kissed apples hung heavy, cluster by cluster (송이송이), reddish (아지벌게)..." — (From "林檎 - Apple" in "北國點景 - Scenes of the North Country")
Although not identical to the scene idealized and depicted by the author of this "北國點景," scenery of apple orchards resembling (彷佛) it is currently dotted (點在) within the village. Perhaps because the soil quality (토질) is suitable (適良), fruit trees are generally common here, and apple trees (林檎나무) are especially abundant. The orchards during the spring blossoming (개화기) and autumn ripening (熟實期) periods, along with the larch (이깔 - 낙엽송) seedlings (苗種) scattered across the wide fields, constitute the village's beautiful scenery and also form an important part of the local agricultural products.

3. Assembly Hall (會館) and Mind (頭腦)

Though serene and beautiful, the scenery here is generally static.
Entrusted to the charm of an old town, it is extremely static (靜的) and not dynamic (動的). There might be poetry of quiet reminiscence (회고), but there is no poetry of vibrant reality. There is no movement (움즉임) or effort seen trying to respond to the reality that constantly moves (動) and changes (변천).
This can be clearly inferred (暸然) just by looking at the Youth Assembly Hall (靑年會館), which shows its damaged, ugly appearance year after year without any sign of repair. As an assembly hall whose exterior was quite magnificent for such a village, considering the effort likely put into its birth, the way it carries on its distorted form for so long is even more pathetic to see. Though numerous signboards are hung up uselessly, the exterior is damaged, and cold winds blow inside—and yet, the only thing it's occasionally used for is as a wedding hall—truly a pitiful situation.
Another example of the static scenery here is what I consider the minds (두뇌) of the general intellectual class. Their minds are not just stopped—they are afflicted with stagnation (침체증). Their minds this year are the same as their minds last year; their minds two years ago might be the same as their minds five years ago, or perhaps even ten years ago. That is, the temporal effects of these vastly different (相距) intervals of 2 years, 5 years, 10 years... are entirely identical within the minds of these highest intellectual elements. There might be 2 steps of retreat (退却), but not 1 step of advance (전진). Not even half a step of development (발전). In their prime 5 or 6 years ago, they might have had the youthful spirit (풋기개) resembling that of patriots (志士). Where is that spirit now (安在뇨)! The ethical stagnation of their minds will now transform into the comfortable ethics (倫理) of their lives. Their minds, waking or sleeping, strive for and revere the enrichment (윤택) of their personal lives. This is their ethic. Whether the plight of a close friend struggling in hardship (궁境) pricks their "tender" tear glands (淚腺) or not, it is enough for them alone to diligently buy land (전지) and houses (주택) and install radios (라듸오) with the gold they had saved up. As days go by, in inverse proportion to the stagnation of ethics, the ethics of gold "sharpens" (첨예화) their minds, bringing about an untimely gold rush era here.
This stagnation of minds and the resulting static scenery—is it the fault of the "peaceful" scenery of this town, overflowing with the poetic charm of green moss? If so, perhaps that retrospective poetry should be shot dead with an arrow. From there, this "peaceful" but powerless cradle will take steps as a battlefield (戰野) for just struggle (싸홈), and begin to carve (색여가게) the records of living history, one trace, two traces at a time.

4. Wellside Meeting (샘ㅅ젼 會議)

Because the scenery is static and minds are stagnant, the residents cannot hold truly fresh dreams, and their thoughts and actions are merely saturated and wandering within the circle (圈內) of daily life. Perhaps it is because they are saturated with comfortable lives that they are lethargic (무기력) and cannot hold fresh dreams, consequently leading to stagnant minds and static scenery.—For dreams always arise from lack, demands arise, and struggles arise.
Generally saturated with daily life, their idle thoughts and attention naturally turn towards idle <34> subjects (제목). They waste their attention on the personal affairs (일신상) of neighbors or unnecessary trivial matters (鎖事). This applies commonly to both men and women. It is particularly severe (尤甚) among the female class (女級), and it seems the so-called "wellside meeting" (샘전회의) is still prevalent among them today. It is more pitiful than laughable that this vulgar relic (상스런 유물) of the leisured class from old times still rampages (횡행) in this busy era (다사한 시기). Even if not at the wellside—a wellside meeting at least has a pastoral (牧歌的) cuteness—wherever they gather, regardless of the place, they hold meetings, discuss slander and insults (中傷辱說) against neighbors, and create uncontrollable so-called rumors (風說). A rumor composed of three parts truth and seven parts falsehood becomes entirely false after passing through the mouths of two or three people. The person uttering the falsehood might feel pleased, but why do they not consider how much hurt (상처) that false rumor inflicts upon the person who is its subject (제재)? Reckless people (막난 분들)! It is the way of the unenlightened to take excessive interest in others' affairs, but this is extremely distressing and pitiful for bystanders to witness. Whether someone's pot (솟) is crookedly hung or not, whether the bridge of a bride's nose is well-formed (잘녹하다 - likely meaning well-shaped/pretty) or not, is there any need to have even a speck (틔끗만콤) of interest? Such barbaric customs (蠻風) should be mutually refrained from, even for the dignity (위용) of the hometown.

5. Conclusion (結語)

Having a long "history" (*史) is nothing to boast about. Because it is a meaningless old *史.
Having beautiful scenery is nothing to boast about. Because it amounts to no more than powerless, static, retrospective "poetry" (詩).
The idle barbaric customs must be corrected, the stagnant minds must be renewed (쇄신), the dilapidated assembly hall must be repaired (수축), and thus the struggle (싸* - likely 싸움, struggle/fight) must begin, and the creation of a new *史 (history) must be undertaken. Only then will the true *史 (historical) record of this town begin, and the static scenery will also start to * (likely 動, move) by itself.
The hometown shall shine!
Kyŏngsŏng shall brighten!
—October 26, 1931.

My Hometown, Swept by the Stormwind (暴風)

Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi (崔貞熙)

The mountains are high and the water is clear; I believe this is sufficient to indicate the geography, history, and character of my hometown.
To the northwest, Ŭnjusan Mountain (銀柱山) rises high (놉히 소사 잇고); to the southeast, Kŭmbongsan Mountain (金峰山) lies crouched (업드러저 잇스며) like a circle, and in front of it, a small mountain (小山) lies elongated. The plain is very wide, and the five grains are abundantly produced here, so it should not be too insufficient (?) to fill the bellies of my hometown people. In front, crossing (것처서) this plain, flows a river; its name is called (일너서) Namdaech'ŏn (南大川).
My hometown, Tanch'ŏn (端川) (also called Pokchu - 福洲), seems to peacefully sleep while listening leisurely to the sound of this river flowing—I should mention that it is the land that gave birth to Yi Tonghwi (李東輝 - name followed by 略-원문, indicating omission in the original) in the old days and nurtured him for a long time.<35>
One can glimpse that many historical relics (古蹟) still remain, testifying to the shining history of olden days.
Now, to give (이약이 하다 - dialectal/older form of 이야기하다) one or two examples, the fortress ruins, though faint (미미하나마), leave traces (姿跡); the Changsadae (壯士臺 - Warrior's Terrace) on Kŭmbongsan; the legend that on a large rock at "Changswikkŏri" (장쉬꺼리 - 壯士街, Warrior's Street), a strongman's (壯士) footprint and staff mark (집행이 자죽) were left—these are sufficient proof.
Tanch'ŏn is neither a city nor a port. It was, so to speak, a town (읍) slightly removed (버서진) from the village filled with a rural atmosphere. Furthermore, a place designated (명칭을 어든) as a park has even been established (설치되여 잇스니); it is named (일홈한다) "Kŭmgok Park" (金谷公園).
This park was established (설비해 노은 것) 6 or 7 years ago when a wealthy kisaeng (기생), having reached her final years (末路), donated funds. Pavilions (亭座) were built, and several stone monuments (비석) were erected; these seem to commemorate (조상하다) the lonely spirit (넉) of the unfortunate old kisaeng who passed away without children.

The earth turned, bringing news of spring even to my hometown in the North Country (北國).—The zinc-colored (亞鉛색) sky—the biting wind (疾風) that seemed to flay the flesh—the deeply piled snow—when this transforms (一變), first the mind's eye melts, and when the white snow on the peak of Ŭnjusan (銀柱山峰) becomes barely visible, the ice (어름) on the Namdaech'ŏn begins to thaw.
Then, when leaves (입피 - older spelling of 잎이) sprout on the plum and apricot trees and buds are about to bloom, the hometown people, who had been confined (갓첫든) by the cold (치위), begin to release their energy (에네루기 - energy).
The spring days in my hometown were so wonderful.
The clear sky—the warm sun shining down (나려 쪼이는)—the gentle scenery seeming to doze quietly—
And on moonlit nights, if one climbed Kŭmbongsan or Sosan (小山) and looked down at the town center (城內) soaked (저즌) in moonlight—the mottled (어룽어룽하게) roof tiles (기와짱) of wealthy houses resembling a viper's skin (독사의 겁질)—the Namdaech'ŏn river water—the distant "Korijang" (고리장) sea—none failed to stir a poet's emotions. When the buds bloom and the flowers (꼿치 - older spelling of 꽃이) fall, the whole world turns green. The heat haze that lingered (자젓든) on the mountainside and hills also vanishes somewhere (어대로인지).
When the sounds of farmers' weeding songs are heard, students who had gone to the cities return home (귀성) and seem busy for a while, holding lecture meetings (강연회) or speech contests (웅변대회).
However, when the students leave again and harvest time approaches, the hometown sky becomes exceptionally clear, and all the scenery seems to sink dead into the autumn air (파뭇처 버러진다).
When this happens, the people of the hometown all become very busy (奔忙) preparing food for the long winter.

The women of my hometown are vigorous (씩씩하다). Thus, they have already declared themselves the strong ones (强者) and rather conquer the noisy men. They have no learning (학식). But what was it that allowed them to escape the designation of being parasites (기생충) of men? The women of the hometown, regardless (헤아리지 안코) of cold winters or hot summers—without wetting (뭇치지 안코) even a drop of water on their fingers, unlike women who sit (안저서) inside rooms holding (피여 물고) cigarettes, becoming nothing more than playthings (노르개감) for men, something those women cannot even imagine—are always continuously engaged in labor.
And the customs (풍속) of the hometown were beautiful. They do not practice discriminatory treatment based on the rotten-smelling (고린 냄새나는) division of *yangban* (nobility) and *ssangnom* (commoners) seen in the Southern Provinces (南鮮地方). Although it is said that masters (상전) and servants (종) existed (이섯다고) in the old days, perhaps because the hometown people growing up amidst intelligent nature realized early on that "humans are all the same," they reportedly abolished (업새버려 주엇다고) the designation of servant entirely. Thus, my hometown people were happy. It was a Tanch'ŏn that had escaped the class stratum where anyone could make a living if they worked hard.

Adding frost to snow (雪上加霜 - idiom for making things worse). Another layer (겁) of suffering has arisen: a factory, which we cannot help but hate now—a net factory (그물공장)—has been built, exploiting (부레먹는다) poor people by paying wages (임금) of 10 *jeon* a day, a sum horrifying even to hear. This kind of situation, one might say, is a phenomenon not even found in large factories in the cities.
Small-capital shops are disappearing, only the power of large capitalism grows stronger, the phrase (언구) "if you work hard, you can make a living" has turned into a rotten truth, and the beautiful scenery of the old days has rusted (녹이 스러바렷다) blue-green. However, the hometown people do not lose courage.
The bugle sound (라팔소리) echoing (울여오는) from Sosan (to observe the wake-up and bedtime hours, blown at 6 AM and 10 PM) seems to promise the future brightness of the hometown.<36>

**Summary**

This collection features five female writers reflecting on their hometowns in northern Korea (Pukch'ŏng, Chinnamp'o, P'yŏngyang, Kyŏngsŏng, Tanch'ŏn) during the Japanese colonial period (1931). They express nostalgia for the natural beauty and childhood memories associated with these places, describing landscapes, local products (like Namp'o apples), and customs. However, beneath the nostalgia, there's a strong current of lament over the changes brought by modernization and colonial influence: the intrusion of Japanese businesses (factories, *geta* marks on beaches), economic hardship replacing former self-sufficiency, social stagnation, and the perceived decay of traditional ways alongside the physical environment. The writers, particularly Song Kyewŏl and Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi, highlight the resilience and economic contributions of Hamgyŏng Province women, contrasting their industriousness with perceived idleness or changing roles elsewhere. Hwang Aesidŏk praises P'yŏngyang's historical significance and potential but notes the outflow of talent. Yi Kyŏngwŏn criticizes the intellectual stagnation and superficiality in Kyŏngsŏng, calling for renewal and engagement with reality. Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi mourns the exploitation in Tanch'ŏn's new factory but ends on a hopeful note symbolized by the bugle calls.

From a historical perspective, these accounts are valuable for their firsthand descriptions of northern Korean towns under Japanese rule, capturing the complex interplay of nostalgia, local pride, and critical observation of socio-economic transformation. They offer insights into the perceived impact of Japanese economic penetration (factories, changing streetscapes), the evolving roles and recognized economic power of women in specific regions like Hamgyŏng-do, contrasting views on progress and tradition, and the psychological effects of colonial modernity on local identities and communities. The criticisms of stagnation and calls for action (Yi Kyŏngwŏn) also reflect contemporary intellectual currents.

산이 조코 물이 맑고 들이 넓고―그 우에 또한 바다가 갓갑다. 안개 깁흔 아참의 마을을 俯瞰할 수 잇는 壽星門(南門)이 잇고 녯 역사를 이야기 하고도 남는 성벽의 테두리가 잇고 滿月과 弦月이 번가라 빗기는 勝岩山이 잇고 멀니 바다를 바라보는 觀海寺가 잇고 갓가히 바다의 절규를 듯는 元帥臺가 잇고 동으로 약 半里 許에는 愛蘭 아란島와 풍물이 恰似하리라고 생각되는 獨津浦口가 잇다.
이 가지가지의 勝景을 傷할가 두려워함인지 그 속에 자라는 한 포기의 나무까지라도 스스로 성장에 조심하고 致趣에 유의하는 든 하니 놀나지 마라. 성 안에는 동양에서 읏듬가는 한 포기의 垂楊이 잇다. 일홈 업지 안은 화가가 일본과 中國의 각지를 遍路巡*한 끗테 이곳에 니르러 드듸어 이 垂楊을 발견하고 賞嘆不已하야 명명한 것이니 동양에서 읏듬간다는 것이 그다지 과장된 찬사는 아닐 것이다.
이 그윽하고 아릅다운 풍물을 가진 이 고을에 그것을 읇고 표현하는 시인이 업슴은 웬일인가. 가난한 삼천리에 겨우 한 사람의 시인을 보냇슬 뿐이요 그 뒤 소식 아득함은 웬일일가. 가지가지의 아름다운 풍물을 가진 이곳으로서 녯 성터에 걸닌 푸른 초생달이 유구한 흐름을 노래하고 겨울 새벽 한 잠자리에 들너오는 비장한 海吼音에 「바다로 달니는 사람들」의 비극을 구상하는 시인이 더 잇서도 조흠직하다.

2. 林檎園

「언덕 우에서 시작되여 경사를 지으면서 개울가까지. 뻐친 능금밧. 북국의 찬 눈에 녹아 개울가 버들가지 물 올을 때 자지빗 능금나무 가지가지에 햇빗 흘너 동으로 십리 남으로 십리 평퍼즘한 능금밧이 기름지게 아름아름 빗낫다. 들의 보리 이삭 패고 마을 박게 피리소래 고요할 때 능금꼿 폭신하게 언덕을 싸고 욱어진 꼿향긔<33> 언덕 넘고 밧을 넘고 개울 건너 들을 건너 마을까지 살낭살낭 흘너왓다. 남쪽 나라 레몬향긔 꿈꾼 것 업시 이곳에 능금꼿이 곳 마을 사람의 꿈이엿다. 마을의 처녀 다홍초마 닙고 시집갈 때 능금나무 꼿 지고 들에 황금파도 치는 느즌 가을 서리 마즌 능금 송이송이 아지벌게 무거웟다...」―(「北國點景」 중 「林檎」에서)
이 「北國點景」의 작자가 理想化시켜 묘사한 점경과 흡사하지는 안으나 彷佛한 林檎밧의 풍경이 현재 마을 속에 點在하야 잇다. 토질이 適良함으로인지 대체 이곳에는 과수가 흔하고 더욱이 林檎나무가 만타. 봅 개화기와 가을 熟實期의 과수원은 넓은 들에 산재한 이깔(낙엽송)의 苗種와 함께 마을의 아름다운 풍경이며 아울너 이곳 농산물의 중요한 부문을 짓는 것이다.

3. 會館과 頭腦

그윽하고 아름답기는 하나 一體로 이곳 풍경은 정지된 풍경이다.
녯 고을다운 운치에 맛겨 극히 靜的이요 動的은 못된다. 고요한 회고의 詩는 잇슬지언정 생생한 현실의 詩는 업다.―각각으로 動하고 변천하야 마지아니하는 현실에 應하야가랴고 하는 움즉임이 업고 노력이 보이지 안는 것이다.
이것은 몃 해를 가도 修理될 줄을 모르고 훼손된 채로의 흉한 면모를 그대로 보이고 잇는 靑年會館만을 보아서도 暸然히 짐작되는 일이다. 탄생될 때에 매우 힘도 들엇슬만치 이러한 마을로서는 외모가 상당히 장한 회관인만콤 찌그러진 자태를 오래동안 지니고 나가는 樣은 더 한층 보기에 딱하다. 공연히 간판만 수두룩 걸녓슬 뿐이지 외모는 상하고 안에는 찬바람이 도는―그 주제에 각금 쓰인다는 것이 긔ㅅ껏 결혼식장으로 박게는 쓰이지 안코―실로 딱한 노릇이다.
또 한가지 이곳의 정지된 풍경의 예증으로 나는 이곳 일반 지식계급의 두뇌를 든다. 그의 두뇌는 정지―되엿다느니 보다도 침체증에 걸녀잇다. 금년의 그의 두뇌는 작년의 그의 두뇌요 재작년의 그의 두뇌는 5년 전의 그의 두뇌요 혹은 10년 전의 그의 두뇌인지도 모른다. 즉 2년 5년 10년... 의 각각 심히 相距가먼 이 각 시간의 시간적 효과가 이 최고 지식분자의 두뇌 속에서는 전연 동일한 것이다. 2步의 退却은 잇슬 지언정 1步의 전진은 업다. 半步의 발전도 업다. 5,6년 전의 壯期에는 그래도 志士 然한 풋기개는 잇섯스리라. 그 기개 지금에 安在뇨! 그의 두뇌의 윤리의 침체는 이제는 그의 생활의 태평한 倫理로 변하야 버릴 것이다. 그의 두뇌는 자나깨나 그의 개인 생활의 윤택을 도모하고 숭상한다. 이것이 그의 윤리인 것이다. 친한 친구의 궁境에서 헤매이는 꼴이 그의 「다정한」 淚腺을 찌르거나 말거나 그 홈자나 알뜰이 저축하얏든 황금으로 전지를 사고 주택을 사고 라듸오를 설치하면 그만인 것이다. 날이 가면 갈사록 윤리의 침체와 반비례하야 황금의 윤리가 그의 두뇌를 「첨예화」시켜 여긔에 때 아닌 황금광 시대를 출현시키는 것이다.
이 두뇌의 침체와 정지된 풍경의 초래―그것은 푸른 이끼 끼인 詩趣가 넘치는 「평화한」 이 고을 풍물의 죄일까. 그러타면 모름즉이 그 회고적 詩를 화살으로 쏘아 죽여야 할 것이다. 거긔에서 「평화로운」 그러나 무력한 이 요람은 정당한 싸홈의 한 戰野으로서의 거름을 떼어노을 것이며 생생한 역사의 기록을 한자리 두 자최 끌로 색여가게 될 것이다.

4. 샘ㅅ젼 會議

풍경이 정지되고 두뇌가 침체되엿슴으로 주민들은 끔직이 신선한 꿈을 가질 수 업고 그들의 의상과 행동은 겨우 일상생활의 圈內에서 포화되고 방황할 뿐이다. 하기는 여유 잇는 생활에 포화된 까닭에 무기력하야 신선한 꿈을 가지지 못하고 따라서 두뇌가 침체되고 풍경이 정지되는 지도 모른다.―항상 하지 못한 곳에서 꿈이 생기고 요구가 생기는 싸홈이 생기는 것이니까.
일반으로 일상생활에 포화된 까닭에 한가한 그들의 생각과 주의는 자연 한가한<34> 제목 우에 향하게 된다. 그들은 隣人의 일신상에나 혹은 불필요한 鎖事에 그들의 주의를 낭비한다. 이것은 남녀를 고통하고 다 그런 것이다. 특히 女級에 尤甚한 것이니 그들 사이에는 지금에도 소위 「샘전회의」가 성행되여 잇는 모양이다. 이 녯적부터의 유한계급의 상스런 유물이 이 다사한 시기에 의연 횡행함은 가소롭다느니 보다 가엽슨 일이다. 샘전이 아니라도―샘전이라면 牧歌的 귀염성이나 잇지―그들은 모이는 곳마다 곳을 불구하고 회의를 연고 隣人의 中傷辱說을 토의하야 것잡을 수 업는 소위 風說를 맨드는 것이다. 3분의 진실에 7분의 거짓을 가미한 풍설을 두 사람, 세 사람의 입을 건너가는 동안에는 온전한 거짓이 되여버린다. 그 거짓을 뱃는 사람은 유쾌하는지 모르나 그 것짓 풍설의 그의 제재가 되는 사람에게는 얼마나한 상처를 끼치는가를 웨 짐작지 못하는가. 막난 분들! 께이지 못한수록 남의 일에 지나처 관심을 가지랴고 하는 법이나 이것은 엽사람이 보기에는 극히 괴로웁고 딱한 일이다. 남의 집 솟이 삐뚜로 걸녓거나 말거나 신부의 코등이 잘녹하거나 말거나 틔끗만콤이라도 관심을 가질 필요가 업지 안은가. 이러한 蠻風은 고향의 위용을 위하야서라도 서로서로 삼가야 할 일이다.

5. 結語

「*史」가 깁다고 자랑할 것은 못된다. 무의미한 낡은 *史이기 때문에.
풍경이 아름답다고 자랑할 것은 못된다. 무기력하고 정지된 회고적 「詩」에 지나지 아니하기 때문에 한가한 蠻風을 교정하고 침체된 두뇌를 쇄신하고 이즈러진 회관을 수축하야 써 싸*을 시작하고 새 *史의 창조에 착수하여야 할 것이다. 그때에 비로소 이 고을의 참된 *史의 기록이 시작될 것이며 정지되엿든 풍경도 스스로 *하기 시작할 것이다.
고향은 빗나리라!
鏡城을 밝으리라!
―31.10월26일.

暴風에 쓸니는 나의 故鄕

崔貞熙

산이 놉고 또한 물이 맑으니 이것으로 나의 고향의 지리와 역사 그리고 성격을 표시하기에 충분할 것이라고 밋는다.
서북편으로 銀柱山이 놉히 소사 잇고 동남측으로 金峰山이 둥그러케 업드러저 잇스며 그 압에 小山이 길게 누어잇다. 평야는 몹시 넓으며 여기에서 오곡이 풍부하게 생산되니 나의 고향 사람들의 배를 불니기에는(?) 그닥지 부족치 안을 것이다. 압흐로 이 평야를 것처서 흐르는 강이 잇스니 일홈을 일너서 南大川이라 한다.
이 강물의 흐르는 소래를 유유하게 들으면서 고요히 잠자는 뜻한 나의 고향―端川(일명을 福洲라고도 한다)은 그 녯날에 李東輝 (略-원문)을 나핫스며 그를 오래동안 길너준 땅이라는 것을 말해둔다.<35>
아직까지도 녯날의 빗나는 역사를 증명하는 古蹟 등이 만히 남어잇슴을 엿볼 수 잇다.
인제 한두 가지의 예를 들어 이약이 한다면 미미하나마 姿跡을 남겨둔 성터이며 金峰山의 壯士臺―「장쉬꺼리」(壯士街)의 큰바위 돌 우에 壯士 발자죽과 집행이 자죽을 남겨 노앗다는 전설 등을로서도 넉넉히 증명할 수 잇는 것이다.
端川은 도시도―그리고 항구도―안이다. 말하자면 농촌기분이 가득 차고 잇는 마을에서 조곰 버서진 읍이엿다. 게다가 공원이라고 명칭을 어든 장소까지 설치되여 잇스니 「金谷公園」이라고 일홈한다.
이 공원은 6,7년 전에 돈 만흔 기생이 末路에 니르러 자금을 던저서 설비해 노은 것인데 亭座도 만드러 놋코 비석도 두서너 곳에 세워 노앗스니 이는 자식도 업시 세상을 떠나버린 불상한 늙은 기생의 외로운 넉을 조상하는 듯 십다.

지구는 돌아서 北國의 내 고향에도 봄소식을 전하여 주엇다.―亞鉛색의 하늘―사를 어이는 듯한 疾風―몹시 싸힌 눈―이것이 一變해지면 먼저 마음의 눈이 녹고 銀柱山峰의 힌눈이 보일 듯 말 뜻 하면 南大川 어름이 풀니기 시작한다.
그래서 매화나무, 살구나무에 입피 돗고 봉오리가 피려고 하면 치위에 갓첫든 고향의 사람들은 이로부터 에네루기를 발산한다.
고향의 봄날은 너무나 조왓다.
청량하게 개인 하늘―따듯하게 나려 쪼이는 태양 아래―고요히 졸고 잇는 뜻한 부드러운 풍물―
그리고 달 밝은 밤이면 金峰山이나 小山에 올나서 달빗헤 폭- 저즌 城內를 내려다 보면―독사의 겁질 모양으로 어룽어룽하게 된 부자집 기와짱―南大川 강물―멀니 「고리장」 바다―어느 것이나 시인의 감정을 가만히 두게 만들지 안엇다. 봉오리가 피고 꼿치 떠러지면 왼 세상은 파랏케 변하여진다. 산허리와 언덕 우에 자젓든 아지랑이도 어대로인지 슬그머니 가버린다.
농부들의 김매는 타령소리가 들여올 때면 도회지에 갓튼 학생들이 귀성해서 강연회이니 웅변대회니 하느라고 한참동안 분주한 듯 십다.
그러나 학생들이 다시 떠나가고 추수할 때가 되여가면 고향의 하늘이 몹시 맑어지고 모-든 풍경은 가을 공기 속에 죽은 듯이 파뭇처 버러진다.
이러케 되면 고향의 인민들은 다- 각각 한겨을에 먹을 것을 준비하느라고 몹시 奔忙하게 지낸다.

고향의 여성들은 씩씩하다. 그래서 强者라고 임이 자신하여스며 떠드는 남성들을 오히려 정복한다. 그들은 학식도 업다. 그러나 그들노 하여곰 남성의 기생충이라는 명칭에서 버서나게 한 것은 무엇이엿든가? 고향의 여성들은 치운 겨을이나 더운 여름이나 헤아리지 안코―손까락에 물 한 방울 뭇치지 안코 담배나 피여 물고 방안에 들어안저서 남성의 노르개감 밧게 못되는 여성들로서는 도저히 상상도 못할―노동을 언제나 계속하고 잇다.
그리고 고향의 풍속은 아름다웟다. 南鮮地方에서 보는―고린 냄새나는 양반 쌍놈이라는 것을 가지고 차별대우를 하지 안는다. 녯날엔 상전이 잇고 종이 이섯다고 하나 총명한 자연 속에서 자라는 고향 사람들은 「인간은 맛찬가지라」는 것을 일즉이 깨달엇든 까닭인지 종이라는 명목을 다- 업새버려 주엇다고 한다. 이리해서 내 고향 사람들은 행복스러웟다. 누구를 물론하고 힘써 일하면 먹고 살 수 잇는 계급층에서 탈출한 端川이여섯다.

(14행 략-원문)
雪上에 加霜이다. 또 한 겁의 고통이 생겻스니 지금에 우리로서 미워하지 안을 수 업는 공장― 그물공장이 건설되여서 하로에 10전이라는 듯기에도 끔직스러운 임금을 주어가면서 돈 업는 사람들을 부레먹는다. 이러한 사실은 도회지의 큰 공장에서도 차저볼 수 업는 현상이라고 하겟다.
소자본의 상점은 업서저가고 대자본주의 세력만이 강대하여갈 뿐이고 「힘써 일하면 먹고 살 수 잇다」든 言句도 썩은 진리로 변하고 말엇스며 녯날의 아름답든 풍물도 파-랏케 녹이 스러바렷다. 그러나 고향 사람들은 용기를 일치 안는다.
小山에서 울여오는 라팔소리(朝起시간과 취침시간을 직히기 위해서 아참 여섯시와 저역 열시에 부는 라팔)는 고향의 미래의 광명을 약속하는 듯 십다.<36>
