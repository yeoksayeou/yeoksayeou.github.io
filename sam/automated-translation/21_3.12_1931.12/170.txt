Publication Date: 1931-12-01
Article Title: 三千里 「壁新聞」 제5호, 各國의 國花 (Samch'ŏlli "Wall Newspaper" No. 5, The National Flowers of Various Countries)
Author: Not specified
Article Type: 소식 (News/Information)

Regardless of which country in the world, every country has a flower that it loves. Just because a flower is nice to look at and pretty doesn't mean any country will love it as its national flower. Since a country loves a certain flower based on its national character, one should be able to know the national character of that country by looking at its national flower.

For example, aren't peoples with a continental character like China (支那) fond of the Peony (牧丹), and aren't people from island countries like Ireland fond of the white clover—a small and pure flower?

Now, if we list the national flowers of various countries, they are as follows:

China (支那), Peony (牧丹)               Italy (伊太利), Daisy flower (데-지꼿)
Egypt (애급), Lotus (蓮)                 Japan (日本), Cherry Blossom (櫻花)
England (英國), Rose (薔薇)               Persia (페루샤), Tulip (츄-립푸)
France (佛蘭西), Iris (蔦尾)             Peru (페루), Sunflower (向日葵)
Germany (獨逸), Cornflower (失車草)             Scotland (스콧트랜드), Thistle (薊)
Greece (希臘), Olive (橄欖)               Spain (스페인), Orange (오렌지)
Ireland (아일랜드) White Clover (百구로-바)      Wales (웰-스) [Unclear: 菲一]<53>

**Summary**

This short article from the December 1931 issue of *Samch'ŏlli* introduces the concept that national flowers reflect the national character of a country, citing China's Peony and Ireland's White Clover as examples. It then provides a list of national flowers for various countries including China, Egypt, England, France, Germany, Greece, Ireland, Italy, Japan, Persia, Peru, Scotland, Spain, and Wales (though the Welsh flower name seems corrupted). From a historical perspective, the article is interesting as it was published during the Japanese colonial period. Notably, it lists Japan's national flower but omits any mention of Korea or its own national flower, the Mugunghwa, reflecting the political realities of the time. The use of older terminology like "支那" for China and specific Hanja transcriptions for Western countries and flowers also provides insight into the language and naming conventions of 1930s Korea.
