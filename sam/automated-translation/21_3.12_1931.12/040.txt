Magazine Title: 삼천리 (三千里)
Publication Date: 1931년 12월 01일
Article Title: 三千里 「壁新聞」 제1호, 名唱의 報酬 (Samch'ŏlli "Wall Newspaper" No. 1, The Fees of Famous Singers)
Author: Not specified
Article Type: 소식 (News/Dispatch)

From time to time, famous singers' competitions are held at the Chosŏn Theater (朝鮮劇場) or Tansŏngsa (團成社). When invited for these events, how much remuneration do the organizers give the famous singers (名唱) who perform for one night?
For top-class singers like Yi Tong-baek (李東伯), Song Man-gap (宋萬甲), Kim Ch'ang-hwan (金昌煥), Kim Ch'ang-nyong (金昌龍), Pak Nok-chu (朴綠珠), and Kim Ch'o-sŏn (金楚仙), it is usual to receive from 10 to 20 Won. For other self-proclaimed famous singers, it is said to be less than that.

When the Kyŏngsŏng Broadcasting Station (京城放送局) invites famous personalities for radio broadcasts, how much remuneration do they give? Usually, it is customary to send a box of sweets worth 2 or 3 Won per appearance. For lectures that require a bit more effort, cash payment of about 5 Won per session is given. Also, famous singers are given 10 Won per session, but if they bring along someone who plays the *changgo* (drum), 3 or 4 Won out of that is given to the drummer (*kosu*). It is said that even famous *kisaeng* (名流 기생) like Pak Nok-chu and Kim Ch'o-sŏn only end up with an income of 6 or 7 Won.<8>

**Summary**

This news brief from 1931 details the performance fees paid to famous traditional Korean singers (명창, *myŏngch'ang*) and other figures in colonial Seoul (Kyŏngsŏng). Top singers like Yi Tong-baek and Pak Nok-chu received 10-20 Won for a one-night theater performance, while lesser-known singers earned less. Radio appearances paid significantly less: lecturers received about 5 Won in cash (or a small gift box), while famous singers received 10 Won, out of which they had to pay their accompanying drummer (고수, *kosu*) 3-4 Won, leaving even famous *kisaeng* performers with only 6-7 Won. Historians might find the specific monetary figures useful for understanding the economic status of artists and the value placed on traditional performance versus modern media like radio in colonial Korea. It also sheds light on the payment structures, including the responsibility of lead performers to pay their accompanists.
