女性群像
						金乙漢(記), 李承萬(畵)

Rosa of Pukch'on

It was the morning of a day that was still too early to be called spring, yet a bit too late for winter.
At R Girls' School in C-dong, a sudden commotion arose. The school was turned upside down because someone had scattered dozens of seditious documents, though whose doing it was remained unknown.
The documents in question were mimeographed leaflets, half-sheets bearing the inflammatory content, "Students, do XX!" and so forth.
The first to discover them was an old man named Kim Ch'ŏmji, who worked as a school servant. Unable to read, Kim Ch'ŏmji didn't think much of it and was about to tear them up and discard them. However, a teacher who happened to come to the restroom and see them ran off as if some world-shaking event had occurred, reporting it to the head teacher and the principal, causing the issue to eventually blow up.
Dozens of police officers, hastily summoned by the school authorities' phone call, insisted the culprit was definitely inside the school and began investigating each classroom.
From the first year to the second year, from the second year to the third year—but nothing unusual was found in the students' desks. The red-eyed police officers, ignoring the trembling students, then started searching the fourth year. Yes. The fourth year—indeed, over a hundred leaflets appeared in one student's desk.
"Here it is! Here it is!"
As the police shouted as if they had found something edible, from this corner and that, the students' voices murmured in unison, "Oh no, how could it be in Hye-yŏng's desk..."
The student named Hye-yŏng, from whose desk the problematic leaflets emerged, looked pale as if she too was startled, and the principal and staff, who had been following behind the police, also widened their eyes, thinking, "How could it be her!"<66>
"Are you the owner of this desk?"
"Yes!"
"Then come over here."
The police tried to drag the student named Hye-yŏng away. Seeing this, the staff members belatedly and frantically tried to intervene, explaining that Hye-yŏng's family was quite prominent and that she was a very diligent and well-behaved student. But what use could it possibly be? Thus, Hye-yŏng, leaving her clamoring classmates behind, was eventually taken away to S Police Station.
But for Hye-yŏng, it was truly, truly incomprehensible. And she had never seen or heard of those leaflets in her life; she wondered who could have put them in her desk. More than the fear of being taken to the police station, the curiosity and strangeness of the situation overwhelmed her.
"What strange things happen these days. Who on earth could have put them there?"
As she muttered this to herself, the car carrying Hye-yŏng arrived at S Station in no time. Taken to the interrogation room on the second floor, Hye-yŏng was soon questioned by a police inspector with a handlebar mustache.
"Your permanent address?"
"Iksŏn-dong XX, Kyŏngsŏng-bu."
"Name?"
"Min Hye-yŏng."
"Age?"
"Twenty."
"What is your father's name?"
"Min Yŏngch'ŏl."
"What? Min Yŏngch'ŏl?"
The inspector suddenly raised his voice, asking again<67>
"You mean Baron Min Yŏngch'ŏl?"
And when Hye-yŏng quietly replied,
"Yes-." he nodded again, saying,
"Hmm."
"So, what about these leaflets?"
"I don't know anything about them."
"What do you mean you don't know? They came out of your desk; if you don't know, who would?"
"But what can I say other than I don't know, when I don't know?"
"Don't lie! The police station is not a place to be fooled by a girl like you, so you must tell the truth exactly as it is. Otherwise, you'll be in big trouble?"
The inspector's voice gradually took on an angry tone.
"You should speak obediently when asked like this. Otherwise, you'll face serious consequences, so understand that."
"..."
"So. Who gave you these leaflets?"
"No matter how much you ask, I really don't know. When I came to school this morning and put my book bundle in, there was nothing there, but later I saw they were inside. How would I know who did what?"
"But since these came out of your desk, it's no use pretending you don't know anything now. Think about it. Your father received the title of Baron for his meritorious service during the Japan-Korea annexation. Wouldn't it be rather embarrassing for your father if the daughter of such an honorable man distributed leaflets like this? Even if you really did this, we'll treat you with special leniency considering your father's face, so wouldn't it be better to tell the truth quickly?"
The smooth-talking inspector tried coaxing her like this as well. But how could she admit to something she knew nothing about from the start? Hye-yŏng denied it to the end.
"So. You really don't know?"
"I don't know."
"You absolutely don't know?"
"No matter what, I don't know."
Hye-yŏng soon started sobbing.
"Shōganai yatsu da." (He's a helpless fellow.)
The interrogating inspector, seemingly giving up, left Hye-yŏng alone in the bare interrogation room and went outside.
Hye-yŏng, the only daughter of Baron Min, who had never known the hardships of the world until suddenly facing this situation, could only weep out of shock and fear.
"What will happen?"
"Will I go to prison?"
"But surely not?"
"Father will do something."
While Hye-yŏng was thinking such thoughts with a pounding heart, in the chief's office next door, Baron Min, who had rushed over after receiving a phone call, was indeed discussing something with the inspector who had interrogated Hye-yŏng.
"So? While we don't necessarily think Your Excellency's daughter is connected to this leaflet incident, since many leaflets were found in her desk, we can't help but be suspicious."
"But surely my daughter couldn't possibly be involved in such a thing? Besides, didn't she firmly deny the facts? So, cut the chatter and release her. If anything happens to her, I, old Min Yŏngch'ŏl, will take responsibility..."
The first statement was the inspector's, the latter Baron Min's.
Thus, Hye-yŏng, who had fallen into a trap, was released safely that same night without having to endure the smelly detention cell, thanks to her father being a Baron who had rendered meritorious service during the annexation.

"Young Mistress! A letter has arrived!"
The morning two days later—feeling somewhat under the weather, Hye-yŏng had decided not to go to school and had just gotten out of bed around ten o'clock when she received a letter from the female servant from the servants' quarters.<68> The sender was Yi Myŏngja, a student in the same class at the same school. Opening the envelope, she found the following message written in pencil.
"Dear Hye-yŏng ŏnni,
You must have been very surprised earlier. How painful and heart-wrenching it was for me to see you being taken away by those people! And I felt so sorry I wished I could find a mouse hole to crawl into. Why? Please forgive me, ŏnni. It was I who put the leaflets in your desk. You might be very surprised to hear this, but it is the absolute truth. I had a certain mission to distribute them, but I got caught midway, failed to achieve my goal, and couldn't manage the remaining ones, so I unavoidably put them in your desk. Because of this, you suffered a temporary disgrace, but unlike our family, I suppose nothing serious will happen to you. I am now on my way, seeking a distant northern land for a more important purpose. What is the important purpose, you ask? That is something you, ŏnni, who grew up like a doll (please forgive the rudeness), would not yet understand. However, please understand just one thing: my lifelong wish is to work for the most numerous unfortunate people in the world, or for the most numerous weak people in the world. And one more thing to remember is that only when everyone in society becomes happy can oneself become truly happy; one's own self can never be happy when everyone else has fallen into misfortune. Think about it. Right now, ŏnni, thanks to your father, you are living happily in fine clothes and food, but how long will that last? Even if almost all of us end up starving on the streets, will ŏnni's family alone really prosper? In the end, everything is a matter of time. There is only the difference between X-ing sooner and X-ing later. Therefore, whether it's ŏnni's family with much wealth and high status, or my poor family without a single penny, misfortune is ultimately the same for both. Then what should we do? There is only one way for all of us to become happy together. That is, for the weak and the weak to join together and become one great force – isn't this single thing the driving force that will make us happy? They say that even weak arrows, when bundled together by ten, cannot be broken. Though we are weak, if we unite, we can become one great force and decide our own affairs. Anyway, I am leaving this place for a while now to become a worker in the task of making the many unfortunate people happy. For the sake of the work to make the most numerous unfortunate people in the world happy, there are no parents, no siblings, no homeland... Anyway, please forgive what happened earlier. It's not that I have no intention of turning myself in soon, but for the sake of a greater task, I must cherish my body for a while longer, so I am leaving like this..."
Reading this, Hye-yŏng felt she had finally solved the puzzle of the leaflet incident. But Yi Myŏngja?... Although she knew Yi Myŏngja studied hard while living frugally because her family was poor, she had never dreamed that Yi Myŏngja, who was a month younger than her, could have done such a thing. And when she read the passage about going to work for the many unfortunate people, her heart somehow fluttered and pounded. Then, that evening's newspaper reported something even more frightening. It was an article titled "Sinŭiju Special Dispatch." "A young woman named Yi Myŏngja, a student at Seoul's R Girls' School who is also connected to the Sin'uhoe, was arrested by Sinŭiju authorities while crossing the border with two suspicious-looking young men. Upon investigation, it was determined that they are major criminals also involved in the Seoul XX leaflet incident."
Seeing this newspaper article, Hye-yŏng was startled once again.<69>
"See! These days, schoolgirls have horns growing out of their backsides, it's worrying. Instead of studying properly, they foolishly run around doing who knows what, ruining their lives. How nice that looks!"
Baron Min, who had also read the newspaper article about Yi Myŏngja, flushed visibly and scolded his daughter like this.

It was on a summer day, less than half a year after that incident, that Baron Min Yŏngch'ŏl's only daughter, Min Hye-yŏng, suddenly went missing. At that time, Baron Min, having spent all the tens of thousands of gold coins he received when granted his title—money that seemed like it would last a lifetime—was facing a rapid decline in family fortunes. He had been planning to barely salvage the situation by marrying off his only daughter Hye-yŏng to a wealthy man in the Yŏngnam region, so his degree of disappointment was even greater. And after Hye-yŏng suddenly left home—in her room, there was a letter that read:
"I absolutely cannot enter into a political marriage of convenience. And for us, there is a more important task that must be done before things like marriage, so I especially cannot marry so quickly. Therefore, I am leaving your side for a while to make the most numerous unfortunate people in the world happy, and consequently, to make myself happy too. Please forgive my lack of filial piety."
Along with this letter, the following separate verse of a song was written:
All the cripples of the world
Who cannot even move freely within their own homes
All the cripples of the world
Clasp each other's hands firmly, shake hands tightly
Whether a thousand or ten thousand
One foot planted on this ground
One arm holding the XX
Let's advance, let's advance, forward, forward
Advancing together, united
What is there to fear?
Cripples, unite!
Become whole people this way!
"Damn it! That wicked girl. How perverse..."
Seeing this, Baron Min's anger flared to the heavens. He had never dreamed that Hye-yŏng, whom he still saw as just a child, would take such action. Moreover, thinking about how this would instantly shatter his own crucial plans for the future made his fury grow even stronger. But in the next moment, as if resigned that "the force of the times cannot be helped," he couldn't help but let out a long sigh to himself.
"Times have changed!"
He muttered again in his heart.

Rosa of Pukch'on—where has she gone now? Nobody knows. But there can be no doubt that she is somewhere, working to cut the X of the pitiful cripples.<70>

**Summary**

The story, set in 1931 Seoul, revolves around Min Hye-yŏng, the daughter of a Korean Baron. When seditious leaflets are found in her school desk, she is arrested but quickly released due to her father's status. She later receives a letter from her classmate, Yi Myŏngja, confessing she planted the leaflets as part of her mission to work for "unfortunate people" before fleeing north (and being subsequently arrested). Disillusioned by her family's declining fortune, her father's plan for her arranged marriage, and inspired by Myŏngja's actions and ideals, Hye-yŏng leaves home herself, leaving behind a letter explaining her desire to work for social justice and a revolutionary poem urging unity among the oppressed ("cripples").

This story offers insight into Korean society under Japanese rule. It highlights the involvement of young female students in clandestine anti-colonial or socialist movements (indicated by leaflets, secret missions, mentions of helping the weak, the Sin'uhoe connection, and potential censorship "XX"). It contrasts the privilege and eventual decline of the collaborator aristocracy (Baron Min) with the growing radicalism among youth. The reference to "Rosa of Pukch'on" possibly links local activism to international figures like Rosa Luxemburg, suggesting an awareness of global revolutionary ideas. The narrative also touches upon the conflict between traditional values (filial piety, arranged marriage) and emerging modern ideals of individual purpose and social change among young women.

봄이라기는 아즉 일고 겨을이라기에는 좀 느진 어느날 아츰의 일이엿다.
C洞에 잇는 R여학교에서는 별안간 소동이 이러낫다. 그것은 누구의 소위인지 수십장의 불온문서를 뿌리고 간 자가 잇서 학교안이 발큰 뒤집힌 것이다.

이것을 처음 발견하기는 학교의 사환으로 잇는 김첨지라는 로인인데 문자를 볼 줄 모르는 김첨지는 그리 대수롭게 녁이지 안코 그대로 찌저 업새려 하얏섯다. 그러나 마츰 변소에 왓다가 이를 보게 된 어떤 교원 하나 이 무슨 천하의 일대사나 생긴 거와 가티 뜀박질을 하야가며 교무주임과 교장에게 보고를 한 까닭에 문제느 필경 크게 버러지고야 마럿다.
학교 당국의 전화로 황급히 달려온 수십 명의 경관대는 범인은 확실히 학교 안에 잇다는 것을 주장하야 각 학급의 수사를 시작하얏다.
제 일학년으로부터 제 이학년에 제 이학년으로부터 제 삼학년에―그러나 학생들의 책상 속에는 아무 이상도 업섯다. 눈이 벌개진 경관대는 벌벌 떠는 학생들을 본체만체하고 이번에는 또 제 사학년을 뒤지기 시작하얏다. 그러타. 제 사년―어떤 학생의 책상 속에서 과연 백여 장의 격문이 나타낫다.
『여기 잇다. 여기 잇서!』
마티 무슨 먹을거나 발견한 것과도 가티 경관들이 이처럼 소리 칠 제 이모통이 저모통이 학생들의 입에서는 또 『아이그 엇저면 蕙英이 책상에서...』 소리가 일제히 흘러나온다.
