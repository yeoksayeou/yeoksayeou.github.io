**Body Text Translation:**
A Collection of Scribbles by Various Visitors to the Company Office (No Particular Order)

Follow the 『Hundred Generations of Clear Wind (百世淸風)』
Hŏ Hŏn (許憲)

Sometimes listening, sometimes speaking, the heart still trembles,
Calling out loudly, singing long, the intention is renewed.
Indulging in poetry while drunk brings deep, deep regret,
Good and evil are manifold, the truth not yet seen.
Sŏ Chŏng-hŭi (徐廷禧)

Coming to the company office today, I heard about Mr. So-and-so's affair, and I want to shout, "Oh, how magnificent!" Ah! Such wealth and nobility.<125>
Wŏn Se-hun (元世勳)

Climbing up to the 『Samch'ŏlli (三千里)』 building, Changan Taero (長安大路) connects to the heavens!
Kim Yak-su (金若水)

Shall we call the Samch'ŏlli Sa building at the Chongno intersection "The Air Clipper (에-이스크립퍼) of Fantasy"?
Hong Yang-myŏng (洪陽明)

Sitting alone in a high room—the spirit of a hero.
Chu Yo-han (朱耀翰)

Looking down at the people coming and going from the 3rd floor of Samch'ŏlli, what always looks lively is the stride of the new women. Men's spirits are dead, their shoulders slumped, but where does this vitality of women alone come from?
They say it's because men exhaust their energy dressing up their wives in blouses and skirts, even if they are made of rayon (人造絹), that they look that way.
Kim Kyŏng-jae (金璟載)

The Samch'ŏlli Sa office space looks to be roughly (얼는 目측해서) about 420 square *cha* (方尺); how about using just 200 square *cha* of that differently? That is to say, let's make it the Chosŏn Central Headquarters for the "Lumpen Intelligentsia (룸폔, 인테리겐챠)". Who knows, perhaps something might come out of it! Just as George Sand (조-지, 싼드) and Musset (뭣셰) and other writers and philosophers of that time<126> gathered in one place for conversation and discussion, calling it a "Salon (싸롱)", and truly great philosophy and literature came out of those "Salons"—if you don't like the sound of "Lumpen Intelligentsia Headquarters," let's just call it the Chongno "Salon".
Yi Chŏng-sŏp (李晶燮)

The weather's nice (日氣 죠의). Let's have a drink and look down upon Seoul.
Looking down from the 3rd floor of Samch'ŏlli—you need to have had a drink to really do it.
Tong-in told me to sign, so here it is.
An Sŏ (岸曙)

"Scribble (낙서)" means to dash off some writing, but sitting here starving until 4 or 5 in the afternoon, do I have the courage to squeeze out my circumstances again?
If only I had a drink!
Hey, P'a-in (파인)! You intensely love the simple spirit of the robust rural fellows, don't you! Think again, think thrice—what about it?
Chŏk Ku (赤拘)

Perhaps because it's the editorial office of Samch'ŏlli Sa, it's right on the 3rd-floor peak in the middle of Chongno. It's a bit difficult to climb up when you've skipped lunch, but anyway, the pleasure of climbing up and looking down is like the pleasure of looking across the Samch'ŏlli (Three Thousand *Ri*).<127>
Looking down, the streetcars, automobiles, bicycles, men, and women are all busily coming and going, so why am I idly singing this sorrowful old song? But when I think about it, my head is indescribably busy! Are the fragments of fleeting thoughts (想華), breaking off and then suddenly connecting, destined to just fall like scattered blossoms (落花) and disappear?
Pak Yŏng-hŭi (朴英熙)

Writing for Free? Truly Flat/Plain (眞平), No Thanks! (眞平屋 = Naengmyeon Restaurant)
Kim Tong-in (金東仁)

Spring comes to the human world, fragrance plays with color.
Paek Hwa (白華)

Looking outside from the Samch'ŏlli editorial department, the tall buildings seem to pierce the clouds, their energy magnificent, but pitiful crowds in their ragged clothes walk along the asphalt (애스팔트). I too feel anxious, and my stomach is empty (배가 곺다).
No Ch'un-sŏng (盧春城)<128>

**Summary**

This article compiles short, informal entries written by various intellectuals and writers visiting the Samch'ŏlli magazine office in June 1934. The contributors offer diverse observations and reflections: philosophical musings, comments on the bustling Chongno street scene viewed from the office window, social commentary on gender dynamics and appearances, a proposal to turn part of the office into a "salon" for the "lumpen intelligentsia," complaints about hunger or the desire for a drink, and brief poetic or cynical remarks. The entries provide a glimpse into the thoughts, moods, and everyday concerns of Korean intellectuals in colonial Seoul. For historians, the article is interesting for its candid portrayal of intellectual life, the casual references to poverty and social critique (like Kim Kyŏng-jae's comment on men's perceived lack of vitality or No Ch'un-sŏng's contrast of tall buildings and ragged people), and the mention of creating a "salon" space, reflecting aspirations for intellectual community and discourse amidst the colonial context.

三千里社 사무소가 얼는 目*해서 420方尺 가량은 되니 그 중 200平方尺만을 달리 사용함이 엇덜가. 즉 그것으로써 朝鮮中央 「룸폔, 인테리겐챠」 총본부로 하잔 말이다. 혹 그에서 무엇이 되어 나올지도 모르지! 『조-지, 싼드』와 『뭣셰』 등 당시의<126> 문인 철학자가 한군데 모아 이야기와 討論을 한데로써 그것을 『싸룡』이라 하엿고 정말 그 『싸롱』에서 훌륭한 철학 문학이 나왓다. 『룸폔, 인테리』 총본부란 말이 듯기 실흐면 종로 『싸롱』이라 하지.
李晶燮

日氣죠의. 한잔 들고 서울을 내려다보세.
三千里 3층에서 내려다보는 것도 한잔 늘고서야 말이지.

한잔 먹엇스면!
