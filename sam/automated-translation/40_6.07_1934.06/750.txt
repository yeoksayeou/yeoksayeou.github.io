This Month's Letter Collection

Respectfully Submitted, For Your Tranquil Eyes,
Regarding the recent request for a Chinese poem (漢詩) due to a certain feeling, I have absolutely no grounding in Chinese poetry, so there is no way for me to comply. I ask for your understanding.
P.S. I also gratefully read the May issue of your esteemed magazine. In the introductory remarks titled "Recollections of Figures from the End of the Korean Empire," phrases such as the statement that I "enlisted in the military again" after returning to Korea are completely erroneous, as I have been engaged solely in religious work since my return until the present day. This substitutes another's life story for my own, thereby inviting negative criticism from observers; this is something you should particularly consider. Also, referring to Mr. Yu Kil-chun (兪吉濬氏) as Prime Minister is also a misprint; please issue a correction.
Furthermore, the statement that I associated with Mr. Kim Ok-kyun (金玉均氏) while he was in exile in Tokyo is incorrect. Political figures remember that my period of exile was after Mr. Kim's death. These are errors in the article; please kindly correct them.
Kwŏn Tong-jin
To Mr. Kim Tong-hwan

Respected Sir, The manuscript you requested is extremely troublesome. However, as it is a major issue that cannot be ignored, I am sending it separately, so please receive it. If I were to put it in a single phrase, "Originally, common insects fly far away from the smell of gunpowder, and in fearsome situations, various petty individuals scurry for cover like rats." If that is still unclear, perhaps one should just offer an exclamation like "Under the bright moon and sparse stars, the magpie flies south." In any case, it seems he sought refuge within the XX-powered carapace, using this opportunity. I end my words here and pray for your well-being and tranquility.
Kim Yak-su
To Mr. Kim Tong-hwan

Early Summer
I received your esteemed instructions today, the day before the deadline, transferred from elsewhere. In any case, complying with your esteemed request this time is almost impossible. As for myself, (abridged) I am a person living such-and-such a life. For such a person to write a poem under that theme—it is utterly impossible, and even if I were to write something that could pass, it would surely be off-topic. Thus, it is impossible either way. Overall, I think this time's idea was not well-conceived. Respectfully,
15th day, 3rd month, Kapsul year
I hope the manuscript titled "To the Concerned Personage" (有憂의 人士에 與함) is returned without being lost.

To Esteemed Elder Brother Kim Tong-hwan

Readers saw the love poem of the famous Sŏngch'ŏn (成川) kisaeng Puryong (芙蓉) on page 132 of the published May issue. Regarding this poem, I will speak as I have heard. This poem consists of 18 stanzas (次第), but it seems the 16th and 18th couplets were not included. The two couplets are as follows, and since it is Puryong's love poem, it is said there is also an 18-stanza response from her counterpart. Please publish that in the next issue.
16. Waiting a thousand leagues for someone is hard, waiting is hard; is the gentleman's affection so fickle?
Leaving the gate three times to look, leaving to look; ask her, what is the maiden's resentment?
18. Sincere joy, broad-mindedness, a good gentleman; crossing the river with delight, new affection under the candlelight, happily facing each other.
Do not let a frail young woman return to heaven bearing resentment; may her sorrowful spirit not follow you under the moon.
Addendum: Thinking without thinking, forcibly climbing Moranbong Peak (牧丹峯); alas, crimson beauty ages, trying to forget is hard, determination is strong (floating work) Blue Pavilion (碧樓) "Always grieving the green's* decay" (*衰), sadly chanting a white-haired poem. This is how I heard and understood it.
Kim Wŏn-jo, Ulsan (蔚山), North Kyŏngsang Province (慶北)

Respectfully Replying, I duly received your esteemed letter dated April 23rd. As the master does not compose poetry, we cannot meet your esteemed requirement. Please understand with generosity.
Kyŏngsŏng-bu Sungin-dong 81
Pak Yŏng-hyo (朴泳孝) Family Office
To Mr. Kim Tong-hwan

Received your gracious letter, replying. Apologies for the delay.
I received both copies of your esteemed magazine and pray from afar for the development of "Samch'ŏlli." Since the day Mr. Pak passed away, I have been confined to bed, diagnosed with pleurisy, and have been suffering for three months now. Fortunately, having found the excellent Dr. Kim, although I wasn't hospitalized, I received daily treatment, achieving results better than hospitalization. Although still bedridden, I have recently been able to move slightly and barely manage to hold a pen for correspondence. I am thinking of perhaps going out after about a week. These days, while lying down, I gaze at the sunlight and plants outside the garden with the joy of a small child. I am now keenly feeling the necessity of health, which I used to neglect. I sympathize with your magazine's new plans, and though I intend to write the manuscript you mentioned, my energy does not yet permit it. However, once I fully recover, if the thought occurs, I will write and send it immediately. Perhaps next month.
I pray for your continued good health.
Yi Hyo-sŏk
To Mr. Kim Tong-hwan

Respectfully, In this season of spring flowers, I pray for the ever-increasing prosperity of your esteemed company and shout 'Hurrah!' with raised hands for the continued publication of Samch'ŏlli.
Elder Brother, please strive hard for Samch'ŏlli and elevate the comprehensive, or rather, literary magazine we anticipate to a high standard.
Last year-end, I was very disappointed not to meet you (when I went up to Seoul) as you were traveling.
Thank you very much for worrying about my health.<180> I have been bedridden since December and am still completely abstaining from food and drink due to a stomach ailment. I barely manage to move and sustain myself by consuming small amounts of nutritious substances daily. I am indescribably weak. On top of that, after finishing "Rainbow" and writing a novel for Tokyo, my nerves have also weakened. However, through my indomitable fight against illness, I am gradually recovering, so please do not worry at all. Believe me, I will certainly not die before producing a masterpiece in our language.
There is no particular reason why I must go to the Tonga Ilbo. Sending regards via letter is sufficient.
I dearly wish to attend your company's lecture meeting this time, but as mentioned above, it is unavoidable. I trust, Elder Brother, that you will forgive me for the sake of my future.
Also, I must write for the May and June issues of a certain Tokyo magazine, and a creative piece for another major magazine by the end of the month. Whipping my weak body, I am struggling to write 4 or 5 pages a day. However, I am determined to write for Samch'ŏlli, so I will write and send something when the opportunity arises later.
Please generously forgive just this. As I discussed with Mr. Yi Kŭn-mu, I will actively support Samch'ŏlli.
(Around the 26th of this month, my short story "(A Man Called Kwŏn)" will be published by Kaizōsha (改造社). It includes 7 pieces such as "Garbo" (がルボウ). However, "Pursued People" (奮起者 - The Roused) was banned and removed. Price: One Yen).
I will make my first trip to Tokyo in early May. I will return home in late May or early June (health permitting).
I hope for an opportunity to meet you, and please work steadily to produce a magazine comparable to Kaizō.
Please convey my regards to Messrs. Pak Yŏng-hŭi, Yu Chin-o, and Kim Pyŏng-je.
Chang Hyŏk-chu
To Mr. Kim Tong-hwan

Respectfully Conveying Deepest Regards, I have received your repeated kind attentions but failed to send thanks, causing me much trepidation and shame. I humbly present one piece, "The Provincial Governor"; the writing is clumsy and the brushwork rough. If it is unusable, discarding it would also be fortunate. There are matters I should discuss in person, but circumstances prevent it, so I send this message instead.
Hong Mok-ch'un

It has been quite some time since I received your letter.
I was very grateful. Due to being bedridden for a long time, I cannot offer anything worth seeing, but merely rummaged through old manuscripts and transcribed something suitable. This is only to avoid going against your wishes; please forgive me.
I pray for your distant well-being and the fine growth of Samch'ŏlli.
May 14th, Afternoon, from Ch'ŏnggup'o
Sin Sŏk-chŏng

Respectfully, Although it has been some days since receiving your order, amid extreme busyness, not only has the deadline passed, but I originally do not compose poetry. Moreover, forcing thoughts merely for the sake of writing poetry, without it coming from true feeling, is something I cannot do.
Kim Ch'ang-je<181>

**Summary**

This collection comprises letters sent to Kim Tong-hwan, likely the editor of *Samch'ŏlli* magazine, in mid-1934. Several prominent figures respond to requests for contributions, mostly declining due to lack of expertise (Kwŏn Tong-jin, Pak Yŏng-hyo's office, Kim Ch'ang-je), prior commitments, or poor health (Yi Hyo-sŏk, Chang Hyŏk-chu, Sin Sŏk-chŏng). Some letters offer corrections to previous articles, notably Kwŏn Tong-jin refuting claims about his post-return activities and association with Kim Ok-kyun, and Sŏl T'ae-hŭi providing errata for his own article. Others discuss literary matters, like Kim Wŏn-jo adding missing verses to a kisaeng's poem and requesting its counterpart, or Chang Hyŏk-chu mentioning his recent publications and upcoming trip to Tokyo. The letters provide glimpses into the writers' personal lives, health struggles, and engagement with the magazine and the broader literary/political scene. For historians, these letters offer valuable insights into the networks and interactions among Korean intellectuals during the colonial period, the operational aspects of a major cultural magazine like *Samch'ŏlli* (soliciting content, handling corrections), personal health challenges faced by writers, and direct commentary on historical facts and figures (like Yu Kil-chun and Kim Ok-kyun) as perceived by contemporaries. Chang Hyŏk-chu's letter is particularly revealing about the pressures of writing for both Korean and Japanese publications, including dealing with censorship ('Pursued People' being banned).
