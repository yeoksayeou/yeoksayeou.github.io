기사형태	문예기타 (Literary Miscellaneous)

예루살넴을 떠나며
						德富蘆花

한번을 예루살넴에서 安息日을 마젓다, 來日은 나자레 데베리아 湖水를 向하려 생각하먜 종일 집에 드러 업듸어 짐을 꾸리다.

橄欖山의 저녁 빗은 아름다워 꿈가튼 예루살넴, 등불이 반작 어린다. 4,000餘里 東方으로부터 와서 놉흐신 구름자도 집지 못하고 이제 벌서 北으로 가려한다.

覺悟한 바이지만 새삼스러히 俗에 又 俗된 「예루살넴」이여 하고 失望한 나는 떠나려하매 뒤가 끌닌다. 등불아래, 客譜冊쓰기를

「엇재서 죽은 者의 사이에서 산 자를 차즈려 하는고, 그는 여기 잇지 안코, 다시 復活하섯 네」하고 主께서 말슴하섯다.

내왓다, 와서 보앗노라 1900年은 지나갓다, 그리스도는 게시지 안코, 다만 남어잇는 것은 하늘의 푸름, 힌뫼 그리고 그저 함부로 예루살넴의 王이 되려 하는 人子의 鄙劣뿐이다」

明治 39年 6月 13日 日記에서<117>

**English Translation**

Leaving Jerusalem
						Tokutomi Roka

Once, I observed the Sabbath in Jerusalem. Thinking of heading towards Nazareth and Lake Tiberias tomorrow, I stayed indoors all day, lying down and packing my luggage.

The evening light on the Mount of Olives is beautiful; dreamlike Jerusalem, where lamps begin to twinkle. Having come over 4,000 *ri* from the East (東方), unable even to grasp His lofty shadow, I am now already preparing to go North.

Although it was something I had anticipated, I feel disappointed anew, thinking, "Oh, Jerusalem, worldly and yet more worldly!" As I prepare to leave, I feel a lingering attachment. Beneath the lamp, I write in my guestbook:

"Why do you seek the living among the dead? He is not here; He has risen again," the Lord said.

I came, I came and saw. 1900 years have passed. Christ is not here. All that remains is the blue of the sky, the white mountains, and simply the baseness (鄙劣) of the Son of Man who recklessly tries to become the King of Jerusalem.

From the diary entry of Meiji 39 (1906), June 13th<117>

**Summary**

The author, Japanese writer Tokutomi Roka, reflects on his departure from Jerusalem after a pilgrimage. He finds the contemporary city disappointing and "worldly," contrasting it with the spiritual ideal associated with Christ. While acknowledging the scriptural message of the Resurrection ("He is not here; He has risen"), he laments that after 1900 years, what remains is nature and the "baseness" of human ambition vying for power in the holy city. For historians studying colonial Korea, the publication of a Japanese intellectual's travelogue about a Christian holy site in a popular Korean magazine like *Samch'ŏlli* is noteworthy. It reflects the complex cultural dynamics of the period, showing the circulation of Japanese cultural products and ideas in Korea, while the theme of disillusionment with a revered place might have resonated indirectly with Korean readers experiencing their own political and cultural predicaments under colonial rule.
