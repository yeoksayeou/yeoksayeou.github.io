Strange Tales of a Summer Night

The Strange Incident in the Sericulture Room

Paengnangsong

K Agricultural School is located within the grounds of the Kongja Myo (孔子廟 - Confucian Shrine) on the back hill, a little ways away from our town. The lecture hall (講室), where scholars once gathered like clouds from all directions to read Confucius and Mencius, has now been renovated with glass windows and wooden floors, becoming quite a Western-influenced classroom. Next to the classroom, what was likely a storehouse (꼬간) for ritual vessels (祭器) used during the spring and autumn ancestral rites (春秋祭享 - *likely typo in original for 祭享*), was also remodeled into a spacious room designated as the sericulture room (養蠶室).
At that time, there were 32 students in the second year of our school. Being far from Sŏul, students came from over ten counties, treating the agricultural school like a middle school, so most were living away from home. Their ages were generally high, mostly over twenty. Among them were several students aged twenty-five, twenty-six, or even thirty.

The silkworms were about to enter their third molt (第三眠) and needed to be moved onto the silkworm trays (蠶箔). Even after just one night's sleep, the white, silver-like silkworms grew noticeably larger.
That day, all the students went out to the practice farm (實習農園) early in the morning with hoes and shovels to tend to the neighboring mulberry field (桑田), which was dark brown.<155>
We returned late that day, marching in rows back to the dormitory (긔숙사). Everyone was exhausted. Although we had eaten lunch, we were hungry. Our stomachs growled with appetite (食慾).
Returning utterly drained, just as we entered the main gate and were about to go into the sericulture room (養蠶室) next to the main hall (大成殿) of the Kongja Myo, the student named S who was at the front shouted,
"Ack! Someone's going in there!"
We had securely locked the sericulture room and fastened the latch when we left for the mulberry field in the morning. There was no reason for the door to be open, nor for anyone to be entering the sericulture room.
It was a summer night, nearly the end of August, and in the sweltering heat, hearing that shout made cold sweat break out on our bodies.
"Where? Where!"
"Just now, a man in white clothes slipped right through that door."
"Right, I saw him too. Definitely a person!"
According to the students who saw him, a very tall man dressed all in white walked across the wooden floor and went straight through the door near the dormitory over there.
Even before this, strange ghost stories (怪談) had been circulating around the school dormitory. One student claimed that while going to the latrine early one morning a few days ago, something white stood tall by the pillar of the main hall, and when he shouted in surprise, it vanished.
Another student reported seeing something white standing tall on the roof of the lecture hall (講堂) when returning late one moonless, pitch-dark night after watching moving pictures in town.
Teacher Sakada (坂田敎師), attributing it all to the illusions (幻像) of youth who enjoy dreams and strange things (怪異), just laughed it off. Then he gathered the murmuring students and lectured them, saying,
"In modern times, there are no such things as ghosts (鬼神). Believe in science (科學)."
Yet, today again, a white figure was seen disappearing like a cloud into the sericulture room – and not just by one person, but by three or four students in the front row (前列).
All the students trembled with an unknown sense of foreboding (不吉) and fear.
Among them, three or four older students and pranksters shouted,
"Eh, you cowards!"
They pushed forward, ran to the door where the figure had supposedly entered, and yanked it forcefully.
However, the door remained closed and wouldn't open. Just then, Teacher Sakada also returned from the practice field, still carrying his teaching pointer.<156>
Hearing the story, Teacher Sakada sneered again, stepped forward alone, took out the key, and opened the locked door. He boldly strode inside, went through each room, flicked on the light switches, and illuminated the place brightly.
"Alright, everyone come in. Is there anything here besides silkworms?"
He smiled. The students, who had been standing frozen in a circle in the sericulture room yard wondering what was happening, finally looked at each other and laughed along.

However, that night, a strange incident occurred in the sericulture room. The students on duty (당번) from early evening were scheduled to change shifts at midnight.
When it was time for the shift change, one group of students returned to the dormitory, while another group went from the dormitory to the sericulture room.
Among the new shift was the student named Kyŏngsik, from Kwagwan, who had been the first to spot the "white person" earlier that day.
Kyŏngsik was tall with large eyes and was known among the students for being somewhat timid.
It must have been around three in the morning, when the newly arrived duty students were giving the silkworms their first feeding of mulberry leaves.
Kyŏngsik, who was feeding silkworms in Room No. 5 (第五號室) with the other duty students, suddenly cried out,
"Ack!" and collapsed, his tall frame sprawling onto the floor.
"What is it? What is it!"
"That person from before was here. That person!"
He pointed a finger towards a corner of the wall.
The other students, startled, looked towards the wall, but there was nothing there.
However, Kyŏngsik had collapsed in terror. Not only had he collapsed, but white foam frothed at his lips, his eyes rolled back showing only the whites, and his limbs thrashed about like someone dying.
He lost consciousness.
Finally, the dormitory bell was rung in the middle of the night. Sleeping students woke with wide eyes and gathered around, and Teacher Sakada also rushed over, still in his nightwear (네마끼 - nemaki).

Even Teacher Sakada was frightened this time. He took off his top, straddled Kyŏngsik's chest, and began artificial respiration. He ordered another student to fetch cold water from the well in a bucket (빠켓츠 - bucket) and continuously splashed it on Kyŏngsik's face.
The gathered students all shouted together,
"Kyŏngsik! Kyŏngsik!"
In the deserted grounds of the Kongja Myo, like a place devoid of people, the memory of us calling the name of our friend who had fallen into a state of suspended animation (假死狀態) in the dead of night is truly unforgettable even now.
Outside was silent. Inside was silent. Teacher Sakada, perhaps finally scared himself, his face<157> pale, continued the artificial respiration, thick sweat dripping from his forehead.
After about 30 minutes of this, Kyŏngsik finally woke up, as if stirring from a light sleep.
We all breathed a sigh of relief.
Kyŏngsik stood up, looking dazed, like someone who had lost their mind.

After this incident, Kyŏngsik's health deteriorated. He lay ill in the warmest part of the dormitory room for about two weeks before returning to his hometown. That day was a Saturday, and we went to see Kyŏngsik off (천별) at D, a small port town about 5 *ri* from the county seat.
Kyŏngsik, seemingly sad to part from his dear school friends, stood on the deck and continuously waved a white handkerchief.

The next day, we heard sad news.
Kyŏngsik had committed suicide by throwing himself into the sea on his way back home.
This is the strange true story from a summer night that I experienced, already 10 years ago.

The Groom Who Fell from His Horse
Ch'osŏn

This is a tragedy that occurred one summer night some years ago.

In our village, the whole community lived together harmoniously like one big family. Whenever any house had a celebration, the entire village would gather to help make rice cakes and sweet rice drink (甘酒 - kamju).
So, on feast days, the whole village would be filled with harmony and laughter.
It was the day of the celebration for Pyŏngnam (丙南), one of the young men in our village.
That day, Pyŏngnam, dressed in the groom's formal attire (사모관대), mounted a horse, with the *sup'allyŏnkkot* (壽八蓮 - ceremonial flowers) and the wooden goose (木雁 - *kirogi*) carried before him. Shouting the horse-urging cry (권마성) "Ŏ-ŏ-," he left the village for the bride's house. We followed behind the horse to the village entrance. (In our village, there were no cars, so wedding ceremonies were still conducted in the old style.)
Pyŏngnam, who had gone to the lower village to fetch his bride, finally returned to our village around 3 PM,<158> after completing the wedding ceremony (醮禮) at the bride's house, with the bride riding in a palanquin.
Just like before, we went out to the village entrance to greet him.
As it was the happiest day of his life, Pyŏngnam's mouth was stretched in a wide grin, his eyes full of laughter. The bride was heavily adorned with rouge (연지) and powder (분), making it difficult to distinguish her nose from her mouth. She was said to be a renowned beauty in the upper and lower villages, and her willow-leaf-shaped face seemed lovely.
At that moment, there was an old bachelor (총각) among us, known for being the biggest joker, chatterbox (떠버리), entertainer, and brawler. Perhaps intending to give everyone a good laugh that day too, he boldly stepped up beside the bride's horse, which was following the groom amidst the horse-urging cries, lifted the covering cloth, peered at the bride's face, and said,
"Auntie, you're pretty!"
The bride blushed and lowered her head. Everyone burst out laughing with a "Wah-". The bachelor, satisfied that we laughed, chuckled "Hahaha" himself, then dashed up beside the groom's horse and shouted,
"You lucky guy! Butcher a cow!"
Just then, unfortunately, the horse bolted for some reason, and the groom tumbled head over heels, falling to the ground.
A village elder (존위) standing with us saw this and sighed deeply,
"Oh dear!"
This was because there is an old saying (넷말) passed down in our village that falling from a horse on one's wedding day is extremely unlucky.

That night, the groom, who had entered the bridal chamber (新婦房), disappeared like a cloud around dawn.
The bride waited for a long time, thinking the groom had gone to the latrine (변소), but he didn't return. The groom's mother found it very strange and sent people to his friends' houses in the village to look for him, wondering if the prank-loving village bachelors were playing a trick (신랑 다루기 - *sillang darugi*, traditional hazing of the groom).
However, no matter how much they searched, the groom was nowhere to be found. We, who had been drinking and making noise in the neighboring house, also found it extremely odd and searched everywhere, but could find no trace of him.
A night of anxiety passed.
The next day, and the day after (사흔 날 - third day), we searched the entire village two or three times. But whether he vanished into the sky or sank into the earth, the groom remained unseen.
Then, suspicious rumors began to circulate in the village: that the bride, being so beautiful, must have had a lover before marriage, and had called him over on the first night to kill the groom.
The groom's parents initially paid no attention to the rumors, but as time passed and they grew desperate, finding the idea plausible, they eventually questioned (문초) the bride harshly.<159> The bride vehemently denied (번명) it, as if struck by a bolt from the blue (쳥쳥벽력). She then stopped eating properly and just kept crying.
Since the groom remained missing for so long, the local police substation (주재소) eventually learned of the matter, took the bride into custody, and interrogated her.
However, the bride did not confess, and there wasn't a shred of evidence.
The case truly became completely baffling (오리무중).

As they say, "rumors last a month." After a month passed and the talk began to fade from the village, one day, unexpectedly, the groom reappeared, staggering down from the back mountain, his clothes torn and his face blackened. Imagine the shock of the villagers and the joy of his parents!
And tragically, the man had lost his mind (精神喪失者), unable to remember anything from the past. No matter how much they asked how he ended up on the mountain that evening or how he survived there, he just grinned foolishly and kept trying to go back to the mountain, barely restrained by his family.

The groom who had lost his mind finally regained his senses this summer, three years later.
A forensic psychiatrist (法醫學者) who heard this story commented,
"Thinking about the extremely joyous event of marriage, and then suddenly falling from the horse, likely caused temporary insanity."

The Strange Sound from the Midnight Corpse Room
Tabangok Kŏsa

This happened when I was at K Hospital, perhaps the summer two years ago. The kisaeng Yŏnhŭi (姸姬) died six days after being admitted.
Her illness was tuberculosis (페병). She was tall and slender, with a pale complexion (얼골빗), so I suspected she had a predisposition for tuberculosis. Sure enough, when the doctor saw her, he immediately dismissed her claim,
"It's just a cold."
with
"Even if it's a cold, it's a serious one."
"I'm busy at home, and with so many visitors, I can't rest peacefully. Teacher, please admit me for a few days as a form of convalescence (휴양)."
She added that she would stay here for three or four days and then go to the Sambang mineral springs (약수터) for the summer.<160>
Since she insisted, I couldn't refuse, but I didn't explicitly tell her to stay or leave, just letting her be. Then, she sent her servant to bring her bedding. She had her pajamas and washbasin brought, bustling around and admitting herself all on her own.
That night, a young man came to visit. Assuming he was the lover smitten with Yŏnhŭi, I pretended not to notice as he passed the pharmacy (藥局). The man, also tall like a kisaeng reaching the ceiling beams, was quite plump. He was said to be from Chinnamp'o or Haeju, working for some company in Sŏul. Shortly after he entered the kisaeng's room, perhaps ordered by him, an employee from Mitsukoshi (미스꼬시 - 三越) arrived carrying a fruit box filled with bananas (빠나나), pears (배), and apples.
From inside the room, sounds of playing Hwatu (화토 - flower cards) could be heard late into the night, with calls like "Pine and Crane!" (송학) and "Paulownia!" (오동).
The next morning, I learned from another kisaeng from the same district who came to visit the patient that this man was rumored to have attempted suicide by poison (음독자살) a few years ago after being rejected by Yŏnhŭi while working at some bank. He survived, but now they were deeply involved again, inseparable. She concluded with a pout, clearly disapproving.
The man came every night. Sometimes he had his servant bring a fishbowl containing goldfish for her amusement. He seemed utterly devoted.
The two got along well like this, but on the fourth or fifth day, a sharp, silk-tearing scream suddenly erupted from Room No. 7 (where the kisaeng was staying). I rushed in to find the man, furious, holding a dagger (단도) he must have gotten from somewhere, about to stab the woman. Blood was already flowing from her arm; she had apparently been stabbed once already.
From their argument, it seemed the kisaeng had another lover besides this man, and it had just been discovered.
"Was that farmland in Chŏllado so desirable?"
This suggested she had perhaps ensnared some rich man's son from Chŏllado who had come to Sŏul for cherry blossom viewing (사꾸라 구경), treating him as a prize catch (붕 - 鳳). It seemed this was discovered through letters. Also, hearing him say,
"Wasn't deceiving me once enough? You deceive me again?"
made it clear that this was indeed the situation. Patients from adjacent rooms and nurses, all awakened, came out, and the fight was barely stopped. The man, his eyes bloodshot, trembled violently, muttered to himself for a while, and then left abruptly.

After this incident, the kisaeng's condition worsened rapidly. Her illness took a severe turn. That night, she vomited blood and fell into a coma,<161> dying early the following evening.
She was already terminally ill, but the incident with the man had drastically aggravated her condition.
That night, her body was left in the hospital room behind a folding screen. Her family home was said to be in P'yŏngyang, and a telegram had been sent, but they couldn't arrive before she passed away. Her kisaeng friends were also unreachable, perhaps out gambling (and even if notified, it was doubtful whether any loyal friend would come to watch over a corpse in the middle of the night). So, the body spent the night alone in the room.
Having completed the report to the police, I fell into a deep sleep in the night duty room (숙직실). I was startled awake by some noise. It must have been around two in the morning.
Listening carefully, I heard a quiet, mournful weeping sound, definitely coming faintly from the dimly lit room containing the kisaeng's corpse. No one should have entered the corpse room; what could it be? Goosebumps prickled my skin.
I quietly opened the small side door (새이문) and gently woke the director, Dr. K, who was sleeping. He rubbed his eyes, got up, listened for a moment, and his face turned pale.
Weeping from the corpse room in the dead of the pitch-dark night... ghosts, spirits, demons!
Such ominous thoughts raced through our minds.
However, we couldn't make a big deal out of it. If we made noise and alerted others, the rumor "Ghosts weep at night in Hospital X!" would spread, and the hospital would surely have to close its doors.
Finally, with the director in front and me behind, steeling ourselves, we cleared our throats loudly, approached the corpse room, listened, and then flung the door open. At that moment,
"Ack!"
The doctor in front fell backward.
There... there lay the man who had fought with her and left. Having entered somehow, he was lying beside the corpse, holding it and weeping.
(End)<162>

**Summary**

This article presents three short, eerie tales from 1930s Korea, framed as "Strange Tales of a Summer Night." The first recounts a mysterious sighting of a white figure in a sericulture room at an agricultural school, leading to a student's nervous breakdown and eventual suicide. The second tells of a groom who disappears on his wedding night after an unlucky fall from his horse, only to reappear a month later mentally shattered, eventually recovering years later. The third describes a kisaeng dying of tuberculosis in a hospital, whose distraught lover is later found weeping over her corpse in the middle of the night. From a colonial history perspective, the stories reflect a society grappling with modernization and lingering superstition. The presence of a Japanese teacher ("Sakada") promoting science against belief in ghosts, the mention of modern institutions like hospitals and agricultural schools alongside traditional practices (Confucian shrine, old-style weddings), the social dynamics involving kisaeng and their patrons, and the reference to the police substation ("주재소") offer glimpses into the blend of old and new, Korean and Japanese influences, and social anxieties of the period.

잡지명	삼천리 제6권 제7호
발행일	1934년 06월 01일
기사제목	녀름밤의 怪話
기사형태	문예기타

녀름밤의 怪話
養蠶室의 怪變

白浪星

K농업학교는 우리 고을서 조곰 떠러저 잇는 뒷동산 공자묘(孔子廟) 안에 잇다. 옛날에는 사방에서 구름가치 모아오든 선비들이 공자 맹자를 읽든 강실(講室)을 지금은 뜨더 고처서 유리창도 하여 박고 널마루도 노아서 제법 화양절충의 교실이 되엇다. 교실 엽헤 아마 녯날에는 춘츄제항(春秋祭亭) 지낼 때에 제긔(祭器)를 너튼 꼬간인 듯한 곳을 그 역시 개조하여 너르다란 공실을 만들고 거기를 양잠실(養蛋室)로 정하엿다.
그때 우리 학교 2년생이 모다 32명 서울서 멀니 떠러저 잇는 곳이라 농업학교도 중학교라고 10여 군에서 학생들이 몰려와서 타관 객지 사는 사람이 대부분이엇다. 그리고 년령도 모다 놉하서 스무살 이상이 보통이엿다. 그 중에는 이십오륙 삼십에 나는 학생도 몃치 잇섯다.

누에는 제삼민(第三眠)에 드러가기에 오라지 안어서 잠박(蠶箔)에 올녀야 할 때엿다. 하로 밤만 자고 이러나도 하-얀, 은가튼 누에가 쑥쑥 커 가는 것이 헌저하게 엿다.
학생들은 그날 모다 아츰부터 암갈색(暗渴式)의<155> 이웃 뽕밧(桑田)을 정리하느라고 바구미와 괭이를 들고 실습농원(實習農園)으로 나갓다.
우리들은 그날 늣게야 도로 열을 지어 긔숙사로 도라왓다. 모다 곤하엿다. 점심은 먹엇지만 시장하엿다. 우리들의 배는 식욕(食慾)으로 울엇다.
긔운 한푼업시 도라오니 마구 대문을 드러서서 공자묘 대성전(大成殿) 엽헤 잇는 양잠실(養蠶室)로 드러가려할 때에 압헤 섯든 S라는 학생이
「악! 저기 사람이 들어간다」하고 소리를 첫다. 양잠실은 우리들이 아츰에 뽕밧흐로 갈 때에 꼭꼭 잠그고 열대를 채이고 나갓다. 양잠실 문이 열일 까닭도 업고 양잠실에 사람이 들어갈 수도 업섯든 것이다.
그것이 팔월도 거지반 가는 녀름밤이엇스니 무덥든 더위에 더구나 그 소리를 듯는 우리들 몸에서는 진땀이 옷쑥 뱨젓다.
「어듸 어듸!」
「인재 바로 힌 옷 입는 사내가 저 문 속으로 쑥 드러가드라」
「올타 나도 보앗다. 분명이 사람이다!」
보앗다는 학생의 말에 의하면 키가 훨신 큰 사내가 아래위를 하얏케 입고 저 널마루를 거러 바로 저쪽 긔숙사 엽헤 잇는 문으로 드러가드라 한다.
그러치 안어도 요사히 학교 긔숙사를 싸고 이상한 괴담(怪談)이 도라 다닌다. 어느 학생은 일전 새벽에 변소로 가다가 대성정 귀둥 뉘로 힌 것이 웃둑 섯다가 하도 이상하여 소리를 질넛더니 사라지드라고도 한다.
또 엇든 학생의 말에 의하면 달 업는 침침칠야에 읍에 나려가서 활동사진 구경을 하고 도라오는대 강당(講堂) 지붕 우에 역시 힌 것이 웃둑 서서 잇더란 소문도 낫다.
모다 꿈과 괴이(怪異)를 조와하는 쳥춘들의 일이라 환상(幻像) 때문에 그러하다고 담임 사가다선생(坂田敎師)는 웃고 말엇다. 그리고는
「현대에는 유영(鬼神)라고 업는 것이요. 과학(科學)을 밋으시요」
하고 수선수선하는 학생들을 몰라노고 휸계하엿다.
그리든 까리에 오늘 또 양잠실에 힌 사람이 구름가치 사라지는 것을 보앗다고-그도 한 사람뽄 아니고 압섯 열에(前列) 섯든 학생 삼사명이 보앗다 함이다.
전반학생은 모다 알 수 업는 불길(不吉)과 공포에 떨엇다.
그 중에 나먹은 학생과 작난 조와하는 학생 삼사명은
「에이 뭇난 자식들!」
하고는 압장 서서 지금 들어가드란 그 문 잇는 데로 달려가서 문을 와락 당기어 보앗다.

시체는 그날 밤 평풍을 둘너 병실에서 재우게 하엿다. 본가가 평양이라하야 전보젓스나 임종에 미처 오지 못하엿고 동무 긔생들도 노름 불녀가서 알닐 수도 업서 (알고도 밤중에 송장직혀주려 오는 살틀한 동무가 잇슬는지도 의문이지만) 그 한밤은 시체가 혼자 방에서 작다.
나는 경찰에 보고까지 다 마추고 숙직실에서 곤한 잠을 들엇다가 어대서 무슨 소리에 깜작놀라여 깨엇다. 새벽 두시나 되엿슬가.
가만히 긔를 엿들으니 고요히 늣겨 우는 소리인데 분명히 히미하게 울을 켜노은 긔생시체방에서 난다. 그 시체실에는 아모도 들어간 이가 업슬 터인데 웬일일고 하고 생각하니 소름이 옷속끼엇다.
나는 새이문을 가만이 열고 원장인 K선생 자는 것을 두다려 깨엇다. 선생도 눈을 부비며 이러나서 한참 듯더니 얼골이 팔해진다.
심심칠야 이 한밤중에 시체실에서 우는소리 귀신 유령 악마!
우리 머리우으론 이런 불길한 생각이 각고 지나갓다.
그러나 이 일을 크게 알닐 수는 업다. 엇재서 그런고하니 소리를 내여 남에게 알녓다가는 아모개 병원에선 귀신이 밤중에 운다! 하는 소문만 나면 병원문은 다치는 날이 된다.
