Samch'ŏlli Confidential Room
Political and Ideological Circles Insider Report

Recent Opinions of Tōkyō Politicians Regarding the Chosŏn Problem

Some time ago, private political figures in Tōkyō, such as Mr. Uchida Ryōhei, expressed the following opinion in a document titled "A Plan for the Chosŏn System":
"Moreover, unless the political aspirations of the Korean people are guided appropriately and alleviated through some method, there is always a concern of unrest.
At an appropriate time, a suitable system should be established, granting the right of political participation and self-governance... (remainder omitted - original text)"

Opinion of the Editor-in-Chief of the Seoul Press

A while ago, the editor-in-chief of the English-language newspaper, the Seoul Press, wrote in an article titled "The Past, Present, and Future of Korean Governance": "Anyone who knows how much trouble the British Parliament and Government had because of the Irish Party cannot help but worry that Korean representatives might form a third party and gain enough power to sway the parliament's situation. I believe it would be wiser to prevent this danger by establishing a Chosŏn Assembly."

The Governor-General of Chosŏn's Secret Fund

It is a fact guessed by everyone that there must be a large sum of secret funds (機密費) in the Government-General, used silently without being clearly recorded in the books, but few know exactly how much that amount is.
According to this company's investigation, the secret fund solely in the hands of the Governor-General amounts to 350,000 won per year. This amount was originally 500,000 won during the terms of Governors-General Itō, Terauchi, and Saitō, but it was reduced to 350,000 won during Governor-General Yamanashi Hanzō's time due to budget cuts from the central government. Besides the Governor-General, each Bureau (局) also has some amount, though not much, but it is observed that the secret funds used by the Administrative Superintendent (政務總監) and the Police Affairs Bureau Director (警務局長) likely reach a considerable sum.

Mr. Ch'oe Rin's Annual Salary

Mr. Ch'oe Rin, who recently became an Imperially Appointed Advisor (勅任參議) to the Central Council (中樞院), receives 1,800 won per year. Calculated monthly, this is exactly 150 won; calculated daily, it is 5 won.

Army and Navy Secret Funds

So, the Governor-General of Chosŏn's secret fund is as described above, but how much secret funding is circulating in Tōkyō?
Looking at this year's budget, the government's total secret fund for one year is a staggering 12.7 million won. Among this, the largest spenders are:
Ministry of the Army: 7.3 million won
Ministry of the Navy: 1.71 million won
Following these are the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, Ministry of Home Affairs, etc. It is said that the reason the Ministry of the Army's spending is so high is due to extensive use in subtle areas unknown to the public, following the Manchurian Incident and the Shanghai Incident.

Metropolitan Police Superintendent General's Secret Fund

How much secret funding does the Superintendent General (警視總監) of the Tōkyō Metropolitan Police, the political capital, have? It is said to be 150,000 won annually, and the Director of the Police Security Bureau (警保局長) at the Ministry of Home Affairs receives 50,000 won.
It is difficult to ascertain precisely how much secret funding the Provincial Police Chiefs (各道 警察部長) in Chosŏn use, but according to rumors, in provinces ranked first class for security like Kyŏnggi-do, P'yŏngan-namdo, P'yŏngan-pukto, Hamgyŏng-pukto, and Kyŏngsang-namdo, it is 3,000 won, while in other provinces it is said to be less than 2,000 won.
However, the secret funds mentioned above for the Superintendent General, the Police Security Bureau Director, or the Provincial Police Chiefs are reportedly not used solely by them; a portion is distributed to subordinate Police Station Chiefs (各 署長) for their use.

Female Activists: Where Are They Now?

Ms. Hŏ Chŏngsuk, after serving her sentence for the Kwangju Student Incident, immediately established the Sunlight Therapy Clinic (太陽光線治療所) in P'anpal-tong and became its director, operating sunlamps.
Ms. Sim Ŭnsuk became the wife of the young critic Hong Kimun and is busy managing her household as the young mother of two sons and one daughter.
Ms. Hwang Sindŏk became the wife of Im Pongsun, who was a reporter for the Tong-A Ilbo and an executive of the Sŏul Youth Association, and is raising pigs, cucumbers, and vegetables in Sinsŏl-li outside Tongdaemun Gate.
Ms. Kang Agŭnnia, after serving her sentence, occasionally visits the prison to see her lover Ha P'irwŏn, and was heard expressing a desire to become a car driver, but recent news is unclear.
Ms. Cho Wŏnsuk went to her husband's family home in Cheju-do, raising the beloved child born in Shanghai, helping with fishing, and weeding the vegetable patch.
Ms. Chŏng Ch'ilsŏng went down to Taegu, opened knitting classes, and spent time reading. Recently, like a weeping willow swaying in the spring breeze, she brought her leisurely steps to Sŏul's Chinjukae (Myeongdong area), but her whereabouts have been unknown since.
Chŏng Chongmyŏng is busy going back and forth to Chŏng-dong for trial these days, struggling with the Yongsan Work Association (龍山工作會) case, unsure how many years of imprisonment she might receive.

Mr. Pak Sŏgyun's Self-Funded Trip

It is widely known that Mr. Pak Sŏgyun, the president of the Maeil Sinbo immediately preceding Mr. Yi Sanghyŏp, accompanied Saitō (齋藤, current Prime Minister), who was the Governor-General of Chosŏn at the time, to England for the 1930 London Naval Conference. However, it is said that although Mr. Pak was an attendant (隨員) at this naval conference, he paid his travel and expenses himself. Afterwards, he resigned as Vice President of the Maeil Sinbo and wandered around Kando and Manchukuo, and it is reported he recently returned to Sŏul. According to one knowledgeable source, the trip to London must have cost at least 7,000-8,000 won. Considering the examples of Mr. Hŏ Hŏn, Mr. Kim Sŏngsu, or Mr. Ch'oe Rin, who spent at least several tens of thousands of won each on a trip around the world, it seems a correct observation that it wouldn't have cost less than that.

Dr. Pak Ch'anghun and Research Funds

When Dr. Pak Ch'anghun was at Kyŏngsŏng Medical College (京城醫學專門學校), before it became Kyŏngsŏng Imperial University, Dr. Tokumitsu (德光), an authority in pathology, went to Germany for research. At that time, Director Shiga Kiyoshi (志賀潔) specifically recommended Dr. Pak Ch'anghun from among the cloud-like gathering of talented scholars to head the pathology department. At that time, Dr. Pak was provided with 8,000 won for pathology expenses. With this in hand, Dr. Pak laid the foundation for his great success today through cadaver dissection and pathological research.
On another note, the longest doctoral dissertation in all of Japan is said to be that of Dr. Matsushita (松下) of Kyōto, who is now a member of the Diet. The second longest is the dissertation "An Anthropological Study of Koreans" by Dr. Kubo (久保) of Kyŏngsŏng Medical College, famous for the skull incident (頭蓋骨事件). Dr. Pak Ch'anghun's dissertation was also quite long, at 1,500 pages.

Academic Affairs Bureau Director's Invitation to Writers

On May 4th, invitations came in the name of Watanabe Toyohiko (渡邊豊日子), Director of the Academic Affairs Bureau (學務局長) of the Government-General, addressed to writers, painters, and musicians.
"Please come to the Unhyŏn Palace Annex (雲峴宮 別莊) outside Chahamun Gate on May 5th."
Receiving this invitation caused an unexpected stir in the literary, music, and art circles. Some speculated: Was this an attempt to create a literary control organization, just as Matsumoto *hō kyokuchō (?) in Tōkyō called upon Kikuchi Kan, the late Naoki Sanjūgo, and popular novelist Shirai Kyōji? Or was it to discuss including writers' works in the newly compiled high school textbooks? Or, since the location was the Unhyŏn Palace Annex, was it perhaps to reward a writer who wrote a novel based on Unhyŏn Palace with a monetary gift (金一封)? Such rumors circulated.
The gentlemen who bathed in the glory of the invitation on that day were as follows:
From the literary world: Ch'oe Namsŏn, Yun Paengnam, Kim Soun, Yi Kwangsu, Yi Ŭnsang, Kim Tongin, Chu Yohan (7 people).<17>
From the music world: Pak Kyŏngho, Hyŏn Chemyŏng, Kim Yŏnghwan (3 people).
From the art world: Yi Hanbok, Ko Hŭidong, Kim Yongjin, Ch'oe Usŏk (4 people).
From the social sphere: Yi Chŏngsŏp, Son Chint'ae (2 people).

Finally, the appointed day, May 5th, arrived at 1:00 PM sharp. Cars from the Government-General and the Central Council were sent beforehand to the residences of the invitees to escort them respectfully, so all the aforementioned individuals entered the Unhyŏn Palace Annex on time.
Hyŏn Chemyŏng and a few others even donned formal frock coats ("후록코-드"). Some had fresh haircuts, others wore new clothes.
Only two invited individuals, Kim Yŏnghwan and Yi Ŭnsang, were absent due to unforeseen circumstances; all others were seated.

Advertising Rates of Ōsaka Mainichi and Asahi

In Chosŏn, the advertising rates for Tong-A Ilbo, Chosŏn Ilbo, and Chungang Ilbo are all set at 15 chŏn per line (行). Therefore, to place a single column advertisement (廣告한段), one must pay 23 won and 25 chŏn. To place a full-page advertisement (i.e., 13 columns on 1 page), the regulation fee is 302 won and 25 chŏn per day per page. It is correct to assume that advertisements for Sinsŏngdang's medicine, Rōto Eye Drops from Tōkyō/Ōsaka, Ajinomoto, Jintan, Kaōru, etc., all follow this standard.
So, how much does the Ōsaka Mainichi Shimbun, which claims a circulation exceeding 1 million copies and distribution throughout the East, including Manchuria, China, India, Hawaii, etc., charge? According to inquiries from a certain source, the Ōsaka Mainichi charges 1 won 55 chŏn per line, and the Ōsaka Asahi charges 5 chŏn less, at 1 won 50 chŏn. This amounts to 232 won 50 chŏn per column and 2,325 won per page. Exactly 10 times the rate of Chosŏn newspapers.

Kikuchi Kan's Annual Salary of 10,000 Yen

Tōkyō novelist Kikuchi Kan (菊池寬), famous for earning well and being favored by contemporary women readers, is reportedly internally decided to join the Ōsaka Mainichi and Tōkyō Nichinichi newspaper companies as a consultant (囑託). He is expected to receive an annual salary of 10,000 yen, whether he writes serialized novels or not.

30 Won for a Dance Pose

In the May issue of "Shufu no Tomo" (主婦之友) published in Tōkyō, dance poses of the Korean female dancer Ch'oe Sŭnghŭi appeared. It is heard that lending photos like that earns a fee of 30 won each time.

Manuscript Fees at Each Company

Manuscript fees paid by newspaper companies mostly cover the cost of purchasing literary reviews, poems, creative works, etc., published in the Arts and Culture section (學藝欄) from writers. Each company has its own limit. Specifically:

Chosŏn Ilbo's Arts and Culture section is allowed to spend up to 60 won per month.
This is separate from the manuscript fees paid for serialized novels like "That Woman's Life" (그 女子의一生), "The Tale of Im Kkŏkjŏng" (林巨正傳), or "Minch'on" (民村).<18> This budget is specifically for the literary reviews and other essays published daily.

Tong-A Ilbo does not have a strictly fixed limit; the Arts and Culture Editor, Sŏ Hangsŏk, has some discretionary leeway. However, due to the cost-saving policy in principle, they do not spend more than other companies. Therefore, for daily published manuscripts, submissions from contributors are published for free, while solicited manuscripts are paid 2 won per column (一段). It is heard that they even measure the length of a column with a ruler to decide if they should pay more per line. Thus, expenditures are roughly 50-60 won per month. This, of course, does not include the fees for "Midnight Sun" (深夜의 太陽), "Equator" (赤道), "Beacon Fire" (烽火), etc.

Chungang Ilbo reportedly spends at a rate of 30 to 40 won per month. Therefore, for the manuscript published at the very beginning of the Arts and Culture page, they pay about 1 won per installment. Naturally, the fees for serialized novels like "Immortal Cry" (不滅의 喊聲) or "Prison Cow Star" (牢牛星 - possibly a symbolic title) are not included in this.
Maeil Sinbo is said to spend up to 300 won per month altogether, including Sangsŏp's "When the Peonies Bloom" (모란꼿 필 때), Ubo's "New Life" (새生命), Yi Posang's "The Tale of Im Kyŏngŏp" (林慶業傳), the illustration fees for Yun Hŭisun's work for "When the Peonies Bloom," and other literary reviews, poems, etc.
To break it down further: Sangsŏp's manuscript fee is 2 won 50 chŏn per installment; the illustrations, being by an outside artist, Mr. Yun Hŭisun, cost 50 chŏn per installment; Mr. Yi Posang receives 1 won; Ubo receives 3 won per installment, totaling 90 won per month. From this, approximately 40 won is spent on manuscript fees for the monthly Maeil Sinbo. This is roughly how it breaks down.

Mr. Chang Hyŏkchu's Diligence

Mr. Chang Hyŏkchu, who appeared like a comet in the East Asian literary scene and is respected by Tōkyō literary critics, is currently writing two medium-length pieces of 80-90 pages each by August/September, commissioned by a major Tōkyō magazine. He has also undertaken a long novel of about 700 pages for a major publishing house and is steadily writing 4-5 pages daily while battling illness; the long novel is reportedly nearing completion. His health, which was previously quite frail, is said to have recovered significantly recently.

Works Advancing into Overseas Literary Circles

As evidence that Korean literature is reaching world standards, many works by our writers are being translated into foreign languages.
Specifically, Ch'unwŏn's "Tragic History of Tanjong" (端宗哀史) and "Tale of Hŏ Saeng" (許生傳) are being translated into English by a Catholic priest. Mr. Chang Hyŏkchu's "Path of Hungry Ghosts" (餓鬼道) has already been translated into Esperanto, and recently, "Path of Hungry Ghosts" and "The Inspired Ones" (奮起者) are being translated into Russian, while "The Pursued People" (追はれる人人) is being translated into French. Last year, Ch'oe Sŏhae's "Escape" (脫出記) was translated into Chinese vernacular (白話譯).
Belonging to slightly older news, "The Tale of Ch'unhyang" (春香傳), a representative work of modern literature and the greatest masterpiece among literary legacies, has already been translated into English, French,<19> and Russian, receiving much praise from literary critics in those countries.

Financial and Business World Insider Report

The Assets of His Highness King Yi

The assets of the Yi Royal Family (李王 御一家), leaked from a certain source (某處에서 洩聞컨대), are said to exceed 10 million won, combining forests, land, buildings, and stock certificates of Chosŏn Bank, Chosŏn Industrial Bank, and the Oriental Development Company. What is the basis for this estimation? Currently, the annual expenses of the Office of the Yi Household (李王職) are around 3 million won, of which 1.5 million won comes from the Imperial Household Ministry (宮內省) as Imperial Household funds. The remaining 1.5 million won is covered by income from forests, stock dividends, etc. Therefore, even assuming a 10% return, it is reasonable to estimate fixed assets at 12 million won; even assuming an 8% annual return, estimating it at 9 million won is considered appropriate.
On another note, it is said that the Royal Family no longer owns fishing grounds (漁場).

However, since then, there has been silence.
Investigating the subsequent developments, if Hwasin were to construct a 4 or 5-story concrete building, it would require throwing down at least 400,000-500,000 won, up to several million won. That construction cost...

Retirement Bonus Amounts

When Mr. Yanabe (矢鍋), the current Chairman of the Financial Cooperatives Federation (金融組合聯合會), left the Chosŏn Industrial Bank (殖産銀行), he received a lump-sum retirement payment (退職一時金) of 150,000 won, which is the highest record for retirement bonuses in Chosŏn. Currently, among various institutions in Chosŏn society, places that provide severance pay (慰勞金) upon retirement due to "old bones being unable to bear the duties" (老骨-不堪其任) include Tongil, Hansŏng, Sangŏp, Haedong Banks; Posŏng College, Hwimun School, Paejae School; Taech'ang Co., Kyŏngsŏng Spinning Co.; and newspaper companies like Tong-A Ilbo Co.
Tong-A Ilbo Co. requires 10 years of continuous service for 10 times the monthly salary. Hansŏng Bank and other banks generally give 10 to 15 times. Companies reportedly give from 5 times up to about 20 times.

Kyedong Palace Site Sold for 94,000 Won

Kyŏngsŏng Kŏnmul Co., Ltd. (京城建物株式會社), led by Mr. Sin T'aejong and others, who reportedly saw great profit by purchasing the site of Mr. Paek Ingi's house in Insa-dong, Sŏul, and constructing new houses, has now won the bid for the approximately 5,000 p'yŏng site of the former Kyedong Palace (桂洞宮집터) from the Office of the Yi Household (李王職) for 94,000 won via auction. Sikto-won and its Management

Sikto-won (食道園) on Namdaemun Street, along with Myŏngwŏlgwan (明月舘), Kugilgwan (國一舘), and Songjugwŏn (松竹園), was favored by the high society of the capital as a place of pleasure and amusement (花明柳暗의 風柳處) frequented by those indulging in the demimonde (狹斜). Recently, it has been undergoing renovations: decorating the interior in Western style, installing new corridors, hanging large mirrors (體鏡) everywhere, which weren't there before, and decorating the entrance with vermilion-painted walls (朱爛畵壁).<22> To the ordinary eye, it seemed thousands of won were being invested in decorations.
It was so conspicuous that rumors gradually spread through word of mouth. Investigating where such large sums of money suddenly came from and why they were so hastily renovating the facilities, it was recently revealed that the actual X [backer/owner?] is XXXX.
For this reason, these days, a colorful stage resembling the Huarong Road scene from "Romance of the Three Kingdoms" (三國誌 속 華龍道 場面) has been newly set up in the second-floor plaza, and kisaeng performances (妓生演奏) are held every night. This is reportedly organized by the Tourist Association (觀光協會), with an admission fee of 1 won.<23>

**Summary**

This "Confidential Room" article from the June 1934 issue of Samch'ŏlli magazine provides insider news snippets across various sectors of colonial Korean society. It touches upon Japanese political opinions regarding Korean self-governance, the significant secret funds allocated to the Governor-General and Japanese military/police, the salaries and activities of prominent figures like Ch'oe Rin and female activists (Hŏ Chŏngsuk, Hwang Sindŏk, etc.), the financial aspects of media (newspaper editorial budgets, advertising rates, manuscript fees, Chosŏn Ilbo's deficit), the literary scene (writers like Chang Hyŏkchu, translations abroad, a meeting with the Academic Affairs Bureau Director), business developments (Hwasin department store expansion plans, Sinsŏngdang company formation, high land prices in Seoul vs. New York), and social life (retirement bonuses, restaurant renovations like Sikto-won). The piece reveals details about the colonial administration's finances (especially secret funds), the economic realities faced by Korean media and businesses, land speculation, and provides updates on the lives of notable individuals, including formerly imprisoned activists adapting to life under surveillance or constraint. Historians might find the details on secret funds, comparisons of costs (advertising, land, salaries), the monitoring/engagement with cultural figures by the authorities, and the post-prison lives of activists particularly valuable for understanding the nuances of control, economy, and social dynamics in 1930s colonial Korea.

조선 총독의 機密費

총독부에 機密費라 하야 장부에 明記치 안코도 소리업시 쓰이는 機密費가 거액이 잇스리라 함은 누구나 추측하는 사실이지 만은 과연 그 금액이 얼마나 되는가 함을 아는 이가 별로 업다.
本社의 조사에 의하건대  총독의 손에 잇는 機密費만 年 35만원이라는데 이것도 처음 伊藤, 寺內, 齊藤의 각 총독까지는 50만원이든 것이 山梨半造 총독 때에 이르러 중앙정부로부터 예산의 삭감을 당하야 35만원으로 줄엇다는데 총독 외에 각 局에도 얼마씩 다 잇스나 그 금액은 얼마 만치 못하지만 政務總監과 警務局長이 쓰는 機密費는상당한 거액에 달하리라고 관측한다.
