Magazine Title: 삼천리 제6권 제7호 (Samch'ŏlli Vol. 6, No. 7)
Publication Date: 1934년 06월 01일 (June 1, 1934)
Article Title: 無頭無尾集-難題目 (Headless, Tailless Collection - Difficult Titles)
Article Type: 잡저 (Miscellaneous Writings)

A: Poet Yi Ha-yun (異 河潤氏) has published a translated poetry collection titled "Scentless Flower Garden" (失香의 花園), but don't you think the title sounds difficult? Wouldn't it have been easier to understand and better to say if it were titled "Scentless Garden" (失香園), like Milton's Paradise Lost (失樂園)? Though it might sound like blasphemy against a sacred poetry collection.

B: Speaking of which, shall I also utter a word of blasphemy against a "sacred poetry collection"? Mo Yun-suk's (毛允淑氏) title "Shining Region" (빗나는 지역) was difficult too. Is the word "region" (지역) really that commonly used?

A: An-sŏ's (岸曙) "Song of the Bonfire Spark" (홰파리의 노래) is also a phrase one cannot readily figure out. Starting with the noun "bonfire spark" (홰파리) itself...

R: Pang In-gŭn's (方仁根氏) recent "Incense Burner of the Demonic City" (魔都의 香불) is also a considerably incomprehensible noun. Why do they attach such unknowable names? This is truly something worth researching.<128><128>

**Summary**

This short article from 1934 features a dialogue between unnamed speakers (A, B, R) critiquing the trend of contemporary Korean writers using obscure or difficult titles for their literary works. They cite Yi Ha-yun's "Scentless Flower Garden," Mo Yun-suk's "Shining Region," Kim An-sŏ's "Song of the Bonfire Spark," and Pang In-gŭn's "Incense Burner of the Demonic City" as examples of unnecessarily complex titles, questioning their comprehensibility and common usage. The speakers find this trend puzzling and worthy of investigation. For historians, this piece offers a glimpse into the literary discussions and aesthetic concerns within Korean intellectual circles during the colonial period, specifically regarding the accessibility and naming conventions of modern Korean literature as it was developing. It reflects a tension between artistic expression aiming for novelty or depth and the desire for clarity and broader understanding among the readership.
