Article Title: 古都의 絶代名妓, 主로 平壤妓生을 中心삼고 (Peerless Famous Kisaeng of the Old Capital, Focusing Mainly on P'yŏngyang Kisaeng)
Author: 金山月
Article Type: 잡저 (Miscellaneous writing/essay)

Peerless Famous Kisaeng of the Old Capital, Focusing Mainly on P'yŏngyang Kisaeng
						Kim Sanwŏl

When one mentions P'yŏngyang, besides it being the ancient capital of Wanggŏm, there are not a few famous things, but among them, "P'yŏngyang kisaeng" are particularly renowned, even among the famous. This fame did not arise just today, but truly, they have been famous since olden times. Some people refer to P'yŏngyang kisaeng as "P'yŏngyang's famous product (명물-)," but calling them a "famous product" somehow doesn't sound good. That person might excuse themselves by saying, "Calling them a 'famous product' doesn't mean I view kisaeng like some kind of object," but in any case, referring to kisaeng as "P'yŏngyang's famous product" does not give a good feeling upon hearing it.

Just because it doesn't give a good feeling, does that mean the term "famous product" cannot be used? – one might ask, but it is an undeniable fact that P'yŏngyang kisaeng have been famous in various ways since olden times.
Even without listing them one by one, just quietly thinking about them will likely be more impressive. There was OOO who acted as a "spy," there was a peerless "vamp," and there were poets who surpassed (凌駕) the writers of the time. Even if they were not born and raised in P'yŏngyang, they were anyway famous kisaeng of their era centered around P'yŏngyang.<184>
As society transitioned from feudalism to capitalism, despite almost everything changing drastically (天壤之差), why is it that "P'yŏngyang kisaeng" remain famous as ever?...

As for why P'yŏngyang is famous for kisaeng, I do not possess the knowledge to investigate that. When it comes to singing, they customarily sing the Susimga (愁心歌, Song of Sorrow) – as for why the Susimga emerged in P'yŏngyang (or P'yŏngan Province), I only have the knowledge I read in Mr. Kim T'aejun's (金台俊) essay on Kayo. That is, due to the factional policies (派閥政策) of the Yi Dynasty (李朝), people from the Western Provinces (西道) were not employed, so they always felt sorrow (愁心) and came to sing the Susimga.
For the people of P'yŏngan Province, who possess the spirit of a "fierce tiger emerging from the forest" (猛虎出林 氣象), how painful must their feelings (心情) have been to sing "...noja noja-" in the Susimga?
There is much room for sympathy. I hear that people from P'yŏngan Province were not appointed to official posts (certainly not high ones) because they possessed the spirit of a fierce tiger emerging from the forest.
Since Yi T'aejo (李太祖) himself said back then, "Do not employ people from the Northern Provinces (북도인, Puktoin)," it seems he greatly feared them.
From this perspective too, it can be said that the fame of kisaeng from P'yŏngyang (or the P'yŏngyang vicinity) in the old days was not accidental. (Fame here means possessing outstanding beauty (拔群 美貌) and extraordinary talent.)

And what if we look at the P'yŏngyang kisaeng of today?
From the moment they decided to "start" becoming kisaeng, they must have come out with the confidence that their face wouldn't be lacking anywhere, or that if someone were as clever as them, they could handle clients.
However, the world is about competition. Moreover, kisaeng need, in addition to beauty, charm, and skills, an element of the "vamp."
If that woman lacks this element (要素) more than others, she will already fall behind (落伍) as a kisaeng.
Then that woman will become pessimistic.
Now, what if we examine their competition numerically?
The current total number of members in the Kisŏng Kwŏnbŏn (箕城券番) is 252. Among them, 19 are on leave (휴업), and 26 are on temporary leave (림시휴업).
So, the kisaeng who are actually called upon are ultimately 207. Then, are these 207 kisaeng called every night? Without waiting for an answer, of course, not all are called. Then how many are called on a single night, and how many are not? And how many are called twice, three times, or even more in one night?
Those called once a night are 66, those called twice are 47,<185> those called three times or more are 21, and those not called at all number, regrettably, 71. (This is from a survey in December, Showa 8 [1933]) (*Translator's note: The text says 1937, but Showa 8 is 1933. Given the publication date of 1934, 1933 seems correct.*)
These 71 kisaeng who are not called just sit there, having put on makeup (?) beautifully for nothing, and end up sleeping alone in empty rooms without cheer. No, that's a misunderstanding; among them, there are some who have even more fun than the kisaeng who are called. How many lustful visitors might stealthily open the door with a smile?
"Oh, you don't know how long I've waited..."
The man who gently embraces the smiling woman—
"Hmm, you waited, right? That's why I brought a present, here—"
And flashing in his palm is a jeweled ring. But how many such men could there really be?... Probably not that many.
The majority, on the contrary, yawn tens, no, hundreds of times on a winter night, straining their ears wondering if perhaps a rickshaw (人力車) might be coming.
But what can be done? As the lonely room deepens meaninglessly...
A sigh, like a shiver, escapes unknowingly. That sound will echo too sharply in her own ears. A handsome man, seemingly seen sometime, faintly comes to mind. But it's all emptiness. Thinking, "Right now, at XX Hall, the sounds of new janggu drums, Susimga, Paettaragi, laughter, charm, alcohol – all mingled together must be flashing brightly," is nothing more than a cruel illusion (幻像) that fuels the unknown flames of hatred in her heart.
In other words, one must know that while there is the not insignificant number of 136 who are called, there are, on the other hand, 71 who sigh.
"She became a kisaeng, but she needs to be called..."
These words are by no means cynicism (히니꾸, hiniku - Japanese for irony/sarcasm). There's a story about a certain kisaeng who, after being called only once in three years, spent day after day thinking her luck would finally turn, only to end up quitting kisaeng life (廢業, p'yegŏp - 폐업). Before we laugh at this fact, isn't there something to think about?...

Kisaeng are also human.
When we see those women dragging skirts that are too long for their short jackets, we somehow realize an unknowable, serene emotion (情緖) flowing from their hearts.
But they too are human. By gender, they are women.
By age, they are in their youth. It's not that they became kisaeng to boast of their youth – a blossoming flower – but because they are young, they gained the qualification to become kisaeng.
However, the sorrow of being a kisaeng.
This likely belongs to those mentioned earlier who are not called.<186> But even being called does not necessarily mean there is no sorrow.
Sleeping during the day, they have to strain their voices almost all night, and force laughter even when they don't feel like it. Therein lies the sorrow. They must force open drowsy eyes, wash their face with cold water, and sing "...ehe-" without any real excitement. Is that all? They must give a good impression to the customer, no matter what.
"Hey—sing Yangsando! Yeah..."
"What Yangsando? Forget that stuff and sing Sŏngjup'iri or Yukchabaegi! That's real singing. You don't even know how..."
Each one demands this song, that song, making it bewildering to know which rhythm to dance to.
"Ho ho, alright, I'll sing Yangsando and then Yukchabaegi."
Even if she says this slyly, each one insists,
"No! Do Yukchabaegi first."
"Hey—hurry up and sing Yangsando."
They'll probably keep wrangling like this.
(How can I watch this disgusting scene without feeling nauseous...)
Even thinking this inwardly is truly useless. Because at that moment, she is a toy sold for that time...

From the numbers mentioned earlier, it was said that 19 are on leave (휴업) and 26 are on temporary leave (림시휴업).
So, how did these leaves and temporary leaves come about?
Needless to say, they are the losers (落伍者) of the competition. Among them, some might be related to doctors (醫聖, *possibly typo for 醫生, doctor*) and have had children. Or, even if they haven't given birth, there are those women, whether fortunate or unfortunate, who are several months pregnant (姙娠).
In any case, isn't the fact that she started as a kisaeng and ended up taking leave already a sign of falling behind?...
Anyway, as they somehow get older and reach around twenty-five, what path do they take? Assuming they start being called at sixteen, by twenty-three, that's a full six years. During that time, do they save enough money for their clothing, food, and shelter (衣食住) to be abundant? And then what do they become? They probably won't become the wives of wealthy men. Although Mr. An Sŏgyŏng said (albeit in a cartoon) that even retired kisaeng become fine ladies, not even one in a hundred will become a "first" wife. If they become the "second" (쎄컨드) of a rich man, that's lucky, but mostly they become *that* (the mistress/wife) of men who run inns or restaurants.
Even if they end up like that, they cannot shed the traces of their former kisaeng days. No, if they were to shed the kisaeng traces, they might already be boycotted ("포이콧트") by the "has-beens" ("해쓰").

What does it mean for a kisaeng to have "made it" (출세, meaning some degree of success)?
Money. Just money. Unconditionally, grabbing money would mean success. (Of course, anyone would say they've succeeded if they have money).<187>
However, this simple problem is not easily solved. Then, does everyone have only one ultimate goal? Is it as someone said, "Truth is only one"?...
Then, people's paths toward that single goal will differ. The cloth merchant sells fabric, the salaried worker saves, the speculator watches for a good "chance," the gold miner discovers a vein, the laborer sells their strength, and the kisaeng tries to grab money using beauty, charm, and "vamp"-like methods. Isn't this how everyone's path is different? There's a line in Paettaragi, "...The occupations by which all people under heaven earn a living are each different..." and indeed it is so.
To live, or
To live a little better...
With such calculations (心算), some people follow this path, and some follow that path.
And some people suddenly switch from that path to this path.
These days, walking the streets of P'yŏngyang, one can often see innocent girls of just thirteen or fourteen, their faces powdered white, bodies wrapped in various fine clothes, pushing past each other.
This phenomenon can be seen many times more often than a few years ago.
To live a little better, they put their cute granddaughters into kisaeng school. Even grandmothers who used to spit at the mention of kisaeng in the past now say enviously,
"Still, I hear being a kisaeng is the most profitable..."
However, one must know that, on the other hand, the wallets of the handsome men who call kisaeng for entertainment have also become several grams lighter.
The young masters who used to sell rice entrusted to warehouses without their fathers' knowledge and come into P'yŏngyang to loosen the purse strings for kisaeng seem to have vanished as if an emergency (非常時) has come upon them too. But here, the kisaeng have room for thought.
What kind of partner should they seek?
They have shifted their front line (戰線) from civilians to government officials (官吏).
Accordingly, targeting prosecutors, judges, and other high-ranking officials... they learned Japanese songs and dances.
What used to be "...Have a drink..." (한잔 드르세요).
has become "... Sa- ippai oagari nasai ne-" (さ-いっばい おあがり なさいね-, Please, have a drink).
So their speech has also changed.
Consequently, among the officials,
"...So-and-so is good at 'Naniwa bushi' (なにはぶし)," and "So-and-so plays the shamisen (三味線) quite well," they gain a good reputation.
Therefore, popularity among these overseas people (*likely referring to Japanese officials*) means becoming a truly popular person (人氣者).<188>
Though somewhat awkward to say, Cho Chinsil (曹眞實) and Yi Ilchihwa (李一枝花) are popular among them.
Not to mention the veteran No Ŭnhong (盧銀紅).
Four or five years ago, there was the incident where a kisaeng named Chŏng Nanhong (鄭蘭紅) followed the then P'yŏngnam Police Chief (平南 警察部長), Mr. XX (of course, as a "second") all the way to his hometown in Shikoku (시고꾸).
She returned after about a year. We don't know how much money she brought back across the Korea Strait (玄海灘), but we can well imagine that Nanhong did not cross the Strait and the scenic Seto Naikai (세도나이카이, Seto Inland Sea) just because she fell for the police chief in his forties with a bristly black beard.
At that time, in a letter Nanhong sent to her mother while crossing the Korea Strait, she wrote:
"...Mother, don't worry. I am not leaving for good. I am embarking on this unwanted long journey so that I can provide well for you later. But Mother! You have absolutely nothing to worry about regarding my well-being. ..."
Seeing this, we can sufficiently glimpse her state of mind.
However, we did not expect her to play a role like OOO from the old days back then. (This is perhaps a slightly cynical remark...)
So, is she now, as she said she would, providing well for her mother and spending enjoyable days?...

And Chang Yŏnhong (張蓮紅) and Pak Yŏngdo (朴英道), who until two years ago were renowned as famous kisaeng (名妓) not just in P'yŏngyang but throughout Chosŏn, suddenly crossed over to Shanghai (上海). Pak reportedly committed suicide last winter.
We didn't have high expectations when they went to Shanghai. There's no need to reiterate why there wasn't much to expect. But Pak truly died a worthless death. It is heard that she committed suicide after being rejected by Chŏng X T'aek (鄭X鐸), one of the few handsome men (?) in Chosŏn.
If she had to die, there were plenty of places to die in P'yŏngyang without going all the way to faraway Shanghai. Of course, the circumstances entangled in her death must be complex, but anyway, her actions cannot be praised. But what is Chang Yŏnhong, who remains there, dreaming of?...
During her time in P'yŏngyang (在壤), Yŏnhong gathered praise for her unique beauty, being called a "beauty of sorrow" (哀愁 美人), a "beauty of autumn," having "vamp eyes," but news from Shanghai is unclear. It is heard that she was recently detained at the consulate for some incident, but the nature of that XX incident is unknown. Perhaps for them, this is not a downfall (落伍) as kisaeng but a leap (飛躍)? One hopes she does not take the same path as Pak Yŏngdo <189> in the end. Anyway, Chang and Pak* were the top figures at Kisŏng Kwŏnbŏn until two or three years ago, or perhaps it's more accurate to say they stood at the forefront? It seems they felt weary (倦怠) of kisaeng life. Of course, there might also be the reason that they strongly opposed the Kisŏng Kwŏnbŏn changing from a cooperative system (組合制) to a joint-stock system (株式制) until the end, and then withdrew...

Having written this far, I see I haven't written anything particularly insightful and have far exceeded the planned page count. But it can't be helped. This is due to the author's lack of skill... Since the page count has already been exceeded, let me add a fragment of my impressions.—

The waves of the Taedong River, touched by the spring breeze, ripple softly like silk, folding this way and that. The water is so clear and blue that a housewife who came from the countryside to see P'yŏngyang reportedly dipped the hem of her skirt in the river water. To see if the blue water would stain it.
Truly blue, blue waves—
The weeping willows of Yanggakto (羊角島)—(The author is familiar with Yanggakto because of the outer fortress (外城)).—sprouting here and there, hang over the waves, swaying in the gentle breeze (惠風) blowing from the sea. And the swallows flying by occasionally. The hazy shimmer of distant mountains—it was a spring afternoon on the Taedong River that made even the spring sun seem faint.
And that day was a Sunday.
As usual, I was strolling along this side of the riverbank, overlooking Yanggakto.
Looking up the bank, a group of girls, their skirt hems fluttering like the river waves, were coming this way—
They were child kisaeng (童妓). Sacred children of thirteen or fourteen, full of innocence (天眞爛漫)—
Ah, in the old days when this land prospered, how picturesquely such scenes

古都의 絶代名妓, 主로 平壤妓生을 中心삼고
						金山月
평양이라 하면 녯날 왕검(王儉)의 서울이란 것 이외에 유명한 것이 적지 안으나 그 중에도 『평양기생』이라고 하면 유명한 중에도 「센치」한 것이다. 이것은 오늘날 와서 유명해진 것이, 안이라 실노 녯날부터 유명한 것이다. 엇던 사람은 평양 기생을 가르쳐 『평양의 명물-』이라고 하나 『명물』이라고 하면 엇전가 듯기가 조치 못하다. 그 사람의 말이 『명물』이라고 해서 뭐 기생을 엇던 물건과 가티 보는 것은 안이라』고 변명하겟지만 하여튼 기생을 『평양의 명물이다』하고 말하면 듯기에 조흔 감정을 못 준다.
조흔 감정을 못 준다고 해서 『명물』이란 말을 못 쓸 리가 어듸 잇느냐?-고 할넌지 모르나 평양기생이 넷날부터 이모저모로 유명하엿든 것만은 부인할 수 업는 사실이다.
그들을 일일히 들지 안어도 가만히 생각해봄이 더욱 인상적일 것이다. 「스파이」역활을 한 OOO이가 잇섯고 절세의 「벰파여」가 잇섯고 그리고 당시의 문장들을 릉가(凌駕)하는 시인(詩人)이 잇섯다. 이들이 평양서 나서 평양서 자라지는 안엇다 할망정 좌운간 평양을 중심으로 한 당대의 유명한 기생들이엿다.<184>
봉건사회에서 자본주의 사회에로 변천해옴에 따러 모-든 것이, 천양지차(天壤之差)로 거의 밧김에도 불구하고 『평양의 기생-』하면 여전이 유명함은 무슨 까닭일가?...

엇재서 평양은 기생이 유명한가? 에 대하야 구명할 지식을 나는 못 가젓다. 그들은 소리라면 으레 수심가(愁心歌)를 부르는데-엇재서 평양은 (또는 평안도는) 수심가가 생겻느냐? 하는데 대해서는 김태준(金台俊)씨 가요론에서 읽은 지식밧게 업다. 즉 리조(李朝)의 파벌정책(派閥政策)에서 서도(西道)의 사람은 써주지 안어서 향상 수심이 생겨 수심가를 부르게 됏다고 한다.
맹호출림(猛虎出林) 기상(氣象)을 가진 평안도ㅅ 사람으로서 『...노자노자-』하니 수심가를 부르게 된 심정(心情)은 그 얼마나 아펏겟는가?
너무나 동정할 여지가 잇다. 듯건대 평안돗 사람은 맹호출림 기상을 가젓기 때문에 관직에 (무론 노픈 관직에) 써주지 안엇다고도 한다.
그 넷날 리태조(李太祖)가 『북도인은 물용(勿用)하라』 하섯다니 그는 북도인을 뭇척 두려워한 모양이다.
이러한 데서도 넷날 평양 (또는 평양부근-) 기생이 유명한 것도 우연이 안이라고도 할 수 잇겟다. (유명햇다는 것이 발군(拔群)의 미모(美貌)와 비범한 재질을 가젓다는 것을 의미하는 것이다)

그리고 오늘의 평양 기생을 살펴보면 엇더한가?
그들이 기생이 되겟다고 「스타-트」한 것부터 내 얼골은 어듸 가든지 빠지지 안켓다. 또는 나만치 령리햇으면 손님을 골릴 수 잇겟다 하는 자신을 가지고 나온 것일 것이다.
그러나 세상은 경쟁이다. 더구나 기생은 미모와 애교와 기외에 좀 「벰파여」의 요소가 필요하다.
만일 그 녀자가 이 요소(要素)가 남보다 결여(缺如)됏다고 하면 그 녀자는 벌서 기생으로서 낙오(落伍)될 것이다.
그러면 그 녀자는 비관할 것이다.
이제 여기에 그들의 경쟁을 수자로서 살펴보면 엇더한가?
현재 기성권번(箕城券番) 총인원(總人員)은 252명이다. 그 중에서 휴업이 19명. 림시휴업이 26명.
그러닛간 불니우는 기생은 결국 207명이다. 그러면 이 207명의 기생이 매일 밤 불니는가? 대답을 기다리지 안어도 무론 다 못 불니는 것이다. 그럼 얼마나 하로ㅅ 밤에 불니고 얼마나 못 불니는가? 그리고 하롯 밤에 두 번 또는 세 번 또는 그 이상 불니는 이가 얼마나 되는가?
하로ㅅ 밤에 한번 불니는 이가 66명 그리고 두 번 불니는 이가 47명<185> 세 번 이상 불니는 이가 21명 도모지 못 불니는 이가 앗갑게도 71명이나 된다. (이것은 昭和 8년12월(1937) 조사다)
이 71명-이란 못 불니는 기생은 공연히 곱게 화장(?)만하고 안젓다가 시름업시 독수공방에 홀노 자게된다. 아니 그것은 오해 그 중에는 불니우는 기생보다 오히려 더 자미를 보는 이가 잇다. 방싯-하니 문을 가만히 열고 차저 오는 호색객들이 과연 얼마나 될넌지?
『아이 얼마나 기다렷는지 모루겟다우...』
하며, 방긋이 웃는 그 녀자를 슬그머니 껴안는 그 남자-
『웅 기다렷겟지 그러기 내 선물 사 가지고 왓지 자-』
하면서 손바닥에서 번쩍 빗나는 것은 보석반지 그러나 이런 이가 과연 몃 명이나 될넌지?... 그리 만치는 못할 것이다.
그 반면의 대다수는 겨을 밤이면 하픔을 몃 십번 아니 몃 백번이나 하며 혹시나 인력거(人力車)가 아니오나? 하고 귀를 기우리군하는 것이다.
그러나 어이할가? 쓸쓸한 방안이 속절업시 기퍼감을...
