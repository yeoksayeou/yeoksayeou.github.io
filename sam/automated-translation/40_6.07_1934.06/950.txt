十七篇創作民謠(民謠 五作家 作品集)
						李光洙 金岸曙 朱耀翰 琴童 金東煥
Miari

Ch'unwŏn (Yi Kwangsu)

If you go to Miari,
There are many graves.
Which grave is it,
My beloved's grave?

Which grave is it,
Not my beloved's grave?
This sorrowful verse,
Please accept it like this.<270>

Ŏyŏngdo Island

Kim Ansŏ

A white sail under the blue sky, bright sun,
On a fishing boat laden with spring, patterned in silver thread, silk, and gold brocade.
Seagull circling the port,
Where is Ŏyŏngdo Island? I want to go to Ŏyŏngdo.

Eoya deoya, the boat departs,
On the white water like silver and gold thread, only the spring breeze gently blows.
I want to go to Ŏyŏngdo.
The gently blowing wind, where is Ŏyŏngdo Island?<271>

When the Railway is Laid

Chu Yohan
(Unauthorized reproduction or reuse prohibited)

In Sakchu and Kusŏng,
They say gold nuggets are found.
Tangdi, tangdi, tangdidang, didada, dingda, dingdading
About you and me,
Only rumors spread.

Crossing the Tuman River ferry
By an iron bridge.
Tangdi, tangdi, tangdidang, didada, dingda, dingdading
Exchanging a single word,
Why is it so difficult?

In Yusŏng and Onyang,
The hot springs are good.
Tangdi, tangdi, tangdidang, didada, dingda, dingdading<272>
My father-in-law and mother-in-law,
Their marital harmony is also good.

At Wŏnsan Harbor,
The fishing is grand.
Tangdi, tangdi, tangdidang, didada, dingda, dingdading
My sister-in-law with bobbed hair,
Her bottom is large.

At Najin sea,
It's far from the ships' arrival.
Tangdi, tangdi, tangdidang, didada, dingda, dingdading
Inside the young lady's belly,
What is contained?

In Yangyang and Kangnŭng,
When the railway track is laid.
Tangdi, tangdi, tangdidang, didada, dingda, dingdading
Old maid and bachelor,
Lay down your worries too.
(In the Kyŏngsŏng region, unmarried women are called 'saeksi').<273>

Yearning Chejudo Island

P'ain (Kim Tonghwan)

Ah, it's wonderful, Chejudo Island for me,
Where bright red camellias bloom in the snow,
Further inflaming this burning heart.

Ah, it's wonderful, Chejudo Island for me,
A deer deep in the mountains of Hallasan,
Drinking water, then calling for its mate, that sound.

Ah, it's wonderful, Chejudo Island for me,
Going out to the sea to dive for pearls,
Even if there are no pearls, scoop up the moon and return.

Ah, it's wonderful, Chejudo Island for me,
A bamboo flute from Chŏllado washed ashore by the waves,
Picking it up and playing makes me yearn for my homeland.<274>

To Block Your Departing Path

P'ain (Kim Tonghwan)

Late spring is like a dream,
The candle hasn't burned down,
Yet this night is dawning.

Steps to leave when dawn breaks,
How sorrowful, the crescent moon
Is already about to cross the western peak.

To block your departing path,
I hid one straw shoe,
But you went out in just your socks.

With the willow by the back river,
I blocked the path you'd pass,
But you turned back via the shortcut.

Gathering an armful of azaleas,
If I scatter them on your departing path,
Will you turn back, reluctant to step on them?<275>

Song of Everlasting Sorrow (Changga) (Ballad)

P'ain (Kim Tonghwan)

Ah-, the hero of a generation, Kim Okkyun, couldn't meet his time,
Abandoned by his homeland, ah-, at the Shanghai Bund,
He shed red blood.
Ehe-e, this is the sorrow (恨) of a thousand autumns and ten thousand years.

Ah-, flowers bloom in the wind, and a beautiful woman (佳人)
Blooms in the arms of a great man (丈夫), ah-, a great man is here,
But where is the peerless beauty (絶代佳人)?
Ehe-e, this is the sorrow (恨) of a thousand autumns and ten thousand years.

Ah-, comrades from the Eight Provinces gathered at Chongno,
Then scattered again, ah-, when will we
Meet each other again?
Ehe-e, this is the sorrow (恨) of a thousand autumns and ten thousand years.<276>

There, Those Ladies

Yi Kwangsu (Ch'unwŏn)

There, those ladies,
Stop crying so much.
Is it your husband's grave?
Is it your son's grave?

Who among us
Will not die eventually?
Just because one died first,
Is there reason to be sorrowful?<277>

Song of Parting (From Ch'unhyangjŏn)

Kŭmdong

Even the thousand strands of willow by the stream across the stars,
Cannot hold onto your departing form?
Even Ch'unhyang's shedding ten thousand lines of tears,
Cannot block your departing path?

Gazing at the crescent moon, singing love songs,
The swing spot at Kwanghallu where we used to meet,
That it would become the place of parting,
Sending you off today, how could this happen!<278>

In the Early Rice Paddy

Kim Ŏk

Has water reached the early rice paddy?
Has water reached your heart?
In the mercury-like white water, the fresh new spring is reflected.

In the mirror-like clear water, the mountain lies down and sleeps.
In your heart welcoming the new spring,
Your beloved lies down and dreams.<279>

Boat Song of the Taedong River

P'ain (Kim Tonghwan)

1.

A boat flowing on the Taedong River,
Loading a 4,000-year dream,
Where does it go this night?

Who plays from the boat landing?
A plaintive flute sound
Wanders, weeping alone.

In the dim light of a silk lantern,
Even the willows of Nŭngnado Island
Let down their hair and sigh.

2.

If you leave this night,
When will you return?
Departing boat, please stop.

Even if you go, dear friend,
Tune the strings of the four-stringed zither (四絃琴),
And play a sorrowful tune before you go.

If you cannot finish playing,
Hang it on the willow branch,
So it weeps on its own in the river breeze.<281>

Lone Blooming Camellia

P'ain (Kim Tonghwan)

1.

I am in the snowfield,
A lone blooming camellia.

Because you told me to bloom,
I bloomed even in the snow.

Because you said you would come,
I stood waiting till dusk.

2.

Waiting and waiting,
If you do not come,

Before this night is over,
I will fall in the snowstorm.

I am, in the snowfield,
A lone blooming camellia.<281>

Sky of Home

P'ain (Kim Tonghwan)

When the apricot blossoms bloom
On the apricot tree (살구낭게),
When the apricot blossoms bloom,
By the river in my hometown,
By the river in front,
Yearning T'ansiri,
Might she still be standing there?

Even in the rain, the spring rain,
Wetting her skirt,
Playing grass fights, flower fights (풀싸홈 꼿싸홈),
Spending the day,
Under the tree, T'ansiri,
Might she still be standing there?

Wandering in foreign lands (他關客地),
A lonely existence,
When the apricot blossoms bloom,
Yearning for the old days,
Looking towards the sky of home,
I weep alone.<282>

Strawberry Runner

Ch'unwŏn (Yi Kwangsu)

On our baby's grave (우래기 = 우리 애기),
Pull out the weeds, but
The strawberry runner that sprouted,
Please don't damage it.

When the vine spreads,
And strawberries form,
And ripen bright red,
Perhaps she might come out.<283>

Yes, That's Right, Elder Sister

Ansŏ (Kim Ansŏ)

Yes, that's right, elder sister.
How many wrinkles do you live with?
The stream water flows to the sea.
The stream water also flows to the sea.

Yes, that's right, elder sister.
Untrustworthy, isn't it?
This heart that sets in the West and circles the East.
This heart that sets in the West and also circles the East.

Yes, that's right, elder sister.
You cannot see, can you?
The flower blossom lives trusting the wind.
The flower blossom also lives trusting the wind.<284>

Bloomed in Vain This Year Too

P'ain (Kim Tonghwan)

The fire burning by the riverbank
Is the August Rugosa Rose (海棠花).
Do you know this heart,
Burning bright red?

Avoiding wind and rain,
I stood carefully,
Wondering if your longed-for footsteps
Might happen to pass by.

The seagull by the river
Goes and comes back too;
The sandcastle on the waves,
If it collapses, it's built again, but

Once fallen, endlessly,
I spend this year in vain.
Where can I appeal?
Bloomed in vain this year too.<285>

Song of the Lumpen

P'ain (Kim Tonghwan)

1.

When the sun sets at Chongno intersection,
Wearing an old hat, old shoes, an old suit,
A hero who hasn't met his time becomes a lumpen (룸펜),
Gathering in this alley and that street.

2.

Don't laugh calling me lumpen, lumpen, lumpen (룸펜 룸펜 룸펜),
Napoleon (나파륜) too was a lumpen in Corsica (꼴시카),
Chiang Kai-shek (장개석) too was a gambler (투전꾼) in Shanghai.
If abandoned, not having met one's time, anyone becomes a lumpen.

3.

When the spring wind blows in April and the season comes,
Like the kudzu vine (츩넝쿨) blooming up at Tengwang Pavilion (藤王閣),
Yesterday's lumpen is today's hero.
Don't laugh calling me lumpen, lumpen, lumpen.<286>

**Summary**

This collection presents seventeen original poems styled as folk songs (minyo) by five prominent Korean writers of the era: Yi Kwangsu (Ch'unwŏn), Kim Ansŏ, Chu Yohan, Kŭmdong, and Kim Tonghwan (P'ain, Kim Ŏk also appears but is Kim Ansŏ's other pseudonym - correction: Kim Ŏk is a separate poet, An Sŏ is Kim Ansŏ. Let's re-verify authors. The list is 李光洙 金岸曙 朱耀翰 琴童 金東煥. Kim Ŏk's poem "올벼논에" is included, attributed to 金億. Let's assume 金億 = Kim Ŏk is another contributor despite not being in the initial list, or perhaps Kŭmdong is a pseudonym for Kim Ŏk? Or the initial list is just the main contributors? Let's stick to the listed authors plus Kim Ŏk. Re-evaluating the initial list: 李光洙 (Yi Kwangsu), 金岸曙 (Kim Ansŏ), 朱耀翰 (Chu Yohan), 琴童 (Kŭmdong), 金東煥 (Kim Tonghwan). Kim Ŏk (金億) is definitely another author whose poem is included. P'ain is Kim Tonghwan. Ch'unwŏn is Yi Kwangsu. Ansŏ is Kim Ansŏ. Okay, proceed.) The poems cover diverse themes including grief and remembrance (Miari, Strawberry Runner), longing for specific places (Ŏyŏngdo, Chejudo), reflections on modernization and its social impact (When the Railway is Laid), love and separation (Song of Parting, To Block Your Departing Path), historical reflection (Song of Everlasting Sorrow about Kim Okkyun), pastoral scenes (In the Early Rice Paddy), and social commentary (Song of the Lumpen). The poems adopt folk rhythms and language, often expressing personal emotions against backdrops of nature or specific locations across Korea.

From a colonial history perspective, the collection is interesting as it showcases prominent literary figures engaging with the folk song form during a period of cultural suppression and transformation. While often personal or lyrical, some poems subtly touch on themes relevant to the era: the mention of modernization projects like railways (potentially reflecting Japanese development), the evocation of specific Korean locales fostering national identity (Chejudo, Taedong River, etc.), the lament for historical figures like Kim Okkyun (a failed reformer), and the social observation in "Song of the Lumpen" reflecting economic hardship or displacement. The blend of traditional forms with modern sensibilities and the expression of Korean identity and emotion through familiar landscapes and themes would have resonated with contemporary readers navigating colonial rule.
