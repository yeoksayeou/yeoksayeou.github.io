**Body Text Translation:**

An Account of Autopsying 208 Corpses
						By Pak Ch'anghun (朴昌薰), M.D.

To this day, I have dissected some two hundred and eight human corpses. Half of them were Korean people, and half were people from overseas (海外). Among these two hundred and eight, there were also many corpses of children (兒屍), and there were women who, had they lived, would just now be like flowers about to bloom. <朴昌薰:이삼6-714401>

However, when holding the autopsy scalpel (解剖刀), peeling off the skin, and cutting away the bone, a corpse was nothing more than an identical object.

Among the two hundred and eight who lay stretched out on my autopsy table and had their bodies dissected by the tip of my scalpel, there were many well-known figures (知名) and many so-called great men (傑物). But before a corpse, there are no heroes, no great figures, no talented men, no beauties—none at all.

The moment life departs, all corpses are equal. There is no distinction of high or low status, noble or base birth, male or female, old or young. Even a millionaire, upon death, is just a corpse; even the snow-white skin of a peerless beauty, upon death, is merely an object like paper (조히) or wood.

Therefore, in my eyes, the concept of which is more cherished or more detested disappears, and any preconception of treating one corpse with more reverence than another vanishes completely, not even by a hair's breadth.
Before the scholar (學者), there is only human bone and human flesh.

However, sometimes, even while busily moving the tip of the autopsy knife, I find myself thinking:
What is life (生命)?
What is the soul (靈魂)?

It feels so futile. How could a person who was vigorously alive just moments ago become a corpse lying on the autopsy table in an instant? Where could there be such a futile transformation? Death and life, that fleeting existence! Or the soul! What could it be?<144> What is life, that it changes from absolute being (有) to absolute nothingness (無)? And I agonize for a while over the mystery of life (生命의 神秘), the inscrutability of the soul (靈魂不可思議).

But who can explain this? Who can reveal this riddle?
When a clock spring unwinds, it goes tick-tock and then may stop, but if you wind the spring again, it goes tick-tock once more. But has anyone ever rewound the spring of human life once it has stopped?

Indeed, many medical scholars, while handling corpses, become drawn to the mystery of life and, seeking to solve it, become philosophers (哲學者). Having experienced it myself, I find this quite natural. Perhaps one day I too might shed these pathologist's (病利學者的) clothes and step into philosophy (절학) to study the mysteries of the universe. To repeat myself, after dissecting two hundred and eight corpses over about twenty years, my thoughts have merely arrived once again at the great "riddle":
"Truly, what is life?"

Twenty years ago from now, the corpse of a death row inmate (死刑首) hanged (絞首) at Sŏdaemun Prison lay stretched out in the autopsy room (解剖室) of Kyŏngsŏng Medical College (京城醫藥專門學校 解剖室). At that time, the professor (꼬수) of the pathology department, Dr. Kubo (久保博士), stood beside me and ordered me to perform the autopsy.

Without hesitation, I grasped the autopsy knife and began the dissection. This was my very first autopsy.
To be honest, at that time, I was both scared and repulsed by the corpse. Had Dr. Kubo and the nurse not been beside me, I probably would have thrown down the knife and run out the door.
Yet, my curiosity surged.
There was also the impulse, as a medical scholar, to know.

First, I grasped the corpse's hand. A chill went down my spine. It was as cold as ice. I knew that human flesh becomes extremely cold upon death, but could it possibly be this frigid?

Then, I removed the white cloth covering it and looked at the face. He looked to be in his forties, with a handsome face and a sturdy man (壯漢) with a well-developed, prominent (隆隆) physique.
His eyes were wide open showing only the whites, and his tongue stuck way out, about a span long, clamped tightly between his teeth, and blood had pooled (갑엇다) there. Being a prisoner hanged by the neck, he must have suffered greatly at the moment of death; his eyes were wide open, his tongue stuck out, and the traces of agony on his facial expression were clearly visible (역역하엿다).

After opening the abdomen, meticulously (삿삿치) taking out and examining the five viscera (五臟), opening the chest and observing the lungs (페), and then examining all the other organs (器官),<145> I removed the skull (頭盖骨) and weighed it on a scale. It was
175 grams.
In the academic circles of Tōkyō, it was said that the late Prime Minister Katsura Tarō (首相 桂太郞) had the heaviest brain at 175 grams. And the theory that the heavier the skull, the greater the person (偉人), is a consensus among scholars East and West, ancient and modern. Not only I, but Dr. Kubo too was astonished at how heavy it was. Having dissected over two hundred bodies since then, I have yet to find a corpse with such a heavy skull.

Having dissected roughly 200 bodies, it's impossible to remember each one distinctly, but the case of this death row inmate remains unforgettable, both because it was my very first and because his skull was exceptionally heavy. That face, biting its tongue, eyes wide open, with prominent cheekbones, remains as vivid today, twenty years later, as if I could draw it.

And there is one more case I remember clearly. It was the second corpse.
That body belonged to a woman said to be nineteen or twenty, also a death row inmate from Sŏdaemun Prison. She had reportedly conspired with her lover to poison her husband just one month after getting married. Her face was long like a willow leaf, and her nose bridge was delicate (산틋하얏다). Had she powdered her face, applied rouge, and used camellia oil to make her hair shine, she would have been a lovely beauty (미인).

That woman lacked female genitalia in her lower region (下部). We wondered among ourselves how a woman who had even poisoned her own husband could lack such a thing, and the memory of us laughing about it remains to this day.

When I cut the woman's arm, three or four drops of dark blood emerged. It is known that when a person dies, ordinarily all the red blood gathers in the lungs (폐), and there is little blood left in the blood vessels of the limbs (사지혈관), but this woman's blood vessels had remarkably little blood.
Had she lived, this passionate and sorrowful beauty (佳人) would surely not have lacked the blood, hot as fire, flowing abundantly through her limbs—

However, while I handle others' corpses as casually as kneading dough, the corpse of my own kin was truly frightening and repulsive.
When my mother passed away (도라가섯슬), how terrifying that mourning room (殯室) where her body lay was! Though family and relatives came and went, I ultimately could not bring myself to set foot in there. At night, I kept the light in the adjacent room (겻방) brightly lit (환-하게 켜노코), trembling, unable to sleep a wink. Truly, even thinking about it myself, it's something I cannot understand.
Less than three months after my mother passed away, I sold that house. Eventually, even the house itself felt repulsive and frightening.

On this occasion, there is one more thing I wish<146> to record. It is that initially, I had absolutely no intention of studying medicine.
I originally have a weak stomach. Twenty years ago in Seoul, places like Suhadong (水下町) truly had many snakes (구렁이). If I merely saw a snake while walking down the street, I couldn't eat for the rest of the day. And on the dinner table my mother prepared, if, for example, a dead fly floated in the soy sauce dish, I would not only refuse the soy sauce but push the entire table away, unable to eat. Not only could I not eat, but I would end up vomiting what I had already eaten.
Because this is my constitution, I initially had no thought of pursuing medicine, which involves squeezing pus and cutting flesh. However, my family insisted that I study medicine. Our family traditionally practiced medicine for generations. Even now, among the medicinal herb dealers (藥種商) in the Kurigae (구리개) area and the traditional Korean medicine doctors (漢方醫師) throughout Seoul, there are many of my relatives.
I could not refuse my parents' strong urging (强權).

At that time, a grand dream crossed my mind. It was
to become "Sun Yat-sen (孫逸仙)."
Being a young man full of fantasies and grand aspirations, I wanted to become the Sun Yat-sen of China. He too initially specialized in medicine (醫學專門). But didn't he later become a politician (政治家)?
Moreover, Chosŏn at that time was under the rule of Governor-General Terauchi (寺內總督時代).
I judged that to achieve my ambition, I would have to go to America (米國), and to go to America, permission would likely not be granted for ordinary reasons, so I should say I was going to study medicine. Therefore, pretending I couldn't resist my parents' wishes, I decided it was best to first enroll in Kyŏngsŏng Medical College. Thus, I set foot in Kyŏngsŏng Medical College.

For some time after enrolling, the coursework was uninteresting, and the practical training was tedious. However, I underwent a significant change here. It was the influence of the renowned scholar, Dr. Kubo (久保博士).
Dr. Kubo had only graduated from Nagasaki Medical College (長崎醫專) and lacked any particular academic faction (學閥) backing, but his fervent research enthusiasm (硏究熱), sharp intellect (머리 조흠), and sincere attitude toward science made him a rarely seen figure.
Also, Dr. Kubo was quite simple-minded and had strong biases (偏性), so among the many students, he passionately favored (熱愛) two or three talented ones and treated the rest like worn-out shoes. And being simple and ignorant of worldly affairs, he would speak frankly without considering how others might perceive his words.
It's awkward to say it myself, but the professor cherished and loved me greatly. Consequently, I naturally came to follow the professor, inevitably falling under his influence, and thus genuinely developed an interest in medicine.<147>
What began as a strategic enrollment in medical school eventually came to be regarded as my true calling (天職).

Speaking of Dr. Kubo, one thing comes to mind: the professor once said something like this:
"The more developed the smiling muscles (笑筋 - muscles running from the mouth toward the ear) are, the more civilized the nation's people. According to my anthropometric measurements (計測學上), Westerners have more developed smiling muscles than Japanese people, and Korean people's are not well-developed." At that time, some mischievous student, perhaps displeased by these words, retorted:
"Well then, if you judge civilization solely based on smiling muscles, among Japanese people, actors, who laugh and cry and make facial expressions actively day and night, must have the most developed smiling muscles. So, does that mean the most civilized people in Japan are the 'clowns (광대)'?"
There was even such an incident. The theory about smiling muscles is actually an internationally recognized academic theory, originally conceived by the famous anthropometrist from Denmark (丁抹), Dr. Martin (말-틴).
Thus, the immutable theorem (定理) arose that "civilized people have developed smiling muscles, while uncivilized people's are poorly developed." Dr. Kubo apparently reached this conclusion by measuring hundreds of Korean corpses with a ruler (尺).

However, one day, a strange incident occurred. The professor brought a skull, which he cherished and kept in his research lab, to the classroom for a lecture, and then left. Afterwards, the skull disappeared, presumably taken by a student.
Well, this was serious. For a scholar's vital skull to disappear like this—Dr. Kubo was furious.
If you look up the Dong-A Ilbo (東亞日報) from that time, the details will be clear, so I won't say more, but this led to public criticism surrounding Dr. Kubo.
It all stemmed from the professor's simple-mindedness.

On another note, when I die, I intend to donate my body for autopsy. In the West or even in Tōkyō, it's customary for people of reasonable standing to offer their bodies for research purposes through voluntary autopsy donation (特志解剖).
In Chosŏn, available corpses are limited to those of executed criminals, patients who died receiving free treatment (無料患者), or deceased vagrants or travelers (行旅病死亡者) handed over by the Kyŏngsŏng Municipal Office (京城府). For the advancement of medicine, I wish everyone would offer their bodies for academic use.

Finally, as I lay down my pen, I pray for the eternal rest (冥福) of the two hundred and eight corpses that were poked and cut by the tip of my autopsy scalpel on my autopsy table over the past twenty years.<148>

**Summary**

Dr. Pak Ch'anghun reflects on his twenty years dissecting over 200 corpses, half Korean and half foreign. He notes how death equalizes everyone, stripping away status and beauty, leaving only biological material for the scientist. Yet, dissecting evokes profound questions about life and the soul, mysteries he feels remain unsolved despite his medical work, sometimes tempting him towards philosophy. He vividly recalls his first autopsy on a defiant-looking hanged prisoner with an unusually heavy skull, and a young female prisoner executed for murdering her husband who strangely lacked external genitalia. Pak confesses his squeamishness, his initial disinterest in medicine (pursuing it strategically to eventually study politics abroad like Sun Yat-sen), and how the passionate, albeit eccentric, Japanese professor Dr. Kubo ignited his genuine interest in pathology. He mentions Kubo's controversial research comparing "smiling muscles" across races and an incident where a prized skull disappeared. Pak concludes by advocating for body donation in Chosŏn for medical progress and offering a prayer for the souls whose bodies he dissected.

From a historical perspective, the article offers insights into colonial Korean medical education under Japanese influence (Kyŏngsŏng Medical College, Dr. Kubo), prevailing scientific theories (anthropometry, racial comparisons via anatomy like 'smiling muscles'), the sources of cadavers for dissection (executed prisoners, the indigent), and societal attitudes towards death and autopsy (Pak's personal fear vs. call for donation). The mention of Sun Yat-sen reflects the political aspirations some Koreans held during the colonial period, seeing education as a potential pathway. The anecdotes about specific autopsies, particularly the death row inmates, provide a grim window into the colonial penal system and the pathologist's unique viewpoint.
