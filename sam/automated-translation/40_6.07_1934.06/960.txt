오! 나의 햇님-伊太利 民謠
(O! My Sun - Italian Folk Song)

맑기도하다 환한우리햇님
How clear it is, our bright sun
구진비걷고 하늘도높다
The gloomy rain has cleared, and the sky is high
실바람살에 물감풀어놀로려
In the gentle breeze, as if playing with 풀어놓으려 (unfurled?) paint
紅실에남실떤다 오! 내햇님
It trembles like red thread, rippling, O! my sun

(-Chorus-)

Mature love, shining eyes
오! 그대의눈 나의사랑 우리님
Oh! Your eyes, my love, my dear
窓을감돌아 빚을떨치고
Circling the window (窓), shedding light (빚)
그대는혼자 분셰수하고
You alone wash your face (분셰수)
깃버서웃나 얼사노래가락
Laughing with joy (깃버서웃나)? Hooray (얼사), the tune of the song
紅실에남실떤다 오! 내햇님
It trembles like red thread, rippling, O! my sun

봄노래-草川民謠
(Spring Song - Ch'och'ŏn Folk Song / Grass River Folk Song)

진달내꼿 송이송이 山에웃을때
When azalea flowers (진달내꼿) bloom cluster by cluster, smiling on the mountain (山)
라라라 레라라라 해는라라라
La la la, re la la la, the sun is la la la
우리窓에 어우러저 모다라라라
Gathered together at our window (窓), all (모다) la la la
學校길 五里五里 모다라라라
The five-ri (五里) road to school (學校), all (모다) la la la

보리밭속 종달새에 하늘이울때
When the sky rings with skylarks in the barley field
라라라 레라라라 해는라라라
La la la, re la la la, the sun is la la la
송아지도 엄메엄메 모다라라라
The calf also goes "moo moo" (엄메엄메), all (모다) la la la
홰를치고 암닭수닭 모다라라라
Flapping their wings from the perch (홰를치고), hens and roosters, all (모다) la la la

밭두덩에 냉이꽃이 패여오를때
When shepherd's purse flowers bloom (패여오를때) on the ridges of the field
라라라 레라라라 해는라라라
La la la, re la la la, the sun is la la la
바닷가에 모래모래 모다라라라
The sand, sand by the seashore, all (모다) la la la
하얀얼골 빨안얼골 모다라라라
White faces, red faces (빨안얼골), all (모다) la la la

별별은파라케 바다를퍼돌고

**Summary**

This article presents Korean translations of four folk songs by Yun Kŭgyŏng, published in the magazine *Samch'ŏlli* in 1934. It includes lyrics for two Italian folk songs ("O Sole Mio," "Santa Lucia"), one Russian folk song ("The Song of the Volga Boatmen," titled "Boat Song"), and one song titled "Spring Song" identified as "草川民謠" (Ch'och'ŏn Folk Song or Grass River Folk Song), which appears Korean in theme and content despite the article's overall title "Overseas Folk Songs." The translations use Korean language characteristic of the 1930s, including older spellings and vocabulary. For historians, the article reflects the introduction and popularization of Western folk music in colonial Korea through translation. The inclusion of a potentially Korean song under the "Overseas" category is intriguing—it might indicate an adaptation of a foreign melody, a misclassification, or a deliberate juxtaposition. The translations themselves are valuable linguistic artifacts showcasing Korean language usage and translation practices of the era.
