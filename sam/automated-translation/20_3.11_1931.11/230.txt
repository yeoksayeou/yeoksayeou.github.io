My Year Behind Bars, Various Stories from a Year Ago
						Yun Yŏngok

It was exactly this time last year when, after enduring three months as a defendant, treading through the quietly disappearing white snow to the prosecutor's office starting in February 1929, I received a sentence of one year in prison and was serving time at Hamhŭng Prison. Carrying a yoke (목도) on my thin shoulders, I worked, stumbling this way and that, pouring sweat.

The workday was 11 hours long, and tormented by heat and hunger, by the time the evening electric lights came on and we returned to our cells, our bodies were utterly exhausted. However, until 9 PM, we had to sit rigidly with knees pulled up, just blinking our eyes, 졸고 (dozing off) while anxiously waiting for the bell signaling sleep. During my time buried there, in a prison damp with the tears and sighs of young women, there were many sad, funny, and painful things.

Sometime during that cold autumn, fierce snowstorms relentlessly beat against the dim iron bars. Two young women lay groaning in sickbeds in Cell 7. The woman known as No. 26 had received a six-year sentence and still had four years remaining, while the other woman was being released upon completing her sentence, leaving still sick.

At 8:30 AM, while working in the factory, suddenly the clanging sound of an iron gate and the pitiful cries of a sick person were heard. Looking out the cell door, I saw one sick prisoner following the departing sick prisoner. With a pale, gaunt face, her head wrapped in a white towel, barely stepping over the threshold, she cried out, "Go well, dear sister. I feel like I will die here. Please, please find my young children who are crying and wandering looking for their mother at home. Please, I beg you." As she said this, she collapsed right there. The departing prisoner, unable to turn away, just stood there, and the two sick women held each other and wept. At that moment, prompted by the guard's urging, she finally left. All the prisoners watching this wept until the collars of their red prison uniforms were soaked. At that time, a young hunchbacked woman (곱쟝이), about three ch'ŏk <48> tall, sitting nearby, let out a long, deep sigh and said poetically, "Flower-like lives, what is this red clothing? What is this bean-mixed rice? This life, tough as the cold floor, refuses to break." Hearing this, all the prisoners burst into laughter. Now, there was a woman called No. 4 in the factory who farted quite often – whenever she laughed, she would inevitably fart. So, a stream of farts would come out, short ones. Then, a guard standing nearby said, "What kind of woman farts like that? If you fart, you'll be punished," and XX [cursed]. The prisoner retorted, "Hey! What am I supposed to do if a fart comes out? It hurts my stomach if I hold it in! You keep giving us bean-mixed rice, making us fart – so what do you want us to do?" Guard: "So you're saying you did nothing wrong?" Prisoner: "So? The prisoners' ages ranged mostly from 17 to 23; there were many young women. There were also several elderly mothers. Hamhŭng Prison had few prisoners, so work couldn't be assigned regularly. We did whatever we were told, this or that. All the laundry for male and female prisoners was done in the women's section. Also, the padded winter clothes were made by the women, and to get slightly thicker winter blankets or clothes, you needed a few kind-hearted and bold women prisoners. However, among the educated women, there were only my older sister and myself. There was a set weight for the cotton padding. So, when the cotton fell short of the required weight, I would put it in. Therefore, without the guards knowing, I would steal 3 or 4 sheets (쟝) of cotton and add them. If a guard happened to pick it up and found it heavy, they would tear it open again, and I would put it back in, adding more again. Since all the prisoners did this by agreement, even if the two guards were very sharp, they were often deceived. The clothes had to be quickly taken to the men's section (男監), so they were taken as they were. Even for quilted socks (다비), even if they weren't worn out, we would deliberately cut them with scissors and patch them with 2 or 3 layers. When caught doing this, we would face punishment (징벌). The rice portion given for one meal was divided into three meals. But because we were fellow prisoners, sympathy never left us, and we continued such rule-breaking unchangingly. In the cell where I stayed, there were about 10 young women aged 17 to 22. Their passion for learning to read was incredibly strong. So, we requested one volume of a Korean language (조선어) textbook and another book. During the day, we went out to the factory to work, and in the evening, after returning, we used the time before sleep to study. At night, the chief guard and section guards would occasionally come by and peep through a tiny hole. Wondering if perhaps some food <49> had appeared!! When they left, the women would get up, put water in a washbasin (세수댕이), dip their fingers in the water, and practice writing characters on the wooden floorboards (널쟝). Seeing these women practicing, I felt deeply moved and found the strength to teach them. However, when winter came, their fingers would freeze, making it impossible to write, which was very inconvenient. So, I got pencil lead (연필알) from the factory, hid it in my mouth, brought it into the cell, and practiced on the floorboards. Among them, however, was one spy (스파이) who reported it to the guard. The report went all the way up to the chief guard, so inevitably, I had to bow my head to the ground until my forehead peeled, begging for forgiveness. All the women (omitted in original)

Even before the dawn brightens the iron bars, the bell rings to wake up, and all the prisoners shudder. When we wake up, the blankets are covered white with frost (이슬), and the wooden floorboards (넑쟝) are wet as if submerged in water. Prisoners sleeping in such conditions go out again to the factory with stark naked bodies (샛발간 몸둥이). The path there is outside, so we walk through accumulated snow and ice. Then, we are given clothes as cold as sheets of ice (어름쟝), eat a lump of bean-mixed rice breathlessly, and start working. Among them, those sewing clothes often get lost in many thoughts, pricking their fingers with the needle more than ten times a day. After bending and straightening my back for a while, the cold would disappear, and sweat would flow from my hands and forehead. At such times, what always comforted me was a comrade of mine who was still a defendant (被告). Peeking out through the dimly visible iron bars, she would throw words of comfort: "Ah, comrade! How painful must it be? How cold must it be? Are you hungry? But comrade, don't despair because you have nothing, don't cry because it's painful. **Summary**

Yun Yŏngok recounts her year spent in Hamhŭng Prison around 1930 after being sentenced for reasons unstated. She details the harsh conditions: grueling 11-hour workdays, meager food (bean-mixed rice), cold, lack of sleep, and emotional distress. The narrative includes poignant vignettes: the tearful farewell between two sick inmates, a humorous incident involving a prisoner's uncontrollable flatulence and a guard's reaction, acts of solidarity like secretly adding extra cotton padding to winter clothes, and the prisoners' earnest desire to learn Korean, studying secretly at night despite the risks and discomfort. Yun reflects on the camaraderie and mutual support among the inmates, particularly recalling comforting words from another prisoner, and connects her prison experience to the sight of laborers years later. For historians, this memoir provides a rare, firsthand female perspective on prison conditions during the Japanese colonial period, highlighting the specific hardships faced by women inmates (including lack of adequate clothing/bedding, types of forced labor like sewing and laundry), the demographic makeup (many young women), the prisoners' resilience and resistance through small acts of defiance and mutual aid, and the pursuit of education (specifically Korean language) even under severe repression. The mention of political undertones ("comrade," "laughter will shatter the heavens") hints at the potential political nature of her or others' imprisonment.

1일 작업시간은 11시 동안인데 더위와 주림으로 시달니다가 저녁 전긔불이 올때면 감방으로 드러오는데 퍽도 몸이 고달펏슴니다. 그러나 밤 9시까지는 딱딱 무릅을 끌고 눈만 깜박어리며 잠자라는 종소래만 안타가이 기다리면서 졸고 안저 잇지요. 젊은 부녀들의 눈물과 한숨으로 눅눅히 저즌― 감옥에 나도 함께 파무처 지내는 동안에 슬푼 일도 우스운 일도 괴로운 일도 만하엿슴니다.
그 언제이든가 추운 거을이엇슴니다. 사나운 눈보라가 침침한 철창을 마음것 부드칠 때 병석에서 신음하든 젊은 두 부인이 제 7방에 잇섯슴니다. 26호라는 여자는 6년이란 징역을 밧고 아직도 4년의 형기가 남어 잇섯고 한 여자는 만기출옥이 되어 그냥 병든 몸으로 출옥하게 되엇슴니다.
아츰 8시 반 공장에서 일하는데 별안간 철깍 하는 쇠문소래와 병자의 애처러운 울음소래가 들녓슴니다. 그래서 간방문을 내여다 보닛간 출옥하는 병자의 뒤를 따라 갓치 잇든 병자 한 사람이 뱃뱃히 여윈 얼골에 힌 수건으로 머리를 동이고 겨우 문지방을 넘어 나오면서 부대 잘 가시오 아우님.- 나는 이곳에서 죽을 것 갓해요. 부대 집에서 엄마를 찻고― 울고 헤매는 어린 자식들을 좀 차저보아 주시오. 부대부대 붓탁이오 하면서 나오다가 그대로 쓸어지겟지요. 잇는 이 보다 떠나는 이에 발길은 참아 돌아서지 안어서 그냥 두 병자가 서로 붓들고 우는 것을 보앗슴니다. 때에 간수의 독촉 소래에 그만 떠나고 말엇슴니다. 이것을 보는 모든 죄수들은 붉은 옷깃이 다- 젓도록 울엇슴니다. 그때에 신장이 3척 가량＜48＞ 되는 젊은 곱쟝이 안젓다가 긴-한숨을 한번 길-게 쉬면서 하는 말이 꼿갓흔 인생 붉은 옷이 웬 말인고 콩조밥이 웬 말인고 찬바갓치 질긴 목슴 끈허지지를 안는다. 하면서 시 갓흔 말을 하지요. 그래서 모든 죄수들은 우슴이 터지엿슴니다. 그런데 공장에 4호라는 여자가 이섯는데 방귀를 참 잘 꾸는데- 우슴을 우스면- 얼마든지 방귀가 나옴니다. 그래서- 줄방귀가 줄-- 작고 나오지요. 그러닛간 엽헤 잇던 간수가 하는 말이- 여자가 무슨 방귀를 그럭케 꾸어 방귀를 꾸면 징벌을 주어 하면서 XX겟지요. 죄수의 말이 「안이 여보시요. 방귀가 나오는 것을 엇덧케 하란 말이요! 참으면 배가 압흐지요-콩조밥은 짝구 주어 방귀를 만드러 노으면서- 그럼 엇덧케 하란 말이오.」 간수 「그래 잘못하지 안햇단 말인냐」 죄수 「그래 콩밥먹고 방귀를 꾸는 것이 웨? 잘못이란 말이요.」 하닛간 간수가 (중략-원문)
