Article Title: 便紙(對話) (Letter (Dialogue))
Author: 항가리아 모르낼(원작), 金岸曙(역) (Original by Ferenc Molnár, Translated by Kim An-sŏ)
Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

Letter
						Original by Hungarian Molnár, Translated by Kim An-sŏ

A spacious and splendid room, its furnishings display beautiful taste and refinement. Antique-style furniture is arranged along the walls, and a beautiful carpet covers the floor. It is a clear, clean spring morning. An electric heater hums. However, that sound is its final song, as the heater will be removed tomorrow.

The Husband sits at the desk, smoking a Luzon cigar and reading the newspaper. It is clear to anyone that he is currently satisfied with the spring, with the newspaper, with the cigar, and indeed with the entire plan of his life. However, upon closer inspection, it is also true that a certain weariness is visibly apparent. Suddenly noticing someone outside the door, he goes to open it himself instead of the maid. The postman enters.

Husband: A letter?
Postman: Yes, sir. It's the mail. (Hands over a bundle of letters and leaves.)
Husband: Thank you for your trouble. (Takes the letters and places them on the desk.)
Wife: (From the inner room) Who came, dear?
Husband: No one. Just the postman.
Wife: Didn't anything come for me?
Husband: I'll see if something came or not. Just hurry up and get ready.
Wife: I’m almost done getting ready… (Sound of a wardrobe door closing.)
Husband: (Rummaging through the letters, humming something cheerfully.) This one’s a catalog from the Oriental Bank. A new public bond offering… should I get one? Ah, this one has a prize contest from “Movie Times.” “Write down the name of your favorite movie actress and send it in.” Good. Who should I write? And this one must be the bill reminder from the Riding Club. Is it two months overdue? This is a wedding announcement addressed to my wife. (Calls towards the inner room.)
Hey, Myŏngju?
Wife: Yes?
Husband: Yŏng'ae is getting married.
Wife: To whom?
Husband: To a man named Hyŏngp'al.
Wife: Haven't you seen the letter that came for me?
Husband: Wait. I'll be right out. (Sorts through the letters.)
Wife: What kind of man is this Hyŏngp'al?
Husband: Hyŏngp'al? Why wouldn’t you know? He’s a young, handsome man. (Picks one letter from the pile and looks at it strangely.) What could this be? It’s addressed to Myŏngju, on thick grey paper, the handwriting is a man’s… Where on earth did it come from? It’s from P'yŏngyang. From P'yŏngyang! What story could be inside this? It’s handwriting I haven’t seen before. (Examines the envelope closely, front and back.) It’s strange, no matter how I look at it. (His expression gradually darkens.) Without a word, without asking anything, without needless suspicion, I should just give this letter to Myŏngju. That’s the right thing to do. Give this letter to Myŏngju.<79> That’s the right thing to do. I mustn’t let on, not by a glance or a slip of the tongue, that this letter, more than the other letters, more than Yŏng'ae’s wedding announcement, has given me strange thoughts. That’s the right thing to do. I shouldn’t be suspicious! Even so… (Still looks at the letter uneasily.) No, no. There is reason to be suspicious. This sort of thing isn’t right.

Wife: (From the inner room) Maid! Take these yellow velvet slippers. And wipe the black ones well and bring them back. Understand?

Husband: What is she doing, taking up all this time getting ready? Giving it to her as is is the right thing, but truly, the cunning approach would be to close my eyes tightly and… (Moves to tear open the letter.) Yes, truly, the cunning approach seems to be to tear it open. Since Myŏngju hasn’t even put on her slippers yet, asking for the yellow velvet ones to be taken away and the black ones to be brought after wiping, it looks like getting ready will take time. Right, using this time… It’s wrong. But this is my right… (Moves to tear the letter again, then stops.) I must think carefully. Since the letter was addressed to my home, it’s unlikely they wrote anything I shouldn’t know. There probably isn’t anything suspicious. There’s probably no secret. Isn’t it delivered just as properly as the Oriental Bank’s new bond offering or Yŏng'ae’s wedding announcement? Still, I can’t say there aren’t two possibilities. First, they might think I’m still traveling—when I sent a letter from P'yŏngyang, I said I’d be back in four days, but I returned earlier than scheduled. Given the circumstances, perhaps I’m destined to discover my wife having fun with some fellow, unconsciously acting out the role of the husband in a comedy. In that light, perhaps this single letter will allow me to grasp my wife’s true nature. Another thing, the fact that my wife asked twice if a letter came for her clearly shows she’s expecting one, and it’s true she’s been interested in the mail lately. Moreover, this morning it’s even more apparent; asking not just once but several times is strange, no matter how I look at it.

Wife: Didn’t a letter come for me?

Husband: (His expression changes.) What did you say…?

Wife: (After a short pause, in a firm voice) Didn’t a letter come for me? Why don’t you answer?

Husband: (As if having decided) No, it didn’t. (Quickly puts the letter into his jacket pocket.)

Wife: Maid, come fasten the buttons on this dress.

Husband: How can I tear it open? (Puts his hand in his pocket.) This blasted letter feels like it’s burning through my pocket. It feels like a hole will burn through. Not through the outside, but into my own flesh. Stealing my wife’s letter… this is the first time I’ve done such a bad thing. To snatch my wife’s letter… should I call it snatching? No, I stole it. I definitely stole it. It’s despicable. Even if I don’t tear open the letter, haven’t I already done something worthy of contempt? Now I can’t just give it to her either. But I can’t just stand here hesitating like this. In any case, I’ve interfered too deeply. Didn’t I lie to my wife and steal the letter that came for her? Violating my wife’s privacy is no small transgression. Playing detective on one’s own wife is extremely base. Is that all? It’s not like my wife has committed any obvious crime. What would I find out if I tore open the letter? Perhaps it’s a request for a donation from some charity, or perhaps a notice from some shop to buy goods. Or maybe it’s a letter from a poor relative asking for assistance, or perhaps it’s an invitation—there’s no way to know. Rather than suffering like this <80> because of my conscience, perhaps I should just close my eyes, confess everything, reveal my foolish shame, and be done with it. Or perhaps, to preserve my dignity amidst my own wrongdoing, I should just throw this letter into the embers and be done with it? And then maybe buy my wife something nice… there are things that look expensive without costing too much money…

Wife: (From inside) There’s no fun in wearing this dress. Maid, bring out that bright blue one. The bright blue color will be better anyway.

Husband: Well, it seems Myŏngju hasn’t changed her clothes yet. Meanwhile, should I think this matter over a bit more and decide how to handle it?

Wife: (From inside) No. That one probably doesn’t look good either. Maid, what do you think? Isn’t it better to wear this one after all?
Maid: (From inside) Of course. The weather is warm, so please wear that one, miss.
Wife: (From inside) This dress looks good, right? Then don’t bother taking out the bright blue one.

Husband: That maid is quite helpful. Alright, what should I do? How should this problem best be handled? If I tear open this letter and it starts with “My dearest beloved…” what should I do? (Lets out a sigh.) How can I assert that such a thing is impossible? (Yet, as if enjoying this worry) Alternatively, if the letter begins with the phrase “My dear squirrel,” that would be even less amusing. Does it make sense for another man to call my wife “My dearest beloved”? However, if one considers it a respectful term showing reverence, it might not be a cause for great concern. But if he has become familiar enough to call another man’s wife “squirrel,” this is unforgivable. Isn’t it clear proof that Myŏngju has become some fellow’s plaything? That truly is an unbearable disgrace. If my angelic wife is playing the role of a “squirrel” satisfying some man’s desires, that truly is a terrifying thought. I absolutely cannot forgive it.

Wife: (From inside) Still, maid, wouldn’t wearing the bright blue dress suit me better?

Husband: (Muttering to himself) She can’t seem to make up her mind which would be better. While she’s dithering about which dress to wear, she has no idea how her own happiness might turn out. Yes, maybe this letter is written like this: “My dearest angel, I want to meet secretly this afternoon. I am just waiting for you to come.” Or maybe it’s written, “My dear squirrel, let’s meet this afternoon in our secret nest. I delight in you crawling over my shoulders like a squirrel.” Who knows? Either way, it’s unpleasant, and above all, the wording is unbearably disagreeable. If that’s the case, what should be done? If this were so, I haven’t considered what to do in such a situation; indeed, I never dreamed I would be having such thoughts in my own home. From today onwards, on this path called married life, am I now obliged to investigate every little stop along the way, wondering about this or that? By the way, why haven’t I ever suspected my wife until now? In plays and novels, doesn’t the husband suspect his wife’s fidelity or conduct months before the truth is revealed? The husband usually notices things like his wife’s “strange behavior,” or “restless movements,” or “loss of appetite for several weeks,” or “a gloomy demeanor suppressing a cheerful spirit.” Thus, suspicion gradually grows in the husband’s mind, naturally leading him to notice unfavorable actions, doesn’t it?<81> Alright, let’s say suspicion has now arisen in my mind. Then I cannot simply let it remain as suspicion. Rather than taking action, I must carefully consider the situation. I cannot act decisively without grasping absolutely certain, absolutely clear, irrefutable evidence. Yes, after obtaining absolutely certain evidence, I must pursue the culprit relentlessly, or else catch my wife in the act, or confront her when her face is red and she cannot make any excuses. If I make accusations without such definite proof, the situation might become unpleasant instead. Therefore, a wise man burns the dangerous bridge before him. Since it seems likely to lead to an unfortunate situation, burning the dangerous bridge that easily leads to unhappy consequences is the right thing to do. Then I shall throw this letter into the fire. It’s not that I particularly want to know its contents anyway; what does it matter what it says? If I don’t know, that’s the end of it. Let me throw this letter into the embers. Now Myŏngju seems to have finished changing. By the time she appears in this room, nothing will remain but the ashes of the misfortune I was contemplating. This must be the wise approach. (He stands up, takes the letter out of his pocket, and goes towards the brightly burning stove. Just then, his wife enters. The husband, startled and flustered, fumbles awkwardly trying to put the troublesome letter back into his pocket.)

Wife: What you're hiding is a letter, isn't it?

Husband: (As if dumbfounded) Me?

Wife: What do you mean, ‘me’? Isn't that a letter you just stuffed into your pocket? Why are you trembling like a murderer being interrogated? Stop it. I know. A letter came from some woman.

Husband: It's not like that. It’s a letter from a friend.

Wife: (Calmly) Stop lying. Who do you think you're fooling? Just looking at your face, I can tell whether you're lying or telling the truth. Hand over that letter.

Husband: What do you want it for?

Wife: Never mind what for, just hand it over.

Husband: (Sullenly) I won't give it to you. (Moves closer to the stove.)

Wife: Why are you going near the stove?

Husband: I should just burn this letter and be done with it. (Moves to burn the letter.)

Wife: (Grabbing her husband's arm) You can't burn it! Don't! Now, hand over the letter. Let me have a look. What kind of woman sends a letter that can't be shown to the wife? Who sent it?

Husband: I told you I can't show it... Don't do this. This letter didn't come for me. Or rather, it came addressed to me, but I can't show it to you.

Wife: Stop talking nonsense and give me the letter. You don't have the nerve to tell a single lie. Don't you know your own affairs? Your job is always to be honest and confess everything without hiding anything. Now, give me the letter from that woman.

Husband: (Standing bravely near the stove until now) Does that make sense? I can't show you the letter. Rather than show this letter, it would be better to burn this arm in the fire. (Shakes off the arm his wife is holding and throws the letter into the fire. The letter burns fiercely.)

Wife: (Shouting) Don't throw it in! Argh, you threw it in!

Husband: (Panting) Why wouldn't I burn such a thing?

Wife: (Crying) Well done. From now on, whatever you say, I will never believe you. You have a secret. A terrible secret. Hiding some woman somewhere, that’s why<82> you couldn't show me the letter? If you had just shown it to me, I wouldn't resent you like this. Do you think hiding it like that means no one will know? From now on, I won’t be fooled by you anymore. Just because you burned the letter, do you think your crime disappears? It doesn't disappear. No matter how you try to explain!

Husband: It's not like that, actually that letter was...?

Wife: I don't want to hear it! Who do you think believes your words? Why did you suddenly get startled and throw that letter into the fire? Thinking nothing would happen then? Don't say anything. I don't want to hear any more of it now. (Goes into the inner room, crying.) Truly foolish, you've been deceiving me all this time? (The husband, looking displeased as if things hadn't gone well, picks up his hat and cane, opens the door leading outside, and leaves.)

Postscript: This is a free translation of the Hungarian Molnár's work, and none of the original's charm could be conveyed. (Translator) <116>

**Summary**

This one-act play, translated from Hungarian author Ferenc Molnár, depicts a husband's spiraling suspicion. Upon receiving the mail, he finds a letter addressed to his wife, Myŏngju, from P'yŏngyang, written in unfamiliar male handwriting. He agonizes over whether to open it, eventually deciding against it but lying to his wife that no letter arrived for her. Consumed by guilt and imagining incriminating contents ("My dearest beloved," "My dear squirrel"), he debates confessing or burning the letter. As he moves to burn it, his wife enters. She catches his furtive movements, assumes the letter is from another woman, and demands to see it. In a panic, the husband throws the letter into the stove, confirming his wife's suspicions and destroying her trust. From a colonial Korean history perspective, the play is interesting as an adaptation of a European work, reflecting the reception of Western culture and dramatic forms. It portrays the psychological anxieties and shifting dynamics within an urban, likely middle-class, Korean household of the era, grappling with themes of modernity, marital trust, and privacy, all while using the Korean language and specific locations like P'yŏngyang, offering linguistic and social insights from 1931.

便紙
						항가리아 모르낼(원작), 金岸曙(역)
넓고 화려한 방, 그 설비는 善美로 아름답은 취미를 보인다. 담벽에는 고대풍의 가구가 버려노혓고 마루에는 아름답은 타펫이 깔녓다. 맑고 새끗한 봄날 아츰 電熱器가 응응 소리를 내인다. 그러나 그 소리는 최후의 노래로 전열기는 明日이면 뜻어내일 것이다.
남편은 책상 압헤 안저서 呂宋煙을 피여 물고 신문을 본다. 그는 지금 봄에 대하야 신문에 대하야 여송연에 대하야 또는 그 자신의 全 생애의 기획에 대하야 만족해하는 것이 누구에게나 알 수가 잇다. 그러나 자세히 보면 분명히 알 만큼은 권태한 빗이 보이는 것도 사실이다. 갑작이 문 밧게 누구가 온 것을 보고 그는 어멈 대신으로 몸소 나아가 문을 여니 우편부가 들어온다.
남편  편지요?
체부  녜. 편지올시다. (편지 뭉텅이를 주고 갓다.)
남편  수고햇슴니다. (편지를 바다다가 책상 우에 놋는다.)
안해  (안방에서) 누가 오서슴닛가?
남편  안야. 체부가 왓서.
안해  나한테 무어 오지 안엇서요?
남편  왓나 아니왓나 내 볼께. 빨니 준비나 해요.
안해  독촉은 다 됏는데 (의거리門 닷는 소리가 낫다.)
남편  (편지들을 두적두적하면서 기색이 조와 무어라고 노래를 한다.) 이놈은 東洋銀行 목록이로구나. 新公債募集이야 하나 드러볼가. 이것은 「映畵時代」 懸賞이 잇구나. 「당신이 조와하는 영화 여배우의 이름을 적어보내십시요.」 조쿠나. 누굴 써서 보낼가. 또 이놈은 乘馬俱樂部 회비독촉이지. 두달치나 밀녓지? 이것은 녀편네게 오는 결혼통지서고. (안방을 향해 부른다.)
여보  明珠?
안해  웨요?
남편  永愛가 결혼을 한다고.
안해  누구하고=
남편  亨八이라는 사람하고.
안해  나한테 편지온 것 업서요?
남편  기달녀요. 내 곳 나갈께. (편지들을 골은다.)
안해  亨八이란 엇던 사낸가요.
남편  亨八이란 웨 몰나? 점고 잘생긴 사내지. (편지들 속에서 한 장을 골나 들고 이상히 드려다 보면서) 이것이 무엇일고. 明珠한테 온 것인데 도틈한 회색 조희에 필적이 사내솜씨니... 대관절 어듸서 왓슬가. 平壤서 온 거지. 平壤서! 이 속에 무슨 이야기가 잇슬가. 보지 못하든 필적이야. (자세히 封筒을 바로 보고 뒤집어 보면서) 암만해도 이상한걸. 안해  듯기 실허요. 당신의 말을 누가 미들줄 암닛가. 웨 깜작 놀내서 그 편지를 불에다 집어 너헛서요. 그러면 아모 일도 업슬줄 알고 아못 말 말아요. 인제는 다 듯기 실허요. (울면서 안방으로 들어간다.) 참말 몰낫드니 여태 나를 속여왓서? (남편은 일이 잘 아니 되엿는 듯시 얼골빗이 조치 안아서 모자와 단장을 집어들고 길까로 난 문을 열고 나아간다.)
