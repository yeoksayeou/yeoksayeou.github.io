Autumn's Emotion, the Bride's Cheerfulness
						Yi Kyŏngwŏn

Port's Sorrow
-The drawn-out, sobbing notes
of the autumn violin's
sad weeping sound
pierce my soul, pierce my soul (Verlaine)
-Reciting such sad poetry makes my heart ache and sting. -It aches and stings just like the heart of a sailor (마드로스) weeping with nostalgia.
-The oval-white mast, the thick funnel, the white deck, the massive steamship (긔선)—why does everything look so sorrowful today? Today's departure feels like a final farewell.
-Don't cry. Please don't cry. Ah—those eyes, those tears always melt my heart.
-Perhaps the wind is strong at this port, making tears fall.
-Please stop crying. I'll gently wipe them with a handkerchief. I am smiling. Look. Suni smiles like this. Smiles.
-Ah! I hear the departure whistle. Well then...
-Please have a safe trip. When this autumn passes, I will wait for winter. When winter comes, spring won't be far behind, right? I will count the days, count the days and wait.
-Take care of your weak body, and raise the child in your belly well.
-Hurry and board the ship. Until the ship's shadow disappears, until the white seagulls turn black, I will stand here waving my handkerchief. Looking at the sea, waving my handkerchief. I will send my dreams far beyond the horizon.

Moonlight's Character
A garden on a moonlit night.
The cold moonlight shines brilliantly upon the clumps of grass, trees, and pond. Just as one feels choked when drinking water too quickly all at once, everything in the garden seems choked by the overly abundant moonlight.
A young man sits on a bench, his posture quite disheveled from long meditation and anguish.
From afar, the mournful tune (哀調) of a balalaika drifts over. Its player, presumably a wandering exile, plays a heart-piercing, sorrowful tune (哀調) that flows ceaselessly.
Perhaps also choked by the moonlight, it certainly feels like sobbing—a dizzying, sorrowful tune (哀調) in every phrase.
The young man suddenly stands up from the bench. Resolve shines in his eyes. In his hand, somehow, a Browning pistol (쁘로닝) is gripped. The young man raises the chilling weapon and points it at his temple.
The next moment, with the sound of a gunshot, the young man's body stumbles and falls onto the grassy patch.
Only the cold moonlight—the guilty moonlight—shines brilliantly upon the corpse.
A fallen leaf, blown by the wind, lonely flutters down and covers the young man's bleeding forehead (니마).

Prison Cell Tragedy
-My beloved wife (안해)! The path I take is not one of defeat. Seeing the thousands of comrades who follow me growing day by day, <114> I walk this path with peace of mind. The defeat of one person like me is not the defeat of the class I belong to. I hope you will not be too surprised by this final letter—
He wrote this letter in blood and tucked it into the cotton padding of clothes to be secretly sent out just last night.
Another day passed, and tonight arrived, his heart sinking further into a dark pit. He finally decided on tonight.
Moonlight slanted in through the high window. The moonlight crossed the dark prison cell and drew a cold, straight line on the opposite wall.
Through the window, he could guess the season was autumn by the look of the leaves on the trees on the hill outside and the attire of the women passing by on that hill. At night, he felt the deepening autumn through the cold moonlight shining in the window—and indeed, this moonlight and the world outside on the hill had brought him to his recent state of mind. The magic of the season had severed his nerves strand by strand and shredded his emotions piece by piece.
Huddled in a dark corner away from the moonlight, he crawled sluggishly towards the patch of moonlight. From between the pages of a book brought in during visitation, he picked out something formless and held it up to the moonlight. It was a single strand of hair. At the same time, it was his wife (안해) within the prison cell. With this single strand of hair, he had long thought of his wife and yearned for the outside world.
He put the moonlight-soaked strand of hair into his mouth and swallowed it. This was his final, desperate love sent to his wife in this world.
Next, he turned directly towards the moonlight. His face, twitching and contorting, seemed to be both laughing and crying, but neither laughter nor tears flowed. He was definitely grimacing towards the moonlight. But the sound of the grimace did not escape his lips—his mouth had already forgotten laughter, forgotten tears, forgotten speech.
He held this grimacing expression for five minutes, ten minutes, twenty minutes—truly for half an hour.
Around the time half an hour had passed, his emaciated body collapsed powerlessly right there.
It was an extremely quiet posture, like a pile of ashes collapsing inward.
The unfeeling moonlight still shone coldly upon his face.
The dreams of his comrade in the next cell must have been troubled.
From the mouth of the suicide who bit his tongue, a stream of fresh blood overflowed.
Somehow, the emotion of autumn feels like this.
Its expression is melancholic, its character gloomy.
This pure nihilist seems to try to govern even human life with its nihilistic (니히리스틕한) feelings. When one receives its sentimental gaze, sorrow arises, and when one encounters its gloomy character, tragedy is born.
But the strange thing is that this nihilist, on the flip side of its nihilism, possesses a cheerfulness quite distant from it.
We must excavate this cheerfulness from it, absorb it, and also transcend (揚棄) it.
Autumn's cheerfulness. For example—

**Summary**

This 1931 Korean text, written as a play or scenario by Yi Kyŏngwŏn, explores the dual nature of autumn. It begins by depicting autumn's stereotypical melancholy and despair through vignettes: a sorrowful farewell at a port, a woman's suicidal jump from a wharf after a presumed violent act, a young man's suicide by gunshot in a moonlit garden, and a political prisoner's suicide by biting his tongue in his cell after sending a final letter to his wife. However, the author argues against this purely nihilistic view, suggesting autumn also possesses an underlying, contradictory cheerfulness. This is illustrated in the final scene of a wedding, where the bride breaks decorum by laughing brightly, infecting the groom, officiant, and guests with her joy. The piece contrasts tragic ends driven by autumnal despair with the unexpected, life-affirming brightness found even in the same season. For historians, the article is revealing of the era's sensibilities and potentially reflects the oppressive atmosphere of colonial Korea through its depictions of suicide and despair, especially the political prisoner's tragic end which hints at resistance and its consequences. The final scene's emphasis on spontaneous joy offers a counter-narrative, perhaps suggesting resilience or the persistence of human connection amidst hardship. The blend of traditional sorrowful tropes with a modern cinematic/theatrical presentation (camera directions, scene descriptions) is also noteworthy.
