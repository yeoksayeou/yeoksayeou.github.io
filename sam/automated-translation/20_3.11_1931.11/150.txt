Recent Yang Kŭnhwan, Whose Release Date is Imminent, Meeting Report on Him Recently, Sitting and Writing an Autobiography, Poems, and a Novel
From Tokyo, Chu Ch’ŏngp’ung

On September 15th, at 10 AM, guided by my acquaintance Mr. Hong Sŏn (洪宣) (a comrade who worked with Yang Kŭnhwan in the Tongjihoe (同志會) at the time), I made my way to Kosuge Prison (小管刑務所) in Tokyo Prefecture. It was to visit Yang Kŭnhwan (梁槿煥).

Thus, after waiting for about an hour in the cold-winded corridor of the prison, we finally met Yang Kŭnhwan, who walked out vigorously with a guard behind him, in the visiting room.

The three of us exchanged firm, firm handshakes. From then on, for about 30 minutes, we talked about various things. I will now report the contents.

A height of 5 feet 2 inches, a round face, long feet which, immediately after the assassination of Min Wŏnsik (閔元植), were said to have provided the clue for his arrest because the geta sandals he wore were unusually large, and bright, sparkling eyes!

To my eyes, he was full of vigor. He looked healthy. The demeanor always seen in bold, large-minded people who have nothing to fear and nothing to hide seemed to have grown soundly upon this health.

I asked,
"So, how is your health?"
"Until recently, I was very sturdy, but lately, I feel a bit weakened. However, it's not to the extent that comrades should worry."

If he, who says his health has declined, has such good color and vigor, how full of spirit must he have been <33> in his earlier healthy days? I asked again about his recent life in prison, and he, with even a slight smile lingering on his face, replied,
"Until last year, I often rebelled quite a bit for the sake of weak comrades inside prison, but recently, shall I say I have become quite meek."

Then, continuing, he said that during the full 8 years he has been in prison, by obtaining brush and paper through either rebellion against or requests to the prison warden and guards, he has written some works. These include:
One full-length novel of about 1,000 pages, written in Japanese (of course, they wouldn't let him write in anything but Japanese) under the title
"Sahae ŭi Kwang" (四海의 光 - Light of the Four Seas),
which has been completed. Also, an autobiography written under the title
"Na ŭi Huhoe" (나의 後悔 - My Regret),
and a
"Sijip" (詩集 - Poetry Collection)
compiled from about 300 poems, each over 130 lines long, have been completed. He says he doesn't know which day he will be released, but when he gets out into the world, he intends to publish these. The moment I heard his own account that over the long period of nearly 10 years, he has calmly written essays, poems, and novels, the volume of which already exceeds 2,000 manuscript pages, I received a strange shock. (Omission - original text)

Within that dark cell surrounded by cement, steel bars, and icy air, he must have found time, perhaps writing one poem a day, one page every two days. Yang Kŭnhwan, called a felon by the world, must have poured his entire heart and soul into these three works over 8 years. What might the traces of his tears, sighs, and laughter be? Wanting to see the manuscripts, I looked again at his face, and Yang, the man of will reminiscent of rock, fixed his piercing gaze on a corner of the wall, staring as if to bore through it.

As the visiting time grew increasingly imminent, I asked him if he had any hopes. He replied,
"I wish to meet my elderly mother in my hometown once."
As he spoke, hot teardrops instantly welled in his eyes, rolled down his cheeks, and his whole body trembled silently. I couldn't bear to look directly and turned away. Mr. Hong, who came with me, also lowered his head. In this quiet and heavy moment, he wept softly. How could he not cry? As a son, ah, when would these complex tears, held back for 8 years, be washed away refreshingly?

After a while, having barely calmed his emotions, he mentioned <34> that according to a recent letter from his hometown, his elderly mother has become very frail lately, and word came that she might pass away within two years. Then, grasping my wrist tightly again, he requested,
"My daughter Chŏngja (貞子) (14 years old this year), born from a Japanese wife when I was in Tokyo, should be staying at Mr. Yu Yŏngjun's (劉英俊) house in Kyŏngsŏng (京城). Please ensure she doesn't harm her health by studying too hard and grows up well, so that we can meet when I get out of prison later."
He asked this two or three times and finally asked me to convey his regards to all the press organizations in Korea and to friends, both known and unknown.

Urged by the guard, after more than 30 minutes (remainder of original text missing - Kukp'yŏn) <35>

**Summary**

This 1931 article recounts Chu Ch’ŏngp’ung's prison visit to Yang Kŭnhwan, a Korean independence activist nearing release after 8 years for assassinating Min Wŏnsik. Despite declining health, Yang appeared vigorous and revealed he had spent his imprisonment writing extensively: a 1000-page novel in Japanese ("Light of the Four Seas"), an autobiography ("My Regret"), and a large collection of poetry. The interviewer was struck by Yang's resilience and intellectual output under harsh conditions. The meeting concluded emotionally, with Yang expressing deep longing to see his aging mother and asking the interviewer to check on his daughter living in Kyŏngsŏng. For historians, this piece offers a poignant glimpse into the personal life and mindset of an imprisoned activist during the colonial period, showcasing his sustained intellectual resistance through writing, his enduring familial ties, and the emotional toll of his long incarceration. The mention of specific works written in prison provides valuable insight into his thoughts and activities during this time.

出獄期 迫頭한 最近 梁槿煥, 自敍傳과 詩, 小說을 쓰고 안졋는 그의 最近 會見記
						東京에서 朱淸風
나는 지난 9월 15일 오전 10시에 친지 洪宣씨(당시 梁槿煥과 가치 同志會의 일을 하든 동지)의 안내로 東京府 下 小管刑務所로 거름을 옴겻다. 梁槿煥을 차저보기 위함이다.
그래서 우리는 형무소의 찬바람 도는 廊下에서 약 1시간 가량이나 기다리다가 마츰내 간수를 뒤에 세우고 활발하게 거러 나오는 梁槿煥을 면회실에서 맛나게 되엇다.
우리 세 사람은 굿고 굿은 악수를 교환하엿다. 그로부터 우리들은 약 30분 동안 여러 가지 이약이를 하엿다. 그 내용을 이제 보도하리라.
5척 2촌의 키, 둥글둥글한 얼골, 閔元植 암살 즉후에 그가 신든 게다짝이 유달니 커서 체포의 단서를 지엇다는 길다란 발, 빗나는 어글어글한 두 눈빗!
내가 보기에는 그는 원기가 왕성하엿다. 건강하게 보엿다. 조곰도 겁내거나 조곰도 숨길 것이 업는 담대하고 규모 큰 사람에게서 늘 보여지는 그 기상은 이 건강 우에서 건전하게 자란 것이란 늣김을 준다.
나는
「그래 몸은 엇더세요?」
「이전까지는 대단히 튼튼하엿더니 요지간은 좀 약해진 듯 하오. 그러나 동무들이 염녀할 정도까지는 아니지요.」
건강을 상하엿다는 그가 그러케 혈색도 조코 원기도 잇거든 이 전날 건강하엿슬＜33＞ 때야  엇더케나 패기에 찻슬가. 나는 다시 최근에 옥중에서 지내는 이약이를 무르매 그는 싱글싱글 미소조차 얼골 우에 남기며
「작년까지도 옥중에 드러 잇는 약한 동지들을 위하여 그네들에게 만흔 반항도 하엿지마는 최근의 나는 퍽 온순하여젓다 할가요.」
그러고는 다시 말을 이어서 그동안 옥에 잇슨지 만 8년 동안 형무소장에게 와 감옥의 감독자들에게 혹은 반항을 통하여 혹은 부탁을 통하여 붓과 조히를 어더서 저술한 것이 잇는데 그것은
「四海의 光」
이라는 題로 일본문으로 쓴(물론 일본문으로 밧게 더 쓰게 못하니까) 약 천 頁 가량 되는 장편소설이 하나 탈고 되엇고 그러고 또
「나의 後悔」
라는 題로 쓴 자서전과 한 首가 130구 이상 되게 쓴 시 300여 편을 모아서 만든
「詩集」
한 권을 탈고하엿는데 이제 어느날 출옥하게 될는지 모르지만 세상 박갓헤 나가는 날 이것을 출판하겟노라고 한다. 거지 반 10년 갓가운 긴 세월을 두고 그는 悠悠自若하게 논문을 쓰고 시를 쓰고 소설을 써서 그 분량이 이미 2000여 매를 초과하엿다는 그의 자술를 듯는 순간 나는 이상한 충격을 밧엇다.(중략-원문)
세멘트와 강철 창살과 차듸찬 공기에 싸힌 저 어둑컴컴한 감방 내에서 그는 틈을 어더서 하로에 한 수를 짓고 이틀에 한 장을 쓴 것이리라. 세상에서 일커르는 중죄범인인 梁槿煥은 8년 동안을 두고 이 세 개의 저술에 全心力을 담엇스리니 그의 눈물과 한숨과 우슴의 자최는 과연 엇던 것일가. 원고를 보고 십흔 생각에 그의 얼골을 다시 처다보니 암석을 생각케 하는 의지의 人 梁은 형형한 눈빗을 벽의 한모통에 던지고 그곳을 뚜러질 듯시 바라보고 잇다.
면회시간이 점점 迫到함으로 그에게 나는 무슨 희망이 업느냐고 무르니까 그는
「고향에 게신 노모를 한 번 만나고 십노라.」
하며 두 눈으로는 금시에 뜨거운 눈물방울이 돌고 구으러 뺨 우로 흐른다. 그리고는 전신이 소리업시 떨고 잇섯다. 나는 참아 正視하지 못하여 외면하엿다. 갓치 갓든 洪씨도 머리를 숙엿다. 고요하고도 무거운 이 순간 그는 작고 우는고나. 엇더케 아니 울가. 人子 되어서 아아 8년을 참아 내려오든 이 복잡한 눈물 어느때에 시원히 씨츨 것인가.
한참만에 겨우 격동을 진정한 그는 최근에＜34＞ 고향에서 온 편지에 의하면 그의 늙은 어머니는 근래에 노약이 심하여 아마 2년 안으로 세상을 떠나실 것 갓다고 소식이 왓다 한다. 그러고는 다시 나의 손목을 꽉 붓잡으며
「東京 잇슬 때에 일본부인의 몸에서 난 딸 貞子(올에 14살)가 京城 劉英俊씨 댁에 나잇슬 터인데 너무 공부하느라고 몸을 상하는 일이 업시 잘 자라다가 내가 후일 옥에서 나가거던 맛나게 하여달나.」
고 두 세 번 부탁하고 마즈막으로 조선 안에 잇는 각 언론기관과 未知旣知의 친구들에게 문안하여 달나고 당부한다.
