Giants Amidst the Storm Clouds
The Character of Stalin
By Chin Yŏngch'ŏl

1
When speaking of the archetypal dictator of the bourgeoisie, no one will forget Italy's Mussolini; similarly, when speaking of the representative of the proletarian class dictatorship, Stalin cannot be omitted. Just so, Stalin is a figure standing at the forefront of the world's two great political poles, leading and commanding the class XX at the head of the global proletarian movement. Since Stalin is such a globally significant figure, whether one agrees with the proletarian movement or not is a separate issue, and it would not be too much of a fault to dissect the hidden aspects of such a great man.
As mentioned above, because Stalin is at the vanguard of the class poles, those on the side of the bourgeoisie slander, insult, and hate Stalin, while those on the side of the proletariat, quite conversely, praise, respect, and admire him. Thus, due to differing class interests, the figure of Stalin transforms into either an enemy or a leader. Such a phenomenon, as long as class antagonism and XX exist, cannot but be affirmed as rather natural. Setting aside such discussions for later, let us calmly explore from a third-party perspective what kind of person Stalin is—a man who is both an enemy and a leader to hundreds of millions—and what activities he is engaged in.

2
Stalin first began to make a name for himself within Russia after the 1917 revolution, when the Red-White conflict was most intense, by demonstrating his skill as a military leader. <18> Although the famously known Trotsky was the People's Commissar for Army and Navy Affairs at the time, in terms of actual military leadership, one could not help but point to Stalin as the highest authority. It was an anecdote from the era of War XX-ism that wherever Trotsky commanded and suffered defeat, Stalin would be sent, and he would invariably achieve victory after victory. From this time, Stalin's name as a military man began to resound throughout the country.
Later, Stalin succeeded Lenin as the leader, organizer, and builder of the Russian Communist Party, taking on the important role of Responsible Secretary of the Russian Communist Party from 1921, when Lenin's illness became critical. From this point, Stalin effectively became Lenin's complete successor and the number one figure within the Russian party. Thus, he has continued his steady activities to this day. His name became internationally known again after Lenin's death in 1924, starting from the theoretical confrontation with Trotsky, which led to internal strife and oppositional struggle within the party. At this time, the bourgeoisie who hated Soviet Russia excessively boasted about Russia's internal discord. It was from here that Stalin's name became known even to the bourgeoisie.
Subsequently, after purging the Trotsky faction from the party, Stalin's name was incessantly heard among the international proletariat due to the struggle against the new opposition faction of Zinoviev (then Chairman of the Third International Party) and others since 1926, coupled with the determination of the guiding theory for the Chinese Revolution and its proper guidance. Furthermore, since recently overcoming the Bukharin faction, which held rightist prejudices, and initiating the famous Five-Year Plan for economic construction to industrialize the Soviet Union, Stalin has become a subject of discussion worldwide, eliciting either criticism or praise, making his name a global point of interest.

3
Now, let us re-examine what this figure's past life was truly like! Stalin was born in 1879 in Tiflis, a small city and industrial center in South Russia, as the son of a Georgian cobbler. The Georgian people currently occupy the area between Lake Baikal and Outer Mongolia, with a population of about 500,000, and are part of the Soviet Union. Being a nameless person from an uncivilized Asian tribe under the Tsarist autocracy at the time, who could have known that a Stalin would emerge in later generations, and who would have meticulously recorded his family lineage? Although the person who knows oneself best currently would be oneself, since he himself is a class warrior, there is no way he would announce his familial relations, so there is no way to know the details.
Stalin, born into such a family from an uncivilized land, received education at home until the age of 15, and only at 15 did he enter the <19> theological seminary belonging to the Orthodox Church in the city of Tiflis. This was in 1893, five years before the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party was organized, a time when Russia was actively importing Western liberal ideas, bourgeois liberal thought was flourishing, and simultaneously, Marxist ideas were beginning to spread due to the growth of the working class. Consequently, while political groups of bourgeois liberalism such as the "Land and Freedom" Party and the "People's Freedom" Party emerged, workers' organizations like the "Workers' Liberation" and the "South Russian Workers' League" were also forming in industrial centers like Petrograd and South Russia. These ideological trends inevitably spread to the student population, causing conflicts among them.
Thus, class-based organizations also emerged among students. At this time, in 1897, Stalin became active as the leader of the Marxist student group at his school. Stalin was then merely an 18-year-old boy. However, it is said that even then, Stalin fully understood that he was receiving double XX as a member of a minority nationality within Tsarist Russia. Seeing this, one can understand that Stalin's later becoming the greatest theorist on the national question was by no means a coincidental event that happened overnight.
The reason Stalin became the leader of the student group within his school was that he had already established contact with the workers' X-legal organization called the "Workers' Liberation Front League." Since the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party, founded in March 1898, was established based on such workers' party organizations, Stalin naturally came under its guidance. Thus, under the underground guidance of this organization, he actively participated in the workers' organizational movement and economic strikes within the city of Tiflis.
Through these activities, Stalin joined the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party in 1898, and from then on, he emerged onto the streets as a complete professional XX-ist.

4
Since then, Stalin, along with Lenin, has been a model Bolshevik who committed absolutely no errors in the proletarian movement within the party until today. He was first arrested in May 1900 while active as a member of the Tiflis City Committee of the Russian Social Democratic Labour Party, leading a workers' strike under the committee's guidance. Subsequently, in February 1901, after two years in preliminary hearing (豫審) at the infamous Batumi prison, he was sentenced to exile (流刑) in Siberia.
However, he soon escaped from there and operated as an X-legal person. Stalin's characteristic lies in his unconditional obedience to party orders. If the party ordered him to go and work in a certain region, he would never leave that place. Thus, even if arrested and exiled while working as an X-legal person in a city, he would always escape and return to that city within two or three months. Such iron will created the Stalin of today.
It is said he employed such methods consistently, despite being imprisoned and exiled three times. While engaged in these activities, Stalin was elected to the Party Central Committee in 1912, and during the 1917 XX, he was one of the seven leaders guiding the XX. He then worked as a party organizer until becoming the interim Responsible Secretary in 1921, a position he has held continuously since, serving as the party's supreme leader to this day. Currently, he is not only the leader and organizer of the Russian Communist Party but also undeniably the leader and supreme authority of the Comintern, recognized as the most brilliant vanguard of the world proletarian movement.

5
Finally, let's talk about his private life. There is talk that he is originally <20> so rough (粗暴) in nature that few people maintain personal friendships with him. This apparently became an issue even within the party when Lenin was alive and caused considerable problems during the struggle against the opposition factions. However, since this minor personal characteristic has caused no hindrance to the party's major work, it hasn't become a significant issue, but he himself always acknowledges and speaks of possessing this rough (粗暴) nature.
Also, it is an anecdote that Stalin has never been abroad, either in exile or for any other reason, since he emerged onto the movement scene. Consequently, rumors circulate that Stalin speaks no foreign languages and knows nothing about foreign affairs. However, his not going into exile abroad was due to his strict adherence to party discipline, never leaving the region assigned to him. It is said that although not fluent, he can get by in German (獨逸語).
He is also often reputed to be uneducated. Of course, given that he dropped out of the Tiflis Seminary (a secondary school) and there's no mention of him having a student life after becoming a professional XX-ist, one might say so from a bourgeois perspective. However, he possesses the richest understanding of Marxism. Especially concerning the national question, he is a theorist second to no scholar in the world.
Currently, he is said to live with his wife and three children in the house outside Moscow where Lenin used to reside. He reportedly spends an average of 15-16 hours a day expending energy on duties at the party headquarters within the Kremlin Palace (고레물닌宮). Despite these complex duties, it is said he does not forget any minute detail and retains memory of even the smallest matters throughout the entire party.
What will this man of steel, Stalin, now set in motion and how, at the forefront of the global class X-front? This is a point of attention in the turbulent world situation. (End)

Gandhi's Arrival at a London Station
By Kim Ch'unsik in London

I enclose a photograph here. This is the scene of India's Gandhi meeting Chaplin in the slum district of X-Street after arriving here. Yesterday, I went to Manchester on business and arrived back in London on the evening train. The "London Times" and various other local newspapers carried articles suggesting Gandhi and Chaplin might meet in the slums.
When Gandhi arrived in London, I went to the Central Station and joined the crowd to welcome him, but I had no opportunity to meet him. So, on this day, I rushed to his lodging, hoping somehow to create an opportunity to meet Gandhi.
Perhaps London citizens were less interested in the issue of the Indian people's liberation than in the curiosity of seeing an exotic guest named "Gandhi"—a peculiar being who, as a vegetarian, rejects 20th-century machine civilization. Likewise, regarding Chaplin, perhaps they were less interested in him as a nihilistic thinker or in American cinema than in seeing Chaplin the individual, a comedian who had broadcast numerous scandals and strange tales from the Hollywood sky. Moved by this "journalism" mentality, even gentlemen with dignified beards stood at the entrance of the slum area to witness the meeting of the two men. The newspaper circulated rumors that Chaplin, wary of reporters, <21> would not appear openly but would surely show up in disguise—his trademark shabby hat, suit, cane, and mustache that prompts laughter.
However, to confess honestly, I met neither Gandhi nor Chaplin that day. The London citizens who waited all day ended up, like me, seeing no spectacle at all.
That evening, the "Idle Talk" section of the newspaper reported that their scheduled meeting was canceled because too many reporters and onlookers were guarding the passages of the slum area, and they decided to meet secretly some other day.

After this incident, I directly asked a key figure of the Indian National Congress party here in Britain for a letter of introduction and visited Gandhi's lodging not just once or twice, but each time, Gandhi was incredibly busy. He had gone to the India Round Table Conference venue to negotiate with British Prime Minister MacDonald, so my repeated visits inevitably ended in failure.
Eventually, I met the secretary of the Indian delegation. I asked him to convey that a Korean university student studying in Britain had visited several times seeking to meet Gandhi, and I left my lodging address. The next day, a letter arrived from the secretary, purportedly a message from Gandhi:
"It is regrettable that I cannot find an opportunity to meet now, as I meet with the British side during the day and with the Indian delegates at night for conferences. However, if you visit again in about a week, I will meet you."
I now intend to meet Gandhi in a week. I will write and send my account of the interview then, but for now, let me conclude this letter by recounting the scene of meeting Gandhi inside Victoria Station. Please read with amusement.

The newspaper report that Gandhi, released from Bombay jail in India just days prior to attend the India Round Table Conference, was arriving in London today seemed to have caused extraordinary excitement and wonder among British citizens. From before the train's arrival time, a large crowd gathered in the square in front of the station, police security was tight, and prominent figures from the British government also gathered one after another at the station.
Until moments before his arrival, I couldn't help but feel a strange excitement, recalling the song his friends supposedly sang during his elementary school days in India, "Look! The great British rule over the colonized people. For what reason? For what reason?", his subsequent indignation, his time as a lawyer in South Africa striving to save his fellow Indians, his activities leading the Indian masses during the Great War, and the scenes of anti-British movements like the salt monopoly protests across India last winter. Honestly speaking, (middle part omitted - original text) ... so at the station (latter part omitted - original text) <22>
Gandhi recently said something like this:
"Our goal is complete self-government. And it lies in joining the British Empire on terms of absolute equality. Among my comrades too, there are those who say that British politicians will never grant India a status of absolute equality. However, I hold a different opinion.
The day is coming when the center of the Indian nation will be New Delhi, not Downing Street.
When we achieve India's independence, it must be under not just the British flag, but under both the British and Indian flags. etc., etc."
Time is short now, so I will stop here. I will write a detailed account of my interview with Gandhi later and send it to "Samch'ŏlli."

Meeting with Commander-in-Chief Chang Hsüeh-liang - A Recollection from a Year Ago -
By Yi Kŭngno, Doctor of Economics

Looking at the newspaper extras distributed daily with the ringing of bells throughout Seoul lately, it seems Mr. Chang Hsüeh-liang is currently sitting in Beiping, anxiously plotting something regarding the volatile situation in the Three Eastern Provinces. This morning, it was reported he even sent his confidant Ku Wei-chün to Chiang Kai-shek in Nanking.
What comes to mind now is my first meeting with this adventurer of the Far East, which was on the evening of October 20th last year.
The meeting place was the Northeast Border Defense Army Command Headquarters (東北邊防軍司令長官府) in Fengtian (奉天). I had arranged the meeting beforehand by requesting an introduction from Chief of Staff Hŭi Hap (熙洽), and went there after receiving a formal official document (公文) asking me to come that evening.
Driving through the crowded streets of Fengtian city this way and that, I arrived at the Command Headquarters. There were so many visitors at the entrance; about ten cars were parked, and countless rickshaws.
At the main gate, six regular soldiers stood guard sternly with bayonets fixed, questioning each arriving guest. Here, one could glimpse the strict military discipline of the Northeastern Army.
I showed the introduction from the Chief of Staff, received permission to enter, and followed the guide into the Western-style first reception room.
There was no tranquil atmosphere of leisurely gazing at the southern mountains here; instead, an extremely tense feeling prevailed. The demeanor of the uniformed officials busily passing through the corridor, <23> the constant ringing of telephones, the loud sound of horse hooves from the barracks—everything conveyed the air of a new-era military living in the atmosphere of a new age.

Across from me in the first reception room sat three obese Westerners—perhaps British diplomats or American financial capitalists—also waiting for the Commander-in-Chief to appear, just like me.
The coming and going of figures affiliated with Britain and America (英米系) surrounding Manchuria and Mongolia (滿蒙)!—In this small fact, we could discern the foreshadowed elements of the great Manchurian Incident (滿蒙大事變) that would occur a year later.
After a short while, an old man appearing to be around 60 called out "Mr. Yi (李선생)" and led us to another adjacent reception room, informing us that the Commander-in-Chief would arrive shortly. The floor of that room was covered with a tiger skin, and a portrait of his father, Chang Tso-lin (張作霖), hung on the wall. It gave an impression of simple decoration.
Barely enough time to smoke a cigarette had passed when Mr. Chang Hsüeh-liang, accompanied by a uniformed staff officer, came out and politely greeted me.
The instant he appeared, he gave less the impression of a rugged warlord commanding legions than that of a gentle nobleman who might have just emerged from reading Confucius and Mencius in a thatched hut.
Picking up the name card I had presented, he asked me to pronounce my name in Korean and smiled broadly.
Regarding the content of our conversation, when I asked how he could protect the Koreans residing in Manchuria (在滿朝鮮人), who were constantly suffering persecution, he replied that he would protect them. He added that he was in the process of creating a plan to dispatch officials, like diplomatic personnel, even to the border regions to protect the education, agriculture, lives, and property of Koreans.
I conveyed the opinions and sentiments of people within Korea regarding the recent Ji-Dun Line Incident (吉敦線事件). He remarked that while the incident that had already occurred could not be helped, he would handle matters appropriately in the future. I will omit the rest of the conversation here.
After about 30 minutes, the meeting concluded. Escorted to the gate by a young officer, I started back. Dusk had already fallen upon the broad streets of Fengtian, with electric lights flickering restlessly here and there amidst the settling darkness. From within the barracks gate, the sound of the evening bugle echoed off the ancient, venerable walls of Shenyang Castle (瀋陽城).
At that time, I had gone under the commission of the Sin'ganhoe (新幹會) and various organizations in Seoul to investigate the truth of the Ji-Dun Line Incident and simultaneously console our compatriots in the affected areas.
That he has already lost his base in Fengtian and is observing the Northeast from a corner of Beiping—who can say that the transformations of human affairs are constant? (End) <24>

**Summary**

This multi-part article from the November 1931 issue of *Samch'ŏlli* presents profiles of three major contemporary figures: Joseph Stalin, Mahatma Gandhi, and Chang Hsüeh-liang. The main piece by Chin Yŏngch'ŏl offers a detailed account of Stalin's life, rise to power, ideological struggles (with Trotsky, Zinoviev, Bukharin), key policies (Five-Year Plan), personality, and global significance, attempting a neutral stance while acknowledging the polarized views he evoked. Following this are two shorter reports: Kim Ch'unsik writes from London about the public excitement surrounding Gandhi's visit for the Round Table Conference, a failed attempt to witness a meeting between Gandhi and Charlie Chaplin, and his anticipation of interviewing Gandhi soon. Yi Kŭngno provides a personal recollection of meeting Chang Hsüeh-liang in Fengtian (Mukden) in October 1930, discussing the protection of Koreans in Manchuria and the Ji-Dun Line Incident, poignantly contrasting Chang's former power with his current diminished status in Beiping after the Manchurian Incident.

From the perspective of Korean colonial history, the article is revealing. Firstly, the extensive and relatively detailed profile of Stalin, written from a declared "third-party perspective" but clearly knowledgeable about communist discourse, indicates the level of interest in and information available about Soviet Russia and communism among Korean intellectuals, even under Japanese censorship (indicated by the 'XX' placeholders). Secondly, Yi Kŭngno's account of meeting Chang Hsüeh-liang directly addresses the precarious situation of Koreans in Manchuria and Korean efforts (via Sin'ganhoe) to engage with Chinese authorities for their protection, highlighting a key issue for the diaspora. The timing, just after the Manchurian Incident began, underscores the rapidly changing and dangerous geopolitical landscape Koreans faced. Kim Ch'unsik's piece on Gandhi reflects the global anti-colonial sentiment and the interest Koreans took in other independence movements, drawing implicit parallels. Historians might find the detailed portrayal of Stalin, the direct account of negotiations concerning Koreans in Manchuria, and the contemporary perspective on global figures like Gandhi valuable for understanding Korean intellectual currents, diaspora issues, and international awareness during this period.

기사제목	風雲 中의 巨物들
기사형태	논설

風雲 中의 巨物들
스탈-닌의 人物
陳榮喆

1
뿌르조아지-의 전형적 독재자를 말할 때는 누구나 伊太利의 뭇솔리니-를 망각치 안을 것이오 푸로레타리아-트의 계급적 독재의 대표자를 말할 때에는 또한 스탈닌을 빼어 놋치 못할 것이다. 이만콤 스탈닌은 세계의 정치적 兩大 極의 선봉으로써 세계 무산계급운동의 선두에 서서 계급적 XX을 지도하고 호령하고 잇는 인물이다. 스탈닌은 이와 가티 세계적으로 문제되는 인물이니만콤 무산계급운동에 동의를 하고 안이 하는 것은 별문제로 하고 이러한 위대한 인물의 그 이면을 해부하여 보는 것도 과한 허물은 안일 것이다.
우에 말한 바와 갓치 스탈닌은 계급의 양극의 선봉임으로 뿌르조아지- 편에 선 사람들은 스탈닌을 중상하고 悔辱하고 증오하지만은 푸로레타리아-트 편에 잇는 사람들은 또한 이와 정반대로 이를 찬양하고 존경하고 흠모한다. 이와 갓치 계급적 이해관계가 다른 데에서 스탈닌이란 인물은 적으로도 변하고 지도자로도 변하게 되는 사람이다. 이와 갓흔 일은 계급의 대립이 잇고 XX이 잇는 이상 오히려 정당한 일이라고 단언하지 안을 수 업는 것이다. 이러한 이야기는 다음으로 미루우고 여하간 수억 천만의 적도 되고 지도자도 되는 스탈닌은 어떠한 인물이며 어떠한 활동을 하고 잇는 사람인가를 제삼자적 입장에서 냉정히 탐색하여 보기로 한다.

2
스탈닌이 위선 로서아 안에서 일홈이 나게 되기는 1917년 혁명 이후 赤白의 싸홈이 가장 격렬하여 젓슬 때 군사적 지도자로써 그의 수완을 발휘하는 데서부터 일홈이 나기 시작한＜18＞ 것이다. 당시 육해군 인민위원장으로 저- 유명한 트로츠키-가 잇섯지만은 사실상 군사적 지도에 잇서서 최고의 권위로는 스탈닌을 지목하지 안을 수가 업섯든 것이다. 트로츠키가 지휘하야 패전하는 곳마다 스탈닌을 보내면 언제든지 百戰百勝을 하엿든 것이 戰時 XX主義 시대에 한 일화이엿든 것이다. 이때부터 스탈닌은 군사가로써 그의 일홈이 전국에 흔날니기 시작하엿든 것이다.
그 후에 스탈닌은 레닌의 뒤를 니어 로시아 공산당의 지도자이오 조직자이오 건설자로써 로서아 공산당 책임비서의 중임을 갓게 되기는 1921년 레닌의 병이 위독을 전할 때 부터이엿든 것이다. 이때부터 스탈닌은 사실상 레닌의 완전한 후계자로써 로시아 당내에 제 1인이 된 것이다. 그리하야 오날까지 꾸준한 활동을 하여왓든 것이오. 다시 그가 세계적으로 일홈이 나기는 1924년에 레닌의 死後 처음으로 트로츠키-와의 이론상 對峙로부터 당내에 內訌이 잇서서 대립투쟁을 하게 된 때부터이다. 이때에 쏘베트 로시아를 미워하든 뿌르조아들은 로서아의 內訌을 너무도 크게 자랑하게 되엿든 것이다. 여기에서 스탈닌의 일홈은 뿌르조아들에게 까지 들니게 되엿든 것이다.
다음에 계속하야 트로츠키- 일파를 당내에서 배제한 이후 1926년 이래 지노비에프(당시 제 3국제당 의장) 일파의 신반대파와의 투쟁과 아울러 中國革命의 지도적 이론의 결정과 밋 그 정당한 지도로 인하야 萬國 푸로레타리아-트 사이에 스탈닌의 일홈은 쉬지 안코 들니게 된 것이다. 더욱히 최근에 우익적 편견을 가젓든 북하-린 일파를 극복하고 쏘베트 연방의 공업화를 위하야 유명한 경제건설 5개년 계획안을 창시하면서부터 스탈닌은 세계 방방곡곡에서 혹은 비난 혹은 찬미하게 됨으로써 그의 일홈이 세계적으로 문제꺼리가 되게 된 것이다.

3
그러면 이 인물의 과거의 생애는 과연 어떠하엿든가를 다시 살펴 보기로 하자! 스탈닌은 1879년에 南露의 공업중심지인 지프리스란 적은 도시에서 조르쟈 종족의 洋靴 직공의 아들로써 태여낫든 것이다. 조르쟈 종족은 현재 빠이칼 湖와 外蒙古 사이에 자리를 차지하고 잇는 약 50만의 인구를 가진 주민인데 쏘베트 연방 안에 들어 잇다. 당시 쓰아-ㄹ 전제정치 하에서 일홈도 업는 亞細亞 미개족의 한 사람이엿스니 후세에 엇지 스탈닌이 생겨날 줄 알고 누가 그 家系를 잘 기록해 두엇슬 것인가? 현재 자기를 잘 아는 사람은 자기 자신 밧게 업스련만은 그 자신이 계급전사이니 만콤 자기의 가족적 관계를 발표할 리는 만무한 것임으로 이를 자세히 알 길은 업는 것이다.
스탈닌은 이러한 未開地人의 가정에 태여나서 15세까지 가정에서 교육을 밧다가 15세 되논 때 비로소 지프리스 市의＜19＞ 정교파에 속하는 신학교에 입학하게 되엿든 것이다. 때는 1893년 로서아 사회민주노동당이 조직되기 5년 전 임œ¼로 로서아에서는 盛히 서구의 자유주의사상을 수입하게 되여 뿌르조아 자유주의사상이 발호하엿스며 한편으로는 노동계급의 성장으로부터 맑스주의사상이 물들기 시작한 때이엿다. 그리하야 한편으로는 뿌르조아 자유주의의 「토지와 자유」黨 「인민의 자유」黨 등의 정치적 집단이 발생되는 일방 공업중심지인 페트로 그라-드와 南露에는 노동자 해방과 南露노동자 동맹 등이 생겨날 때이엿다. 이러한 사상적 경향은 학생층에도 감염되여 서로 충돌을 이르키게 하지 안을 수가 업섯든 것이다.
그리하야 학생들에게도 계급적 조직이 생기게 되엿든 것이다. 이때 즉 1897년에 스탈닌은 자기 학교의 맑스주의학생단의 首領으로써 활동하게 되엿다. 그때 스탈닌은 겨우 18세 밧게 안이 되는 소년이엿다. 그러나 그때 발서 스탈닌은 帝政露西亞 안에서 약소민족이란 이중의 XX을 밧는다는 것을 충분히 이해하고 잇섯다고 한다. 이것을 보면 후일 스탈닌이 민족문제에 대한 가장 큰 이론가가 된 것도 결코 一朝一夕에 된 우연한 일이 안인 것을 알 수 잇는 것이다.
스탈닌이 자기학교 내에서 학생단의 수령이 된 것은 발서 그때부터 스탈닌 자신은 노동자 해방전선동맹이란 노동자의 X합법적 조직과 연락을 매저 오게 된 까닭이다. 그 후 1898년 3월에 창립된 로서아 사회민주노동당은 이러한 노동당의 조직을 기초로 하야 성립되게 되엿슴으로 스탈닌도 자연히 이 조직의 지도를 밧게 되엿다. 그리하야 이 조직의 지하적 지도에 의하야 지프리스 市 안의 노동자의 조직운동과 경제적 스트라이크에 적극적으로 참가하게 되엿든 것이다.
이러한 활동을 하게 됨으로써 1898년에 스탈닌은 로서아 사회민주노동당에 가입하게 되엿고 그때부터 완전한 직업적 XX가로 街頭로 나오게 된 것이다.

4
이래 스탈닌은 당내에서 오날까지 레닌과 함게 무산계급운동에 잇서서 전혀 과오를 범하지 안이한 볼세비키-의 모범이엿든 것이다. 그가 처음으로 체포되기는 1900년 5월 로시아 사회민주노동당 지프리스시위원으로 활동하면서 이 위원회의 지도에 의한 노동자의 스트라이크를 지도하는 때이엿다. 그 후 1901년 2월에 유명한 빠스무-감옥에서 2년간 豫審에 잇다가 시베리아 流刑의 刑을 밧엇든 것이다.
그러나 곳 이곳을 탈출하야 X합법인으로써 활동하엿다. 스탈닌의 특성은 당의 명령이라면 무조건 복종에 잇는 것이다. 당에서 어느 지방에 가서 활동하라고 명령을 하면 언제든지 그곳을 떠나지 안는다는 것이다. 그리하야 X합법인으로 활동하면서 그 도시에서 체포되여 流刑을 당하엿드라도 2, 3개월이면 언제든지 다시 그 도시로 탈출하야 돌어오는 것이엿다고 한다. 이러한 철갓튼 의지가 오날의 스탈닌을 맨든 것이다.
그리하야 3차나 투옥 流刑을 당하엿스나 언제나 그러한 방법을 취하엿다 한다. 스탈닌은 이러케 활동을 해 오는 동안에 1912년에 당 중앙위원으로 피선되엿고 1917년 XX 당시에는 XX을 지도하든 7인조에 한 사람이 된 것이다. 그리하야 당의 조직자로써 활동하다가 1921년에 임시로 책임비서가 되면서 이래 그냥 그 자리를 차지하게 되여 오날까지 당의 최고의 지도자로써 활동하게 된 것이다. 현재 그는 로시아 공산당의 지도자요 조직자인 것은 물론이지만은 또한 콤민테룬의 지도자이요 최고의 권위인 것도 또한 부정할 수 업는 바이며 세계 무산계급운동의 가장 光輝잇는 선봉으로 지목되여 잇는 것도 사실이다.

우리가 교환한 담화의 내용은 만주에 잇서서 늘 박해를 밧고 잇는 在滿朝鮮人에 대하야 엇더케 보호하여 줄 수 업겟느냐 하니까 그는 보호 하겟노라 하면서 변방지방까지, 외교원 가튼 분을 派遺하여 조선인의 교육, 조선인의 농업, 조선인의 생명재산은 보호하려고 案을 만들고 잇는 중이라 대답한다.
나는 금번의 吉敦線事件에 대한 조선내지 인사의 의사와 여론을 전하엿더니 이미 이러난 사변은 엇절 수 업스나 압흐로는 선처하겟다는 말을 남기엇다. 그 밧게 交話는 여기에서 略 하려 한다.
약 30분 후에 회견을 끗마치고 청년장교의 문박까지 바래다 주는 전송을 바드며 回路에 오르니 벌서 奉天의 너른 市街에는 황혼이 내러 덥힌 속에 이곳저곳에 전등만 躁然하게 명멸하며 병영문 안으로 서는 저녁 나팔소리 瀋陽城의 고색 창연한 성곽을 울닌다.
나는 그때 吉敦線事件의 진상을 조사하는 동시에 그 피해지의 우리 동포를 위문하려, 新幹會와 在京 각 단체의 사명을 밧고 갓든 것이다.
그는 이미 奉天의 근거를 일코 北平 一隅에서 東北을 조망하고 잇다 하니 人世의 轉變을 누가 有常하다 할가.(끗)＜24＞
