Secret Notes of a House Call Doctor

The Death and Last Testament of a Young Man with Lung Disease
By Chŏng Sŏk-t'ae, Director of Chisŏng Hospital

It was late autumn in Taishō 12 (1923), and dry leaves fell listlessly one by one in the chilly wind. At that time, I was working as a doctor at a municipal sanatorium in the suburbs of Tokyo, a place housing about a thousand patients solely with lung disease.
Here, tuberculosis patients, all confronting death, groaned in the final stages of their lives, coughing up blood day and night.
I wish to chronicle with tears one tragic event that occurred in this desolate and agonizing world.
Mr. F, a talented student attending Tokyo Chuo University, was a brilliant 22-year-old youth devoted to studying philosophy. However, it seemed the tuberculosis bacilli had been consuming his entire body for quite some time, and he was already in the third stage, his hemoptysis increasing in volume.
Was it a twist of fate that I became this young man's attending physician? Over the course of about a month, as we met and I treated his illness, we developed a mutual human affection.
He was terribly lonely. His soul always shivered as if walking on the ice sheets of the North Arctic Ocean (北永洋).
A gaunt face, a thin waist, yet a prominent nose and shining eyes—nervous yet with talent apparent in his appearance, he truly drew my attention and affection.
He would close the philosophy book he was reading, bend over coughing rackingly, spit out blood, and then, with eyes showing immense trust in me, even manage a quiet smile on his face.
"Doctor, the world is truly desolate, isn't it? Even knowing it's a desolate world, I still find myself endlessly longing for this world."
Then, after closing his eyes for a moment.
"But I must go, mustn't I? Since I am destined to leave this world. I have already braced myself for death. Death. Death!
Why do you doctors try to hold onto a life that is ultimately bound for death, even giving Campor injections until the very last moment? It only adds pointless suffering to the patient—."
I could guess his state of mind. What could be fearful in the eyes of one who had already <30> prepared for death? Wouldn't mountains, rivers, plants, birds, and beasts all be meaningless? Especially when it comes to the affairs of human life, transient as morning dew.
What meaning could a doctor's Campor injection needle hold for such a person—?
Not many days later, the final farewell between Mr. F and me inevitably arrived. Rushing to Mr. F's bedside upon the nurse's urgent call, I found his breathing already extremely labored.
He opened his grey eyes, looked at me, and weakly extended his gaunt, gaunt hand. I firmly grasped his ice-cold hand. It was the last handshake exchanged in this world. Perhaps it was the very last hand Mr. F, having lived in the world for 22 years, would exchange with any living being.
I took out my stethoscope. In my pitiful heart, there was nothing but the earnest desire to save his final departing life through the stethoscope.
At that moment, seemingly refusing to let the stethoscope touch his chest, he turned his head slightly to the side, then looked up at me again with those grey eyes, as if having some grievance, gazing intently at me from afar. His breathing was already short, the blood in his heart had stopped circulating, and it seemed human words could no longer come from his mouth.
In those grey eyes looking at me, there flowed a desperate appeal. There flowed a feeling of entrusting something. Unable to bear it, I squeezed his hand tightly.
"I know your intention."
As if saying that—, and didn't he then grasp my hand with all his might? In the throes of death (死의 斷末魔), what was he trying to appeal to me?
His eyes flickered incessantly, like some signal. I did not forget to signal back (返電) with my own eyes.
But those grey eyes soon closed, and after drawing a final breath, he never exhaled again. The hotly beating blood in his heart stopped circulating altogether.
He had departed this world.
A chill began to spread through his entire body, and in an instant, like wood or stone, he transformed into an inanimate object, a piece of wood, a stone. But the hands we held in life still did not separate.
After a while, the nurse and I concluded the death watch for him, who had no parents or siblings, wiped our tears, and ah—knowing that his death had now truly become death—moved him to the morgue. When moving the body, the nurse brought two letters she found under his bed. One was for his parents, and the other was for me. With trembling hands, I opened the envelope.
"Dear Doctor,
This body and soul came from nothingness and now return to nothingness. Having possessed such a futile life, what could I have possibly hoped for from the world during that time? Yet, being human, at this juncture, I find human society endlessly dear.
Now I must go the way I am destined to go. Somehow, my surroundings feel lonely. Endlessly cold.
Dear Doctor, residing in this cold world, though it was but a brief moment, I caused you truly unspeakable trouble, yet I grieve that I have nothing with which to express my gratitude. Dear Doctor, after I die, please use this body, my final and only possession, for your research by cutting it with a knife and staining it with injections. Then I, vanishing into the underworld, will smile with satisfaction.
Please, then, be well. I am leaving."
The next evening, following Mr. F's last wish (遺志), his corpse was placed on the dissection table (해부대), and I picked up the scalpel. At that moment,
"Pitiful Mr. F! Which sky are you wandering through now? Your friend from life takes a knife to your flesh. Ah, just say it hurts, even once. Cry out!"
I could not help but offer a silent prayer (黙禱). <31>
On the cold dissection table, the foul smell of blood, human skin, and bones lay scattered gruesomely.
Mr. F, it has already been 10 years since then; how quickly time flies. The same autumn has come, and fallen leaves drift listlessly. If your spirit exists, surely occupy my dreams for just one night—
Night of October 14th, 20:00 hours. Record.

The Death of a Child and a Night in a Fishing Village
By Yang Pong-gŭn, Director of Kyŏngsŏng Hyŏphwa Clinic

It was a late autumn night, five years ago from now. I found myself hurrying on an urgent house call to a small fishing village (漁村) on the East Coast called ㅇㅇP'o. After crossing a small stream (川), breathlessly climbing over a high mountain pass, then winding around two or three small mountain bends, and finally cresting a hill, I could see the flickering lights of the fishing village lanterns here and there in the darkness beyond. The sound of waves, driven by the rough coastal wind, crashed noisily, swoosh-swash.
In the moonless high sky, only stars twinkled, and faintly from far across the sea, the "Uh-ah-uh-ah" calls from fishing boats sounded mournful. All this scenery sent a chill through my heart. Following the person guiding me hastily, I walked across the sandy gravel shore and entered a small, one-room hovel (오막사리) right by the sea.
Before I could even close the sliding door, "Ah, Doctor! Please save our Taepburi (대뿌리) [Child's name, meaning 'Bamboo Root,' implying a wish for a long life]. If you just save our Taepburi, whatever your kindness requires, I'll do it... Aigo, aigo, our Taepburi is dying. Please hurry and give him an injection or something. His nose is already getting cold. Aigo, how pitiful, how unfair. Our Taepburi..." Assailed thus by the mother's anguished, rapid urging, I barely had time to collect myself, simply swept up in the desperate scene. First, I felt the child's forehead a couple of times with my palm, realized he had a high fever, took out a thermometer to place under his armpit, and at the same time quickly pulled out my stethoscope to listen here and there on his chest and back, making the diagnosis: "Acute pneumonia (급성폐렴)." The mercury in the thermometer showed a high fever of 40.5 degrees Celsius. The child, lying limp across his mother's lap, his breathing grew increasingly rapid as I watched, and his complexion turned pale. I hurriedly took out the injection kit (注射匣) from my bag and gave him three Campor (칸풀) injections in succession. However, the Campor's strength was too weak to revive the already failing heart, and it was too late. "Taepburi, Taepburi, where are you trying to go? After losing five, six children as they came, I finally held onto you late in life. Your father, over 50, treasured you like a precious jewel (금지옥엽), never letting you out of his sight, yet because of that cursed money, he's out boating day and night, unaware you're sick. Where is he now on this windy night, battling the waves? Taepburi, are you really leaving without seeing your father's face one more time? Ah, Taepburi. Was it because Mommy was always out diving for abalone (전복) that you starved for milk and got sick? What cursed illness could reach this state in just two days, yesterday and today? Ah, Heaven (황천) is heartless too..." Heedless of his mother's anguished cries, the child's eyes closed shut, and his heart stopped. In the low, narrow room, barely furnished, faintly illuminated by the dimly flickering lamp at the head of the bed, how must the dead Taepburi's face have appeared in his mother's eyes? "Taepburi, Taepburi, go peacefully, go well. Born to these wretched poor parents, you couldn't even nurse properly... Now be reborn into a family with lots of money <32><Ref. Article 39 - Original Text> and blessings, live long and well.
Ah, Taepburi..." Thus, his mother, while mourning the departure of his little spirit, seemingly leaving his chilling body for some far-off land, simultaneously imbued her farewell song (餞別) for his young soul with curses against the harsh reality of their impoverished life, where they staked their lives daily on the vast sea. The wind grew rougher, rattling the window, and the sound of the waves, swoosh-swash, became even more tumultuous. The mother's weeping (鳴咽聲)! Taepburi's limp corpse! Should this be called a human tragedy (人情悲劇), like a drop in the vast ocean (渺蒼海之一粟), occurring on the edge of the dark, storm-tossed sea? Ah, where indeed was Taepburi's young spirit headed? <39>

**Summary**

This article presents two poignant memoirs from Korean doctors practicing in the early 1930s. The first, by Dr. Chŏng Sŏk-t'ae, recounts his experience treating a young, philosophical tuberculosis patient (F) in a Tokyo sanatorium. The patient questions the purpose of prolonging suffering and ultimately wills his body for medical research after his death. The second, by Dr. Yang Pong-gŭn, describes a harrowing house call to a poor fishing village (ㅇㅇP'o) on the Korean East Coast, where he arrives too late to save a young child (Taepburi) from acute pneumonia. The mother's grief highlights the extreme poverty and harsh realities faced by villagers dependent on the sea.

From a colonial history perspective, the article offers glimpses into Korean experiences under Japanese rule. The first story shows a Korean doctor working within the Japanese medical system (a Tokyo sanatorium) and treating a Japanese (?) patient (F, studying at Tokyo Chuo University - the patient's nationality isn't explicitly stated but context suggests possibly Japanese or a Korean student in Japan). It reflects philosophical currents of the time regarding life, death, and modernity. The second story starkly portrays the impoverished conditions in rural Korea, the lack of accessible healthcare leading to preventable death, and the fatalistic despair of the poor, offering a ground-level view of life far removed from colonial development narratives. The use of Japanese era names (Taishō) and locations (Tokyo) alongside Korean settings underscores the period's context.

往診醫師의 秘藏 手記
肺病 靑年의 死와 遺書
至誠病院長 鄭錫泰

大正 12년 느진 가을 쌀쌀한 바람에 마른 나무닙이 한아 둘 시름 업시 떠러진다. 그때 나는 東京 市外의 시립요양소라는 폐병환자만 천여명을 수용하고 잇는 곳에서 의사로 잇섯다.
여기에는 모다 死를 응시하고 잇는 폐결핵환자가 인생의 말기에 신음하면서 주야 피를 토하고 잇섯다.
이 쓸쓸하고도 괴로운 세계 속에서 이러난 비참한 사실 한 가지를 눈물로 역그려 하노라.
東京 中央大學에 다니는 수재 F군은 22세의 총명한 철학만 연구하고 잇든 청년이건만 肺菌에 전신을 먹히기 시작한 지 상당히 오래인 모양으로 이미 제 3기에 들고 잇서 그의 각혈분량은 더욱 만하엿다.
내가 이 청년의 주치의가 되엇든 것도 운명의 작란이라 할가. 그와 약 1개월 동안을 서로 맛나고 병을 보아주고 하는 사이에 나와 그는 인간적으로 서로 친애하는 사이가 되엇다.
그는 몹시 고독한 몸이엇다. 그의 영혼은 늘 北永洋 어름장 우를 것는 드시 추워하엿다.
여윈 얼골 가는 허리 그러나 선듯 올나선 콧날과 빗나는 눈 신경질이면서도 才氣가 외모에 나뜨는 그는 실로 나의 注意와 친애를 끄으럿다.
그는 보는 철학책을 덥고 허리를 굽혀 쿡쿡 하고는 피를 배아터 버린 뒤 나를 몹시도 신뢰하는 눈찌로 얼골에 고요한 우슴조차 띄면서.
「선생님 세상이 정말 쓸쓸함니다 그려. 쓸쓸한 세상인 줄 알면서도 역시 이 세상이 끗업시 그리워 짐니다.」
그러고는 한참 눈을 감엇다가.
「그러나 저는 역시 가야 하지요. 이 세상을 떠나야 할 몸이니까요. 저는 벌서 죽엄을 각오하고 낫서요. 죽엄. 죽엄!
결국 오겟다는 죽엄을 의사인 선생들은 엇재서 최후의 一刻까지 캄폴주사를 노으면서까지 붓잡자고 하리까요. 환자에켄 부질 업시 고통만 더하는요―.」
나는 그의 심리를 짐작할 수 잇섯다. 이미＜30＞ 죽엄을 각오한 그의 눈압헤 무엇이 두려운 것이 잇슬가. 산천초목 飛鳥走獸 어느 것인들 무의미하지 안을가. 더구나 草露 가튼 인생의 行事에 이르러서리요.
이러한 사람에게 의사가 놋는 캄풀주사의 침 한 대 거기에 무슨 뜻이 잇슬가―.
그후 몃칠이 못 되어 필경은
F군과 나와는 최종의 마즈막 고별이 잇게 되엇다. 간호부의 급고를 따라서 F군이 드러 누운 병상으로 달려가니 이미 그의 숨결은 몹시 급하여젓다.
그는 회색 눈을 떠서 나를 보더니 여위고 여윈 손을 겨우 내민다. 나는 차듸찬 손을 꼭 쥐엇다. 이 세상에서 마즈막 교환하는 악수이다. 아마 F군은 22년 동안 세상에 사라 오다가 맨 마즈막으로 세인의 생물들과 교환하는 손길이리라.
나는 청진기를 끄내엇다. 애처러운 가슴에 청진기를 통하야 그의 마즈막 가는 목숨을 구하여 주고 십흔 정성 밧게 업섯다.
그때 끄는 청진기를 가슴에 대이는 것을 거절하는 모양으로 골을 약간 엽흐로 돌니더니 다시 나에게 무삼 설원이나 잇는 드쉀œ 그 회색 눈을 떠서 나를 먼-히 처다본다. 그는 이미 숨은 차고 심장의 피는 돌기를 끈처서 입에서 인간들이 쓰는 말이 나오지 안는 모양이다.
그가 나를 바라다 보는 그 회색 눈 거기에는 무엇인가 호소하는 간절한 정이 흘너 잇다. 무엇인가 부탁하는 심정이 흘러잇다. 나는 참지 못하여 그의 손을 꼭 쥐엇다.
「그대의 뜻을 아노라.」
하드시―, 그리하엿더니 그는 필사의 힘으로 내 손을 쥐지 안느냐. 死의 斷末魔 우에서 그는 나에게 무엇을 호소하려는고.
그의 눈은 무슨 신호인가 삘 사이 업시 한다. 나도 눈으로 返電하기를 잇지 안엇다.
그러나 그 회색 눈도 조곰 잇다가 감기여지고 마즈막 호흡도 드러 삼킨채 영영 내뱃지 못하고 뜨겁게 뛰는 심장의 피도 돌기를 아조 끈치고 말엇다.
그는 이 세상을 떠나고 만 것이다.
그의 몸에는 찬 긔운이 전신으로 퍼지기 시작하더니 삽시간에 나무나 돌 모양으로 한 개의 무생물로 한 개의 목석으로 化하고 마는구나. 그러나 우리가 생전에 쥐엇든 손은 아직도 떠러지지를 안엇다.
한참만에 간호부와 나는 부모형제도 업는 그의 임종을 끗내고 눈물을 씨스며 그의 죽엄-이제는 벌서 죽엄이 된 것임을 아아―, 시체실로 옴기엇다. 시체를 옴길 때에 간호부는 그의 침대 밋해 잇더라고 유서 두 장을 가지고 왓다. 한 장은 그 부모에게 또 한 장은 나에게 하엿든 것이다. 나는 떨니는 손으로 그 봉피를 뜨덧다.
「선생이시어
이 몸과 령혼이 허무로부터 나와서 이제 또한 허무로 도라가나이다. 이러케 허무한 생을 가젓스매 그동안에 세상에 무엇이 바랄 것이 잇섯사어리까만은 역시 인생이라 이 마당에 섬애 인간사회가 끗업시 그립도소이다.
이제 가게 된 길을 가야할 것이다. 엇전지 신변이 웨로워집니다. 끗업시 치워짐니다.
치운 세상에 게신 선생이시어 잠간이라도 실로 무어라 말할 수 업는 괴롬을 끼첫사오나 선생에게 감사한 뜻을 표할 아모 것도 가진 것이 업슴을 슬퍼함니다. 선생이시어 저의 마즈막이요 또 유일의 재산인 이 육신을 죽은 뒤에 칼로 찟고 주사로 물드러서 선생의 연구에다 써 주시소서. 그리면 지하에 사라지는 저는 만족하여 웃겟슴니다.
그러면 부대 안녕히 게십시오. 저는 감니다.
그 이튼날 저녁 때 F군의 遺志대로 그의 죽엄을 해부대 우에 올려 노코 나는 칼을 들엇다. 그 순간
