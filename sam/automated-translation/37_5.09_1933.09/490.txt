Publication Date: 1933년 09월 01일
Article Title: 高句麗城址過次 (Passing the Site of a Koguryŏ Fortress)
Author: 石松 (Sŏk Song)
Article Type: 시 (Poem)

高句麗城址過次
						石松

님의 터 잇다 함을
말로만 듯고 가네
예까지 지경임은
누구나 인정커든
엇지타 혈육바든 이 몸이
이제 겨우 알니요.

타는 이 나리는 이
모도다 새 얼골을
강낭이 밧은뵈나
주인은 어대간고
창파가 철뚝을 치니
상전벽해.

시악시의 한숨에는
강낭 숩히 한들한들
나그내의 가슴에는
大陸熱이 푹푹지네.
언제나 님과 나와도
우서불 날 잇슬가.

강낭 밧헤 부는 바람
시악시의 한숨이오
이 가슴에 타는 불은
고렷적에 부튼 불을
바람과 불이 합한다면
무엇인들 못살느리.
(大連旅順間에서)  ＜50＞
＜49＞

Passing the Site of a Koguryŏ Fortress
						Sŏk Song

That the Master's site exists,
I only heard in words as I went.
That the border reached this far,
everyone acknowledges,
But how is it that this body, inheriting the bloodline,
only now comes to know?

Those getting on, those getting off,
all are new faces.
Corn is visible in the fields,
but where did the owners go?
The blue waves strike the embankment;
mulberry fields have turned into the blue sea (滄桑).

With the maiden's sighs,
the corn grove sways gently.
In the traveler's heart,
Continental Fever (大陸熱) steams intensely.
Will there ever be a day
when the Master and I can laugh together?

The wind blowing in the cornfield
is the maiden's sigh;
The fire burning in this heart
is the fire ignited in the Koryŏ* era (*likely meaning Koguryŏ).
If wind and fire combine,
what could possibly not be revived?
(Between Dalian and Lüshun) <50>
<49>

**Summary**

This poem, written by Sŏk Song while traveling between Dalian and Lüshun, reflects on the lost history and territory of the ancient Korean kingdom of Koguryŏ. The poet laments only now realizing the extent of Koguryŏ's former domain, contrasting the past glory with the changed landscape and unfamiliar people of the present. A sense of loss and longing pervades the verses, mingling with a fervent "Continental Fever" – a burning desire or ambition related to the mainland – and culminating in a hopeful rhetorical question about the potential for revival if the people's spirit (fire) and the current circumstances (wind) unite. For historians, the poem is significant as an expression of Korean nationalism and historical consciousness under Japanese colonial rule. It uses the legacy of Koguryŏ, whose former territories included parts of Manchuria, to symbolize lost sovereignty and perhaps implicitly critique the current political situation, channeling historical grief into a yearning for future resurgence. The mention of "Continental Fever" is also notable, reflecting broader geopolitical currents and aspirations of the time.
