Nollyang
Ella diŏ ŏhŏya
Ne wŏl ne ro구나.
Along the green willow path
Entering North Fragrant Mountain (北香山).
Eheiye ŏiya
Ne wŏl ne ro구나.

Spring water (春水) flows abundantly (落落)
Wild geese fly gracefully (훨훨)
The tall straight pine (落落 長松)
Crackles, snaps, breaks
And falls
Only willow branches remain
Chihaja chŏlsiguna.

Panga T'aryŏng (Pounding Song)
Good, it is good
In the falling leaves blown
By the wind
The weeping Kimsaeng (김생)
If heard unfeelingly <80>
Though there's no related old man (관게할배)
The vast blue sky (悠悠蒼天) is earnest
The sound, the sound
Eheru, it is sorrow.

The refrain is good
Looking up
Ten thousand valleys (萬壑) and a thousand peaks (千峰)
Looking down upon myself
It is a white sandy field (白沙地).
The bent and old tall pine (長松)
Cannot withstand the fierce wind (廣風)
Half dancing
A red-railed, painted pavilion (朱欄畫閣) hangs in the blue sky (碧空).
Parrots (鸚鵡) and peacocks (孔雀)
Come flying gracefully.

Susimga (Song of Sorrow)
In those vast and hazy (蒼茫) clouds
News of the departed is completely cut off (頓絶).
When will those clouds lift
To see the bright moonlight?

Moon white (月白), snow white (雪白), heaven and earth white (天地白)
Mountains deep, night deep (山深夜深), each heart full of sorrow (各 愁心).
Ah, the feelings of being in a foreign land (客地 心事)
Are truly sorrowful (哀然). <81>
<80-81>

**Summary**

This article presents the lyrics of three Korean folk songs: "Nollyang," "Panga T'aryŏng" (Pounding Song), and "Susimga" (Song of Sorrow). These songs express themes of nature, travel, longing, and sorrow, using vivid imagery drawn from the Korean landscape and traditional sentiments. For historians, this selection provides valuable insight into the folk culture and lyrical traditions preserved and published during the Japanese colonial period. The inclusion of these songs in a popular magazine like *Samch'ŏlli* highlights an effort to maintain Korean cultural identity, and the older orthography reflects the linguistic state of Korean in the 1930s.
