Took the Wrong One
Inside a third-class passenger car, a Salvation Army member, with a very impolite manner of speaking, said to the passenger next to him,
"Aren't you thinking of going to heaven?"
The passenger asked back, "What about you?"
Salvation Army member: "Me? Of course, I am going to heaven."
Passenger: "Then you've taken the wrong train. Change trains at the next station. This train is bound for Pongch'ŏn (奉天)."
Salvation Army member: "Hmm..."

On Credit
Beggar: "Please spare a penny."
Master: "I only have a 1 Won bill... do you have change?"
Beggar: "No, I don't."
Master: "If you could give me 99 Chŏn in change, I would give you this money."
Beggar: "Sir (나리닙). Let's do this. From now on, I will come to beg at your house (宅) ninety-nine times, but please don't give me anything then, just give me that 1 Won now."
Master: "Just smacks his lips!" <77>

**Summary**

This article presents three short, humorous anecdotes or jokes reflecting everyday life and interactions. The first involves a child's misplaced attempt at tidiness, putting a banana peel in a stranger's pocket. The second features a witty passenger deflating a presumptuous Salvation Army member by pointing out their train is heading to Pongch'ŏn (Mukden), not heaven. The third tells of a clever beggar proposing a "credit" system to receive a 1 Won bill when the master lacks change. For historians, these jokes offer glimpses into colonial Korean society: common settings like trains (segregated by class), interactions between Koreans and figures representing Western influence (Salvation Army member, man in Western suit), social hierarchies (master and beggar), and the prevalence of Japanese currency (Won/Chŏn). The Pongch'ŏn joke subtly situates the setting within the broader context of travel routes connecting Korea and Manchuria during that period.

잘못 탓소
