Sŏl T'aehŭi
Although I feel somewhat embarrassed responding to your esteemed question, I reply as follows, fearing that not answering might be discourteous.
In my humble view, Western thought, surging like wild waves – types seeking only profit (惟利), only form (惟樣), only individualism (惟個人主義) – has long since receded. Instead, there are timeworn, yellowed scrolls: the Shi (詩), Shu (書), Yi (易), Chunqiu (春秋), Yong (庸), Xue (學), Lun (論), Meng (孟), Lao (老), Zhuang (藏), etc. These might indeed be considered rare and precious books, the likes of which are not even glimpsed in the libraries of people today. <55>

**Summary**

This article from the September 1934 issue of Samch'ŏlli magazine presents survey responses from several prominent Korean intellectuals (Hŏ Hŏn, Pang Ŭngmo, Sŏl T'aehŭi, Hong Yangmyŏng, Kim Ch'angje, Chang Hyŏkchu, and one anonymous contributor) about their ten most cherished books or current readings. The lists reveal a diverse range of interests, spanning traditional East Asian classics (Confucian texts, Korean history, Chinese literature), contemporary Japanese publications on law, politics, and economics, Western philosophy (Hegel, Spencer, Russell), literature (Tolstoy, Gide, Dostoevsky), history, social science, and even Christian texts like the Bible. The responses reflect the complex intellectual landscape of colonial Korea, where traditional scholarship coexisted and interacted with modern Japanese and Western thought. For historians, the article offers valuable insight into the reading habits and intellectual orientations of the Korean elite during this period, showcasing the assimilation, adaptation, or rejection of various cultural and ideological influences under Japanese rule. Sŏl T'aehŭi's explicit preference for East Asian classics over "Western thought" and Kim Ch'angje's inclusion of the Bible alongside Confucian and Western texts are particularly revealing of the diverse intellectual currents.
