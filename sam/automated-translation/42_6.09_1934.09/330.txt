Publication Date: 1934-09-01
Article Title: 「最近의外國文壇」座談會 (Symposium on "Recent Foreign Literary Circles")
Article Type: 대담·좌담 (Interview/Symposium)

**Symposium on "Recent Foreign Literary Circles"**
August 14th, 5 PM to 10 PM, Upstairs at Paekhap Garden (百合園), Chongno (鐘路), Seoul
Attendees:
Mr. Chu Yohan (朱耀翰): Graduate of Meiji Gakuin (明治學院), Tokyo First Higher School (東京第一高等學校), Nanjing Jinling University (南京金陵大學); Former Editorial Director at Tonga Ilbo (東亞日報) and Choson Ilbo (朝鮮日報); Currently in the business world.
Ms. No Ch'ŏnmyŏng (盧天命): Graduate of English Department, Ewha Women's College (梨花女子專門學校), Kyŏngsŏng (京城); Currently a member of the Arts and Culture Department at Chungang Ilbo (中央日報).
Mr. Yi Hŏn'gu (李軒求): Graduate of Hwimun High Common School (徽文高普), French Literature Department at Waseda University (早稻田大學); Executive of the Theatrical Arts Research Society (劇藝術硏究會).
Mr. Chŏng Chiyong (鄭芝鎔): Graduate of Hwimun High Common School (徽文高普), English Department at Tokyo Higher Normal School (東京高等師範); Currently a teacher at Hwimun High Common School (徽文高普).
Mr. Kim Kirim (金起林): Graduate of Aesthetics Department, Nihon University (日本大學), Tokyo; Currently a reporter at Choson Ilbo (朝鮮日報), member of the Kuinhoe (九人會).
Ms. Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi (崔貞熙): Graduate of Sookmyung Girls' High School (淑明女高), Central Childcare School (中央保育學敎).
Mr. Yi Hayun (異河潤): Graduate of Paejae High Common School (培材高普), French Literature Department at Waseda University (早稻田大學); Currently a member of the Theatrical Arts Research Society (劇藝術硏究會).
Ms. Yi Sŏnhŭi (李善熙): Currently a reporter at Kaebyŏk Publishing Company (開闢社).
Mr. Kim Ansŏ (金岸曙): Graduate of Osan School (五山學校), English Literature Department at Keio University (慶應大學); Former Lecturer at Posŏng College (普成專門), former employee at Tonga Ilbo (東亞日報); Member of the World Esperanto Association (萬國에스페란토會員).
From this Magazine: Kim Tonghwan (金東煥) <214>

**What Issues Are Being Discussed in Foreign Criticism Circles?**

**Magazine:** Thank you all for coming despite the pouring rain. Tonight, we'd like to briefly touch upon the various trends in foreign literary circles, which are advancing at such a rapid tempo—specifically, the realms of criticism, poetry, novels, and theater. Especially since many authorities in foreign literature systems who have dedicated themselves to special research are present, we hope you will share your specialized knowledge in those areas.

**Yi Hŏn'gu:** Perhaps because Romain Rolland (로-망,로-란) and Henri Barbusse (안리, 발퓨-스) received invitations from Soviet Russia (쏘비엣 露西亞) but kept saying they would go and still haven't, it seems there isn't any major sensational issue being put forward in the French (佛蘭西) literary scene. However, among the things drawing the most attention is the new rise of the anti-war (反X) faction, which is always an old issue yet always a new topic. Weren't Rolland (로-만,로-망) and Barbusse (발-퓨스) originally individuals who strongly advocated anti-war (反X) thought around the time of the World War, based on Monro (몬로) or Clarétie (크라루테)? Now again, the flames of war are starting to ignite everywhere in Europe (歐羅巴) or the world, and human peace, 15 years after the Great European War, is once again facing a crisis. From a pacifist or humanitarian standpoint, these individuals are crying out that through anti-war (反X) literature opposing war (XX) which "destroys civilization," they will rescue the French people from their dangerously frantic state and, furthermore, rescue all of humanity. What's particularly noteworthy is Gide's (지이도) shift. Gide has been much discussed again in the Tokyo literary scene since last year, but wasn't this writer originally a nationalist (國粹主義的) writer who intensely admired French tradition, sentiment, and French ethnic history? Yet, last year he shifted towards an internationalist tendency.
I hear that what spurred Gide's (지이도) shift was likely the atrocities of the Nazis (나치스). Once Germany (獨逸) fell under Nazi rule, they tried to use every aspect of art, from one to ten, as a propaganda tool for political maneuvering. Feeling great fear and hatred <215> towards such dictatorial politics of a single nation, Gide (지이드) seems to have leaned towards internationalism, thinking that France's state-standard politics would eventually fall into the same trap someday.
Those who remember the "February 9th" incident (2月9日事件) will know I don't need to explain further.
Besides this, people like René Arcos (루네,아루,코-스) also, following Lenin's (레-닌) dictum that "Literature cannot exist apart from action," are increasingly participating directly in the peace movement as writers; this much seems to be true.
And on the flip side of all these currents, patriotic literature is also being strongly advocated. This isn't necessarily the rise of a classical patriotism aiming to tear down the current French Republic and restore the monarchy, but rather the advocacy of patriotic literature with a tendency to keep the French tradition and blood alive forever. In a word, the French critical scene, like other foreign critical scenes, seems to have nationalism versus internationalism, the relationship between politics and literature, and the revival of classics as its main topics.

**Kim Kirim:** However, to say that the assertions of Gide (지이드), Romain Rolland (로망,로-망), or Henri Barbusse (안리발퓨스) are the same as socialist literary theory would certainly not be true. As Mr. Yi Hŏn'gu (李軒求氏) mentioned earlier, those people, angered by the way political parties and politicians put the state first (like the Nazis), try too hard to confine the people within special contracts, trample on the people's free will, and demand that literature exist for the government and all arts for the praise of the state—reacting against such "styles" of action, they have shed the stifling nationalism to try living in brotherly love as members of humanity. It doesn't mean Gide has become a communist. Rolland (로-망, 로-망) might be called a humanitarian in a broad sense, but he isn't doing so as a communist praising the Soviets.
Therefore, the internationalism discussed within world thought today, while indeed supranational, does not necessarily seem to contain a socialist connotation.

**Magazine:** We understand the trends in the French (佛蘭西) critical scene well from both of your remarks. Now, could someone speak about German literature? Mr. Sŏ Hangsŏk (徐恒錫氏) for German literature, Mr. Ham Taehun (咸大勳氏) for Russian literature, and Mr. Kim Chinsŏp (金晉變氏) for English literature all said they would come but haven't arrived yet. We'll hear more from them as they arrive.
Anyway, as mentioned earlier, tonight is about the general trends in literary circles of various countries worldwide. Let's first make a pilgrimage through each country's literary scene in order.

**Yi Hayun:** Speaking of the German (獨逸) literary scene, there's a monster in Germany. That is Hitler (힛틀러) and Hitler's loyal hound, Propaganda Minister Goebbels (겟베르스). Where Goebbels' (겟베르스) voice reaches, there is no free art <216> and no literature. Goebbels' voice now holds terrible power over all of Germany, over all Germans. Can any literary group or writer utter a single line of poetry or a word contrary to German state policy? Goebbels shouts loudly: Writers who drink the rain and dew falling on German mountains and rivers must write works for Germany. Therefore, extol the spirit that is heroic (in other words, praise Hitler), nationalist (國粹的) (in other words, Germany is number one in the world), praises great action (in other words, entrust political power to the Nazis for millions of years). This means establishing Nazi party literature. All writers must become propaganda tools for German policy. Thus, in Germany, world-class writers, thinkers, and scientists with a global spirit have been expelled beyond the borders, leaving only "government-patronized writers" (御用文學者).
Germany seems quite extreme. The censorship is so severe that even programs for concerts must reportedly undergo inspection by the Propaganda Minister. If one were to create literature outside the Nazi party line, could even a single line be published anywhere? Consequently, there's naturally no reason for the critical scene to be active.
Therefore, thoughtful Germans seem to avoid contemporary literature and greatly revere the works and words of past national great legacies like Goethe (꾀-터), Hauptmann (하웁트맨), Schiller (실렐), etc.

**Magazine:** And Russia (露西亞)?

**Chŏng Chiyong:** The Russian (露西亞) literary scene is the most awkward to talk about. I don't know the details, but I hear that RAPP (랍프) was recently dissolved. This fact seems to provide evidence that "literature is not politics" and "writers are not politicians." For a while, immediately after the revolution, everyone was excited, and proletarian writers had to be Soviet party members, and proletarian literature had to be propaganda literature. That tendency seems to have been liquidated now. They finally discovered that trying to drag literature down the path of a propaganda tool resulted in nothing. It was quite a late awakening.
Also, in the Russian literary scene now, there's a tendency to dig up and re-evaluate the works and criticism of past writers like Tolstoy (톨스토이), Turgenev (투르게네프), Pushkin (퓨-시킨), Chekhov (체홉), Dostoevsky (떠스또엡스키-) – writers previously dismissed as bourgeois – praising them as precious literary heritage. At places like the Moscow State Theater (莫斯科國立劇場), <217> they reportedly stage *Resurrection* (復活), *The Night Before* (그 전날밤), *The Cherry Orchard* (櫻花동산), etc., more often than plays by newly emerged writers. Of course, there must be many new writers and critics, but anyway, the main theme in the critical scene seems to be the issue of classical revival.

**Magazine:** Yes, I understand. Then, the English (英吉利) literary scene?

**Kim Kirim:** The English (英吉利) critical scene also revolves around the debate between nationalism and internationalism, the revival of classics, the literary heritage issue, and so on. Besides that, there's the issue of H.G. Wells' (H,G웰스) science fiction. Last autumn, I believe, he published a book titled *The Shape of Things to Come* (將來할事物의 形象). In it, he made predictions like a great plague would sweep the world in 1935, 1937, and 1942, or that Japan would make a major advance into Asia a few years later, leading the United States (米國) to do something, reasoning with numbers and historical inference, which caused a big stir. Next, it seems psychological literature based on Joyce's (죠이스) stream-of-consciousness, grounded in psychoanalysis, is currently much discussed. Everyone in the modern age is suffering, and the analysis of the hysterical mental state of these suffering modern people is certainly an interesting issue. Bernard Shaw (빠-너드 쇼) is firing off his usual satire, and apart from that, there doesn't seem to be much else going on.
However, one technical issue common to both the English and French (佛蘭西) literary scenes seems to be that Proust's (뿌르스토) technique in France is drawing much attention from critics. In contrast to the rambling style of traditional novelists, he speaks clearly and briefly, injecting "speed" into literature, expressing things concisely, almost like poetry. That seems to be attracting considerable attention.

**Magazine:** What about the American (亞米利加) or Chinese (中國) literary scenes?

**Chu Yohan:** In the Chinese (中國) literary scene, Chiang Kai-shek's (蔣介石) suppression is severe, so most famous critics and writers publish anonymously, making it difficult to discern who is presenting which issues or writing which works. The critics and writers appearing in Chinese newspapers and magazines now are people I don't know at all. Although it's said that Lu Xun (魯迅), Zhou Zuoren (周作人), Tian Han (田漢), and other communist writers are writing a lot, they are all using pseudonyms. Also, the level of censorship by the Nationalist government is so harsh that distinctive critiques cannot emerge.

**Kim Ansŏ:** As for the American (亞米利加) literary scene, although Sinclair Lewis (씽클레이 루이스) winning the Nobel Prize has drawn global attention, perhaps because it's a country where machine civilization has reached its peak, literary criticism and the like don't seem to unfold under general public scrutiny. One can only say that Sinclair (싱크레이) still holds sway. What's interesting might be Black literature, but it seems no great works or writers have yet emerged from the Black race.

**Magazine:** Lastly, please tell us about Catholic literature (캐톨니즘 文學). This issue of Catholic literature has also been debated in our literary circles, with Mr. Chŏng Chiyong (鄭芝鎔氏) sparring with Mr. Im Hwa (林和氏).

**Chŏng Chiyong:** When speaking of French (佛蘭西) literature, one cannot avoid mentioning Catholic literature. <218> It's rare to find a French writer who hasn't been baptized by the spirit of Catholicism (캐톨니즘精神). As is common with Christian literature in general, Catholic literature is also fundamentally based on the sublime keynote of peace for humanity. Generally, it's non-war literature, anti-war literature, and reveres classical fragrance. Famous writers up to now include Chesterton (최-스), Belloc (발락), Jean Cocteau (잔, 곡토), etc. I could speak in more detail, but that's the general gist.

**Trends in Poetry, Novels, and Drama**

**Magazine:** Now, please tell us about the trends in general literary fields like novels, poetry, popular songs, and drama.

**Yi Hŏn'gu:** A noteworthy trend is the resurgence of Shakespearean (섹스피어) drama throughout Europe (歐羅巴) right now. This is true in Russia (露西亞), England (英吉利), and France (佛蘭西). They are staging them as classical works, unchanged. There's a tendency to absorb the life views and social perspectives of people from the past as they were. Ibsen (인센) is less common. At places like the Kamerny Theatre (카메네프劇場) in Russia, besides Shakespeare, they are currently staging works like *La Dame aux Camélias* (椿姬) and *Resurrection* (復活) with great enthusiasm. Generally speaking, modern plays tend to have more scenes (場) than acts (幕). For instance, *Napoléon* (나포레스) performed in Paris (巴里) consists of 1 act and 34 scenes.

**Yi Hayun:** In my opinion, Russian (露西亞) writers seem to be currently searching for a new starting point for new literature. Bringing in Greek works, Shakespeare (쎅스피어), Tolstoy (톨스토이) – isn't this evidence of their struggle to find that starting point?

**Chŏng Chiyong:** I suppose so. The search for classics is likely due to groping for a new path. It seems not just Russian literature, but literature in other countries as well.

**Kim Ansŏ:** There are many popular songs (流行歌) in the West too, but their popular songs are different. Hearing about Spain (西班牙), Italy (伊太利), and France (佛蘭西), which love songs the most, it seems that famous poets write folk songs (民謠) or pure poetry, which then pass through the hands of good composers to become very easy and beautiful popular songs (流行歌謠), spreading to the general public.

**Kim Kirim:** However, I cannot approve of popular songs (流行歌). Rather than having a hundred popular songs that cater to the vulgar masses (俗衆), it's more respectable to have one or two poems that are lofty, deep, and beautiful, even if understood by only one or two people. Moreover, I get a headache from the vulgarity and low tone of the popular songs circulating in Korean (朝鮮) society recently. <219> It's utterly deplorable. Singing about superficial emotions means they will just melt away like spring snow. While the aspect of enjoying them together with the masses is good, does that mean songs that touch upon the profound emotions of humans cannot emerge?

**Chŏng Chiyong:** Songs like those by Schipa (스킵파-) of Spain (西班牙) are good, aren't they?

**Kim Kirim:** Those are folk songs (民謠). They are good because they are folk songs that artistically express the unique sentiments of the populace.

**Chu Yohan:** There has been much discussion about the distinction between folk songs (民謠) and popular songs (流行歌). I see it this way: Folk songs are songs that naturally remain from among popular songs. For instance, suppose hundreds or thousands of popular songs flow morning and evening, and in the meantime, the bad ones are all eliminated (淘汰), and only the good ones among them remain after several years, or even after several hundred or thousand years. I see those as folk songs. Among the various songs flowing on the streets of Seoul today, those that remain after 10 or 30 years will likely possess long lives as excellent folk songs.

**Kim Ansŏ:** In any case, popular songs that allow one to cry and laugh together with the masses are something that general poets cannot afford to overlook.

**Magazine:** You are too silent. Do the three female writers have anything they've felt about recent foreign literary circles? Or what are the works you most enjoy reading?

**No Ch'ŏnmyŏng:** Well... Regarding the English (英吉利) literary scene, what the others said earlier is generally correct, I believe. Back when I was at Ewha College (梨專), being in the English department, I tried to read some English literature, but recently I haven't been able to read much. What I am reading are works by Turgenev (투르게네프) and Thomas Mann (토-마스).

**Ch'oe Chŏnghŭi:** I also don't have much knowledge about foreign literature, so I don't really have anything to say. I'll just listen tonight. The foreign authors whose works I enjoy reading are Balzac (발작크) and Romain Rolland (로-망, 로-망).

**Yi Sŏnhŭi:** I also read various things, but the works I read most often and am always moved by are Goethe's (께-데) works.

**Chu Yohan:** One more thing I'd like to add is that when we talk about writers currently popular in foreign literary circles, we often point to the masters of that country's literature who are already in their period of decline. For example, Bernard Shaw (빠너드 쇼-) and H.G. Wells (H,G 웰스) in the English (英吉利) literary scene, Henri Barbusse (안니, 발푸-스) and Romain Rolland (로만 로-랑) in the French (佛蘭西) literary scene, and Gorky (꼴키) in Russia (露西亞) are all writers past 50 or 60, who have retreated to the second line. In reality, if you look into that country's literary scene, while they might be considered representative writers, those who are currently writing prolifically and constantly providing new issues are among the young and vigorous group whose names are not yet known worldwide. Just looking at the Tokyo literary scene, young writers like Hayashi Fusao (林房雄) and Kobayashi Takiji (小林喜多), and others are more active than Kikuchi Kan (菊池寬) or Shimazaki Tōson (島崎藤村). The Chinese (中國) literary scene is similar; Tian Han (田漢), Zhou Zuoren (周作人), and Lu Xun (魯迅) have all retreated to the second line, and the majority of writers appearing in newspapers and magazines are people we <220> don't yet know.

**How Does the State Treat Writers in the West?**

**Magazine:** We often say that Korean (朝鮮) newspaper companies neglect (학待) the arts and culture pages. While spending hundreds of Won (圓) on unimportant matters, they are stingy about paying manuscript fees to writers, and while dedicating numerous pages to worthless, low-brow articles on the society page (三面), <221> they do not generously allocate space for the literary section (文藝欄), we lament. So, how do foreign newspapers handle their literary sections?

**Yi Hŏn'gu:** In foreign newspapers, the literary section (文藝面) seems to focus primarily on reviews of new publications (新刊批判).

**Chŏng Chiyong:** I hear there's a newspaper in Paris (巴里) published exclusively for literature (文藝新聞).

**Kim Ansŏ:** Ordinary newspapers don't seem to place much emphasis (置重) on the literary section either; they tend to favor publishing new book reviews or writers' travelogues. Instead, since there are newspapers specializing solely in literature, those presumably fill their entire pages with criticism, poetry, and novels.

**Magazine:** Do prominent writers in foreign countries also write serialized novels (連載) for newspapers? And do they publish them as standalone books afterwards?

**Kim Ansŏ:** In foreign countries, famous writers generally don't write serialized novels for newspapers. Most write the entire work at once, as a "manuscript for direct publication" (書き卸し), send it to a publishing house (出版書肆), and publish it directly. Renowned critics then review these published standalone books in newspapers as new publications (新刊批評). That's how the quality of the work is decided. Therefore, the importance of the new book review section (新刊批評欄) is far greater than we imagine. Especially for emerging writers who have painstakingly written and published a work, the psychological state of waiting to see what renowned critics will say is truly astonishing, they say.

**Yi Hayun:** That's right. Most are "manuscript for direct publication" (書き卸し), and like in Korea (朝鮮) or Tokyo, it's rare for works to come out as serialized novels for the masses. However, serialization in magazines is said to be common. Wasn't Dostoevsky's (떠스토엡스끼-) *Poor Folk* (가난한 사람) first published in a magazine called *Northern Review* (北方評論) or something?

**Kim Kirim:** Since there are many "manuscript for direct publication" (書き卸し) works, the role and scope of activity for publishing houses (出版書肆) are truly broad and important, people say. In Korea (朝鮮) too, good, bold publishing houses that can directly publish large works of 500 or 600 manuscript pages need to emerge soon.

**Chu Yohan:** That's right. That's the most urgent task. And following that, Korean (朝鮮) writers too should make efforts to write directly in English, French, or Russian as "manuscripts for direct publication" (書き卸し) and release them into the foreign publishing market. I believe the time for that has already arrived.

**Favorite Foreign Movie Actors**

**Magazine:** We've listened to rather serious talk for a long time, so let's move on to a lighter topic now. Just for fun, please tell us your favorite foreign movie actors (映畵排優).

**Yi Sŏnhŭi:** Anna Sten (안나,스텐) is truly captivatingly good. The most recent movie she appeared in was *Storm over Asia* (激情의 嵐 - Translator note: This seems incorrect, likely refers to *The Storm* or another Sten film. *Storm over Asia* is Pudovkin's 1928 film). She appeared opposite the famous male character actor Jannings (냐닝스). She's an intelligent, witty, and seductive woman. I like her. <222>

**Yi Hŏn'gu:** In the past, I liked Garbo (갈포) and Dietrich (테-도릿히). I watched almost every film they were in. Recently, I like Dorothea Wieck (도로에야 힉-) from *Mädchen in Uniform* (制服의 處女). Jeanette MacDonald (짜켓트, 케-나) was good too.

**Kim Kirim:** Among men, Laughton (라후도). Among women, I haven't yet discovered anyone I'd fall head over heels for.

**Chŏng Chiyong:** No matter what, Emil Jannings (에밀 야닝스) is the best. That terrifying power that presses close to emotion—as an actor with such power, Emil Jannings is truly an outstanding genius.

**Kim Ansŏ:** I also like Emil Jannings (에밀, 냐닝스) very much. He's great. As a character actor, he'd rank number one.

**Magazine:** Thank you very much for sharing such diverse thoughts over such a long time.
-(End)- <223>

**Summary**

This 1934 symposium transcript captures a discussion among prominent Korean intellectuals about contemporary trends in international literature, covering France, Germany, Russia, Britain, the US, and China. Key themes include the tension between nationalism and internationalism (particularly Gide's shift and Nazi influence), the relationship between politics and literature (Soviet RAPP dissolution, German state control), the revival of classical literature (Tolstoy, Shakespeare), psychological novels (Joyce), and technical innovations (Proust). The conversation also touches upon theater trends, popular songs vs. folk songs, state support for writers (Poet Laureate, academies), the role of literary criticism in newspapers, publishing practices ("manuscript for direct publication"), and personal preferences for film actors.

From a historical perspective, the article is revealing. It shows Korean intellectuals in the colonial period actively engaging with global literary and political currents, demonstrating their awareness of international debates about art's role in society, particularly under rising authoritarianism (Nazism, Soviet shifts, Chinese censorship). Their discussion about the need for strong Korean publishing houses and writers reaching international audiences reflects nationalist aspirations for cultural recognition amidst colonial constraints. The participants themselves represent a significant cross-section of the Korean cultural elite of the time.

外國評壇에선 얻든 問題가 論議되는가
本社=비가 철철 오는데 不拘하시고 이러케 여러 분께서 오서 주시니 깃부외다. 오늘 저녁은 너무도 急激한 뎀포로 進步되고 잇는 外國文壇의 여러 가지 趨勢에 對하여 卽 評論界, 詩壇, 小說壇, 演劇界 等 各方面을 모다 한번 스처 고보 십슴니다. 特이 이 座席에는 外國文學에 對하여 特別한 硏究를 거듭하여 오시는 海外文學系統의 여러 權威께서도 만히 叅席하여 주섯스니 그 方面의 專門智識을 기우려 주기를 바람니다.
李軒求=「로-망,로-란」과 「안리, 발퓨-스」가 쏘비엣 露西亞의 招請을 밧고도 간다간다 하며 아직 단녀오지 아니한 까닭인지 佛蘭西文壇에는 그러케 큰 센세-슌 거리라 할 問題라고 提出되어 잇지 안은 듯 합데다. 다만 그 中에도 가장 注目을 끄으는 것은 언세든지 묵은 問題면서 늘 새로운 問題거리라 할 X戰派의 새로운 擡頭가 잇지요. 元來 「로-만,로-망」이나 「발-퓨스」는 「몬로」라든지 「크라루테」에 據하여 世界大戰 前後하야 反X思想을 몹시 鼓吹하든 분들이 아니엇슴니까. 그것이 지금 다시 歐羅巴나 世界 어느 곳에나 戰爭의 火熖이 到處에서 일기 시작하여 歐洲大戰後 15年을 지난 오늘의 人類平和는 다시 危機에 빠지고 잇슴니다. 이에 이 분들은 平和主義 또는 人道主義的 立場으로써 「文明을 破毁」하는 XX에 反對하는 反X文學을 通하야 佛蘭西人民을 그 미처 날뛰는 危險狀態에서 救出하고 나아가서는 世界人類를 救出하여 낼 것이라고 부르짓고 잇는 것이지요. 더구나 注目할 일은 「지이도」의 轉向이지요. 「지이도」는 昨年부터 東京文壇에서도 다시 크게 떠들지만은 元來 이 作家로 말하면 佛蘭西의 傳統과 情調와 또 佛蘭西 民族的 歷史를 몹시 讚美하든 國粹主義的 作家가 아니엇든가요. 그러든 그가 전년 인터내수날的 傾向으로 발길을 옴기어 노앗지오.
드른 즉 「지이도」의 轉向에 拍車를 加한것은 아마 나치스의 暴狀인 듯 하다해요. 獨逸이 나치스 治下에 들자 한낫부터 열까지 藝術의 모든 部門을 政治工作의 宣傳道具로 쓰자 하엿지요. 이러한 一國의 獨裁的 政治에 큰 恐怖와 憎惡을＜215＞ 늣긴 「지이드」는 佛蘭西의 國家標準의 政治도 어날은 結局 여기에 떠러지고 말니라 생각하야 國際主義로 轉身한 傾向도 잇는 듯 해요.
「2月9日」事件을 記憶하는 이는 나의 說明을 더 길게 要求치 안을 줄 암니다.
이밧게 「루네,아루,코-스」가튼 사람도 모다 레-닌류의『行動을 떠나서는 文學이 存在할 수 업다』하야 文學者들로서 人類의 平和運動에 直接 叅加하는 이들이 늘어가고 잇는 것만은 事實인 듯 합데다.
그리고 이 모든 潮流의 反面에 愛國文學 亦是 盛이 提唱되어 잇스니 이것은 何必曰 現在의 佛蘭西 共和政을 따려 부시고 王政으로 復舊하자는 式의 古典的 愛國主義의 擡頭가 아니라 佛蘭西의 傳統과 피를 永遠히 살니자는 그러한 傾向을 가진 愛國文學의 提唱인 듯 합데다. 一言으로 弊하자면 佛蘭西評壇 亦 外國評壇모양으로 國民主義 對 國際主義의 論戰 政治와 文學과의 關係 古典復興 等이 主要한 論題로 되어 잇는 듯 해요.

朱耀翰=그리고 한가지 부처 말하고 십흔 것은 우리가 外國文壇에서 現在 流行하는 作家거니하고 말하는 이가 대개 그 나라 文學의 大家들로 이미 老衰期에 든 作家을 가르침이 되지오. 假令 英吉利文壇에 빠너드 쇼- H,G 웰스 佛蘭西文壇에 안니, 발푸-스, 로만 로-랑도 露西亞의 꼴키도 다 5,60以上間 第二陣에 물너선 作家지요. 實상 그 나라 文壇을 드려다 보면 그네가 代表作家라고는 할 수 잇슬는지 모르지만 그보다 現在 作品도 만히 쓰고 늘 새問題를 提供하는 것은 世界的으로 아직 일홈이 알려지지 못한 少壯群속에서 잇지요. 東京文壇만하여도 菊池寬, 島崎藤村보다 林房雄, 小林喜多 그 外의 젊은이가 더 만히 活躍하는 모양으로 또 中國文壇도 그러치만 田漢, 周作人, 魯迅는 다 第二陣에 물너선 이들이고 新聞雜誌에 오르나리는 多數作家는 우리가＜220＞ 아직 모르는 이들이 大部分이지요.

西洋서는 國家에서 文學者를 얻더케 待遇하는가
本社=外國서는 文學上天才-卽 偉大한 詩人이나 作家를 國家에서 엇더케 優待함니까.
異河潤=英國서는 「欽定詩人」(或 桂冠詩人이라고도한다)이란 制度가 잇서 王이 特히 勅令으로 한사람을 選定하여 國家에서 그의 生活費는 勿論, 社會的으로 놉흔 地位를 保障하여 주지요. 지금은 「메루빌」이란 사람이 欽定詩人으로 되어 잇지요.
本社=「메루빌」이라니 엇든 사람임니까.
金岸曙=悲劇 「바다의悲劇」이란 著書가 잇지요. 엇재든 海洋詩人으로써 바다를 題材로 하고 쓴 戱曲과 詩歌가 만치요.
李軒求=佛蘭西에는 翰林院制度가 잇지요. 國家에서 藝術家를 爲하야 翰林院을 만들어 노코 거기에다가 자랑할 만한 文學者를 모서 오는데 定員이 꼭 잇지요. 25名이든지 36名이든지요. 그래서 定員數에 缺員이 업스면 아모리 有名한 詩人이라도 드러갈 수 업게 그 制度는 嚴格합니다. 有名한 「에밀,졸라」도 끗끗내 못하고 말엇지요. 지금은 詩王 「볼,풀」 等이 叅加하여 잇지요.
鄭芝鎔=앗가 말한 英吉利의 欽定詩人 等도 英吉利文學史를 들추어 보면 「테니슨」以前에는 그러케 有名한 詩人들이 업서요 「테니슨」이 되면서부터 一般的으로 仰慕하는 地位가 되엇지요. 그 以前은 흔히 王政鷗歌하는 보잘 것 업는 御用詩人을 갓다가 定하엇스니까 모다 시언한 作家들이라고까지는 못하엇지요.
金起林=그 박게도 「아까데미」賞, 노-벨賞 等 外國서는 國家나 社會에서 文學者를 만히 保護하드군요.

外國의 新聞雜誌文欄은 엇더한가
本社=우리는 늘 朝鮮의 新聞社에서 學藝面을 학待한다고 말을 함니다. 重要치안는 일에는 數百圓씩 支出하면서 文人에 稿料支出하기에는 인색하여 하며 또 無價値한 三面의 低級記事를 爲하여는 多數한 紙面을 내어 던지면서＜221＞ 文藝欄을 爲하여는 그러케 시언하게 紙面을 割愛하지 안는다고 歎息함니다. 그러면 外國 新聞서는 文藝欄을 엇더케 取扱하옵니까.
李軒求=外國 新聞에서는 文藝面이라 하면 아마 新刊批判에 主力하는가 봅데다.
鄭芝鎔=巴里에는 專門으로 내이는 文藝新聞이 잇다드군.
金岸曙=普通新聞에는 亦是 文藝欄을 그러케 置重하지 안코 그저 新刊批判이라거나 文人의 旅行記가튼 것을 즐겨 싯고 그대신 文藝만을 專門으로 發行하는 新聞이 잇스니까 그런데서는 紙面의 全部를 評論과 詩歌小說로 채우나 봅데다.
本社=外國서도 有數한 作家들이 新聞에 連載하는 長篇을 씀니까. 그래서 그것을 單行本으로 하여 냄니까.
金岸曙=外國서는 대개 有名한 作家면 新聞에 連載小說을 쓰지 안코, 거지반 한꺼번에 다 써서 卽 「書き卸し」를 하여서 出版書肆에 보내어 그냥 出版하지요. 이 出版된 單行本을 著名한 評論家가 新聞에 新刊批評으로 붓을 들지요. 그래서 비로소 그 作品의 好不好가 決定이 되지요. 그러기에 新刊批評欄의 重要性이란 우리가 想像하는 것보다 훨신 더 重要함니다. 더구나 新進作家가 애를 써서 作品을 써서 出版을 하여 노코 著名 批評家가 무에라고 評아니하나 하고 기다리는 心理란 참으로 놀납다고 합데다.
異河潤=그러치요. 대개 「書き卸し」이고 朝鮮이나 東京과 가치 大衆을 위한 連載小說로 世上에 나옴은 드물다 하지오. 다만 雜誌에 連載함은 만타 합데다. 떠스토엡스끼-의 「가난한 사람」들도 처음에 「北方評論」인가 한 雜誌에 실니지 안엇서요.
金起林=「書き卸し」作品이 만흐니만치 出版書肆의 役割과 活動의 範圍는 참으로 널고 또 重要하다고들 합데다. 朝鮮서도 어서 5百枚 6百枚하는 큰 長篇을 그냥 出版할 조흔, 大憺한 出版書肆가 나와야 하지요.
朱耀翰=그러치요. 그것이 急先務지요. 그리고 그에 따라 朝鮮作家들도 英語라든지 佛蘭西말이라든지 露西亞말로 直接 「書き卸し」하여 外國出版市場에 내놋는 努力을 하여야 하겟서요. 그러할 時機가 벌서 到達된 줄 알어요.
