Yukchabagi (Six-Beat Song)

Over there on Kalmibong ridge,
Rain comes pouring down heavily.

Wrapping a rain cloak
Around my waist,
Shall I go weed the fields?

Tambagwi T'aryŏng (Tobacco Ballad)

Tobacco, oh tobacco,
Tobacco from Tongnae or Ulsan.
I heard your country was good,
Why did you come out to Chosŏn?<60>

My country is good too, but
I came to tour the Chosŏn region.
Did you come out to give silver?
Did you come out to give gold?

No silver and no gold,
I came bearing tobacco seeds.

Over there, over there at the foot of that mountain,
I planted tobacco.
At night, it tastes the cold dew,
In the daytime, it receives the sun's warmth.

Outer leaves appear, inner leaves appear,
Gradually it grows and flourishes.

Panga T'aryŏng (Milling Ballad)

Half my life is over and I've grown old,
I cannot become young again.

A swallow came from Kangnam,
Carrying a single gourd seed.
It soars high up in the wide sky,
Looking into this house and that house.

Sangsabyŏlgok (Song of Longing and Parting)

Parting becomes a fire,
Burning my insides.

If my tears became rain,
They could extinguish the pouring fire.

Nyŏngbyŏn Ka (Song of Nyŏngbyŏn)

O Tongdae in Nyŏngbyŏn!
Ehe-i-heya, four units, stay well in peace!
For me too, next year's spring is a good season,
Let's meet once again.<61>

Kwaejina Ch'ingch'ing

Kwaejina ch'ingch'ing na-ne
In the sky, there are many stars,
On the riverside, there are many pebbles.

Kwaejina ch'ingch'ing na-ne
In the mudflat paddies, there are many snails,
In the bamboo grove, there are many nodes.

Menari (Menari Song)

O Menari flower, Menari flower,
When that flower blooms,
Farm work begins;
When that flower fades,
Let's finish the farm work.

Ŏllŏllŏl sangsadwi
Ŏŏdwiyŏ sangsadwi

O Menari flower, Menari flower,
When that flower blooms,
Don't boast of your splendor.
Even ninety days of spring scenery
Pass by in an instant.

Ŏllŏllŏl sangsadwi
Ŏŏdwiyŏ sangsadwi<62>

**Summary**

This article presents a collection of seven Korean folk songs (minyo) from various genres, including work songs (Yukchabagi, Panga T'aryŏng, Menari), ballads (Tambagwi T'aryŏng, Nyŏngbyŏn Ka), a love song (Sangsabyŏlgok), and a festive refrain (Kwaejina Ch'ingch'ing). The songs cover themes of labor, nature, parting, regional identity, and the passage of time, reflecting common folk sentiments. The inclusion of the "Tobacco Ballad," discussing the origin of tobacco in Korea, is particularly notable.

From a historical perspective, this collection is valuable as it documents folk culture during the Japanese colonial period (1930). The "Tobacco Ballad's" dialogue format, questioning why tobacco came to Chosŏn, might subtly reflect contemporary sentiments about foreign introductions or influences. The preservation of regional songs like "Nyŏngbyŏn Ka" and specific song types like "Menari" provides insight into local traditions and the Korean language (including older spellings) used at the time, offering linguistic and cultural data for historians studying colonial Korea.
