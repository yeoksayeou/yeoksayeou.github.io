Lies
						Sin Puch'ul

If one assumes a bird only cries, won't there be a time it laughs?
How can a flower only laugh? Surely there is a time when it cries too.
Humans, pretending only they are right, only tell lies.<291>

**Summary**

This short poem contrasts the natural expressions of birds and flowers, which experience both sorrow (crying/wilting) and joy (singing/laughing), with the behavior of humans. Humans are depicted as hypocritical, always pretending to be right while speaking falsehoods. From a colonial history perspective, this poem, published in 1936 Korea, subtly critiques human duplicity and self-righteousness. While ostensibly a general commentary on human nature, historians might find it reflects a veiled criticism of the prevailing social or political atmosphere under Japanese rule, where truth might have been suppressed and false narratives enforced. The author's name, Sin Puch'ul (申不出), possibly a pen name, could itself hint at hidden meanings or the difficulty of open expression.
