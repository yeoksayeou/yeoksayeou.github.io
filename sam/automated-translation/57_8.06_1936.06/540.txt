**Body Text Translation:**
Ten Years Later
						Yi Kiyŏng

Kyŏngsu, having heard a few days prior that the proofs for the magazine manuscript being sent to print would start coming out today, hurriedly ate breakfast and ran straight to the D Printing House.
When he went up to the factory office on the second floor, the clerks were already seated and engrossed in their respective tasks. Proofreading the manuscript for a monthly magazine all by himself was no small annoyance. Moreover, when the typesetting was clumsy, making the proof sheets covered in red marks, his head ached unbearably. Only after seeing the third and fourth proofs could he finally correct the typos. In any case, after being tormented by this work daily for 5 or 6 days, his face would become gaunt as if he had suffered a serious illness. Yet, he couldn't call it misfortune. Although his salary was barely a pittance, if he quit, his family would immediately be unable to survive. As the difficulty of living worsened day by day <268> and jobs became scarce, some people, unaware of his inner struggles, even envied him for having an occupation. That's why he dreaded it when country folk occasionally came to Seoul and invariably visited him, each asking him to get them a job. Whenever he heard such requests, Kyŏngsu was dumbfounded.
How could he find jobs for others when he himself lacked a proper profession and was suffering considerable material and mental hardship? However, if he spoke honestly about his situation, those unsophisticated people might misunderstand him as cold-hearted. So, he would usually reply with kind words, saying he'd see about it gradually—that he would look for such a position as soon as one became available. But that was a barefaced lie that made his own face flush with shame.

Looking back on his past experiences, Kyŏngsu chewed on bitter memories anew. After graduating from common school in his hometown and drifting as a low-level clerk in the township office, he set his mind on studying and came up to Seoul. Having been praised as a prodigy since childhood, he had some self-confidence, thinking surely there would be a place for him somewhere. But once he arrived, it was completely different from what he had thought; there wasn't a single place to set foot. So, he came up several times only to return empty-handed. No one would hire him—a man with no money, no connections, and meager education. Finally, in despair, as he was about to give up, he happened to apply for a literary contest (懸賞文藝) sponsored by the magazine company where he now worked. Luckily, he won, and through that connection, he obtained the job of a reporter (記者).
However, it was truly a case of meager meals and numerous chores. Writing manuscripts, editing, proofreading, mailing—there was simply no respite. Instead of escaping the sorrow of unemployment (失職), it only made him feel the sorrow of employment. At home, the hardships of life gnawed at him, and outside, wasn't he being driven like a carriage horse under someone else's control? A life without freedom of mind or body only bred impatience and anguish. Is this human life? Is this the life of man, the lord of all creation? Why can't everyone learn as they wish, work as they wish, and thus display their innate talents?... No matter how much he protested like this <269>, it was useless. The heavy burden of life pressed down on him increasingly like a thousand *kŭn*, allowing not even a moment's rest or a sideways glance.
Whenever he thought of his predicament, confronted by harsh reality, he recalled the dream story of the worn-out carriage horse he read in "Crime and Punishment" (罪와 罰). And he compared himself to that horse.
—The carriage horse, pulling a heavy load, gasps under the carter's merciless whip, desperately trying to pull. But the load is too heavy for its strength. It won't move. Still, the whip falls relentlessly like rain. The horse kicks out with its hind legs unbearably. The onlookers burst into laughter. The carter, even angrier, whips furiously. Still, the horse cannot go. Finally, the carter picks up a senseless iron bar and strikes without mercy. The horse leaps, mustering its last ounce of strength to try and pull. But it collapses right there on the ground and dies.
Indeed, how was his life any better than this pitiful carriage horse? The silent whip struck down incessantly above his head. He, groaning under the heavy burden of life, could neither avoid the blows nor cast off the load. He couldn't pull the load that taxed his strength, nor could he not pull it. So, what would become of such a life? The final fate of the pitiful horse! Wasn't that a symbol of his own future? The more this thought occurred to him, the more terrifying fear he felt.
And yet, if there were people who envied him just because he wore a Western suit jacket and carried the business card of a magazine reporter, how truly absurd was that? What cruel cynicism (히니꾸냐).

Kyŏngsu was still pondering these thoughts while waiting for the first proof to come out. He lit a cigarette and sat down when a typesetter came in, manuscript in hand, asking Kyŏngsu about a hastily written character in an essay manuscript. It was an economics paper by Mr. K, notorious for his terrible handwriting.
After identifying the character for him, Kyŏngsu looked at the typesetter again. His face seemed extremely familiar—. But his thoughts grew hazy, unable to pinpoint who it was. The typesetter also seemed to recognize him and stared back for a while.
"Excuse me, but is your hometown Seoul?"
Kyŏngsu, finally overcome with curiosity, asked first.
"No, sir. I'm from the countryside. From XX province."
"Your face is very familiar."
"Really!"
"Ah, now I remember! Aren't you Mr. Chŏng Inhak?" <270>
"Yes, I am. How do you know me?"
"I'm Kim Kyŏngsu."
"Kim Kyŏngsu... Let me see, where did I meet you, well..."
The typesetter just seemed puzzled, apparently not recognizing Kyŏngsu well.
"Come now—didn't we spend a month together over 10 years ago, down in P'unggi, Kyŏngsang Province? We were kinder to each other than anyone back then."
"Ah, that's right! Now I remember. But I wouldn't have recognized you at all."
The typesetter finally showed recognition.
"Indeed, it's good to see you. It's been over 10 years, so it's understandable—you've changed quite a bit too, old friend (노형도)."
"Changed is an understatement. I've gotten quite old. Ah! Looking closely, I think I can see traces of your old face. Still, your eyes are sharper than mine... I didn't recognize you right away... haha."
"So, when did you start learning the printing trade, and when did you come to Seoul? Truly, you've learned a fine skill."
"Fine skill, you say? It's just something I do because I can't die. What..."
The typesetter suddenly replied awkwardly, as if embarrassed. He glanced sideways at the office clerks.
"Not at all... Well, you must be busy, so let's talk quietly later when we meet. Truly, I never dreamed we'd meet like this."
"I know... Well then, I'll see you again."
And Inhak, ignoring the hand Kyŏngsu extended for a handshake, turned abruptly and left. For a long while after Inhak was gone, Kyŏngsu stared at the spot where he had walked away. He was incredibly glad to have unexpectedly met Inhak, but Inhak's somewhat distant attitude towards him was disappointing.
If he possessed a progressive consciousness, there would be no reason (할니니가) for him to be so ashamed of being a worker. That's why Kyŏngsu had treated him frankly. But why on earth did he lack a dignified spirit? Was it because he was still trapped in feudal consciousness? No, that couldn't be right either. If so, how could he have liquidated that kind of life? How could he have abandoned the lumpen (룸폔생활) life and become a worker? His personality had always been modest. One shouldn't misunderstand that. He possessed a healthy body. Wasn't that what enabled him to lead his current life and bravely walk at the forefront of the times? If so, perhaps he actually pitied <271> Kyŏngsu's life as a reporter? Just as a well-ripened ear of grain bows its head lower, perhaps that's why he spoke humbly of his own life earlier?...
That's right. His current life must appear merely pitiful before Inhak. It could be likened to revealing the true nature of a prostitute (賣笑婦). A magazine reporter! A pulp journalist for a popular interest magazine! Truly, how much benefit could it bring to human society? If he could find his life in a justifiable sense, he should rightly exterminate such degenerate magazines acting out of corrupt self-interest (빙공영사). This thought made Kyŏngsu blush involuntarily.

Ten years—there's a proverb that if you apprentice for 10 years, there's nothing you can't do in the world, and also that in 10 years, even mulberry fields can turn into seas.
The more Kyŏngsu traced back his past of 10 years ago, the more believable such sayings became. Truly, how incredibly must the world have changed in 10 years? He could feel it, first of all, in Inhak's changed life. Yet, he himself felt as though he had been asleep for 10 years and just woken up. Truly, what had he been doing during that time? Hadn't he just been sleeping mindlessly?
It was 10 years ago. In his youth, Kyŏngsu was lured by a friend and accidentally left home, embarking on the aimless path of a wandering life (방낭생활). It was right after graduating from common school. He wandered downwards through Ch'ungch'ŏng Province, then Chŏlla Province, and then entered Kyŏngsang Province. Finally, around the time he was prospecting for gold with a hammer, exploring the depths of Sobaeksan mountain, he chanced upon Inhak, who was also chasing similar illusions, at an inn in the old town of P'unggi. They became close thereafter. Finding someone from a neighboring county in the same province (Ch'ungch'ŏngdo) in a foreign land made them feel like old friends at first sight, like meeting spring in a foreign land. However, their dream of striking it rich showed little prospect of realization, and consequently, lacking means to support themselves, their hardships as travelers became severe. Eventually, they had no choice but to return to their respective hometowns. Since parting then, they had lost contact until today. Who would have known that today, by chance, Inhak would appear as a printing worker, and he as a magazine reporter, to have a historic reunion? But a worker and a reporter! A factory laborer and a frail, half-baked intellectual! It was an absurdly vast difference (운양의 차이엿다) from their identical lumpen (룸폔생활) lives of 10 years ago. <272> One person was walking the great path of humanity, while the other wandered in darkness like a prostitute. Even if it meant enduring any hardship—if he could live for truth, he wanted to live it with the spirit of a great martyr. A life where one's ideals and actions align—a life where ideals and reality correspond—what a noble life that would be! At times, it might be painful. But the greater the pain, the greater it would be. The greater the pain, the holier it would be. Could it be likened to a pebble (小石) tossed about in a raging torrent (激流)? The more it collides, the more it is polished, revealing a clean luster without gathering any foul moss.
If so, wasn't his current life like stagnant water collected in a puddle—no, like the sewage mud settled at its bottom? The more Kyŏngsu lived this life, the more he felt its contradiction, and the more he suffered pangs of conscience. Yet, possessing the weakness that prevented him from escaping his current life, his self-reflection ended at self-reflection, and his guilt repeated itself, making him feel self-loathing. If only he could stop thinking such thoughts, couldn't he at least lessen the pain, even in a meaningless life? He whipped himself again, thinking he was a more pitiful animal than the carriage horse pulling a heavy load.

After that—Kyŏngsu tried to get closer to Inhak. The more he thought about his old friend, whom he now saw in a new light (괄목상대한), the more a fresh sense of friendship arose.
A few days later, taking advantage of the factory's day off, Kyŏngsu went to visit Inhak. Inhak, who lived in a rented room paid monthly (삭을세빵사리) in Yongduri outside Tongdaemun (East Gate), was at home.
"Mr. Kim, what brings you here (웬일니심니까)?" <273>
As soon as Kyŏngsu called from outside the gate, Inhak came out wearing only a simple jacket and ankle bands, bowing his head hastily with a flustered expression.
"Since you didn't come to visit, I came to see you first," Kyŏngsu said with a frank laugh. Based on his previous experience, he didn't offer a handshake.
"This is too uncomfortable... I meant to visit, but I just couldn't find the time."
"Of course, I understand. Especially since you live so far away."
Kyŏngsu stood holding his cane, looking at Inhak with a pleased expression.
"But you've come all this way, and there's nowhere proper for you to sit... This is how life is (이럿탐니다)," the host said, unconsciously glancing back inside the gate, hesitating uneasily.
Kyŏngsu felt disappointed that Inhak was treating him rather awkwardly. If it were him, no matter how humble the room, he would have grabbed his sleeve, pulled him inside, introduced him to his wife as a friend he met in a foreign land 10 years ago, and even had the children greet him. But he couldn't understand why Inhak was acting so strangely. Was it because, as birds of a feather flock together (유뉴상종), there was a gap in consciousness between their different lives?
"The weather's warm, what's the point of sitting inside? If you don't have anything urgent, let's go for a walk (산뽀나)."
"Well..."
Inhak looked somewhat troubled, scratching his head for a moment. Sensing this, Kyŏngsu asked, looking at Inhak again.
"Do you have something urgent to attend to?"
"No? Nothing special..."
"Then, put on your clothes and come out. It's been a long time since we met quietly, let's share a bowl of makgeolli (막걸니라도) or something and talk about our feelings (서회나). Do you remember eating bulgogi and drinking back in Kyŏngsang Province?"
"Haha... Right. Those were good times. Then, please wait just a moment."
"Okay—"
Shortly after, Inhak came out wearing a fedora and a white *turumagi* (Korean overcoat). They walked out to the streetcar line and headed towards Ch'ŏngnyangni. The warm early April weather made the rich colors of spring particularly noticeable today, viewed against the unusually blue sky and through the purple haze on the distant mountains.
Kyŏngsu felt cheerful. When they reached the bridge near Ch'ŏngnyangni, he stopped, suggesting they smoke a cigarette before going on. Clear water trickled over the sandy beach beneath the bridge. He took out his cigarette case and shared one with Inhak. Being out in the suburbs after such a long time, it felt as if the wandering days of the past, flowing away like water 10 years ago, had returned before his eyes.
"Let's talk about our pasts. How have you been all this time? When did you come to Seoul?..."
Kyŏngsu finally asked about Inhak's experiences. He was most curious and intrigued to know what path he had taken to become a printing worker.
"What's the use of talking about the past? Then and now, I'm just struggling with the hardships of life."
"That may be so, but... seriously, when did you come up to Seoul?" <274>
"It's been about 6 or 7 years."
"But were you in your hometown before that?"
"Yes! At that time, well, with Mr. Kim... When I went back home, the family situation was getting worse and worse. The following year, my father passed away, and living alone in a single room (단간살님이), I couldn't wander around again, nor would there have been any point in doing so (그란댓자 무슨 소용이 있어야지요). So, I even tried teaching at a village school (하계도) for a few years, wouldn't you know. Hehe."
"Ha—"
"But that didn't work out either. The children gradually started running off to modern schools. So, I had no choice but to pack up my family and belongings (남부여대로) and come up to Seoul without much thought (불계하고). What could I do? Having no education from relations, I couldn't do labor, and to start a business, did I have capital? Did I have experience? So, after coming to Seoul and enduring all sorts of hardships, a friend who worked at a printing house—he was just a typesetter himself—suggested I try apprenticing at a printing house—saying since I was literate (식자가 있으니), I could learn to be a compositor (문선공을)—so, thanks to that friend's connection, I started as an apprentice, and for the past few years, I've been nominally working as a proper worker, earning a salary, so to speak. But goodness, how can one manage with this kind of living? With so many mouths to feed..."
Inhak stopped speaking and looked at Kyŏngsu, smiling wryly as if crestfallen. Kyŏngsu, having finally heard Inhak's story in detail, was undeniably moved (감구지리가 없지 안엇다).
"Never mind the past; now you've found a good occupation. Of course, there must be many hardships, but instead, the peace of mind and the freedom from constraints in this life are good (좃음니다)."
Kyŏngsu spoke earnestly, expressing thoughts he usually held, speaking sincerely.
But what was this? Hearing these words, Inhak's expression suddenly changed, subtly revealing a look of displeasure (긔색을).
"Nonsense! Please don't joke like that. How desperate must one be (여북해야) to live as a worker?"
Kyŏngsu, who had held high expectations for Inhak, was quite disappointed. "No, I'm definitely not joking. It's my kind of life that's truly insignificant (오죽잔케 살기 때문에)..."
"Goodness, what are you saying!... Please don't disparage me like that just because I became a laborer. No, what's wrong with your life, sir (선생님)? Truly, because you were so talented (조시니까) from way back, even after entering the Seoul scene... By the way, when did you study so much? Your reputation is high." <275>
Suddenly, Kyŏngsu felt a surge of displeasure. He couldn't figure Inhak out at all. At first, he thought the other man was also being cynical (히니꾸). If so, he could have easily accepted it. However, from the beginning, the way Inhak called him 'Teacher' (선생님) and used unusually respectful language had felt subtly disappointing. Still, he attributed it to the unfamiliar atmosphere arising from their different lives and tried to reconsider for a while. But Inhak's gradually revealed, blatant attitude was entirely different. He meant those words sincerely. No, he seemed to still possess the consciousness of 10 years ago.
"I have absolutely no intention of disparaging or mocking anyone. Especially not someone I'm meeting after such a long time. Would there be any reason (할니) to say such pointless things?"
Kyŏngsu finally spoke seriously, his expression somewhat embarrassed. Inhak made no reply. A heavy silence flowed between them. The sound of the flowing water suddenly seemed louder. They stared absently at the distant mountains for a while.

Kyŏngsu flicked the cigarette butt, now burning his fingertips, onto the stream. The moment the butt hit the water, the ember died, and it floated away. Kyŏngsu watched it absently, then suddenly turned his gaze away and asked Inhak, "Shall we go?"
"Let's (그라지요)."
They started walking. But Kyŏngsu suddenly felt unwell. He felt a sense of loss, as if he had lost something. His earlier cheerful mood vanished, and he gradually succumbed to depression.
He took Inhak into a Chinese restaurant and had a simple lunch. Even eating, the food tasted bland. Noticing the sudden change in Kyŏngsu's expression, Inhak seemed even more distant, which made Kyŏngsu feel increasingly unpleasant.
Forcibly suppressing these feelings, he tried again to probe Inhak's mind. But Kyŏngsu couldn't find even a single ray of the light he sought within Inhak's heart. His consciousness still embraced the darkness of ten years ago. His consciousness was still lumpen...
Truly, it was astonishing. Inhak had surprised him like this for a second time.
But this time, the surprise <276>—how great a disappointment it brought him!
Kyŏngsu and Inhak—what a strange contrast (콘트라스트)! What a ridiculous contradiction! The one leading a physically proper life had a rotten spirit. The one whose spirit was still sound had a rotten body. If both couldn't lead a life sound in both aspects, couldn't at least one person lead a whole life? He wanted to give his spirit to Inhak. If he couldn't live such a life himself, he wanted to at least add the spirit attached to his own half-paralyzed body to Inhak's. Rather than both becoming half-paralyzed, he wanted to make at least one person whole. Wasn't making one person whole not just for that one person's sake, but also a way to save both? Why? Because the great human ideal yearns for the perfection of body and soul...
Lumpen life degrades humans below animals. Similarly, lumpen consciousness degrades the worker. Only the possessor of a sound consciousness, acquired through a sound life, can illuminate the path ahead like the sun, can't they (안은가)!
Kyŏngsu left the restaurant and parted ways with Inhak. Although he had no particular place to go, unable to control his gloomy feelings, he saw a train bound for Wŏnsan arriving just then and walked straight to the station. He blindly bought a ticket to Ch'angdong.
Kyŏngsu boarded the train amidst the crowd of people. An unusual, desolate silence pressed down on him. He listlessly gazed out the train window at the distant scenery. A strange emptiness wandered through the sky of his imagination like a frail butterfly.
His chest felt heavy for a moment. It was more for Inhak's sake than his own. Had the ten years truly been in vain?... Is human consciousness really this difficult to change?...
He looked back again at himself and Inhak from ten years ago. Back then, weren't both of them equally lumpen, inside and out? But now, even if half-paralyzed, hadn't one side of their lives been revolutionized? Couldn't that be seen as progress of the times? As a process of consciousness?...
As this thought occurred, he found himself smiling wryly (고소하엿다) again.—

Kyŏngsu got off the train at Ch'angdong Station and walked out into the refreshing open fields. The afternoon sunset was already beating down warmly <277> on his back. But spring—the spring that seemed to be coming for months—still hadn't arrived. It would be a while before the flowers bloomed. The ice had certainly melted, and the water flowed noisily, but the stubborn spring still seemed far away. Ah, when will this spring ever come?...
Kyŏngsu looked up at the peaks of Tobongsan, which seemed to pierce the high sky, and walked aimlessly into the fields. The sun blazed, seemingly pulling in the spring light... Unconsciously, a whistle escaped Kyŏngsu's lips. The sharp whistle sound, like some kind of signal, rose high into the quiet air. (End)
April 6th, Byŏngja year (1936), in the rain, at the foot of Inwangsan <294>

**Summary**

The story follows Kyŏngsu, a disillusioned intellectual working as a low-paid journalist for a popular magazine in 1930s Seoul. He feels trapped and compares his life to a suffering carriage horse. He unexpectedly reunites with Inhak, a friend from his wandering youth ten years prior. Kyŏngsu initially idealizes Inhak, who is now a printing worker, seeing his manual labor as more authentic. However, he is deeply disappointed to find that Inhak still holds onto old 'lumpen' consciousness, looking down on his own working-class status and envying Kyŏngsu's superficial position as an intellectual. The story contrasts their physically and mentally 'rotten' states, lamenting the difficulty of achieving a whole, conscious existence amidst societal constraints and the slow arrival of 'spring' (change/enlightenment).

From a historical perspective, the story offers insights into the struggles and disillusionment of Korean intellectuals under Japanese colonial rule, particularly the tension between intellectual aspirations and harsh economic realities ("sorrow of employment"). It portrays the perceived divide and lack of solidarity between intellectuals and the working class, highlighting the persistence of pre-modern or 'lumpen' consciousness even among those engaged in modern labor. Kyŏngsu's internal conflict and his ultimate disappointment in Inhak reflect the complex social and ideological landscape of the time, including the nascent, often frustrated, development of class consciousness. The final imagery of the delayed spring subtly evokes a longing for national or social liberation.

十年後
						李箕永


몇 일 전에 인쇄에 부칠 잡지 원고에 교정이 오늘부터 나온다는 말을 드른 경수는 아침을 재촉해서 먹고 그 길노 바로 D인쇄소로 달녀갓다.
그는 이칭에 있는 공장사무실노 올나가 보니, 별서 사무원들은 느러앉어서 제각각기 맡은 일에 열중하고 있엇다.
한달에 한번식 발행하는 잡지원고의 교정을 혼저 맡어노코 보기는 여간 성가신 노릇이 안이다. 더구나 문선이 서툴너서, 준ㅅ장에 뻘겅글짜 투성이를 만들게 하는데는, 골치가 압허서 견딀 수 없다. 그런 것을 삼준 사준을 본 뒤에야, 겨우 오자를 메쿨 수가 있는데 어떠튼지 이런 일노 5, 6일 동안을 날마다 시달니고나면, 그는 마치 중병을 치른 것처럼 얼굴이 축낫다. 그러타고 불행을 말할 수도 없다. 월급은 쥐꼬리만치 밧게 못받지만, 그나마 고만두면, 당장 식구들이 살 수 없는 형편이다. 갈수록 생활난이 심야<268> 취직이 어려운 세상인 만콤, 남들은 이런 속은 모르고, 직업을 가젓다고 자기를 부러워하는 축도 있다 .그래서 시굴사람들이 간혹 서울을 왓다갈 때는 의례히 찾어와서 제각금 취직을 식혀달나는데는 질색할 노릇이다. 그런 말을 들을 때마다 경수는 기가 막혓다.
지금 자기도, 직업다운 직업을 갓지 못해서, 물질적으로나, 정신적으로나, 여간 고통을 받고 있는 것이 안닌데 어떠케 남의 직업을 구해줄 수 있겟는가. 그러나 이런 사정을 솔직하게 말하면 투박한 그들은 도리혀, 박정하다고 오해를 할는지 몰나서, 그는 조흔 말노, 차차 두고 보자고-그런 자리가 있는대로 구해보겟다는 대답을 하군 하엿다. 그러나 그것은 자기 얼굴이 간지러운 멀정한 거짓말이엿다.

경수는 지나온 경험을 도리켜 보아도 씨라린 기억이 새로이 씹힌다. 고향에서 보통학교를 졸업하고 나서, 면서기로 고원으로 굴너단이다가 공부에 뜻을 두고 서울노 올나올 때는-어려서부터 재동이라는 칭찬을 드럿든 그는 미상불 자부심도 다소 생겨서, 설마 어듸든지, 부칠 손이 잡히려니 하엿는데, 막상 올나와 보니, 생각든 바와는 아주 딴판으로서, 어듸 하나, 발부칠 곳이 없엇다. 그래 그는 몇 차례를 올나와서 헛물만 켜고 나려갓다. 돈 없고 발년 없고 학력조차 박약한 그를 누구나 채용해주지 안엇다. 마참내, 그는 실망한 끝에, 할 수 없이 단렴하랴는 차에, 그 때 마침 지금 있는 잡지사에서, 모집하든 현상문예(懸賞文藝)에 응모(應募)한 것이 요행으로 당선되자, 그런 발년으로 기자(記者)란 직업을 얻게 된 것이다.

인학은 말을 끈치자, 경수를 처다보며 허굽흔드키 실쭉 웃는다. 경수는 인학의 소경역을 비로소 자세히 듯고, 어느듯 감구지리가 없지 안엇다.
「지난 일이야 하여간, 지금은, 조흔 직업을 잘 얻으섯요. 문론 고생되는 점도 만켓지만, 그 대신 마음편코, 아모 거리낄 것 없는 생활이 좃음니다. 」
하고, 경수는, 참으로 진정에서, 흐르는 말을 평소에 생각하든 그대로, 진중하게 말하엿다.
그런데, 웬일이냐? 이 말을 드른 인학은 별안간 표정이 달너지며, 실죽한 긔색을 은연이 나타나 뵈인다.
「천만에! 그런 농담은 마십시요. 여북해야, 직공생활을 함니까?」
인학에게 만흔 기대를 가젓든 경수는 자못, 낙망하엿다. 「아니, 나는 결코 농담으로 하는 말이 안님니다. 나같은 생활이야말노, 오죽잔케 살기 때문에...」
「원, 별말슴을!...노동자가 되엿다고 그처럼 비양하지는 마십시요. 아니 선생님 생활이 어떼서 그라서요? 참, 원악 그전부터 재주가, 조시니까, 서울바닥에 드러서도,..그런데 어느 틈에 글공부는 그러케 하섯서요. 소문이 높으시니」<275>
고만 경수는 와락, 불쾌한 생각이 치미럿다. 그는 인학을 어떠케 종잡을 수가 없엇다. 처음에는 저편에서도 히니꾸를 하는 줄만 아럿다. 만은 그러타면, 그는 얼마든지 그것을 감수했을 것이다. 그러나 경수는, 그가 처음부터, 선생이라고 부르는 게이나 유달니 존경하는 말이 도리여 섭섭하게 들녀왓는데, 그는 그래도 그것을 서로 생활이 같지 안은 생소한 분위기로 알고, 다시 한동안은, 돌녀 생각해 보기도 하엿다. 그러나 차차 그의 노골적으로 드러나는 태도는, 전혀 그런 것이 안이엿다. 그는 진정으로, 그런 말을 한다. 아니 그는 오히려, 10년 전의 의식을 그대로 가지고 있는 상 싶엇다.
「나는 결코 누구를 비양거리거나, 조롱하랴는 그런 생각은 조곰도 없오. 더구나 오래간만에 만나는 자에게 그런 시럽슨 말을 할니 있나요.」
하고 경수는 비로소 정색을 하며, 다소간 무색한 표정으로 말하엿다. 인학은 아모 댓구도 안는다. 그 동안에 무거운 침묵이 흘넛다. 흐르는 물소리가 갑작이 높이 들닌다. 그들은 무료한드시 서로 하동안 먼산을 처다보고 있엇다.



경수는 손톱이 뜨거워진 담배토막을 냇물 위로 던젓다. 담배토막은 물위로 떠러지는 순간에, 불이 꺼지고 둥둥 떠간다. 경수는 무심이 그것을 바라보다가 별안간 발작적으로 시선을 도리키며 「고만 갈가요」.
하고, 인학에게 무럿다.
「그라지요.」
그들은 거름을 떼노앗다. 그러나 경수는 갑작이 기분이 조치 안엇다. 그는 마치, 무엇을 일은 것처럼, 서운한 생각이 드러갓다. 그는 아까까지 유쾌하든 기분이 사러지고, 차차 우울증에 사로잡혓다.
그는 인학을 다리고 청요리 집으로 드러가서 간단한 점심 요기를 하엿다. 음식을 먹어도 맛이 없다. 별안간 경수의 표정이 달너진 눈치를 채자 인학은 더욱 설음설음한 구석을 보이는 것이, 경수로 하야금 한칭 불유쾌하게 하엿다.
그는 이런 심사를 강잉히 눌느고, 다시 인학의 심중을 캐보앗다. 그러나 경수는 인학의 마음속에서, 자기가 발견하랴는 광명은 한가닥도 찾어낼 수 없엇다. 그의 의식은, 여전히 십년 전의 암흑을 그대로, 안꼬 있엇다. 그의 의식은 여전히 룸폔이엇다...
참으로 그것은 놀나운 일이엿다. 그는 인학으로 하야금, 이러케 두 번채 놀내엿다.
그러나 이번의 놀나움<276> 그것은 자기에게 얼마나 큰 실망을 주엇든가?
경수와 인학이-그것은 참으로 기이한 콘트라스트다! 이 얼마나, 가소로운, 모순의 대립이냐? 육체적으로 정당한 생활을 하는 사람은 정신이 썩엇다. 정신이 아직, 성한 사람은, 육체가 썩엇다. 만일 두 사람이, 다 같이 양면으로 건전한 생활을 못할진대, 차라리 한 사람이나, 온전한 생활을 못할 것인가? 그는 자기의 정신을 인학에게 주고 싶다. 그런 생활을 자기가 못할진대, 차라리 반신불수와 같은 자기 몸에 붓튼 정신을 그에게나 보태주고 싶음이엿다. 두 사람이 똑같은 반신불수가 되느니, 차라리, 한 사람이나, 성한 사람을 만들고 싶다. 그것은 한 사람의 성한 사람만 위하는 것이 아니라 역시 두 사람을 다 같이 건지는 일이 안일가? 웨그러냐 하면 인간의 위대한 이상은 영육의 완전을 동경하기 때문에...
룸폔생활은 인간을 동물 이하로 타락식힌다. 그와 마찬가지로, 룸폔의식은 노동자를 타락식힌다. 오직 건전한 생활에서 체득하는, 건전한 의식의 소유자야말노 그의 압길에, 태양과 같은 광명을 빗처올 수 있지 안은가!

경수는 창동역에서 기차를 내던지고, 시원한 들 가온대로, 거러나왓다. 어느듯 오후의 석양이 훗훗하게 등허리를 <277> 내리쪼인다. 그러나 봄은- 벌서, 몇 달 전부터 오랴는 봄은, 아직도 오지 안엇다. 꽃이 필나면, 머럿다. 어름은 확실이 풀녀서 물은 소리치고 흐르건만 완구한 봄은 아직도 먼 것 같다. 아, 이 봄은 언제나 오랴는가?...
