巴里懷古
						呂運弘

A thought that hasn't left my mind (腦裡) since returning from Paris (巴里) is that in certain gloomy alleys of Paris, there were still many houses using oil lamps (石油불). And if you ask whether these were only places like basements (地下室), that wasn't the case; externally, they were imposing (堂堂한) 4 or 5-story granite (花崗石) buildings (建物). Also, I thought that Paris's sewage system (下水道) facilities (設備) were incomplete (不完全), making it a relatively unclean (不潔) place for a modern city (近代 都市).<42>

**Summary**

The author, Yŏ Unhong, recalls his impressions of Paris, focusing on unexpected aspects that contradicted its image as a modern metropolis. He notes the surprising persistence of oil lamps even in substantial granite buildings and the inadequate sewage system, leading him to perceive Paris as relatively unclean despite its grandeur. For historians of colonial Korea, this piece offers a glimpse into a Korean intellectual's perspective on the West during the 1930s. It shows a critical engagement with Western modernity, observing its imperfections rather than uncritically admiring it, which might reflect broader attitudes among some Korean elites navigating colonialism and modernization.
