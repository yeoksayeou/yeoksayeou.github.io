Statements of Various Gentlemen
1. Sir, are you a nationalist or a socialist?
2. Sir, will you become a practitioner/activist or a scholar?
3. Sir, in terms of thought, whose influence have you received the most?

Chŏngju Osan High Common School, Yi Sŭnghun
1. I am a nationalist.
2. I will just engage in education.

Kyŏngsŏng, Kahoe-dong 26, Ch'oe Rin
1. I do not wish to tell others what kind of ideologue I am. However, if forced to speak, shall I say I am a nationalist with socialist elements added?
2. I wish to become a practical activist (實際운동가) with a firm scholarly basis.
3. For this one, I will avoid a clear answer for now.

Kyŏngsŏng, Sungin-dong 127, Yi Kwangsu
Simple words are easily misunderstood. Among them, the word 'nationalism' has little fixed, common content.
1. I am a nationalist.
2. My goal is to write novels and be a newspaper reporter.
3. Tolstoy, Jesus, Buddha, Dosan (島山) <18>

Kyŏngsŏng, P'yŏng-dong, An Chaehong
1. I do not enjoy limiting myself as an adherent of one ideology (一主義者). Fundamentally, my disposition is to always walk the best path, a sort of principle of life (生活原義), firmly based on the chains of history and the state of society. In that sense, the path I have recently been walking is that of a nationalist who closely observes the class situation. I believe this tendency is quite established.
2. Whether I will become a scholar (學究) or a practical activist is not something I can decide myself. As things stand now, it seems necessary as a scholar to establish a definite theoretical basis and system regarding the current situation. However, I have never decided, nor do I intend to decide, to remain strictly a scholar at any time. I just do not wish to deviate from a genuine scholarly theoretical basis even if I were to engage in practical activism.
3. Lately, I have not been greatly inspired by any single individual. However, pioneers of the masses who were practical fighters throughout their lives and also superior theoretical leaders would inspire me the most.

Kyŏngsŏng, Wŏn-dong, Song Chinu
1. I am a nationalist.
2. I intend to do my best as a newspaper reporter.
3. In my youth, I was greatly influenced by Chinese Classics (漢學), but thereafter, I was greatly influenced by liberal democracy (自由民主主義).

Sinnae, Ch'adae-okchŏng 51-ŭi 3, Kim Kyŏngjae
<19>
1. I don't know if this matches your question, but I aim for a life that conforms to natural evolution.
2. I hope that what I think can be embodied in reality.
3. Darwin and Marx

Kyŏngsŏng, Insa-dong 153, Han Kukchong
Reply: In accordance with your question, I answer as follows.
1. I am neither a nationalist nor a socialist; I am an independent, omnidirectional non-aligned ideologue (八方無極主義者).
2. I do not aim to be a scholar or a practical activist; I aim for action through inaction (無爲而化) or natural evolution.
3. As a thinker of the Heavenly People (天民), I have my own inherent individualistic thought, so I only take responsibility for guiding the later people (後天國民); I have not been influenced by anyone.

Sinnae, Sungin-dong 61-ŭi 36, Pak Wŏnsŏ Bang Yi Sŏk
1. Answer: Of course, a socialist.
2. Answer: I aim to be a scholar.
3. Answer: During my time at Waseda University (早大), I was greatly inspired by Mr. Ōyama Ikuo (大山郁夫).

Kyŏngsŏng, Kyŏngun-dong 88, Ch'ŏndogyo Youth General Alliance, Pak Wan
1. I intend to realize the Innaech'ŏn (人乃天, Man is Heaven) ideology advocated by Master Suun (水雲) of Ch'ŏndogyo (天道敎).
2. I intend to become a practical activist.
3. I was most deeply (§Œ히) influenced by the revolutionary thought of Master Suun.

Sinnae, Tangju-dong 94, O Hwayŏng
<20>
Respectfully reply:
Please forgive my inability to reply promptly to your question due to being away.
1. Nationalism
1. Influence of Wŏllam (月南)

Sinnae, Iksŏn-dong 166-ŭi 5, Myŏng Chese
Reply:
I wish for the ever-increasing development of your company and reply as follows to the matters inquired about.
As noted below:
1. Nationalism
2. Practical activism
3. I haven't been influenced by anyone, but I resonate with Gandhism (깐듸주의).

Sinnae, Unni-dong 46-ŭi 1, Kim Ch'angje
Reply: It is too rash to write about such significant questions on a single postcard, but since you have asked, not answering might be even more disrespectful, so I briefly state as follows.
1. I am neither a nationalist nor a socialist. "If this cannot be called an ideology," I am a Christian ideologue (기독교주의자). However, it can be said that within Christian ideology, socialism is included more than nationalism. Only, it is clear that it is not about struggle (non-resistance, 無抵抗) and not materialism (唯物論).
2. I do not wish to separate theory (學理) and practice (實際) in such a way. Of course, in one's own life, one side may be more emphasized, but I have not set such a definite goal. "Learning is Living" (學卽生活) is my motto.
3. I am a Christian. Therefore, it is <21> natural that I would have been greatly influenced by Christian figures. In philosophy, Kant (캔트) and Eucken (어이켄); in literature, Hugo (유고) and Tolstoy (톨스토이) seem to have had the most influence.

P'yŏngyang-bu, Sangsugu-ri 33, Kil Sŏnju
Respectfully reply:
1. Religious Ideology (宗敎主義)
2. Practical (實際)
3. Christian Spirit (기독정신)

Kyŏngsŏng, Sup'yo-chŏng, Yu Chint'ae
1. I am a nationalist.
2. I am not destined to become a scholar.
3. I was greatly influenced by scholars of Chinese Classics (漢學者), but there is no one I revered as a teacher distinctly enough to point out.

Kyŏngsŏng, An'guk-dong, Han Yongun
1. -
2. -
3. As a Buddhist, the influence of Śākyamuni Buddha (釋迦牟尼) was the greatest.

Sinnae, Kye-dong 15-chi 12, Sŏng Sanghun
1. Politically, I advocate national XX-ism (민족XX주의); economically, I advocate fairness in distribution.
2. It seems difficult to distinguish between a scholar and a practical activist. Perhaps I will advance as a polemicist (論客), a sort of intermediate type?
3. I have been influenced ideologically by the writings of H.G. Wells (에취지, 웰스), Lenin (레닌), and Inomata Tsunao (猪俁津南雄).

Kyŏngsŏng, Ch'ŏngun-dong, Pae Sŏngnyong
1. When it comes to ideology (주의), I understand that one must devote one's entire life to it, regardless of <22> which ideology it is. It is something indispensable for anyone who intends to determine their path forward with the clearest thought and utilize their limited efforts most effectively, but I believe ideology, at least, is different from ideals or fantasies of "it would be good if it were so." Real life and ideology cannot be separated, so to be called an adherent of some ideology means being faithful enough to die for that ideology. I believe such an ideology is by no means as cheap as the term is commonly used these days. It is not that I live without any goal, but looking back at my past experiences and life (經歷 生涯), I have not yet made practical efforts worthy of calling myself a nationalist or a socialist. In conclusion, I cannot call myself an adherent of any ideology.
2. While it is true that I try to pay attention as much as possible to theoretical fields that do not depart from reality, I certainly do not wish to aim solely at scholarship.
3. Ideologically, I have nothing particular to mention as having received the most influence from someone. Rather than saying I received much influence, if I were to speak of receiving many impressions, perhaps it was from the *Confessions* (戱悔錄) of Rousseau (릇소), who is called a pioneer of the French Enlightenment movement, when I first started reading books on humanities long ago? Even then, there were points in his arguments I couldn't agree with, but I remember even now having reconsidered his arguments concerning the 'Order of Human Degeneration' <23> and the 'Harm of Culture' with great interest several times.

Kyŏngsŏng, Chongno, Yi Hwǎng
1. Socialist
2. Not a scholar.
3. There isn't anyone I can clearly point to as 'this person'.

Sinnae, Wŏn-dong 9-bŏnji, Yu Ŏkkyŏm
1. This will likely be determined after I leave this world.
2. Judging by my current work, it might be appropriate to say I aim for scholarship.
3. I have received the most influence from Jesus Christ.

Chungoe Ilbo Sa, Yi Chŏngsŏp
The 5 questions in the January issue of your magazine can be seen as a great success, but I view these 3 questions this time as a great failure. However, unable to just waste a 1.5-chon postcard, I intend to respond to your company's mental test.
1. There is no need to say what kind of ideologue I am. Why? Because the nature of my profession忌 dislikes affirming what my ideology is. This is an honest confession.
2. Since I have consistently been a scholar (엽대 학구), it will likely be easy to continue a scholarly life in the future.
3. In sociology, Durkheim (뛸케임); in natural philosophy, Poincaré (포앙카레); in philosophy, Bergson (베르그쏜), etc. However, being originally of shallow learning and meager talent (淺學菲才), I am not worth discussing with (不足與言). <24>

Kyŏngsŏng, Hongp'a-dong 2, Chu Yohan
1. I was born with a skeptical nature. Therefore, I lack the courage to declare any ideology. Speaking vaguely and about immediately pressing issues, I would say I am a nationalist.
2. I lack the courage to state this definitively either.
3. A. J. Thomson (탐슨), T. Huxley (헉슬리), B. Russell (럿셀), etc.

Sinnae, Kyŏngun-dong 88, Yi Tonhwa
1. I am a Suun-ist (水雲주의자). Suun-ism is an ideology that treads firm reality under a grand ideal, so its content has three stages: Supporting the Nation (輔國), Pacifying the People (안민), and Earthly Paradise (지상천국). These three stages can be interpreted initially as national XX (민족XX), secondly economic liberation (경제해방), thirdly earthly paradise.
2. I am a person incapable of either. Based on thought alone, I am closer to a scholar.
3. I highly revere the character of Master Ch'oe Suun (崔水雲).

Sinnae, Kwanghwamun-t'ong, Im Wŏn'gŭn
Well. As for myself, I don't call myself any kind of ideologue. Nor do I have anything worth calling myself. I merely think, as a living being – a living being that is human, possessing reason – that I want to lead a more human life, and that I should. Therefore, regarding whether I am scholarly or involved in practical activism, I haven't clearly drawn such a boundary line myself. Also, because I myself am such a vaguely <25> defined target, there is nothing particular to note about receiving the most ideological influence from anyone.

Sinnae, P'yŏng-dong 75-ŭi 17, An Chaehak
I apologize, but please forgive my situation where I cannot answer even one item.

Sinnae, Kwanch'ŏl-dong 140, Kim T'agwŏn
1. No ideology (無主義)
2. Scholarship is preparation, Practice is practice (實際)
3. Laozi (老子)

Chosŏn chi Kwang Sa, Kim Tonghyŏk
1. I am neither a nationalist nor a socialist. In other words, I am a blank slate (白紙).
2. I am originally not a scholar. Therefore, I do not aim for scholarship. However, I do research what is necessary. So, I intend to try to do everything I am responsible for.
3. Just as my ideology is a blank slate, the influence I have received from anyone is extremely small.

Sinnae, Songhyŏn-dong 29, Song Pong'u
1. I am a socialist.
2. Let's skip this answer.
3. I was greatly influenced by Marx (맑쓰).

Sinnae, Nagwŏn-dong, Pak Ch'anghun
1. Nationalism
2. Looking at Korean history as a whole, I believe separating scholarship and practice would lead to limping (跛行) to that extent.

Sinnae, Ta'ok-chŏng, Na Hyesŏk
1. I avoid answering.
2. When a good time comes in the future, I intend to engage in the women's movement. <26>

**Summary**

This 1930 survey published in *Samch'ŏlli* magazine asked prominent Korean figures about their ideological leanings (nationalist vs. socialist), career aspirations (scholar vs. activist), and primary intellectual influences. The responses reveal a wide spectrum of thought among intellectuals during the Japanese colonial period. Many identified as nationalists, some with socialist leanings, while others declared themselves socialists, Christians, followers of Ch'ŏndogyo's Suun, or even "non-aligned" or "blank slates." Several emphasized practical action (activism, journalism, education, women's movement), while others leaned towards scholarship, though often stressing the connection between theory and practice. Influences ranged from Korean figures like Suun and Dosan (An Ch'angho) to international thinkers like Tolstoy, Marx, Gandhi, Jesus, Buddha, Western philosophers (Kant, Eucken, Bergson, Russell), scientists (Darwin, Huxley, Thomson, Poincaré), and political figures (Lenin, Rousseau). Some respondents were notably cautious or refused to define themselves, perhaps reflecting the political constraints of the time. This survey is valuable for historians as it provides direct insight into the diverse ideological currents, self-perceptions, and intellectual engagements of Korean elites navigating colonialism, highlighting the complex interplay between nationalism, socialism, religion, and Western/global thought in shaping modern Korean identity and aspirations. The reluctance or careful phrasing of some responses also hints at the risks associated with expressing political views openly.
