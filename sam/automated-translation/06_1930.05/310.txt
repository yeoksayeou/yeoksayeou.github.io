기사제목	名文의 香味, 上海에서 (The Fragrance of Fine Writing, from Shanghai)

기사형태	기행문 (Travelogue)

名文의 香味, 上海에서
						李光洙 (Yi Kwangsu)

Our party, having seen the first snow cover the peaks of Yŏnsan near Yongamp'o, boarded the ship and after several days passing through Yŏngk'ou, Taeryŏn, Yŏnt'ai, and Ch'ingtao, spent last night below the Usong Fort. This morning, though the sunrise is hazy, we are sailing up the calm, waveless Hwangp'o River. Bathed in the yellowish morning sunlight reflected in the soft yellow hue of the willows on both banks dyed by the silt, we head towards the Shanghai Bund, known as the London of the East. The electric lights on the buoys marking the water's depth, placed at intervals, still flicker faintly, not yet extinguished. On the stout dredgers engaged in dredging work, the smell of coal smoke from newly boiling water in the engines swirls aimlessly. Our ship proceeds very cautiously around the wide river bends, the sound of the propeller barely audible. Passengers have packed their bedding and luggage and come out onto the deck, eager to see the Shanghai cityscape. Some lean against the ship's rail, pointing out landmarks to newly arrived passengers, asking "Where is that? Where is this?" Others, lonely travelers with no companions to talk to, stare blankly without a focus. Still others, faces full of joy, pace back and forth. The ship's crew, having changed their clothes and polished their shoes, stroll on the bridge, while the sailors are busy scrubbing the deck with mops and brooms. As this is my first visit, my nerves are strangely excited, my body fidgeting. Contemplating the vast journey ahead, long sighs escape me.

Through the western mist, a large, indistinct shape, like an octagonal mirror, flashes reflecting the sunlight as it gradually draws nearer. I heard it's a passenger ship plying the Yangtse River; it looks like a three-story building constructed on a large wooden base. On the railing, three or four Western children, seemingly siblings, wearing neat, snow-white clothes with hats askew, seem to be mocking our ship. The pigtailed passengers on our ship shout back insults. Looking back, three or four steamers follow behind us, just like our ship, moving slowly. It seems that because the river is narrow, entry is prohibited at night, so they all waited at the Usong entrance and are only now heading up to the Shanghai Bund in the morning.

Gradually, pavilions, factories, and ranch-like structures appear sporadically through the dense groves of young trees. The dark fog in the estuary grows thicker. After some time, one or two ships anchored midstream come into view. Over there, at a not-so-large pier, a dilapidated steamer with its bottom missing seems to be hoisted in the air, awaiting repairs. Ahead, what looks like a spider's web on the masthead might be the wireless telegraph of a Republic of China warship. Going a bit further up and passing a wide bend, we suddenly enter a different world. Through the fog, four and five-story tall buildings stand packed like teeth in a comb. On the left and right banks of the narrow river, steamers and sampans are layered upon layers. Those flying the white-and-blue flag high on their masts are ships about to depart. Now, the sights and sounds of a bustling, chaotic city, like chaotically鳴ing musical instruments, create a confusion of colors, shapes, and waves in the atmosphere. Passing the arrogant American, British, and French ironclad warships standing proudly in the center, listening to the clear military music emanating from them, our ship carefully docks its starboard side at the southern bank pier. As if etiquette and decorum are playthings only for leisurely times, even the gentlemen and ladies who were quite composed during the voyage push forward, shoving aside those beside them, each trying to disembark first, heedless of those before or behind. It seems like the beastly nature of humanity is revealed, making one feel pity and amusement for this humanity that prattles on about civilization and morality. Perhaps this is because they are uncivilized Orientals, but even seeing a Westerner among us kicking an Oriental aside to get off first, while revealing his greater physical strength, could not be permitted as a sign of advanced morality. Being inherently not quick, I stood stiffly in a corner and disembarked last. My companion was arguing with some people over luggage. Those people were saying in a very friendly tone:
"Let me carry your luggage for you."
"No thanks. Go away."
"Oh, don't be like that. Let me just carry it for a moment."<72>
"Hey you, get away."
I thought my companion's behavior towards these seemingly kind people was too harsh. How could I have known? They say that even if they move the luggage just one step, they demand one or two won. As those people insisted on taking the luggage, clinging on, my friend laughed and said, "You have to swear at them in English; if you use their language, they look down on you." Then, eyes wide, he yelled:
"Go get away!" stomping his foot and raising his fist. Only then did they bow their heads deeply, mutter something, and run off. I felt very uncomfortable for those pitiful compatriots. Why have they lost all sense of shame? Are they not the people who possess the four hundred provinces, four hundred million brethren, and five thousand years of culture, with Yao, Shun, Confucius, and Mencius? How did they come to fear "go damn" (꼬 땜) more than their innate nature and meekly accept insults from those residing in their own land? They have become so accustomed to contempt, so very used to it, that they now think it's something they rightly deserve. Furthermore, they are people raised in an excellent and abundant natural environment. The primary reason for their corruption and downfall is abandoning their rural hometowns to covet the splendid ease of the city. The second reason is their lack of the pedigree of being a strong nation, the standard of *yangban* in the modern world. The third is their delusion that anyone can receive the splendid ease enjoyed by civilized people simply by coming to a civilized city, without knowing that the qualification for city life—civilized life—comes from receiving a civilized education. Besides these, the tens of thousands of rickshaw pullers in Shanghai, whose spirits are turned beastly and whose health and lives are shortened by excessive labor and lack of nutrition and comfort, and the people who wander day and night looking only for opportunities to steal—I wonder if this is all punishment received as the karmic consequence (罪障) of forgetting "plowing and digging" (耕鑿).

We boarded a shrimp-shaped sampan, weaving through small and large boats, passing the bow of a French warship, narrowly avoiding collision with a small steam launch darting down like an arrow, and rode the waves left by the large ships to land safely on the opposite Bund—Huangp'ot'an.

The main road parallel to the riverbank is also called Huangp'ot'an. Broad-leafed poplar trees (白楊木) stand elegantly scattered along the riverbank, and in their shade, trams, horse carriages, automobiles, and rickshaws rush back and forth dizzily. The imposing stone buildings housing companies and banks are said to be the finest here. The Hwip'ung Bank (滙豊銀行 - HSBC), which controls the finances of the great nation of China, drew particular attention. Lined up next to it are the P'aegi Bank (白耳義銀行 - Belgian Bank?), famous for its wealth, and banks from every other nation; it's said there isn't a single one without at least a branch here. The fact that China's financial center is concentrated in this tiny cat's forehead (貓額) sized Huangp'ot'an Bund gives a strange impression to the visitor from afar. When I imagine the mouths of these banks reaching into every corner of the four hundred provinces, sucking the lifeblood (膏血) of the four hundred million hapless Chinese people through things like mines and railways, I shudder. Imagining China's documents (文券) for salt tax, customs duties, postal tax, etc., pawned and waiting for their terms to expire inside the dark vaults behind those large glass windows, tears indeed fall at the spectacle of this great old nation (老大國) on the verge of bankruptcy and ruin. Not far away, enclosed by a sturdy iron fence where exotic trees and plants seem to doze, is Shanghai's famous Huangp'ot'an Park. Standing at the gate, swaying as if dozing, is a tall Indian policeman (印度巡査) with a dark face, his mustache curled from ear to ear, his head wrapped sharply in a crimson turban – unmistakably an Indian Sikh policeman. The British and American Concessions cooperate in all matters, using Indian policemen—or rather, police assistants (巡査補)—to guard their territory. Only the French Concession at the western end uses Annamese policemen. In other words, the Anglo-Saxons unite as Anglo-Saxons, symbolizing their shared glory of conquering India by having Indians guard the streets and gates. The French, representing the Latin race and the glory of ancient Rome and expressing modern Latin prestige (威嚴), use the Annamese they govern as police assistants. What's more noteworthy is that the Indian police are forbidden from cutting their hair or shaving their beards (斷髮削鬚), preserving India's ancient customs on their heads. The Annamese are also forbidden from shaving their heads (朔髮) and must wear a particular kind of inverted-pot-shaped hat. Similarly, when Chinese are employed as drivers (御者) for horse carriages, they are made to wear strange, traditional Chinese attire (服色). This is just like how Westerners make their servants wear bizarre outfits to enhance the dignity of their own ministers and high officials (政丞判書), turning them into objects of ridicule, and also like cherishing these unfortunate races as interesting antiques (骨董品). I've digressed too much. We entered Huangp'ot'an Park. It could be said that the natural essences of the six continents are gathered here; plants characteristic of each continent and climate zone have been transplanted and skillfully arranged in this small, palm-sized plot, achieving a marvel of contrast and harmony perhaps more exquisite than what the Creator (造化翁) himself arranged since the dawn of time (天地開闢). Clicking my tongue at the development of human intellect, which could be called excessive, we took a rickshaw and sped towards Nanjing Road (英大碼路 - possibly Ying Damalu, referring to Nanjing Road), the busiest and most splendid street in bustling Shanghai. The four, five, six, seven-story brick Western-style buildings (洋舘) lining both sides make us feel as if we are scrambling confusedly on a narrow, thousand-fathom cliff path. On the perfectly straight, whetstone-smooth brick road (磚石길), trams and automobiles run ceaselessly. The people inside are not idle sightseers like me, but busy people for whom even the fast automobiles seem too slow. The door handles of those buildings must spark from friction; four, five, six telephone sets<73> ring constantly; the clatter of typewriters (活字板소리 - likely 'hwaljap'an', printing plate/type-board, used metonymically for typewriter) and copying machines (寫字機소리)! Ah, what a bustling world. Shanghai's population is said to be only one million, but why are there so many people? It seems as if not a single person is napping or playing Baduk or Changgi indoors; all one million have poured out, rushing east, west, south, north, their feet barely touching the ground.

Amidst this, standing tall as if saying "I know nothing of other worlds," calmly raising one arm then the other to control the chaos of people and vehicles (人車), the Indian policeman is like a stone Buddha placed in the middle of the busy street. Indeed, his emptiness is amidst this bustle, yet this busyness has no relation to him. It is entirely unconnected (沒交涉).

My heart pounding as we passed imposing opium dens (鴉片廛) and gun shops (銃砲廛), we sped on for some time, and here was the French Concession (法界). In less than ten minutes, we had passed through China and Britain to reach France. Though separated by only one street, the French Concession was quiet and desolate, a different world. Wondering if this was a symbol of the decline of its mother country, a subtle sadness arose. However, the cleanliness of the roads, the long rows of trees (長林), and the refined elegance (宿灑함) of the houses displayed a light, poetic Latin style. (Remainder omitted - original text)<74>

**Summary**

Yi Kwangsu describes his arrival in Shanghai in 1930, traveling up the Hwangp'o River past foreign warships to the Bund. He observes the chaotic energy of the city, the imposing Western banks controlling China's finances, and the stark contrast between the bustling British/American Concessions and the quieter French Concession. He expresses pity and critique towards the perceived degradation of Chinese people, particularly laborers and those soliciting work aggressively at the docks, attributing their plight to leaving rural life for illusory urban comforts and lacking the status of a "strong nation." He notes the colonial hierarchy manifest in the use of Indian and Annamese police by Western powers, viewing their enforced traditional attire as a form of exoticization and subjugation. The piece blends vivid travel description with reflections on modernity, colonialism, national identity, and moral decline. From a Korean colonial history perspective, the article is revealing for its complex viewpoint: Yi, himself a subject of Japanese colonialism, observes another nation under quasi-colonial foreign influence. His critique of Chinese "degradation" and yearning for "strong nation" status reflects anxieties common among Korean intellectuals of the period, caught between admiration for Western/Japanese modernity and resentment of colonial domination. Historians might find his observations on the foreign concessions, the role of Western capital, the visible hierarchy among Asians under colonial rule (Indian/Annamese police serving Western powers), and his specific interpretation of Chinese social issues particularly interesting.
