발행일	1930년 05월 01일
기사제목	웃는 낫이 그리워(외 1편) (Missing a Smiling Face (and 1 other))
필자	政蘆風
기사형태	시 (Poem)

웃는 낫이 그리워
						政蘆風

웃는 낫이 그리워
손녹이는 젊으니 웃는 얼골 그리워
바람찬 겨울밤엔 숫장사 하고 십소.＜75＞

가슴타는 젊으니 웃는 얼골 그리워
뜨거운 녀름철엔 어름장사 하고 십소.

사랑하는 젊으니 웃는 얼골 그리워
꼿픠인 봄날에는 꼿장사 하고 십소.

님생각
-모스크바의 K에게-

가지잘닌 양버들엔 푸른빗예고
갈바람에 우수수수 불려서도나
넝울잘닌 포도낭구 댕그런포긴
삼동치위 다지나고 봄바람불면
푸른이삭 알롱알롱 도다올르리

머나먼 북편나라 벌판을바라
떠나가신 우리님은 어대게신고
사시장천 어름깔닌 북국이오니
가을인들 어쩌오리 봄인들오리
집차저올 기약인들 헬수잇스리.＜76＞
＜75-76＞

Missing a Smiling Face
						Chŏng Nop'ung

Missing a smiling face
Missing the smiling face of the youth warming their hands
On cold winter nights, I want to be a charcoal seller. <75>

Missing the smiling face of the youth with a burning heart
In the hot summer season, I want to be an ice seller.

Missing the smiling face of the beloved youth
On spring days when flowers bloom, I want to be a flower seller.

Thoughts of the Beloved
-To K in Moscow-

On the willow whose branches were cut, a hint of green
Though blown rustling by the autumn wind
The round stump of the grape vine whose tendrils were cut
When the chill of the three winter months has passed and the spring breeze blows
Green shoots will sprout, speckled here and there.

Looking toward the fields of the distant northern land
Where has our beloved, who departed, gone?
It is a northern country where ice spreads throughout the four seasons
How could autumn come, how could spring come?
Can the promise of returning home ever be counted upon? <76>

**Summary**

The first poem, "Missing a Smiling Face," expresses a simple, heartfelt longing to see the smile of a young person, imagining being a charcoal seller, ice seller, or flower seller just for that chance. The second poem, "Thoughts of the Beloved - To K in Moscow," contrasts the renewal of nature in spring with the uncertain fate of a loved one in distant, cold Moscow, questioning if they will ever return. From a historical perspective, the poems capture a sense of longing and separation common during the colonial era. The specific mention of "K in Moscow" in the second poem is particularly intriguing, possibly hinting at political exile, socialist connections, or studies abroad in the Soviet Union, which were significant routes for some Koreans seeking alternatives or refuge from Japanese rule.
