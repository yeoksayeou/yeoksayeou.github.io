**Body Text Translation:**
Escape of the Irish President
						Ch'unch'up'ilbu from Dublin City, Ireland

Sudden Arrest
I was ultimately arrested and have ended up here in this Dublin prison where they put state offenders. My cell is a shared room that can accommodate only about 30 people, but I hear there are about 500 Irish people imprisoned in this jail for political and social activism. Among them, the famous Ms. Skeffington, who, like me, was arrested by British authorities, is also in the women's ward of this prison. When I think of her presence beyond that high wall—pale-faced, yet never knowing despair—I realize my own mind is rather put at ease.

But listen. Upon entering the cell, I discovered a strange fact. An Irish inmate next to me, looking to be in his 40s, asked,
"Where do you live?"
So I deliberately replied,
"I live in Guangdong, China."
He asked again, "Were you also arrested for political activities?"
"No, I didn't participate directly, but I was arrested while watching what you all were doing."
"Ah, that's good then. You came at a good time."
"Why is that?"
"Our President is held in a solitary cell on the other side of this prison. Wouldn't it be something to boast about back home, having stayed in the same prison as the President?" he said, stroking his beard, whitened by many years of prison hardships.
"President? That must be a lie. Who designated him president? Isn't Ireland still under British rule?"
"Hmph, who nominated him? We, the prisoners inside, nominated him as President. He will lead us to a good place. If you think it's a lie, take a peek during exercise time."
"What's his name?"
"De Valera," he said. "He was born in this city of Dublin. He's in his thirties this year and was a mathematics teacher at Dublin University before playing a major role during the Easter Rising incident in 1916."
"And you people designated such a person as President?"
"That's right, you guessed it. It was decided by us Irish prisoners inside the prison."
he said. The name "De Valera" made me suddenly alert. His name frequently appeared in English-language newspapers worldwide, and whenever the Irish question came up, one thought of the Sinn Féin party, and with the Sinn Féin party, one thought of De Valera; his name was that famous. Spending even one night in the same prison as him was undeniably a significant connection. Calming my excited mind, I waited for the chance to be taken out for exercise or a walk. In Irish prisons, although inmates couldn't speak to each other, everyone could see each other's faces.

Now, let me briefly explain how I came to be in this prison. Please don't ask the reason why I wanted to cross over to Ireland. There is a significant purpose, but I shall remain silent about it for now. However, as I was in a position where I couldn't properly obtain a travel permit from the British government, I decided to enter illegally (密航). On the morning of the 24th of last month, I left London station, paid a large sum of money to secretly board a coal freighter at Liverpool harbor, landed at a small Irish port, disguised myself, and sought out the famous Irish female activist I had long heard of, Ms. Skeffington. While staying at her warehouse-like house on the outskirts of Dublin for about two weeks, meeting various people I needed to see, somehow the British detectives found out <56> and one morning, their cordon resulted in both Ms. Skeffington and myself being thrown into this prison. Such is the unpredictability of human destiny! When crossing the Pacific and Atlantic Oceans, how could I have imagined this body would be cast into a prison in Ireland, a remote place thousands of miles away? Now, even seeing a hawk soaring freely in the sky above makes me yearn for the mountains and rivers of my homeland and my brethren in my native land.

Strange Female Students' Folk Song
Around sunset that day, escorted by guards, I finally went out for a walk on the lawn in front of the prison buildings. At that moment, the Irish man who had told me about the President earlier pointed something out with his finger. I quickly turned my gaze in that direction and saw a middle-aged man in worn Western clothes, with a roundish face, large eyes, and a forehead broad like Napoleon's. He was merely strolling aimlessly on the lawn.
"So that's the famous De Valera. The influential leader of the Sinn Féin party since 1916, who showed elusive and unpredictable activity," I muttered inwardly without realizing it. People who are outstanding in some way tend to make a unique impact. I, too, felt a kind of electric shock upon first seeing him.

Just then, from beyond the prison's red wall, the beautiful melody of an Irish folk song (民謠) sung by young female students gently stirred the quiet space. The female students, likely a group of thirteen or fourteen, were singing from afar, yet the sound of their song was clearly audible inside the prison. With a lonely heart, I too stopped walking and carefully listened. Is it different anywhere else? Finding myself in a solitary, unfamiliar place, this quietly sung song from outside threw an indescribable sorrow (哀愁) into my heart.
However, to my ears, familiar only with English, there was no way to understand the song sung in Irish. So I asked the Irish friend beside me,
"Hey, what song is that?"
"I don't know either," he said, shaking his head.
"You don't know? Don't you know your own country's language?"
"It's Irish, alright, but it's not just ordinary words. It seems like some kind of code (暗號)."
Hearing this, I too found it strange and listened more attentively to the melody. The thought "Something is strange" was not mine alone; everyone seemed pressed by an indefinable premonition, and the eyes of the prisoners out for a walk shone strangely.

Speeding Automobiles
The next day, the peaceful Sunday church bells flowed through every alley of Dublin city. That evening, perhaps a group returning from a walk in the suburbs, about ten boisterous Irish girls, possibly drunk, came staggering up to the front of the prison, humming tunes. They then surrounded the guard who was watching the double iron gates of the prison. One of the girls said,
"Grandpa! What should I give you?" Then another girl beside her added,
"Let's give him cookies. Poor thing, grandpa can't even go flower viewing in the blossoming spring. Hey everyone, let's take this grandpa flower viewing over on that hill." Saying this, the several female students pulled at his arms and pushed his waist. Although old enough for his hair to be half-white, the old man, feeling the touch of the soft skin of the young girls who weren't drunk, eventually, despite protesting, "Hey girls, this won't do, it won't do," was playfully dragged away by the female students up the back hill.

At that moment, truly just as the guard's figure disappeared, five strange automobiles, appearing as if from nowhere, sped towards the prison gate like arrows. Simultaneously, the gate of the cell block where De Valera was held opened silently, and Mr. De Valera, in just his stocking feet, flew like a bird, crossing the prison yard in an instant and leaping onto one of the waiting automobiles outside the gate. <57>
"Hey! Big trouble! A prison break!"
Seeing this spectacle, the numerous guards finally rushed out in panic, commandeered two cars, and began pursuing the cars that sped away. However, there were five fleeing cars and only two pursuing guard cars. The five lead cars split off, one down this alley, another down that alley, all five scattering in different directions. Which car should they pursue, believing De Valera was in it? After a short while, having lost track of all five cars, the two prison cars returned empty-handed. This was the scene of the famous President's escape. It was learned later that the folk song sung by the female students on the back hill the day before was indeed a code, meaning:
"The cell key is being sent this way. Hide it, and tomorrow at sunset, when you hear the commotion of female students outside the gate, break out and escape."
It's said that the female students were all De Valera's former students or similar, who had been striving for months to rescue their teacher and finally succeeded. Word spread that De Valera subsequently crossed the Atlantic in a submarine and escaped to some country.

Success After Escape
Then, on March 23, 1921, on a roadside outside Dublin city, British authorities apprehended a man whose behavior seemed suspicious. Upon investigation, finding him highly suspicious, they threw him into Portobello prison. During further interrogation, who would have known? He was none other than De Valera himself, the one who had astonished the whole world by escaping from Dublin prison not long before. De Valera had spread rumors that he had escaped across the Atlantic, while he himself remained, disguised, actively working centered around Dublin city.
However, even Britain could not defy the force of history moving the world. The then British Prime Minister, Mr. Lloyd George, and Mr. De Valera, representing the Irish people, met for talks at the Prime Minister's residence on Downing Street, London. Ultimately, in December 1921, Ireland became a "Free State." I too, after being confined in that prison for a couple more months, was fortunately released unharmed. Forced out by a deportation order from the British authorities, I went to a small island in the Mediterranean Sea, and then returned to Dublin to write this account. Although this happened several years ago, because it was something I experienced myself, the impression remains too vivid to forget, which is why I am writing this letter here. (End) <58>

**Summary**

This article recounts the narrator's experience of being arrested in Dublin, Ireland, alongside the famous activist Ms. Skeffington, and imprisoned with Irish nationalists. While incarcerated, the narrator learns about Éamon de Valera, hailed as "President" by fellow inmates, and witnesses his daring escape from prison, orchestrated with coded messages sung by female students and a coordinated getaway. The article concludes by mentioning De Valera's later recapture and the eventual negotiations with British Prime Minister Lloyd George that led to the establishment of the Irish Free State in 1921, followed by the narrator's release and deportation. From the perspective of Korean colonial history, the article is significant for its detailed portrayal of the Irish struggle for independence, likely intended to resonate with Korean readers under Japanese rule. The narrator's assumed identity and perspective (a sympathetic observer, possibly Korean using a pen name), the focus on nationalist figures like De Valera and Skeffington, and the dramatic depiction of resistance against a colonial power (Britain) would have served as an inspirational, albeit potentially factually blended, parallel for the Korean independence movement. Historians might find the specific details of the escape narrative (even if inaccurate regarding location/timing), the portrayal of prison life for political offenders, and the use of a Korean magazine to disseminate stories of foreign anti-colonial struggles particularly interesting.

그런데 여보세요. 나는 감방에 드러오자 히구한 사실을 발견하엿슴니다. 그것은 나의 겻헤 잇는 한 40먹어 보이는 애란죄수 한 사람이
「너는 어듸 사느냐?」하고 뭇길래 일부러

「데, 벨라」라고하여 이 「따부린 시의 출생이란다. 금년은 서른몃살인데 「따부린」대학의 수학선생으로 잇다가 1916년의 부활제(復活祭)란리 때에 크게 활약한 분이라네..」

怪異한 女學生民謠
그날 석양 때즘하야 나는 끗끗내 간수의 호위를 바더가면서도 감옥들 압헤 잇는 잔디밧흐로 산보를 나왓소이다. 그럴 때에 앗가 나에게 대통령 이약이를 들려주든 그 애란사람이 손끄락으로 무엇을 가르처주더이다. 나는 창황이 시선을 그곳으로 보내니 얼골이 둥그스럼하고 눈이 크고 나파륜의 이마박가치 이마가 훨신 너른 헌양복을 걸친 장년 한 사람을 보앗슴니다. 그는 무심히 잔디밧 우를 배회할 뿐

무엇이나 빼어난 사람은 어듸인가 류달니 충격을 주는 법임넨다. 나도 그의 초면에서 일종 전긔와 가튼 충격을 밧엇소이다.

「모르다니 너이 나라말을 네가 모르느냐?」
「애란말은 애란말인데 그저 말이 아니야. 아마 무슨 암호(暗號)갓구나.」
그 말을 듯고 나도 이상하여 그 멜노듸-를 더욱 주의하여 드럿소이다. 「무슨 일인가 이상하다.」하는 생각을 가진 것은 나만이 안이고 누구든지 모다 알 수 업는 에감에 눌니운 모양으로 산보하든 죄수들의 눈동자는 이상하게도 빗을 발하더이다.

疾走하는 自動車
이 일이 잇슨 그 이튼날 大地에는 평화한 주일날 성종소리가 온- 따부린 전시의 골목골목을 흐르고 잇섯슴니다. 그날 저녁 아마 교외로 산보갓다가 도라오는 일행이겟지요. 말광양이 애란처녀 십여명이 슬취하엿는지 빗슬빗슬 몸을 탈며 콧노래도 부르면서 이 감옥압까지 왓슴니다. 그래서는 감옥의 쌍바라지 쳘문을 직히고 잇는 간수를 에워쌋슴니다. 그중에 한 게집아이가
「할아버지! 내 무엇줄가?」하면 겻헤 아히가 또
「과자를 드리지. 가엽서라 꼿피는 봄철에 할아버지는 꼿노리도 못하고 그래 우리우리 이 할아버지 대리고 저 언덕으로 꼿노리 가자꾸나.」그리면서 여러 녀학생들은 팔도 끄을고 허리도 밀치면서 나은 먹어서 머리깔조차 반백이 되엇슬망정 보들보들한 게집아희의 살결이 다이면 슬치안은 그 노인은 필경에 「이 게집애들아 이건 안된다. 안된다.」
하면서도 녀학생들에게 와-끄을니어 뒤산으로 올라갓슴니다.
그럴 때에 실로 겨우 간수의 그림자가 사라질 때에 감옥문 압헤는 어듸서 소사왓는지 괴이한 자동차 다섯대가 쏜살가치 달려왓슴니다. 그와 동시에 감옥안의 「데벨라」가 잇는 감문이 소리업시 열니자 맨 버선바닥의 「데벨라」씨가 나는 새와가치 몸을 날려 삽시간에 감옥 뜰을 지나 문전의 자동차 우에 뛰어올낫슴니다.＜57＞
「얏! 큰일낫다. 파옥도주다!」
하고 그 광경을 보든 다수한 간수들은 그제야 창황이 내다라 자동차 두 대를 논우어 타고 압서가는 그 자동차를 추격하기 시작하엿소이다. 그러나 쫏기는 자동차는 다섯대요 쫏는 간수의 자동차는 두 대라 압서가든 다섯대의 자동차가 이 골목으로 한 대갸 빠지고 저 골목으로 한 대가 빠지고 다섯대가 모다 제 각금 헤어저 버렷스니 어느 자동차에 「떼벨라」가 탓다고 추격을 하리까. 얼마 잇다가 다섯대 자동차의 종적을 모다 일은 두 대의 감옥자동차는 헛되히 도라왓슴니다. 이것이 저 유명한 대통령탈옥의 광경이엇소이다. 뒤에 아라본즉 어적게 뒤동산에서 부르든 녀학생의 민요는 확실히 암호로 그 뜻은
「감방 엘쇠는 이러케 보내니 그것을 감추어 두엇다가 래일 석양에 문밧게서 녀학생의 소란한 소리가 나거든 파옥도주하여라.」
하는 것이엇다 함니다. 그 녀학생들이 모다 「데벨라」의 제자 등으로 은사를 구하여 내기에 여러 달 로력하다가 끗내 성공한 것이라 하더이다. 데벨라는 그 뒤 잠항정을 타고 태서양을 건너 엇든 나라에 도망하엿다는 말이 펴지엇슴니다.

脫獄後의 成功
그러다가 1921년 3월 23일에 「따부린」시외의 엇던 길가에서 영국관현은 행색이 수상한 사내 한 명을 붓잡엇슴니다. 취조하여 본즉 아모래도 이상하기에 「포-도뻬로」의 감욱에 집어 너헛는데 재차의 취조에 엇지 아럿스리요 그가 전세게를 놀래이든 따부린 감옥을 얼마전에 도망한 「데벨라」 그이엇슴니다. 데벨라는 태서양을 건너 도망갓다고 풍문을 내여노코 저는 변장하고서 여전히 「따부린」시를 중심으로 활약하고 잇섯드람니다.
그러나 세게를 움지기는 력사의 힘에는 영국도 엇지할 수 업든지 때의 영국재상「로이도,쪼-지」씨와 애란민중을 대표한 「데벨라」씨는 론돈, 따부닝그, 스트릿의 수상관저에서 회젼하게 되어 끗끗내 애란은 1921년 12월에 「자유국」이 된 것이외다. 나도 그 뒤에 두어달 동안 그 감옥에 가치어 잇다가 요행 무사히 석방되어 영국관현의 추방명령으로 쫏기어서 지중해의 어느 조고만한 섬 속에 가 잇다가 다시 따부린에 와서 이 글을 적삽나이다. 비록 몃해 전 일이라 할 터이나 내 몸이 당한 일이라 그 인상이 너무도 이처지지 안어서 여기 편지하는 터이외다.(끗)＜58＞
