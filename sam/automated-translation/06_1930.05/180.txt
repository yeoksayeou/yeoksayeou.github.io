五年만의 朝鮮社會
						林元根
Korean Society After Five Years
						Im Wŏn'gŭn

Putting forward such a title might make it sound as if I had left Korea during that time and returned after wandering around somewhere overseas, but in reality, that is not the case. While I have continued to live with my feet planted firmly on Korean soil, during that period, I left the society of free people and lived a life behind bars—the life of prisoner society. My dark prison life began five years ago, on October 30th, Taishō 14 (1925), and I began to walk under the blue sky again starting January 1st of this year. Thus, the time spent inside prison can be said to have been very long, but since the time I have mingled and lived in real society is merely less than three months, whatever I might say now about the state of change in our society during that time, I believe it will inevitably be nothing more than momentary, impressionistic, and superficial observation.

What struck me first was that the content of the three newspapers managed by our people—Chosŏn, Tong-a, Chungoe—had expanded significantly in terms of quantity. Of course, to some extent, there might be aspects where the quality has also become more substantial than before, but when reading passages that unfortunately end in roundabout allusions, unable to freely write the articles they wish to write, or when seeing symbols like OO or XX mixed here and there within articles, compared to the old days five years ago, it seems difficult to say that the content has become richer in any way. However, looking at the quantity, what was barely able to publish 4 pages a day in the past has now reached the point of publishing 8 pages a day, either as morning and evening editions or just evening editions. I consider this alone to be considerable progress.

And regarding the content of the articles, seeing that pieces related to literature and criticism occupy nearly half of the daily published pages in any newspaper, I think one can glimpse the trend of literary promotion in Korean society during this period. After my release from prison, I immediately returned to my hometown and spent about a month in quietude, dedicating most of my time solely to avidly reading newspapers. Thus, sometimes, not skipping even a single novel or a line of poetry, I read excessively from page 1 to page 8, to the point where it could almost be called pathological, even straining my eyes on the advertisement sections at times. And, though it sounds funny, I sometimes muttered to myself, <47> "Wow, in the meantime, many poets and critics have emerged in Korean society. Whatever the case, it's a very good phenomenon."

At the same time, I was very pleased that the number of monthly magazines managed by our people had increased to over ten types during that period. Now is not the place to judge the quality of their content, nor do I possess any skill in critique myself, so I am simply glad that, whatever the case, even if only in quantity, the number of substantial books has increased. I think this is akin to the feeling of a thirsty person seeing a well in the distance and just jumping for joy. The magazines that did not exist in our society five years ago and that I newly encounter today, I believe, are Pyŏl Kŏn'gon (別乾坤), Nongmin (農民), Haksaeng (學生), Sin In'gan (新人間), Taejung (大衆), Kongnon (公論), Ahi Saenghwal (아히生活), Chosŏn Chubo (朝鮮週報), Taejo (大潮), Samch'ŏlli (三千里), and others.

And next, I think the social situation has become very complex during this time. To speak more frankly, today's Korean society seems to have experienced much larger political movements compared to five years ago, and the nerves of the populace seem to have become extremely sensitive. The ceaseless whirlwind of arrests (檢擧), more than anything, seems to speak most eloquently of the instability of the times. In the old days, five years ago, if a three-column article with prominent large type about some secret society (秘密結社) or some group's what-not was published in the social affairs section, one would gasp, "Gosh, what is this?" and eagerly await further reports with curiosity. However, today, it is almost no exaggeration to say that the social affairs section of nearly every day's newspaper is entirely filled with articles concerning political movements—just filled with news of the uncovering of some secret society, the arrest (被檢) of someone for something, trials (公判), and so on—so that from the perspective of a reader's psychology, which used to await special news daily, it now feels rather tedious and monotonous to some extent. Truly, even just through the newspaper pages like this, one naturally comes to know that the state of today's Korean society has become extremely rough. In any case, it can be sufficiently inferred that Korean society is constantly moving, in whatever form.

Next, it seems that Koreans' pockets have become even lighter. Of course, I know it is highly unscientific for me, lacking accurate numerical statistics, to speak only of vague estimations, but generally speaking, it seems the financial world has become extremely impoverished, and people's hearts have grown more desperate. Whenever I meet old friends I knew well, or whenever I hear stories about the household affairs of family members, I have never heard even slightly comfortable words from their mouths, and I have almost never failed to hear that money has become much tighter than before. The recent emergence of massive groups of unemployed people (失業群) pouring onto the streets seems to be almost a global phenomenon, but especially in our society, where industry is underdeveloped, the trend appears particularly severe, and it seems the governing authorities (爲政當局者) are now also discussing countermeasures for this. Of course, the problem of Koreans' livelihood difficulties (生活難) did not begin today, and it is not something to be newly reiterated now, but more than anything <48>, since eating and living is the most crucial issue, when looking at the gait of the unemployed, shifting their powerless footsteps with drooping arms, when seeing the dilapidated thatched hovels and servants' quarters (옴악사리 행랑방), when seeing the crowds of beggars groveling under the Sup'yo Bridge (水標다리), same as in the old days—passing by the throng of beggars under the Sup'yo Bridge, I unconsciously remarked, "Aighu! Those wretched fellows are still making the space under that bridge their homes." —I realized that even though much time may have passed, the economic condition of Koreans is still no different from the old days. It even seems that, in fact, it has worsened further. Whether in Seoul or the countryside, it seems there are only people crying because life is difficult, which is truly a very pitiful situation.

As mentioned just now, because the livelihood difficulties of Koreans worsen day by day, and economic issues inevitably take precedence over everything else for them, today's Koreans seem to be racking their brains about economic problems even more than those of last year or the year before. In any case, since the essence of living beings is to sustain life by eating, it seems an inevitable trend for them, who are arguably suffering economic ruin, to think more seriously about this issue—about how they can manage to eat and live. In the provinces, numerous foundations like unions (組合) or associations (會) unseen in former days have sprung up locally, and in urban areas, as many people are gathered, various consumer cooperatives (消費組合) and movements promoting domestic products (物産獎勵運動) seem to exist. Particularly, the movement promoting domestic products, which had been in a state of temporary stagnation, appears to have re-emerged with a new formation. However, reflecting on past precedents and considering the modern economic system, it is questionable how much of even a passive effect its results will yield. Nevertheless, seeing these various movements in the economic sphere, it seems that considerations regarding the economy generally occupy a much greater portion of the minds of Koreans today than before.

And one day, early in the morning, I reached the Andong intersection and saw countless students hurrying towards their school gates. The Andong intersection is quite a large square even within Seoul city, but the throng of several hundred male and female students, rushing with hurried steps, each heading towards their own school gate, completely covered that square. Of course, it was also true that many students passed through here every early morning in the past, but I do not think the number of students on an early morning five years ago could have been such an absolute majority as it was on this morning. Witnessing this scene, I was reminded of Kudanzaka (九段坂) in Tokyo in the morning. Just as it's said that even a student lazy about studies, after sleeping in late, intends to go for a morning bath but, upon seeing the stream of students pouring down Kudanzaka like a torrent, immediately becomes one of them (Continued on page 5) (From Mr. Im Wŏn'gŭn, page 47), I want to recommend to the lazy students in Seoul city to go see the early morning at the Andong intersection. No, not just students, but I thought I wanted to show that magnificent early morning scene to all our people. What does such a joyful phenomenon tell us? Of course, it means that such educational fervor and passion for learning are boiling over among our Korean society members. Furthermore, the lively strides of the male and female students, their cheerful voices, their neat attire—they seemed to cast infinite hope and new light upon those who watched them. Others may not know, but at least I myself, looking at the vigorous figures of these members of the second generation (第二 國民), thought that our path ahead is full of auspicious signs of fortune. Truly, it seemed that new lifeblood was pulsing and strength was overflowing within them. With these few words, I have written down my impressions after release from prison as they came to me. <49> <5>

**Summary**

Released after five years in prison (1925-1930), Im Wŏn'gŭn observes significant changes in Korean society. He notes the quantitative expansion of Korean-run newspapers (Chosŏn, Tong-a, Chungoe) and the proliferation of new magazines, though censorship limits qualitative depth. Society has become politically charged, marked by frequent arrests and news of "secret societies," indicating heightened social unrest under colonial rule. Economic hardship has worsened dramatically, with widespread poverty and unemployment evident both in Seoul and the countryside, leading to increased focus on economic solutions like cooperatives and domestic product promotion movements, albeit with skepticism about their effectiveness. Conversely, Im is struck by the vastly increased number of students, whose energy and enthusiasm he sees as a sign of hope and burgeoning educational fervor, representing a positive force for Korea's future. The article offers a valuable snapshot of colonial Korea in 1930, highlighting the simultaneous pressures of political repression and economic decline alongside the resilience seen in the growth of media and education. Historians might find the firsthand account of censorship's effects, the description of social anxiety stemming from political crackdowns, the stark portrayal of economic suffering contrasted with the hope placed in the younger, educated generation particularly interesting.

필자	林元根
기사형태	세태비평

五年만의 朝鮮社會
						林元根
이런 제목을 내여건다면 맛치 그 동안에 朝鮮을 떠나서 어듸매인 海外를 도라단이다가 도라온 것가치 들니지마는 그 實은 그런 것이 아니라 살아오기는 依然히 朝鮮땅에 발을 붓치고 살아오면서도 그 동안 朝鮮의 實社會 일은 바 자유인의 사회를 떠나서 鐵窓生活-囚人社會의 생활을 하여왓다는 것임니다. 내가 컴컴한 監房生活을 하게된 것은 지금으로부터 5年前 卽 大正14年 10月 30日부터이엇고 다시 푸른 하날을 머리우에 이고 단니게 된 것은 今年 1月 1日부터이외다. 이와가치 감옥안에 잇섯던 동안은 매우 길엇다고 할 수 잇스나 實社會에 석겨 산 동안이 불과 석달 내외인 까닭에 내가 이제 그동안의 우리 社會變遷相에 대하야 무어니 무어니 할지라도 그것은 오즉 一時一時의 卽咸的 皮相的 관찰에 지나지 못할 것으로 생각하는 바임니다.

나에게 제일 먼저 늣겨진 것은 우리 사람들의 경영인 3개 신문-조선 東亞 中外 등의 내용이 양적으르 보아 매우 확장된 것이엇슴니다. 물론 어느 정도까지는 質로 보아도 前日의 그것보다 충실하여 진 것이 不無하겟지마는 쓰고 십흔 기사를 마암대로 쓰지 못하고 안타가웁게도 迂廻的 暗示로 끗난 구절을 읽어볼 때에나 或은 OO 或 XX等의 符號가 이곳저곳 기사중에 석겨잇는 것을 볼 때에는 5년전 녯날에 비하야 아모리 하야도 내용이 더욱 풍부하여젓다고는 말하기 어려울 것 갓슴니다. 그러나 양으로 보아 前日에는 僅僅 1日 4面 발행밧게 못되던 것이 오날에 니르러는 或은 朝夕刊 或은 夕刊만으로 1日 8面式을 발행하게 되엿스니 그갓만으로도 저 자신으로는 만흔 진보라고 생각함니다.
그리고 기사내용에 드러 文藝와 評論에 관한 것이 어느 신문에 잇서서나 모다 매일 발행되는 紙面의 半分이나 점령하고 잇는 것을 보아 그間 朝鮮社會의 文藝促進의 추세를 窺知할 수 잇다고 생각함니다. 나는 출감 후로 즉시 고향에 도라가 한 달 동안이나 한적한 날을 보내면서 오즉 신문 耽讀에 대부분의 시간을 제공하엿섯슴니다. 그래서 엇던 때에는 소설 한아 詩1行을 빼어노치 안코 1面으로부터 8面까지 너무 甚하게 보는 때에는 거의 병적이랄만치 廣告欄에까지도 視力을 쓴 때가 잇섯든 것이외다. 그리고는 우수운 말갓지마는 나 흔자말로 『야참 그 동안에 朝鮮社會에 詩人도 만히 생겨낫고 評論家도 만히 생겨낫고나. 엇재던 매우 조흔 현상이다』라고 부른＜47＞ 적도 잇섯슴니다.
동시에 나는 그 동안 우리 사람 경영의 월간잡지가 10여종이나 늘어 난 것을 매우 깃버하엿슴니다. 지금 이 자리에서 內容如何를 論斷할 것도 아니고 또한 나 자신 論評에 대한 아모런 技能도 업는 까닭에 그저 엇잿던지 분량만이라도 다만한 책이라도 늘어진 것을 깃버하는 것이외다. 이것은 맛치 목마른 자가 머-ㄴ 곳에 잇는 우물을 바라보고 그저 깃버 날뛰는 기분과 同類일 것으로 생각함니다. 5年前 우리 사회에 업든 것으로 오날 내가 새로 대하게 된 잡지는 別乾坤 農民 學生 新人間 大衆 公論 아히生活 朝鮮週報 大潮 三千里 등이라고 생각함니다.

그리고 다음으로는 그 동안 社會相이 매우 복잡하여젓다고 생각되는 것임니다. 보다는 솔직하게 말하자면 오날의 朝鮮社會는 5년전의 그것에 비하야 정치적으로의 움지김이 매우 커젓고 민중의 신경들이 매우 예민하여진 것 갓슴니다. 쉬지 안는 檢擧의 旋風은 무엇보다도 가장 雄辯으로 時局의 불안정을 말하는 것 갓슴니다. 5년전 녯날에만 하야도 社會面 기사중에 뚜렷한 初號活字로 무슨 秘密結社이니 무슨 團의 무엇이니 하는 3段記事가 揭載되여잇스면 『에쿠 무엇인가?』하고 가삼을 놀내고는 호기심을 가지고 새로운 그 後報를 긔대렷던 것임니다. 그러나 오날에 와서는 거의 엇던 날 신문에나 社會面記事는 모도 다 풀늬틔칼 무부멘트에 관한 것으로 그저 무슨 秘密結社의 발각이나 무슨 무엇의 被檢이니 公判이니 엇저니 하는 등의 기사만으로 滿載가 되여 잇다고하야도 과언이 아닐만치 되여잇기 때문에 날마다 특별한 뉴스를 긔다리던 독자심리로는 도로혀 어느 정도까지 支離하고 單調하다는 늣김을 가지게 되는 것도 갓슴니다. 참말로 이 가치 新聞紙面만을 통하야서도 오날의 朝鮮社會相이 몹시 거치러젓다는 것을 자연히 알게 됨니다. 하여간 朝鮮社會相이 엇더한 형태로서나 늘 動하고 잇는 것만은 넉넉히 推量됨니다.
