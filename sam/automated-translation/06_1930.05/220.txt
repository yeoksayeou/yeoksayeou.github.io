A Proposal for a New Morality Between the Sexes
                        Yun Sŏngsang, Hwang Sindŏk, Na Hyesŏk, Chŏng Sŏkt'ae

New Chastity Value and New Morality
Yun Sŏngsang

Date & Time: April 6th, Morning
Meeting Place: Kyŏnji-dong, Downtown

Reporter= To establish a morality between the sexes suitable for the progressing new era, I will now ask several questions. Firstly, there is a tendency among us to greatly overestimate "chastity" (貞操), talking about "chastity," "chastity." What does the chastity of an unmarried woman mean, and what does the chastity of a married woman mean?
Yun= As you asked, morality changes with the times, and consequently, the morality between the sexes will naturally change with the times. And I believe it must change. It's such a weighty question that I don't know if my answer will be sufficient to establish a morality between the sexes suitable for the new era... But indeed, there is definitely a tendency among us to overestimate "chastity." This may be related to the deep-rooted concepts received from traditional morality, but taking a step further, isn't it perhaps because we have forgotten something more important? This might get a bit long, but various important matters compel us. Should we just stay shut in the inner rooms, adopting an attitude of 'I don't care what happens to society'? Kollontai asked, if there is an extremely pure woman who is inactive in society and a somewhat impure woman who is active in society, which one would we demand? We would naturally have to choose the latter. Chastity is undoubtedly precious. However, no matter how precious chastity is, it's problematic to think that its preservation is possible only through one's own strength. Don't we see real examples where chastity is brutally violated due to livelihood struggles? I heard that in places like Japan, the chastity of married women is considered more important than that of unmarried women, but in my opinion, I don't think there's such a different view of chastity between unmarried and married women.

Reporter= Most of Chosŏn's moral scholars (道學者) are extreme chastity supremacists. According to their words, "Lose your life, but don't lose your chastity, that's how you become a virtuous woman (烈婦貞女)." Should "chastity" be considered as important as life, or yielding a step, as important as life? For example, when a woman is caught by a villain (惡漢) and forced into sexual activity, should she accept being killed to protect her chastity? Or should she offer her chastity and preserve her life?
Yun= If a concept can even take away life, then the establishment of this concept is a crucial issue. We often see the tendency to discard precious lives like worn-out shoes due to mistaken concepts. The example you asked about is something we sometimes see in reality. Ultimately, it's the mistaken concept that took their lives. As I mentioned earlier, they are people who don't know something greater than chastity. It's as if they were born into the world solely for "chastity." If that's the case, isn't our living too meaningless...

Reporter= There is the word "purity" (순결) in the world. Does it mean physical cleanliness? Or does it also mean mental cleanliness? If it only means mental cleanliness, couldn't the word "purity" also be applied to "that woman who remarried (改嫁) after crying and separating from an unhappy marriage"?
Yun= Increasingly difficult questions are coming up. "Purity"! Well. The purity spoken of in the world varies. Often it means physical cleanliness, but didn't people like Jesus say that one can commit adultery even in one's heart? Japan's Yanagi Sōetsu defined a virgin primarily as someone who possesses the heart of a virgin, but they are all extreme idealists. When we undergo physical changes, we inevitably undergo mental changes as well. If we must use the word purity, it would refer to both physical and mental cleanliness. However, people are people. We must not forget that "absolute purity" cannot exist for us. We cannot use the word "impure" for "that woman who remarried after crying and separating from an unhappy marriage." Rather, it would be considered much purer than being crushed for a lifetime by a partner one doesn't care for, constrained by traditional morality.

Reporter= In terms of Eastern customs, physical change is taken as the mark of purity. For instance, if a married woman has sexual intercourse (性的 交通) with a man other than her specific husband in unavoidable circumstances (e.g., forced by a villain (惡漢), husband traveling for several years leading to physiological loneliness, husband having a chronic illness, or feeling insufficiency with cohabitation with the husband alone), she is immediately called an adulteress (姦通女), leading to divorce commotions. This takes just one momentary physiological fact and completely destroys the large entity called a family, which had been building its foundation for decades. Is this fact (adultery) so important? Is purity so valuable? Some men in America reportedly treat their wives' associating with other men no more seriously than if the wife had an extra drink alone or went out for an evening to watch motion pictures. This might be too extreme, but...
Yun= As I mentioned earlier, the standard of purity is not fixed and differs depending on each individual's concept. In other words, it's because we always lack a philosophy of life, meaning we fail to understand more deeply what kind of beings humans are, that we often stage unexpected tragedies of broken mirrors (破鏡, i.e., divorce). However, one important fact is that it's wrong if we focus solely on the satisfaction of sexual instincts in the union of the sexes. When thinking about oneself, one does not possess health, beauty, talent, personality, etc., all equally. Similarly, seeking the entirety of one's desires from a partner in a sexual union is ultimately unreasonable. In short, enduring physiological loneliness or sexual insufficiency while thinking of the partner is a virtue (美点) that can be found more often in humans among animals. Self-control (克己) is a precious act in humans, needless to say, and I think this concept of self-control is especially necessary sexually. In America, they say a husband associating with other women is taken so lightly, but if associating doesn't extend to sexual acts, it wouldn't be considered so serious. However, if association between opposite sexes<51> means sexual pleasure, then we must resolutely raise a banner against such decadent (頹廢) behavior.

Reporter= Looking at today's married men and women, many are physiologically adults but economically minors, leading to many cases where men and women unable to manage a household life unite = because of this, many harms and tragedies arise = wouldn't a system like in foreign countries, where marriage doesn't always mean cohabitation, be necessary? That is, not forming a household until economic independence is achieved.
Yun= Well, even if economic independence is achieved, if living separately (分居) is deemed necessary, avoiding cohabitation would be good. Moreover, it's true that there are many harms and tragedies arising from lifestyle instability. As much as possible, living separately until achieving economic independence seems necessary. Under the current system (現制度), complete economic independence is unattainable, thus the tragedy between the sexes arising from economic problems absolutely cannot be eliminated. Although our effort should be towards uniting after achieving as much independence as possible for both sexes, discussing absolute perfection ultimately becomes empty talk (空論). <52>

Marital Love, Love Suicides, Sex Education
Hwang Sindŏk

Date & Time: April 7th, 3 AM
Place: Susong-dong, Downtown

Reporter= Are "love" and "chastity" the same thing, or can they be different things depending on the circumstances? For example, if you love someone, must your chastity be dedicated only to that one person? Conversely, even if chastity couldn't be maintained due to unavoidable circumstances, could you still love only that lover?
Ms. Hwang= I believe there will be many cases where "love" and "chastity" should be treated as separate things. In other words, there can be cases where one loves but cannot maintain chastity for that person, and cases where one doesn't love but faithfully maintains chastity.

Reporter= So, does that mean love absolutely cannot be divided between two, but "chastity" can sometimes... just a little bit... be divided without issue?
Ms. Hwang= You ask in such a difficult way. First, in this world we live in, aren't there so many such examples? Look—let's say a virtuous woman is raped. At that time, her body was defiled, but would her love for her husband have changed? No. That cannot be. How can we limit this only to unavoidable cases like rape? It's common in our country too, but when a husband (지아비) is away for many years, or in various other unavoidable circumstances, aren't we seeing how often it becomes impossible to maintain physical fidelity due to physiological or environmental factors? Originally, love and chastity should be the same thing, but as treated in Kollontai's novels, as society becomes more complex, we must realize beforehand that there are many cases where they become different things.<52>

Reporter= So, when faced with a situation like the rape mentioned earlier, should one just endure it for a moment instead of taking the path of sacrificing one's life like in the old days?
Ms. Hwang= Of course. The world exists to be lived in. When faced with the choice between losing one's life and losing one's chastity, taking the path to preserve one's life first is naturally the right thing to do.

Reporter= Then let me give an example. Suppose the miraculous event where Yi Toryŏng (리도령) suddenly appeared as a secret royal inspector on the day of the old governor's birthday feast for Ch'unhyang (춘향), who was imprisoned, never happened. In order to meet Yi Toryŏng even once in her lifetime, wouldn't it have been right for Ch'unhyang to temporarily serve the old governor? This is assuming the old governor would have definitely killed Ch'unhyang that day if she refused...
Ms. Hwang= That's a different issue. If Ch'unhyang's love for Yi Toryŏng was absolute, then for Ch'unhyang herself, living or dying would no longer have been the issue. On this point, I've heard people criticize Yun Simdŏk this way and that, but I think such matters are not for third parties to judge. Actually, I was quite close to the late Yun Simdŏk from our time in Tokyo, and I, at least, can understand the psychology that led her to choose the path of death. Therefore, while love and chastity being the same is the principle, it shouldn't be immediately attacked as immoral if they aren't. Also, in situations of 'love or death,' there might be times when "chastity" moving pragmatically (功利的) is good, and times when it's unpleasant.

Reporter= In any case, shouldn't committing "love suicide" (情死) like Yun Simdŏk be rejected?
Ms. Hwang= Why reject it? Perhaps from a societal moral perspective, one might blindly reject it, but when considering human nature, I believe there are many cases that cannot be judged uniformly. (Remainder omitted in original)

Reporter= Also, isn't it necessary to establish decent social institutions (사교긔관) to help young men and women form free unions? For example, something like a marriage brokerage (결혼중개소)?
Ms. Hwang= I believe it would be necessary.

Reporter= However, even looking at today's new women (新女姓) who graduated from girls' high schools (고등녀학교), many do not seek divorce even when they clearly realize their marriage was a mistake. Isn't it better for schools providing sex education (性敎育) to teach not only the physiological aspects but also broadly about such moral aspects? In this respect, shouldn't modern school education be seen as a failure?
Ms. Hwang= There's no question of failure or not. Have we ever educated girls with our own hands? It's all been entrusted to others. In Western women's schools, they were entrusted to Western hands, and otherwise, all entrusted to others' hands for education. Wouldn't what they know be Western ways or the morals and knowledge of other countries? Let's talk about that after we have educated them with our own hands according to what we believe is right. Also, the suggestion to provide sex education not just physiologically but also broadly in the moral sphere is very good, but to do that, more fundamental ideological issues would need to be taught. And there's a reason why new women don't readily agree to divorce. It's the economic relationship. As long as all economic power lies with men, this demand from women is justified, meaning men who want a divorce must first absolutely take responsibility for the woman's living expenses, I believe.

Reporter= Lastly, aren't there cases where marital love feels tiresome?
Ms. Hwang= Sometimes, yes. Often, tragedies like divorce occur at such times. Therefore, it would be good for adult couples to strive to extinguish that boredom by taking trips together every few years or living separately for a time. <53>

Companionate Marriage, Trial Marriage
Na Hyesŏk

Date & Time: April 2nd, 5 PM
Place: Met in Kyŏngsŏng Insa-dong

Reporter= Is the purpose of our marriage for a man to get a wife, or for a woman to get a husband (지아비), or is it to get sons and daughters who will continue our bloodline?
Ms. Na= Well, it would be to get a husband or a wife. Children are merely by-products, I believe.

Reporter= Then, should "sexual desire" and "reproduction" be considered entirely different things?
Ms. Na= They can't be called entirely different, but they aren't things that can be confused either.

Reporter= Then, if the main purpose of marriage is indeed to get that spouse, if it turns out the marriage was a mistake, shouldn't divorce naturally follow?
Ms. Na= That should be the case. However, divorce isn't that easy, so to find out whether the marriage will truly be happy or not, something called trial marriage (試驗結婚) is being advocated in Europe (구라파) recently, I hear.

Reporter= Living together for 3-4 years, separating if disliked, and staying together for life (偕老同穴) if liked?
Ms. Na= That's right.

Reporter= Would such a marriage style be suitable for Chosŏn?
Ms. Na= Aren't some new couples taking the lead already implementing it? It seems that way.

Reporter= What are the characteristics of trial marriage?
Ms. Na= Since it's already a trial, neither party bears absolute obligation regarding the outcome. Simply put, even if they divorce, issues like alimony (慰藉料) or violation of chastity (貞操蹂躪) wouldn't arise. A marriage based on mutual agreement reserves the right to divorce from the beginning, which is good.

Reporter= It would be good to teach such new morals to the many female students today. When it comes to sex education, educators seem to know only how to teach the physiological aspects but not the new paths in thought and morality. This seems to be a major failing (通弊) of modern times.
Ms. Na= I agree. In matters between the sexes, scientifically teaching the physiological aspects is good, but it's even more necessary to provide enlightenment on moral and ideological aspects, such as birth control (산아제한) or what trial marriage is. Educators' efforts should rightly be focused there.

Reporter= So, that trial marriage, which necessitates methods like birth control, would be a way to prevent frequent divorces and would have the effect of making the union and separation of men and women much freer?
Ms. Na= That could be said.

**Summary**

This 1930 roundtable discussion from the magazine Samch'ŏlli features four Korean intellectuals (Yun Sŏngsang, Hwang Sindŏk, Na Hyesŏk, Chŏng Sŏkt'ae) debating the need for a "new morality between the sexes" in Korea. They challenge traditional, rigid views on chastity (貞操), arguing it's overvalued, especially when compared to life itself or societal contribution. The participants discuss the separation of love and chastity, the justification for preserving life over chastity in extreme situations, the limitations of traditional marriage, the potential benefits of trial marriage (試驗結婚) and companionate marriage (友愛結婚), the failure of contemporary sex education to address moral and ideological issues, and the necessity of birth control (산아제한), with Dr. Chŏng providing explicit (though partially censored in the text) advice on contraceptive methods including withdrawal and post-coital actions, as well as recommending a German product. The discussion reflects a significant engagement with modern Western ideas about relationships, sexuality, and female autonomy, set against the backdrop of Korean tradition and colonial modernity.

For historians, this article is significant as it showcases progressive Korean intellectual discourse during the Japanese colonial period, particularly concerning gender roles, marriage, and sexuality. It highlights the challenges to Confucian patriarchal norms, the influence of Western thought (mentioning Kollontai, Yanagi Sōetsu, American examples, European trial marriage), and the articulation of distinctly modern concerns like economic independence for women, the critique of marriage solely for procreation, and the open discussion of contraception and abortion. The frankness about sexual matters, including specific contraceptive techniques and references to surveys on sexual behavior in America (despite the censorship denoted by 'X'), is particularly striking for a publication from this era in Korea. It reveals anxieties about modernity alongside aspirations for social change, especially regarding women's lives and choices.
