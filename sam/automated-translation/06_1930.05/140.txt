The Poisoning Beauty Kim Chŏngp'il Stands in Court
						By Oryuch'on Kŏsa

The trial of the husband-poisoning beauty Kim Chŏngp'il (金貞弼) was opened on October 10th, Taishō 15 [1926], from 10:30 AM in Courtroom No. 7 of the courthouse in Chongno, Sŏul. As a young woman born in Myŏngch'ŏn, Hambuk province, who had carved her name into the hearts of many, Kim Chŏngp'il's public trial caused a strange sensation in the world. Kim Chŏngp'il, age 20, who blossomed beautifully like an azalea flower in the beautiful mountains and streams of the Pukkwan region, appeared haggard that day, perhaps exhausted from prison life. However, inasmuch as she drew the curiosity of the capital's (長安) people calling out "Beauty! Beauty!", her face, long like a willow leaf, possessed a rare beauty. Her nose bridge stood sharp as if carved, her lips were red and thick, as often seen in passionate (情熱的) women, and her voice, completely without affectation, was enough to touch the hearts of all people – she could be called a woman among a hundred, a woman among a thousand (百人之女 千人之女). At that time, as a reporter for the G Newspaper Company (G新聞社), I sat in the seat right next to Kim Chŏngp'il, busy taking down the words flowing from her lips, but that too was already 7 or 8 years ago. However, my impression of Kim Chŏngp'il still remains clear.

Leaving aside for now the fundamental question (問題) of whether Kim Chŏngp'il truly poisoned her husband (本夫) or not as an eternal mystery (千古의 疑問), let's briefly record the general course of the poisoning incident (事件). Kim Chŏngp'il's beauty (美貌) was famous even in her village (同里). Thus, she married a man from that village named Kim Hoch'ŏl (金浩哲), but for some reason, their marital bond (夫婦의 誼) was not good. Then, not many months later, her husband became severely ill (몹시 若閟하면서) and died. The Myŏngch'ŏn Police Substation (明川駐在所), finding suspicious points in the cause of death (死因), had doctor Ch'oe Sŭngnyŏl (崔承烈) perform an autopsy (解剖). The result, quite unexpectedly, determined (判明) it was poisoning by ingestion of yellow phosphorus (黃燐). If it was poisoning, suspicion (嫌疑) inevitably fell upon the wife, Kim Chŏngp'il, about whom unpleasant rumors (風聞) (namely, that she had a lover (姦夫)) had already been circulating. At the police station and courthouse, Kim Chŏngp'il completely denied the poisoning allegation, stating, "From the fourth day after our marriage (結婚), my husband's <40> private parts (局部) discharged pus, and he was bedridden, so there were no matters concerning sexual duties (性職上). It's just that there were so many rats in my in-laws' house (屍家) that I had bought and kept yellow phosphorus (黃燐)." Thus, after repeated investigations, she ultimately received a death sentence (死刑 宣告) at the Ch'ŏngjin courthouse and had now come to Sŏul for the appeal trial (覆審公判). At the time, from what I saw, Kim Chŏngp'il appeared to be a woman whose face did not show the cruel heart that seemed capable of killing someone. While people with a murderous nature (好殺性) usually have characteristics (特徵) like a strange murderous glint (殺氣) in their eyes or a nose too pointed as if to invite disaster (禍), Kim Chŏngp'il only gave the impression of being a good, beautiful, and therefore pitiful woman (女性). If you call this physiognomy (骨相學) as seen by a newspaper reporter, perhaps you'll laugh it off as unbelievable, but being a newspaper reporter, I have seen many felons (凶徒) sentenced to death in court, and it was a fact that, compared to others, she did not give such an impression.
The crowd of over 2,000 gathered inside (廷內) and outside (廷外) the court that day, upon seeing her face, probably did not, like me, see her as a woman possessing a murderous nature.

Kim Chŏngp'il, the famous poisoning beauty bearing an eternal question (千古의 疑問), is likely still sitting in Sŏdaemun Women's Prison (西大門 女監獄), whether on snowy days or windy days, shedding tears over past memories (追憶). Although her life sentence (終身刑) has likely received several pardons (赦), turning her into a long-term prisoner (長期囚), when she sees the light of day (天日) again, will she emerge still possessing her former beauty? Or has her hair already turned white like a scallion root from worry and sighs, and her face become covered in wrinkles, turning her into a haggard old woman (老婆)...? <41>

**Summary**

This 1930 article reflects on the 1926 trial of Kim Chŏngp'il, a young woman famed for her beauty, who was convicted of poisoning her husband in Myŏngch'ŏn. The author, a journalist who covered the trial, recalls the public sensation surrounding the case and his personal impression of Kim Chŏngp'il. He describes her striking appearance but notes the absence of any facial features suggesting a "murderous nature," contrasting her with other criminals he'd seen. Despite her death sentence (later commuted to life, potentially reduced further), the author leaves her guilt as an "eternal question" and wonders about her fate and appearance after years in Sŏdaemun Women's Prison. For historians, the article offers insight into public fascination with crime and female figures in colonial Korea, the contemporary application of subjective judgments like physiognomy even by journalists, and provides details about the colonial legal process (autopsy, trials, appeals, prison system) surrounding a sensational case.
