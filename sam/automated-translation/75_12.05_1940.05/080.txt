Meeting of "Military Instructors"
Amidst the manifold affairs of the military season (軍의 事 多端한 秋), our company (本社) held this mobile roundtable discussion, interviewing gentlemen currently serving as military instructors at professional schools and middle schools, directly training students, to discuss various issues concerning military education, hoping it may offer some assistance to the military education and training of the youth of Korea (朝鮮) and to volunteer soldier training.
(Professional School Side - 2 participants)
Pak Ch'angha (朴昌夏), First Lieutenant, Army Cavalry, Military Instructor, Yonhui Professional School (延禧專門學校)
Cho Ch'ŏrho (趙喆鎬), Second Lieutenant, Army Infantry, Military Instructor, Bosung Professional School (普成專門學校)
(Middle School Side - 2 participants)
Fujikawa Sadao (藤川定雄), Warrant Officer, Army Infantry, Military Instructor, Jungang Middle School (中央中學校)
Takemoto Hisanosuke (竹本久之助), Warrant Officer, Army Infantry, Military Instructor, Hwimun Middle School (徽文中學校)<62>

Stories from the Tokyo Military Academy Days

We would be grateful if you could share interesting stories, times you got into trouble, moments that moved you, hardships faced during your military academy days, and mention any classmates, whether Japanese, Korean, or Chinese, who have become successful. Are there any successful classmates regardless of being Japanese, Korean, or Chinese?
Cho Ch'ŏrho - Not a classmate, but two or three years ahead of me, Chiang Kai-shek (蔣介石) graduated from the Tokyo Military Academy I attended. Besides him, among my classmates were about 70 Chinese exchange students; they now hold key positions in Chinese regiments or divisions, while it seems only I haven't achieved success. Among the Japanese, I don't recall anyone particularly notable. Among Koreans, there are Colonel Hong Saik (洪思翊), currently at the Koa-in (興亞院), Kim Sŏkwŏn (金錫源) who went to the mainland, and also Yi Ŭngjun (李應俊) and Pak Ch'angha (朴昌夏).
As for whether there are any romantic stories in military life, well, I did get into trouble once. Among our teachers, there was one with a strangely shaped head. His head looked like two blocks of fermented soybean paste (메주덩이) stuck together, protruding front and back, exactly like the mess tins (「니오모이」) used in the barracks, so we nicknamed him "Niomoi." Then as now, students greatly enjoyed nicknaming teachers, and I guess I wasn't an exception. One day, that teacher walked past us on the school grounds, and while teasing him quietly with some Japanese students, saying "There goes Niomoi," it unfortunately reached his ears. It seems he already knew "Niomoi" was his nickname. He caught me, reprimanded me sternly, and I was punished with three days of confinement (禁足罰). That was one time I got into trouble.
I entered the military academy at 18 and graduated in Taishō 3 (1914), at age 22. At that time, there were 43 Korean students. Initially, Koreans were not taught in the same classroom as Japanese students but studied in a separate room. Also, the shoulder insignia for Japanese was red, while for Korean students it was pink, but about a year or two after I enrolled, that system was abolished entirely. Chinese students also studied separately in a different room back then. This must have been around Meiji 42 or 43 (1909-1910). A moving experience from my student days was during a race (競走) at the military academy where one company of 150 men ran from Tokyo to Narashino (習志野), about 70-80 *ri* (approx. 27-31 km) away.<63> My breath grew short, my legs ached, and most students dropped out midway. Only 20 people endured to the end and reached the destination. The fact that I was one of them, having persisted till the end, still moves me when I think about it now. Running 70-80 *ri* was truly challenging. I took pride in my health and youthful vigor back then.
As for hardships, I experienced them during the grand army maneuvers (陸軍大演習). At that time, I was with the 29th Regiment in Sendai (仙台), and it was a large-scale forced march advancing from Yonezawa (米澤) all the way to Hiroshima (廣島). We crossed treacherous mountain passes repeatedly, fought fierce mock battles (惡戰苦鬪) with the mock enemy (假敵軍), and advanced. My feet were completely blistered and torn, ripped to shreds, reaching a point where I could barely take a single step (寸步). But I had to keep marching. The hardship then is truly beyond description. They say even in actual combat, such forced marches are rare. On top of that, after bivouacking (野營) for over ten days, when marching the next day, we would fall asleep while walking. Seeing that, I realized human physiological responses are quite strange. We were walking, yet dozing off, nodding along. In other words, one is asleep but still moving forward.
Fujikawa - I didn't attend the military academy; I enlisted as a volunteer soldier (現役志願兵) in the Hiroshima Regiment in Taishō 3 (1914). I was 19 then. Since it was direct military life, not student life, I spent every day in disciplined routine, so there weren't many particularly interesting events. That's not to say there were no romantic stories or stories involving women, but as I am currently teaching middle school students, I think it best to avoid them for the sake of the cadets.
A moving experience was during the military review (觀兵式) for Emperor Taishō's enthronement (御卽位) in Taishō 4 (1915). Two or three representatives from our regiment were sent to Tokyo, and being chosen as one of them to attend in Tokyo was an unparalleled honor and deeply moving for me. I came to Korea (朝鮮) in March of Taishō 5 (1916) and was assigned to the 20th Division. As for participating in actual combat, I was dispatched during the Siberian Intervention (西伯利亞出兵) in Taishō 7 (1918), also during the Jinan Incident (濟南事變), and during the recent Incident, I was at Zhanggufeng (長鼓峯) for four months. You ask if there were romances with Russian women in Siberia, or various interesting or difficult experiences? I didn't reach the front lines then; I was a soldier guarding the railway lines, so I had no contact with foreign women<64> and no experience of actual combat. During the Jinan Incident too, I didn't reach the actual site but moved around Lüshun (旅順), Fengtian (奉天), Tieling (鐵嶺), etc., engaged in garrison duty. During the recent Zhanggufeng battle, by the time I arrived at the front, the ceasefire had already taken place, so I don't have any particular experiences to share.
Among my comrades who achieved success, there are no Koreans or Chinese, but among the Japanese, there is Major Hirata (平田少佐), who died honorably in North China during the current China Incident.
Takemoto - I also did not attend the military academy but was promoted from being an enlisted soldier (現役兵). I was dispatched to Beijing (北京) during Sun Wen's (孫文氏) revolution in Meiji 45 (1912), and I also went to Fengtian (奉天) during the battle between Zhang Zuolin (張作霖) and Guo Songling (郭松齡). However, I will refrain from discussing those events here. Since I experienced military service rather than student life, it was always a disciplined life following the same routine, and there wasn't much special amusement in it. Therefore, I don't have any particularly interesting stories. However, a pleasant thing was when the soldiers I trained in the barracks consistently showed disciplined and good performance; that was quite gratifying. Also, I most enjoyed marksmanship training, practiced diligently, and when the 79th Division was established, I went there and spent 11 long years in honorary shooting (名譽射擊 - now there's something called flag shooting (旗射擊), but honorary shooting no longer exists), so though it sounds like boasting (自矜), I am confident in my shooting skills. I won the honorary shooting competition six times, which was truly delightful. Another pleasant thing is that military service is constant hardship for training, so rest periods are exceptionally pleasant.
Among friends from the same barracks who became successful is Colonel Mikuriya Masayuki (御廚正幸), who died an honorable death in Shanghai (上海) during the current China Incident. He was a graduate of the Army University (陸軍大學), had been to Europe (歐羅巴), and was a very promising figure. Among Koreans, there is Mr. Kim Ŭngjun (金應俊 - likely Yi Ŭngjun mentioned earlier by Cho?), and no Chinese.
Did I participate in the current China Incident? No, I couldn't participate in this incident. I did participate in the Jinan Incident, but like Mr. Fujikawa, I didn't reach the front lines and moved around Lüshun, Fengtian, Tieling, etc., with Mr. Fujikawa, engaged in garrison duty.
Pak Ch'angha - I entered the Kyŏngsŏng Samch'ŏngdong Military Academy (京城三淸洞武官學校) at age ten, and at thirteen, I entered the Army Cadet School (陸軍幼年學校). To enter, one needed to be over 4 *ch'ŏk* 8 *ch'on* (approx. 145 cm) tall, but I was originally short, and being young, my height was<65> less than 4 *ch'ŏk* 8 *ch'on*. When they said I couldn't enter, I was so mortified that I visited the office of Captain Nishida Tsuji (西田辻大尉), who was an advisor and instructor at the military academy then (he was a nobleman), and pleaded while crying profusely.
"If I had failed the academic subjects, I could understand, but I passed all the academic exams with good scores. Being rejected simply because I'm short is truly unbearable. Please let me enter. Even if I'm short, if you just let me in, I'll keep up with everything others do."
As I kept crying and insisting, the instructor was moved and, disregarding the rules, granted me an unprecedented admission. So I got in, but as you can see even now, I am short and have a weak constitution, completely lacking the qualifications of a soldier. Since the uniforms were all ready-made (既成服), there was no way a uniform would fit a short and thin body like mine. All the uniforms were too big, and wearing them, I looked truly ridiculous, like a cat in a raincoat (고양이 우장). The uniform jacket came down above my knees, looking like a short overcoat, the sleeves were long, so I had to roll them up several times, and furthermore, the collar (「에리」) was so wide my chin (택 - likely 턱/chin) would sink right into it. Looking like this, how could I go anywhere? The military academy had a leave day once a week, and while my friends jumped for joy and went out to the streets or their homes, I was too embarrassed by my comical and laughable appearance to go out. We had to wear the uniform.
Also, my physique was always frail, making it extremely difficult to cope with military life, which I managed only barely. Consequently, my life was always one of hardship and suffering. However, I never abandoned my initial resolution and persevered to the end, which is how I came to possess such robust health today.
I entered the main course at the Tokyo Military Academy around age 16 or 17, became a cavalry second lieutenant at 21, and a cavalry first lieutenant in Taishō 8 (1919). After graduating from the military academy, I was in the 23rd Regiment, attached to the 3rd Brigade in Morioka (盛岡). People might think being in the cavalry is very easy because you just ride horses, but it involves considerable hardship. If a horse walks all day or walks a lot, you have to massage its legs with both hands until the muscles relax. You must always keep the hooves clean and apply hoof oil (蹄油) to prevent laminitis (蹄葉炎). Additionally, numerous other tasks require attention, such as stable conditions,<66> saddlebags (旅襄), metal fittings (金具), bits (자갈), etc., which is quite laborious. However, I know such hardships are indispensable for tempering body and mind. During maneuver drills or grand army maneuvers, the hardship and fatigue are truly indescribable. Due to lack of sleep from bivouacking, it's common to bump one's head against telephone poles while marching the next day. But I believe such hardship is a flavor (滋味) and joy of being a soldier. Among my successful classmates, there was a Japanese aide-de-camp to the Emperor (侍從武官) who visited Korea some years ago, but I can't recall his name right now. Among Koreans are Colonel Hong Saik, Lieutenant Colonel Kim Sŏkwŏn, who returned from China this time and is now in the XX Regiment on the mainland, and Lieutenant Colonel Yi Ŭngjun, who is in the XX Regimental District.

Training Policy (Military)

What are your personal training policies for how you wish to militarily discipline (訓育) Korean students? Also, please tell us about the physical differences between Japanese and Korean students, or the superior and inferior/weak points (劣弱点) of Korean students' physiques.
Fujikawa - I have established my personal training policy as follows. While it is natural to uphold the Imperial Will (御聖旨) and base it on the school principal's training policy, personally, I aim to conduct military education under the spirit of making strong, upright, cheerful, and excellent subjects of the Imperial Nation (皇國臣民) who will contribute to society.
Regarding the physical differences between Japanese and Korean students, I have previously been at a Japanese middle school, and this is my first time coming to a Korean middle school; moreover, it has only been five days since I arrived, so I cannot offer a critical assessment yet.
Takemoto - My training policy primarily focuses on spiritual discipline (精神上訓育). The key core of my training policy is to cultivate discipline, responsibility, a sense of duty, and ultimately, to perfect them as human beings (人格).
Regarding the physical differences between Korean and Japanese cadets, the health of Korean cadets decidedly surpasses and is superior to that of Japanese cadets.
Pak Ch'angha - My training policy is, of course (無論), to cultivate a sound spirit in a sound body to create excellent citizens. A strong physique alone is insufficient, and a sound spirit alone<67> is also insufficient. My effort is solely focused on making them individuals of self-annihilation for the public good (滅私奉公) for society and the state, with both body and spirit sound.
Regarding the physical superiority or inferiority between Japanese and Korean students, looking from an impartial standpoint, I believe Korean students are better. During the entrance examination for our Yonhui Professional School this time, almost all (擧皆) examinees could lift a 6-*kwan* (approx. 22.5 kg) barbell (扛擧) about 10 times, showing astonishing health.
Cho Ch'ŏrho - From a straightforward perspective, I also see that the physique of Korean students is far superior to that of Japanese students. My training policy is also not particularly different; following the maxim "a sound mind in a sound body" (건전한 정신은 건전한 육체에), I wish to temper sound minds and bodies. Thus, I want to nurture spirited and brave youths (意氣勇兒) who contribute to society and the state, becoming a light for the construction of Greater East Asia (大亞細亞建設).

Volunteer Soldiers and Military Education

The Volunteer Soldier System (志願兵制度) recently implemented in Korea is showing very good results. This system is seen as very beneficial for the training of Korean youth (朝鮮靑年訓練) and is expected to greatly aid the state. Please share your impressions of this system, your observations from visiting volunteer soldier training centers, and its relationship with military education.
Pak Ch'angha - I am one of the councilors (評議員) for the volunteer soldier training center, and I view this volunteer soldier system very positively. Whether for tempering mind and body, creating sound citizens, or otherwise, I see it as an excellent guiding institution (指導機關) for Korean youth, who often lack experience in group living and disciplined life. Regarding its connection to military education, since the volunteer training centers are currently providing Imperial military education, there isn't much specific to say about the relationship.
Cho Ch'ŏrho - I absolutely approve and greatly welcome the implementation of the volunteer soldier system. I actually think its implementation was rather late. I wish all Korean youths could be trained at least once. For our Korean youth, who are very deficient in group living, disciplined life, sense of responsibility, sense of duty, etc. (등등이 매우 박약한), I want Imperial military training to be provided to create excellent and sound young men. If possible, I hope for the implementation of compulsory military service (義務兵役), not just volunteer service. Thus, I sincerely hope (懇望하여 마지 않습니다) that Korean youth will also fulfill their responsibilities and duties for society and the state and become excellent young people. Regarding military education as well,<68> I believe the Empire's military education is the best in the world, and not just military training, but even police training (순사 훈련), I believe the Empire's training is the best.
Takemoto - It's regrettable that I haven't yet visited a volunteer soldier training center, but from what I've heard, I was truly astonished and moved that in just six months of training, they lead lives as splendid as those of mainland soldiers in the military, perhaps even better, certainly not worse. Furthermore, that with only six months of military education, they not only show such exceptionally excellent results but also achieve great outcomes without falling short even when deployed to the front lines, is truly worthy of admiration (찬탄을 不禁하는 바입니다).
In my opinion, since Koreans have good physiques, providing them with the Empire's military education will surely make them excellent citizens, and I hope this system becomes widely adopted (보급되기를 희망합니다).
Fujikawa - I visited a volunteer soldier training center and was truly surprised. Regardless of whether they become soldiers in the future or not, I approve of undergoing this military training. I believe that by receiving this military training, even if they don't become soldiers later, they will bring much benefit to society. Raising people of passion (熱) and strength (힘) along with a burning spirit is truly a joyous task. We must indeed become people of passion and strength under a burning spirit.
As you may have seen if you visited, the first thing that impresses you at the volunteer soldier training center is the latrine (변소). The latrine is so impeccably clean that one could sit there reading or relaxing. I have never before seen such a clean latrine, so well-scrubbed and maintained, in any military unit either on the mainland or in Korea. This alone shows how faithfully those volunteer soldiers adhere to group living, disciplined life, and their responsibilities and duties.

Instructions and Critiques Regarding Foreign Youth/Student Training

Please share your knowledge about the training of foreign youth and students that you have seen or read about, and provide your critique. What aspects are good, and what are bad? Please enumerate their superior and inferior points.<69>
Takemoto - I haven't researched foreign military education extensively, but there are aspects, even if foreign, that I would like to emulate. In Germany (獨逸), youth undergo half a year of labor education (勤勞敎育), and everyone must receive this education regardless of social status or rank. I forget his name, but there was a magnate (巨富) in Germany who owned a prestigious company. His son went to study in America (亞米利加). A few years later, when the son reached the age and time for labor education, his mother, without informing her husband, wrote to her son, ordering him: "Though school education is undoubtedly good, suspend (中止) it, return home immediately, undergo the half-year labor training, and then go back to America (米國) to study." Upon receiving this letter, the son promptly returned home. As the son of a wealthy and high-status family, he personally underwent the very difficult labor service training and then went back to America. This kind of national spirit (國民精神) and youth training education is something worth learning from.
Pak Ch'angha - I haven't particularly studied foreign military education, but I believe every country has at least one aspect worth emulating. And the Japanese Empire (日本帝國) is, in fact, a latecomer compared to Europe and America (歐米), which are advanced nations; that is true. Therefore, the Empire has comprehensively reviewed (總覽) the military education of various countries, adopted the best aspects, and added its own original elements. Thus, I believe the Empire's military education is the best.
Fujikawa - I haven't researched foreign military education, nor have I read much about it in books, so I am a complete blank slate (白紙) on that topic.
Cho Ch'ŏrho - I revere the Yamato Spirit (大和精神) and believe nothing in the world can match it. However, this doesn't mean I completely disregard foreign military education. Germany has its Germanic spirit, Italy (伊太利) has its Italian spirit, and Britain (英國) has its British spirit; I believe each possesses its own characteristics. Among these, I think good aspects are worth emulating. Alongside the Yamato Spirit, I particularly admired the "Sokol" movement (「쇼콜」운동), which once held great influence in Europe and caused significant ripples in general society. Movements like Sokol are among the most outstanding examples of foreign youth training education, and I would like to instill the spirit of this movement, along with the Yamato Spirit, in Korean youth. When I was previously involved with the Korean Boy Scouts (朝鮮뽀이스카우트 - 少年斥候隊), headquartered in Keijō (京城), I greatly admired this "Sokol" movement. (End)<70>

**Summary**

This 1940 roundtable discussion features four military instructors (two Korean, two Japanese) discussing their experiences and views on military training, particularly concerning Korean youth in colonial Korea. They share anecdotes from their own military academy days (Tokyo Military Academy), compare the physiques of Korean and Japanese students (generally favoring Koreans), outline their personal training philosophies (emphasizing discipline, spirit, loyalty to the Emperor, and becoming useful subjects), and express strong support for the recently implemented Volunteer Soldier System in Korea, seeing it as crucial for instilling discipline and national spirit in Korean youth, with some hoping for eventual compulsory service. They briefly touch upon foreign youth training models, praising German labor service and the Sokol movement, while ultimately asserting the superiority of Japan's system, which incorporates the best foreign elements with the unique Yamato Spirit.

The article is historically significant as it provides a direct window into the perspectives of military personnel involved in implementing Japanese colonial assimilation policies through military education in schools just before the Pacific War intensified. It reveals the emphasis on physical and spiritual discipline aligned with Japanese imperial ideology ("subjects of the Imperial Nation," "Yamato Spirit," "Imperial Will"), the praise for the Volunteer Soldier System as a tool for shaping Koreans, and the casual acceptance of ethnic hierarchy (e.g., initial segregation, different insignia) and derogatory terms ("Chinain"). The instructors' comments, particularly the Korean officers' endorsement of the system and Japanese superiority, offer valuable insights for historians studying colonial collaboration, militarization of education, and the complex identities of Koreans serving within the Japanese imperial structure.

기사형태	대담·좌담

「軍事敎官」회의
軍의 事 多端한 秋에 際하여 본사에서는 朝鮮 청년제군의 군사교육훈련을 위해서와, 지원병 훈련에 다소의 도음이 될까하여 특히 현재 전문 학교와 중등 학교에서 군사교관으로 계셔서 학생제군을 직접 훈련하고 있는 諸氏를 찾아 군사교육에 대한 제문제를 토의하는 이동좌담회를 開하다.
(전문학교 측 2인)
延禧專門學校 軍事敎官 陸軍騎兵中尉 朴昌夏
普成專門學校 軍事敎官 陸軍步兵少尉 趙喆鎬
(중학교 측 2인)
中央中學校 軍事敎官 陸軍步兵准尉 藤川定雄
徽文中學校 軍事敎官 陸軍步兵准尉 竹本久之助<62>

東京士官學校 時代 이야기

사관학교 시절에 재미있던 이야기시든지 혹은 혼나시던 이야기와 또는 감격했던 일과 고생스러웠던 이야기, 그리고 동창생으로 출세한 사람은 누구누구신지 말씀해 주시면 고맙겠습니다. 내지인이나 朝鮮人이나 支那人을 불문하고 출세한 동창생이 없으신지요?
趙喆鎬-동급생은 아니지만 나보다 2년인가 3년을 앞서서 蔣介石이가 내가 통학하던 東京 육군사관을 졸업했으며, 그 밖에도 나와 동창으로 支那人 유학생이 70여명이 있었으나 그들은 지금 다 支那연대나 사단의 요직으로 있는데 나만 출세 못한 셈이지요. 내지인으로는 별로 기억나는 이가 없고, 朝鮮人으로는 현재 興亞院에 있는 洪思翊 대좌와 내지에 가 있는 金錫源군, 그밖에 李應俊군, 朴昌夏군들입니다.
그리고 군대 생활에 무슨 로맨스같은 이야기가 있겠음니까마는 한번 혼난 일은 있지요. 우리를 가르치던 선생 중에 머리가 怪常하게 생긴 선생이 있었는데, 그 머리가 어떻게 생겼는고 하니 메주덩이 두 개를 붙여 놓은 것처럼 앞뒤로 돌출해서 똑 병영에서 쓰는 「니오모이」와 같이 생겼기에 「니오모이」라고 별명을 지었지요. 그때나 지금이나 학생들은 선생 별명 짓기를 대단히 즐기는 성질이 있어서 역시 나도 그 축에 빠지지 않은 모양인데, 하루는 교정에서 그 선생이 우리 앞으로 지나가기에 내지인 학생들과 함께 저 「니오모이」하고 가만히 놀린다는 것이 그만 그 선생의 귀에 들어가서 들켰지요. 그 선생도 그 「니오모이」가 자기 별명인 줄 미리부터 안 모양이여서 나를 잡아다 놓고 단단히 책망한 뒤에 3일간 금족벌을 당해서 혼나본 일이 있죠.
내가 사관학교에 입학하기는 18세時요, 졸업하기는 大正 3년, 즉 22세時였습니다. 그 때 朝鮮人 학생은 43명이였고, 처음에는 朝鮮 사람은 내지인 학생과 한 교실에서 공부를 시키지 않고 별실에서 공부했으며, 견장도 내지인은 붉은 색이었으나 朝鮮人 학생은 분홍색이였던 것이 내가 입학한지 1년인가 2년 후에 그 제도가 전부 폐지되고 말았지요. 그때 支那人 학생도 역시 별실에서 따로 공부했었지요. 그것이 明治 42,3년경의 일인가 봅니다. 학생 시절에 감격했던 일로는 사관학교에서 一中隊 150명이 東京에서 7,80리되는<63> ナラシノ(習志野)까지 구부로(競走)를 하는데 숨이 차고, 다리가 아파서 중도에서 학생들이 거의 다 떨어지고 끝까지 참아가며 목적지까지 달려간 사람이 20명만이 였습니다. 그런데 그 중에 제가 한사람으로 끝까지 따라간 것이 지금 생각해도 감격해요. 7,80리를 닫게 되니 실로 벅차지요. 그 시절의 내 건강과 젊음의 혈조가 어지간했다는 것을 자부했지요.
그리고 고생해 보기는 육군 대연습때인데, 그때 내가 仙台 29연대에 있을 때며, 米澤에서 廣島까지 뻐쳐서 진격하는 대규모의 강행군이였습니다. 그때 험악한 준령을 넘고 또 넘어서 假敵軍과 惡戰苦鬪하며 진격하는데 발은 왼퉁 부르틀 대로 부르트고, 터질 대로 터져서 산산히 찢어저 寸步를 전혀 옮겨 놓을 수 없는 최후 경지에까지 다달았으나 행진하지 않을 수는 없고, 참 그때의 고생은 이루 형용할 수가 없습니다. 실전에도 이런 강행진은 별로 없다고 하드군요. 게다가 십여 일씩 야영까지 하고 나니 그 이튿날 행진할 때에는 걸으면서 잠을 자게 돼요. 그때 보니 사람의 생리적 작용이란 참 이상하드군요. 길을 가기는 가는데 그냥 끄덕끄덕 조을고 가요. 이를테면 자기는 자는데 가기는 간단 말이지요.
藤川-저는 사관학교에는 다녀보지 못했고, 大正 3년에 현역 지원병으로 廣島 연대에 입대했습니다. 그때 제 나이 19세였으며, 학생 생활이 아니고 직접 군대 생활이니만치 매일 규율적 생활을 보내게 되므로 이렇다 할 재미있는 일은 별로 없지요. 그렇다고 로맨스같은 이야기가 전혀 없는 것도 아니고, 따라서 여자의 이야기도 없는 바는 아니지만 현재 제가 중학생의 교편을 잡고 있느니만치 생도들을 위해서 기피하는 것이 좋을까합니다.

訓育方針(軍事的)

여러분 개인으로서는 朝鮮人 학생을 어떻게 군사 훈육을 시키고 싶으시다는 훈육 방침과 또 내지인 학생과 朝鮮人 학생과의 체질상 차이라든지 혹은 朝鮮人 학생의 체질상 우수점과 劣弱点을 말씀해 주십시오.
藤川-저는 제 개인으로서의 훈육 방침을 이렇게 세웠습니다. 御聖旨를 바뜰고 따라서 학교 교장의 훈육 방침을 기조로 할 것은 물론이지만, 제 개인으로서는 강하게, 바르게, 명랑하게, 그리고 훌륭하고 사회에 공로가 있을 황국신민을 만들려는 정신 밑에서 군사 교육을 시키려고 합니다.
그리고 내지인 학생과 朝鮮人 학생과의 체질상 차이에 있어서는 제가 이때까지 내지인 중학교에 있었고, 朝鮮人 중학교에 들어와 보기는 금번이 처음이며, 따라서 들어온지 5일밖에 되지 않으니 이렇다고 비판해 말할 수는 없군요.

志願兵과 軍事 敎育

금번 朝鮮에 志願兵制度가 실시되여 매우 좋은 성적을 나타내고 있으며, 이 제도는 朝鮮청년훈련 上 매우 좋다고 보며, 따라서 국가에도 많은 도음이 되리라고 보는데, 이 제도에 대한 귀하의 감상과 및 지원병 훈련소를 참관하신 소감, 기타 군사 교육과의 관계 등을 말씀해 주십시오.
朴昌夏-저는 지원병 훈련소 평의원 중의 한사람인데 이 지원병 제도를 매우 좋다고 봅니다. 심신을 단련하기 위해서나, 또는 건전한 국민을 만들기 위해서나, 그 밖에 단체 생활이나 규율 생활에 결함이 많은 朝鮮 청년에게 훌륭한 지도 기관이라고 봅니다. 군사 교육과의 관계에 있어서는 현재 지원병 훈련소에서 제국의 군사 교육을 시키고 있으니까 별로 그 관계를  말할 것이 없겠지요.
趙喆鎬-저는 지원병 제도가 실시된 것을 절대 찬성이요 대환영입니다. 이 제도의 실시가 오히려 늦었다고 저는 생각합니다. 朝鮮 청년을 모조리 한번씩 훈련시켰으면 하고 원합니다. 단체 생활의 결함이 많고, 규율적 생활, 책임감, 의무감 등등이 매우 박약한 우리 朝鮮 청년에게 제국 군사 훈련을 시켜서 훌륭하고 건전한 청년들을 만들고저 원합니다. 될 수만 있으면 지원병이 아니고 의무병역이 실시되기를 희망하는 바입니다. 그래서 朝鮮 청년들도 사회나 국가를 위해서 책임과 의무를 다하고 훌륭한 청년이 되어지기를 懇望하여 마지 않습니다. 군사 교육에 있어서도 제국의 군사 교육이<68> 세계에서 제일이라고 나는 생각하며, 군사 훈련만이 아니라 순사 훈련에 있어서도 제국의 훈련이 제일이라고 저는 믿습니다.
竹本-저는 지원병 훈련소를 아직 참관하지 못한 것이 유감이나 내가 알아본 바에 의하면 불과 6개월간의 훈련에 내지 군인의 군대 생활에지지 않게 훌륭한 생활을 보내며, 오히려 낳으면 나았지 못하지는 않다는 것에는 실로 경탄과 감격했습니다. 그리고 6개월간의 군사 교육에 그와 같이 비상히 훌륭한 성적을 나타날 뿐 아니라 전지에 나가서도 조금도 떨어지지 않는 큰 성과를 나타내니 참 찬탄을 不禁하는 바입니다.
내 생각에는 朝鮮 사람은 체질도 좋고 하니 거기에 제국의 군사 교육을 시켜 놓으면 훌륭한 국민이 되리라고 자신하며 이 제도가 널리 보급되기를 희망합니다.
藤川-저는 지원병 훈련소에 가서 참관했었는데 참 놀랐어요. 장차 군인이 되든지 아니 되든지를 불문하고 이 군사적 훈련을 거치는 것을 저는 찬성하며 이 군사적 훈련을 받으므로 말미암아 장래 군인이 되지 않을지라도 사회에 많은 유익을 끼치게 되리라고 생각합니다. 불타는 정신과 함께 열과 힘의 사람을 길러내는 일은 참으로 기쁜 일입니다. 우리는 모름지기 불타는 정신 밑에서 熱의 사람이 돼야 할 것이오 힘의 사람이 돼야 할 것입니다.
여러분도 가보셨겠지만 지원병 훈련소에 가면 爲先 경탄하게 되는 것은 변소입니다. 변소가 어떻게도 깨끗한지 거기에 앉아서 글을 읽고 놀아도 좋다고 보았습니다. 나는 아직 內地나 朝鮮 내에 있는 군대에서 그렇게 깨끗하게 소제하고 걷우는 청결한 변소는 처음 보았습니다. 이것만 보아도 그들 지원병들이 얼마나 단체 생활과 규율 생활과 및 책임 의무에 충성을 다한다는 것을 알 수가 있을 것입니다.

外國 靑年學生 訓練에 대한 指示과 批判

귀하가 보고 읽으신 외국 청년학생 훈련에 대한 지식과 및 그것에 비판을 가해 주십시오. 좋은 것은 어떤 점이 좋으며, 나쁜 점은 어떤 점이 나쁜지 그 우수한 점과 열등한 점을 열거해 주시기를 바랍니다.<69>
竹本-저는 외국 군사 교육에 대해서 많이 연구해 보지는 못했으나 이러한 점은 외국 것이라도 모방하고 싶습니다. 獨逸에서는 청년들에게 반년간 근로 교육을 시키는데 그것에는 신분, 지위 상하를 불문하고 다 그 교육을 받아야 합니다. 제가 그 이름은 잊었으나 獨逸에 훌륭한 회사를 가진 거부가 있었는데 그의 아들이 亞米利加에 가서 유학하게 됐어요. 그런데 몇 해 후에 그 아들되는 청년이 근로 교육을 받을 시기와 연령이 되여서 이것을 안 그 어머니가 자기 남편에게도 알리지 않고 아들에게 편지하기를 「無論 학교의 교육도 좋지만 中止하고 본국으로 어서 돌아와서 반개년간 근로 훈련을 받은 후에 다시 米國에 가서 공부하라」고 명했대요. 그 아들은 이 편지를 받고 곧 본국에 돌아와서 지위도 있고 돈도 많은 부자의 자식으로써 대단히 어려운 근로 봉사의 훈련을 친히 받고 다시 米國으로 갔다는 이야기가 있는데 이러한 국민 정신과 청년 훈련 교육을 본받을 점이라고 생각합니다.
朴昌夏-저는 별로 외국 군사 교육을 연구는 못했으나 각국마다 한가지씩은 모방할 점이 있다고 봅니다. 그리고 日本 제국은 사실에 있어서 歐米보다 후진국이오 歐米가 선진국인 것은 사실입니다. 그러기 때문에 제국은 각국의 군사 교육을 전부 總覽하고 가장 좋은 점을 취한데다가, 독창적인 것까지 가해 놓았으니 제국의 군사 교육이 나는 제일이라고 생각합니다.
藤川-저는 외국 군사 교육에 관해서 연구도 못해 봤고, 또 서적으로도 별로 읽지 못해서 거기에 대해서는 통 백지외다.
趙喆鎬-저는 大和 정신이라는 것을 숭배하고 세계에서 그 정신을 따를 것이 없다고 봅니다. 그렇다고 외국의 군사 교육을 전혀 무시하느냐 하면 그렇지는 않습니다. 獨逸이면 獨逸적인 그 무슨 정신이 있고, 伊太利면 伊太利적인 그 무슨 정신이 있고, 英國이면 英國적인 그 무슨 정신이 있어서 각각 그 특징을 가지고 있다고 봅니다. 그 중에서 좋은 것이면 모방해도 좋다고 보며 저는 특히 大和 정신과 함께 歐羅巴에서 큰 세력으로 한때 일반 사회에 큰 파문을 던지고 있던 「쇼콜」운동이라는 것을 숭배했습니다. 「쇼콜」운동 같은 것은 외국 청년 훈련 교육 중에서 가장 우수한 것이여서 大和 정신과 함께 이 운동의 정신도 朝鮮 청년에게 고취하고 싶습니다. 저는 예전 京城에 본부를 둔 朝鮮뽀이스카우트(少年斥候隊)에 관계했을 때에도 이 「쇼콜」운동을 대단히 숭배했습니다.
