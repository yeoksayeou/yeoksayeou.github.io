Magazine Title: 삼천리 제12권 제5호
Publication Date: 1940-05-01
Article Title: 戱曲 犧牲(全二幕) (Play: Sacrifice (Complete Two Acts))
Author: 姜瑛熙 (Kang Yŏnghŭi)
Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

Play: Sacrifice (Complete Two Acts)
Kang Yŏnghŭi

Time: Modern day
Place: A small town
Characters:
Yŏnghun (University student)
Ch'anghun (His younger brother)
Father (Yŏnghun's father, a township clerk)
Mrs. Yi (Yŏnghun's mother)
Grandmother (An old woman over sixty)
Uncle (Yŏnghun's paternal uncle, a merchant)
Taesŏp (Yŏnghun's friend, a literary youth)
The events begin at the home of Yŏnghun, a middle-class family in the countryside.

Act 1

Stage
Downstage left are the kitchen and the main inner room (anpang). Upstage right is the men's outer room (sarang). The sliding doors <328> of the sarang are wide open, revealing a room decorated quite nicely. In front of the sliding doors is a narrow wooden porch (t'oenmaru). Between the inner room and the outer room – that is, center stage – is a large wooden-floored hall (maru). The paper-covered lattice doors (paraejimun) of the large hall are wide open, showing mulberry trees in the backyard and flowering plants beneath the wall.
On the maru are a cupboard and a large earthenware jar (tuji), and on the hanging shelf (silgŏng) are dining tables (sang), etc. To the left is the main gate for entry and exit, and to the right is a path leading to the backyard. Beside the path stands a persimmon tree.

An early summer morning.

As the curtain rises, the Mother is clearing the breakfast table, and the Grandmother is sitting on the maru, smoking tobacco. The Father is in the sarang, dressed in a khaki-colored, close-collared Western suit (tsumeeri). Yŏnghun, in a white shirt (waishyassŭ), sits on the t'oenmaru, flipping through a newspaper. Father watches Yŏnghun intently as he looks through the paper...

Father: (Buttoning his suit) Why is it that when you read the newspaper, you don't look at the politics section, but only read the useless arts and culture section and novels?
Yŏnghun: (Smiling faintly) Well, it depends on one's interests. Why don't you read the arts and culture section or novels, Father? ...It's the same for me as it is for you.
Father: A son over twenty speaking so carelessly in front of his father...
Grandmother: He says he's over twenty, but what does a child who grew up foolishly know? (Smiles faintly)
Father: (Coming out onto the maru) Are all children who grow up foolishly like this? It's because he doesn't want to become a proper person. -Pause- (To Yŏnghun) Come here a moment.
Grandmother: He'll gradually mature now...
△Yŏnghun scratches his head, crosses to the large maru, and kneels silently before his father.
Father: (Lighting a cigarette and pulling out his watch) There are only about 30 minutes left now, so let's hear your answer from last night. ...So, what have you decided?
Yŏnghun: (Head bowed, silent)
Father: Why don't you answer? ...Above all, you must listen to your father's words.
Grandmother: If you don't listen to your father, whose words will you listen to? Even if parents tell you to die, though you might not actually be able to die, you should at least pretend to.
Yŏnghun: (Slightly distressed) Father! I really must live according to my own individuality (個性).
Father: Hmph, you people are quick to talk about individuality or ideals, but that's all said after listening to your parents. It's not that I don't know what you're saying. I'm someone who reads the newspaper every day and understands... I'm not unaware of the words that fellow Taesŏp is always <329> muttering about, like feudal ideology or convention.
Yŏnghun: Then why can't you understand me?
Father: You look down on your father because I'm just a township clerk (myŏnsŏgi), don't you? Hahaha... It's not that I don't understand your feelings right now. I know well your desire to pursue literature in this era (時代). But how can you pursue literature without eating?
Yŏnghun: I know that well too.
Father: What do you know... You think you know everything, but in this father's eyes, you still have a long way to go.
Yŏnghun: In parents' eyes, children always seem young.
Father: No. Listen to me. -Of course, as a father, it would be nice for everyone if I could let my child do what he wants. But because we Korean people are poor – you might not know yet. Looking at our house from the outside, everyone says we're rich (富者), but if you look closely, there's nothing. That's why I, over forty, am still doing the job of a township clerk. Wouldn't I have become a township head (myŏnjang) if things were just a little better? So, give up everything, and since you're already enrolled in the law department anyway, prepare for the Higher Civil Service Examination (高文). -Pause- If you can't do it, you can just have your younger brother or your own children do it, can't you?
Yŏnghun: Can't Ch'anghun be the one to become a high official (高等官)?
Father: He is who he is, and you are you. You are my eldest son. So you must listen to me. ...If only your grandfather hadn't passed away so early, I would undoubtedly be a judge by now. But because your grandfather passed away early, was there anyone in the house? So I stopped studying, came straight down from Seoul, took care of your grandmother, and have lived like this until today. ...It's a father's wish to have his son achieve the high official position I couldn't.
Yŏnghun: Still, how can I prepare for the Higher Civil Service Examination when my heart isn't in it?
Father: (Raising his voice) So, even after explaining it clearly until now, you still don't understand?
Yŏnghun: (At a loss, silent)
Father: (Gently) You are weak-willed. So, if you just listen well to your father, there will be no trouble in the future either.
Yŏnghun: But...
Father: (Standing up abruptly) What do you mean, 'but'? No matter how immature a child is, there should be some sense of propriety. Is the sight of your father, approaching fifty, working as a township clerk, so pleasant to see?
Yŏnghun: So what can be done?
Father: I'm truly dumbfounded. With a child like that, what can become of him? <330> -Pause- And yet, believing he's my child, the family struggles, sending tuition fees every month, sending medicine...
Grandmother: (Coming down to the lower level) Listen carefully to your father's words. Now I see you're following that Taesŏp fellow's example...
(She steps down into the yard and goes towards the backyard.)
Mrs. Yi: (Wiping her hands on her apron, coming out of the kitchen) Why are you nagging the child like that when he hasn't even been home for many days?
Father: What does a woman know to interfere? This woman has spoiled the child completely.
Mrs. Yi: They say if things go well, it's one's own merit, and if they go badly, it's the ancestors' fault. Why bring me into it at the end? (Enters the inner room)
Father: What did you say? How can the household function like this?
Yŏnghun: Why are you doing this? What did Mother do wrong? -Pause- (Weakly) I will think about it carefully.
Father: I don't want to hear any more. Do as you please.
Yŏnghun: I haven't thought about this matter just once or twice either. When I first went over to Tokyo, I hesitated and hesitated, enrolling in the law department which wasn't in my heart. But I have never studied law. I intended to show results greater than the high official position, Father's long-cherished desire, through literature. But then...
Father: Stop it. I know everything you're trying to say. There's no use talking further, so if you consider your father your father, it's not too late now, prepare for the Higher Civil Service Examination. -Pause- No high official has come from this village yet. So if you just become a high official, imagine. -Pause- (Pulls out his watch) Now! There's no need to wait for an answer anymore, so I'm going in. (Steps down to the lower level)
At this moment, Ch'anghun comes out from the backyard, carrying weeds he has cut in a basket (taraekki). The Grandmother follows him.
Father: Why are you carrying weeds you've cut?
Ch'anghun: To give to the school pig.
Father: You only do school work, this rascal. You don't do any housework at all.
Ch'anghun: Hey, the teacher told me to...
Father: So, did the teacher tell you to only do school work and not work when you go home?
Ch'anghun: Oh, Father, listen properly. The teacher told me to gather weeds for the pig.
Grandmother: Then from today, please gather some weeds for our pig.
Ch'anghun: (Sticking out his tongue) Pfft! (Puts the weeds down in the yard, goes up onto the maru, gets his book bundle, and comes down)
Grandmother: Ah! Look at that rascal, who is he looking at...
Yŏnghun: (Silent, smiles) <331>
Ch'anghun: Father, the kids going to middle school, even during vacation, they tell us to come to school and study. ...Give me just ten chŏn.
Father: What for?
Ch'anghun: The teacher told us to bring money for exam papers.
Father: Are exam papers ten chŏn?
Ch'anghun: Yes!
Grandmother: Goodness, that schooling costs a lot of money too.
Ch'anghun: (Receiving the money, looking at Grandmother) Tch! My older brother's school costs several times more than ours...
Grandmother: Well, that's because it's a higher school...
Yŏnghun: (To Ch'anghun) How do you know?
Ch'anghun: Last month, on Grandfather's errand, I went to the post office and sent money, so I know well.
Father: This rascal, you only notice things like that.
Ch'anghun: Oh, Father, ...give me five more chŏn. I don't have a single pencil.
Father: You use pencils well too. Just study that much.
(Gives the money and heads towards the main gate)
Ch'anghun: Father, your hat.
Father: Ah, I forgot.
Ch'anghun: Eh- hehehe...
Yŏnghun: (Brings the hat to Father)
Grandmother: Have a good day.
Ch'anghun slings the basket over his shoulder, tucks the book bundle under his arm, and exits with Father.
-Exit-
Yŏnghun goes into the sarang, takes a cigarette and a book, and sits at the edge of the maru.
-The sun shines down.-
The cool sound of cicadas is heard.
Grandmother goes up onto the maru, takes her tobacco pipe, and sits down next to Yŏnghun.
Grandmother: Reading a book again? Since you're home, rest a bit. Tell your granny some stories about Japan (日本)...
Yŏnghun: (Lighting a cigarette) What is there to talk about?
Grandmother: You say Japan is even bigger than Seoul, don't you have any stories to tell your granny? -Pause- Then, shall I tell you one? Listen.
Yŏnghun: (Turning a page) Grandmother's stories are always about getting married, what else?
△Mrs. Yi comes out of the inner room with sewing materials, smiling faintly.
Grandmother: Hey, don't you want to get married? (Pointing to Mrs. Yi) Look at your mother. She smiles like that whenever marriage talk comes up.
Mrs. Yi: Oh, Mother...
Grandmother: Hey, about that matter. Scholar Yu's granddaughter from across the water. She graduated from elementary school last spring and is at home, isn't she? You probably know her well too. <332>
Yŏnghun: No, who is Scholar Yu?
Mrs. Yi: Why, the big tiled-roof house across the water.
Grandmother: Truly, isn't that family's tradition so good? In the old days, what connection would our families have for marriage? At best, our family were just local functionaries (ajŏn). Times have changed, and since you attend university, their side approached us first.
Yŏnghun: (Silent. Bitter smile)
Grandmother: So your father immediately checked the marital harmony (kunghap), and it turns out she's an older match for you. An eighteen-year-old bride is exactly your match. (Looking at Mrs. Yi) Child's mother, isn't that right?
Mrs. Yi: Yes!
Yŏnghun: (Silent. Smiles faintly)
Grandmother: This fellow is smiling because we're talking about marriage! (As if talking to herself) Kids these days don't feel embarrassed even when talking about marriage. (To Yŏnghun) Didn't marriage talk start for your father when he was twelve? He was so utterly embarrassed that he couldn't eat breakfast or dinner with the family for several days.
Mrs. Yi: People these days mostly get married after twenty, so what's there to be embarrassed about when they know everything?
Yŏnghun: A person has to walk that path once in their life, what's there to be embarrassed about?
Grandmother: Oh, look at this! ...So you seem eager to get married right away. -Pause- These days, people prefer 'New Women' (新女性) who wear pointy shoes like the female teacher at the school behind the house, but that's no good. First of all, they must know their parents.
Yŏnghun: Do New Women not know their parents?
Mrs. Yi: How could a child born from parents' bodies not know their parents? But can they sew clothes, can they cook rice? Looking at the teacher behind the house, yesterday she was grilling meat while looking at a book! So didn't the meat get completely burnt black... After trying and trying, she couldn't do it, her face turned red, and she asked me to grill some for her!
Grandmother: New Women only know how to preen themselves excessively; can they even properly make the clothes they wear?
Yŏnghun: Perhaps there are some people like that. But is everyone like that? Only the uneducated ones are like that.
Grandmother: Hey, don't say that. In your eyes, they might all look like fairies (仙女), but in the eyes of us old folks, they don't look like much more than dog dung. -Pause- It's all useless. Isn't it enough if they just finish elementary school, know how to write and read letters? Staying at home, serving their parents-in-law well, and serving their husband well, what more could one ask for? And... producing chestnut-like sons-.
Yŏnghun: What's the use of having babies dangling one after another <333> when there's nothing to eat?
Mrs. Yi: Hey, they say everyone is born with enough to eat.
Grandmother: (Gently) Hey, Yŏnghun! You've seen Scholar Yu's granddaughter, right? -Pause- Why, the girl who was in the second grade when you graduated from elementary school, the one with the round face and dark complexion. Now her face has blossomed like a gourd flower. And her hair is long and flowing like three bundles of hemp... Hey, girls these days, even if they just go to elementary school, cut their hair short and blunt like a sparrow that lost its tail feathers.
Mrs. Yi: They make the 1st and 2nd graders get bobbed hair, they say.
Grandmother: Living for a long time, one sees all sorts of strange sights, really.
Yŏnghun: Is cutting hair that bad? In my opinion, it seems light and good.
Grandmother: That's what you all say. -Pause- Hey Yŏnghun! How about Scholar Yu's granddaughter? Does she appeal to you? If you like her, they say they'll hold the wedding even next spring. ...The reason your father hasn't said anything to you yet is probably because you haven't been home many days, and also because of the talk about the Higher Civil Service Examination, like the old state examination (kwagŏ), rather than marriage talk. As time passes, your father will surely tell you everything in detail. -Pause- Times have changed, indeed... they say they have to discuss it with you.
Yŏnghun: How would I know a girl I saw when I was young? And I don't want to get married yet. I want to graduate from school, earn the money for marriage, and then get married. I feel sorry enough that my parents are putting me through school, but marriage too...
Grandmother: It's a parent's duty to educate their children and see them married. ...How do you young people know so much?
Yŏnghun: And even if I were to get married, how can I marry without knowing her personality or anything? I should at least see her face...
Mrs. Yi: Their side says it's fine for the parties involved to meet and see each other, son.
Grandmother: What is this child saying? Even if their side says that, we can't do that for the sake of appearances. (Pointing to Yŏnghun) What if he sees that family's precious young lady and happens not to like her? ...Putting that family aside, first, think about the young lady's future. A person must have propriety and consideration...
Yŏnghun: (Flopping backward onto his back) Oh, I don't know.
Grandmother: If you don't want to marry into that family, then that's that. As for me, I don't have much longer to live, so I wanted to see a granddaughter-in-law, and is it easy to find such a good match? I just mentioned it because there was such a place. (Stepping down to the lower level) I know exactly what's on your mind. Try bringing a 'New Woman' or <334> whatever into our house. It will just become a chaotic mess...
Yŏnghun: (Sitting up) Grandmother, is eighteen really the perfect match?
(A look of contemplation)
Grandmother: (Looking intently at Yŏnghun) -Pause- This fellow seems to be involved in something like romance (戀愛). Kids these days are just... (Clicks her tongue)
Yŏnghun: (Smiling, mimicking her) Grandmother, how do you know about romance?
Grandmother: Why, you think I wouldn't know? (Steps down into the yard and exits through the gate) ...A woman knowing too much is also not good.
-Grandmother exits-
-Pause-
Mrs. Yi: I don't know. You're a man over twenty now, aren't you old enough to know what needs to be known? How can a marriage, which happens only once in a lifetime, be decided in a day or two? In the past, children were young, so parents decided as they pleased... Hey Yŏnghun, think carefully and do what's best so that neither you nor the family has complaints.
Yŏnghun: Every time I see you doing the kitchen work alone, Mother, I think I should get married quickly too. But...
Mrs. Yi: (Getting up and going towards the inner room) I'm fine. Isn't it my fate to be doing this late into my life after coming to your house?
Yŏnghun: (Silent, a look of deep thought)
-Silence-
Grandmother: (Voice) I was just on my way to your house.
Uncle: (Voice) Oh, I've been so busy lately, I couldn't even visit Mother, and I heard Yŏnghun is here too, -Pause- Mother, I went to Seoul the other day and just arrived this morning.
Yŏnghun hears the voices and steps down into the yard.
Grandmother: (Voice) I had no idea. Even living in the same town, people are so busy with nothing...
Uncle: (Entering through the gate) It's my fault. The day I left, something suddenly came up, so I couldn't even visit my elder brother...
-Uncle, Grandmother enter-
Uncle wears a turumagi (overcoat) and a fedora, but looks rather shabby.
Yŏnghun: (Silent, bows to Uncle)
Uncle: So, have you been well, and studying hard? Why does your health seem no better than last year? (As if to himself) When one is away from home, health must come first.
Grandmother: Absolutely, health is the most important.
Mrs. Yi: (Coming out of the inner room, at the edge of the maru) Oh my, why haven't we seen you at all?
Uncle: Yes, hahaha, well, being busy here and there, it naturally <335> turned out that way.
Yŏnghun: Yesterday, when I visited my younger aunt, I heard you had gone to Seoul. When did you arrive?
Uncle: Hmm, I just came via my house. I also just heard at home that you had arrived. -These days, with the economic police around, there isn't a single rice merchant in this town, except me, who hasn't been slapped. So, something urgent came up, and I just returned from a trip to Seoul.
Grandmother: You people travel to Seoul as if it were your main house. As for me, I've lived past sixty, but what is Seoul? I haven't even ridden a train...
Uncle: (Stepping up onto the lower level) Hahaha, I'll take you there sometime. (Takes out something wrapped in paper from his vest pocket and gives it to Mrs. Yi) These are pencils for Ch'anghun. I wanted to buy something, but what was there to buy? Everything was just expensive... So I bought a few packs of needles.
Mrs. Yi: (Accepting them) Oh my goodness, buying needles when you don't have money... (To Yŏnghun) You knew your uncle went to Seoul, why didn't you say a word about it when you came home?
Yŏnghun: (Sitting on the t'oenmaru) I thought everyone at home already knew.
Uncle: (Sitting on the maru) Oh, about that. I came down from Seoul on the same train with Taesŏp today. ...Gosh, that person is like Hong Kiltong (洪吉童), flashing in the west (西), flashing in the east (東), I really don't understand him. Talking about literature or art, gosh, one should stay put in one place...
Yŏnghun: (Silent)
Uncle: If that person had just attended school properly, he might not be in a high official's chair by now, but he probably would have been at least a county clerk (kun sŏgi). Needlessly getting involved in ideology or whatever, getting expelled midway, coming home, and just playing around until this day, who would like that...
Grandmother: Hey, Officer O still frequently visits that house these days.
Uncle: As long as Taesŏp is alive, it will probably continue like that. Truly, if one understands, how could one do such things? Just one mistake, and he becomes unable to live like a proper person... (To Yŏnghun) By the way, Yŏnghun! It seems you two correspond with each other. Taesŏp said you should be home by now. So he'll probably come looking for you soon. (Gently) Don't associate closely with people like that. You never know what might happen in a person's life.
Yŏnghun: (Silent, bitter smile)
Uncle: Why are you laughing, you rascal? So, really, how much have you prepared for the Higher Civil Service Examination? Since you are weak, don't study too excessively. <336>
Yŏnghun: The Higher Civil Service Examination?
Uncle: Yes. Are you confident? In the old days, wouldn't it be an immediate pass? ...Every time I use the abacus, I deeply regret not having studied. If I were just your age now... just...
Yŏnghun: (Weakly) I'll do it gradually.
Uncle: Gradually? In a world where countless people fail even after studying for ten years, what kind of talk is that... Look here. From now on, you must take charge of this house. Can your elder brother work as a township clerk forever? And look again. Isn't Ch'anghun going to middle school next year? You think our family is doing well because we send you money every month. In reality, if you look closely, it's an unspeakable state of poverty ("pinbosang" - 貧乏相, appearance of poverty). Do you know how much debt I have now, supposedly doing business? ...The outside may look bright, but the inside is pitch black, that's the world, and also our family.
Grandmother: You just have to study and pass the exam right away, that way there's a reward for studying, isn't there?
Yŏnghun: (Silent)
Uncle: Well! I have to go to the grain inspection office again. Gosh, why am I so busy, yet not a single penny is made. Only debts keep increasing like taffy in June...
Grandmother: Since I'm already out, I should stop by your house too.
-Grandmother, Uncle exit-
-Pause-
Yŏnghun: Phew! Being placed in the middle and grilled like this, how can one live!
Mrs. Yi: Who's grilling you? It's because you can't control your own feelings. -Pause- The elders' words are all correct, so understand that.
Yŏnghun: Oh, stop it. (Flops down onto the t'oenmaru)
-Silence-
Sound of a dog barking. Outside the gate-
Taesŏp: (Voice) Yŏnghun! (Voice slightly louder) Is Yŏnghun there?-
Yŏnghun: (Sitting up) Is that Taesŏp?
Taesŏp: (Entering through the gate) Oh... you're here.
-Taesŏp enters-
Taesŏp wears a turumagi and is hatless. He wears Lloyd glasses, and his hair is unkempt.
Mrs. Yi: Oh, you've come! Why act like a stranger? Come right in instead of calling from outside...
Taesŏp: (Smiling faintly) How are you?
Yŏnghun: (Stepping down into the yard) Have you been well?
They shake hands.
Taesŏp: Well, I'm just at home, so I'm doing fine like this, but <337> you (touching the back of Yŏnghun's hand)... this hand still hasn't gained any flesh.
They hold hands, silently smiling sadly at each other for a while.
Mrs. Yi sees this scene and goes into the inner room.
Yŏnghun: Come up. (Goes and sits on the large maru)
Taesŏp: (Sitting on the edge of the maru) How are things in Tokyo (東京) these days? ...Let's start with the mundane questions first.
Yŏnghun: (Offering a cigarette) What's there to talk about Tokyo? It's obvious. Like a squirrel spinning its wheel, it's the same day in and day out. By the way, I just heard from my uncle, you went to Seoul and came back?
Taesŏp: (Lighting a cigarette) I'm in trouble because I can't make up my mind right now. Honestly, I go back and forth to Seoul frequently out of boredom, but it's not like I have ambitions in the literary world. Just because I go often, I guess you could say I know the literary gossip well.
Yŏnghun: How can you say you can't make up your mind? Where else would there be someone with a will as strong as an iron lump like yours?
-Pause- So, how are things in the literary scene these days?
Taesŏp: The literary scene? ...It's probably better to read even a pulp magazine (三文雜誌) than to hear it from my mouth. But what? An iron-lump-like will (意志)? Hmph, doesn't an iron lump melt when heated in fire?
Yŏnghun: It melts, sure, but doesn't the iron remain as iron?
Taesŏp: It remains, sure, but what use is iron that has lost its soundness?
Mrs. Yi comes out from the inner room carrying peaches on a tray.
Mrs. Yi: Here! Have some of these. ...Gosh, I really don't understand what you two are talking about. (Laughs)
Taesŏp: Oh my, where did these peaches come from?
Yŏnghun: If Mother understood what we talk about, her fate would be sealed.
Mrs. Yi: What fate do children have? Really...
The three laugh. They eat peaches.
Mrs. Yi puts laundry into a basin and steps down into the yard.
Mrs. Yi: (To Taesŏp) You met your friend after a long time, so stay and play, and have lunch before you go. Okay? (Tucks the basin under her arm, to Yŏnghun) If Grandmother comes, tell her I went to the front stream to do laundry, okay?
Yŏnghun: Yes!
△Sound of cicadas is heard.
-Mrs. Yi exits-
-Pause-
Yŏnghun: Until now, I've silently endured being grilled by the entire family about the Higher Civil Service Examination (高文) issue and the marriage issue. <338> ...But I haven't given a definitive answer yet.
Taesŏp: (Silent, contemplative expression)
Yŏnghun: I know there are two paths before me now! One is to drink the bitter cup of sacrifice for my parents, and the other is to choose the path of my own individuality (個性)... -Pause- If I choose the latter, I intend to quit school immediately. If I cannot fulfill my parents' long-cherished desire (宿望)! their entire hope, what's the point of studying using the tuition money from my parents?
Taesŏp: Having read the essay you published the other day, I thought I could guess your recent state of mind to some extent... but reading it, I couldn't help but worry. Above all, your health weakening day by day, and along with it, your will! The fact that I say I can't make up my mind - that I've lost my mental composure - might prove that my own will has weakened, but I can't see it solely as a weakened will. Why? Because firstly, I struggle, saying I won't be dominated by reality, but when I look back at myself already being dominated, what can I do... is there any way?
Yŏnghun: (Silent, head bowed)
Taesŏp: (Weakly, voice slightly lower) Since last year, my father has been telling me to join the County Agricultural Association (郡農會) as an advisor, but I've resisted like this until today... -Pause- Anyway, I want to taste the other side of reality (裏面), that is, the bitter and sweet tastes of reality, more thoroughly. So I'm thinking of trying a wandering life for a bit. But I don't want to leave my hometown. It's not because I have any attachment, but it feels like I'm the only one who can water the withering hometown.
Yŏnghun: Then why try to follow the path of wandering?
Taesŏp: I knew you'd say that. Based on my experiences so far, the time seems ripe...
Yŏnghun: What's the need to even look for the right time? Just step out naked...
Taesŏp: No, reality isn't like that. But, though we talk about reality, reality, is there really any need to be bound even by family? If you were to drink the bitter cup of sacrifice, do you think anyone would erect a monument (碑) saying 'Here lies a dutiful son (孝子)'? ...Perhaps this might sound calculating. No, this too is reality...

**Summary**

The play "Sacrifice" depicts the tragic story of Yŏnghun, a university student torn between his literary aspirations and his family's insistence that he pursue the prestigious Higher Civil Service Examination (Kōbun) for financial stability and social status. Pressured relentlessly by his father, uncle, and grandmother, who represent traditional values and parental expectations overriding individual desire, Yŏnghun reluctantly attempts the exam. This internal conflict, compounded by the pressure, leads to a severe illness (likely tuberculosis). Despite passing the exam, his health deteriorates rapidly. In the second act, set four years later, Yŏnghun lies dying, lamenting his unfulfilled dreams and the sacrifice of his individuality. His final wish is for his younger brother, Ch'anghun, to be granted the freedom he never had, a plea his father sorrowfully grants as Yŏnghun dies.

For historians, the play offers insight into the generational conflict and intense pressures within Korean families during the colonial period. The father's desire for his son to become a high official reflects both personal ambition and a common aspiration for social mobility and security under colonial rule. Yŏnghun's plight highlights the struggles of intellectuals caught between traditional obligations (filial piety) and modern ideals (individuality, artistic expression), often leading to personal tragedy. The mention of economic police and the uncle's precarious business hints at the broader socio-economic anxieties of the time. Ch'anghun's eventual awakening and rejection of the path forced upon his brother signifies a potential shift in the younger generation's perspective.

제1막

舞臺
下手에 부엌과 안방이 있고, 上手에는 사랑이 있다. 사랑<328> 미다지가 활짝 열리어 있는데, 그 방 안에 장식이 그럴 듯하게 보인다. 미다지 앞에는 툇마루가 있으며 안방과 사랑사이에,- 즉 무대 정면에 큰 마루가 있다. 큰 마루 바래지문이 활짝 열리어 있는데, 뒤안의 뽕나무와 담 밑에 꽃나무 등이 보인다.
마루에는 찬장과 두지가 놓여 있고, 실겅에는 床 등이 얹히어 있다. 좌편에는 드나드는 대문이 있고, 우편에는 뒤안으로 통하는 길이 있다. 길 옆에는 감나무 한 구루가 서 있다.

초여름의 아침.

막이 열리면, 어머니는 밥상을 치우고, 祖母는 마루에 앉아서 담배를 피우고 있다. 아버지는 사랑에서 국방색 쓰메에리 양복을 입고 있으며, 영훈은 와이샤쓰 바람으로 툇마루에 앉아 신문을 뒤적이고 있다.- 父, 영훈 신문 뒤적이는 것을 유심히 보면서...
