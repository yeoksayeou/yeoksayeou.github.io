Magazine Title	三千理 第12巻 第5号
Publication Date	1940년 05월 01일
Article Title	雨雪 (Rain and Snow)
Author	姜亨求
Article Type	소설 (Fiction/Novel)

雨雪
						姜亨求
(Oh abundant harvest, come to this land. The candle flame on the altar is solemn)
I am a person who raises chickens (養鷄).
Raising these two hundred or so chickens, it's utterly dark ahead, wondering how I'm supposed to feed these creatures in a year of bad harvest.
Whatever others might do, I quietly decided that the best strategy was to buy up as much feed (飼料) as possible beforehand and hoard it. Driven by this greed, I busied myself arranging for money. The money was finally arranged on the morning of the day when only one meal's worth of feed remained. And so, I took three carts that day and went to the Kyŏngsŏng Feed Company (京城飼料會社) to load up on chicken feed. Impatiently, I first went to check the warehouse. But the wind had already blown through there.
The vast warehouse, comprising three buildings, was utterly empty, with only the cold wind swirling pointlessly inside, and four or five sparrows chirping within. On the carts out front, laborers (人夫) lay sprawled out, gazing up at the clearly <270> clearing sky, blatantly ignoring my presence there.
(What would laborers know?)
While I could ignore them in return, I also felt a sense of desperation.
"Is there absolutely no feed?"
I asked.
"There isn't," one replied.
"Will none be coming in anymore?"
"Go ask at the office."
It was an unpleasant answer. It seemed they were subtly taking out their frustration on me, as the lack of goods coming into the warehouse meant they were the first to lose their jobs.

In the office, a single clerk was on the phone. "Aha, aha," he responded curtly, occasionally cutting off sharply with "We don't have any." Judging by this, it seemed he was also refusing an order for feed over the phone. Hanging up the receiver awkwardly, the clerk turned to me and, before I could say anything, preemptively stated,
"We don't have any feed."
However, I took a step closer to him.
"This is a disaster. I can't just let living animals starve to death. If they were human, they'd have the sense to endure it. But these creatures can't do that, and I can't bear to watch them squawking for food."
In response, the clerk listened blankly, then asked,
"You can buy sacks of rice in the countryside, right?"
I could only interpret this as suggesting that if my chickens were so precious, I should sell rice to feed them.
"But I can't feed them rice either."
"I wasn't talking about chickens."
(Not talking about chickens?)
Did this clerk hear anything I said? There's a limit to looking down on someone. It was infuriating that he was changing the subject after I had spoken so earnestly. The insult I'd received from the laborers earlier combined with this, and I was about to raise my voice, but the thought of "only one meal's worth of feed left" suppressed my anger, keeping me standing there.

The clerk made another call somewhere, asking for someone named Kim-sang (긴상), and then said,
"How is that request going?"
Listening to the response from the other end, he kept saying,
"Yes, yes,"
bowing slightly. It seemed he had made some kind of request to this Kim-sang person. After a while of "yes, yes," the clerk <271> said,
"This is terrible. We only have enough for tonight, you see. I was really counting only on you, Kim-sang, and didn't even mention it elsewhere. That's right, yes, yes. Still, if you put in some effort, Kim-sang, couldn't it be done? Yes? Well, that's true. Absolutely not, I don't know. Starting tomorrow, I'll have to come to your house, Kim-sang, to beg for food. What are you telling me to do then? Starve? Haha, yes, yes. I'm begging you. That's right. Thank you."
The clerk bowed again while hanging up the phone, then
"Ah!"
he sighed, and looking at me, said,
"This isn't the time to be worrying about chicken feed."
The clerk seemed somehow more weary than before.
"Yes,"
was my meaningless reply.
"It's hard to buy rice in Seoul..."
"Ah."
"It's not like that in the countryside, is it?"
"Not yet."
"Could you sell me some rice?"
The clerk was now pleading with me.

I now regret using that situation for commercial negotiation (商畧).
"If you could just arrange the feed for me, would that much be difficult?"
"Well, the feed has to come in first."
"Why are you being like this? They say they can hand it over at the station, don't be too difficult."
"Who says that?"
At this, the clerk chuckled briefly, which I took as confirmation that my guess had hit the mark.
"I'll manage the rice somehow, please just arrange the feed for me."
Mimicking what the clerk had done at the phone, I also bowed before him. Still, the clerk hesitated to speak, then finally said,
"Actually, it's not that... well..."
He stopped mid-sentence, clearly suspicious of me, so I added conviction to my words,
"How many sacks of rice do you need?"
"For now, just to get by, maybe ten sacks (가마)."
"Oh, that much?"
I boasted as if I had plenty of rice right at home. In the realm of business dealings, pretending to have what you don't, as much as possible, is the formula <272> for advancing negotiations (商談) more favorably. I don't think it had an immediate effect, but anyway, the clerk hinted a little at the company's internal situation.
"Actually, it's not that feed isn't coming in at all. Small amounts do arrive, but the large chicken farms and ranches have already contracted for it in advance, so there's nothing left to reach the smaller places."
Where doesn't the power of capital reach? I feigned admiration at the clerk's words.
"Hah!"
"It's the same with rice. No matter how much the economic police investigate, those who have money buy it up secretly whenever they can. It's only needy people like us who buy by the small measure (*toe*)."
I couldn't immediately think of words to console the clerk's lament, making the situation rather awkward. He even offered me a cigarette.
"Looking at it that way, the countryside is better. You can farm and eat as you please."
He seemed to greatly envy me for living in the countryside.
"The countryside is like that too. Is there really a difference between Seoul and the countryside for the poor? The city is actually better, since goods are concentrated there..."
Drawn into the clerk's conversation, the feed issue took a backseat, and our talk about rice lengthened. We became somewhat detached from our initial purposes, perhaps because we had shared our respective difficult circumstances.
"Then I'll trust you completely."
"Don't worry. In return, I'll trust you completely too."
"I'll do my best."
"Thank you."
We agreed on the date and our mutual conditions. When I left, the electric clock at the tobacco shop across the street struck four, its second hand spinning busily.

The enormous shadow called famine spread like a spiderweb over my mind.
Having seen that only one meal's worth of chicken feed remained, what would I feed them tomorrow morning? I instructed the cart driver to go ahead and share even a little of the human food rations for the time being, and then I went to visit poor Ham-gun outside Sŏdaemun.
Ham-gun rented a gatekeeper's room (行廊房) in someone else's house for a monthly fee (사글세). As soon as I looked for him, his wife came out and greeted me.
"Where did Ham-gun go?"
"He went to buy rice. How are you managing to buy rice, Teacher? He's having such a hard time."
If money were truly such a worry, his wife probably wouldn't <273> be able to talk about it to others. Rice, being such a mainstay of life, made people proactive.
"He went to buy rice?"
"Yes."
At my repeated question, his wife deliberately blushed.
"Where did he go?"
"Probably the rice shop just up ahead."
"So you've really gotten the hang of managing the household now."
"What hang? We're in trouble."
"Just hold on to that thought."
"So, will things get progressively worse?"
It wasn't as if I knew what the future held when I said that, nor was it my place to speak carelessly even if I did know. More pressing was my desire to see, as quickly as possible, the sight of the usually steadfast Ham-gun standing there holding a rice sack. Saying "I'll be back," I set out to find the rice shop just up ahead. The shop was a blue-painted building directly facing the street after one alley turn. I remembered Ham-gun once telling me it was a rice shop that had been there since before the road widening. He said the owner (쥔) was incredibly stingy (깍쟁이), notoriously skilled at short-measuring with the *toe* (되질), cheating customers out of a handful (웅쿰) per *toe*. As a result, he had lost favor in the neighborhood and was on the verge of losing his business. Perhaps because of this preconception, the shop indeed didn't look very prosperous. In front of it, at a glance, well over a hundred people stood lined up, holding sacks, gourds, and other containers. It was getting towards evening and the air was gloomy; perhaps they lacked the energy to chatter, but the line was quite orderly and disciplined. I was initially surprised at how well-trained they seemed, but then I understood: policemen patrolled back and forth, suppressing people's spirits with swift strokes of their switches (회차리).
Ham-gun was easy to spot. Holding a book bag instead of a sack, his gaze was fixed towards Sŏdaemun Prison, as if counting its windows, and he seemed unwilling to look away.
I silently approached Ham-gun and nudged his side.
Startled, Ham-gun exclaimed,
"Oh!"
and offered his hand, asking when I'd arrived.
"I came today. Well, you've become quite the dutiful husband now, haven't you?"
Ham-gun gave a wry smile and said,
"Come join the show."
Not understanding what he meant, I asked,
"What show?"
He replied,
"The Street Without Sunshine." <274>
Still confused, I asked again,
"What?"
Disappointed that I didn't catch on quickly, he said,
"That's the Tsukiji Little Theater (築地小劇場),"
gesturing towards the rice shop with his chin. When Ham-gun and I were in Tokyo, we often went to the Tsukiji Little Theater to see modern plays (新劇). Once, on a scorching summer day, we went to see a play called "The Street Without Sunshine." Like the people waiting to buy rice now, we stood in line for about two hours before finally getting in. Ham-gun must have been drawing a comparison. Hearing his words, even the presence of the policemen seemed similar to the scene back then.
"Back then, we went seeking nourishment for the spirit, but now we've come seeking nourishment for the mouth."
"Indeed, that's true."
Though it was only a few years ago, it felt like a story from the distant past. While the temporal distance between now and then was unclear, we felt a spatial nostalgia.
"Therefore, there's a sense of life's reality even more intense than back then."
"That's also true."
"Consequently, there's nothing to be ashamed of, that's what I wanted to say."
Ham-gun smiled wryly again and asked me for a cigarette.
"What's there to be ashamed of?"
"My wife still hasn't come to her senses."
"Why?"
"She insists that I be the one to buy the rice."
"Thanks to her, you get to enjoy this luxury."
"What luxury?"
I gestured with my chin towards a young woman standing several places ahead of him and showed him my little finger. Ham-gun looked at the woman, then said,
"Ah, that reminds me. Hey, back then, why..."
He lowered his voice, pulled my ear close to his mouth, and whispered,
"That beauty, you know? Hey, back then, because of that dimple (에꾸보), I didn't feel bored at all."
'Back then' also referred to when we went to the Tsukiji Theater. The woman Ham-gun remembered was right in front of us then. When she smiled, a dimple like a whirlwind appeared on her cheek. Her plump shoulders, clearly visible through her thin clothes, had also held a great attraction for me. Afterwards, we gave her the nickname 'Dimple' (에꾸보) as a term of endearment (애층명사), often wondering about her at night and hoping to meet her again somewhere, but we never did. Ham-gun, bored from standing so long (지리한), must have been reminiscing about such things while gazing at Sŏdaemun Prison.
"I'm going to tell your wife. That you came to buy rice but only thought <275> about women and kept glancing sideways at the gatekeeper's wife."
"That's a brilliant idea. Then maybe she won't send me out to buy rice anymore. That's a brilliant idea."
I had no response to that.

They didn't sell more than three *toe* (升) of rice per person. For one *mal* (ten *toe*), they included mixed grains. One woman knowingly declared that since Seoul women had lived comfortably eating only refined rice until now, they were selling this half-milled rice to make them pound it themselves. Humans are animals that cannot live without attaching reasons to things at any time. Even spending just a short while among them reveals a kaleidoscope (萬華鏡) of city life (市井). The masses, for me, are like a reservoir, an entity of unfathomable depth. A woman swinging her rice sack grumbled,
"Might as well go begging for food than deal with this..."
Judging by her attire, she wasn't a gatekeeper's wife. I surmised she was trying to restore her trampled pride through such behavior, but I generally dislike such caricatured attitudes (戱畵的態度). I believe one should calmly conform to national policy, whether it's one *toe* or two *toe*. However, this solemn civic duty arises only when the administration's (爲政者) governance is absolutely fair. If merchants, using the emergency as a pretext, make several times their usual profit through exploitation, the responsibility should rightfully fall on the administrators.
The line in front of the rice shop gradually shortened. As people bought their rice and left, those behind shuffled forward cautiously, careful not to lose their spots. Then, when the line had shrunk by more than half, disorder began to break out at the front. When the police officer brandished his switch and threatened them, things quieted down momentarily, but then people started peering over the shoulders of those ahead, craning their necks sideways. The line became even more chaotic than before. It was because the rice was almost gone. The officer ejected one person from the line. This caused even more commotion, and amidst the confusion, an old man pushed past others to get ahead.
"Hey, Mister!"
A young man grabbed him sharply.
"What's it to you?"
The old man yelled and charged.
"Are you the only one who matters?"
"Who said I'm the only one?"
"Then why are you pushing and taking someone else's place?"
"You brat, who do you think you're talking to so informally? Don't you have parents?"
The old man asserted his age. The police officer <276> gently admonished him, "You shouldn't do that," likely out of respect for his age. Despite this, an elderly woman, who had been fidgeting anxiously behind Ham-gun for some time, brazenly tried to cut in front. When Ham-gun blocked her, the grandmother jumped up and down, clinging to Ham-gun's arm, shouting,
"What's a guy in a Western suit doing buying rice like this?"
At the words "guy in a Western suit," the tense crowd burst into laughter. Ham-gun's face turned red, and feeling slightly embarrassed myself, I said,
"Let's go."
But Ham-gun didn't listen. The line reached just a few people ahead of him, and then the rice ran out. The old woman, who had stubbornly pushed her way into Ham-gun's spot and stood there fussing, remained in place and cursed,
"Well, I'll be damned!"
Just then, the streetlights came on.

That evening, I took Ham-gun and his wife to a department store restaurant. There, the rice shortage seemed entirely unrelated. Dishes with more than half the rice left, mixed with scattered cigarette butts and tissue scraps (부시레기) on the tabletops (食卓), covered by other piled-up dishes – these things conspicuously caught my eye. I told myself it was just the ingrained nature of a lifelong poor person and tried to ignore it. But then I thought of the old woman at the rice shop, and unexpectedly worried about how she might have managed her dinner. The poor person's nature freely roamed the spacious restaurant, even dredging up memories of my own leftover-meal (殘飯) life during my Tokyo days.
Ham-gun, who had been busily bringing spoonfuls to his mouth, drank a cup of tea, stretched his back, and said,
"Rice is really the best."
His wife added, supporting her husband's words,
"Beef bone soup (설렁탕) has rice in it too, but honestly, you can't eat it for two days straight as a proper meal (끄니)."
Ham-gun drank another glass of water and then,
"Now I feel alive,"
he said, blinking his large eyes rapidly, the double eyelids seeming quite cumbersome.
"But really, this greeting is too late. What brought you here that you're spending money like this?"
"Because of feeding (멕이) the chickens."
"Ah, right. I completely forgot. Are your daughters all well?"
This was Ham-gun's habit. Knowing how incredibly precious my chickens were to me, whenever he met me, he might forget to ask about my mother's well-being, but he never once forgot to ask about the chickens' health. He called them daughters because I raised them from chicks (병아리) <277> and because they were hens, thus feminizing (女性化) them.
"How could they be well?"
"Why not?"
"Why not? Think about it. Isn't it obvious?"
He paused his spoon for a moment, then pointed at the rice bowl with it and asked in a low voice,
"Is it this?"
I nodded.
"But why is that?"
"The corn, sorghum, and other grains that used to come as feed are now all being used as human food."
"That's serious trouble."
"It is serious trouble."
"So what will you do?"
"What choice is there but to wait and see (觀望)?"

The next day, I took the first train home. Last night at the inn, the couple in the next room hadn't let me sleep at all after turning off their lights, so I was extremely tired. Still wearing my overcoat and hat, I went into the chicken coop (鷄舍).
The chickens, dozing scattered here and there in the sun, all rushed towards me at once. Raised by my hand, they recognized me like this. "Gae-aek gaek," they cried sharply, pecking at my coat tails, one flying up to perch on my hat – all gestures demanding feed. Even the geese (게산이), shifting their heavy bottoms, pecked at my feet going "Whae-aek whaek." Though this too was surely because they were hungry, I had nothing to give them, which was simply distressing.
I wanted to get out of the coop as quickly as possible. This was a feeling I was experiencing for the first time. Usually, seeing these two hundred or so white bodies with bright red combs gathered together was as pleasing as looking at flowers. The sound of chickens laying eggs and geese honking seemed like a peaceful symphony, so much so that I used to bring a desk and chair into a corner of the coop to read. But now, wanting to escape even for a moment – how despicable was that?
However, their hungry cries pierced my nerves sharply like thorns. The more they cried, the harder it became to look at them. I tried to rush out, but surrounded by them, I couldn't even step aside without tripping over them.
I raised my hand and pretended to scatter feed, deceiving them. They all rushed towards where my hand gestured, then surrounded me again.
While I was repeating this act, my mother entered the coop, <278> and they swarmed towards her too. Mother pushed the chickens away with her foot, came towards me, and worriedly asked,
"What are we going to do about this?"
"Didn't you give them anything this morning?"
"I gave them the leftover sorghum."
"We need to give them more."
"Aren't people going to eat?"
"Then how can I bear to watch this right now?"
"You took on this useless task and now you're agonizing over it."
Reluctantly, Mother brought a gourd-bowl full of sorghum she had set aside to eat in the spring. Delighted, I scattered it for them.
Goo-goo-. Goo-goo-.
They're eating. They're eating.
Pushing others aside to eat, they were no different from the people in front of the rice shop, but seeing the chickens do it was somehow endearing.
"Bring more!"
"There's no more now. That was also set aside for us to eat in the spring."
"Bring it!"
I raised my voice at my mother.
"The egg yield has also dropped sharply (알두 쇠배 안났다)."
"How many?"
"It might not even be worth it for the owner (쥔두 못 될라)."
It was a 70% decrease (7활 감이었다). Still, there was nothing to be done. I couldn't expect eggs without maintaining the chickens' livelihood.
"If you wish to receive, you must first give what is yours." I hold this proverb (箴言) as the creed for my chicken farming and agriculture.
My view on farming is not to conquer and exploit nature, but to obtain life's necessities by harmonizing (調和) with and nurturing (助長) it.
And yet, I couldn't sustain the chickens' lives, and in return, they didn't lay eggs for me – this state continued for several days.
Due to malnutrition, the chickens' once bright red combs turned pale, their movements became sluggish and awkward, and they only pecked listlessly at useless clam shells and sand. Pitifully clucking like this all day having barely eaten, they would line up on the roost when the sun set. It was so heart-wrenching that I often avoided going into the coop.
Then one morning, Mother came out after going into the coop.
"Hey, look at this,"
she called to me in my room.
"What is it?"
"Just come out and look."
From her tone, I could guess it wasn't anything pleasant. Indeed, Mother <279> was holding a dead chicken in her hands.
"Why did it die?"
"I don't know."
Its outstretched legs conveyed a cold sensation to my hand, and its closed eyelids couldn't see me. I felt the feed trough. There wasn't a speck of feed. There might have been some other disease, but I couldn't ignore the fact that its strength had failed and its resistance was low because it hadn't eaten its fill. I didn't have the skill to bring a dead chicken back to life.
It might sound ridiculous to be sad about a chicken dying, one that I had fed, but I had put considerable effort into raising them. Raising incubator-hatched chicks without a mother (에미 없이) is never easy.
When a chick that had been playing well until then suddenly starts nodding off constantly, opening its yellow beak wide and chirping "ppiyak ppiyak," I would wrap it in cotton wool and place it in a warm spot near the heated floor, then take it out again and endlessly blow on its bottom (똥구녕). After repeating this several times, eventually, it would open its round eyes wide, flap its tiny, barely formed wings (나래), and try to escape my grasp. If I gently (자긋이) tightened my hand, I could feel the chick's heartbeat and its warm body temperature transferring to my palm, conveying a sense of precious life.
If I placed feed on my palm, it would peck ticklishly (간즈럽게), then flap one wing, poop, fly down to the floor, and run around. If I shooed it,
"Hwo-i,"
it would hide under my knee.
"It's attached,"
Mother would say upon seeing this. Through such devotion, I learned the proper temperature for chicks, recognized their illnesses, mastered treatments, and completely refuted the common saying (定評) among Korean farmers in the countryside that
"Chicken farming fails because you can't raise the chicks."
Chickens raised like this were now being sacrificed one by one in my coop.

"This can't go on."
While I repeated this phrase like a habit, the most I managed was to deceive them by saying it was for human consumption and buy a couple of *sŏm* (섬, sacks) of corn that our township (面) had procured as relief grain for disaster victims (災民), providing temporary relief (救急). Beyond that, I had no other solution.
So, feeling frustrated, I thought about the promise I made with the feed company clerk, intending to at least find out the business situation. I thought that even if it was unlikely he would specially divert feed just because I brought rice, <280> he might at least tell me the true situation. Based on what he said, I would then decide whether to sell the chickens or do something else.
However, buying rice was not easy even in the countryside. People rarely sold it, wanting to get more than the official price (公定價格). I managed to obtain two sacks (가마) with difficulty. One was for Ham-gun.
To take rice from our township to another region required an export permit (搬出許可) from the township head (面長). I went to see the township head and explained the situation.
"If you take this region's food to another region, what will the residents here eat? Isn't that right? Think about it carefully."
The words of the township head, who sported a figure-eight (八) mustache, needed no careful thought; they were undeniably correct. Without another word, I left and started scheming how to transport it secretly.
In principle, I supported the controlled distribution of rice, and I usually thought ill of others who engaged in secret transport. Yet, when I myself planned to do it, it's strange, perhaps, that I felt no pang of conscience.
Perhaps I possess such a selfish nature (아리적근성).

As a method for secret transport, I decided to bury the rice inside bundles of firewood.
Here, it might be convenient to briefly talk about the firewood sellers in our region. Our area is in Koyang (高陽), about 60 *ri* from Seoul, and has been famous for firewood selling since ancient times. In the past, they called it *hoematso*, loading pine branches onto cows to sell. Now, they are called *ttalttalikkun* (딸딸이꾼), pulling carts (구루마). The name *ttalttal* comes from the sound the cart makes, *ttal-ttal-ttal-ttal*. So, I had a *ttalttalikkun* pull the cart with the rice buried in the wood, while I followed behind, pretending to be just an ordinary pedestrian (步行). At a marketplace called *Saemak*, they inspected the rice. Two young men dressed in Civil Defense Corps (警防團) uniforms meticulously checked suspicious luggage heading towards Seoul. They checked cargo trucks particularly closely.
Pulling just the firewood is heavy enough, but with two sacks of rice buried inside, the *ttalttalikkun* seemed considerably (엥가니) strained. It was pitiful to see him sweating as he pulled, but I couldn't rashly (섯불리) help push, fearing the Civil Defense members might notice. So, I could only go into a tavern and watch the Civil Defense members' actions from afar. Countless *ttalttalikkun* passed by. Since there were so many, they were let through, but just as our *ttalttalikkun* was about to arrive, an old man carrying three or four *mal* of rice was caught. The old man pleaded, saying he was taking it to his son in Seoul, but the corps members' attitude was firm. The confiscated rice was to be sold <281> right there in the marketplace.
Now, our cart arrived.
The *ttalttalikkun* tried to act casual to avoid the scrutiny of the Civil Defense members, but just watching him made me feel strained too. A member holding a metal skewer asked,
"Nice wood. Where's it from?"
At his voice, I closed my eyes.
"It's Pyŏkkol wood,"
the *ttalttalikkun*'s voice sounded. Pyŏkkol (壁골) is where the wood comes from.
"Must be worth about ten won."
The member only estimated the wood's price and, like the others, let him pass. I felt a wave of relief. The *ttalttalikkun* just kept pulling, straining (직숙으리고?). However, just then, a cargo truck arrived behind us, and the members rushed towards it. Our rice had completely passed the checkpoint. I hurried (힝옇게) after the cart, patted his shoulder, and praised his effort.
"That was lucky."
He grinned and wiped the sweat from his forehead with his jacket sleeve.
"I really thought we'd get caught."
"Tell me about it."
"We were lucky."
We should have just been moderately pleased about our luck, but celebrating it all the way to the next tavern was, thinking back now, the cause of the trouble. We were hungry, and though it was just sour barley wine, we drank our fill. As we stepped out of the tavern, a Civil Defense member riding a bicycle stopped abruptly.
"Who owns this wood?"
Instantly, I inwardly exclaimed,
"Informed on (密告)!"
The *ttalttalikkun* said,
"It's not for sale,"
and glanced sideways at me.

I was arrested on the spot and transported by bus (뻐스) to the police station in the county seat (읍). Through the bus window, the barley fields looked parched, dust rising from them. In the stream on the right side, following the road, the scant water was frozen here and there. Across it, the mountain was stripped bare of trees, revealing patches of earth (산피둠).
It looked like snow might fall; the sky was overcast, and all the passengers hoped for snow.
Being transported, my mind felt strangely calm. It felt as though something I had wanted to experience had coincidentally happened. Should I be like this? Shouldn't there be some psychological judgment? I thought about this to myself, but aside from hoping for a lot of snow to fall from the heavily overcast sky, <282> I had no other thoughts or premonitions.
The Civil Defense member accompanying me was smoking inside the bus and got reprimanded by the female conductor. This was the same person who had earlier yelled,
"You bastard!"
and delivered a sharp slap to the *ttalttalikkun*'s cheek. I saw the puller's face turn pale and his legs tremble uncontrollably. I felt sorry that he had received an unjust blow because of me, and wondered idly what explanation he would give his wife for that red cheek.
But the informing (밀고) bothered me. Since I wasn't caught red-handed but apprehended later after being chased down, it was clearly due to informing. Who on earth would do such a thing, and what did they gain from it? Being informed on is unpleasant. It often happens to people who have lost favor with others. I thought I had tried my best (하너라고) to be good to others, but one never knows.
"But what does it matter?"
Yet, curious, I finally asked the Civil Defense member.
"How did you know I was carrying rice?"
"They said you talked about it at the tavern."
"I see."
I nodded. The trouble came from my own mouth. I regretted blaming others, even for a moment.
"A marketplace person heard it and came to tell us. How would we know otherwise?"
"Do they get anything for informing like that?"
"The confiscated rice is sold at the market, so ultimately, they do it so they can sell it themselves."
"Doesn't much rice come into the market either?"
"The country folk won't release the rice."
So the informing wasn't meant to harm me specifically, but was also an act of defending their own livelihood?
The bus rattled (들거불거리고) and honked, climbing over a large pass and speeding towards the county seat. The largest building in sight, made of brick, was the police station. It was newly built last year, and I had even been invited to the completion ceremony.

I waited for a long time in the interrogation room.
Through the glass window under a plaque reading "Korea and Manchuria as One" (鮮滿一如), the training ground (道場) was visible, along with a row of striking posts (格劍臺) set up there. Outside, snow was fluttering down. Children ran up and down the alley, catching snowflakes in their hands. I silently prayed for a heavy snowfall. As I sat there absently, a man with two stars on his insignia came in, briefly asked for my address and name, inquired about what I had done, and when I admitted the facts, said the detailed interrogation would be tomorrow. <283> The snow was falling a bit more heavily.
It was decided that I would spend the night (쉬기로 됐다) in the detention cell. My companions for the night numbered eight in total, including myself, four of whom were there for violating economic police regulations. This too was a reflection of the times. As I entered, one man rubbed his first two fingers together and winked, the code asking if I was a gambler (놀음꾼).
When it was time to sleep, the eight of us lying head to head reminded me of the chickens perched on their roost.
After finally drifting off to sleep through repeated pleading (신언고언), I woke up, and the night stretched endlessly boring. I wondered if it was five o'clock yet, but the office clock struck one, adding a bone-chilling loneliness to the mix. The guard must have been adding coal to the stove. Through the bars, the ceiling flickered with light, and the sound of coal burning hissed softly. The sound of the guard turning pages was distinct, and even more so, the ticking of the office clock sounded plaintively sharp.
One, two, three... I thought maybe I'd fall asleep while counting to a thousand, but I lost count before fifty, distracted by other thoughts.
It was a tedious night. The clock striking again from the office.
One, two. Still five hours until dawn, still pitch black.
My stomach rumbled. It must have been the cold floor. I forced myself to hold back the urge to defecate (便意), but finding the time tedious anyway, I thought I'd go to the toilet. There was a urinal inside the cell, but no toilet for defecation. They said it was designed that way when built, and consequently, they boasted that the detention area didn't smell bad.
I spoke to the guard.
"Let me go defecate."
"Hold it. It'll be light soon,"
came the sleepy reply.
"I can't hold it."
I repeated this several times and even stomped my feet like a child. Reluctantly, the guard came shuffling in his slippers, opened the lock with a clatter (델커덕), and said,
"In a famine year, what on earth (왼걸) did you eat so much of to be shitting in the middle of the night?"
Inside the cell, someone clicked their tongue.
Through the barred toilet window, the outside was white with snow. I didn't know how much had fallen, but remembering the old man on the bus earlier looking at the sky and saying,
"We need a lot of snow for the barley to have a good harvest,"
I finished my business and then stood on tiptoe (발돋움) to look outside again in detail (자세). I pressed my <284>(Continued on page 327) face close to the glass window, and it fogged up mistily white (뽀-얗게) from my breath. As I raised my hand to wipe the fogged glass, the guard shouted from behind.
"What are you doing?"
Coming out of the toilet, I said,
"It snowed."
The guard retorted,
"So what if it snowed, you bastard? Get back inside,"
he ordered sternly (호령을 했다). Back in the cell, I tucked my cold hands under my armpits to warm them. I heard the guard muttering to himself (혼잣먈로),
"We need more snow."
The office clock struck three. Still four hours left. A tedious night. (February 1, Gyeongjin Year)<327>

**Summary**

This fictional story by Kang Hyŏnggu, set in 1940 Korea, depicts the narrator's desperate struggle to procure feed for his two hundred chickens amidst severe wartime shortages. Facing empty warehouses and bureaucratic indifference, he resorts to the black market, agreeing to illegally transport rice to Seoul in exchange for promised feed. His attempt fails when he is informed on and arrested. The narrative vividly portrays the pervasive scarcity affecting daily life—from urban dwellers lining up for meager rice rations under police supervision to the narrator's ethical dilemmas and eventual conflict with the law, reflecting the harsh realities and anxieties of colonial Korean society during a period of intense resource control and economic hardship.

For historians, the story offers valuable glimpses into the socio-economic conditions of late 1930s/early 1940s Korea under Japanese rule. It details the mechanisms of rationing (rice, feed), the emergence of black markets, the role of the economic police and local enforcement bodies like the Civil Defense Corps (警防團), the stark contrast between widespread scarcity and visible waste among the privileged (department store scene), and the psychological toll of deprivation on individuals, forcing morally compromising decisions. The casual integration of Japanese elements (company names, honorifics like '-sang', references to Tokyo's Tsukiji Theater) alongside Korean suffering also subtly underscores the colonial context.

선만일여(鮮滿一如)라 쓴 현판 밑 유리창으로 도장(道場)이 보이고 거기 주욱 세워 논 격금대가 보였다. 밖에서는 눈이 퍼떡이었다. 아이들이 눈을 손에 받으며 골목을 아래 위로 달렸다. 제발 많이 오기를 나는 빌며 무심히 앉았을려니까, 별 두 개 붙인 사람이 들어와서 간단히 주소 성명을 묻고, 나의 한 일을 따져 묻기에 사실이라 대답했더니, 자세한 것은 내일 취조한다 했다.<283> 눈은 조금씩 더 날렸다.
