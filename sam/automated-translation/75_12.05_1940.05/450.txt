**Header Section:**
Magazine Title: 삼천리 (Samch'ŏlli) Vol. 12, No. 5
Publication Date: May 1, 1940
Article Title: 戱曲 今日의 夫婦 (全一幕) (Play: Today's Couple (One-Act))
Author: 洪龍澤 (Hong Yongt'aek)
Article Type: 희곡·시나리오 (Play/Scenario)

**Body Text Translation:**
Play: Today's Couple (One-Act)
						Hong Yongt'aek
Characters
Song Uk ............................ 37 years old
Hyera (Song Uk's wife) ................ 26 years old
Chŏngja (Song Uk's daughter) ................ 14 years old
Pak-ssi (Song Uk's ex-wife, Chŏngja's biological mother) ... 40 years old
Hyeju (Hyera's younger sister) ................. 23 years old
P'owŏl (Song Uk's friend) ............. 36 years old
Kŭmnan (Hyera's friend) ............. 27 years old
Beggar old woman
Beggar child
Water seller
Other offstage voices
Time - Modern day - Autumn. A certain Sunday
Place - Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul)
Stage - Song Uk's house.<297>

Stage Description

To the right, a single-leaf main gate. Entering the gate, one sees the kitchen building (婦屋). At the right end is the detached room where Chŏngja resides. Connected to it is the central wooden floor hall (maru). Connected to the maru, on the left, is the main inner room (anpang), but only its sliding door (障子門 - shōji door, here meaning paper sliding door) is visible; the interior is not seen. The detached room and the maru occupy most of the stage.
At the end of the maru sits a dressing table (경대). In one corner of the maru, a desk. On the desk, books and magazines are scattered horizontally and vertically. An oil painting hangs on the back wall. The inner room's interior is unseen, only its sliding door visible.
Chŏngja's room has its double sliding doors wide open. Clothes are hung messily on the wall, and a small desk sits in one corner.
In one corner of the yard is a platform for soy sauce jars (장독대), and beside it, a few flowerpots hold several kinds of wilting flowers.
As the curtain rises—
(Hyera sits before the dressing table, humming a tune, patting her cheeks, busily applying makeup. At this moment, Kŭmnan hides herself outside the slightly open main gate, peeks towards the maru, then kicks the gate with her shoe, making a "thump" sound, and hides behind the gate again. Hyera, startled, stops applying makeup.)
Hyera - Who is it? (Pause - louder) Who is it?
Kŭmnan - (Trying to suppress laughter, giggles)
Hyera - (Softly) Who iiis it?
Kŭmnan - Ahem... (Loud cough)
Hyera - It's Kŭmnan, isn't it? Who are you trying to fool...
Kŭmnan - Hohohoho, (Laughs affectingly, enters carrying a handbag.)
Hyera - (Resuming her makeup) Gosh, you startled me.
Kŭmnan - Hohoh, did you drop the baby?
Hyera - What? How can I drop a baby I haven't even conceived?
Kŭmnan - (Sitting on the edge of the maru) Seriously, why haven't you conceived yet?
Hyera - Goodness— how could I manage with a baby in this kind of household? Don't say such dreadful things.
Kŭmnan - What's dreadful about it? Not being able to have a baby makes you a cripple. And besides, Mr. Song needs you to pop out a baby to snap him to his senses.
Hyera - Hmph, try anything you like... You think that lazy bum (느름뱅이) would pull himself together and look for a job? He'd rather starve where he sits.
Kŭmnan - That's where you're wrong. It's only when a baby is born that a man truly feels the weight of marriage. Realizing the burden will gradually increase, he starts looking for a way to survive. And you too. Instead of whining and thinking about how you'll starve living with Mr. Song and whether you should break up, if you're going to break up, do it decisively now. If you're going to live together anyway, then have a baby, get your husband a job, and live an interesting life, quarreling and making up.
Hyera - Ugh, I hate hearing this. (Makes a rubbing motion with her hands as if praying) Please stop. Please, please, save<293> such sermons for after I'm dead...
Kŭmnan - What's this? I'm offering advice out of concern. Just watch if you don't listen to me. See if you don't ruin your life.
Hyera - Hoho, really, are you drunk? (Resumes applying makeup) Talking nonsense by yourself. (Pause) Anyway, why did you come alone?
Kŭmnan - I came alone, should I have come as half a person?
Hyera - No, I mean, not your current guy, but that new lover (고이비도) you got recently.
Kŭmnan - Oh, that... that half-baked painter?
Hyera - Why half-baked again?
Kŭmnan - What else if not half-baked? He hasn't painted anything but made a name for himself. (Pause) And then he asked me to be his model (모텔)? Really.
Hyera - (Finishes her makeup and sits back from the dressing table) Why, are you tired of him already? For a while, you were saying you hated the stuffy villa and were thinking of breaking up with the old man, making a fuss, and then you snatched up the painter...
Kŭmnan - Hmph, still, listen to me. Ahem, being a so-called New Woman (新女性), you know, for a while, eyes were drawn to sportsmen, but lately, if you look closely, having affairs with artists like painters, poets, or novelists seems to be the trend. Hoho, so I tried imitating others, but that's how it is. Ahem (loud cough).
Hyera - Just get on with it. I'll do the coughing.
Kŭmnan - With so-called artists, first, they consider their art as their life. So, let's say, love or passion, they pour it all into their art, meaning there's no sweetness from the start. So, it's truly dry and uninteresting. (Pause) Second, artists are too spiritual about their art. So, they're utterly poor. And third, they lack physical appeal. Their bodies are weak, and they often suffer from chest ailments...
Hyera - Hoho, you've really seen the hidden life of artists inside out.
Kŭmnan - Just listen. Look at that painter the other day. Hoho, it makes me laugh... Once, sometime ago (Pause) Oh wait, this wasn't the painter, it was, uh, that playwright from some new theater troupe.
Hyera - What? A playwright? So you've been involved with a playwright too? Huh? Gosh, you really...
Kŭmnan - (Cutting Hyera off sharply) Just sit still and listen. Wasn't I at a teahouse in Myŏngchijŏng (明治町) with that playwright once?
Hyera - And then?
Kŭmnan - Well, this masterpiece, even after entering the teahouse, didn't say a single interesting word, just (gestures with head) sat there with his head<299> buried. I got so annoyed I stood up abruptly and said let's go, and hoho— only then did he sluggishly get up. So I went outside first, didn't I? But I waited quite a while, and he didn't come out, and then, well, hohohoho.
Hyera - Just tell me already. Stop laughing.
Kŭmnan - Well, hohohoho, (Laughs affectingly) well, he was busy searching his suit pockets, hohohoho.
Hyera - Hohoho, so what happened?
Kŭmnan - Look here. He said he put the money in his back trouser pocket, but there was a hole, and it fell out somewhere, and he asked if I could possibly cover the tea bill— that's what he said. Hoho, it was so funny...
Hyera - So you paid the tea bill?
Kŭmnan - What else could I do? That's how artists are, dripping with poverty from head to toe. That's why I'm telling you, pull yourself together early on. Otherwise, you'd better roll up your sleeves and go out to earn a living, fully prepared with the sincerity and resolve to support your husband...
Hyera - What? Hmph! I'm sick and tired of it now, dear. You were absolutely right. Since high school, for some reason, I unknowingly found artists sublime...
Kŭmnan - (Cutting Hyera off) And that's why you married Song, who already had a respectable wife and even a child?
Hyera - (Nodding) Yes. But now I hate it to death. (Pause) Can I ever see a cheerful face? He's always gloomy. And always idling around, slickly. What? He doesn't know others' suffering and complains about being bothered...
Kŭmnan - That's why I hate seeing it. I'd rather go back to being a rich man's mistress. What's wrong with being a mistress? Let others look down on me; as long as I'm comfortable, that's all that matters.
Hyera - Phew! (Sighs) I need to figure something out now too. Hoho. (Pause) Moreover, I hate seeing that girl Chŏngja. If it weren't for her, I might somehow try to live with Song.
Kŭmnan - Hmph, looks like there's still some affection left for Song tucked away somewhere?
Hyera - No, really, the one I hate the most right now is that girl. If I get rid of her, Song is mine to control. (Pause) Since he can't earn a living, he listens well to what I say, does as he's told.
Kŭmnan - Hoho, a slave.
Hyera - (Smiling) Of course. I don't know about the future, but right now, he lives in the palm of my hand. But Chŏngja, maybe because she's not my child, I hate her terribly. The way she just watches everything slyly like a little fox—. And you know, once you start hating someone,<300> the hatred just grows and grows. (Pause) Well, when I think about church or the words of Jesus, only then do I feel like a sinner, thinking I shouldn't hate anyone— but not even two hours pass before I hate her again. But seeing Song, or being afraid of my sister, I can't just kick her out.
Kŭmnan - Hmph. Complaining about your fate. I hate hearing it. It ruins the mood. (Pause) Anyway, hey, does that novelist still come over to visit? Uh, P'owŏl or P'osu, that gentleman?
Hyera - Oh, Mr. P'owŏl! I don't know. Do I ever have time to see? I'm always at the company! Or maybe he comes when I'm not here...
Kŭmnan - But I saw his novel in a women's magazine the other day, did you see it? "Yŏsŏng" (女性 - Women)?
Hyera - I don't know when I last read a Korean magazine. Why, was something good published?
Kŭmnan - Yes, it was good. It used you two as a model (모텔).
Hyera - Us? What did it say?
Kŭmnan - It's quite interesting, isn't it? The title is "The Path They Take," and the paths of all the characters in it are different.
Hyera - How so?
Kŭmnan - It's exactly your family situation.
Hyera - Just tell me clearly. Roughly what the novel is about...
Kŭmnan - The heroine is a Christian, but only outwardly; she's quite the hypocrite. And the hero is an artist, solely devoted to one path. So, it goes without saying that a hypocritical, vain woman and a literary student pursuing only truth cannot get along, right? So, a new family formed out of a moment's amusement gets entangled in a web of suspicion and conflict, and eventually breaks apart. Then they each find their own path and go their separate ways.
Hyera - ...
Kŭmnan - So isn't it obvious it's modeled after you two?
Hyera - (Lost in thought, then mutters to herself) That damn bastard.
Kŭmnan - Why are you cursing P'owŏl for that?
Hyera - ...
Kŭmnan - (Takes off her shoes, throws her handbag into the middle of the maru, and enters the inner room) Still, I like P'owŏl. He's ruggedly manly.
Hyera - Oh my! What's with this girl...
Kŭmnan - (Voice only from inside the room) Why, what's wrong— Hey, do you have any good records (레코-드)?
Hyera - Look for them.
-Pause-<301>
(Record sound. First piece is "Kojō no Tsuki" (古城の月 - The Moon over the Ruined Castle). Next piece is the popular song "Wasurecha iya yo" (忘れちやいやよ - Don't Forget Me).)
-Pause-
(Hyeju, simply dressed in a white blouse (저고리) and black skirt (치마), enters carrying a Bible and hymnal.)
Hyeju - Sister, are you here? Why the popular songs?
Hyera - Is that you, Hyeju? Come on in. Kŭmnan is here.
Kŭmnan - (Peeking her head out from the room) Oh, Hyeju? (Hides her head back in the room) This isn't good. A seminary student has arrived, and I'm playing popular songs...
Hyeju - Hoho. That sister always teases (히니꾸) me whenever she sees me. (Record sound stops.)
Hyera - It's just because she's a tomboy—.
Kŭmnan - What? (Comes out and sits on the maru) Why am I a tomboy? Look at me, I'm quite the virtuous wife (賢夫人).
Hyera - Right. Since your belly is full, you don't nag anymore, so you're a virtuous wife instead.
Hyeju - (Smiling)
Kŭmnan - Sure, whatever, as long as I'm called a virtuous wife...
Hyeju - (Stepping onto the maru) What brings you here today?
Kŭmnan - I'm always free, why only today? Hyeju just hasn't seen me. Are you doing well at school?
Hyeju - Yes.
Kŭmnan - You're staying in the dormitory, right?
Hyeju - Yes.
Kŭmnan - Oh, that must be nice. Still in your school days.
Hyera - Jealous?
Kŭmnan - Of course.
Hyeju - (Laughs.)
Kŭmnan - Hyera pays your tuition (學費), right?
Hyeju - Yes, thanks to my sister...
Kŭmnan - But Hyera?
Hyera - Why, what story are you going to tell now?
Kŭmnan - So you pay for your sister's tuition but make your daughter drop out before even finishing elementary school? That's quite something for a Christian to do.
Hyera - (Signals Kŭmnan with her eyes not to let Hyeju see.)
Kŭmnan - (Winks one eye shut and pouts her lips.)
Hyeju - Oh, sister, what are you saying...
Kŭmnan - Hoho, Gomen nasai (고멘나사이 - Sorry). I was just talking nonsense.
Hyeju - Hoho, I thought... (Turning to Hyera) Where did brother-in-law and Chŏngja go?
Hyera - They all went out on their own business.
-Pause-
Hyeju - Shall we sing a hymn? With sister Kŭmnan...
Kŭmnan - I haven't even been to the threshold of a church yet.<302>
Hyeju - Why don't you believe? Please start believing, even from today. If you don't believe in God, your heart will be greatly troubled. Vanity too. Things like materialism, hatred towards others, or the despair from failure, or pessimism— it's all because of not believing in God. It's the lack of spiritual nourishment. Please believe in Jesus starting today.
Kŭmnan - Well, someone like me has too many sins...
Hyeju - That's why you should believe in Jesus. To wash away those sins.
Hyera - Looks like Kŭmnan might become a believer today too, hoho.
Kŭmnan - Hey, you should be the faithful one— hoho.
Hyeju - Why? Do you know how long my sister has been a believer? She's even been baptized...
Kŭmnan - Hyera has? (Looks at Hyera.)
Hyera - (Again, blinks at Kŭmnan.)
Kŭmnan - (Smiles faintly.)
Hyeju - Please start believing today. Okay? (Pause) Come on, sister, let's sing a hymn.
(At this moment, the clock in the inner room strikes two.)
Kŭmnan - Oh my, it's already two o'clock.
Hyeju - Why, do you have an appointment somewhere?
Kŭmnan - Yes, I was supposed to meet someone at one-thirty. Hyera, hurry up and get ready?
Hyera - Right, Hyeju, could you watch the house for a bit?
Hyeju - Where are you going, is it something nice?
Hyera - Yes, going to meet someone at Ch'ŏnyŏnjŏng (天然亭). (Goes into the inner room.)
Hyeju - Just when I came over, everyone is...
Kŭmnan - Hoho, sorry. We'll meet again next time. (Steps down onto the stepping stone.)
-Pause-
Hyera - (Comes out of the room having changed clothes, speaking to Hyeju) Then I'll be back. (Steps down onto the stepping stone, puts on her shoes, and exits through the gate.)
Kŭmnan - Well, goodbye then. (Starts towards the gate, then) Oh, my handbag. (Turns abruptly and walks back towards the maru.)
Hyeju - (Hands the handbag from the maru to Kŭmnan, tossing it) Is this it?
Kŭmnan - Yes, thank you. (Catches it and exits through the gate.)
Hyera - (Voice only) Have you lost your mind buying candy?
Hyeju - Goodbye to one, and have a good trip to the other.
Kŭmnan - (Voice only) Yes—hoho.
Hyera - (Voice only) Okay.
-Pause-
Hyeju - (Humming, goes to Song Uk's desk and rummages through the books.)
-Pause-
(Chŏngja carefully opens the gate and enters, hesitatingly glancing at the maru and inner room. Hyeju continues rummaging through the books.)
Chŏngja - Auntie, you're here?<303>
Hyeju - (Turns her head sharply as if startled) Oh my, you scared me. How did you come in without making a sound?
Chŏngja - (Smiles faintly and climbs onto the maru to sit.)
Hyeju - What's that wound on your face?
Chŏngja - (Bows her head deeply.)
Hyeju - (Moves closer to Chŏngja) Why, did you get hurt?
Chŏngja - Huh? (Pause) Uh, while playing...
Hyeju - Hmm? How do you play to get such a wound on your face? (Pause) By the way, why haven't you been coming to church lately?
Chŏngja - ...
Hyeju - (Gently) Why haven't you been coming, hmm?
Chŏngja - ...
Hyeju - Tell me, hmm?
Chŏngja - Mother— told me— not, not to go.
Hyeju - Mother did?
Chŏngja - -Pause-. She told me to work at home—. So lately, she hasn't even sent me out for errands.
Hyeju - (Blinks, lost in thought, then) Okay, then, I'll talk to Mother, so make sure you come regularly from next time. You're a bad child if you don't go to church, okay?
Chŏngja - Yes.
Hyeju - (Picks up her hymnal and Bible and steps down into the yard) You're not going anywhere, right?
Chŏngja - No.
Hyeju - Then don't go anywhere, study and watch the house. I'm leaving now. (Pause) Take care, Chŏngja. (Exits)
Chŏngja - Yes.
-Pause-
Chŏngja - (Goes to the dressing table, looks at her reflection, and gently presses the wound.)
-Pause-
Beggar Old Woman - (Opens the gate slightly) Please give some rice.
Chŏngja - (Looks out.)
Beggar Old Woman - Please give some rice. Oh, I'm so hungry.
Chŏngja - (Runs out, opens the gate, stares intently at the beggar old woman, then) Wait a moment.
Chŏngja - (Goes into the kitchen, frequently glancing towards the door, hesitates, then scoops cold rice into a gourd dipper, hurries out, and gives it to the beggar old woman.)
Beggar Old Woman - Oh, thank you so much. Such a kind child. May you grow up to enjoy wealth and honor. (The beggar old woman disappears down the back alley. Chŏngja leans against the gate, watching the beggar old woman's retreating figure blankly.)
-Pause-
Chŏngja - (Takes the empty gourd dipper back to the kitchen, climbs onto the maru, and sits idly.)<304>
Water Seller - (Enters carrying water, making a creaking sound. Followed by a beggar child outside the gate.)
Beggar Child - Ma'am, please give some rice.
Chŏngja - (Gets up and stares blankly outside the gate.)
Water Seller - (Goes into the kitchen, pours the water, and comes out, speaking to Chŏngja) Are you alone?
Chŏngja - Yes.
Beggar Child - Please give some rice, Ma'am!
Water Seller - Hmph, the beggar knows you're alone and keeps coming. Just now, a beggar grandmother stopped by your house too, right?
Chŏngja - (Nods her head.)
Water Seller - See? Knowing you're alone, the swarm of beggars keeps coming. Big trouble now. Just watch, see if hundreds don't show up now. (Exits through the gate, speaking to the beggar child) Hey kid! Nobody's home— Hmph, a perfectly healthy kid begging. Did you come from the countryside too?
Beggar Child - Yes. Please give some rice.
Water Seller - (Going down the alley) Hey kid! I told you, the master's not home.
Chŏngja - (Goes to the gate, stares intently at the beggar child) Did you come from the countryside?
Beggar Child - (Nodding) Uh-huh.
Chŏngja - (Lost in thought, then) Come in here. Quickly, okay?
Beggar Child - (Enters the yard holding a rice container.)
Chŏngja - Do you not have a mother either?
Beggar Child - I have one.
Chŏngja - Where is she?
Beggar Child - Begging for rice like me.
Chŏngja - Are there many beggars where you live?
Beggar Child - (Nods.)
Chŏngja - (Runs into the detached room and searches the desk drawer.)
-Pause-
Chŏngja - (Comes back to the beggar child) Hey? Someone moved my mother's picture. (Pause) Do you know my mom?
Beggar Child - Don't know. (Pause) Don't you have any rice?
Chŏngja - Rice? I just gave the little bit I had to the beggar grandmother.
Beggar Child - Don't you have money either?
Chŏngja - I don't have money. Shall I give you a book? A worn-out one...
Beggar Child - Don't want it. Don't you have even a little rice?
Chŏngja - None, what can I do?
Beggar Child - (Looks around the kitchen area, then opens the gate and leaves.)
Chŏngja - (Leans against the gate, watching the beggar child's retreating figure for a while, then goes back up to the maru, rummages through Song Uk's desk drawers, murmuring to herself) Who hid it somewhere? Mother's picture.
(Song Uk enters.)
Song Uk - (Going up onto the maru) Are you alone?
Chŏngja - (Hesitates, then goes into the detached room) Yes.<305>
Song Uk - (Takes off his hat, hangs it on the wall, looks into the detached room) What's that on your face?
Chŏngja - (Fiddles with the ribbons of her blouse, head bowed low, silent.)
Song Uk - Did Mom hit you again?
Chŏngja - ...
Song Uk - Why did she hit you this time?
Chŏngja - (Bursts into tears.)
Song Uk - Was it about the streetcar ticket again?
Chŏngja - ...
Song Uk - You have to tell me, okay? Chŏngja. (Soothingly) Tell me straight. Hmm? Where did you get the streetcar ticket?
Chŏngja - (Sobbing) I borrowed it from Sunok... At school yesterday, for the outing... sob... we had to take the streetcar... sob, sob. But Mother asked where I stole it from... sob—.
Song Uk - She hit you for that?
Chŏngja - (Just cries.)
Song Uk - (Sighs heavily, goes to the desk, bows his head deeply, and sits in anguish.)
(P'owŏl enters.)
P'owŏl - (Opens the gate slightly, peers inside, then hides outside the gate again and whistles sharply.)
Song Uk - (Lifts his head wearily) P'owŏl?
P'owŏl - (Enters carefully, showing his little finger to Song Uk.)
Song Uk - (Smiling sadly) She's not here, not here. Come on up.
P'owŏl - (In a deep voice) Not here? Haha, visiting you once makes me sweat, really sweat.
Chŏngja - (Stops crying.)
P'owŏl - By the way, you don't look well lately. Maybe it's because you haven't cut your hair and your beard is shaggy. (Goes up onto the maru and sits.)
Song Uk - Do I look unwell?
P'owŏl - Absolutely, like half a person. Being tormented by the wife again, I suppose? (Pause) By the way (looks into the detached room), why did you make Chŏngja cry?
Song Uk - Chŏngja, aren't you going to greet this teacher?
Chŏngja - (Just wipes her tears with both hands.)
P'owŏl - Did Madam Hyera play the hero again?
Song Uk - (Nods.)
P'owŏl - Tch, pathetic fool. How long are you going to watch this? More than anything, isn't poor Chŏngja pitiful?
Song Uk - It's heartbreaking.
P'owŏl - Do this, it's simple. Either find Chŏngja's biological mother and entrust her, or else send her to your cousin in Suwŏn. And you, just take off for somewhere like Tonggyŏng (Tokyo) or the Manju (Manchuria) region— take a spin. To rest your head<306> a bit too.
Song Uk - Man, are you dreaming? Look at my hair now, look at my beard.
P'owŏl - No, if you really decide to leave, I'll try to arrange something.
Song Uk - Your words alone are appreciated.
P'owŏl - Then?
Song Uk - Well—.
P'owŏl - Tch, this guy. (Pause) Think about it deeply. You still have some attachment, don't you?
Song Uk - ...
P'owŏl - (Lights a cigarette) Oh right, you're even skipping cigarettes these days, right? (Pulls out four packs of "Midori" from his suit pocket and hands them to Song Uk) Here, I bought cigarettes. Smoke up.
Song Uk - You even bought me cigarettes. (Opens one pack, takes one out, and lights it. Takes a deep drag, exhales, then) Do you have some money left?
P'owŏl - Yes, I do. I sold some manuscript pages recently. Why, what do you need it for? A haircut? (Pulls out several bills from his suit pocket.)
Song Uk - No, not much (gestures towards the detached room) just give our Chŏngja a few pennies. So she can buy some sweet pancakes (호떡).
P'owŏl - Oh right, actually, I was going to buy some snacks but (lowering his voice) I didn't know if your wife would be here, so I came empty-handed. Here, (waves a 1-won bill) Chŏngja. Take this and buy whatever you want to eat, okay?
Song Uk - No, just give her ten chŏn (十錢 - 10 sen), just ten chŏn. If she has too much money, it'll just cause trouble.
P'owŏl - Haha, right. (Goes into the detached room, gives Chŏngja a 10-chŏn coin) Go buy candy. Okay? Want more? (Gives another 10 chŏn) Here, hurry. (Gives the money and returns to sit beside Song Uk) By the way, what's that wound on her face, huh?
Song Uk - She says she got it while playing.
P'owŏl - (Looking at Chŏngja) Oh, then it's fine. That's how you grow up.
Chŏngja - (Clutching the money in one hand, wiping tears with the other, exits through the gate.)
Song Uk - Business must be good lately.
P'owŏl - Sure, am I like you? I just always spend. (Hands three bills to Song Uk) This isn't much, but use it for a haircut and a drink.
Song Uk - Thank you. If you give it, I'll take any amount. Hehe. (Smiles sadly, receives the money, and puts it in his suit pocket.)
P'owŏl - Hehe, do you know what money that is?
Song Uk - Money for a haircut and drinks, what else?
P'owŏl - Hahahaha, haven't you seen "Yŏsŏng"?
Song Uk - The magazine company doesn't send it, and I don't go to bookstores, so<307> how could I see any magazines?
P'owŏl - Your wife too?
Song Uk - I don't know... Why?
P'owŏl - Then that's fortunate. If she finds out now, I'm in for it, really in for it!
Song Uk - Why?
P'owŏl - Huh? Just let it be, haha. (Glances towards the door) Well then, see you again. I have some business to attend to... (Stands up.)
Song Uk - What keeps you so busy? You always seem busy?
P'owŏl - That's how I still manage to eat and live.
Song Uk - By the way, is it true that Paek Sin'ae (白信愛) died?
P'owŏl - Hah, man, are you dreaming? Three have already passed away this year. Han Int'aek (韓仁澤), Sin'ae, Yongch'ŏl (龍喆).
Song Uk - Hmm, they're all dying off well. Should I just die too?

**Summary**

The one-act play "Today's Couple" depicts the breakdown of a marriage in 1940 Kyŏngsŏng (Seoul). Song Uk, an unemployed aspiring artist, lives with his second wife, Hyera, and his teenage daughter from his first marriage, Chŏngja. Hyera, frustrated by poverty and resentful of her stepdaughter, clashes constantly with both Chŏngja and Song Uk. The tension escalates with visits from Hyera's cynical friend Kŭmnan, her pious sister Hyeju, and Song Uk's friend P'owŏl, who has written a thinly veiled story about their dysfunctional family. The climax occurs when Chŏngja's biological mother appears, leading to a confrontation where Hyera violently rejects her. Chŏngja runs after her mother, and Song Uk, finally pushed too far by Hyera's accusations and dismissal of his writing, declares their marriage over and leaves. Hyera is left alone amidst the remnants of their life, sobbing. From a historical perspective, the play vividly portrays the economic hardships and social tensions in colonial Korea, particularly the struggles faced by artists, the precarious position of women (both the "New Woman" like Hyera seeking material comfort and the traditional, abandoned wife like Pak-ssi), and the complex dynamics within families navigating poverty and changing social norms under Japanese rule. The references to contemporary writers and popular culture (Japanese songs, magazines) ground it firmly in its time.
