戱戱曲 放浪詩人 金삿갓
						宋影 (Song Yŏng)

Explanation

Kim Sakkat's real name was Kim Pyŏngyŏn (金柄淵). He was born about 135 years ago from now, on the 13th day of the 3rd month of the Chŏngmyo year (丁卯年) (around 1805 AD, during the late Yi Dynasty, reign of King Sunjo). At that time, the rotten politics that had persisted for hundreds of years had reached its peak (factional strife, purges of scholars, rule by the queen's relatives, etc!).

Among these, one of the biased political practices of the time, the old custom that "Northwestern people should not be appointed to important positions" (「西北人勿爲重用」), was particularly causing public resentment (民怨). This policy, "Northwestern people should not be appointed to important positions," was established out of the groundless fear that power would be usurped because there were many great talents among the Westerners (people from Hwanghae and P'yŏngan Provinces) and Northerners (people from Hamgyŏng Province). The first to raise the banner of revolt against this was the Hong Kyŏngnae Rebellion (洪景來亂) in P'yŏngan Pukto (Sinmi Rebellion, 辛末叛亂).

Kim Pyŏngyŏn's grandfather, Kim Ik-sun (金益淳), was serving as the Anju Pyŏnbangsa (安州邊防使, Frontier Defense Commander of Anju) (also called Sŏnch'ŏn Pusa, 宣川府使, Governor of Sŏnch'ŏn) at the time. In the middle of the night, while deeply asleep after heavy drinking (大醉), he was captured (捕虜) by Hong Kyŏngnae's forces and unavoidably surrendered.

Because of this, after the rebellion was suppressed, Kim Ik-sun was executed (處刑), and his descendants became a ruined family (廢族) <280> (from the time of the 5th generation descendant, Kim Yŏngjin (金榮鎭) (Kim Sakkat's grandson) (currently living), the path to success in official life (榮達) was reopened). Pyŏngyŏn was a man of outstanding literary talent (文才) but could not be appointed to office (等用) because he was from a ruined family. Due to this, he became pessimistic about the world (비관하고서), put on a "sakkat" (삿갓, bamboo hat), wandered for his entire life, and died away from home (客死) in the Tongbok region of Chŏlla Namdo.

When Constructing the Play

1.  I did not focus heavily on facts (partly because the facts are not originally passed down) but rather strove only to express the atmosphere of that era through the life (emotions) of Kim Sakkat. Therefore, an excessive amount of romanticism (浪漫) flows through it.
2.  Regarding whether Sakkat had a stable family or not, due to his status as belonging to a ruined family, historians hold different views (or rather, assumptions (想定)). However, I concluded (단정했다) that he must have had a home, even if it was a hidden one (隱家). And Koksan is the place where he lived when he was raised by his servant (從僕) Kim Sŏngsu. However, for the needs of the play's structure, I temporarily assumed (假定했다) his family was there.
3.  The incident at the writing contest (백일장), the event at the gisaeng Kyehyang's house, and the reunion (해후하다) with Ŭmjŏn are also purely facts created from my imagination (공상).
4.  For the top prize poem (狀元詩) at the writing contest, although in reality it would have been an examination poem (科詩), I substituted one of his nature poems (自然詩), specifically the exquisite (묘하고도) and popular (통속적인) Snow Scene Poem (雪景詩).
5.  And regarding the dialogue terms (even the tone of speech (어투)) of the characters of that time, I tried as much as possible to evoke the scent of that era, but inevitably, modern language is occasionally mixed in, which makes me feel very uncomfortable (매우 마음에 찌부드드하다). - Author -

Stage: Black curtain
A poem is written large and posted (His Poem Chanting of the Hat, 詠笠詩)

一着平生四十秋 (Wearing it once, a lifetime of forty autumns.)

滿天風雨獨無愁 (Amidst sky-filling wind and rain, I alone have no sorrow.)
Kim Sakkat (Chants the poem above)

From stage left (下手), a Shepherd Boy (牧童) carrying a fodder basket (꼴망태) enters and listens.
Woodcutter Boy: Who are you?
Kim Sakkat: It's me.
Woodcutter Boy: Who is 'me'?
Kim Sakkat: Sakkat.
Woodcutter Boy: Isn't a sakkat something you wear?
Kim Sakkat: What one wears is a sakkat, but I too am Sakkat.
Woodcutter Boy: What?
Kim Sakkat: (Laughs loudly, 阿阿大笑)
Woodcutter Boy: Why do you just laugh?
Kim Sakkat: Isn't the world laughable?
Woodcutter Boy: What are you talking about? <282>
Kim Sakkat: This is all talk laughing at the world.
Woodcutter Boy: What do you do for a living, sir?
Kim Sakkat: I am a person who lives wearing only a sakkat.
Woodcutter Boy: Why do you only wear a sakkat?
Kim Sakkat: Why do you wear a sakkat?
Woodcutter Boy: Since I carry a rake and basket, and drive calves, what else would I wear but a sakkat?
Kim Sakkat: (Recites softly) The bushy-haired cowherd carries a whip (챗죽) and wears a sakkat, the old fisherman on the riverbank (강포) holds a fishing rod and wears a sakkat... But listen. The sakkat I wear, I hang it on flower branches when pleasantly drunk (만취되여), and when excitement arises in the middle of the night, I take it off and carry it to go view the moon.
Woodcutter Boy: What?
Kim Sakkat: 醉來脫掛看花樹 (When drunk, I take it off and hang it to see the flowering trees,)
興到携登翫月樓 (When excitement comes, I carry it up to gaze at the moonlit pavilion.)

From stage right (上手), Old Fisherman enters carrying a fishing rod.
Old Fisherman: Whoever you are, you recite well.
Woodcutter Boy: He says he's an elder who lives inside a sakkat.
Old Fisherman: Lives inside a sakkat? Hey there! Whose household are you from?
Kim Sakkat: Sakkat.
Old Fisherman: You mean you have no family name or given name?
Kim Sakkat: What value is a name that cannot make itself known widely (헛날니지 못하는)?
Old Fisherman: Why can't you make it known? You look like you know your letters (글짜). Why don't you take the state examination (과거) and try to rise in the world and make a name for yourself (입신양명)?
Kim Sakkat: Hmph...
Old Fisherman: Why do you just laugh?
Woodcutter Boy: This elder laughs at the drop of a hat, you know.
Old Fisherman: Why?
Woodcutter Boy: (Mimicking Sakkat's tone) The world is laughable, so how can one not laugh?
Kim Sakkat: ...
Old Fisherman: Ah ha! Now I see, old brother, you are no ordinary sakkat-wearer.
Kim Sakkat: The sakkat is an ordinary sakkat, but there's a separate reason why I wear it.
Old Fisherman: What reason is that?
Kim Sakkat: A sakkat is also official attire. A formal hat (갓), a crown (冠), a straw hat (草笠), or a wide-brimmed hat (風子) are all official attire (衣冠). However, all other kinds of official attire are "things for outward show" (것치장거리) worn to display one's prominence to others, but this sakkat alone is not like that. Even jade crowns (玉冠子) and gold crowns (金冠子), and even humble felt hats fear the rain and snow, but doesn't this sakkat alone fear neither rain, nor snow, nor wind?
Old Fisherman: Is that the only reason?
Kim Sakkat: (Chants)
俗子衣冠皆外飾 (The worldly man's robes and hats are all external adornments,)
滿天風雨獨無愁 (Amidst sky-filling wind and rain, I alone have no sorrow.)
Old Fisherman: I feel like that's not the only reason.
Kim Sakkat: Actually, it's not. I wear it because I hate looking at the sky.
Woodcutter Boy: Why do you hate looking at the bright blue sky?
Kim Sakkat: I wear it because I hate looking at the world. <283>
Old Fisherman: Why do you say you hate looking at the world?
Kim Sakkat: Ask the world.
Woodcutter Boy: How can I ask the world?
Kim Sakkat: Then try asking this sakkat.
Woodcutter Boy: What? Really, sir!
Old Fisherman: Hey now, stop talking such nonsense and tell us straight, please! It's frustrating!
Kim Sakkat: If you're frustrated, then follow the fish caught on your hook and head out to the vast and boundless (浩浩滄滄한) sea. Go on.
Old Fisherman: Hey, what is all this talk?
Kim Sakkat: This is all the talk of the sakkat, Hmph...
Old Fisherman: That fellow must be mad.
Kim Sakkat: The whole world is twisted (빗틀니고) and distorted (찌그러젔는데), so how can you tell me to live straight (뚝바로)?
Woodcutter Boy: Then tell me, why did you come to wear the sakkat?
Kim Sakkat: You want to know that much?
Old Fisherman: Truthfully, I'm curious.
Kim Sakkat: (Recites softly) The lowliest among official attire is the sakkat. The "outcast child" (어붓자식) of the world is also the sakkat. Can anyone wearing a sakkat put on airs (뽑내지) saying, "Look at me"? The sakkat's companions are, at best, a calf or a fishing rod. A rake or a hand hoe (호미). A humble hut (오막사리) or the servants' quarters (행낭방).
- This is why I am Sakkat -
Within the sakkat that receives only contempt (賤待) and ridicule (嘲弄), I shall instead mock them! Woodcutting boy!
Woodcutter Boy: (Looks up)
Kim Sakkat: Hunchbacked fisherman!
Old Fisherman: (Approaches)
Kim Sakkat: Do you really want to know that much about 'why I live wearing a sakkat'? Then I will roughly tell you my story. The story I tell is the sakkat's story. The sakkat is me. I am the sakkat. Now, listen well.
- Dark (暗) -
Sound of traditional music (Effect, 効果)

Writing Contest (百日場)
On the pavilion stage (亭上), the Governor of this prefecture sits with neighboring county magistrates (隣邑方伯) and village elders (村翁) (Ch'osi, Chinsa, Saengwŏn, etc.) seated in rows (列座). Below the stage (亭下), general officials (官屬) and young Confucian scholars fill the seats (滿座).

On the stage, the examinations (考試) are conducted.

Time: Late spring afternoon.
Chinsa: (Reads one piece several times, then slaps his knee)
People on stage fix their gaze there.
Chinsa: (Giving it to the Governor) Your Excellency, please look at this. Truly, it is a phoenix (鳳) mixed among chickens, jade (玉) buried among pebbles.
Governor: (Reads it silently) Ahh... indeed, what literary skill! To think that such an outstanding talent (秀才) exists in our district is truly beyond my wildest dreams.
Magistrate: It is all due to Your Excellency the Governor's achievement of good governance (善治之功).
Ch'osi: Indeed it is.
People below the stage become tense.
Governor: Hmph... Not at all. Then shall we make this the top winner (壯元)?
Chinsa: With literary talent (文才) like this, even if he goes up to Hanyang, he's sure to win the top prize at the Alseong examination (알성장원).
Governor: Hmph... Another renowned statesman (名相) will emerge. Hmph... But what about the others?
Chinsa: (Picking up another sheet he had set aside) Next, this one is at least somewhat passable (免無識). We have no choice but to pick this one for second place (부장원), even if forced.
Governor: (Looking at it) (Comparing it to the one in his hand) How much difference is there compared to this one?
Chinsa: Well, they are incomparable. Truly, there is a heaven-and-earth difference (천양지차). <285>
Ch'osi: (Having read it at some point) Humph! Is this even writing? Ah, truly clumsy (拙劣) beyond compare.
Governor: Then, Your Excellency, let's just select one top winner (都壯元). (Picks up the poem deemed clumsy and throws it among the pile of rejected entries) Get rid of this. Now, make the announcement.
Chinsa: (Stands up) Yes. Hey there... The top winner will now be announced. This new winner, the author of this poem, is an extraordinary talent (기재) of the world, a future renowned statesman. If you hear it recited, you will all gauge (斟酌) its quality, but the writing is as vast (浩瀚) as the sea, and the spirit as grand (웅장) as a great mountain. Every year, our district has produced not a few new talents, but never an outstanding literary talent like this new top winner. Truly unprecedented and unrepeatable (前無後無). Everyone, lend your ears and respectfully listen to this new writing. Now, before announcing the name, I will recite the poem. Girls!
Gisaeng 1, 2: Yes... (Step forward and receive the poem sheet) (The two gisaeng clear their throats and recite together in harmony)
Snow Scene (雪景) (In Tang recitation style, 唐吟體)
飛來片片 三春蝶 (Fluttering flakes arrive, butterflies of the third spring month,)
踏去聲聲 六月蛙 (Stepping sounds echo, frogs of the sixth summer month.)
寒將不吉 多言雪 (The cold portends misfortune, much-spoken-of snow,)
醉或以留 更進杯 (Perhaps stay due to drunkenness, offer another cup.)
On and below the stage, the air is filled with emotion, admiration (美仰) (mixed with jealousy, 猜忌).
Governor: (Slapping his knee) Exactly, exactly! The falling snowflakes look like white butterflies fluttering in the third spring month, and the sound of stepping on snow is indeed like the croaking of frogs in summer. Yes, exactly, exactly!
Ch'osi: Truly exquisite, exquisite (절묘절묘)!
(The gisaeng return the poem sheet to the Chinsa and step aside)
Chinsa: Now, let's reveal (띠여 보시도록) the name.
Governor: Let's do so. Whose descendant could it be? Hey—
Ibang: Yes... (Receives the poem sheet from the Chinsa and unfolds the back)
Ibang: Koksan Kim Pyŏngyŏn (谷山 金炳淵), Kim Pyŏngyŏn.
Servant: (Muttering) Koksan Kim Pyŏngyŏn, Kim Pyŏngyŏn. (His expression changes, but he doesn't show it.)
Kim Pyŏngyŏn stands up.
Everyone looks at him.
Governor: Tell him to come up.
Ibang: Kim Pyŏngyŏn! Top Winner Kim Pyŏngyŏn!
(Raises his hand)
(Servant raises his hand)
Military music (吹打) plays.
Kim Pyŏngyŏn ascends to the stage (亭上).
Gisaeng guide (welcome) him.
Yun Sŏngsun (Governor's son) sits with his head bowed.
Kim Pyŏngyŏn bows (挹) to the Governor and others and stands.
Military music stops.
Governor: Who did you say you were?
Kim: I am Kim Pyŏngyŏn. <286>
Governor: Our eyes were dim, discovering a literary talent (文章) like you so late.
Kim: I am overwhelmed with gratitude. Though I am still young (年少) and, moreover, possess shallow learning and meager talent (淺學菲才), despite my limitations (不願하온), I humbly ask for punishment (請罪) for this student's (侍生) audacity (당돌하옴).
Governor: Don't be too modest (謙辭).
Chinsa: "The mind of deference and humility (辭讓之心) is the foundation of propriety (禮之端)"—indeed, it should be so—What texts have you read?
Kim: Nominally, I have pretended to read the Seven Classics (七書), the Book of Rites (禮記), the Spring and Autumn Annals (春秋), the Brief History (史畧), and poetry and prose-poetry (詩賦). However, I have not yet attained the mastery of entering the inner room (入室之妙).
Ch'osi: Ah, not only his writing but also his speech is excellent. His manner (態) of responding readily to questions (應口輒對) has the style of Su Qin and Zhang Yi (蘇張).
Magistrate: His face also has the appearance of an immortal sage (仙風道骨). They say "Body, speech, writing, judgment" (身言書判)—was this phrase indeed meant for this person? Hmph...
Governor: Who is your father?
Kim: His name is An (安) followed by Kŭn (根).
Governor: An'gŭn, Kim An'gŭn—Who is Kim An'gŭn? (Aside, 倚白) What does your esteemed father do?
Kim: He is just at home.
Governor: Kim An'gŭn! Does anyone happen to know him?
Chinsa: (As if remembering) Wait a moment. Kim An'gŭn? Kim An'gŭn, Kim An'gŭn... Then, hey you.
Kim: Yes.
Chinsa: Who is your grandfather (祖父)?
Kim: (Hesitates to answer immediately)
Chinsa: What is his esteemed name?
Kim: (Summoning courage) It is Ik (益) followed by Sun (淳).
Chinsa: (Startled) Kim Ik-sun! Kim Ik-sun! What government post did he hold?
Kim: I am told he served as the Anju Pyŏnbangsa (安州邊防使).
Chinsa: What? You say you are the grandson of Anju Pyŏnbangsa Kim Ik-sun?
Governor: What? You mean Kim Ik-sun who surrendered to the traitor (逆賊) Hong Kyŏngnae?
Magistrate: What kind of incident (變) is this? If he surrendered to a traitor, he is also a traitor. How can the child of a traitor hold his head up and walk around? Huh?
Ch'osi: If he was a traitor, wouldn't his three generations (三族) have been exterminated (滅)? How can his descendant be alive?
Kim: (Confidently) This student (侍生) is certainly the descendant of the Anju Pyŏnbangsa. The state decreed that my grandfather's (한아버님) fault (허물) ended with his generation, and from my father onwards, we are considered to have no connection to it.
Governor: I don't want to hear it. Even if you received His Majesty's (상감마마) great benevolence (鴻恩) and avoided punishment (刑罰), you are still the children of a criminal. What a disgraceful (괫심한) fellow! Relying on your meager talent (才操), how dare you presumptuously (엉강생심) show your face in such a gathering (좌석)?
Chinsa: Absolutely. Look here, Pyŏngyŏn. You are the children of a ruined family (廢族). Having committed a crime that fills heaven and earth, with what sense of shame (염치) do you try to stand shoulder-to-shoulder with the people of the world?
Ch'osi: Then you should consider your status!
Magistrate: Your Excellency the Governor, please take swift action (措處). I cannot bear being under the same roof (簷下) as the son of a criminal; I am truly fearful and apprehensive (悚懼). <287>
Governor: Get down immediately!
Kim: If you command me to go down, I shall go down, but are you not being excessively biased (偏僻)?
Governor: What? You insolent pup!
Chinsa: He truly has the blood of a traitor; his audacity (放孜) is boundless.
Governor: Throw this fellow down!
Ibang: The order is to throw him down!
Servant: Yes, sir! (Drags Kim down)
Kim: On the verge of tears, he falls prostrate (업푸러진다) at the foot of the stairs (階下).
Governor: Listen here, fellow. Your crime of deceiving the eyes of those high and low and acting presumptuously (外濫) is not light, but considering today's auspicious occasion and thinking commendably (가상히) of your meager talent (才操), I grant you a special pardon (赦). From now on (從此 이후로), go into the mountains and spend the rest of your life (여생) among nameless woodcutter boys and herdsmen (樵童牧夫). I believe this is the duty (도리) you ought to uphold.
"That's the traitor's son," the crowd (群衆) murmurs and points fingers (손꼬락질).
Governor: Everyone, listen. Kim Pyŏngyŏn, who won the top prize today, is the descendant of Kim Ik-sun, who was part of the clique (도당) of the traitor Hong Kyŏngnae, who started a rebellion in Yonggang (龍岡), P'yŏngan-do 15 years ago, in the Sinmi year (辛末). Although he avoided the calamity affecting three generations (三族之患) thanks to the state's great benevolence (鴻恩), is he not still the son of a traitor? For this reason, today's top prize is hereby nullified (말살).
Chinsa et al.: Your words are most proper (지당하신)! (Throwing down the poem sheet) Here! Take it away!
Kim: (Receives the poem sheet and tears it up like a madman, 밋친듯이) What use is this? Where is such a law? What connection is there between a fault in one's ancestors and oneself?
Is there no law by which mulberry fields become blue sea (桑田碧海), and magpies (烏鵲) become phoenixes (鳳凰)?
Governor: Say no more!
Kim: How can I not speak? Having suffered such a wrongful matter, how can I remain silent like a mute (벙어리)? Your Excellency (상공)! All you esteemed sirs seated filling the high seats (당상)! How can your hearts be so narrow? Is it not the duty of all you sirs to foster (북돋아 주다) the growing new sprouts (자라나는 새싹)? With what intention do you try to trample down (짓밟으시려고) with your stubborn (완고하신) large feet this newly emerged, weak (軟弱) sprout that knows nothing of the seasons (아무 철 모르는)?
Governor: What is this fellow saying?
Chinsa et al.: That fellow is insolent!
Kim: (In a tearful voice) I am merely a youth (弱冠) who knows nothing. However, having roughly read the old books, I can gauge (斟酌) what kind of people our ancestors were. Our ancestors were merely rotten scholars (腐儒) bound only by chapters and verses (章句).

**Summary**

This text presents the beginning of a play by Song Yŏng about Kim Sakkat (Kim Pyŏngyŏn), a famous wandering poet from the late Chosŏn Dynasty. The preface explains Kim Sakkat's background: his grandfather, Kim Ik-sun, surrendered during the Hong Kyŏngnae Rebellion, leading to the family being stripped of status (廢族). Despite his talent, Kim Pyŏngyŏn faced discrimination, culminating in his rejection at a local writing contest purely due to his ancestry. Angered by this injustice and the hypocrisy of the ruling class, he decides to abandon a settled life, dons a bamboo hat (sakkat) as a symbol of defiance and social critique, and embarks on a life of wandering, using satire and mockery against the world. Act 2 begins 20 years later, showing interactions involving a gisaeng, Kyehyang, and her admirers, setting the stage for further episodes in Kim Sakkat's life. The author explicitly states the focus is on capturing the *atmosphere* of social decay and injustice of the era, particularly the discrimination against people from the northwest (Sŏbukin), rather than strict historical accuracy, employing imaginative elements within the play format.

For historians, this piece offers insight into how historical figures and events like the Hong Kyŏngnae Rebellion and the associated social discrimination (废族, 西北人勿爲重用) were interpreted and dramatized during the Japanese colonial period (1941). The author's commentary reveals a conscious effort to critique past injustices of the Chosŏn era, potentially as an indirect commentary on contemporary issues, while navigating the constraints of the time. The language used, blending older Korean forms with emerging modern usage, is also of linguistic interest.

戱戱曲 放浪詩人 金삿갓
						宋影
「解說」
金삿갓의 본명은 金柄淵이다. 지금으로부터 약 135년전인 丁卯年 3월 13일에 (그때는 李末 純祖朝 西歷 1805年頃)탄생되다. 당시로 말하면 수백년동안 내려오든 썩은 정치가 극도에 달해 있었다. (明黨, 士禍 外戚 집권 등!)
그 중에도 그때 偏僻된 정치의 하나인 「西北人勿爲重用」이란 묵은 폐단이 더욱히 民怨을 사고 있었다. 西人과(黃海, 平安道), 北人은(咸鏡道人) 偉傑이 많음으로 정권을 빼았길가하는 기우때문에 세워진 것이 즉 「西北人勿爲重用」이며 여기에 첫번으로 반기를 들고 일어난 것이 平北의 洪景來亂이다. (辛末叛亂)
金柄淵의 祖父되는 金益淳씨는 당시에 安州邊防使(或稱 宣川府使)로 있었는데 夜半에 大醉해서 잠이 들었다가 洪景來軍에게 捕虜가 되어서 부득기 항복을 했다.
이 때문에 반란평정 후에 金益淳씨는 처형이 되고 그의 자손들은 廢族이＜280＞ 되었다.(5世孫, 金榮鎭씨(삿갓의 孫)(現存)때부터는 다시 榮達의 길이 터젔다) 柄淵은 탁월한 문장이 었으나 廢族이기 때문에 등용이 못 되었다. 이래서 그는 세상을 비관하고서 「삿갓」을 쓰고 일생을 방랑하다가 全南 同福지방에서 객사를 했다.

戱曲을 構成할때
1, 사실에 치중하지 않고(원래 사실이 전해지지 않고 해서) 다만 金「삿갓」의 생활(감정)을 통해서 그때 그 세대의 분위기를 나타내려고만 힘을 썻다. 때문에 도에 지내친 浪漫이 흘는다.
2, 삿갓이 廢族이었든 관계로 일정한 가정이 있었다 없었다 하고들 史家마다 다른 견해(보다도 想定)를 가지고 있다. 그렇나 나는 비록 隱家라도 있었으리라고 단정을 했다. 그리고 谷山은 그가 從僕 金成秀에게 길니울 때에 살든 곳이다. 그러나 나는 극의 구성상 필요에 의해서 그의 가정을 그 곳에 暫定(假定했다)
3, 백일장사건, 기생계향집 사건, 또 音全이와 해후하는 것들도 순전히 나의 공상으로써 창조된 사실이다.
4, 백일장의 狀元詩는 사실로는 科詩이었겠지만 나는 그의 自然詩 중에서 그 중 묘하고도 통속적인 雪景詩로 대신했다.
5, 그리고 그때 인물들의 대화용어(어투까지라도)는 될 수 있는 대로 당시에 냄새를 풍기려고 힘을 썻으나 어쩔 수 없이 현대어가 가끔 섞여저서 매우 마음에 찌부드드하다. -作者-
