**Body Text Translation:**
Acute Gastroenteritis, Common in Spring – Prompt Treatment is Crucial to Prevent it Becoming Chronic
						By Medical Doctor Okabe Taku

As spring deepens, gastrointestinal functions tend to slacken. When gastrointestinal function is inactive, it easily invites various stomach and intestinal disorders. Among these, gastroenteritis (胃腸가다루) is the most common gastrointestinal illness that anyone can easily catch due to careless eating ("食事の不注意"). Therefore, it is necessary to be well-informed about the appropriate measures and treatment methods for such occasions.

There are two types of gastroenteritis: acute and chronic. Most cases start acutely, and if not completely cured, they can sometimes transition into a chronic state. However, cases that develop slowly from the beginning are certainly not few.

If you engage in excessive drinking and eating ("暴飮暴食"), consume spoiled food or drinks, excessively hot food, or overly cold beverages, it often causes sudden abdominal pain accompanied by vomiting and diarrhea. Commonly called "meokkkyeotda" (맥켰다 - indigestion) or "geollyeotda" (걸렸다 - caught something), this is acute gastritis (急性胃가다루).

When afflicted with this illness, the mouth becomes dry, the tongue develops a white coating (白苔), appetite disappears, and food loses its taste. The stomach area feels heavy and sore, with sharp pains, nausea, belching, and a bitter taste filling the mouth. Bad breath (惡臭) occurs, urine output (尿量) decreases, and one may experience constipation (便秘) or diarrhea (泄瀉), along with symptoms like dizziness (眩氣), headache (頭痛), and general fatigue (全身倦怠).

Regarding the appropriate measures, first, eliminate the harmful food and temporarily stop eating to give the stomach and intestines rest. At this time, it is especially important to keep the abdomen (腹部) warm and rest comfortably lying down.

If possible, fast (絶食) for about a day. Initially, start with liquid foods (流動食) like thin rice gruel (미음) or porridge (죽), then gradually introduce easily digestible ("消化키쉬운") foods in small amounts, slowly progressing towards a normal diet (普通食). However, since the mucous membrane (粘膜) of the stomach and intestines is irritated and weakened<92>, even food thought to be easily digestible can unexpectedly cause trouble, reversing the course of recovery just when treatment was going well. This point requires particular caution.

At such times, it is necessary to take medicine like "Wakamoto" (와까모도), which strengthens the cells organizing the stomach and intestines while simultaneously aiding digestion.

"Wakamoto" (와까모도) contains several types of active digestive enzymes (活性消化酵素), including diastase (澱粉消化酵素 - starch digesting enzyme), lipase (脂肪消化酵素 - fat digesting enzyme), and trypsin (蛋白消化酵素 - protein digesting enzyme), thus aiding the digestion of consumed food and promoting the recovery (恢復) of gastrointestinal function. Furthermore, "Wakamoto" (와까모도) is rich in essential components (主要 成分) such as the Vitamin B complex ("비타민B複合體"), vitamins A and D, high-quality protein (高級蛋白質), fats (脂肪), carbohydrates (含水炭素), and minerals (無機質). This sufficiently supplements nutritional deficiencies arising from dietary restrictions, prevents weakening (衰弱), and speeds up the body's (自體) recovery. If management and recuperation during the acute phase are done well like this, gastroenteritis can be treated in a relatively short period (短時日). If the measures are insufficient (不充分), it can easily transition (移行) into a chronic condition or lead to complications (倂發) with other serious diseases, particularly making one susceptible to pulmonary tuberculosis (肺結核).

This "Wakamoto" (와까모도) costs 1 Won 60 Cheon for a 25-day supply (sold at a negotiated price depending on the region) and is sold at pharmacies (藥店) throughout Korea (朝鮮).<93>

**Summary**

This 1941 article by Dr. Okabe Taku advises readers on managing acute gastroenteritis, common in spring. It describes symptoms like abdominal pain, vomiting, diarrhea, loss of appetite, and fatigue, often caused by poor dietary choices. The recommended treatment involves rest, fasting, gradually reintroducing easily digestible foods, and keeping the abdomen warm. The article strongly promotes the supplement "Wakamoto," detailing its digestive enzymes and vitamins (B complex, A, D) as beneficial for recovery and preventing nutritional deficiencies. It warns that improper care can lead to chronic conditions or complications like tuberculosis. For historians, the article is interesting as it reflects public health concerns and medical advice dissemination in colonial Korea. The prominent, almost advertorial, inclusion of a specific Japanese product, "Wakamoto," highlights commercial interests intertwined with health information and the presence of Japanese goods and influence in the Korean market during this period. The mix of Japanese (author, product name, some terms) and Korean text further illustrates the linguistic landscape of the time.
